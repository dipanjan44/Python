<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">World energy resources</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Remaining_oil.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="695" data-file-width="573" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Remaining_oil.svg/250px-Remaining_oil.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Remaining_oil.svg/375px-Remaining_oil.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Remaining_oil.svg/500px-Remaining_oil.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Remaining_oil.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Remaining oil</b>: Breakdown of the remaining 57 <a href="/wiki/Zetta-" title="Zetta-">Z</a><a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a> oil on the planet. The 11 ZJ of future additions to the recoverable reserves could be optimistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>World energy resources</b> are the estimated maximum capacity for energy production given all available resources on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. They can be divided by type into <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuel">Fossil fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fossil fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">Fossil fuel</a></div>

<p>These are the proven energy reserves; real reserves may be up to a factor 4 larger. Significant uncertainty exists for these numbers. Estimating the remaining fossil fuels on the planet depends on a detailed understanding of Earth's crust. While modern drilling technology makes it possible to drill wells in up to 3 km of water to verify the exact composition of the geology, one half of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> is deeper than 3 km, leaving about a third of the planet beyond the reach of detailed analysis.
</p><p>In addition to uncertainty in real reserves, there is significant uncertainty in technological and economical factors that impact what percentage of reserves can be recovered gainfully. In general the easiest to reach deposits are the first extracted. Factors affecting the cost of exploiting the remaining reserves include the accessibility of fossil deposits, the level of sulfur and other pollutants in the oil and the coal, transportation costs, and societal instability in producing regions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coal#World_coal_reserves" title="Coal">World coal reserves</a></div>
<p>Coal is the most abundant and burned fossil fuel. This was the fuel that launched the industrial revolution and has continued to grow in use; China, which already has many of the world's most polluted cities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> was in 2007 building about two coal-fired power plants every week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Coal is the fastest growing fossil fuel and its large reserves would make it a popular candidate to meet the energy demand of the global community, short of global warming concerns and other pollutants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> the proven reserves of coal are around 909 billion tonnes, which could sustain the current production rate for 155 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> although at a 5% growth per annum this would be reduced to 45 years, or until 2051. With the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> it is possible to make liquid fuels such as diesel and jet fuel from coal. In the United States, 49% of electricity generation comes from burning coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a></div>
<p>Natural gas is a widely available fossil fuel with estimated 850 000 km³ in recoverable reserves and at least that much more using enhanced methods to release shale gas.  Improvements in technology and wide exploration led to a major increase in recoverable natural gas reserves as shale fracking methods were developed.  At present usage rates, natural gas could supply most of the world's energy needs for between 100 and 250 years, depending on increase in consumption over time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil">Oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">Oil reserves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">Peak oil</a></div>
<p>It is estimated that there may be 57 ZJ of oil reserves on Earth (although estimates vary from a low of 8 ZJ,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-10">[10]</a></sup> consisting of currently proven and recoverable reserves, to a maximum of 110 ZJ<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oil_Gas_Industry_Stats_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oil_Gas_Industry_Stats-11">[11]</a></sup>) consisting of available, but not necessarily recoverable reserves, and including optimistic estimates for unconventional sources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">oil sands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>. Current consensus among the 18 recognized estimates of supply profiles is that the peak of extraction will occur in 2020 at the rate of 93-million barrels per day (mbd). Current oil consumption is at the rate of 0.18 ZJ per year (31.1 billion barrels) or 85 mbd.
</p><p>There is growing concern that <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil production</a> may be reached in the near future, resulting in severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_price_increases_since_2003" title="Oil price increases since 2003">oil price increases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj111907golddavis_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj111907golddavis-12">[12]</a></sup> A 2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Economy,_Finance_and_Industry_(France)" title="Minister of the Economy, Finance and Industry (France)">French Economics, Industry and Finance Ministry</a> report suggested a worst-case scenario that could occur as early as 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oil2004_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oil2004-13">[13]</a></sup> There are also theories that peak of the global oil production may occur in as little as 2–3 years. The ASPO predicts peak year to be in 2010. Some other theories present the view that it has already taken place in 2005. World crude oil production (including lease condensates) according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_EIA" title="US EIA">US EIA</a> data decreased from a peak of 73.720 mbd in 2005 to 73.437 in 2006, 72.981 in 2007, and 73.697 in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to peak oil theory, increasing production will lead to a more rapid collapse of production in the future, while decreasing production will lead to a slower decrease, as the <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">bell-shaped curve</a> will be spread out over more years.
</p><p>In a stated goal of increasing oil prices to $75/barrel, which had fallen from a high of $147 to a low of $40, <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a> announced decreasing production by 2.2 mbd beginning 1 January 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Political considerations over the security of supplies, environmental concerns related to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> are expected to move the world's energy consumption away from fossil fuels. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> shows that about half of the available petroleum resources have been produced, and predicts a decrease of production.
</p><p>A government moves away from fossil fuels would most likely create economic pressure through <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">carbon emissions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_tax" title="Green tax">green taxation</a>. Some countries are taking action as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, and further steps in this direction are proposed. For example, the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> has proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_European_Union" title="Energy policy of the European Union">energy policy of the European Union</a> should set a binding target of increasing the level of renewable energy in the EU's overall mix from less than 7% in 2007 to 20% by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roadmap21_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-roadmap21-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The antithesis of sustainability is a disregard for limits, commonly referred to as the Easter Island Effect, which is the concept of being unable to develop sustainability, resulting in the depletion of natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Some estimate that, assuming current consumption rates, current oil reserves could be completely depleted by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fuel">Nuclear fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nuclear fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">Nuclear power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_policy" title="Nuclear energy policy">Nuclear energy policy</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_energy">Nuclear energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nuclear energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">Nuclear fuel</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> estimates the remaining uranium resources to be equal to 2500 ZJ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This assumes the use of <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactors</a>, which are able to create more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> material than they consume. <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> estimated currently proved economically recoverable uranium deposits for once-through fuel cycles reactors to be only 2 ZJ. The ultimately recoverable uranium is estimated to be 17 ZJ for once-through reactors and 1000 ZJ with reprocessing and fast breeder reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Resources and technology do not constrain the capacity of nuclear power to contribute to meeting the energy demand for the 21st century. However, political and environmental concerns about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_safety" title="Nuclear safety">nuclear safety</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a> started to limit the growth of this energy supply at the end of last century, particularly due to a number of <a href="/wiki/List_of_civilian_nuclear_accidents" title="List of civilian nuclear accidents">nuclear accidents</a>. Concerns about <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear proliferation</a> (especially with <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> produced by breeder reactors) mean that the development of nuclear power by countries such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_program_of_Iran" title="Nuclear program of Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> is being actively discouraged by the international community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although at the beginning of the 21st century uranium is the primary nuclear fuel worldwide, others such as thorium and hydrogen had been under investigation since the middle of the 20th century.
</p><p>Thorium reserves significantly exceed those of uranium, and of course hydrogen is abundant. It is also considered by many to be easier to obtain than <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. While <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_mines" title="Uranium mines">uranium mines</a> are enclosed underground and thus very dangerous for the miners, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> is taken from open pits, and is estimated to be roughly three times as abundant as uranium in the Earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1960s, numerous facilities throughout the world have burned <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Thorium</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fusion">Nuclear fusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nuclear fusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternatives for energy production through fusion of hydrogen has been under investigation since the 1950s.  No materials can withstand the temperatures required to ignite the fuel, so it must be confined by methods which use no materials.  Magnetic and inertial confinement are the main alternatives (<a href="/wiki/Cadarache" title="Cadarache">Cadarache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">Inertial confinement fusion</a>) both of which are hot research topics in the early years of the 21st century.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">Fusion power</a> is the process driving the sun and other stars. It generates large quantities of heat by fusing the nuclei of hydrogen or helium isotopes, which may be derived from seawater. The heat can theoretically be harnessed to generate electricity. The temperatures and pressures needed to sustain fusion make it a very difficult process to control. Fusion is theoretically able to supply vast quantities of energy, with relatively little pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Although both the United States and the European Union, along with other countries, are supporting fusion research (such as investing in the <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> facility), according to one report, inadequate research has stalled progress in fusion research for the past 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_resources">Renewable resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renewable resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">Renewable resource</a></div>
<p>Renewable resources are available each year, unlike non-renewable resources, which are eventually depleted. A simple comparison is a coal mine and a forest. While the forest could be depleted, if it is managed it represents a continuous supply of energy, vs. the coal mine, which once has been exhausted is gone. Most of earth's available energy resources are renewable resources. Renewable resources account for more than 93 percent of total U.S. energy reserves. Annual renewable resources were multiplied times thirty years for comparison with non-renewable resources. In other words, if all non-renewable resources were uniformly exhausted in 30 years, they would only account for 7 percent of available resources each year, if all available renewable resources were developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy">Solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a></div>
<p>Renewable energy sources are even larger than the traditional fossil fuels and in theory can easily supply the world's energy needs. 89 PW<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tester_2005_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tester_2005-26">[26]</a></sup> of solar power falls on the planet's surface. While it is not possible to capture all, or even most, of this energy, capturing less than 0.02% would be enough to meet the current energy needs. Barriers to further solar generation include the high price of making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a> and reliance on weather patterns to generate electricity. Also, current solar generation does not produce electricity at night, which is a particular problem in high northern and southern latitude countries; energy demand is highest in winter, while availability of solar energy is lowest. This could be overcome by buying power from countries closer to the equator during winter months, and may also be addressed with technological developments such as the development of inexpensive energy storage. Globally, solar generation is the fastest growing source of energy, seeing an annual average growth of 35% over the past few years. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_India" title="Solar power in India">India</a> are the major growing investors in solar energy. Solar power's share of worldwide electricity usage at the end of 2014 was 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">Wind power</a></div>
<p>The available wind energy estimates range from 300 TW to 870 TW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tester_2005_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tester_2005-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Using the lower estimate, just 5% of the available wind energy would supply the current worldwide energy needs. Most of this wind energy is available over the open ocean. The <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> cover 71% of the planet and wind tends to blow more strongly over open water because there are fewer obstructions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_and_tidal_power">Wave and tidal power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wave and tidal power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">Wave power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">Tidal power</a></div>
<p>At the end of 2005, 0.3 GW of electricity was produced by <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renewables2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renewables2006-29">[29]</a></sup> Due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">tidal forces</a> created by the Moon (68%) and the Sun (32%), and the Earth's relative rotation with respect to Moon and Sun, there are fluctuating tides. These tidal fluctuations result in <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> at an average rate of about 3.7 TW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another physical limitation is the energy available in the tidal fluctuations of the oceans, which is about 0.6 EJ (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exa" title="Exa">exa</a><a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Note this is only a tiny fraction of the total <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">rotational energy of the Earth</a>. Without forcing, this energy would be dissipated (at a dissipation rate of 3.7 TW) in about four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half" title="Half">semi</a>-<a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">diurnal</a> tide periods. So, dissipation plays a significant role in the tidal dynamics of the oceans. Therefore, this limits the available tidal energy to around 0.8 TW (20% of the dissipation rate) in order not to disturb the tidal dynamics too much.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Waves are derived from wind, which is in turn derived from solar energy, and at each conversion there is a drop of about two orders of magnitude in available energy. The total power of waves that wash against the earth's shores adds up to 3 TW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal">Geothermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geothermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">Geothermal power</a></div>
<p>Estimates of exploitable worldwide <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal energy</a> resources vary considerably, depending on assumed investments in technology and exploration and guesses about geological formations. According to a 1999 study, it was thought that this might amount to between 65 and 138 GW of electrical generation capacity 'using enhanced technology'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Other estimates range from 35 to 2000 GW of electrical generation capacity, with a further potential for 140 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">EJ</a>/year of direct use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-GEO-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2006 report by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> that took into account the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Geothermal_System" title="Enhanced Geothermal System">Enhanced Geothermal Systems</a> (EGS) concluded that it would be affordable to generate 100 GWe (gigawatts of electricity) or more by 2050, just in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, for a maximum investment of 1 billion US dollars in research and development over 15 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INEL_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-INEL-35">[35]</a></sup> The MIT report calculated the world's total EGS resources to be over 13 YJ, of which over 200 ZJ would be extractable, with the potential to increase this to over 2 YJ with technology improvements - sufficient to provide all the world's energy needs for several <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INEL_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-INEL-35">[35]</a></sup> The total heat content of the Earth is 13,000,000 YJ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-GEO-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">Biomass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Biomass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a></div>
<p>Production of biomass and biofuels are growing industries as interest in sustainable fuel sources is growing. Utilizing waste products avoids a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" title="Food vs fuel">food vs fuel</a> trade-off, and burning <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane gas</a> reduces greenhouse gas emissions, because even though it releases carbon dioxide, carbon dioxide is 23 times less of a greenhouse gas than is methane. Biofuels represent a sustainable partial replacement for fossil fuels, but their net impact on <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions depends on the agricultural practices used to grow the plants used as feedstock to create the fuels. While it is widely believed that biofuels can be carbon-neutral, there is evidence that biofuels produced by current farming methods are substantial net carbon emitters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Geothermal and biomass are the only two renewable energy sources that require careful management to avoid local depletion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydropower">Hydropower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_energy_resources&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hydropower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a></div>
<p>In 2005, hydroelectric power supplied 16.4% of world electricity, down from 21.0% in 1973, but only 2.2% of the world's energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
