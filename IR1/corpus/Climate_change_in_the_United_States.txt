<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in the United States</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(October 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U.S._Temperature_Record_(1950_to_2009)_(PNG).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="686" data-file-width="840" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png/325px-U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png/488px-U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png/650px-U.S._Temperature_Record_%281950_to_2009%29_%28PNG%29.png 2x" width="325"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:U.S._Temperature_Record_(1950_to_2009)_(PNG).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. temperature record from 1950 to 2009 according to the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA)</div></div></div>
<p>Because of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, there has been concern in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and internationally, that the country should reduce total <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> which is relatively high <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a> and is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="List of countries by greenhouse gas emissions">second largest</a> in the world after China, as of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, the United States experienced its warmest year on record.  As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the thirteen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warmest_years_on_record" title="Warmest years on record">warmest years for the entire planet</a> have all occurred since 1998, transcending those from 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1950 to 2009, the American government's surface temperature record shows an increase by 1 °F (0.56 °C), approximately. Global warming has caused many changes in the U.S. According to a 2009 statement by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA), trends include lake and river ice melting earlier in the spring, plants blooming earlier, multiple animal species shifting their habitat ranges northward, and reductions in the size of glaciers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Your explanation here (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some research has warned against possible problems due to American climate changes such as the spread of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> and possibilities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods">floods</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droughts" title="Droughts">droughts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midwest_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-midwest-4">[4]</a></sup> Changes in climate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regions_of_the_United_States" title="Regions of the United States">regions of the United States</a> appear significant. Drought conditions appear to be worsening in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_southwest" title="American southwest">southwest</a> while improving in the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">northeast</a> for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-letters-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> committed in the December 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Climate_Change_Summit" title="Copenhagen Climate Change Summit">Copenhagen Climate Change Summit</a> to reduce carbon dioxide emissions in the range of 17% below 2005 levels by 2020, 42% below 2005 levels by 2030, and 83% below 2005 levels by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrdc.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrdc.org-6">[6]</a></sup> In an address towards the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">U.S. Congress</a> in June 2013, Obama detailed a specific action plan to achieve the 17% carbon emissions cut from 2005 by 2020. He included such measures as shifting from <a href="/wiki/Coal_power_in_the_United_States" title="Coal power in the United States">coal-based power generation</a> to solar and natural gas production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bypass_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bypass-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change is seen as <a href="/wiki/Climate_security" title="Climate security">a national security threat</a> to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and others, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil companies</a> knew that burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">oil and gas</a> could cause global warming since the 1970s but, nonetheless, <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil_climate_change_controversy" title="ExxonMobil climate change controversy">funded deniers for years</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151105_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151105-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20150708_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20150708-10">[10]</a></sup> 2016 was a historic year for billion-dollar weather and climate disasters in U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions_by_the_United_States">Greenhouse gas emissions by the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions by the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHG_per_capita_2000.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="940" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/GHG_per_capita_2000.svg/350px-GHG_per_capita_2000.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/GHG_per_capita_2000.svg/525px-GHG_per_capita_2000.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/GHG_per_capita_2000.svg/700px-GHG_per_capita_2000.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHG_per_capita_2000.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Per capita anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions by country for the year 2000 including land-use change, per the <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="357" data-file-width="722" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg/325px-CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg/488px-CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg/650px-CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg 2x" width="325"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emissions_by_the_elctricity_sector.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking" title="Fracking">Fracking</a> is reducing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by the United States</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions_by_the_United_States" title="Greenhouse gas emissions by the United States">Greenhouse gas emissions by the United States</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_register_of_greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="United States federal register of greenhouse gas emissions">United States federal register of greenhouse gas emissions</a></div>
<p>The United States was the second top emitter in terms of CO<sub>2</sub> from fossil fuels in 2009. It produced 5,420 million metric tons (abbreviated as mt) of the substance, constituting 17.8% of the world's total at the time. The nation was also the second highest emitter in terms of all greenhouse gas emissions, including construction and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>-related changes, in 2005. Specifically, the U.S. produced 6,930 mt (15.7% of the world's total). In the cumulative emissions between 1850 and 2007, the U.S. was at the top in terms of all world nations, involved with 28.8% of the world's total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardianlist_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardianlist-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>'s emissions have outpaced the U.S. in CO2 from 2006 onward. The U.S. produced 5.8 billion metric tons of CO2 in 2006, compared to the 6.23 billion coming from China. Per capita emission figures of China are about one quarter of those of the U.S. population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The single largest source of greenhouse gas emissions in the U.S. is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_generation" title="Power generation">power generation</a>. For example, data from 2012 put that share as 32% of the total compared to the 28% of emissions related to <a href="/wiki/Transportation_in_the_United_States" title="Transportation in the United States">transportation</a>, 20% from industry, and 20% from other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inventory_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-inventory-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to data from the US Energy Information Administration the top emitters by fossil fuels CO2 in 2009 were: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_China" title="Climate change in China">China</a>: 7,710 million tonnes (mt) (25.4%), US: 5,420 mt (17.8%), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_India" title="Climate change in India">India</a>: 5.3%, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Russia" title="Climate change in Russia">Russia</a>: 5.2% and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Japan" title="Climate change in Japan">Japan</a>: 3.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardianlist_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardianlist-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the cumulative emissions between 1850 and 2007 the top emitters were: 1. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">US</a> 28.8%, 2. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_China" title="Energy in China">China</a>: 9.0%, 3. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Russia" title="Energy in Russia">Russia</a>: 8.0%, 4. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Germany" title="Energy in Germany">Germany</a>  6.9%, 5. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Energy in the United Kingdom">UK</a> 5.8%, 6. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Japan" title="Energy in Japan">Japan</a>: 3.9%, 7. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_France" title="Energy in France">France</a>: 2.8%, 8. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_India" title="Energy in India">India</a>  2.4%, 9. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_Canada" title="Energy in Canada">Canada</a>: 2.2% and 10. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Ukraine" title="Energy in Ukraine">Ukraine</a> 2.2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2010-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of trends, carbon dioxide emissions were around 5,000 mt in 1990 and gradually increased to around 6,000 mt, with a peak occurring in 2008. The subsequent decline went on such that 2012 saw about 5,400 mt emitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inventory_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-inventory-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 2016 study U.S. methane emissions were underestimated by the EPA since at least a decade, on the order of 30 to 50 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_and_potential_effects_of_climate_change_in_the_United_States">Current and potential effects of climate change in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current and potential effects of climate change in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change" title="Tropical cyclones and climate change">Tropical cyclones and climate change</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:427px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_(PNG).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="351" data-file-width="800" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_%28PNG%29.png/425px-Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_%28PNG%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_%28PNG%29.png/638px-Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_%28PNG%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_%28PNG%29.png 2x" width="425"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northern_Hemisphere_Snow_Cover_Graph_(PNG).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This graph shows the decrease in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_cover" title="Snow cover">snow cover</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> associated with climate changes from 1966 to 2008.</div></div></div>
<p>A January 2013 'National Climate Assessment' study on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> scholars, stated that climate change would have mixed but net-negative effects in the region by 2050. Specifically, longer growing seasons as well as higher carbon dioxide levels were predicted to increase <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop yield</a> but heat waves, droughts, and floods were also forecast to rise. The report predicted declines in ice cover on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> that would lengthen commercial shipping season although the regions would also suffer from <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> as well as damaging <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> blooms. The negative scenario described in the study used modeling with a 3.8 to 4.9 °F (2.1 to 2.7 °C) range for 2000 to 2050 warming versus the 1 °F (0.56 °C) of historical warming for 1950 to 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midwest_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-midwest-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drought_in_the_United_States" title="Drought in the United States">U.S. droughts</a>, a study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Research_Letters" title="Geophysical Research Letters">Geophysical Research Letters</a></i> in 2006 about the U.S. reported, "Droughts have, for the most part, become shorter, less frequent, and cover a smaller portion of the country over the last century." It also stated that the "main exception is the Southwest and parts of the interior of the West" where "drought duration and severity... have increased."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-letters-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The general effect of climate changes has been found in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Climate_Change" title="Nature Climate Change">Nature Climate Change</a></i> to have caused increased likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> and extensive downpours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattle_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattle-17">[17]</a></sup>  Concerns exist that, as stated by a <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> (NIH) study in 2003, increasing "heat and humidity, at least partially related to anthropogenic climate change, suggest that a long-term increase in heat-related mortality could occur." However, the report found that, in general, "over the past 35 years, the U.S. populace has become systematically less affected by hot and humid weather conditions" while "mortality during heat stress events has declined despite increasingly stressful weather conditions in many urban and suburban areas." Thus, as stated in the study, "there is no simple association between increased heat wave duration or intensity and higher mortality rates" with current death rates being largely preventable, the NIH deeply urging <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">American public health officials</a> and physicians to inform patients about mitigating heat-related weather and climate effects on their bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The question of whether events such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadoes" title="Tornadoes">tornadoes</a>, and other unusual storms have been altered by climate change in the U.S. is a subject of much uncertainty, as found in the aforementioned <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Climate_Change" title="Nature Climate Change">Nature Climate Change</a></i> study. A fundamental problem exists in that records for those such events are of worse quality with poorer details than temperature and rainfall records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattle_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-seattle-17">[17]</a></sup> A comprehensive article in <i><a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Research_Letters" title="Geophysical Research Letters">Geophysical Research Letters</a></i> in 2006 found "no significant change in global net tropical cyclone activity" during past decades, a period when considerable warming of ocean water temperatures occurred. Significant regional trends exist such as a general rise of activity in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> area besides the U.S. eastern coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Looking at the lack of certainty as to the causes of the 1995 to present increase in Atlantic extreme storm activity, a 2007 article in <i>Nature</i> used proxy records of vertical wind shear and sea surface temperature to create a long-term model. The authors found that "the average frequency of major hurricanes decreased gradually from the 1760s until the early 1990s, reaching anomalously low values during the 1970s and 1980s." As well, they also found that "hurricane activity since 1995 is not unusual compared to other periods of high hurricane activity in the record and thus appears to represent a recovery to normal hurricane activity, rather than a direct response to increasing sea surface temperature."  The researches stated that future evaluations of climate change effects should focus on the magnitude of vertical wind shear for answers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The frequency of tornadoes in the U.S. have increased, and some of said trend takes place due to climatological changes though other factors such as better detection technologies also play large roles. According to a 2003 study in <i>Climate Research</i>, the total tornado hazards resulting in injury, death, or economic loss "shows a steady decline since the 1980s". As well, the authors reported that tornado "deaths and injuries decreased over the past fifty years". They state that addition research must look into regional and temporal variability in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Stern_Review" title="Stern Review">Stern Review</a>, warming of 3 or 4 °C (5.4 or 7.2 °F) will lead to serious risks and increasing pressures for coastal protection in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_State" title="New York State">New York State</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">Sea level rise</a> has taken place in the U.S. for decades, going back to the 19th century. As stated in research published by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a></i>, west coast sea levels have increased by an average of 2.1 millimeters annually. In English notation, that equates to 0.083 inches per year and 0.83 inches per decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crop and livestock production will be increasingly challenged. Threats to human health will increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:377px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States,_1895-2011.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="577" data-file-width="670" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States%2C_1895-2011.png/375px-Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States%2C_1895-2011.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States%2C_1895-2011.png/563px-Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States%2C_1895-2011.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States%2C_1895-2011.png 2x" width="375"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Average_Drought_Conditions_in_the_Contiguous_48_States,_1895-2011.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This graph shows average drought conditions in the contiguous 48 states, according to the EPA, with yearly data going from 1895 to 2011. The curve is a nine-year weighted average.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s (EPA) website provides information on climate change: <a class="external text" href="http://epa.gov/climatechange/" rel="nofollow">EPA Climate Change</a>. Climate change is a problem that is affecting people and the environment. Human-induced climate change has, e.g., the potential to alter the prevalence and severity of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weathers</a> such as heat waves, <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">cold waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  A report released in March 2012 by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) confirmed that a strong body of evidence links global warming to an increase in heat waves, a rise in episodes of heavy rainfall and other precipitation, and more frequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_flooding" title="Coastal flooding">coastal flooding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>  The U.S. had its warmest March–May on record in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> (See March 2012 North American heat wave)
</p><p>According to the American government's <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="Climate Change Science Program">Climate Change Science Program</a>, "With continued global warming, heat waves and heavy downpours are very likely to further increase in frequency and intensity. Substantial areas of North America are likely to have more frequent droughts of greater severity. Hurricane wind speeds, rainfall intensity, and storm surge levels are likely to increase. The strongest cold season storms are likely to become more frequent, with stronger winds and more extreme wave heights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> had registered in August 2011 nine distinct <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> disasters, each totalling $1 billion or more in economic losses. Total losses for 2011 were evaluated as more than $35 billion before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2011)" title="Hurricane Irene (2011)">Hurricane Irene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>As shown in the adjacent image, wet and rainy conditions versus moments of drought in the U.S. have varied significantly over the past several decades. Average conditions for the 48 contiguous states flashed into extreme drought in the mid-1930s '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_bowl" title="Dust bowl">dust bowl</a>' era as well as during the turn of the 20th century. In comparison, the mid-2000s decade and mid-1890s experienced only slight drought and had mitigating rainy periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The National Drought Mitigation Center has reported that financial assistance from the government alone in the 1930s dry period may have been as high as $1 billion (in 1930s dollars) by the end of the drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unl_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-unl-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2012 report in <i>Nature Climate Change</i> stated that there is reason to be concerned that American climate changes could increase food insecurity by reducing grain yields, with the authors noting as well that substantial other facts exist influencing food prices as such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_versus_fuel" title="Food versus fuel">government mandates turning food into fuel</a> and fluctuating transport costs. The researchers concluded that U.S. corn price volatility would moderately increase with American warming with relatively modest rises in food prices assuming that market competition and integration partly mitigated climate affects. They warned that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofuels" title="Biofuels">biofuels</a> mandates would, if present, widely increase corn price sensitivity to U.S. warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate scientists have hypothesized that the stratospheric <a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">polar vortex</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> will gradually weaken as a result of global warming and thus influence U.S. conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norm_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-norm-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This trend could possibly cause changes in the future such as increasing frost in certain areas. The magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> noted in December 2014 that ice cover on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> had recently "reached its second-greatest extent on record", showing climate variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norm_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-norm-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy">Policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="571" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png/300px-US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png/450px-US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_CO2_Emissions_1980-2012.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This graph shows U.S. energy-related CO2 emissions from 1980 to 2012 according to the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">EIA</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Climate change policy of the United States">Climate change policy of the United States</a></div>
<p>Federal, state, and local governments have all debated climate change policies, but the resulting laws vary considerably. The U.S. Congress has not adopted a comprehensive greenhouse gas emissions reduction scheme, but long-standing environmental laws such as the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> have been used by the executive branch and litigants in lawsuits to implement regulations and voluntary agreements.
</p><p>The federal government has the exclusive power to regulate emissions from motor vehicles, but has granted the state of California a waiver to adopt more stringent regulations.  Other states may choose to adopt either the federal or California rules.  Individual states retain the power to regulate emissions from electrical generation and industrial sources, and some have done so.  Building codes are controlled by state and local governments, and in some cases have been altered to require increased energy efficiency.  Governments at all levels have the option of reducing emissions from their own operations such as through improvements to buildings, purchasing alternative fuel vehicles, and reducing waste; and some have done so.
</p><p>Political opponents to emissions regulations argue that such measures reduce economic activity in the fossil fuel industry (which is a substantial extractive industry in the United States), and impose unwanted costs on drivers, electricity users, and building owners.  Some also argue that stringent environmental regulations infringe on individual liberty, and that the environmental impact of economic activity should be driven by the informed choices of consumers.  Regulatory proponents argue that the economy is not a zero-sum game, and that individual choices have proven insufficient to prevent damaging and costly levels of global warming. Some states have financed programs to boost employment in green energy industries, such as production of wind turbines.  Areas heavily dependent on coal production have not taken such steps and are suffering economic recession due to both competition from now lower-priced natural gas and environmental rules that make generation of electricity from coal disadvantageous due to high emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> and other pollutants compared to other fuels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_federal_policy_and_international_agreements">History of federal policy and international agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of federal policy and international agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States, although a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signatory" title="Signatory">signatory</a> to the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, under President Clinton, neither <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratified" title="Ratified">ratified</a> nor withdrew from the protocol. In 1997, the U.S. Senate voted unanimously under the <a href="/wiki/Byrd%E2%80%93Hagel_Resolution" title="Byrd–Hagel Resolution">Byrd–Hagel Resolution</a> that it was not the sense of the senate that the United States should be a signatory to the Kyoto Protocol, and in March 2001, the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_W._Bush" title="Presidency of George W. Bush">Bush Administration</a> announced that it would not implement the treaty, saying it would create economic setbacks in the U.S. and does not put enough pressure to limit emissions from developing nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  In February 2002, Bush announced his alternative to the Kyoto Protocol, by bringing forth a plan to reduce the intensity of greenhouse gasses by 18 percent over 10 years. The intensity of greenhouse gasses specifically is the ratio of greenhouse gas emissions and economic output, meaning that under this plan, emissions would still continue to grow, but at a slower pace. Bush stated that this plan would prevent the release of 500 million metric tons of greenhouse gases, which is about the equivalent of 70 million cars from the road. This target would achieve this goal by providing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_credits" title="Tax credits">tax credits</a> to businesses that use renewable energy sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in the case <i><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_v._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Massachusetts v. Environmental Protection Agency">Massachusetts v. Environmental Protection Agency</a></i> that EPA regulation of carbon dioxide is required under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>.
</p><p>President Barack Obama proposed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap-and-trade" title="Cap-and-trade">cap-and-trade</a> program as part of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_federal_budget" title="2010 United States federal budget">2010 United States federal budget</a>, but this was never adopted by Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>President Obama committed in the December 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Climate_Change_Summit" title="Copenhagen Climate Change Summit">Copenhagen Climate Change Summit</a> to reduce carbon dioxide emissions in the range of 17% below 2005 levels by 2020, 42% below 2005 levels by 2030, and 83% below 2005 levels by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrdc.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrdc.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Data from an April 2013 report by the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">Energy Information Administration</a> (EIA), showed a 12% reduction in the 2005 to 2012 period. Just over half of this decrease has been attributed to the recession, and the rest to a variety of factors such as replacing coal-based power generation with natural gas and increasing energy efficiency of American vehicles (according to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Economic_Advisors" title="Council of Economic Advisors">Council of Economic Advisors</a> analysis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_13514" title="Executive Order 13514">Executive Order 13514</a> set various requirements for energy efficiency in federal buildings and operations, including goals for 2015.  That year, Executive Order 13693 set requirements for federal operations generally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In an address to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">U.S. Congress</a> in June 2013, the President detailed a specific action plan to achieve the 17% carbon emissions cut from 2005 by 2020, including measures such as shifting from <a href="/wiki/Coal_power_in_the_United_States" title="Coal power in the United States">coal-based power generation</a> to solar and natural gas production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bypass_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-bypass-7">[7]</a></sup> Some Republican and Democratic lawmakers expressed concern at the idea of imposing new fines and regulations on the coal industry while the U.S. still tries to recover from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">world economic recession</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Boehner" title="John Boehner">John Boehner</a> saying that the proposed rules "will put thousands and thousands of Americans out of work".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christiana_Figueres" title="Christiana Figueres">Christiana Figueres</a>, executive director of the <a href="/wiki/2013_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2013 United Nations Climate Change Conference">UN's climate secretariat</a>, praised the plan as providing a vital benchmark that people concerned with climate change can use as a paragon both at home and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verdict_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-verdict-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>After not participating in previous climate international treaties, the United States signed the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> on April 22, 2016 during the Obama administration. Though this agreement does not mandate a specific reduction for any given country, it sets global goals, asks countries to set their own goals, and mandates reporting.
</p><p>The U.S. submitted its action plan in March, 2015, ahead of the treaty signing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>  Reaffirming the November 2014 announcement it made with China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> the United States declared it would reduce greenhouse gas emissions to 26-28% below 2005 levels by 2025.  This is to be accomplished by several executive actions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Clean_Power_Plan" title="Clean Power Plan">Clean Power Plan</a> - regulating sources of electricity (put on hold by the Supreme Court in February, 2016, pending the outcome of a lawsuit)</li>
<li>New emission standards for heavy-duty vehicles, finalized by EPA in March, 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Department of Energy efficiency standards for commercial buildings, appliances, and equipment<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Various actions to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases other than carbon dioxide, including regulation and voluntary efforts related to methane from landfills, agriculture, coal mines; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbons" title="Hydrofluorocarbons">hydrofluorocarbons</a> (HFCs) reduction through domestic regulation and amendment of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a></li></ul>
<p>In June 2017, President Donald Trump announced <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement</a>, although the exit process specified by the treaty (which Trump said the U.S. would follow) will last until at least November 4, 2020. In April 2018, the Trump administration cancelled NASA's Carbon Monitoring System (CMS) program which helped with the monitoring of CO2 emissions and deforestation in the United States and in other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_the_US_military">Role of the US military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role of the US military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_national_security" title="Climate change and national security">Climate change and national security</a></div>
<p>The US military is an unequivocal validator of climate science, and its current efforts to value true costs and benefits of energy conservation and increased use of renewables can serve as drivers of change, according to a 2014 study from the University of Pennsylvania Legal Studies Department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucla_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucla-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2014 report described the projected climate change as a “catalyst for conflict”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cna_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-cna-50">[50]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">DOD</a> had issued a Fiscal Year 2012 Climate Change Adaptation Roadmap, in which it outlined its vulnerabilities, yet the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> (GAO) found, that installation officials rarely proposed projects with <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">climate change adaptation</a>, because the processes for approving and funding military construction do not include climate change adaptation in the ranking criteria for projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gao_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-gao-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_and_regional_policy">State and regional policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: State and regional policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Across the country, regional organizations, <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>, and cities are achieving real emissions reductions and gaining valuable policy experience as they take action on climate change.  These actions include increasing <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> generation, selling agricultural <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> credits, and encouraging <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">efficient energy use</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-52">[52]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="U.S. Climate Change Science Program">U.S. Climate Change Science Program</a> is a joint program of over twenty U.S. cabinet departments and federal agencies, all working together to investigate climate change. In June 2008, a report issued by the program stated that weather would become more extreme, due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_6-19-08_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_6-19-08-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>  States and municipalities often function as "policy laboratories", developing initiatives that serve as models for federal action.  This has been especially true with environmental regulation—most federal environmental laws have been based on state models.  In addition, state actions can significantly affect emissions, because many individual states emit high levels of greenhouse gases. Texas, for example, emits more than France, while California's emissions exceed those of Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>  State actions are also important because states have primary jurisdiction over many areas—such as electric generation, agriculture, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>—that are critical to addressing climate change.
</p><p>Many states are participating in <a href="/wiki/Regional_climate_change_initiatives_in_the_United_States" title="Regional climate change initiatives in the United States">Regional climate change initiatives</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Greenhouse_Gas_Initiative" title="Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative">Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative</a> in the northeastern United States, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Governors%27_Association" title="Western Governors' Association">Western Governors' Association</a> (WGA) Clean and Diversified Energy Initiative, and the Southwest Climate Change Initiative.
</p><p>Inside the ten northeastern states implementing the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Greenhouse_Gas_Initiative" title="Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative">Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative</a>, carbon dioxide emissions per capita decreased by about 25% from 2000 and 2010, as the state economies continued to grow while enacting various energy efficiency programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>New Policy</b>
</p><p>In January 2017, President Donald Trump announced that to quit <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> on climate change and United States become the only country that drop out of this agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> At the same time, Trump administration shut down the United States Environmental Protection Agency’s climate change web pages, because President Trump claims that climate change is not real.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, Paris Agreement is a global agreement that response to the climate change issue and impacts. Countries are trying to control the greenhouse gases emission and keep the global temperature rise below 2 degree Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Dropping out the agreement is actually impact the economics of United States instead of boosting it. For example, President Trump states dropping out the agreement can create more job opportunities to the United States people, like coal and oil jobs, because coal industries are not limited by the agreement anymore. But, it is actually harming a great number of advanced energy workers in the United States. Most of the workers who are working on solar power, wind power or nuclear power are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_and_consequences">Cost and consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cost and consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013 there were 11 weather and climate disaster events with losses over $1 billion each in the United States. In total these 11 events losses were over $110 billion. 2013 was the warmest year ever in the contiguous United States and about one-third of all Americans experienced 10 days or more of 100-degree heat. Increasing floods, heat waves, and droughts have brought economical problems to farmers business and increased product prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_emissions_trading">Voluntary emissions trading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Voluntary emissions trading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">Emissions trading</a></div>
<p>Also in 2003, U.S. corporations were able to trade CO<sub>2</sub> emission allowances on the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Climate_Exchange" title="Chicago Climate Exchange">Chicago Climate Exchange</a> under a voluntary scheme. In August 2007, the Exchange announced a mechanism to create emission <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/offset" title="wikt:offset">offsets</a> for projects within the United States that cleanly destroy <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>-depleting substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus-level_action">Campus-level action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Campus-level action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many colleges and universities have taken steps in recent years to offset or curb their greenhouse gas emissions in relation to campus activities. On October 5, 2006, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> announced that it plans to purchase 118 million kilowatt hours of wind power, more wind power than any college or university in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Later in the same month, the small campus of <a href="/wiki/College_of_the_Atlantic" title="College of the Atlantic">College of the Atlantic</a> in Maine became the first to vow to offset all of its greenhouse gas emissions by cutting GHG emissions and investing in emissions-cutting projects elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
In May 2007, the trustees of <a href="/wiki/Middlebury_College" title="Middlebury College">Middlebury College</a> voted in support of a student-written proposal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> to reduce campus emissions as much as possible, and then offset the rest such that the campus is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a> by 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>  As of November 2007, 434 campuses have institutionalized their commitment to climate neutrality by signing the American College and University Presidents Climate Commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> On November 2-5th, 2007, thousands of young adults converged in Washington D.C. for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Shift_2007" title="Power Shift 2007">Power Shift 2007</a>, the first national youth summit to address the climate crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_Shift_2007" title="Power Shift 2007">Power Shift 2007</a> conference was a project of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_Action_Coalition" title="Energy Action Coalition">Energy Action Coalition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_ideologies">Political ideologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political ideologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, according to <i>The New York Times</i> and others, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil companies</a> knew that <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">burning oil and gas</a> could cause global warming since the 1970s but, nonetheless, funded deniers for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151105_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151105-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20150708_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20150708-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historical support for environmental protection has been relatively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-partisan" title="Non-partisan">non-partisan</a>. Republican <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> established national parks whereas Democrat <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_Conservation_Service" title="Soil Conservation Service">Soil Conservation Service</a>. This non-partisanship began to change during the 1980s when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> stated that environmental protection was an economic burden. Views over global warming began to seriously diverge among <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> and Republicans when ratifying the Kyoto Protocol was being debated in 1998. Gaps in opinions among the general public are often amplified among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_elite" title="Political elite">political elites</a>, such as members of Congress, who tend to be more polarized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlap2008RepublicanDemocraticGap_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlap2008RepublicanDemocraticGap-82">[82]</a></sup> A 2017 study by the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_American_Progress#Center_for_American_Progress_Action_Fund" title="Center for American Progress">Center for American Progress Action Fund</a> of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">climate change denial</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> found 180 members who deny the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">science behind climate change</a>; all were Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond politicians, there is a variety of views by each political party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> In March 2014, Gallup found that among Democrats, 45% say they worry a great deal about the quality of the environment while the number drops to 16% for Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riffkin-climate_change_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riffkin-climate_change-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warren-gallup_poll_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warren-gallup_poll-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kilmas-climate_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kilmas-climate-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>Political disagreement is also strongly rooted in our potential solutions to addressing <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Strategies such as a <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">Cap and Trade</a> system are still a heated argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 20, 2017, within moments of Donald Trump's inauguration, all references to climate change were removed from the White House website. The U.S. has been considered the most authoritative researcher of this information, and there is concern amongst the scientific community as to how the Trump administration will prioritize the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>In early indications to news media of the first federal budget process under Donald Trump's administration, there are signs that most efforts under the Obama administration to curb U.S. greenhouse gas emissions would effectively be rolled back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2018 the Trump Administration released its <i>Draft Environmental Impact Statement</i> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHTSA" title="NHTSA">NHTSA</a>.  In it was the prediction that on our current course the planet will warm a disastrous seven degrees fahrenheit (or about 3.9 degrees celsius) by the end of this century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Our_Changing_Planet_report"><i>Our Changing Planet</i> report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Our Changing Planet report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1989, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program</a> has issued <i>Our Changing Planet</i>, an annual report summarizing "recent achievements, near term plans, and progress in implementing long term goals."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> The report for <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 2010 was issued on October 28, 2009.
</p><p>Measurement and modeling of climate systems have both improved dramatically in the last three decades, with measurements providing the hard data to calibrate the simulations, which in turn lead to improved understanding of the various systems and feedbacks and indicate areas where more and more detailed observations are needed. Recent developments in <a href="/wiki/Climate_ensemble" title="Climate ensemble">ensemble</a> methods have improved understanding of and reduced uncertainty in hydrologic forcing by incoming radiation, particularly in areas with a complex topology. Multiple complementary model-validated proxy reconstructions indicate that recent warmth in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> is anomalous over at least the last 1300 years; using tree ring data, this conclusion can be extended somewhat less certainly to at least 1700 years. Improved measurement and analysis techniques have reconciled certain discrepancies between observed and projected trends in tropical surface and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropospheric" title="Tropospheric">tropospheric</a> temperatures: corrected buoy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_climate_research_satellites" title="List of climate research satellites">satellite</a> surface temperatures are slightly cooler and corrected satellite and <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> measurements of the tropical troposphere are slightly warmer.
</p><p>Various forcing factors, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>, land cover change, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes" title="Volcanoes">volcanoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution_in_the_United_States" title="Air pollution in the United States">air pollution</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar variability</a>, have far ranging effects throughout the <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(physics)" title="Coupling (physics)">coupled</a> ocean-atmosphere-land climate system. In the short term, effects from <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_carbon" title="Organic carbon">organic carbon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> on radiative forcing are predicted to nearly cancel, but long-term projections of changing emissions patterns indicate that the warming effect of black carbon will outweigh the cooling effect of sulphates. By 2100, the projected global average increase to radiative forcing is approximately 1 W/m<sup>2</sup>.
</p><p>Human activities influence climate and related systems through, among other mechanisms, land usage, water management, and earlier and more significant melting of snow cover due to greenhouse-effect warming. In the southwestern United States, 60% of climate-related trends in river flow, winter air temperature, and snowpack between 1950 and 1999 were induced by humans. In this region, conversion of abandoned farmland to pine forests is projected to have a slight surface cooling effect, with evapotranspiration outweighing decreased <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_climate_change">National climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: National climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2012, the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) reported that the 12-month period July 2011 to June 2012 was the warmest 12-month period on record in the continental United States, with average temperature 3.23 °F above the average for the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAApage6_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAApage6-94">[94]</a></sup> Earlier it was reported that exceptionally warm months between January and May 2012 had made the 12 month previous to June 2012 the warmest 12-month block since record keeping began,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> but this record was exceeded by the July 2011 to June 2012 period. NOAA stated that the odds of the July 2011 to June 2012 high temperatures occurring randomly was 1 in 1,594,323.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAApage6_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAApage6-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1898 through 1913, there have been 27 <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">cold waves</a> which totaled 58 days. Between 1970 and 1989, there were about 12 such events. From 1989 until January 6, 2014, there were none. The one on the latter date caused consternation because of decreased frequency of such experiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="California">California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_California" title="Climate change in California">Climate change in California</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> has taken legislative steps towards reducing the possible effects climate change by incentives and plans for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_car" title="Clean car">clean cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and stringent caps on big polluting industries.  In September 2006, the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Legislature" title="California State Legislature">California State Legislature</a> passed AB 32, the <i><a href="/wiki/Global_Warming_Solutions_Act_of_2006" title="Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006">Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> with the goal of reducing man-made California <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions (1.4% of global emissions in 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>) back to 1990 emission levels by 2020. The legislation grants the Air Resource Board extraordinary powers to set policies, draw up regulations, lead the enforcement effort, levy fines and fees to finance it and punish violators. The technical and regulatory requirements are far reaching. Some of this sweeping regulation is being challenged in the courts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The law is intended to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-carbon" title="Low-carbon">low-carbon</a> technology more attractive, and promote its adoption in production in California.
</p><p>While California's claims<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> of successful energy efficiency policy have been widely accepted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> a 2013 study argued external factors explained ~95% of the appearance of California's relative efficiency gains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> The report cited three key factors: relatively large household size; relatively low household income growth; and US population shift to the Southwest (which increased the average per-capita energy use for the other 49 states).
</p><p>In California, authorities have predicted that the shortage of rain will increase the duration of the fire season, and result in larger fires. Half of the most destructive fires in recorded California history have occurred since 2002. Climate change and intensifying droughts are drying out landscapes. Pests, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pine_beetle" title="Mountain pine beetle">mountain pine beetle</a>, have killed off stands of trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Idaho">Idaho</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Idaho">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Idaho" title="Climate change in Idaho">Climate change in Idaho</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> emits the least carbon dioxide per person of the United States, less than 23,000 pounds a year. Idaho forbids <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-power_plant" title="Coal-power plant">coal-power plants</a>. It relies mostly on nonpolluting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> from its rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattletimes.nwsource.com_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-seattletimes.nwsource.com-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>  Over the last century, the average temperature near <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a>, has increased nearly 1 °F, and precipitation has increased by nearly 20% in many parts of the state, and has declined in other parts of the state by more than 10%. Over the next century, climate in Idaho could experience additional changes. For example, based on projections made by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> and results from the
United Kingdom Hadley Centre’s climate model (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HadCM2" title="HadCM2">HadCM2</a>), a model that accounts for both greenhouse gases and aerosols, by 2100 temperatures in Idaho could increase by 5 °F (2.8 °C) (with a range of 2-9 °F) in winter and summer and 4 °F (2.2 °C) (with a range of 2-7 °F) in spring and fall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>

<p><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/Deval_Patrick" title="Deval Patrick">Deval Patrick</a> has recently signed into law three global warming and energy-related bills that will promote <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_biofuel" title="Advanced biofuel">advanced biofuels</a>, support the growth of the clean energy technology industry, and cut the emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> within the state.  The Clean Energy Biofuels Act, signed in late July, exempts <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> from the state's gasoline tax, but only if the ethanol achieves a 60% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions relative to gasoline. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nevada">Nevada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Nevada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Nevada" title="Climate change in Nevada">Climate change in Nevada</a></div>

<p>Climate change in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> has been measured over the last century, with the average temperature in <a href="/wiki/Elko,_Nevada" title="Elko, Nevada">Elko, Nevada</a>, increasing 0.6 °F (0.3 °C), and precipitation has increased by up to 20% in many parts of the state.  Based on projections made by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> and results from the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a> climate model (HadCM2), a model that accounts for both greenhouse gases and aerosols, by 2100, temperatures in Nevada could increase by 3-4 °F (1.7-2.2 °C) in spring and fall (with a range of 1-6 °F [0.5-3.3 °C]), and by 5-6 °F (2.8-3.3 °C) in winter and summer (with a range of 2-10 °F [1.1-5.6 °C]).  Earlier and more rapid snowmelts could contribute to winter and spring flooding, and more intense summer storms could increase the likelihood of flash floods.  Climate change could affect crop production, reducing potato yields by about 12%, with hay and pasture yields increasing by about 7%. Farmed acres could rise by 9% or fall by 9%, depending on how climate changes.  The region's inherently variable and unpredictable hydrological and climatic systems could become even more variable with changes in climate, putting stress on wetland ecosystems.  A warmer climate would increase evaporation and shorten the snow season in the mountains, resulting in earlier spring runoff and reduced summer streamflow. This would exacerbate fire risk in the late summer.  Many desert-adapted plants and animals already live near their tolerance limits, and could disappear under the hotter conditions predicted under global warming.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York">New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_New_York_City" title="Climate change in New York City">Climate change in New York City</a></div>
<p>Climate change in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> could affect buildings/structures, wetlands, water supply, health, and energy demand, due to the high population and extensive <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> New York is especially at risk if the sea level rises, due to many of the bridges connecting to boroughs, and entrances to roads and rail tunnels. High-traffic locations such as the airports, the <a href="/wiki/Holland_Tunnel" title="Holland Tunnel">Holland Tunnel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Tunnel" title="Lincoln Tunnel">Lincoln Tunnel</a>, and the Passenger Ship Terminal are located in areas vulnerable to flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> Flooding would be expensive to reverse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>  New York has launched a task force to advise on preparing city infrastructure for flooding, water shortages, and higher temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas">Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Texas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Texas" title="Climate change in Texas">Climate change in Texas</a></div>
<p>Over the next century, climate in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> could experience additional changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> For example, based on projections made by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> and results from the United Kingdom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre" title="Hadley Centre">Hadley Centre</a>’s climate model (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HadCM2" title="HadCM2">HadCM2</a>), a model that accounts for both <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>, by 2100 temperatures in Texas could increase by about 3 °F (~1.7 °C) in spring (with a range of 1-6 °F) and about 4 °F (~2.2 °C) in other seasons (with a range of 1-9 °F).  Texas emits more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere than any other state. And if Texas were a country, it would be the seventh-largest carbon dioxide polluter in the world . Texas's high carbon dioxide output and large energy consumption is primarily a result of large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-burning_power_plant" title="Coal-burning power plant">coal-burning power plants</a> and gas-guzzling vehicles (low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miles_per_gallon" title="Miles per gallon">miles per gallon</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>   Unless increased temperatures are coupled with a strong increase in rainfall, water could become more scarce. A warmer and drier climate would lead to greater evaporation, as much as a 35% decrease in streamflow, and less water for recharging groundwater aquifers.  Climate change could reduce cotton and sorghum yields by 2-15% and wheat yields by 43-68%, leading to changes in acres farmed and production.  With changes in climate, the extent and density of forested areas in east Texas could change little or decline by 50-70%. Hotter, drier weather could increase wildfires and the susceptibility of pine forests to pine bark beetles and other pests, which would reduce forests and expand grasslands and arid shrublands.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington">Washington</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Washington">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Washington" title="Climate change in Washington">Climate change in Washington</a></div>
<p>Visible physical effects on the environment within WA State include glacier reduction, declining snow-pack, earlier spring runoff, an increase in large wildfires, and rising sea levels which affect the <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">Puget Sound</a> area.  Less snow pack will also result in a time change of water flow volumes into fresh water systems, resulting in greater winter river volume, and less volume during summer's driest months, generally from July through October.  These changes will result in both economic and ecological repercussions, most notably found in hydrological power output, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Washington#Municipal_water_supply" title="Climate change in Washington">municipal water supply</a> and migration of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Washington#Fish" title="Climate change in Washington">fish</a>.
Collectively, these changes are negatively affecting agriculture, forest resources, dairy farming, the WA wine industry, electricity, water supply, and other areas of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EICC_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-EICC-121">[121]</a></sup>  Beyond affecting wildfires, climate change could affect the economic contribution of Washington’s forests both directly (e.g., by affecting rates of tree growth and relative importance of different tree species) and indirectly (e.g., through effects on the magnitude of pest or fire damage).  Beyond growth rates, climate change could affect Washington forests by changing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a>.
</p><p>Washington State currently relies on hydro power for 72% of its power and sales of hydro power to both households and businesses topped 4.3 billion dollars in 2003. Washington State currently has the 9th lowest cost for electricity in the US. Climate change will have a negative effect on both the supply and demand of electricity in Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WESC_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-WESC-122">[122]</a></sup>  The available electricity supply could also be affected by climate change. Currently, peak <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">stream flows</a> are in the summer. <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">Snowpack</a> is likely to melt earlier in the future due to increased temperatures, thus shifting the peak stream flow to late winter and early spring, with decreased summer stream flow.  This would result in an increased availability of electricity in the early spring, when demand is dampened, and a decreased availability in the summer, when the demand may be highest.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Virginia">West Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: West Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_West_Virginia" title="Climate change in West Virginia">Climate change in West Virginia</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(February 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Warming and other climate changes could expand the habitat and infectiousness of disease-carrying insects, thus increasing the potential for transmission of diseases such as malaria and <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue (“break bone”) fever</a>. Warmer temperatures could increase the incidence of <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a> and other tick-borne diseases in West Virginia, because populations of ticks, and their rodent hosts, could increase under warmer temperatures and increased vegetation.  Lower streamflows and lake levels in the summer and fall could affect the dependability of surface water supplies, particularly since many of the streams in West Virginia have low flows in the summer.  Hay yields could increase by about 30% as a result of climate change, leading to changes in acres farmed and production. Farmed acres could remain constant or could decrease by as much as 30% in response to changes in prices, for example, possible decreases in hay prices.  In areas where richer soils are prevalent, southern pines could increase their range and density, and in areas with poorer soils, which are more common in West Virginia’s forests, scrub oaks of little commercial value (e.g., post oak and blackjack oak) could increase their range. As a result, the character of forests in West Virginia could change. The state of West Virginia is 97% forested, and much of this cover is in high-elevation areas. These areas contain some of the last remaining stands of red spruce, which are seriously threatened by acid rain and could be further stressed by changing climate.  Given a sufficient change in climate, these spruce forests could be substantially reduced, or could disappear.  Higher-than-normal winter temperatures could boost temperatures inside cave bat roosting sites, which has been shown to cause higher mortality due to increased winter body weight loss in endangered Indiana bats (e.g., an increase of 9 °F (−13 °C) during winter <a href="/wiki/Hibernation" title="Hibernation">hibernation</a> has been associated with a 42% increase in the rate of body mass loss).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wyoming">Wyoming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Wyoming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Wyoming" title="Climate change in Wyoming">Climate change in Wyoming</a></div>
<p>On a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per-person" title="Per-person">per-person</a> basis, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> emits more <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> than any other state or any other country: 276,000 pounds (125,000 kg) of it per capita a year, because of burning <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, which provides nearly all of the state's electrical power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seattletimes.nwsource.com_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-seattletimes.nwsource.com-112">[112]</a></sup>  Warmer temperatures could increase the incidence of <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tick-borne_disease" title="Tick-borne disease">tick-borne diseases</a> in Wyoming, because populations of <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">ticks</a>, and their <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodent</a> hosts, could increase under warmer temperatures and increased vegetation. Increased runoff from heavy rainfall could increase water-borne diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Giardia" title="Giardia">giardia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptosporidia" title="Cryptosporidia">cryptosporidia</a>, and viral and bacterial <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a>.  The headwaters of several rivers originate in Wyoming and flow in all directions into the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake_River" title="Snake River">Snake</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> basins.  A warmer climate could result in less winter snowfall, more winter rain, and faster, earlier spring snowmelt. In the summer, without increases in rainfall of at least 15-20%, higher temperatures and increased evaporation could lower streamflows and lake levels. Less water would be available to support <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> generation, public water supplies, fish and wildlife habitat, recreation, and mining.  Hotter, drier weather could increase the frequency and intensity of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>, threatening both property and forests. Drier conditions would reduce the range and health of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponderosa_pine" title="Ponderosa pine">ponderosa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodgepole_Pine" title="Lodgepole Pine">lodgepole</a> forests, and increase their susceptibility to fire.  Climate change also poses a threat to the high alpine systems, and this zone could disappear in many areas. Local extinctions of alpine species such as arctic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gentian" title="Gentian">gentian</a>, alpine <a href="/wiki/Chaenactis" title="Chaenactis">chaenactis</a>, rosy <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_pipit" title="Water pipit">water pipit</a> could be expected as a result of habitat loss and fragmentation.  In cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/Wyoming_Business_Council" title="Wyoming Business Council">Wyoming Business Council</a>, the Converse Area New Development Organization drafted an initiative to advance <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a> development in Wyoming. The <a href="/wiki/Wyoming_Business_Council" title="Wyoming Business Council">Wyoming Business Council</a> offers <a href="/wiki/Grant_(money)" title="Grant (money)">grants</a> for homeowners who want to install <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) systems.
</p>
