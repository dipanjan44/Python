<p><b>Niobium</b>, formerly known as <b>columbium</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Nb</b> (formerly Cb) and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 41. It is a soft, grey, crystalline, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>, often found in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Pyrochlore" title="Pyrochlore">pyrochlore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a>, hence the former name "columbium". Its name comes from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Niobe" title="Niobe">Niobe</a>, who was the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>, the namesake of <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>. The name reflects the great similarity between the two elements in their physical and chemical properties, making them difficult to distinguish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The English chemist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hatchett" title="Charles Hatchett">Charles Hatchett</a> reported a new element similar to tantalum in 1801 and named it columbium. In 1809, the English chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a> wrongly concluded that tantalum and columbium were identical. The German chemist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rose" title="Heinrich Rose">Heinrich Rose</a> determined in 1846 that tantalum ores contain a second element, which he named niobium. In 1864 and 1865, a series of scientific findings clarified that niobium and columbium were the same element (as distinguished from tantalum), and for a century both names were used interchangeably. Niobium was officially adopted as the name of the element in 1949, but the name columbium remains in current use in metallurgy in the United States.
</p><p>It was not until the early 20th century that niobium was first used commercially. Brazil is the leading producer of niobium and <a href="/wiki/Ferroniobium" title="Ferroniobium">ferroniobium</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> of 60–70% niobium with iron. Niobium is used mostly in alloys, the largest part in special <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> such as that used in gas <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a>. Although these alloys contain a maximum of 0.1%, the small percentage of niobium enhances the strength of the steel. The temperature stability of niobium-containing <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> is important for its use in <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a>.
</p><p>Niobium is used in various <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> materials. These <a href="/wiki/Type-II_superconductor" title="Type-II superconductor">superconducting alloys</a>, also containing <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, are widely used in the <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnet" title="Superconducting magnet">superconducting magnets</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI scanners</a>. Other applications of niobium include welding, nuclear industries, electronics, optics, <a href="/wiki/Numismatics" title="Numismatics">numismatics</a>, and jewelry. In the last two applications, the low toxicity and iridescence produced by <a href="/wiki/Anodizing" title="Anodizing">anodization</a> are highly desired properties.
</p>

<p>Niobium was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">identified</a> by English chemist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hatchett" title="Charles Hatchett">Charles Hatchett</a> in 1801.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatchett_1802a_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatchett_1802a-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatchett_1802b_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatchett_1802b-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatchett_1802c_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatchett_1802c-5">[5]</a></sup> He found a new element in a mineral sample that had been sent to England from <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, United States in 1734 by John Winthrop F.R.S. (grandson of <a href="/wiki/John_Winthrop_the_Younger" title="John Winthrop the Younger">John Winthrop the Younger</a>) and named the mineral <i>columbite</i> and the new element <i>columbium</i> after <i><a href="/wiki/Columbia_(name)" title="Columbia (name)">Columbia</a></i>, the poetical name for the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noyes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Noyes-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1853_Mining_Journal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-1853_Mining_Journal-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <i>columbium</i> discovered by Hatchett was probably a mixture of the new element with tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Noyes_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Noyes-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Subsequently, there was considerable confusion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolla_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolla-9">[9]</a></sup> over the difference between columbium (niobium) and the closely related tantalum. In 1809, English chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a> compared the oxides derived from both columbium—columbite, with a density 5.918 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, and tantalum—<a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a>, with a density over 8 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, and concluded that the two oxides, despite the significant difference in density, were identical; thus he kept the name tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolla_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolla-9">[9]</a></sup> This conclusion was disputed in 1846 by German chemist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rose" title="Heinrich Rose">Heinrich Rose</a>, who argued that there were two different elements in the tantalite sample, and named them after children of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>: <i>niobium</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Niobe" title="Niobe">Niobe</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Pelopium" title="Pelopium">pelopium</a></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelop_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelop-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This confusion arose from the minimal observed differences between tantalum and niobium. The claimed new elements <i>pelopium</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ilmenium" title="Ilmenium">ilmenium</a></i>, and <i>dianium</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dianium_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dianium-12">[12]</a></sup> were in fact identical to niobium or mixtures of niobium and tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilmen_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ilmen-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The differences between tantalum and niobium were unequivocally demonstrated in 1864 by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wilhelm_Blomstrand" title="Christian Wilhelm Blomstrand">Christian Wilhelm Blomstrand</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilmen_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ilmen-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henri_Etienne_Sainte-Claire_Deville" title="Henri Etienne Sainte-Claire Deville">Henri Etienne Sainte-Claire Deville</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_J._Troost" title="Louis J. Troost">Louis J. Troost</a>, who determined the formulas of some of the compounds in 1865<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilmen_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ilmen-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> and finally by Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in 1866, who all proved that there were only two elements. Articles on <i>ilmenium</i> continued to appear until 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>De Marignac was the first to prepare the metal in 1864, when he <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> niobium chloride by heating it in an atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nauti_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nauti-17">[17]</a></sup> Although de Marignac was able to produce tantalum-free niobium on a larger scale by 1866, it was not until the early 20th century that niobium was used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp">incandescent lamp</a> filaments, the first commercial application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> This use quickly became obsolete through the replacement of niobium with <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, which has a higher melting point. That niobium improves the <a href="/wiki/Microalloyed_steel" title="Microalloyed steel">strength of steel</a> was first discovered in the 1920s, and this application remains its predominant use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1961, the American physicist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Kunzler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eugene Kunzler (page does not exist)">Eugene Kunzler</a> and coworkers at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> discovered that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-tin" title="Niobium-tin">niobium-tin</a> continues to exhibit superconductivity in the presence of strong electric currents and magnetic fields,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> making it the first material to support the high currents and fields necessary for useful high-power magnets and electrical power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>. This discovery enabled — two decades later — the production of long multi-strand cables wound into coils to create large, powerful <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">electromagnets</a> for rotating machinery, particle accelerators, and particle detectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geballe_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-geballe-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_the_element">Naming the element</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming the element">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Columbium</i> (symbol "Cb")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> was the name originally bestowed by Hatchett upon his discovery of the metal in 1801.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatchett_1802b_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hatchett_1802b-4">[4]</a></sup> The name reflected that the type specimen of the <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> came from America (<a href="/wiki/Columbia_(name)" title="Columbia (name)">Columbia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson_1809_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson_1809-22">[22]</a></sup> This name remained in use in American journals—the last paper published by <a href="/wiki/American_Chemical_Society" title="American Chemical Society">American Chemical Society</a> with <i>columbium</i> in its title dates from 1953<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>—while <i>niobium</i> was used in Europe. To end this confusion, the name <i>niobium</i> was chosen for element 41 at the 15th Conference of the Union of Chemistry in Amsterdam in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contro_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contro-24">[24]</a></sup> A year later this name was officially adopted by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) after 100 years of controversy, despite the chronological precedence of the name <i>columbium</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contro_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Contro-24">[24]</a></sup>  This was a compromise of sorts;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contro_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Contro-24">[24]</a></sup> the IUPAC accepted <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> instead of wolfram in deference to North American usage; and <i>niobium</i> instead of <i>columbium</i> in deference to European usage. While many US chemical societies and government organizations typically use the official IUPAC name, some metallurgists and metal societies still use the original American name, "<i>columbium</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patel_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-patel-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gree_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gree-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Niobium is a <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">lustrous</a>, grey, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Group_5_element" title="Group 5 element">group 5</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> (see table), with an electron configuration in the outermost <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell" title="Electron shell">shells</a> atypical for group 5. (This can be observed in the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> (44), <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> (45), and <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> (46).)
</p>

<p>Although it is thought to have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> crystal structure from absolute zero to its melting point, high-resolution measurements of the thermal expansion along the three crystallographic axes reveal anisotropies which are inconsistent with a cubic structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Therefore, further research and discovery in this area is expected.
</p><p>Niobium becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> temperatures. At atmospheric pressure, it has the highest critical temperature of the elemental superconductors at 9.2 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pein_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pein-29">[29]</a></sup> Niobium has the greatest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor#Meissner_effect" title="Superconductor">magnetic penetration depth</a> of any element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pein_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pein-29">[29]</a></sup> In addition, it is one of the three elemental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_II_superconductor" title="Type II superconductor">Type II superconductors</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>. The superconductive properties are strongly dependent on the purity of the niobium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moura_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moura-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>When very pure, it is comparatively soft and ductile, but impurities make it harder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The metal has a low <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture#Capture_cross_section" title="Neutron capture">capture cross-section</a> for thermal <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> thus it is used in the nuclear industries where neutron transparent structures are desired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The metal takes on a bluish tinge when exposed to air at room temperature for extended periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubber_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rubber-34">[34]</a></sup> Despite a high melting point in elemental form (2,468 °C), it has a lower density than other refractory metals. Furthermore, it is corrosion-resistant, exhibits superconductivity properties, and forms <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> layers.
</p><p>Niobium is slightly less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a> and more compact than its predecessor in the periodic table, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>, whereas it is virtually identical in size to the heavier tantalum atoms, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup> As a result, niobium's chemical properties are very similar to those for tantalum, which appears directly below niobium in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> Although its corrosion resistance is not as outstanding as that of tantalum, the lower price and greater availability make niobium attractive for less demanding applications, such as vat linings in chemical plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_niobium" title="Isotopes of niobium">Isotopes of niobium</a></div>
<p>Niobium in the Earth's crust comprises one stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>93</sup>Nb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-35">[35]</a></sup> By 2003, at least 32 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> had been synthesized, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> from 81 to 113. The most stable of these is <sup>92</sup>Nb with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 34.7 million years. One of the least stable is <sup>113</sup>Nb, with an estimated half-life of 30 milliseconds. Isotopes that are lighter than the stable <sup>93</sup>Nb tend to decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">β<sup>+</sup> decay</a>, and those that are heavier tend to decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">β<sup>−</sup> decay</a>, with some exceptions. <sup>81</sup>Nb, <sup>82</sup>Nb, and <sup>84</sup>Nb have minor β<sup>+</sup> delayed <a href="/wiki/Proton_emission" title="Proton emission">proton emission</a> decay paths, <sup>91</sup>Nb decays by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a>, and <sup>92</sup>Nb decays by both <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">β<sup>+</sup></a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">β<sup>−</sup></a> decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>At least 25 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> have been described, ranging in atomic mass from 84 to 104. Within this range, only <sup>96</sup>Nb, <sup>101</sup>Nb, and <sup>103</sup>Nb do not have isomers. The most stable of niobium's isomers is <sup>93m</sup>Nb with a half-life of 16.13 years. The least stable isomer is <sup>84m</sup>Nb with a half-life of 103 ns. All of niobium's isomers decay by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomeric_transition" title="Isomeric transition">isomeric transition</a> or beta decay except <sup>92m1</sup>Nb, which has a minor electron capture branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Niobium_minerals" title="Category:Niobium minerals">Category:Niobium minerals</a></div>
<p>Niobium is estimated to be the 34th <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">most common element in the Earth’s crust</a>, with 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Some think that the abundance on Earth is much greater, and that the element's high density has concentrated it in the Earth’s core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patel_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-patel-26">[26]</a></sup> The free element is not found in nature, but niobium occurs in combination with other elements in minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup> Minerals that contain niobium often also contain tantalum.  Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrocolumbite" title="Ferrocolumbite">columbite</a> ((Fe,Mn)(Nb,Ta)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">columbite–tantalite</a> (or <i>coltan</i>, (Fe,Mn)(Ta,Nb)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-37">[37]</a></sup> Columbite–tantalite minerals (the most common species being columbite-(Fe) and tantalite-(Fe), where "-(Fe)" is the Levinson suffix informing about the prevailence of iron over other elements like manganese<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>) are most usually found as accessory minerals in <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatite</a> intrusions, and in alkaline intrusive rocks. Less common are the niobates of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a>.  Examples of such niobates are <a href="/wiki/Pyrochlore" title="Pyrochlore">pyrochlore</a> ((Na,Ca)<sub>2</sub>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>(OH,F)) (now a group name, with a relatively common example being, e.g., fluorcalciopyrochlore<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> (correctly named euxenite-(Y)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>) ((Y,Ca,Ce,U,Th)(Nb,Ta,Ti)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>). These large deposits of niobium have been found associated with <a href="/wiki/Carbonatite" title="Carbonatite">carbonatites</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonate</a>-<a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous_rocks" title="Igneous rocks">igneous rocks</a>) and as a constituent of pyrochlore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyrochlore_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyrochlore-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The three largest currently mined deposits of pyrochlore, two in Brazil and one in Canada, were found in the 1950s, and are still the major producers of niobium mineral concentrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> The largest deposit is hosted within a carbonatite intrusion in <a href="/wiki/Arax%C3%A1" title="Araxá">Araxá</a>, state of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">Minas Gerais</a>, Brazil, owned by CBMM (<a href="/wiki/Companhia_Brasileira_de_Metalurgia_e_Minera%C3%A7%C3%A3o" title="Companhia Brasileira de Metalurgia e Mineração">Companhia Brasileira de Metalurgia e Mineração</a>); the other active Brazilian deposit is located near <a href="/wiki/Catal%C3%A3o" title="Catalão">Catalão</a>, state of <a href="/wiki/Goi%C3%A1s" title="Goiás">Goiás</a>, and owned by <a href="/wiki/China_Molybdenum" title="China Molybdenum">China Molybdenum</a>, also hosted within a carbonatite intrusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tesla_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-tesla-51">[51]</a></sup> Together, those two mines produce about 88% of the world's supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1-52">[52]</a></sup> Brazil also has a large but still unexploited deposit near <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Gabriel_da_Cachoeira" title="São Gabriel da Cachoeira">São Gabriel da Cachoeira</a>, state of <a href="/wiki/Amazonas_(Brazilian_state)" title="Amazonas (Brazilian state)">Amazonas</a>, as well as a few smaller deposits, notably in the state of <a href="/wiki/Roraima" title="Roraima">Roraima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-g1-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>The third largest producer of niobium is the carbonatite-hosted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobec" title="Niobec">Niobec</a> mine, in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Honor%C3%A9,_Quebec" title="Saint-Honoré, Quebec">Saint-Honoré</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Chicoutimi" title="Chicoutimi">Chicoutimi</a>, Quebec, Canada, owned by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magris_Resources&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magris Resources (page does not exist)">Magris Resources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niobec-magris_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-niobec-magris-53">[53]</a></sup> It produces between 7% and 10% of the world's supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tesla_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-tesla-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-g1-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg"><img alt="Grey and white world map with Brazil colored red representing 90% of niobium world production and Canada colored in dark blue representing 5% of niobium world production" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg/310px-World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg/465px-World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg/620px-World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Niobium_Production_2006.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Niobium producers in 2006 to 2015</div></div></div>
<p>After the separation from the other minerals, the mixed oxides of tantalum <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_pentoxide" title="Tantalum pentoxide">Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> and niobium <a href="/wiki/Niobium_pentoxide" title="Niobium pentoxide">Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> are obtained. The first step in the processing is the reaction of the oxides with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 14 HF → 2 H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 10 HF → 2 H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The first industrial scale separation, developed by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">de Marignac</a>, exploits the differing solubilities of the complex niobium and tantalum <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluorides</a>, dipotassium oxypentafluoroniobate monohydrate (K<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>]·H<sub>2</sub>O) and dipotassium heptafluorotantalate (K<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>]) in water. Newer processes use the liquid extraction of the fluorides from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aqueous</a> solution by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvents" title="Organic solvents">organic solvents</a> like <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-37">[37]</a></sup> The complex niobium and tantalum fluorides are extracted separately from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvent" title="Organic solvent">organic solvent</a> with water and either precipitated by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_fluoride" title="Potassium fluoride">potassium fluoride</a> to produce a potassium fluoride complex, or precipitated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> as the pentoxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] + 2 KF → K<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>]↓ + 2 HF</dd></dl>
<p>Followed by:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] + 10 NH<sub>4</sub>OH → Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>↓ + 10 NH<sub>4</sub>F + 7 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Several methods are used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction_(chemistry)" title="Reduction (chemistry)">reduction</a> to metallic niobium. The <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of a molten mixture of K<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> is one; the other is the reduction of the fluoride with <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>. With this method, a relatively high purity niobium can be obtained. In large scale production, Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is reduced with hydrogen or carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup>  In the <a href="/wiki/Aluminothermic_reaction" title="Aluminothermic reaction">aluminothermic reaction</a>, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> and niobium oxide is reacted with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 12 Al → 6 Nb + 2 Fe + 6 Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Small amounts of oxidizers like <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrate" title="Sodium nitrate">sodium nitrate</a> are added to enhance the reaction. The result is <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferroniobium" title="Ferroniobium">ferroniobium</a>, an alloy of iron and niobium used in the steel production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Ferroniobium contains between 60 and 70% niobium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tesla_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-tesla-51">[51]</a></sup> Without iron oxide, the aluminothermic process is used to produce niobium. Further purification is necessary to reach the grade for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductive" title="Superconductive">superconductive</a> alloys. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam_melting" title="Electron beam melting">Electron beam melting</a> under vacuum is the method used by the two major distributors of niobium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aguly_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aguly-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chou_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chou-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Companhia_Brasileira_de_Metalurgia_e_Minera%C3%A7%C3%A3o" title="Companhia Brasileira de Metalurgia e Mineração">CBMM</a> from Brazil controlled 85 percent of the world's niobium production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lucchesi2013_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-lucchesi2013-59">[59]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> estimates that the production increased from 38,700 tonnes in 2005 to 44,500 tonnes in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2006_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2006-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2007_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2007-61">[61]</a></sup>  Worldwide resources are estimated to be 4,400,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2007_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2007-61">[61]</a></sup> During the ten-year period between 1995 and 2005, the production more than doubled, starting from 17,800 tonnes in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS1997_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS1997-62">[62]</a></sup> Between 2009 and 2011, production was stable at  63,000 tonnes per year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> with a slight decrease in 2012 to only 50,000 tonnes per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Niobium_compounds" title="Category:Niobium compounds">Category:Niobium compounds</a></div>
<p>In many ways, niobium is similar to <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>. It reacts with most nonmetals at high temperatures; with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> at room temperature; with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> at 200 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>; and with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> at 400 °C, with products that are frequently interstitial and nonstoichiometric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup> The metal begins to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidize</a> in air at 200 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup> It resists corrosion by fused <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkalis</a> and by acids, including <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup> Niobium is attacked by <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and hydrofluoric/nitric acid mixtures.
</p><p>Although niobium exhibits all of the formal oxidation states from +5 to −1, the most common compounds have niobium in the +5 state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup> Characteristically, compounds in oxidation states less than 5+ display Nb–Nb bonding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_and_sulfides">Oxides and sulfides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Oxides and sulfides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Niobium forms <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> +5 (<a href="/wiki/Niobium_pentoxide" title="Niobium pentoxide">Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> +4 (<a href="/wiki/Niobium_dioxide" title="Niobium dioxide">NbO<sub>2</sub></a>), +3 (<span class="chemf nowrap">Nb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup> and the rarer oxidation state, +2 (<a href="/wiki/Niobium_monoxide" title="Niobium monoxide">NbO</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Most common is the pentoxide, precursor to almost all niobium compounds and alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-4"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cardarelli_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cardarelli-68">[68]</a></sup> Niobates are generated by dissolving the pentoxide in <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> solutions or by melting it in alkali metal oxides. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Lithium_niobate" title="Lithium niobate">lithium niobate</a> (LiNbO<sub>3</sub>) and lanthanum niobate (LaNbO<sub>4</sub>). In the lithium niobate is a trigonally distorted <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_(structure)" title="Perovskite (structure)">perovskite</a>-like structure, whereas the lanthanum niobate contains lone <span class="chemf nowrap">NbO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-5"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup> The layered niobium sulfide (NbS<sub>2</sub>) is also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nowak_31-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nowak-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Materials can be coated with a thin film of niobium(V) oxide <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a> processes, produced by the thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Niobium(V)_ethoxide" title="Niobium(V) ethoxide">niobium(V) ethoxide</a> above 350 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg"><img alt="Watch glass on a black surface with a small portion of yellow crystals" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="500" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg/180px-Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg/270px-Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg/360px-Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niobium_pentachloride_solid.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sample of niobium pentachloride (yellow portion) that has partially hydrolyzed (white material).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="711" data-file-width="1100" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/180px-Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/270px-Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/360px-Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niobium-pentachloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ball-and-stick model of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium_pentachloride" title="Niobium pentachloride">niobium pentachloride</a>, which exists as a <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a></div></div></div>
<p>Niobium forms halides in the oxidation states of +5 and +4 as well as diverse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">substoichiometric compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_54-6"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aguly_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aguly-57">[57]</a></sup> The pentahalides (<span class="chemf nowrap">NbX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>) feature octahedral Nb centres. Niobium pentafluoride (NbF<sub>5</sub>) is a white solid with a melting point of 79.0 °C and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium_pentachloride" title="Niobium pentachloride">niobium pentachloride</a> (NbCl<sub>5</sub>) is yellow (see image at left) with a melting point of 203.4 °C. Both are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrolyzed" title="Hydrolyzed">hydrolyzed</a> to give oxides and oxyhalides, such as NbOCl<sub>3</sub>. The pentachloride is a versatile reagent used to generate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Niobocene_dichloride" title="Niobocene dichloride">niobocene dichloride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">(C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>NbCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The tetrahalides (<span class="chemf nowrap">NbX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) are dark-coloured polymers with Nb-Nb bonds; for example, the black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> niobium tetrafluoride (NbF<sub>4</sub>) and brown niobium tetrachloride (NbCl<sub>4</sub>).
</p><p>Anionic halide compounds of niobium are well known, owing in part to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acidity</a> of the pentahalides. The most important is [NbF<sub>7</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>, an intermediate in the separation of Nb and Ta from the ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-37">[37]</a></sup> This heptafluoride tends to form the oxopentafluoride more readily than does the tantalum compound. Other halide complexes include octahedral [NbCl<sub>6</sub>]<sup>−</sup>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>10</sub> + 2 Cl<sup>−</sup> → 2 [NbCl<sub>6</sub>]<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>As with other metals with low atomic numbers, a variety of reduced halide cluster ions is known, the prime example being [Nb<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>18</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrides_and_carbides">Nitrides and carbides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nitrides and carbides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary compounds</a> of niobium include <a href="/wiki/Niobium_nitride" title="Niobium nitride">niobium nitride</a> (NbN), which becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a> at low temperatures and is used in detectors for infrared light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The main <a href="/wiki/Niobium_carbide" title="Niobium carbide">niobium carbide</a> is NbC, an extremely <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refraction_(metallurgy)" title="Refraction (metallurgy)">refractory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> material, commercially used in cutting <a href="/wiki/Tool_bit" title="Tool bit">tool bits</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niobium_metal.jpg"><img alt="Three pieces of metallic foil with yellow taint" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1209" data-file-width="1428" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Niobium_metal.jpg/220px-Niobium_metal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Niobium_metal.jpg/330px-Niobium_metal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Niobium_metal.jpg/440px-Niobium_metal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niobium_metal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A niobium foil</div></div></div>
<p>Out of 44,500 tonnes of niobium mined in 2006, an estimated 90% was used in high-grade structural steel. The second largest application is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2006_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2006-74">[74]</a></sup> Niobium alloy superconductors and electronic components account for a very small share of the world production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2006_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2006-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steel_production">Steel production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Steel production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Niobium is an effective <a href="/wiki/Microalloyed_steel" title="Microalloyed steel">microalloying</a> element for steel, within which it forms <a href="/wiki/Niobium_carbide" title="Niobium carbide">niobium carbide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niobium_nitride" title="Niobium nitride">niobium nitride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patel_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-patel-26">[26]</a></sup> These compounds improve the grain refining, and retard recrystallization and precipitation hardening. These effects in turn increase the toughness, strength, formability, and weldability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patel_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-patel-26">[26]</a></sup> Within microalloyed <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steels</a>, the niobium content is a small (less than 0.1%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heister_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-heister-75">[75]</a></sup>) but important addition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HSLA_steel" title="HSLA steel">high strength low alloy steels</a> that are widely used structurally in modern automobiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patel_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-patel-26">[26]</a></sup> Niobium is sometimes used in considerably higher quantities for highly wear-resistant machine components and knives, as high as 3% in Crucible CPM S110V stainless steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>These same niobium alloys are often used in pipeline construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eggert_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-eggert-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillenbrand_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hillenbrand-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superalloys">Superalloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Superalloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg"><img alt="Image of the Apollo Service Module with the moon in the background" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="904" data-file-width="1140" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg/220px-Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg/330px-Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg/440px-Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_CSM_lunar_orbit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Apollo 15 CSM in lunar orbit with <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module#Service_Propulsion_System" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">the dark rocket nozzle</a> made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">niobium-titanium</a> alloy</div></div></div>
<p>Quantities of niobium are used in nickel-, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>-, and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> in proportions as great as 6.5%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heister_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-heister-75">[75]</a></sup> for such applications as <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a> components, <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>, rocket subassemblies, turbo charger systems, heat resisting, and combustion equipment. Niobium precipitates a hardening γ''-phase within the grain structure of the superalloy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donachie_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donachie-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>One example superalloy is <a href="/wiki/Inconel" title="Inconel">Inconel 718</a>, consisting of roughly 50% <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, 18.6% <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, 18.5% <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, 5% niobium, 3.1% <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, 0.9% <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, and 0.4% <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-super_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-super-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> These superalloys were used, for example, in advanced air frame systems for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemini_program" title="Gemini program">Gemini program</a>. Another niobium alloy was used for the nozzle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_service_module" title="Apollo service module">Apollo Service Module</a>. Because niobium is oxidized at temperatures above 400 °C, a protective coating is necessary for these applications to prevent the alloy from becoming brittle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hightemp_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-hightemp-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niobium-based_alloys">Niobium-based alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Niobium-based alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>C-103</b> alloy was developed in the early 1960s jointly by the <a href="/wiki/Wah_Chang_Corporation" title="Wah Chang Corporation">Wah Chang Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> Co.  <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Union_Carbide" title="Union Carbide">Union Carbide</a> Corp., <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> Co. and several other companies were developing <a href="/wiki/Niobium_alloy" title="Niobium alloy">Nb-base alloys</a> simultaneously, largely driven by the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>.  It is composed of 89% niobium, 10% <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a> and 1% titanium and is used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket" title="Liquid rocket">liquid rocket</a> thruster nozzles, such as the main engine of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Modules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hightemp_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-hightemp-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>The nozzle of the <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(rocket_engine_family)#Merlin_Vacuum_(1C)" title="Merlin (rocket engine family)">Merlin Vacuum</a> series of engines developed by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> for the upper stage of its <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> rocket is made from a niobium alloy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSPO_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSPO-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reactivity of niobium with oxygen requires it to be worked in a <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">vacuum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert atmosphere</a>, which significantly increases the cost and difficulty of production.  <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_arc_remelting" title="Vacuum arc remelting">Vacuum arc remelting</a> (VAR) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam_melting" title="Electron beam melting">electron beam melting</a> (EBM), novel processes at the time, enabled the development of niobium and other reactive metals.  The project that yielded C-103 began in 1959 with as many as 256 experimental niobium alloys in the "C-series" (possibly from <b>c</b>olumbium) that could be melted as buttons and rolled into <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet</a>.  Wah Chang had an inventory of hafnium, refined from nuclear-grade <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_alloy" title="Zirconium alloy">zirconium alloys</a>, that it wanted to put to commercial use.  The 103rd experimental composition of the C-series alloys, Nb-10Hf-1Ti, had the best combination of formability and high-temperature properties. Wah Chang fabricated the first 500-lb heat of C-103 in 1961, ingot to sheet, using EBM and VAR. The intended applications included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbine_engine" title="Turbine engine">turbine engines</a> and liquid metal <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchangers</a>.  Competing niobium alloys from that era included FS85 (Nb-10W-28Ta-1Zr) from Fansteel Metallurgical Corp., Cb129Y (Nb-10W-10Hf-0.2Y) from Wah Chang and Boeing, Cb752 (Nb-10W-2.5Zr) from Union Carbide, and Nb1Zr from Superior Tube Co.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hightemp_82-2"><a href="#cite_note-hightemp-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superconducting_magnets">Superconducting magnets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Superconducting magnets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Modern_3T_MRI.JPG"><img alt="Room-high yellow-grey medical machine with a man-size hole in the middle and a stretcher directly in front of it" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Modern_3T_MRI.JPG/220px-Modern_3T_MRI.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Modern_3T_MRI.JPG/330px-Modern_3T_MRI.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Modern_3T_MRI.JPG/440px-Modern_3T_MRI.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Modern_3T_MRI.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 3-<a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">tesla</a> clinical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">magnetic resonance imaging</a> scanner using niobium superconducting alloy</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Niobium-germanium" title="Niobium-germanium">Niobium-germanium</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Nb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>Ge</span>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-tin" title="Niobium-tin">niobium-tin</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Nb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>Sn</span>), as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">niobium-titanium</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> are used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_II_superconductor" title="Type II superconductor">type II superconductor</a> wire for <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_magnet" title="Superconducting magnet">superconducting magnets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> These superconducting magnets are used in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">nuclear magnetic resonance</a> instruments as well as in <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> uses 600 tons of superconducting strands, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Thermonuclear_Experimental_Reactor" title="International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor">International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor</a> uses an estimated 600 tonnes of Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn strands and 250 tonnes of NbTi strands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alstrom_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-alstrom-87">[87]</a></sup> In 1992 alone, more than US$1 billion worth of clinical magnetic resonance imaging systems were constructed with niobium-titanium wire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geballe_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-geballe-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_superconductors">Other superconductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other superconductors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG/220px-A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG/330px-A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG/440px-A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_1.3_GHz_nine-cell_superconducting_radio_frequency.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1.3 GHz 9-cell SRF cavity made from niobium is on display at <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Superconducting_radio_frequency" title="Superconducting radio frequency">superconducting radio frequency</a> (SRF) cavities used in the <a href="/wiki/Free-electron_laser" title="Free-electron laser">free-electron lasers</a> <a href="/wiki/FLASH" title="FLASH">FLASH</a> (result of the cancelled TESLA linear accelerator project) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_x-ray_free_electron_laser" title="European x-ray free electron laser">XFEL</a> are made from pure niobium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Cryomodule" title="Cryomodule">cryomodule</a> team at <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a> used the same SRF technology from the FLASH project to develop 1.3 GHz nine-cell SRF cavities made from pure niobium. The cavities will be used in the 30-kilometre (19 mi) <a href="/wiki/Linear_particle_accelerator" title="Linear particle accelerator">linear particle accelerator</a> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Linear_Collider" title="International Linear Collider">International Linear Collider</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> The same technology will be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCLS-II" title="LCLS-II">LCLS-II</a> at <a href="/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory">SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PIP-II" title="PIP-II">PIP-II</a> at Fermilab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>The high sensitivity of superconducting niobium nitride <a href="/wiki/Bolometer" title="Bolometer">bolometers</a> make them an ideal detector for <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> in the THz frequency band. These detectors were tested at the <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz_Submillimeter_Telescope" title="Heinrich Hertz Submillimeter Telescope">Submillimeter Telescope</a>, the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole_Telescope" title="South Pole Telescope">South Pole Telescope</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Receiver_Lab_Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Receiver Lab Telescope (page does not exist)">Receiver Lab Telescope</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Pathfinder_Experiment" title="Atacama Pathfinder Experiment">APEX</a>, and are now used in the HIFI instrument on board the <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">Herschel Space Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroceramics">Electroceramics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Electroceramics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lithium_niobate" title="Lithium niobate">Lithium niobate</a>, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferroelectric" title="Ferroelectric">ferroelectric</a>, is used extensively in mobile telephones and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_modulators" title="Optical modulators">optical modulators</a>, and for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Surface_acoustic_wave" title="Surface acoustic wave">surface acoustic wave</a> devices. It belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">ABO<sub>3</sub></a> structure ferroelectrics like <a href="/wiki/Lithium_tantalate" title="Lithium tantalate">lithium tantalate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barium_titanate" title="Barium titanate">barium titanate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Niobium_capacitor" title="Niobium capacitor">Niobium capacitors</a> are available as alternative to  <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_capacitor" title="Tantalum capacitor">tantalum capacitors</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> but tantalum capacitors still predominate. Niobium is added to glass to obtain a higher <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, making possible thinner and lighter <a href="/wiki/Glasses" title="Glasses">corrective glasses</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypoallergenic_applications:_medicine_and_jewelry">Hypoallergenic applications: medicine and jewelry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Hypoallergenic applications: medicine and jewelry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Niobium and some niobium alloys are physiologically inert and <a href="/wiki/Hypoallergenic" title="Hypoallergenic">hypoallergenic</a>. For this reason, niobium is used in prosthetics and implant devices, such as pacemakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Niobium treated with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> forms a porous layer that aids <a href="/wiki/Osseointegration" title="Osseointegration">osseointegration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like titanium, tantalum, and aluminium, niobium can be heated and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodize" title="Anodize">anodized</a> ("reactive metal <a href="/wiki/Anodizing" title="Anodizing">anodization</a>") to produce a wide array of <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescent</a> colours for jewelry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> where its hypoallergenic property is highly desirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Numismatics">Numismatics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Numismatics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2004_Austria_25_Euro_150_Years_Semmering_Alpine_Railway_front.jpg"><img alt="Coin with a dark green cener and a silvery outer rim. The rim reads: Republik Österreich 25 Euro. The centere shows electric and a steam driven locomotive" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="220" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c9/2004_Austria_25_Euro_150_Years_Semmering_Alpine_Railway_front.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2004_Austria_25_Euro_150_Years_Semmering_Alpine_Railway_front.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 150 Years <a href="/wiki/Semmering_railway" title="Semmering railway">Semmering Alpine Railway</a> Coin made of niobium and silver</div></div></div>
<p>Niobium is used as a precious metal in commemorative coins, often with silver or gold. For example, Austria produced a series of silver niobium <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> coins starting in 2003; the colour in these coins is created by the <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> of light by a thin anodized oxide layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> In 2012, ten coins are available showing a broad variety of colours in the centre of the coin: blue, green, brown, purple, violet, or yellow. Two more examples are the 2004 Austrian €25 <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Austria)#2004_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Austria)">150 Years Semmering Alpine Railway commemorative coin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> and the 2006 Austrian €25 <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Austria)#2006_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Austria)">European Satellite Navigation commemorative coin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
The Austrian mint produced for Latvia a similar series of coins starting in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
with one following in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
In 2011, the Royal Canadian Mint started production of a $5 <a href="/wiki/Sterling_silver" title="Sterling silver">sterling silver</a> and niobium coin named <i>Hunter's Moon</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
in which the niobium was selectively oxidized, thus creating unique finishes where no two coins are exactly alike.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The arc-tube seals of high pressure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_lamp" title="Sodium vapor lamp">sodium vapor lamps</a> are made from niobium, sometimes alloyed with 1% of <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>; niobium has a very similar coefficient of thermal expansion, matching the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sintered" title="Sintered">sintered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_tube" title="Arc tube">arc tube</a> ceramic, a translucent material which resists chemical attack or <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> by the hot liquid sodium and sodium vapour contained inside the operating lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>Niobium is used in <a href="/wiki/Arc_welding" title="Arc welding">arc welding</a> rods for some stabilized grades of stainless steel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> and in anodes for cathodic protection systems on some water tanks, which are then usually plated with platinum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Niobium is an important component of high-performance heterogeneous catalysts for the production of acrylic acid by selective oxidation of propane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p><p>Niobium is used to make the high voltage wire of the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">Solar Corona</a> particles receptor module of the <a href="/wiki/Parker_Solar_Probe" title="Parker Solar Probe">Parker Solar Probe</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Niobium has no known biological role. While niobium dust is an eye and skin irritant and a potential fire hazard, elemental niobium on a larger scale is physiologically inert (and thus hypoallergenic) and harmless. It is frequently used in jewelry and has been tested for use in some medical implants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p><p>Niobium-containing compounds are rarely encountered by most people, but some are toxic and should be treated with care. The short- and long-term exposure to niobates and niobium chloride, two chemicals that are water-soluble, have been tested in rats. Rats treated with a single injection of niobium pentachloride or niobates show a <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) between 10 and 100 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haley_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haley-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> For oral administration the toxicity is lower; a study with rats yielded a LD<sub>50</sub> after seven days of 940 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haley_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haley-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p>
