<p><b>Manganese</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Mn</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 25. It is not found as a <a href="/wiki/Free_element" title="Free element">free element</a> in nature; it is often found in <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> in combination with <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. Manganese is a metal with important industrial metal <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> uses, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steels</a>.
</p><p>Historically, manganese is named for <a href="/wiki/Pyrolusite" title="Pyrolusite">pyrolusite</a> and other black minerals from the region of <a href="/wiki/Magnesia_(regional_unit)" title="Magnesia (regional unit)">Magnesia</a> in Greece, which also gave its name to <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> ore <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>. By the mid-18th century, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish-</a><a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> had used pyrolusite to produce <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>. Scheele and others were aware that pyrolusite (now known to be <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>) contained a new element, but they were unable to isolate it. <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gottlieb_Gahn" title="Johan Gottlieb Gahn">Johan Gottlieb Gahn</a> was the first to isolate an impure sample of manganese metal in 1774, which he did by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction-oxidation" title="Reduction-oxidation">reducing</a> the dioxide with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Phosphate_conversion_coating" title="Phosphate conversion coating">Manganese phosphating</a> is used for rust and corrosion prevention on <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>. Ionized manganese is used industrially as <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> of various colors, which depend on the oxidation state of the ions. The <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a> are powerful oxidizers. Manganese dioxide is used as the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> (electron acceptor) material in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc-carbon_battery" title="Zinc-carbon battery">zinc-carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_battery" title="Alkaline battery">alkaline batteries</a>.
</p><p>In biology, manganese(II) ions function as <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a> for a large variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> with many functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Manganese enzymes are particularly essential in detoxification of <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> free radicals in organisms that must deal with elemental <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. Manganese also functions in the oxygen-evolving complex of photosynthetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a>. While the element is a required trace mineral for all known living organisms, it also acts as a <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxin" title="Neurotoxin">neurotoxin</a> in larger amounts. Especially through inhalation, it can cause <a href="/wiki/Manganism" title="Manganism">manganism</a>, a condition in mammals leading to neurological damage that is sometimes irreversible.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3368" data-file-width="5424" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/200px-Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/300px-Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/400px-Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Manganese_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Electrolytically refined manganese chips and a 1 cm<sup>3</sup> cube</div></div></div>
<p>Manganese is a silvery-gray <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that resembles iron. It is hard and very brittle, difficult to fuse, but easy to oxidize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">[4]</a></sup> Manganese metal and its common ions are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-magnet-5">[5]</a></sup> Manganese tarnishes slowly in air and oxidizes ("rusts") like iron in water containing dissolved oxygen.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_manganese" title="Isotopes of manganese">Isotopes of manganese</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring manganese is composed of one stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>55</sup>Mn. Eighteen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been isolated and described, ranging in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> from 46 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>46</sup>Mn) to 65 u (<sup>65</sup>Mn). The most stable are <sup>53</sup>Mn with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.7 million years, <sup>54</sup>Mn with a half-life of 312.3 days, and <sup>52</sup>Mn with a half-life of 5.591 days. All of the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives of less than three hours, and the majority of less than one minute. The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> before the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>55</sup>Mn, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and the primary mode after is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-6">[6]</a></sup> Manganese also has three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganese is part of the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> group of elements, which are thought to be synthesized in large <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> shortly before the <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> explosion. <sup>53</sup>Mn decays to <sup>53</sup><a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">Cr</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.7 million years. Because of its relatively short half-life, <sup>53</sup>Mn is relatively rare, produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> impact on <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Manganese isotopic contents are typically combined with <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> isotopic contents and have found application in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_geology" title="Isotope geology">isotope geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a>. Mn–Cr isotopic ratios reinforce the evidence from <a href="/wiki/Aluminium-26" title="Aluminium-26"><sup>26</sup>Al</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladium-107" title="Palladium-107"><sup>107</sup>Pd</a> for the early history of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>. Variations in <sup>53</sup>Cr/<sup>52</sup>Cr and Mn/Cr ratios from several <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a> suggest an initial <sup>53</sup>Mn/<sup>55</sup>Mn ratio, which indicates that Mn–Cr isotopic composition must result from <i>in situ</i> decay of <sup>53</sup>Mn in differentiated planetary bodies. Hence, <sup>53</sup>Mn provides additional evidence for <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthetic</a> processes immediately before coalescence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="521" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG/120px-Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG/180px-Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG/240px-Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chlorid_manganat%C3%BD.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_chloride" title="Manganese(II) chloride">Manganese(II) chloride</a> crystals – the pale pink color of Mn(II) salts is due to a spin-forbidden 3d transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The most common <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> of manganese are +2, +3, +4, +6, and +7, though all oxidation states from −3 to +7 have been observed. Mn<sup>2+</sup> often competes with Mg<sup>2+</sup> in biological systems. Manganese compounds where manganese is in oxidation state +7, which are mostly restricted to the unstable oxide Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, compounds of the intensely purple permanganate anion MnO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>, and a few oxyhalides (MnO<sub>3</sub>F and MnO<sub>3</sub>Cl), are powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidizing agents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">[4]</a></sup> Compounds with oxidation states +5 (blue) and +6 (green) are strong oxidizing agents and are vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="666" data-file-width="279" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg/120px-KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg/180px-KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg/240px-KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KMnO4_in_H2O.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aqueous solution of KMnO<sub>4</sub> illustrating the deep purple of Mn(VII) as it occurs in permanganate</div></div></div>
<p>The most stable oxidation state for manganese is +2, which has a pale pink color, and many manganese(II) compounds are known, such as <a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_sulfate" title="Manganese(II) sulfate">manganese(II) sulfate</a> (MnSO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_chloride" title="Manganese(II) chloride">manganese(II) chloride</a> (MnCl<sub>2</sub>). This oxidation state is also seen in the mineral rhodochrosite (<a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_carbonate" title="Manganese(II) carbonate">manganese(II) carbonate</a>). Manganese(II) most commonly exists with a high spin, S = 5/2 ground state because of the high pairing energy for manganese(II). However, there are a few examples of low-spin, S =1/2 manganese(II).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saha_1703–1708_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saha_1703–1708-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saha_1703–1708_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saha_1703–1708-9">[9]</a></sup> There are no spin-allowed d–d transitions in manganese(II), explaining why manganese(II) compounds are typically pale to colorless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The +3 oxidation state is known in compounds like <a href="/wiki/Manganese(III)_acetate" title="Manganese(III) acetate">manganese(III) acetate</a>, but these are quite powerful <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizing agents</a> and also prone to <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> in solution, forming manganese(II) and manganese(IV). Solid compounds of manganese(III) are characterized by its strong purple-red color and a preference for distorted octahedral coordination resulting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jahn-Teller_effect" title="Jahn-Teller effect">Jahn-Teller effect</a>.
</p><p>The oxidation state +5 can be produced by dissolving manganese dioxide in molten <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Manganate (VI) salts can be produced by dissolving Mn compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>, in molten alkali while exposed to air. Permanganate (+7 oxidation state) compounds are purple, and can give glass a violet color. <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">Potassium permanganate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_permanganate" title="Sodium permanganate">sodium permanganate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barium_permanganate" title="Barium permanganate">barium permanganate</a> are all potent oxidizers. Potassium permanganate, also called Condy's crystals, is a commonly used laboratory <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> because of its oxidizing properties; it is used as a topical medicine (for example, in the treatment of fish diseases). Solutions of potassium permanganate were among the first stains and fixatives to be used in the preparation of biological cells and tissues for electron microscopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of the name manganese is complex. In ancient times, two black minerals from <a href="/wiki/Magnesia_(regional_unit)" title="Magnesia (regional unit)">Magnesia</a> (located within modern Greece) were both called <i>magnes</i> from their place of origin, but were thought to differ in gender. The male <i>magnes</i> attracted iron, and was the iron ore now known as <a href="/wiki/Lodestone" title="Lodestone">lodestone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, and which probably gave us the term <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a>. The female <i>magnes</i> ore did not attract iron, but was used to decolorize glass. This feminine <i>magnes</i> was later called <i>magnesia</i>, known now in modern times as <a href="/wiki/Pyrolusite" title="Pyrolusite">pyrolusite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>. Neither this mineral nor elemental manganese is magnetic. In the 16th century, manganese dioxide was called <i>manganesum</i> (note the two Ns instead of one) by glassmakers, possibly as a corruption and concatenation of two words, since alchemists and glassmakers eventually had to differentiate a <i>magnesia negra</i> (the black ore) from <i>magnesia alba</i> (a white ore, also from Magnesia, also useful in glassmaking). <a href="/wiki/Michele_Mercati" title="Michele Mercati">Michele Mercati</a> called magnesia negra <i>manganesa</i>, and finally the metal isolated from it became known as <i>manganese</i> (German: <i>Mangan</i>). The name <i>magnesia</i> eventually was then used to refer only to the white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnesia_alba" title="Magnesia alba">magnesia alba</a> (magnesium oxide), which provided the name <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> for the free element when it was isolated much later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lascaux_painting.jpg"><img alt="A drawing of a left-facing bull, in black, on a cave wall" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="472" data-file-width="720" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Lascaux_painting.jpg/220px-Lascaux_painting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Lascaux_painting.jpg/330px-Lascaux_painting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Lascaux_painting.jpg/440px-Lascaux_painting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lascaux_painting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some of the cave paintings in <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, use manganese-based pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lascaux_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lascaux-15">[15]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Several colorful oxides of manganese, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">manganese dioxide</a>, are abundant in nature and have been used as pigments since the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>. The cave paintings in <a href="/wiki/Gargas,_Haute-Garonne" title="Gargas, Haute-Garonne">Gargas</a> that are 30,000 to 24,000 years old contain manganese pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganese compounds were used by Egyptian and Roman glassmakers, either to add to, or remove color from glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Use as "glassmakers soap" continued through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> until modern times and is evident in 14th-century glass from <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ItGlass_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ItGlass-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="1064" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg/220px-Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg/330px-Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg/440px-Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gahn_Johan_Gottlieb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Credit for first isolating manganese is usually given to <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gottlieb_Gahn" title="Johan Gottlieb Gahn">Johan Gottlieb Gahn</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Because it was used in glassmaking, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">manganese dioxide</a> was available for experiments by alchemists, the first chemists. <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Gottfried_Kaim" title="Ignatius Gottfried Kaim">Ignatius Gottfried Kaim</a> (1770) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Glauber" title="Johann Glauber">Johann Glauber</a> (17th century) discovered that manganese dioxide could be converted to <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a>, a useful laboratory reagent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> By the mid-18th century, the Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> used manganese dioxide to produce <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>. First, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, or a mixture of dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> was made to react with manganese dioxide, later hydrochloric acid from the <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">Leblanc process</a> was used and the manganese dioxide was recycled by the <a href="/wiki/Weldon_process" title="Weldon process">Weldon process</a>. The production of chlorine and <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorite" title="Hypochlorite">hypochlorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Bleach" title="Bleach">bleaching</a> agents was a large consumer of manganese ores.
</p><p>Scheele and other chemists were aware that manganese dioxide contained a new element, but they were not able to isolate it. <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gottlieb_Gahn" title="Johan Gottlieb Gahn">Johan Gottlieb Gahn</a> was the first to isolate an impure sample of manganese metal in 1774, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction-oxidation" title="Reduction-oxidation">reducing</a> the dioxide with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.
</p><p>The manganese content of some iron ores used in Greece led to speculations that steel produced from that ore contains additional manganese, making the <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Spartan</a> steel exceptionally hard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Around the beginning of the 19th century, manganese was used in steelmaking and several patents were granted. In 1816, it was documented that iron alloyed with manganese was harder but not more brittle. In 1837, British academic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Couper_(academic)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Couper (academic) (page does not exist)">James Couper</a> noted an association between miners' heavy exposure to manganese with a form of <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couper_1837_41–42_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Couper_1837_41–42-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1912, United States patents were granted for protecting firearms against rust and corrosion with manganese phosphate electrochemical conversion coatings, and the process has seen widespread use ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Leclanch%C3%A9_cell" title="Leclanché cell">Leclanché cell</a> in 1866 and the subsequent improvement of batteries containing manganese dioxide as cathodic <a href="/wiki/Depolarizer" title="Depolarizer">depolarizer</a> increased the demand for manganese dioxide. Until the development of batteries with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">nickel-cadmium</a> and lithium, most batteries contained manganese. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc-carbon_battery" title="Zinc-carbon battery">zinc-carbon battery</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_battery" title="Alkaline battery">alkaline battery</a> normally use industrially produced manganese dioxide because naturally occurring manganese dioxide contains impurities. In the 20th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">manganese dioxide</a> was widely used as the cathodic for commercial disposable dry batteries of both the standard (zinc-carbon) and alkaline types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChiuZMnO2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChiuZMnO2-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Manganese_minerals" title="Category:Manganese minerals">Category:Manganese minerals</a></div>
<p>Manganese comprises about 1000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> (0.1%) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>, the 12th most abundant of the crust's elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-24">[24]</a></sup> Soil contains 7–9000 ppm of manganese with an average of 440 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-24">[24]</a></sup> Seawater has only 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> manganese and the atmosphere contains 0.01 µg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-24">[24]</a></sup> Manganese occurs principally as <a href="/wiki/Pyrolusite" title="Pyrolusite">pyrolusite</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">MnO<sub>2</sub></a>), <a href="/wiki/Braunite" title="Braunite">braunite</a>, (Mn<sup>2+</sup>Mn<sup>3+</sup><sub>6</sub>)(SiO<sub>12</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Psilomelane" title="Psilomelane">psilomelane</a> (Ba,H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>Mn<sub>5</sub>O<sub>10</sub>, and to a lesser extent as <a href="/wiki/Rhodochrosite" title="Rhodochrosite">rhodochrosite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_carbonate" title="Manganese(II) carbonate">MnCO<sub>3</sub></a>).
</p>

<p>The most important manganese ore is pyrolusite (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">MnO<sub>2</sub></a>). Other economically important manganese ores usually show a close spatial relation to the iron ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">[4]</a></sup> Land-based resources are large but irregularly distributed. About 80% of the known world manganese resources are in South Africa; other important manganese deposits are in Ukraine, Australia, India, China, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> and Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSMCS2009_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSMCS2009-26">[26]</a></sup> According to 1978 estimate, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_floor" title="Ocean floor">ocean floor</a> has 500 billion tons of <a href="/wiki/Manganese_nodule" title="Manganese nodule">manganese nodules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Attempts to find economically viable methods of harvesting manganese nodules were abandoned in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The CIA once used mining manganese modules on the ocean floor as a cover story for recovering a sunken Soviet submarine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In South Africa, most identified deposits are located near <a href="/wiki/Hotazel" title="Hotazel">Hotazel</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Cape_Province" title="Northern Cape Province">Northern Cape Province</a>, with a 2011 estimate of 15 billion tons. In 2011 South Africa produced 3.4 million tons, topping all other nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mbendi_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mbendi-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganese is mainly mined in South Africa, Australia, China, Gabon, Brazil, India, Kazakhstan, Ghana, Ukraine and Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> US Import Sources (1998–2001): Manganese ore: Gabon, 70%; South Africa, 10%; Australia, 9%; Mexico, 5%; and other, 6%. Ferromanganese: South Africa, 47%; France, 22%; Mexico, 8%; Australia, 8%; and other, 15%. Manganese contained in all manganese imports: South Africa, 31%; Gabon, 21%; Australia, 13%; Mexico, 8%; and other, 27%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSMCS2009_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGSMCS2009-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MangUSGS2006_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MangUSGS2006-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the production of <a href="/wiki/Ferromanganese" title="Ferromanganese">ferromanganese</a>, the manganese ore is mixed with iron ore and carbon, and then reduced either in a blast furnace or in an electric arc furnace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IndMin_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-IndMin-33">[33]</a></sup> The resulting <a href="/wiki/Ferromanganese" title="Ferromanganese">ferromanganese</a> has a manganese content of 30 to 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">[4]</a></sup> Pure manganese used for the production of iron-free alloys is produced by <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(metallurgy)" title="Leaching (metallurgy)">leaching</a> manganese ore with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and a subsequent <a href="/wiki/Electrowinning" title="Electrowinning">electrowinning</a> process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrometI_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-hydrometI-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg"><img alt="Contains reactions and temperatures, as well as showing advanced processes such as the heat exchanger and milling process." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="675" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg/290px-Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg/435px-Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg/580px-Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Manganese_Process_Flow_Diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Process flow diagram for a manganese refining circuit.</div></div></div>
<p>A more progressive extraction process involves directly reducing manganese ore in a heap leach. This is done by percolating natural gas through the bottom of the heap; the natural gas provides the heat (needs to be at least 850 °C) and the reducing agent (carbon monoxide). This reduces all of the manganese ore to manganese oxide (MnO), which is a leachable form. The ore then travels through a grinding circuit to reduce the particle size of the ore to between 150–250 μm, increasing the surface area to aid leaching. The ore is then added to a leach tank of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and ferrous iron (Fe<sup>2+</sup>) in a 1.6:1 ratio. The iron reacts with the manganese dioxide to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_hydroxide" title="Iron hydroxide">iron hydroxide</a> and elemental manganese. This process yields approximately 92% recovery of the manganese. For further purification, the manganese can then be sent to an electrowinning facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManganeseRecovery_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManganeseRecovery-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1972 the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">CIA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Project_Azorian" title="Project Azorian">Project Azorian</a>, through billionaire <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes" title="Howard Hughes">Howard Hughes</a>, commissioned the ship <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_Glomar_Explorer" title="Hughes Glomar Explorer">Hughes Glomar Explorer</a></i> with the cover story of harvesting manganese nodules from the sea floor. That triggered a rush of activity to collect manganese nodules, which was not actually practical. The real mission of <i>Hughes Glomar Explorer</i> was to raise a sunken <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Socialist_Republics" title="Union of Soviet Socialist Republics">Soviet</a> submarine, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_submarine_K-129_(1960)" title="Soviet submarine K-129 (1960)">K-129</a>, with the goal of retrieving Soviet code books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azorian_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-azorian-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manganese has no satisfactory substitute in its major applications in metallurgy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSMCS2009_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-USGSMCS2009-26">[26]</a></sup> In minor applications, (e.g., manganese phosphating), <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> are viable substitutes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steel">Steel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Steel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M1917helmet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="640" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/M1917helmet.jpg/220px-M1917helmet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/M1917helmet.jpg/330px-M1917helmet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/M1917helmet.jpg/440px-M1917helmet.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M1917helmet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. M1917 <a href="/wiki/Combat_helmet" title="Combat helmet">combat helmet</a>, a variant of <a href="/wiki/Brodie_helmet" title="Brodie helmet">Brodie helmet</a>, made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadfield_steel" title="Hadfield steel">Hadfield steel</a> manganese alloy.</div></div></div>
<p>Manganese is essential to iron and <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steel production</a> by virtue of its sulfur-fixing, <a href="/wiki/Deoxidized_steel" title="Deoxidized steel">deoxidizing</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloying" title="Alloying">alloying</a> properties, as first recognized by the British metallurgist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Forester_Mushet" title="Robert Forester Mushet">Robert Forester Mushet</a> (1811–1891) who, in 1856, introduced the element, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Spiegeleisen" title="Spiegeleisen">Spiegeleisen</a>, into steel for the specific purpose of removing excess dissolved oxygen, sulfur, and phosphorus in order to improve its malleability. <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">Steelmaking</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> including its ironmaking component, has accounted for most manganese demand, presently in the range of 85% to 90% of the total demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hydrometI_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-hydrometI-34">[34]</a></sup> Manganese is a key component of low-cost <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MangUSGS2006_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-MangUSGS2006-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Often <a href="/wiki/Ferromanganese" title="Ferromanganese">ferromanganese</a> (usually about 80% manganese) is the intermediate in modern processes.
</p><p>Small amounts of manganese improve the workability of steel at high temperatures by forming a high-melting sulfide and preventing the formation of a liquid <a href="/wiki/Iron_sulfide" title="Iron sulfide">iron sulfide</a> at the grain boundaries. If the manganese content reaches 4%, the embrittlement of the steel becomes a dominant feature. The embrittlement decreases at higher manganese concentrations and reaches an acceptable level at 8%. Steel containing 8 to 15% of manganese has a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of up to 863 MPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Steel with 12% manganese was discovered in 1882 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hadfield" title="Robert Hadfield">Robert Hadfield</a> and is still known as <a href="/wiki/Mangalloy" title="Mangalloy">Hadfield steel (mangalloy)</a>. It was used for British military <a href="/wiki/Brodie_helmet" title="Brodie helmet">steel helmets</a> and later by the U.S. military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aluminium_alloys">Aluminium alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aluminium alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">Aluminium alloy</a></div>
<p>The second largest application for manganese is in aluminium alloys. Aluminium with roughly 1.5% manganese has increased resistance to corrosion through grains that absorb impurities which would lead to <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">galvanic corrosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The corrosion-resistant <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">aluminium alloys</a> 3004 and 3104 (0.8 to 1.5% manganese) are used for most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage_can" title="Beverage can">beverage cans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al3004_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al3004-43">[43]</a></sup> Before 2000, more than 1.6 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of those alloys were used; at 1% manganese, this consumed 16,000 tonnes of manganese.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al3004_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Al3004-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl" title="Methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl">Methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl</a> is used as an additive in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unleaded_gasoline" title="Unleaded gasoline">unleaded gasoline</a> to boost <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane rating</a> and reduce <a href="/wiki/Engine_knocking" title="Engine knocking">engine knocking</a>. The manganese in this unusual organometallic compound is in the +1 oxidation state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">Manganese(IV) oxide</a> (manganese dioxide, MnO<sub>2</sub>) is used as a reagent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of benzylic <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> (where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group is adjacent to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_ring" title="Aromatic ring">aromatic ring</a>). Manganese dioxide has been used since antiquity to oxidize and neutralize the greenish tinge in glass from trace amounts of iron contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ItGlass_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ItGlass-18">[18]</a></sup> MnO<sub>2</sub> is also used in the manufacture of oxygen and chlorine and in drying black paints. In some preparations, it is a brown <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> and is a constituent of natural <a href="/wiki/Umber" title="Umber">umber</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manganese(IV)_oxide" title="Manganese(IV) oxide">Manganese(IV) oxide</a> was used in the original type of dry cell <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> as an electron acceptor from zinc, and is the blackish material in carbon–zinc type flashlight cells. The manganese dioxide is reduced to the manganese oxide-hydroxide MnO(OH) during discharging, preventing the formation of hydrogen at the anode of the battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BattHist_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BattHist-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>MnO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + e<sup>−</sup> → MnO(OH) + <span class="chemf nowrap">OH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The same material also functions in newer <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_battery" title="Alkaline battery">alkaline batteries</a> (usually battery cells), which use the same basic reaction, but a different electrolyte mixture. In 2002, more than 230,000 tons of manganese dioxide was used for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChiuZMnO2_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChiuZMnO2-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BattHist_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-BattHist-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="948" data-file-width="948" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG/150px-1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG/225px-1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG/300px-1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1945-P-Jefferson-War-Nickel-Reverse.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World-War-II-era 5-cent coin (1942-5 identified by mint mark P, D or S above dome) made from a 56% copper-35% silver-9% manganese alloy</div></div></div>
<p>The metal is occasionally used in coins; until 2000, the only United States coin to use manganese was the <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_nickel#1938–1945:_Early_minting;_World_War_II_changes" title="Jefferson nickel">"wartime" nickel</a> from 1942 to 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> An alloy of 75% copper and 25% nickel was traditionally used for the production of nickel coins. However, because of shortage of nickel metal during the war, it was substituted by more available silver and manganese, thus resulting in an alloy of 56% copper, 35% silver and 9% manganese. Since 2000, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dollar_(United_States_coin)" title="Dollar (United States coin)">dollar coins</a>, for example the <a href="/wiki/Sacagawea_dollar" title="Sacagawea dollar">Sacagawea dollar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_$1_Coin_Program" title="Presidential $1 Coin Program">Presidential $1 coins</a>, are made from a brass containing 7% of manganese with a pure copper core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In both cases of nickel and dollar, the use of manganese in the coin was to duplicate the electromagnetic properties of a previous identically sized and valued coin in the mechanisms of vending machines. In the case of the later U.S. dollar coins, the manganese alloy was intended to duplicate the properties of the copper/nickel alloy used in the previous <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony_dollar" title="Susan B. Anthony dollar">Susan B. Anthony dollar</a>. 
</p><p>Manganese compounds have been used as pigments and for the coloring of ceramics and glass. The brown color of ceramic is sometimes the result of manganese compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In the glass industry, manganese compounds are used for two effects. Manganese(III) reacts with iron(II) to induce a strong green color in glass by forming less-colored iron(III) and slightly pink manganese(II), compensating for the residual color of the iron(III).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ItGlass_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-ItGlass-18">[18]</a></sup> Larger quantities of manganese are used to produce pink colored glass.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetravalence" title="Tetravalence">Tetravalent</a> manganese is used as an <a href="/wiki/Activator_(phosphor)" title="Activator (phosphor)">activator</a> in red-emitting <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a>. While many compounds are known which show <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">luminescence</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> the majority is not used in commercial application due to low efficiency or deep red emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> However, several Mn<sup>4+</sup> aktivated fluorides were reported as potential red emitting phorshors for warm-white LEDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> But to this day, only K<sub>2</sub>SiF<sub>6</sub>:Mn<sup>4+</sup> is commercially available for use in warm-white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LEDs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The manganese oxide is also used in <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">portland cement</a> mixtures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arginase.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="491" data-file-width="800" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arginase.jpeg/290px-Arginase.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arginase.jpeg/435px-Arginase.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arginase.jpeg/580px-Arginase.jpeg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arginase.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reactive center of arginase with boronic acid <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_inhibitor" title="Enzyme inhibitor">inhibitor</a> – the manganese atoms are shown in yellow.</div></div></div>
<p>Manganese is an important element for human health, essential for development, metabolism, and the antioxidant system. Nevertheless, excessive exposure or intake may lead to a condition known as <a href="/wiki/Manganism" title="Manganism">manganism</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Neurodegeneration" title="Neurodegeneration">neurodegenerative</a> disorder that causes dopaminergic neuronal death and symptoms similar to <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The classes of enzymes that have manganese <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a> is large and includes <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transferase" title="Transferase">transferases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrolase" title="Hydrolase">hydrolases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyase" title="Lyase">lyases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isomerase" title="Isomerase">isomerases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ligase" title="Ligase">ligases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lectin" title="Lectin">lectins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Integrin" title="Integrin">integrins</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Reverse_transcriptase" title="Reverse transcriptase">reverse transcriptases</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Retrovirus" title="Retrovirus">retroviruses</a> (though not <a href="/wiki/Lentivirus" title="Lentivirus">lentiviruses</a> such as <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>) contain manganese. The best-known manganese-containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptides" title="Polypeptides">polypeptides</a> may be <a href="/wiki/Arginase" title="Arginase">arginase</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Diphtheria_toxin" title="Diphtheria toxin">diphtheria toxin</a>, and Mn-containing <a href="/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase" title="Superoxide dismutase">superoxide dismutase</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mn-SOD" title="Mn-SOD">Mn-SOD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mnzym_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mnzym-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mn-SOD is the type of SOD present in eukaryotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, and also in most bacteria (this fact is in keeping with the bacterial-origin theory of mitochondria). The Mn-SOD enzyme is probably one of the most ancient, for nearly all organisms living in the presence of oxygen use it to deal with the toxic effects of <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), formed from the 1-electron reduction of dioxygen. The exceptions, which are all bacteria, include <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_plantarum" title="Lactobacillus plantarum">Lactobacillus plantarum</a></i> and related <a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus" title="Lactobacillus">lactobacilli</a>, which use a different nonenzymatic mechanism with manganese (Mn<sup>2+</sup>) ions complexed with polyphosphate, suggesting a path of evolution for this function in aerobic life.
</p><p>The human body contains about 12 mg of manganese, mostly in the bones. The soft tissue remainder is concentrated in the liver and kidneys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001-24">[24]</a></sup> In the human brain, the manganese is bound to manganese <a href="/wiki/Metalloprotein" title="Metalloprotein">metalloproteins</a>, most notably <a href="/wiki/Glutamine_synthetase" title="Glutamine synthetase">glutamine synthetase</a> in <a href="/wiki/Astrocyte" title="Astrocyte">astrocytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganese is also important in photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_evolution" title="Oxygen evolution">oxygen evolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> in plants. The <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-evolving_complex" title="Oxygen-evolving complex">oxygen-evolving complex</a> (OEC) is a part of photosystem II contained in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts; it is responsible for the terminal <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_evolution" title="Oxygen evolution">photooxidation of water</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_reactions" title="Light reactions">light reactions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, and has a metalloenzyme core containing four atoms of manganese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> To fulfill this requirement, most broad-spectrum plant fertilizers contain manganese.
</p>

<p>The U.S. Institute of Medicine (IOM) updated Estimated Average Requirements (EARs) and Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDAs) for minerals in 2001. For manganese there was not sufficient information to set EARs and RDAs, so needs are described as estimates for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adequate_Intake" title="Adequate Intake">Adequate Intakes</a> (AIs). As for safety, the IOM sets <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolerable_upper_intake_level" title="Tolerable upper intake level">Tolerable upper intake levels</a> (ULs) for vitamins and minerals when evidence is sufficient. In the case of manganese the adult UL is set at 11 mg/day. Collectively the EARs, RDAs, AIs and ULs are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Dietary_Reference_Intake" title="Dietary Reference Intake">Dietary Reference Intakes</a> (DRIs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MnDRI_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-MnDRI-61">[61]</a></sup> Manganese deficiency is rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a> (EFSA) refers to the collective set of information as Dietary Reference Values, with Population Reference Intake (PRI) instead of RDA, and Average Requirement instead of EAR. AI and UL defined the same as in United States. For people ages 15 and older the AI is set at 3.0 mg/day. AIs for pregnancy and lactation is 3.0 mg/day. For children ages 1–14 years the AIs increase with age from 0.5 to 2.0 mg/day. The adult AIs are higher than the U.S. RDAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The EFSA reviewed the same safety question and decided that there was insufficient information to set a UL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>For U.S. food and dietary supplement labeling purposes the amount in a serving is expressed as a percent of Daily Value (%DV). For manganese labeling purposes 100% of the Daily Value was 2.0 mg, but as of May 27, 2016 it was revised to 2.3 mg to bring it into agreement with the RDA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FedReg_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FedReg-65">[65]</a></sup> A table of the old and new adult Daily Values is provided at <a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">Reference Daily Intake</a>. Food and supplement companies have until January 1, 2020 to comply with the change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAdelay_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDAdelay-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Manganese compounds are less toxic than those of other widespread metals, such as <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> However, exposure to manganese dusts and fumes should not exceed the ceiling value of 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> even for short periods because of its toxicity level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>  Manganese poisoning has been linked to impaired motor skills and cognitive disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>The permanganate exhibits a higher toxicity than the manganese(II) compounds. The fatal dose is about 10 g, and several fatal intoxications have occurred. The strong oxidative effect leads to <a href="/wiki/Necrosis" title="Necrosis">necrosis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membrane</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> is affected if the permanganate is swallowed. Only a limited amount is absorbed by the intestines, but this small amount shows severe effects on the kidneys and on the liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganese exposure in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osha.gov_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-osha.gov-73">[73]</a></sup> People can be exposed to manganese in the workplace by breathing it in or swallowing it. OSHA has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for manganese exposure in the workplace as 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday and a short term limit of 3 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. At levels of 500 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, manganese is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>Generally, exposure to ambient Mn air concentrations in excess of 5 μg Mn/m3 can lead to Mn-induced symptoms. Increased <a href="/wiki/Ferroportin" title="Ferroportin">ferroportin</a> protein expression in human embryonic kidney (HEK293) cells is associated with decreased intracellular Mn concentration and attenuated <a href="/wiki/Cytotoxicity" title="Cytotoxicity">cytotoxicity</a>, characterized by the reversal of Mn-reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> uptake and diminished <a href="/wiki/Lactate_dehydrogenase" title="Lactate dehydrogenase">lactate dehydrogenase</a> leakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_health_concerns">Environmental health concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental health concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manganese_in_drinking_water">Manganese in drinking water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Manganese in drinking water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waterborne manganese has a greater <a href="/wiki/Bioavailability" title="Bioavailability">bioavailability</a> than dietary manganese. According to results from a 2010 study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouchard_138–143_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard_138–143-76">[76]</a></sup> higher levels of exposure to manganese in <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> are associated with increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intellectual_impairment" title="Intellectual impairment">intellectual impairment</a> and reduced <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_quotient" title="Intelligence quotient">intelligence quotients</a> in school-age children. It is hypothesized that long-term exposure due to inhaling the naturally occurring manganese in shower water puts up to 8.7 million Americans at risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elsner_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-elsner-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> However, data indicates that the human body can recover from certain adverse effects of overexposure to manganese if the exposure is stopped and the body can clear the excess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manganese_in_gasoline">Manganese in gasoline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Manganese in gasoline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:206px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="151" data-file-width="204" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif/lossy-page1-204px-Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif/lossy-page1-306px-Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif/lossy-page1-408px-Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif.jpg 2x" width="204"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molecular model of <a href="/wiki/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl" title="Methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl">methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl</a> (MMT)</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methylcyclopentadienyl_manganese_tricarbonyl" title="Methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl">Methylcyclopentadienyl manganese tricarbonyl</a> (MMT) is a <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> additive used to replace lead compounds for unleaded gasolines to improve the <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane rating</a> of low octane petroleum distillates. It reduces <a href="/wiki/Engine_knocking" title="Engine knocking">engine knock</a> agent through the action of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl groups</a>. Fuels containing manganese tend to form manganese carbides, which damage <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exhaust_valve" title="Exhaust valve">exhaust valves</a>. Compared to 1953, levels of manganese in air have dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Many racing competitions specifically ban manganese compounds in racing fuel for carts and minibikes. MMT contains 24.4–25.2% manganese. Elevated atmospheric manganese concentrations are strongly correlated with automobile traffic density. The Level of manganese emitted by MMT fuels has been found to be safe for the general population and vulnerable groups such as infants and the elderly by the EPA and European and Canadian environmental agencies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manganese_in_tobacco_smoke">Manganese in tobacco smoke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Manganese in tobacco smoke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_plant" title="Tobacco plant">tobacco plant</a> readily absorbs and accumulates <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> such as manganese from the surrounding soil into its leaves. These are subsequently inhaled during <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco smoking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> While  manganese is a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalhoutSchulz2011_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-82">[82]</a></sup> studies have largely concluded that concentrations are not hazardous for human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_neurological_disorders">Role in neurological disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Role in neurological disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manganism">Manganism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Manganism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Manganism" title="Manganism">Manganism</a></div>
<p>Manganese overexposure is most frequently associated with <a href="/wiki/Manganism" title="Manganism">manganism</a>, a rare neurological disorder associated with excessive manganese ingestion or inhalation. Historically, persons employed in the production or processing of manganese alloys<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> have been at risk for developing manganism; however, current health and safety regulations protect workers in developed nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osha.gov_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-osha.gov-73">[73]</a></sup> The disorder was first described in 1837 by British academic John Couper, who studied two patients who were manganese grinders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couper_1837_41–42_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Couper_1837_41–42-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manganism is a biphasic disorder. In its early stages, an intoxicated person may experience depression, mood swings, compulsive behaviors, and psychosis. Early neurological symptoms give way to late-stage manganism, which resembles <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. Symptoms include weakness, monotone and slowed speech, an expressionless face, tremor, forward-leaning gait, inability to walk backwards without falling, rigidity, and general problems with dexterity, gait and balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Couper_1837_41–42_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Couper_1837_41–42-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cersosimo_2007_340–346_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cersosimo_2007_340–346-86">[86]</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>, manganism is not associated with loss of the sense of smell and patients are typically unresponsive to treatment with <a href="/wiki/L-DOPA" title="L-DOPA">L-DOPA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Symptoms of late-stage manganism become more severe over time even if the source of exposure is removed and brain manganese levels return to normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cersosimo_2007_340–346_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cersosimo_2007_340–346-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chronic manganese exposure has been shown to produce a parkinsonism-like illness characterized by movement abnormalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guilarte2015_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guilarte2015-88">[88]</a></sup> This condition is not responsive to <a href="/wiki/Management_of_Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Management of Parkinson's disease">typical therapies used in the treatment of PD</a>, suggesting an alternative pathway than the typical <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopaminergic</a> loss within the <a href="/wiki/Substantia_nigra" title="Substantia nigra">substantia nigra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guilarte2015_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guilarte2015-88">[88]</a></sup> Manganese may accumulate in the <a href="/wiki/Basal_ganglia" title="Basal ganglia">basal ganglia</a>, leading to the abnormal movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwakye2015_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwakye2015-89">[89]</a></sup> A mutation of the SLC30A10 gene, a manganese efflux transporter necessary for decreasing intracellular Mn, has been linked with the development of this Parkinsonism-like disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peres2016_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peres2016-90">[90]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lewy_body" title="Lewy body">Lewy bodies</a> typical to PD are not seen in Mn-induced parkinsonism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwakye2015_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kwakye2015-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>Animal experiments have given the opportunity to examine the consequences of manganese overexposure under controlled conditions. In (non-aggressive) rats, manganese induces mouse-killing behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood_developmental_disorders">Childhood developmental disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Childhood developmental disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several recent studies attempt to examine the effects of chronic low-dose manganese overexposure on <a href="/wiki/Child_development" title="Child development">child development</a>. The earliest study was conducted in the Chinese province of Shanxi. Drinking water there had been contaminated through improper sewage irrigation and contained 240–350 µg Mn/L. Although Mn concentrations at or below 300 µg Mn/L were considered safe at the time of the study by the US EPA and 400 µg Mn/L by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, the 92 children sampled (between 11 and 13 years of age) from this province displayed lower performance on tests of manual dexterity and rapidity, short-term memory, and visual identification, compared to children from an uncontaminated area. More recently, a study of 10-year-old children in Bangladesh showed a relationship between Mn concentration in well water and diminished IQ scores. A third study conducted in Quebec examined school children between the ages of 6 and 15 living in homes that received water from a well containing 610 µg Mn/L; controls lived in homes that received water from a 160 µg Mn/L well. Children in the experimental group showed increased hyperactive and oppositional behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouchard_138–143_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bouchard_138–143-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>The current maximum safe concentration under EPA rules is 50 µg Mn/L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_drinking_water_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_drinking_water-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurodegenerative_diseases">Neurodegenerative diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Neurodegenerative diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A protein called <a href="/wiki/DMT1" title="DMT1">DMT1</a> is the major transporter in manganese absorption from the intestine, and may be the major transporter of manganese across the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>. <a href="/wiki/DMT1" title="DMT1">DMT1</a> also transports inhaled manganese across the nasal epithelium. The proposed mechanism for manganese toxicity is that dysregulation leads to oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, glutamate-mediated excitoxicity, and aggregation of proteins<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p>
