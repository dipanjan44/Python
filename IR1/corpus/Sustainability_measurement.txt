<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sustainability measurement</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_accounting" title="Sustainability accounting">Sustainability accounting</a></div>
<p><b>Sustainability measurement</b> is the quantitative basis for the informed management of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The metrics used for the measurement of sustainability (involving the sustainability of environmental, social and economic domains, both individually and in various combinations) are still evolving: they include <a href="/wiki/Ecological_indicator" title="Ecological indicator">indicators</a>, benchmarks, audits, indexes and accounting,  as well as assessment, appraisal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and other reporting systems. They are applied over a wide range of spatial and temporal scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the best known and most widely used sustainability measures include corporate <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_reporting" title="Sustainability reporting">sustainability reporting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">Triple Bottom Line accounting</a>, and estimates of the quality of sustainability governance for individual countries using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Sustainability_Index" title="Environmental Sustainability Index">Environmental Sustainability Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a>. An alternative approach, used by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Global_Compact" title="United Nations Global Compact">United Nations Global Compact</a> Cities Programme and explicitly critical of the triple-bottom-line approach is <a href="/wiki/Circles_of_Sustainability" title="Circles of Sustainability">Circles of Sustainability</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability_indicators_and_their_function">Sustainability indicators and their function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sustainability indicators and their function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal objective of sustainability indicators is to inform public policy-making as part of the process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainability_governance" title="Sustainability governance">sustainability governance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
Sustainability indicators can provide information on any aspect of the interplay between the environment and socio-economic activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Building strategic indicator sets generally deals with just a few simple questions: what is happening? (descriptive indicators), does it matter and are we reaching targets? (performance indicators), are we improving? (efficiency indicators), are measures working? (policy effectiveness indicators), and are we generally better off? (total welfare indicators). One popular general framework used by The <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> uses a slight modification of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> <a href="/wiki/DPSIR" title="DPSIR">DPSIR</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This breaks up environmental impact into five stages. Social and economic developments (consumption and production) <i>(D)rive</i> or initiate environmental <i>(P)ressures</i> which, in turn, produces a change in the <i>(S)tate</i> of the environment which leads to <i>(I)mpacts</i> of various kinds. Societal <i>(R)esponses</i> (policy guided by sustainability indicators) can be introduced at any stage of this sequence of events.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrics_at_the_global_scale">Metrics at the global scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metrics at the global scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Indicators">United Nations Indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United Nations Indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United Nations has developed extensive sustainability measurement  tools in relation to sustainable development <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as well as a <a href="/wiki/System_of_Integrated_Environmental_and_Economic_Accounting" title="System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting">System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Benchmarks.2C_indicators.2C_indexes.2C_auditing_etc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Benchmarks,_indicators,_indexes,_auditing_etc.">Benchmarks, indicators, indexes, auditing etc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Benchmarks, indicators, indexes, auditing etc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the last couple of decades there has arisen a crowded toolbox of quantitative methods used to assess sustainability — including measures of resource use like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">life cycle assessment</a>, measures of consumption like the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> and measurements of quality of environmental governance like the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a>. The following is a list of quantitative "tools" used by sustainability scientists - the different categories are for convenience only as defining criteria will intergrade. It would be too difficult to list all those methods available at different levels of organisation so those listed here are at for the global level only.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_metrics">Resource metrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Resource metrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Part of this process can relate to resource use such as <a href="/wiki/Energy_accounting" title="Energy accounting">energy accounting</a> or to economic metrics or <a href="/wiki/Price_system" title="Price system">price system</a> values as compared to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-market_economics" title="Non-market economics">non-market economics</a> potential, for understanding resource use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
An important task for resource theory (<a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">energy economics</a>) is to develop methods to optimize resource conversion processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> These systems are described and analyzed by means of the methods of mathematics and the natural sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Human factors, however, have dominated the development of our perspective of the relationship between nature and society since at least the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, and in particular have influenced how we describe and measure the economic impacts of changes in resource quality. A balanced view of these issues requires an understanding of the physical framework in which all human ideas, institutions, and aspirations must operate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Economics.2C_oil_and_energy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics,_oil_and_energy">Economics, oil and energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economics, oil and energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oil_imports.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1469" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oil_imports.PNG/300px-Oil_imports.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oil_imports.PNG/450px-Oil_imports.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oil_imports.PNG/600px-Oil_imports.PNG 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oil_imports.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oil imports by country</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_return_on_energy_investment">Energy return on energy investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy return on energy investment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When oil production first began in the mid-nineteenth century, the largest oil fields recovered fifty barrels of oil for every barrel used in the extraction, transportation and refining. This ratio is often referred to as the Energy Return on Energy Investment (EROI or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EROEI" title="EROEI">EROEI</a>).  Currently, between one and five barrels of oil are recovered for each barrel-equivalent of energy used in the recovery process.  As the EROEI drops to one, or equivalently the <a href="/wiki/Net_energy_gain" title="Net energy gain">Net energy gain</a> falls to zero, the oil production is no longer a net energy source.  This happens long before the resource is physically exhausted.
</p><p>Note that it is important to understand the distinction between a barrel of oil, which is a measure of oil, and a <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrel of oil equivalent</a> (BOE), which is a measure of energy. Many sources of energy, such as fission, solar, wind, and coal, are not subject to the same near-term supply restrictions that oil is. Accordingly, even an oil source with an EROEI of 0.5 can be usefully exploited if the energy required to produce that oil comes from a cheap and plentiful energy source.  Availability of cheap, but hard to transport, natural gas in some oil fields has led to using <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> to fuel <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>. Similarly, natural gas in huge amounts is used to power most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Tar_Sands" title="Athabasca Tar Sands">Athabasca Tar Sands</a> plants. Cheap natural gas has also led to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">Ethanol fuel</a> produced with a net EROEI of less than 1, although figures in this area are controversial because methods to measure EROEI are in debate.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth-based_economic_models">Growth-based economic models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Growth-based economic models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Insofar as <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> is driven by oil consumption growth, post-peak societies must adapt. <a href="/wiki/M._King_Hubbert" title="M. King Hubbert">M. King Hubbert</a> believed:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Our principal constraints are cultural.  During the last two centuries we have known nothing but exponential growth and in parallel we have evolved what amounts to an exponential-growth culture, a culture so heavily dependent upon the continuance of exponential growth for its stability that it is incapable of reckoning with problems of nongrowth.

<p>Some economists describe the problem as <a href="/wiki/Uneconomic_growth" title="Uneconomic growth">uneconomic growth</a> or a <a href="/wiki/False_economy" title="False economy">false economy</a>. At the political right, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_Ikle" title="Fred Ikle">Fred Ikle</a> has warned about "conservatives addicted to the Utopia of Perpetual Growth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  Brief oil interruptions in 1973 and 1979 markedly slowed - but did not stop - the growth of world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1950 and 1984, as the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a> transformed <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> around the globe, world grain production increased by 250%. The energy for the Green Revolution was provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> (natural gas), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> (oil), and <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fueled <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>David Pimentel, professor of ecology and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mario_Giampietro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mario Giampietro (page does not exist)">Mario Giampietro</a>, senior researcher at the National Research Institute on Food and Nutrition (INRAN), place in their study <i>Food, Land, Population and the U.S. Economy</i> the maximum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._population" title="U.S. population">U.S. population</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable economy</a> at 200 million. To achieve a sustainable economy <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> will have to be reduced by two-thirds, says the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Without population reduction, this study predicts an agricultural crisis beginning in 2020, becoming critical c. 2050. The <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peaking of global oil</a> along with the decline in regional <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> production may precipitate this agricultural crisis sooner than generally expected. <a href="/wiki/Dale_Allen_Pfeiffer" title="Dale Allen Pfeiffer">Dale Allen Pfeiffer</a> claims that coming decades could see spiraling <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> prices without relief and massive <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> on a global level such as never experienced before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hubbert_peaks">Hubbert peaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hubbert peaks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Hubbert_peak_theory" title="Hubbert peak theory">Hubbert peak theory</a> receives most attention in relation to <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil production</a>, it has also been applied to other natural resources.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Peak_gas" title="Peak gas">Peak gas</a></div>
<p>Doug Reynolds predicted in 2005 that the North American peak would occur in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  Bentley (p. 189) predicted a world "decline in conventional gas production from about 2020".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Peak_coal" title="Peak coal">Peak coal</a></div>
<p>Peak coal is significantly further out than peak oil, but we can observe the example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthracite_coal" title="Anthracite coal">anthracite</a> in the USA, a high grade coal whose production peaked in the 1920s.  Anthracite was studied by Hubbert, and matches a curve closely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>  Pennsylvania's coal production also matches Hubbert's curve closely, but this does not mean that coal in Pennsylvania is exhausted—far from it.  If production in Pennsylvania returned at its all-time high, there are reserves for 190 years.  Hubbert had recoverable <a href="/wiki/Coal#World_coal_reserves" title="Coal">coal reserves worldwide</a> at 2500 × 10<sup>9</sup> metric tons and peaking around 2150(depending on usage).
</p><p>More recent estimates suggest an earlier peak. <i>Coal: Resources and Future Production</i> (PDF 630KB <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>), published on April 5, 2007 by the Energy Watch Group (EWG), which reports to the German Parliament, found that global coal production could peak in as few as 15 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Httpwwwenergybulletinnethtml_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Httpwwwenergybulletinnethtml-35">[35]</a></sup>  Reporting on this Richard Heinberg also notes that the date of peak annual energetic extraction from coal will likely come earlier than the date of peak in quantity of coal (tons  per year) extracted as the most energy-dense types of coal have been mined most extensively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  A second study,
<i>The Future of Coal</i> by B. Kavalov and S. D. Peteves of the Institute for Energy (IFE), prepared for European Commission Joint Research Centre, reaches similar conclusions and states that
""coal might not be so abundant, widely available and reliable as an energy source in the future".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Httpwwwenergybulletinnethtml_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Httpwwwenergybulletinnethtml-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Work by <a href="/wiki/David_Rutledge_(engineer)" title="David Rutledge (engineer)">David Rutledge</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> predicts that the total of world coal production will amount to only about 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigatonne" title="Gigatonne">gigatonnes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NS-37">[37]</a></sup> This
implies that coal is running out faster than usually assumed.
</p><p>Finally, insofar as global <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> and peak in natural gas are expected anywhere from imminently to within decades at most, any increase in coal production (mining) per annum to compensate for declines in oil or NG production, would necessarily translate to an earlier date of peak as compared with peak coal under a scenario in which annual production remains constant.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metals">Metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The price of copper rose 500% between 2003 and 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> was by some attributed to <a href="/wiki/Peak_copper" title="Peak copper">peak copper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard2006_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard2006-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Copper prices later fell, along with many other commodities and stock prices, as demand shrank from fear of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_2000s_recession" title="Late 2000s recession">global recession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">Lithium</a> availability is a concern for a fleet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li-ion_battery" title="Li-ion battery">Li-ion battery</a> using cars but a paper published in 1996 estimated that world reserves are adequate for at least 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  A similar prediction <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> for <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> use in fuel cells notes that the metal could be easily recycled.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phosphorus">Phosphorus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Phosphorus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">Phosphorus</a> supplies are essential to farming and depletion of reserves is estimated at somewhere from 60 to 130 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  Individual countries supplies vary widely; without a recycling initiative America's supply <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> is estimated around 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  Phosphorus supplies affect total agricultural output which in turn limits alternative fuels such as biodiesel and ethanol.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peak_water">Peak water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Peak water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Peak_water" title="Peak water">Peak water</a></div>
<p>Hubbert's original analysis did not apply to renewable resources.  However <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over-exploitation" title="Over-exploitation">over-exploitation</a> often results in a Hubbert peak nonetheless.  A modified Hubbert curve applies to any resource that can be harvested faster than it can be replaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, a reserve such as the <a href="/wiki/Ogallala_Aquifer" title="Ogallala Aquifer">Ogallala Aquifer</a> can be mined at a rate that far exceeds replenishment.  This turns much of the world's underground water <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and lakes <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> into finite resources with peak usage debates similar to oil.  These debates usually center around agriculture and suburban water usage but generation of electricity <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> from nuclear energy or coal and tar sands mining mentioned above is also water resource intensive.  The term <a href="/wiki/Fossil_water" title="Fossil water">fossil water</a> is sometimes used to describe aquifers whose water is not being recharged.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_resources">Renewable resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Renewable resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Fisheries</b>: At least one researcher has attempted to perform Hubbert linearization (<a href="/wiki/Hubbert_curve" title="Hubbert curve">Hubbert curve</a>) on the <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">whaling</a> industry, as well as charting the transparently dependent price of caviar on sturgeon depletion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> of the North Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The comparison of the cases of fisheries and of mineral extraction tells us that the human pressure on the environment is causing a wide range of resources to go through a depletion cycle which follows a Hubbert curve.</li></ul>
