<p><b>Vanadium(IV) oxide</b> or <b>vanadium dioxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula VO<sub>2</sub>. It is a dark blue solid. <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">Vanadium</a>(IV) dioxide is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, dissolving in non-oxidising acids to give the blue <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_ion" title="Vanadyl ion">vanadyl ion</a>, [VO]<sup>2+</sup> and in alkali to give the brown [V<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> ion, or at high pH [VO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> VO<sub>2</sub> has a phase transition very close to room temperature (~66 °C). Electrical resistivity, opacity, etc, can change up several orders. Owing to these properties, it has been used in surface coating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> sensors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and imaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Potential applications include use in memory devices, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> phase-change switches, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> aerospace communication systems and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuromorphic_computing" title="Neuromorphic computing">neuromorphic computing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VO2_structure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2562" data-file-width="2000" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/VO2_structure.png/220px-VO2_structure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/VO2_structure.png/330px-VO2_structure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/VO2_structure.png/440px-VO2_structure.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VO2_structure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> structure. Vanadium atoms are purple and oxygen atoms are pink. The V–V dimers are highlighted by violet lines in (a). The distances between adjacent vanadium atoms are equal in (b).</div></div></div>
<p>At temperatures below T<sub>c</sub> = 340 K (67 °C), <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> P2<sub>1</sub>/c) crystal structure. Above T<sub>c</sub>, the structure is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. In the monoclinic phase, the V<sup>4+</sup> ions form pairs along the c axis, leading to alternate short and long V-V distances of 2.65 Å and 3.12 Å. In comparison, in the rutile phase the V<sup>4+</sup> ions are separated by a fixed distance of 2.96 Å. As a result, the number of V<sup>4+</sup> ions in the crystallographic unit cell doubles from the rutile to the monoclinic phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morin1959_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morin1959-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The equilibrium morphology of rutile <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> particles is acicular, laterally confined by (110) surfaces, which are the most stable termination planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MellanGrau-Crespo2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MellanGrau-Crespo2012-12">[12]</a></sup> The surface tends to be oxidized with respect to the stoichiometric composition, with the oxygen adsorbed on the (110) surface forming vanadyl species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MellanGrau-Crespo2012_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-MellanGrau-Crespo2012-12">[12]</a></sup> The presence of V<sup>5+</sup> ions at the surface of <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> films has been confirmed by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_photoelectron_spectroscopy" title="X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy">X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ManningParkin2004_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ManningParkin2004-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic">Electronic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electronic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the rutile to monoclinic transition temperature (67 °C), <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> also exhibits a metal to <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> transition in its electronic structure: the rutile phase is metallic while the monoclinic phase is semiconducting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_band_gap" title="Optical band gap">optical band gap</a> of VO<sub>2</sub> in the low-temperature monoclinic phase is about 0.7 eV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal">Thermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metallic VO<sub>2</sub> contradicts the <a href="/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law" title="Wiedemann–Franz law">Wiedemann-Franz Law</a> that holds that the ratio of the electronic contribution of the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> (<i>κ</i>) to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> (<i>σ</i>) of a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. The thermal conductivity that could be attributed to electron movement was 10% of the amount predicted by the Wiedemann-Franz Law. The reason for this appears to be the fluidic way that the electrons move through the material, reducing the typical random electron motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Potential applications include converting waste heat from engines and appliances into electricity, or window coverings that keep buildings cool.  Thermal conductivity varied when VO<sub>2</sub> was mixed with other materials. At a low temperature it could act as an insulator, while conducting heat at a higher temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_structure">Synthesis and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Synthesis and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nanostars-it1302.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1020" data-file-width="1242" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/220px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/330px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/440px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nanostars-it1302.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nanostars of vanadium(IV) oxide</div></div></div>
<p>Following the method described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berzelius" title="Berzelius">Berzelius</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is prepared by <a href="/wiki/Comproportionation" title="Comproportionation">comproportionation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(III)_oxide" title="Vanadium(III) oxide">vanadium(III) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium(V) oxide</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">V<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>  +  <span class="chemf nowrap">V<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>   →    4 <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>At room temperature VO<sub>2</sub> has a distorted <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">rutile</a> structure with shorter distances between pairs of V atoms indicating metal-metal bonding. Above 68 °C the structure changes to an undistorted rutile structure and the metal-metal bonds are broken causing an increase in electrical conductivity and magnetic susceptibility as the bonding electrons are "released".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> The origin of this insulator to metal transition remains controversial and is of interest both for <a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">condensed matter physics</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and practical applications, such as electrical switches, tunable electrical filters, power limiters, nano-oscillators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memristors" title="Memristors">memristors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field-effect_transistors" title="Field-effect transistors">field-effect transistors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamaterials" title="Metamaterials">metamaterials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_reflectance">Infrared reflectance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrared reflectance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="713" data-file-width="952" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png/220px-VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png/330px-VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png/440px-VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VO2-SiO2_transmittance.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Transmittance spectra of a <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>/<span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> film. Mild heating results in significant absorption of infrared light</div></div></div>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> expresses temperature-dependent reflective properties. When heated from room temperature to 80 °C, the material's thermal radiation rises normally until 74 °C, before suddenly appearing to drop to around 20 °C. At room temperature <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is almost transparent to infrared light. As its temperature rises it gradually changes to reflective. At intermediate temperatures it behaves as a highly absorbing dielectric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phy1310_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>A thin film of vanadium oxide on a highly reflecting substrate (for specific infrared wavelengths) such as sapphire is either absorbing or reflecting, dependent on temperature. Its emissivity varies considerably with temperature. When the vanadium oxide transitions with increased temperature, the structure undergoes a sudden decrease in emissivity – looking colder to infrared cameras than it really is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ir-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phy1310_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Varying the substrate materials e.g., to indium tin oxide, and modifying the vanadium oxide coating using doping, straining and other processes, alter the wavelengths and temperature ranges at which the thermal effects are observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ir-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phy1310_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nanoscale structures that appear naturally in the materials' transition region can suppress thermal radiation as the temperature rises. Doping the coating with <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> lowers the effect's thermal range to room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phy1310_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_radiation_management">Infrared radiation management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Infrared radiation management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Undoped and tungsten-doped vanadium dioxide films can act as "spectrally-selective" coatings to block <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> transmission and reduce the loss of building interior heat through windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-ir-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Varying the amount of tungsten allows regulating the phase transition temperature at a rate of 20 °C per 1 atomic percent of tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-ir-24">[24]</a></sup> The coating has a slight yellow-green color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other potential applications of its thermal properties include passive camouflage, thermal beacons, communication, or to deliberately speed up or slow down cooling (which could be useful in a variety of structures from homes to satellites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phy1310_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-22">[22]</a></sup>).
</p><p>Vanadium dioxide can act as extremely fast <a href="/wiki/Optical_modulator" title="Optical modulator">optical modulators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> modulators for <a href="/wiki/Missile_guidance" title="Missile guidance">missile guidance</a> systems, cameras, data storage, and other applications. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermochromic" title="Thermochromic">thermochromic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a> between the transparent semiconductive and reflective conductive phase, occurring at 68 °C, can happen in times as short as 100 femtoseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_change_computing_and_memory">Phase change computing and memory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phase change computing and memory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The insulator-metal phase transition in VO<sub>2</sub> can be manipulated at the nanoscale using a biased conducting atomic force microscope tip,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> suggesting applications in computing and information storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup>
