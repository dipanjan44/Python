<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Volcanism</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the process that forms volcanoes and other igneous formations. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plate_tectonics_map.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2285" data-file-width="3380" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Plate_tectonics_map.gif/310px-Plate_tectonics_map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Plate_tectonics_map.gif/465px-Plate_tectonics_map.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Plate_tectonics_map.gif/620px-Plate_tectonics_map.gif 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plate_tectonics_map.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a> map with volcano locations indicated with red circles</div></div></div>
<p><b>Volcanism</b> is the phenomenon of eruption of molten rock (<a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a>) onto the <a href="/wiki/Earth#Surface" title="Earth">surface of the Earth</a> or a solid-surface planet or moon, where <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroclastic" title="Pyroclastic">pyroclastics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_gas" title="Volcanic gas">volcanic gases</a> erupt through a break in the surface called a vent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It includes all phenomena resulting from and causing magma within the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> of the body, to rise through the crust and form <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rocks</a> on the surface.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_processes">Volcanic processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Volcanic processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A204,_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park,_USA,_new_lava_flow,_2007.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="500" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG/220px-A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG/330px-A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG/440px-A204%2C_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park%2C_USA%2C_new_lava_flow%2C_2007.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A204,_Hawaii_Volcanoes_National_Park,_USA,_new_lava_flow,_2007.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Non-viscous lava during an effusive eruption of <a href="/wiki/K%C4%ABlauea" title="Kīlauea">Kīlauea</a></div></div></div>
<p>Magma from the mantle or lower crust rises through its crust towards the surface. If magma reaches the surface, its behavior depends on the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molten" title="Molten">molten</a> constituent <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>. Viscous (thick) magma produces volcanoes characterised by <a href="/wiki/Explosive_eruption" title="Explosive eruption">explosive eruptions</a>, while non-viscous (runny) magma produce volcanoes characterised by <a href="/wiki/Effusive_eruption" title="Effusive eruption">effusive eruptions</a> pouring large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> onto the surface.
</p><p>In some cases, rising magma can cool and solidify without reaching the surface. Instead, the cooled and solidified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> mass crystallises within the crust to form an igneous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrusion" title="Intrusion">intrusion</a>. As magma cools the chemicals in the crystals formed are effectively removed from the main mix of the magma (by a process known as <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(geology)" title="Fractional crystallization (geology)">fractional crystallization</a>), so the chemical content of the remaining magma evolves as it solidifies slowly. Fresh unevolved magma injections can remobilise more evolved magmas, allowing eruptions from more viscous magmas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Driving_forces_of_volcanism">Driving forces of volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Driving forces of volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="429" data-file-width="775" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png/310px-Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png/465px-Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png/620px-Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tectonic_plate_boundaries.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three types of plate boundary.</div></div></div>
<p>Movement of molten rock in the mantle, caused by thermal <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> currents, coupled with <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">gravitational effects</a> of changes on the earth's surface (<a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposition</a>, even <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> impact and patterns of <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a>) drive <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonic motion</a> and ultimately volcanism.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects_of_volcanism">Aspects of volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aspects of volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Volcanoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">Volcano</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volcano_scheme.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1149" data-file-width="1099" height="324" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Volcano_scheme.svg/310px-Volcano_scheme.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Volcano_scheme.svg/465px-Volcano_scheme.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Volcano_scheme.svg/620px-Volcano_scheme.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volcano_scheme.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Cross-section through a <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a> (vertical scale is exaggerated)</b>: <div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;"><div><ol><li>Large magma chamber</li><li>Bedrock</li><li>Conduit (pipe)</li><li>Base</li><li>Sill</li><li>Dike</li><li>Layers of ash emitted by the volcano</li><li>Flank</li><li>Layers of lava emitted by the volcano</li><li>Throat</li><li>Parasitic cone</li><li>Lava flow</li><li>Vent</li><li>Crater</li><li>Ash cloud</li></ol></div></div></div></div></div>
<p>Volcanoes are places where magma reaches the earth's surface.
The type of volcano depends on the location of the eruption and the consistency of the magma.
</p>

<p>These are formed where magma pushes between existing rock, intrusions can be in the form of <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dike_(geology)" title="Dike (geology)">dikes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Layered_intrusion" title="Layered intrusion">layered intrusions</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volcano_tectonic_earthquake" title="Volcano tectonic earthquake">Volcano tectonic earthquake</a></div>
<p>Earthquakes are generally associated with plate tectonic activity, but some earthquakes are generated as a result of volcanic activity <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>(though that itself is ultimately driven by the same forces).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrothermal_vents">Hydrothermal vents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hydrothermal vents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">Hydrothermal vents</a></div>
<p>These are formed where water interacts with volcanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geothermal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-geothermal-3">[3]</a></sup>
These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geysers" title="Geysers">geysers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumaroles" title="Fumaroles">fumaroles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotsprings" title="Hotsprings">hotsprings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudpot" title="Mudpot">mudpots</a>, they are often used as a source of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geothermal_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-geothermal-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_winter">Volcanic winter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Volcanic winter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">Volcanic winter</a></div>
<p>The amount of gas and ash emitted by volcanic eruptions has a significant effect on the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. Large eruptions correlate well with some significant <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Volcanism" title="Climate change">climate change</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forming_rocks">Forming rocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Forming rocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">Igneous rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">Metamorphic rock</a></div>
<p>When magma cools it solidifies and forms rocks. The type of rock formed depends on the chemical composition of the magma and how rapidly it cools. Magma that reaches the surface to become lava cools rapidly, resulting in rocks with small crystals such as <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>. Some of this magma may cool extremely rapidly and will form <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_glass" title="Volcanic glass">volcanic glass</a> (rocks without crystals) such as <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>. 
Magma trapped below ground in thin intrusions cools more slowly than exposed magma and produces rocks with medium-sized crystals. Magma that remains trapped in large quantities below ground cools most slowly resulting in rocks with larger crystals, such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a>.
</p><p>Existing rocks that come into contact with magma may be melted and assimilated into the magma. Other rocks adjacent to the magma may be altered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_metamorphism" title="Contact metamorphism">contact metamorphism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">metasomatism</a> as they are affected by the heat and escaping or externally-circulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal" title="Hydrothermal">hydrothermal</a> fluids.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanism_on_other_bodies">Volcanism on other bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volcanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Volcanism on other bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Io" title="Volcanology of Io">Volcanology of Io</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Venus" title="Volcanology of Venus">Volcanology of Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Mars" title="Volcanology of Mars">Volcanology of Mars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta#Geology" title="4 Vesta">Geology of Vesta</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_solar_terrestrial_planets" title="Geology of solar terrestrial planets">Geology of solar terrestrial planets</a></div>
<p>Volcanism is not confined only to Earth, but is thought to be found on any body having a solid crust and fluid mantle. Evidence of volcanism should still be found on any body that has had volcanism at some point in its history. Volcanoes have indeed been clearly observed on other bodies in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> – on some, such as <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, in the shape of mountains that are unmistakably old volcanoes (most notably <a href="/wiki/Olympus_Mons" title="Olympus Mons">Olympus Mons</a>), but on <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Io" title="Volcanology of Io">Io</a> actual ongoing eruptions have been observed. It can be surmised that volcanism exists on planets and moons of this type in other <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a> as well. In 2014, scientists found 70 lava flows which formed on the Moon in the last 100 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
