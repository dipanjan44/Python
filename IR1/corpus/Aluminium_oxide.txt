<p><b>Aluminium oxide</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> name) or <b>aluminum oxide</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Al</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub>.  It is the most commonly occurring of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide_(compounds)" title="Aluminium oxide (compounds)">aluminium oxides</a>, and specifically identified as <b>aluminium(III) oxide</b>.  It is commonly called <b>alumina</b> (regardless of whether the element is spelled aluminum or aluminium), and may also be called <b>aloxide</b>, <b>aloxite</b>, or <b>alundum</b> depending on particular forms or applications.  It occurs naturally in its crystalline <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> α-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> as the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>, varieties of which form the precious <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">ruby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a>.  Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is significant in its use to produce aluminium metal, as an <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a> owing to its <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a>, and as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> material owing to its high melting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-azom-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">Corundum</a> is the most common naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystalline</a> form of aluminium oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">Rubies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphires</a> are gem-quality forms of corundum, which owe their characteristic colors to trace impurities. Rubies are given their characteristic deep red color and their <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> qualities by traces of <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>. Sapphires come in different colors given by various other impurities, such as iron and titanium.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG"><img alt="Aluminium oxide in its powdered form." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="235" data-file-width="605" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG/220px-Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG/330px-Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG/440px-Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oxid_hlinit%C3%BD.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aluminium oxide in its powdered form.</div></div></div>
<p>Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is an <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">electrical insulator</a> but has a relatively high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> (<span class="nowrap">30 Wm<sup>−1</sup>K<sup>−1</sup></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-properties_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-properties-2">[2]</a></sup> for a ceramic material. Aluminium oxide is insoluble in water. In its most commonly occurring crystalline form, called <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a> or α-aluminium oxide, its hardness makes it suitable for use as an <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a> and as a component in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutting_tools" title="Cutting tools">cutting tools</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-azom-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aluminium oxide is responsible for the resistance of metallic aluminium to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. Metallic aluminium is very reactive with atmospheric oxygen, and a thin <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation layer</a> of aluminium oxide (4 nm thickness) forms on any exposed aluminium surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This layer protects the metal from further oxidation. The thickness and properties of this oxide layer can be enhanced using a process called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodising" title="Anodising">anodising</a>. A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">alloys</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_bronze" title="Aluminium bronze">aluminium bronzes</a>, exploit this property by including a proportion of aluminium in the alloy to enhance corrosion resistance. The aluminium oxide generated by anodising is typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a>, but discharge assisted oxidation processes such as <a href="/wiki/Plasma_electrolytic_oxidation" title="Plasma electrolytic oxidation">plasma electrolytic oxidation</a> result in a significant proportion of crystalline aluminium oxide in the coating, enhancing its <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a>.
</p><p>Aluminium oxide was taken off the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s chemicals lists in 1988. Aluminium oxide is on the EPA's <a href="/wiki/Toxics_Release_Inventory" title="Toxics Release Inventory">Toxics Release Inventory</a> list if it is a fibrous form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRI_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRI-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphoteric_nature">Amphoteric nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amphoteric nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aluminium oxide is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> substance, meaning it can react with both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acids" title="Acids">acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, acting as an acid with a base and a base with an acid, neutralising the other and producing a salt.
</p>

<dd>Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 2 NaOH + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 NaAl(OH)<sub>4</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminate" title="Sodium aluminate">sodium aluminate</a>)</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Corindon_azulEZ.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="420" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Corindon_azulEZ.jpg/180px-Corindon_azulEZ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Corindon_azulEZ.jpg/270px-Corindon_azulEZ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Corindon_azulEZ.jpg/360px-Corindon_azulEZ.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Corindon_azulEZ.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Corundum from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, size about 2×3 cm.</div></div></div>
<p>The most common form of crystalline aluminium oxide is known as <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>, which is the thermodynamically stable form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levin-11">[11]</a></sup> The oxygen ions form a nearly hexagonal close-packed structure with the aluminium ions filling two-thirds of the octahedral interstices. Each Al<sup>3+</sup> center is <a href="/wiki/Octahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Octahedral molecular geometry">octahedral</a>. In terms of its <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a>, corundum adopts a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal" title="Trigonal">trigonal</a> <a href="/wiki/Bravais_lattice" title="Bravais lattice">Bravais lattice</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> of R-3c (number 167 in the International Tables). The <a href="/wiki/Primitive_cell" title="Primitive cell">primitive cell</a> contains two formula units of aluminium oxide.
</p><p>Aluminium oxide also exists in other phases, including the cubic γ and η phases, the monoclinic θ phase, the hexagonal χ phase, the orthorhombic κ phase and the δ phase that can be tetragonal or orthorhombic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levin-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paglia_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paglia-12">[12]</a></sup> Each has a unique crystal structure and properties.  Cubic γ-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has important technical applications. The so-called β-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> proved to be NaAl<sub>11</sub>O<sub>17</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molten aluminium oxide near the melting temperature is roughly 2/3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral" title="Tetrahedral">tetrahedral</a> (i.e. 2/3 of the Al are surrounded by 4 oxygen neighbors), and 1/3 5-coordinated, very little (&lt;5%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral" title="Octahedral">octahedral</a> Al-O is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skinner2013_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skinner2013-14">[14]</a></sup> Around 80% of the oxygen atoms are shared among three or more Al-O polyhedra, and the majority of inter-polyhedral connections are corner-sharing, with the remaining 10–20% being edge-sharing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skinner2013_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Skinner2013-14">[14]</a></sup> The breakdown of octahedra upon melting is accompanied by a relatively large volume increase (~20%), the density of the liquid close to its melting point is 2.93 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aluminium <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> minerals are the main component of <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>, the principal <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>. A mixture of the minerals comprise bauxite ore, including <a href="/wiki/Gibbsite" title="Gibbsite">gibbsite</a> (Al(OH)<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Boehmite" title="Boehmite">boehmite</a> (γ-AlO(OH)), and <a href="/wiki/Diaspore" title="Diaspore">diaspore</a> (α-AlO(OH)), along with impurities of <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxides</a> and hydroxides, quartz and <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Bauxites are found in <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterites</a>. Bauxite is purified by the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + NaOH → NaAl(OH)<sub>4</sub></dd>
<dd>Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> + NaOH → NaAl(OH)<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Except for SiO<sub>2</sub>, the other components of bauxite do not dissolve in base. Upon filtering the basic mixture, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is removed. When the Bayer liquor is cooled, Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> precipitates, leaving the silicates in solution.
</p>
<dl><dd>NaAl(OH)<sub>4</sub> → NaOH + Al(OH)<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The solid  Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> <a href="/wiki/Gibbsite" title="Gibbsite">Gibbsite</a> is then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcined" title="Calcined">calcined</a> (heated to over 1100 °C) to give aluminium oxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-azom-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Al(OH)<sub>3</sub> → Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The product aluminium oxide tends to be multi-phase, i.e., consisting of several phases of aluminium oxide rather than solely <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paglia_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paglia-12">[12]</a></sup> The production process can therefore be optimized to produce a tailored product. The type of phases present affects, for example, the solubility and pore structure of the aluminium oxide product which, in turn, affects the cost of aluminium production and pollution control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paglia_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Paglia-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>For its application as an electrical <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">insulator</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a>, where the conformal growth of a thin film is a prerequisite and the preferred growth mode is <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>, Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films can be prepared by the chemical exchange between trimethylaluminum Al(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Al(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O → Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 CH<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>H<sub>2</sub>O in the above reaction can be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (O<sub>3</sub>) as the active oxidant and the following reaction then takes place:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Al(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + O<sub>3</sub> → Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films prepared using O<sub>3</sub> show 10–100 times lower leakage current density compared with those prepared by H<sub>2</sub>O.
</p><p>Known as alundum (in fused form) or aloxite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CI14835_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CI14835-20">[20]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> communities, aluminium oxide finds wide use. Annual world production of aluminium oxide in 2015 was approximately 115 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>, over 90% of which is used in the manufacture of aluminium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-azom-7">[7]</a></sup> The major uses of speciality aluminium oxides are in refractories, ceramics, polishing and abrasive applications. Large tonnages of aluminium hydroxide, from which alumina is derived, are used in the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolites" title="Zeolites">zeolites</a>, coating <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a> pigments, and as a fire retardant/smoke suppressant.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aluminium_oxide&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(August 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Over 90% of the aluminium oxide, normally termed Smelter Grade Alumina (SGA), produced is consumed for the production of aluminium, usually by the <a href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall–Héroult process">Hall–Héroult process</a>. The remainder, normally called speciality alumina is used in a wide variety of applications which reflect its inertness, temperature resistance and electrical resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Filler:</li></ul>
<p>Being fairly chemically inert and white, aluminium oxide is a favored filler for plastics. Aluminium oxide is a common ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">sunscreen</a> and is sometimes also present in cosmetics such as blush, lipstick, and nail polish.
</p>
<ul><li>Glass:</li></ul>
<p>Many formulations of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> have aluminium oxide as an ingredient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Catalysis:</li></ul>
<p>Aluminium oxide catalyses a variety of reactions that are useful industrially. In its largest scale application, aluminium oxide is the catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Claus_process" title="Claus process">Claus process</a> for converting hydrogen sulfide waste gases into elemental sulfur in refineries. It is also useful for dehydration of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> to alkenes.
</p><p>Aluminium oxide serves as a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst_support" title="Catalyst support">catalyst support</a> for many industrial catalysts, such as those used in <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">hydrodesulfurization</a> and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta" title="Ziegler-Natta">Ziegler-Natta</a> polymerizations.
</p>
<ul><li>Purification:</li></ul>
<p>Aluminium oxide is widely used to remove water from gas streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Abrasive:</li></ul>
<p>Aluminium oxide is used for its hardness and strength. It is widely used as an <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a>, including as a much less expensive substitute for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_diamond" title="Industrial diamond">industrial diamond</a>. Many types of <a href="/wiki/Sandpaper" title="Sandpaper">sandpaper</a> use aluminium oxide crystals. In addition, its low heat retention and low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat" title="Specific heat">specific heat</a> make it widely used in grinding operations, particularly <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">cutoff</a> tools. As the powdery abrasive mineral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aloxite" title="Aloxite">aloxite</a>, it is a major component, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">cue tip</a> "chalk" used in <a href="/wiki/Cue_sports" title="Cue sports">billiards</a>. Aluminium oxide powder is used in some <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a>/<a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> <a href="/wiki/Polishing" title="Polishing">polishing</a> and scratch-repair kits. Its polishing qualities are also behind its use in toothpaste.
</p>
<ul><li>Paint:</li></ul>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alumina_effect_pigment" title="Alumina effect pigment">Alumina effect pigment</a></div>
<p>Aluminium oxide flakes are used in paint for reflective decorative effects, such as in the automotive or cosmetic industries.
</p>
<ul><li>Composite fiber:</li></ul>
<p>Aluminium oxide has been used in a few experimental and commercial fiber materials for high-performance applications (e.g., Fiber FP, Nextel 610, Nextel 720).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mallick_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mallick-24">[24]</a></sup>  Alumina <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanofibers" title="Nanofibers">nanofibers</a> in particular have become a research field of interest.
</p>
<ul><li>Personal armor:</li></ul>
<p>Some body armors utilize alumina ceramic plates, usually in combination with aramid or UHMWPE backing to achieve effectiveness against even most rifle threats. Alumina ceramic armor is readily available to most civilians in jurisdictions where it is legal, but is not considered military grade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Abrasion protection:</li></ul>
<p>Aluminium oxide can be grown as a coating on aluminium by <a href="/wiki/Anodizing" title="Anodizing">anodizing</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Plasma_electrolytic_oxidation" title="Plasma electrolytic oxidation">plasma electrolytic oxidation</a> (see the "Properties" above). Both the <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a> and abrasion-resistant characteristics of the coating originate from the high strength of aluminium oxide, yet the porous coating layer produced with conventional direct current anodizing procedures is within a 60-70 Rockwell hardness C range <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which is comparable only to hardened carbon steel alloys, but considerably inferior to the hardness of natural and synthetic corundum. Instead, with <a href="/wiki/Plasma_electrolytic_oxidation" title="Plasma electrolytic oxidation">plasma electrolytic oxidation</a>, the coating is porous only on the surface oxide layer while the lower oxide layers are much more compact than with standard DC anodizing procedures and present a higher crystallinity due to the oxide layers being remelted and densified to obtain α-Al2O3 clusters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> with much higher coating hardness values circa 2000 Vickers hardness.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2005alumina.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/2005alumina.PNG/220px-2005alumina.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/2005alumina.PNG/330px-2005alumina.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/2005alumina.PNG/440px-2005alumina.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2005alumina.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aluminium oxide output in 2005</div></div></div><p>Alumina is used to manufacture tiles which are attached inside pulverized fuel lines and flue gas ducting on coal fired power stations to protect high wear areas. They are not suitable for areas with high impact forces as these tiles are brittle and susceptible to breakage.
</p><ul><li>Other:</li></ul>
<p>In lighting, transparent aluminium oxide is used in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_lamp" title="Sodium vapor lamp">sodium vapor lamps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Aluminium oxide is also used in preparation of coating suspensions in <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent_lamp" title="Compact fluorescent lamp">compact fluorescent lamps</a>.
</p><p>In chemistry laboratories, aluminium oxide is a medium for <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a>, available in <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> (pH 9.5), <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> (pH 4.5 when in water) and neutral formulations.
</p><p>Health and medical applications include it as a material in <a href="/wiki/Hip_replacement" title="Hip replacement">hip replacements</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-azom-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Oral contraceptive pill">birth control pills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Dosimeter" title="Dosimeter">dosimeter</a> for radiation protection and therapy applications for its <a href="/wiki/Optically_stimulated_luminescence" title="Optically stimulated luminescence">optically stimulated luminescence</a> properties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Aluminium oxide is an electrical <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">insulator</a> used as a substrate (<a href="/wiki/Silicon_on_sapphire" title="Silicon on sapphire">silicon on sapphire</a>) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a> but also as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_tunneling" title="Quantum tunneling">tunnel barrier</a> for the fabrication of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconducting" title="Superconducting">superconducting</a> devices such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_electron_transistor" title="Single electron transistor">single electron transistors</a> and superconducting quantum interference devices (<a href="/wiki/SQUID" title="SQUID">SQUIDs</a>).
</p><p>Aluminum oxide being a dielectric with relatively large band gap is used as an insulating barrier in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capacitors" title="Capacitors">capacitors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Insulation for high-temperature furnaces is often manufactured from aluminium oxide.  Sometimes the insulation has varying percentages of silica depending on the temperature rating of the material.  The insulation can be made in blanket, board, brick and loose fiber forms for various application requirements.
</p><p>Small pieces of aluminium oxide are often used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_chips" title="Boiling chips">boiling chips</a> in chemistry.
</p><p>It is also used to make <a href="/wiki/Spark_plug" title="Spark plug">spark plug</a> <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">insulators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_spraying" title="Plasma spraying">plasma spray</a> process and mixed with <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a>, it is coated onto the braking surface of some <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a> rims to provide abrasion and wear resistance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Most ceramic eyes on fishing rods are circular rings made from aluminium oxide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
