<p><b>Protactinium</b> (formerly <b>protoactinium</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Pa</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 91. It is a dense, silvery-gray <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> metal which readily reacts with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, water vapor and inorganic <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>. It forms various <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> in which protactinium is usually present in the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> +5, but it can also assume +4 and even +3 or +2 states. Concentrations of protactinium in the Earth's crust are typically a few parts per trillion, but may reach up to a few parts per million in some <a href="/wiki/Uraninite" title="Uraninite">uraninite</a> ore deposits. Because of its scarcity, high radioactivity and high toxicity, there are currently no uses for protactinium outside scientific research, and for this purpose, protactinium is mostly extracted from spent <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>.
</p><p>Protactinium was first identified in 1913 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasimir_Fajans" title="Kasimir Fajans">Kasimir Fajans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Helmuth_G%C3%B6hring" title="Oswald Helmuth Göhring">Oswald Helmuth Göhring</a> and named <i>brevium</i> because of the short <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of the specific <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> studied, i.e. protactinium-234. A more stable isotope of protactinium, <sup>231</sup>Pa, was discovered in 1917/18 by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a>, and they chose the name proto-actinium, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> finally named it "protactinium" in 1949 and confirmed Hahn and Meitner as discoverers. The new name meant "(nuclear) <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a>" and reflected that actinium is a product of radioactive decay of protactinium. <a href="/wiki/John_Arnold_Cranston" title="John Arnold Cranston">John Arnold Cranston</a> (working with <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Soddy" title="Frederick Soddy">Frederick Soddy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ada_Hitchins" title="Ada Hitchins">Ada Hitchins</a>) is also credited with discovering the most stable isotope in 1915, but delayed his announcement due to being called up for service in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The longest-lived and most abundant (nearly 100%) naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of protactinium, protactinium-231, has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 32,760 years and is a decay product of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>. Much smaller trace amounts of the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> protactinium-234m occur in the decay chain of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">uranium-238</a>. Protactinium-233 results from the decay of <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>-233 as part of the chain of events used to produce <a href="/wiki/Uranium-233" title="Uranium-233">uranium-233</a> by neutron irradiation of thorium-232. It is an undesired intermediate product in thorium-based <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> and is therefore removed from the active zone of the reactor during the breeding process. Analysis of the relative concentrations of various uranium, thorium and protactinium isotopes in water and minerals is used in <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> which are up to 175,000 years old and in modeling of various geological processes.
</p>

<p>In 1871, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a> <a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">predicted</a> the existence of an element between <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-7">[7]</a></sup> The actinide element group was unknown at the time. Therefore, uranium was positioned below <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> in group VI, and thorium below <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> in group IV, leaving the space below <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> in group V empty and, until the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic tables</a> were published with this structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For a long time chemists searched for eka-tantalum as an element with similar chemical properties to tantalum, making a discovery of protactinium nearly impossible. Tantalum's heavier analogue was later found to be the transuranic element <a href="/wiki/Dubnium" title="Dubnium">dubnium</a>.

They named the new element <i>brevium</i> (from the Latin word, <i>brevis</i>, meaning brief or short) because of its short half-life, 6.7 hours for <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">234</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">91</sub></span>Pa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1250_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1250-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1254_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1254-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scerri_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scerri-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1917/18, two groups of scientists, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Soddy" title="Frederick Soddy">Frederick Soddy</a> and John Cranston of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, independently discovered another isotope of protactinium, <sup>231</sup>Pa having a much longer half-life of about 32,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scerri_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scerri-16">[16]</a></sup> Thus the name <i>brevium</i> was changed to <i>protoactinium</i> as the new element was part of the decay chain of uranium-235 as the parent of actinium (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">πρῶτος</span> = <i>protos</i> meaning <i>first</i>, <i>before</i>). For ease of pronunciation, the name was shortened to <i>protactinium</i> by the  <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">IUPAC</a> in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1251_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1251-18">[18]</a></sup> The discovery of protactinium completed one of the last gaps in the early versions of the periodic table, proposed by Mendeleev in 1869, and it brought to fame the involved scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aristid_von_Grosse" title="Aristid von Grosse">Aristid von Grosse</a> produced 2 milligrams of Pa<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> in 1927,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and in 1934 first isolated elemental protactinium from 0.1 milligrams of Pa<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He used two different procedures: in the first one, protactinium oxide was irradiated by 35 keV electrons in vacuum. In another method, called the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_bar_process" title="Crystal bar process">van Arkel–de Boer process</a>, the oxide was chemically converted to a <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromide" title="Bromide">bromide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a>) and then reduced in a vacuum with an electrically heated metallic filament:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 PaI<sub>5</sub> → 2 Pa + 5 I<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In 1961, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Atomic_Energy_Authority" title="United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority">United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority</a> (UKAEA) produced 127 grams of 99.9% pure protactinium-231 by processing 60 tonnes of waste material in a 12-stage process, at a cost of about 500,000 USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myasoedov_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myasoedov-23">[23]</a></sup> For many years, this was the world's only significant supply of protactinium, which was provided to various laboratories for scientific studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> in the US provided protactinium at a cost of about 280 USD/gram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_protactinium" title="Isotopes of protactinium">Isotopes of protactinium</a></div>
<p>Twenty-nine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> of protactinium have been discovered, the most stable being <sup>231</sup>Pa with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 32,760 years, <sup>233</sup>Pa with a half-life of 27 days, and <sup>230</sup>Pa with a half-life of 17.4 days. All of the remaining isotopes have half-lives shorter than 1.6 days, and the majority of these have half-lives less than 1.8 seconds. Protactinium also has two <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a>, <sup>217m</sup>Pa (half-life 1.2 milliseconds) and <sup>234m</sup>Pa (half-life 1.17 minutes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> for isotopes of protactinium lighter than (and including) the most stable isotope <sup>231</sup>Pa (i.e., <sup>212</sup>Pa to <sup>231</sup>Pa) is <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> and the primary mode for the heavier isotopes (i.e., <sup>232</sup>Pa to <sup>240</sup>Pa) is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> of isotopes of protactinium lighter than (and including) <sup>231</sup>Pa are <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a> isotopes and the primary decay products for the heavier isotopes of protactinium are <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Protactinium is one of the rarest and most expensive naturally occurring elements. It is found in the form of two isotopes – <sup>231</sup>Pa and <sup>234</sup>Pa, with the isotope <sup>234</sup>Pa occurring in two different energy states. Nearly all natural protactinium is protactinium-231. It is an <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha emitter</a> and is formed by the decay of uranium-235, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Beta_particle" title="Beta particle">beta radiating</a> protactinium-234 is produced as a result of <a href="/wiki/File:Decay_chain(4n%2B2,_Uranium_series).PNG" title="File:Decay chain(4n+2, Uranium series).PNG">uranium-238 decay</a>. Nearly all uranium-238 (99.8%) decays first to the shorter-lived <sup>234m</sup>Pa isomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANL_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANL-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium occurs in <a href="/wiki/Uraninite" title="Uraninite">uraninite</a> (pitchblende) at concentrations of about 0.3-3 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts</a> <sup>231</sup>Pa per million parts (ppm) of ore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-7">[7]</a></sup> Whereas the usual content is closer to 0.3 ppm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-27">[27]</a></sup> (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1chymov" title="Jáchymov">Jáchymov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>), some ores from the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> have about 3 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-17">[17]</a></sup> Protactinium is homogeneously dispersed in most natural materials and in water, but at much lower concentrations on the order of one part per trillion, that corresponds to the radioactivity of 0.1 picocuries (pCi)/g. There is about 500 times more protactinium in sandy soil particles than in water, even the water present in the same sample of soil. Much higher ratios of 2,000 and above are measured in <a href="/wiki/Loam" title="Loam">loam</a> soils and clays, such as <a href="/wiki/Bentonite" title="Bentonite">bentonite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANL_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ANL-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_nuclear_reactors">In nuclear reactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In nuclear reactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two major protactinium isotopes, <sup>231</sup>Pa and <sup>233</sup>Pa, are produced from thorium in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>; both are undesirable and are usually removed, thereby adding complexity to the reactor design and operation. In particular, <sup>232</sup>Th via (<i>n</i>,2<i>n</i>) reactions produces <sup>231</sup>Th which quickly (half-life 25.5 hours) decays to <sup>231</sup>Pa. The last isotope, while not a transuranic waste, has a long half-life of 32,760 years and is a major contributor to the long term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotoxic" title="Radiotoxic">radiotoxicity</a> of spent nuclear fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium-233 is formed upon neutron capture by <sup>232</sup>Th. It further either decays to uranium-233 or captures another neutron and converts into the non-fissile uranium-234.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <sup>233</sup>Pa has a relatively long half-life of 27 days and high <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(physics)" title="Cross section (physics)">cross section</a> for neutron capture (the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Neutron_poison" title="Neutron poison">neutron poison</a>"). Thus instead of rapidly decaying to the useful <sup>233</sup>U, a significant fraction of <sup>233</sup>Pa converts to non-fissile isotopes and consumes neutrons, degrading <a href="/wiki/Neutron_economy" title="Neutron economy">the reactor efficiency</a>. To avoid this, <sup>233</sup>Pa is extracted from the active zone of thorium <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactors</a>, during their operation, so that it only decays to <sup>233</sup>U. This is achieved using several meters tall columns of molten <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> with lithium dissolved in it. In a simplified scenario, lithium selectively reduces protactinium salts to protactinium metal which is then extracted from the molten-salt cycle, and bismuth is merely a carrier. It is chosen because of its low <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> (271 °C), low vapor pressure, good solubility for lithium and actinides, and immiscibility with molten <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-b1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_chemical_properties">Physical and chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical and chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Protactinium is an <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> which is positioned in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> to the left of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and to the right of <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, and many of its physical properties are intermediate between those two actinides. So, protactinium is more dense and rigid than thorium but is lighter than uranium, and its melting point is lower than that of thorium and higher than that of uranium. The thermal expansion, electrical and thermal conductivities of these three elements are comparable and are typical of <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metals</a>. The estimated <a href="/wiki/Shear_modulus" title="Shear modulus">shear modulus</a> of protactinium is similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Protactinium is a metal with silvery-gray luster that is preserved for some time in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myasoedov_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Myasoedov-23">[23]</a></sup> Protactinium easily reacts with oxygen, water vapor and acids, but not with alkalis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>At room temperature, protactinium crystallizes in the body-centered <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal</a> structure which can be regarded as distorted body-centered cubic lattice; this structure does not change upon compression up to 53 GPa. The structure changes to face-centered cubic (<i>fcc</i>) upon cooling from high temperature, at about 1200 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exp_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-exp-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The thermal expansion coefficient of the tetragonal phase between room temperature and 700 °C is 9.9<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">6</span></sup>/°C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exp_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-exp-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium is <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> and no magnetic transitions are known for it at any temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It becomes <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductive</a> at temperatures below 1.4 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-super_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-super-33">[33]</a></sup> Protactinium tetrachloride is paramagnetic at room temperature but turns <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a> upon cooling to 182 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium exists in two major <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a>, +4 and +5, both in solids and solutions, and the +3 and +2 states were observed in some solid phases. As the electron configuration of the neutral atom is [Rn]5f<sup>2</sup>6d<sup>1</sup>7s<sup>2</sup>, the +5 oxidation state corresponds to the low-energy (and thus favored) 5f<sup>0</sup> configuration. Both +4 and +5 states easily form <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a> in water with the predominant ions being Pa(OH)<sup>3+</sup>, <span class="chemf nowrap">Pa(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">Pa(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> and Pa(OH)<sub>4</sub>, all colorless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1265_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1265-39">[39]</a></sup> Other known protactinium ions include <span class="chemf nowrap">PaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_and_oxygen-containing_salts">Oxides and oxygen-containing salts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oxides and oxygen-containing salts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Protactinium oxides are known for the metal oxidation states +2, +4 and +5. The most stable is white pentoxide Pa<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, which can be produced by igniting protactinium(V) hydroxide in air at a temperature of 500 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1268_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1268-49">[49]</a></sup> Its crystal structure is cubic, and the chemical composition is often non-stoichiometric, described as PaO<sub>2.25</sub>. Another phase of this oxide with orthorhombic symmetry has also been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-7"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pacl4b_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-pacl4b-50">[50]</a></sup> The black dioxide PaO<sub>2</sub> is obtained from the pentoxide by reducing it at 1550 °C with hydrogen. It is not readily soluble in either dilute or concentrated <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acids</a>, but easily dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-8"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup> The dioxide can be converted back to pentoxide by heating in oxygen-containing atmosphere to 1100 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pacl4b_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-pacl4b-50">[50]</a></sup> The monoxide PaO has only been observed as a thin coating on protactinium metal, but not in an isolated bulk form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-9"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium forms mixed binary oxides with various metals. With alkali metals <i>A</i>, the crystals have a chemical formula APaO<sub>3</sub> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perovskite_structure" title="Perovskite structure">perovskite structure</a>, or A<sub>3</sub>PaO<sub>4</sub> and distorted rock-salt structure, or A<sub>7</sub>PaO<sub>6</sub> where oxygen atoms form a hexagonal close-packed lattice. In all these materials, protactinium ions are octahedrally coordinated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1269_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1269-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The pentoxide Pa<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> combines with rare-earth metal oxides R<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to form various nonstoichiometric mixed-oxides, also of perovskite structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium oxides are <a href="/wiki/Basic_oxide" title="Basic oxide">basic</a>; they easily convert to hydroxides and can form various salts, such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a>, etc. The nitrate is usually white but can be brown due to <a href="/wiki/Radiolysis" title="Radiolysis">radiolytic</a> decomposition. Heating the nitrate in air at 400 °C converts it to the white protactinium pentoxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-target_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-target-54">[54]</a></sup> The polytrioxophosphate Pa(PO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub> can be produced by reacting difluoride sulfate PaF<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> with <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> (H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>) under inert gas atmosphere. Heating the product to about 900 °C eliminates the reaction by-products such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a> and phosphoric anhydride. Heating to higher temperatures in an inert atmosphere decomposes Pa(PO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub> into the diphosphate PaP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, which is analogous to diphosphates of other actinides. In the diphosphate, the PO<sub>3</sub> groups form pyramids of C<sub>2v</sub> symmetry. Heating PaP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> in air to 1400 °C decomposes it into the pentoxides of phosphorus and protactinium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-papo3_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-papo3-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Protactinium(V) fluoride forms white crystals where protactinium ions are arranged in pentagonal bipyramids and <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordinated</a> by 7 other ions. The coordination is the same in protactinium(V) chloride, but the color is yellow. The coordination changes to octahedral in the brown protactinium(V) bromide and is unknown for protactinium(V) iodide. The protactinium coordination in all its tetrahalides is 8, but the arrangement is square antiprismatic in protactinium(IV) fluoride and dodecahedral in the chloride and bromide. Brown-colored protactinium(III) iodide has been reported where protactinium ions are 8-coordinated in a bicapped trigonal prismatic arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1270_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1270-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PaCl5.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="635" height="79" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/PaCl5.svg/220px-PaCl5.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/PaCl5.svg/330px-PaCl5.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/PaCl5.svg/440px-PaCl5.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PaCl5.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coordination of protactinium (solid circles) and halogen atoms (open circles) in protactinium(V) fluoride or chloride.</div></div></div>
<p>Protactinium(V) fluoride and protactinium(V) chloride have a polymeric structure of monoclinic symmetry. There, within one polymeric chain, all the halide atoms lie in one graphite-like plane and form planar pentagons around the protactinium ions. The coordination 7 of protactinium originates from the 5 halide atoms and two bonds to protactinium atoms belonging to the nearby chains. These compounds easily hydrolyze in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1271_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1271-56">[56]</a></sup> The pentachloride melts at 300 °C and sublimates at even lower temperatures.
</p><p>Protactinium(V) fluoride can be prepared by reacting protactinium oxide with either <a href="/wiki/Bromine_pentafluoride" title="Bromine pentafluoride">bromine pentafluoride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bromine_trifluoride" title="Bromine trifluoride">bromine trifluoride</a> at about 600 °C, and protactinium(IV) fluoride is obtained from the oxide and a mixture of hydrogen and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> at 600 °C; a large excess of hydrogen is required to remove atmospheric oxygen leaks into the reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-10"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium(V) chloride is prepared by reacting protactinium oxide with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> at temperature of 200–300 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-11"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup> The by-products (such as PaOCl<sub>3</sub>) are removed by fractional sublimation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pacl5_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-pacl5-45">[45]</a></sup> Reduction of protactinium(V) chloride with hydrogen at about 800 °C yields protactinium(IV) chloride – a yellow-green solid which sublimes in vacuum at 400 °C; it can also be obtained directly from protactinium dioxide by treating it with carbon tetrachloride at 400 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-12"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium bromides are produced by the action of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_bromide" title="Aluminium bromide">aluminium bromide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bromide" title="Hydrogen bromide">hydrogen bromide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrabromide" title="Carbon tetrabromide">carbon tetrabromide</a> or a mixture of hydrogen bromide and <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_bromide" title="Thionyl bromide">thionyl bromide</a> on protactinium oxide. An alternative reaction is between protactinium pentachloride and hydrogen bromide or thionyl bromide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-13"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup> Protactinium(V) bromide has two similar monoclinic forms, one is obtained by sublimation at 400–410 °C and another by sublimation at slightly lower temperature of 390–400 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pabr5b_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-pabr5b-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pabr5_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-pabr5-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium iodides result from the oxides and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_iodide" title="Aluminium iodide">aluminium iodide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_iodide" title="Ammonium iodide">ammonium iodide</a> heated to 600 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-14"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup> Protactinium(III) iodide was obtained by heating protactinium(V) iodide in vacuum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1271_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-g1271-56">[56]</a></sup> As with oxides, protactinium forms mixed halides with alkali metals. Among those, most remarkable is Na<sub>3</sub>PaF<sub>8</sub> where protactinium ion is symmetrically surrounded by 8 F<sup>−</sup> ions which form a nearly perfect cube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1275_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-g1275-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>More complex protactinium fluorides are also known such as Pa<sub>2</sub>F<sub>9</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1271_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-g1271-56">[56]</a></sup> and ternary fluorides of the types MPaF<sub>6</sub> (M = Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs or NH<sub>4</sub>), M<sub>2</sub>PaF<sub>7</sub> (M = K, Rb, Cs or NH<sub>4</sub>) and M<sub>3</sub>PaF<sub>8</sub> (M = Li, Na, Rb, Cs), all being white crystalline solids. The MPaF<sub>6</sub> formula can be represented as a combination of MF and PaF<sub>5</sub>. These compounds can be obtained by evaporating a hydrofluoric acid solution containing these both complexes. For the small alkali cations like Na, the crystal structure is tetragonal, whereas it lowers to orthorphombic for larger cations K<sup>+</sup>, Rb<sup>+</sup>, Cs<sup>+</sup> or NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>. A similar variation was observed for the M<sub>2</sub>PaF<sub>7</sub> fluorides, namely the crystal symmetry was dependent on the cation and differed for Cs<sub>2</sub>PaF<sub>7</sub> and M<sub>2</sub>PaF<sub>7</sub> (M = K, Rb or NH<sub>4</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trif_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-trif-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_inorganic_compounds">Other inorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other inorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oxyhalides and oxysulfides of protactinium are known. PaOBr<sub>3</sub> has a monoclinic structure composed of double-chain units where protactinium has coordination 7 and is arranged into pentagonal bipyramids. The chains are interconnected through oxygen and bromine atoms, and each oxygen atom is related to three protactinium atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pabr5b_44-4"><a href="#cite_note-pabr5b-44">[44]</a></sup> PaOS is a light-yellow non-volatile solid with a cubic crystal lattice isostructural to that of other actinide oxysulfides. It is obtained by reacting protactinium(V) chloride with a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> at 900 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-15"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In hydrides and nitrides, protactinium has a low oxidation state of about +3. The hydride is obtained by direct action of hydrogen on the metal at 250 °C, and the nitride is a product of ammonia and protactinium tetrachloride or pentachloride. This bright yellow solid is stable to heating to 800 °C in vacuum. Protactinium carbide PaC is formed by reduction of protactinium tetrafluoride with barium in a carbon crucible at a temperature of about 1400 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pao2_34-16"><a href="#cite_note-pao2-34">[34]</a></sup> Protactinium forms borohydrides which include Pa(BH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>4</sub>. It has an unusual polymeric structure with helical chains where the protactinium atom has coordination number of 12 and is surrounded by six BH<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1277_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1277-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organometallic_compounds">Organometallic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Organometallic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranocene-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="891" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Uranocene-3D-balls.png/170px-Uranocene-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Uranocene-3D-balls.png/255px-Uranocene-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Uranocene-3D-balls.png/340px-Uranocene-3D-balls.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranocene-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The proposed structure of the protactinocene (Pa(C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) molecule</div></div></div>
<p>Protactinium(IV) forms a tetrahedral complex tetrakis(cyclopentadienyl)protactinium(IV) (or Pa(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>4</sub>) with four <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">cyclopentadienyl</a> rings, which can be synthesized by reacting protactinium(IV) chloride with molten Be(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>. One ring can be substituted with a halide atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1278_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1278-58">[58]</a></sup> Another organometallic complex is golden-yellow bis(π-cyclooctatetraene) protactinium, or protactinocene, Pa(C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, which is analogous in structure to <a href="/wiki/Uranocene" title="Uranocene">uranocene</a>. There, the metal atom is sandwiched between two <a href="/wiki/Cyclooctatetraene" title="Cyclooctatetraene">cyclooctatetraene</a> ligands. Similar to uranocene, it can be prepared by reacting protactinium tetrachloride with dipotassium <a href="/wiki/Cyclooctatetraene" title="Cyclooctatetraene">cyclooctatetraenide</a>, K<sub>2</sub>C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>, in <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cene_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-cene-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although protactinium is located in the periodic table between uranium and thorium, which both have numerous applications, owing to its scarcity, high radioactivity and high toxicity, there are currently no uses for protactinium outside scientific research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANL_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-ANL-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protactinium-231 arises from the decay of uranium-235 formed in nuclear reactors, and by the reaction <sup>232</sup>Th + n → <sup>231</sup>Th + 2n and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. It was once thought to be able to support a nuclear chain reaction, which could in principle be used to build <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Walter_Seifritz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Walter Seifritz (page does not exist)">Walter Seifritz</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Seifritz" title="de:Walter Seifritz">de</a>)</span> once estimated the associated <a href="/wiki/Critical_mass" title="Critical mass">critical mass</a> as <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002750000000000000♠</span>750<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>180 kg</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> However, the possibility of criticality of <sup>231</sup>Pa has been ruled out since then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the advent of highly sensitive <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometers</a>, an application of <sup>231</sup>Pa as a tracer in geology and <a href="/wiki/Paleoceanography" title="Paleoceanography">paleoceanography</a> has become possible. So, the ratio of protactinium-231 to thorium-230 is used for <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> of sediments which are up to 175,000 years old and in modeling of the formation of minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-27">[27]</a></sup> In particular, its evaluation in oceanic sediments allowed to reconstruct the movements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> water bodies during the last melting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Some of the protactinium-related dating variations rely on the analysis of the relative concentrations for several long-living members of the uranium decay chain – uranium, protactinium, and thorium, for example. These elements have 6, 5 and 4 valence electrons and thus favor +6, +5 and +4 oxidation states, respectively, and show different physical and chemical properties. So, thorium and protactinium, but not uranium compounds are poorly soluble in aqueous solutions, and precipitate into sediments; the precipitation rate is faster for thorium than for protactinium. Besides, the concentration analysis for both protactinium-231 (half-life 32,760 years) and thorium-230 (half-life 75,380 years) allows to improve the accuracy compared to when only one isotope is measured; this double-isotope method is also weakly sensitive to inhomogeneities in the spatial distribution of the isotopes and to variations in their precipitation rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-brit-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protactinium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Protactinium is both toxic and highly radioactive and thus all manipulations with it are performed in a sealed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glove_box" title="Glove box">glove box</a>. Its major isotope <sup>231</sup>Pa has a <a href="/wiki/Specific_activity" title="Specific activity">specific activity</a> of 0.048 <a href="/wiki/Curie" title="Curie">curies</a> (1.8 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">GBq</a>) per gram and primarily emits alpha-particles with an energy of 5 MeV, which can be stopped by a thin layer of any material. However, it slowly decays, with a half-life of 32,760 years, into <sup>227</sup>Ac, which has a specific activity of 74 curies (2,700 GBq) per gram, emits both alpha and beta radiation, and has a much shorter half-life of 22 years. <sup>227</sup>Ac, in turn, decays into lighter isotopes with even shorter half-lives and much greater specific activities (SA), as summarized in the table below showing the decay chain of protactinium-231.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANL_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-ANL-26">[26]</a></sup>

<p>As protactinium is present in small amounts in most natural products and materials, it is ingested with food or water and inhaled with air. Only about 0.05% of ingested protactinium is absorbed into the blood and the remainder is excreted. From the blood, about 40% of the protactinium
deposits in the bones, about 15% goes to the liver, 2% to the kidneys, and the rest leaves the body. The biological half-life of protactinium is about 50 years in the bones, whereas in other organs the kinetics has a fast and slow component. So in the liver 70% of protactinium have a half-life of 10 days and 30% remain for 60 days. The corresponding values for kidneys are 20% (10 days) and 80% (60 days). In all these organs, protactinium promotes cancer via its radioactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANL_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-ANL-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-target_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-target-54">[54]</a></sup> The maximum safe dose of Pa in the human body is 0.03 μCi (1.1 kBq), which corresponds to 0.5 micrograms of <sup>231</sup>Pa. This isotope is 2.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">8</span></sup> times more toxic than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocyanic_acid" title="Hydrocyanic acid">hydrocyanic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The maximum allowed concentrations of <sup>231</sup>Pa in the air in Germany is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−4</sup> Bq/m<sup>3</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-target_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-target-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
