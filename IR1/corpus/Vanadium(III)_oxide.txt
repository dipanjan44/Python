<p><b>Vanadium(III) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.  It is a black solid prepared by reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" title="Vanadium pentoxide">V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup>It is a basic oxide dissolving in acids to give solutions of vanadium(III) complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has the <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> It is antiferromagnetic with a critical temperature of 160 K. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Page-4">[4]</a></sup> At this temperature there is an abrupt change in conductivity from metallic to insulating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Page-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon exposure to air it gradually converts into indigo-blue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_dioxide" title="Vanadium dioxide">V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Page-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In nature it occurs as the rare mineral <a href="/wiki/Karelianite" title="Karelianite">karelianite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
