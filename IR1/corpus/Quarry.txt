<p>A <b>quarry</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Open-pit_mining" title="Open-pit mining">open-pit mine</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Dimension_stone" title="Dimension stone">dimension stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">construction aggregate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riprap" title="Riprap">riprap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slate</a> is excavated from the ground.
</p><p>The word <i>quarry</i> can also include the underground quarrying for stone, such as <a href="/wiki/Bath_stone" title="Bath stone">Bath stone</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slabs">Slabs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Slabs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many quarry stones such as <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> are cut into larger slabs and removed from the quarry. The surfaces are polished and finished with varying degrees of sheen or <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">luster</a>. Polished slabs are often cut into <a href="/wiki/Tile" title="Tile">tiles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Countertop" title="Countertop">countertops</a> and installed in many kinds of residential and commercial properties. Natural stone quarried from the earth is often considered a luxury and tends to be a highly durable surface, thus highly desirable.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carrara_12.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1996" data-file-width="2696" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Carrara_12.JPG/220px-Carrara_12.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Carrara_12.JPG/330px-Carrara_12.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Carrara_12.JPG/440px-Carrara_12.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carrara_12.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Extraction work in a marble quarry in <a href="/wiki/Carrara" title="Carrara">Carrara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a></div></div></div>
<p>Quarries in level areas with shallow <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> or which are located close to surface water often have <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> problems with <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a>. Generally the water is removed by pumping while the quarry is operational, but for high inflows more complex approaches may be required.  For example, the Coquina quarry is excavated to more than 60 feet (18 m) below sea level. To reduce surface leakage, a <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> lined with <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> was constructed around the entire quarry. Ground water entering the pit is pumped up into the moat.  As a quarry becomes deeper, water inflows generally increase and it also becomes more expensive to lift the water higher during removal; this can become the limiting factor in quarry depth.  Some water-filled quarries are worked from beneath the water, by dredging.
</p><p>Many people and municipalities consider quarries to be eyesores and require various abatement methods to address problems with noise, dust, and appearance.  One of the more effective and famous examples of successful quarry restoration is <a href="/wiki/Butchart_Gardens" title="Butchart Gardens">Butchart Gardens</a> in Victoria, BC, Canada.
</p><p>A further problem is pollution of roads from trucks leaving the quarries. To control and restrain the pollution of public roads, <a href="/wiki/Wheel_washing_system" title="Wheel washing system">wheel washing systems</a> are becoming more common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quarry_lakes">Quarry lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quarry lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quarry_lake" title="Quarry lake">Quarry lake</a></div>
<p>Many quarries naturally fill with water after abandonment and become <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>.  Others are made into <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>.
</p><p>Water-filled quarries can be very deep with water, often 50 feet or more, that is often surprisingly cold. Unexpectedly cold water can cause a swimmer's muscles to suddenly weaken; it can also cause <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACA-1">[1]</a></sup> Though quarry water is often very clear, submerged quarry stones and abandoned equipment make diving into these quarries extremely dangerous. Several people drown in quarries each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDoL_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDoL-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GEO-3">[3]</a></sup> However, many inactive quarries are converted into safe swimming sites.
</p><p>Such lakes, even lakes within active quarries, can provide important habitat for animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
