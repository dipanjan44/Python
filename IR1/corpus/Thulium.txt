<p><b>Thulium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Tm</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 69. It is the thirteenth and third-last element in the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series. Like the other lanthanides, the most common oxidation state is +3, seen in its oxide, halides and other compounds; because it occurs so late in the series, however, the +2 oxidation state is also stabilized by the nearly full 4f shell that results. In aqueous solution, like compounds of other late lanthanides, soluble thulium compounds form <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a> with nine water molecules.
</p><p>In 1879, the Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve" title="Per Teodor Cleve">Per Teodor Cleve</a> separated from the rare earth oxide <a href="/wiki/Erbium(III)_oxide" title="Erbium(III) oxide">erbia</a> another two previously unknown components, which he called <a href="/wiki/Holmium(III)_oxide" title="Holmium(III) oxide">holmia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thulium(III)_oxide" title="Thulium(III) oxide">thulia</a>; these were the oxides of <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a> and thulium, respectively. A relatively pure sample of thulium metal was first obtained in 1911.
</p><p>Thulium is the second-least abundant of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanides" title="Lanthanides">lanthanides</a>, after radioactively unstable <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a> which is only found in <a href="/wiki/Trace_radioisotope" title="Trace radioisotope">trace quantities</a> on Earth. It is an easily workable <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> with a bright silvery-gray luster. It is fairly soft and slowly <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnishes</a> in air. Despite its high price and rarity, thulium is used as the radiation source in portable <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> devices, and in some <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a>. It has no significant biological role and is not particularly toxic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pure thulium metal has a bright, silvery luster, which tarnishes on exposure to air. The metal can be cut with a knife,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> as it has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 2 to 3; it is malleable and ductile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup> Thulium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> below 32<span class="nowrap"> </span>K, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> between 32 and 56<span class="nowrap"> </span>K, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> above 56<span class="nowrap"> </span>K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium has two major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotropes</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal</a> α-Tm and the more stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal</a> β-Tm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thulium tarnishes slowly in air and burns readily at 150<span class="nowrap"> </span><a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Thulium(III)_oxide" title="Thulium(III) oxide">thulium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Tm + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Tm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Thulium is quite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a> and reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form thulium hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Tm (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Tm(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Thulium reacts with all the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>. Reactions are slow at room temperature, but are vigorous above 200<span class="nowrap"> </span>°C:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Tm (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 TmF<sub>3</sub> (s) (white)</dd>
<dd>2 Tm (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 TmCl<sub>3</sub> (s) (yellow)</dd>
<dd>2 Tm (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 TmBr<sub>3</sub> (s) (white)</dd>
<dd>2 Tm (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 TmI<sub>3</sub> (s) (yellow)</dd></dl>
<p>Thulium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the pale green  Tm(III) ions, which exist as [Tm(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Tm (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Tm<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Thulium reacts with various metallic and non-metallic elements forming a range of binary compounds, including TmN, TmS, TmC<sub>2</sub>, Tm<sub>2</sub>C<sub>3</sub>, TmH<sub>2</sub>, TmH<sub>3</sub>, TmSi<sub>2</sub>, TmGe<sub>3</sub>, TmB<sub>4</sub>, TmB<sub>6</sub> and TmB<sub>12</sub>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In those compounds, thulium exhibits valence states +2 and +3, however, the +3 state is most common and only this state has been observed in thulium solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-7">[7]</a></sup> Thulium exists as a Tm<sup>3+</sup> ion in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. In this state, the thulium ion is surrounded by nine molecules of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Tm<sup>3+</sup> ions exhibit a bright blue luminescence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium's only known oxide is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thulium_oxide" title="Thulium oxide">Tm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>. This oxide is sometimes called "thulia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_and_use_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hist_and_use-8">[8]</a></sup> Reddish-purple thulium(II) compounds can be made by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reduction" title="Chemical reduction">reduction</a> of thulium(III) compounds. Examples of thulium(II) compounds include the halides (except the fluoride). Some hydrated thulium compounds, such as TmCl<sub>3</sub><b>·</b>7H<sub>2</sub>O and Tm<sub>2</sub>(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub><b>·</b>6H<sub>2</sub>O are green or greenish-white.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-concise_encyclopedia_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-concise_encyclopedia-9">[9]</a></sup> Thulium dichloride reacts very vigorously with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. This reaction results in <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thulium hydroxide (page does not exist)">Tm(OH)<sub>3</sub></a> exhibiting a fading reddish color.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Combination of thulium and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalcogens" title="Chalcogens">chalcogens</a> results in thulium <a href="/wiki/Chalcogenide" title="Chalcogenide">chalcogenides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> to produce hydrogen gas and thulium chloride. With <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> it yields thulium nitrate, or Tm(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cool_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cool-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_thulium" title="Isotopes of thulium">Isotopes of thulium</a></div>
<p>The isotopes of thulium range from <sup>145</sup>Tm to <sup>179</sup>Tm. The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> before the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>169</sup>Tm, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, and the primary mode after is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> before <sup>169</sup>Tm are element 68 (<a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>) isotopes, and the primary products after are element 70 (<a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>) isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hand_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hand-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium-169 is thulium's longest-lived and most abundant isotope. It is the only isotope of thulium that is thought to be stable, although it is predicted to undergo <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a>-165 with a very long half-life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> After thulium-169, the next-longest-lived isotopes are thulium-171, which has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 1.92 years, and thulium-170, which has a half-life of 128.6 days. Most other isotopes have half-lives of a few minutes or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nudat_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nudat-13">[13]</a></sup> Thirty-five isotopes and 26 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> of thulium have been detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Most isotopes of thulium lighter than 169 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass units</a> decay via <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">beta-plus decay</a>, although some exhibit significant <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Proton_emission" title="Proton emission">proton emission</a>. Heavier isotopes undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-minus_decay" title="Beta-minus decay">beta-minus decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nudat_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nudat-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thulium was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> by Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve" title="Per Teodor Cleve">Per Teodor Cleve</a> in 1879 by looking for impurities in the <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> of other rare earth elements (this was the same method <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> earlier used to discover some other rare earth elements).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Cleve started by removing all of the known contaminants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">Er</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub>).  Upon additional processing, he obtained two new substances; one brown and one green. The brown substance was the oxide of the element <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a> and was named holmia by Cleve, and the green substance was the oxide of an unknown element. Cleve named the oxide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thulia" title="Thulia">thulia</a> and its element thulium after <a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> place name associated with Scandinavia or <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. Thulium's atomic symbol was once Tu, but this was changed to Tm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium was so rare that none of the early workers had enough of it to purify sufficiently to actually see the green color; they had to be content with <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopically</a> observing the strengthening of the two characteristic absorption bands, as erbium was progressively removed.  The first researcher to obtain nearly pure thulium was <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a>, a British expatriate working on a large scale at <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Hampshire" title="University of New Hampshire">New Hampshire College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Durham,_New_Hampshire" title="Durham, New Hampshire">Durham</a>. In 1911 he reported his results, having used his discovered method of bromate fractional crystallization to do the purification.  He famously needed 15,000 purification operations to establish that the material was homogeneous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>High-purity thulium oxide was first offered commercially in the late 1950s, as a result of the adoption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> separation technology. Lindsay Chemical Division of American Potash &amp; Chemical Corporation offered it in grades of 99% and 99.9% purity. The price per kilogram has oscillated between US$4,600 and $13,300 in the period from 1959 to 1998 for 99.9% purity, and it was second highest for lanthanides behind <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1300" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg/220px-Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg/330px-Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg/440px-Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Madagaskar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thulium is found in the mineral monazite</div></div></div>
<p>The element is never found in nature in pure form, but it is found in small quantities in <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> with other rare earths. Thulium is often found with minerals containing <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>. In particular, thulium occurs in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_of_metals_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-handbook_of_metals-19">[19]</a></sup> However, thulium also occurs in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>. Thulium has not been found in prevalence over the other rare earths in any mineral yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Its abundance in the Earth's crust is 0.5 mg/kg by weight and 50 parts per billion by <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>. Thulium makes up approximately 0.5 parts per million of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, although this value can range from 0.4 to 0.8 parts per million. Thulium makes up 250 parts per quadrillion of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, thulium exists in concentrations of 200 parts per trillion by weight and 1 part per trillion by moles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cool_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-cool-11">[11]</a></sup> Thulium ore occurs most commonly in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> also have large reserves of thulium. Total reserves of thulium are approximately 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. Thulium is the least abundant <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> on earth except for <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thulium is principally extracted from <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> ores (~0.007% thulium) found in river sands, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a>. Newer ion-exchange and solvent-extraction techniques have led to easier separation of the rare earths, which has yielded much lower costs for thulium production. The principal sources today are the ion <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> clays of southern China. In these, where about two-thirds of the total rare-earth content is yttrium, thulium is about 0.5% (or about tied with lutetium for rarity). The metal can be isolated through <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of its oxide with <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> metal or by <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> reduction in a closed container. None of thulium's natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_(chemistry)" title="Compound (chemistry)">compounds</a> are commercially important. Approximately 50 tonnes per year of thulium oxide are produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1996, thulium oxide cost US$20 per gram, and in 2005, 99%-pure thulium metal powder cost US$70 per gram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thulium has a few applications:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laser">Laser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Laser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">Holmium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>-thulium triple-doped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttrium_aluminum_garnet" title="Yttrium aluminum garnet">yttrium aluminum garnet</a> (Ho:Cr:Tm:YAG, or Ho,Cr,Tm:YAG) is an active laser medium material with high efficiency. It lases at 2080 nm and is widely used in military applications, medicine, and meteorology. Single-element thulium-doped YAG (Tm:YAG) lasers operate at 2,01 μm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The wavelength of thulium-based lasers is very efficient for superficial ablation of tissue, with minimal coagulation depth in air or in water. This makes thulium lasers attractive for laser-based surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray_source">X-ray source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: X-ray source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite its high cost, portable X-ray devices use thulium that has been bombarded in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> as a radiation source. These sources have a useful life of about one year, as tools in medical and dental diagnosis, as well as to detect defects in inaccessible mechanical and electronic components. Such sources do not need extensive radiation protection – only a small cup of lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-appl-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thulium-170 is gaining popularity as an X-ray source for cancer treatment via <a href="/wiki/Brachytherapy" title="Brachytherapy">brachytherapy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This isotope has a half-life of 128.6 days and five major emission lines of comparable intensity (at 7.4, 51.354, 52.389, 59.4 and 84.253 keV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Thulium-170 is one of the four most popular radioisotopes for use in industrial <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thulium has been used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductor" title="High-temperature superconductor">high-temperature superconductors</a> similarly to <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>. Thulium potentially has use in <a href="/wiki/Ferrite_(magnet)" title="Ferrite (magnet)">ferrites</a>, ceramic magnetic materials that are used in <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-appl-23">[23]</a></sup> Thulium is also similar to <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> in that it is used in arc lighting for its unusual spectrum, in this case, its green emission lines, which are not covered by other elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Because thulium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluoresce" title="Fluoresce">fluoresces</a> with a blue color when exposed to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet light</a>, thulium is put into <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banknotes" title="Banknotes">banknotes</a> as a measure against <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit" title="Counterfeit">counterfeiting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Photochemistry_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Photochemistry-28">[28]</a></sup> The blue fluorescence of Tm-doped calcium sulfate has been used in personal dosimeters for visual monitoring of radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Tm-doped halides which Tm is in its 2+ valence state, are promising luminescent materials that can make efficient electricity generating windows based on the principle of a luminescent solar concentrator, possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role_and_precautions">Biological role and precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thulium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Biological role and precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Soluble thulium salts are mildly <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a>, but insoluble thulium salts are completely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nontoxic" title="Nontoxic">nontoxic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> When injected, thulium can cause degeneration of the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> and can also cause <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> concentration to fluctuate. Liver damage from thulium is more prevalent in male <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a> than female mice. Despite this, thulium has a low level of toxicity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In humans, thulium occurs in the highest amounts in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>. Humans typically consume several micrograms of thulium per year. The roots of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> do not take up thulium, and the <a href="/wiki/Dry_weight" title="Dry weight">dry weight</a> of vegetables usually contains one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">part per billion</a> of thulium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> Thulium <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_(substance)" title="Powder (substance)">powder</a> are toxic upon inhalation or ingestion and can cause <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosions</a>. Radioactive thulium isotopes can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">radiation poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist_and_use_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hist_and_use-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
