<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of paper</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PulpAndPaperMill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PulpAndPaperMill.jpg/220px-PulpAndPaperMill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PulpAndPaperMill.jpg/330px-PulpAndPaperMill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/PulpAndPaperMill.jpg/440px-PulpAndPaperMill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PulpAndPaperMill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pulp and paper mill in <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, Canada. Although pulp and paper manufacturing requires large amounts of energy, a portion of it comes from burning wood residue.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of paper</b> is significant, which has led to changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_industry" title="Paper industry">industry</a> and behaviour at both business and personal levels. With the use of modern technology such as the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> and the highly mechanized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_harvester" title="Forest harvester">harvesting of wood</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disposable" title="Disposable">disposable</a> paper became a relatively cheap commodity, which led to a high level of consumption and <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>. The rise in global environmental issues such as air and water pollution, climate change, overflowing landfills and <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clearcutting</a> have all lead to increased government regulations. There is now a trend towards <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a> as it moves to reduce clear cutting, water use, greenhouse gas emissions, fossil fuel consumption and clean up its impacts on local water supplies and air pollution.
</p><p>According to a Canadian citizen's organization, "People need paper products and we need sustainable, environmentally safe production."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmental product declarations or product scorecards are available to collect and evaluate the environmental and social performance of paper products, such as the Paper Calculator<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>, Environmental Paper Assessment Tool (EPAT),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or Paper Profile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both the U.S. and Canada generate interactive maps of environmental indicators which show pollution emissions of individual facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pulp_mill" title="Pulp mill">Pulp and paper mills</a> contribute to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_waste" title="Solid waste">land</a> pollution and discarded paper and paperboard make up roughly 26% of solid municipal solid waste in <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pulp and paper generates the third largest amount of industrial air, water, and land emissions in Canada and the sixth largest in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2015, the industry released 174,000 tonnes of emissions to air, water and land (or 5.3%) out of a total of 3.3 million tonnes of emissions released by all industries in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> In the United States the pulp and paper industry released about 79, 000 tonnes or about 5% of all industrial pollutant releases in 2015<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup> Of this total waste released by the pulp and paper industry in the U.S., 66% was released into the air, 10% into water and 24% onto land whereas in Canada, most of the waste (96%) was released into the air.
</p><p>Worldwide, the pulp and paper industry is the fifth largest consumer of energy, accounting for four percent of all the world's energy use. However, the entire paper and printing sector contributes less than 1% to the global greenhouse gas inventory due to the very high use of renewable energy, mostly biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pulp and paper industry uses more water to produce a ton of product than any other industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The de-inking process during paper recycling is also a source of emissions due to chemicals released in the effluent. The European Recovered Paper Council has developed the ‘deinkability scorecard’ so that the printed paper products which have the best recyclability when they are deinked can be identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Worldwide consumption of paper has risen by 400% in the past 40 years, with 35% of harvested trees being used for paper manufacture. <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">Plantation forests</a>, from where the majority of wood for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulping" title="Pulping">pulping</a> is obtained, is generally a <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> and this raises concerns over the <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological effects</a> of the practice. 
</p><p>Much of the wood harvested in North America goes into lumber and other non-paper products.  In the U.S., 36% of the annual timber harvest is used for paper and paperboard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in Canada 21% comes directly from harvested trees. The rest comes from sawmill residues (55%) and recycled paper (24%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Deforestation is often seen as a problem in developing countries but also occurs in the developed world. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodchipping" title="Woodchipping">Woodchipping</a> to produce paper pulp is a contentious <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">environmental issue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Woodchipping_in_Australia" title="Woodchipping in Australia">Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In the 1990s, the New Zealand government stopped the export of <a href="/wiki/Woodchipping_in_New_Zealand" title="Woodchipping in New Zealand">woodchips</a> from native forests after campaigning by environmentalists.
</p><p>Over 6.5 million trees were cut down to make 16 billion <a href="/wiki/Paper_cup" title="Paper cup">paper cups</a> used by US consumers only for coffee in 2006, using 4 billion US gallons (15,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of water and resulting in 253 million pounds of waste. Overall, North Americans use 58% of all paper cups, amounting to 130 billion cups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pcuc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pcuc-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spitzer2009_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spitzer2009-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_pollution">Air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Emissions Inventory in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup> and the Air Pollutant Emission Inventory (APEI) in Canada<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup> compile annual emissions of air pollutants that contribute to smog, acid rain, greenhouse gases and diminished air quality including particulate matter (PM), sulphur oxides (SO<sub>x</sub>), nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>), cadmium, lead, mercury, and <a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">persistent organic pollutants</a> (POPs) such as <a href="/wiki/Dioxin" title="Dioxin">dioxins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexachlorobenzene" title="Hexachlorobenzene">hexachlorobenzene</a> (HCB) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAH). In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Toxics_Release_Inventory" title="Toxics Release Inventory">Toxics Release Inventory</a> (TRI) is a publicly available database maintained by the EPA's TRI Program that tracks the management in the U.S. of certain toxic chemicals that may pose a threat to human health and the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the U.S., total industrial releases of toxic waste into the air were 690 million pounds (313,000 tonnes) in 2015 and pulp and paper accounted for 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-23">[23]</a></sup>  Of the releases to air by the pulp and paper industry, 60% were methanol which is not a persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic (PBT) chemical and is not a carcinogen.  Several PBTs are emitted by the pulp and paper industry at measurable levels, including lead, hexachlorobenzene (HCB), dioxins, furans and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. In Canada emissions of these chemicals by the industry were less than 2% of total emissions in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup> In the U.S., the pulp and paper industry accounted for 22% of total HCB emissions but other PBT emissions were under 2% of national totals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are other important releases to air by the pulp and paper industry. Fine particulate matter (PM<sub>2.5</sub>) consists of particles 2.5 microns in diameter or less which can penetrate the respiratory system and have a serious effect on health. The pulp and paper industry in the U.S. and Canada is responsible for roughly 10% of the industrial releases of PM<sub>2.5</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup> However, the majority of PM<sub>2.5</sub> in the air comes from non-industrial sources such as residential wood combustion, construction, and dust from unpaved roads and when these sources are taken into account, the pulp and paper industry in North America produced only about 0.5% of the total in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">Nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur oxides</a> (SO<sub>x</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) are all emitted during pulp and paper manufacturing. NOx and SOx are major contributors of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and CO<sub>2</sub>  is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> responsible for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. In 2014, the pulp and paper industry in North America was responsible for about 0.5% of the total SO<sub>x</sub> and NO<sub>x</sub> releases from industrial and non-industrial sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-:5-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_pollution">Water pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">Wastewater</a> discharges for a pulp and paper mill contains solids, nutrients and dissolved organic matter such as <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>. It also contains <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelating</a> agents and inorganic materials like <a href="/wiki/Chlorate" title="Chlorate">chlorates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> compounds. Nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus can cause or exacerbate <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of fresh water bodies such as lakes and rivers. Organic matter dissolved in fresh water, measured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_oxygen_demand" title="Biological oxygen demand">biological oxygen demand</a> (BOD), changes ecological characteristics. Wastewater may also be polluted with <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">organochlorine</a> compounds. Some of these are naturally occurring in the wood, but <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp#Chlorine_and_hypochlorite" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">chlorine bleaching</a> of the pulp produces far larger amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-can-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada, the pulp and paper industry released 5% of the total industrial waste disposed of to water in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 2014, 97.5%, 99.9% and 99.8% of effluent samples from pulp and paper mills met regulatory requirements for toxicity tests on fish, biochemical oxygen demand, and total suspended solids, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pulp and paper industry is also associated with important emissions of heavy metals. In Canada, for example, this industry is the third source of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead (Pb)</a> emissions to water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canPb_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-canPb-27">[27]</a></sup> In the U.S., the pulp and paper industry is responsible for 9% of industrial releases to water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2015, the pulp and paper sector was ranked first in the amount of toxic weighted pound equivalents (TWPE) discharged to water by industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-28">[28]</a></sup> Over 92% of this TWPE came from hydrogen sulphide, dioxin and dioxin-like compounds and manganese (Mn) and manganese compounds. Note that 7 pulp and paper facilities accounted for 80% of the hydrogen sulphide discharged and 5 facilities accounted for 93% of the dioxin discharged out of a total of 226 facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-28">[28]</a></sup> The last time the EPA reviewed Mn and Mn compounds (2006) it concluded that discharges were below treatable levels. Levels of discharge have not changed significantly since that time.
</p><p>Recycling the effluent (see <a href="/wiki/Black_liquor" title="Black liquor">black liquor</a>) and burning it, using <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> ponds and employing less damaging agents in the pulping and bleaching processes can help reduce water pollution.
</p><p>Discharges can also discolour the water leading to reduced aesthetics. This has happened with the <a href="/wiki/Tarawera_River" title="Tarawera River">Tarawera River</a> in New Zealand which subsequently became known as the "black drain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rnz_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-rnz-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colour_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-colour-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>One ton of paper pollutes 20,000 gallons of water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paper_waste">Paper waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paper waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Discarded paper and paperboard make up roughly 26% (or 67 million tons) of the 258 million tons of solid municipal waste generated in 2014 and over 14% of the 136 million tons solid municipal waste that ended up in landfills in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-7">[7]</a></sup> Paper waste, like other wastes, faces the additional hazard of toxic inks, dyes and polymers that could be potentially carcinogenic when incinerated, or commingled with groundwater via traditional burial methods such as modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">landfills</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paper_recycling" title="Paper recycling">Paper recycling</a> mitigates this impact, but not the environmental and economic impact of the energy consumed by manufacturing, transporting and burying and or reprocessing paper products.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wood_pulping_process">Wood pulping process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wood pulping process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp#_Environmental_considerations" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">Bleaching of wood pulp §  Environmental considerations</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chlorine_and_chlorine-based_materials">Chlorine and chlorine-based materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chlorine and chlorine-based materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Chlorine</a> and compounds of chlorine are used in the <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleaching of wood pulp</a>, especially chemical pulps produced by the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">kraft process</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_process" title="Sulfite process">sulfite process</a>. In the past, plants using elemental chlorine produced significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxin</a>s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-31">[31]</a></sup>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutants" title="Persistent organic pollutants">persistent and very toxic organic pollutants</a>. From the 1990s onward, the use of elemental chlorine in the delignification process was substantially reduced and replaced with ECF (Elemental Chlorine Free) and TCF (Totally Chlorine Free) bleaching processes. As a result, dioxin production was also significantly reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csiro_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-csiro-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, elemental chlorine was used in 19–20% of kraft pulp production globally, down from over 90% in 1990. 75% of kraft pulp used ECF, with the remaining 5–6% using TCF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-34">[34]</a></sup> A study based on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) data concluded that "Studies of effluents from mills that use oxygen delignification and extended delignification to produce ECF (elemental chlorine free) and TCF pulps suggest that the environmental effects of these processes are low and similar."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDFund_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-EDFund-35">[35]</a></sup>
Most TCF pulp is produced in Sweden and Finland for sale in Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csiro_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-csiro-32">[32]</a></sup> all markets with a high level of environmental awareness.  In 1999, TCF pulp represented 25% of the European market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfpa_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfpa-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>TCF bleaching, by removing chlorine from the process, reduces chlorinated organic compounds to background levels in pulp mill effluent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-37">[37]</a></sup> ECF bleaching can substantially reduce but not fully eliminate chlorinated organic compounds, including dioxins, from effluent. While modern ECF plants can achieve chlorinated organic compounds (AOX) emissions of less than 0.05 kg per tonne of pulp produced, most do not achieve this level of emissions. Within the EU, the average chlorinated organic compound emissions for ECF plants is 0.15 kg per tonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eubackground_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-eubackground-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, there has been disagreement about the comparative environmental effects of ECF and TCF bleaching. On the one hand, paper and chemical industry-funded studies have generally found that there is no environmental difference between ECF and TCF effluents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AET_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-AET-39">[39]</a></sup> On the other hand, an independent peer-reviewed study has found that, comparing conventional, ECF and TCF effluents before and after secondary treatment, “TCF effluents are the least toxic”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarkpea_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tarkpea-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Sulfur.2C_hydrogen_sulfide_and_sulfur_dioxide"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfur,_hydrogen_sulfide_and_sulfur_dioxide">Sulfur, hydrogen sulfide and sulfur dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sulfur, hydrogen sulfide and sulfur dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">Kraft process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_process" title="Sulfite process">Sulfite process</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">Sulfur</a>-based compounds are used in both the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">kraft process</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_process" title="Sulfite process">sulfite process</a> for making wood pulp. Sulfur is generally recovered, with the exception of ammonia-based sulfite processes, but some is released as sulfur dioxide during combustion of <a href="/wiki/Black_liquor" title="Black liquor">black liquor</a>, a byproduct of the kraft process, or "red liquor" from the sulfite process. <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">Sulfur dioxide</a> is of particular concern because it is water-soluble and is a major cause of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>. In 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry_in_Canada" title="Pulp and paper industry in Canada">pulp and paper industry in Canada</a> released about 60,000 tonnes of sulfur oxides (SOx) into the atmosphere, accounting for just over 4% of the total SOx emission from all Canadian industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAC_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAC-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>A modern kraft pulp mill is more than self-sufficient in its electrical generation and normally will provide a net flow of energy to the local electrical grid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Additionally, bark and wood residues are often burned in a separate power boiler to generate steam.
</p><p>Air emissions of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_mercaptan" title="Methyl mercaptan">methyl mercaptan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_disulfide" title="Dimethyl disulfide">dimethyl disulfide</a>, and other volatile sulfur compounds are the cause of the odor characteristic of pulp mills utilizing the kraft process.
Other chemicals that are released into the air and water from most paper mills include the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Bleaching mechanical pulp is not a major cause for environmental concern since most of the organic material is retained in the pulp, and the chemicals used (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_dithionite" title="Sodium dithionite">sodium dithionite</a>) produce benign byproducts (water and, eventually, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a>, respectively).
</p><p>However, the bleaching of chemical pulps has the potential to cause significant environmental damage, primarily through the release of organic materials into waterways. Pulp mills are almost always located near large bodies of water because they require substantial quantities of water for their processes. An increased public awareness of environmental issues from the 1970s and 1980s, as evidenced by the formation of organizations like <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>, influenced the pulping industry and governments to address the release of these materials into the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Environmental NGO pressure was especially intense on Swedish and Finnish pulp and paper companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Conventional bleaching using elemental chlorine produces and releases into the environment large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">chlorinated organic compounds</a>, including chlorinated <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-can-24">[24]</a></sup> Dioxins are recognized as a persistent environmental pollutant, regulated internationally by the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention_on_Persistent_Organic_Pollutants" title="Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants">Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants</a>.
</p><p>Dioxins are highly toxic, and health effects on humans include reproductive, developmental, immune and hormonal problems. They are known to be carcinogenic.  Over 90% of human exposure is through food, primarily meat, dairy, fish and shellfish, as dioxins accumulate in the food chain in the fatty tissue of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-who-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions">Greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Globally, 69% of GHGs come from the energy and transportation industries. The global print and paper industry accounts for about 1% of global carbon dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greenhouse gas emissions from the pulp and paper industry are generated from the combustion of fossil fuels required for raw material production and transportation, wastewater treatment facilities, purchased power, paper transportation, printed product transportation, disposal and recycling.
</p><p>Disposing of paper in landfill sites, and subsequent breakdown and production of methane (a potent greenhouse gas) also adds to the carbon footprint of paper products.  This is another reason why paper recycling is beneficial for the environment.  Paper recovery, instead of landfilling can reduce the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> of paper products by 15 to 25%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>At pulp and paper mills in the U.S., the GHG emission rate expressed in tons of carbon dioxide equivalents per ton of production has been reduced by 55.8% since 1972, 23.1% since 2000, and 3.9% compared to 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">[49]</a></sup> Between 2005 and 2012, U.S. pulp and paper mill purchased energy (from fossil fuels) use per ton of production has been reduced by 8.8%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada, between 2000 and 2012, direct GHG emissions declined by 56% and total energy use by 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-50">[50]</a></sup> Some of this decline is due to the contraction of the forest industry but a large part is due to reduced use of fossil fuels and increased self-generation of power from renewable biomass. Bioenergy accounted for 56% of forest industry energy use in 2012, up from 49% in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-renewable_resources">Non-renewable resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-renewable resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Clay or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> are used as fillers for some papers. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaolin" title="Kaolin">Kaolin</a> is the most commonly used clay for <a href="/wiki/Coated_paper" title="Coated paper">coated papers</a>.
</p>

<p>Some of the environmental impacts of the pulp and paper industry have been addressed and there is movement towards sustainable practices. Using wood from plantation forests addresses concerns about loss of old growth forests.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_forest_management">Sustainable forest management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sustainable forest management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cutting down trees to make forest products such as pulp and paper creates temporary or long-term environmental disturbances in forest habitats depending on how carefully the harvest is carried out. There might be impacts on plant and animal biodiversity, soil fertility and water quality.  However, sustainable forest management practices are a way of using and caring for forests so as to maintain their environmental, social and economic values and benefits over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the U.S., increasing demand for responsibly produced paper provides a financial incentive for landowners to keep their land forested and manage it in a sustainable way - rather than selling it for industrial or housing developments, a primary cause of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in the U.S., according to the U.S. Forest Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This managed land, in turn, provides a host of continuing eco-system services, from clean water, healthy soil and climate change mitigation to recreational opportunities and aesthetic beauty.
</p><p>In Canada, sustainable forest management is supported by a forest management planning process; a science-based approach to decision-making, assessment and planning as well as by regulations and policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_certification">Forest certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Forest certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Promoting and supporting forest certification and the ability to trace the origin of wood fiber helps ensure sustainable forest management and legal logging.  The forest certification systems that are currently the most used are:
</p><p>The Program for the Endorsement of Forest Certification (PEFC), in most European countries as well as growing in other regions of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sustainable Forestry Initiative (SFI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>,
</p><p>The American Tree Farm System (ATFS)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canadian Standards Association (CSA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Forest Stewardship Council (FSC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulp_bleaching">Pulp bleaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pulp bleaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The move to non-elemental chlorine for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleaching" title="Bleaching">bleaching</a> process reduced the emission of the carcinogenic <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">organochlorines</a>. <a href="/wiki/Peracetic_acid" title="Peracetic acid">Peracetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> are used in <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleaching sequences</a> in the pulp industry to produce totally chlorine free (TCF) paper.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling">Recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paper_recycling" title="Paper recycling">Paper recycling</a></div>
<p>There are three categories of paper that can be used as feedstocks for making recycled paper: mill broke, pre-consumer waste, and <a href="/wiki/Post-consumer_waste" title="Post-consumer waste">post-consumer waste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debunking_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-debunking-60">[60]</a></sup> Mill broke is paper trimmings and other paper scrap from the manufacture of paper, and is recycled internally in a <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mill</a>. Pre-consumer waste is material that was discarded before it was ready for consumer use. Post-consumer waste is material discarded after consumer use such as old magazines, old telephone directories, and residential mixed paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>One concern about recycling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">wood pulp</a> paper is that the fibers are degraded with each and after being recycled four or five times the fibers become too short and weak to be useful in making paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paperinfosheet_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-paperinfosheet-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> has found that recycling causes 35% less water pollution and 74% less air pollution than making virgin paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Pulp_mill" title="Pulp mill">Pulp mills</a> can be sources of both air and water pollution, especially if they are producing <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleached</a> pulp. Recycling paper decreases the demand for virgin pulp and thus reduces the overall amount of air and water pollution associated with paper manufacture. Recycled pulp can be bleached with the same chemicals used to bleach virgin pulp, but <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_hydrosulfite" title="Sodium hydrosulfite">sodium hydrosulfite</a> are the most common bleaching agents. Recycled pulp, or paper made from it, is known as PCF (process chlorine free) if no chlorine-containing compounds were used in the recycling process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycled_paper_and_paper_mills">Recycled paper and paper mills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Recycled paper and paper mills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recycling as an alternative to the use of landfills and recycled paper is one of the less complicated procedures in the recycling industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recycled_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recycled-65">[65]</a></sup> Although there is not a landfill crisis at this point in time, it is commonly believed that measures should to be taken in order to lower the negative impacts of landfills, for many hazardous elements are produced and spread because of this enclosure of trash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Most recycled paper is priced higher than freshly made paper, and this tends to plays a deciding factor for the consumer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Because most of the recycled pulp is purchased in an open market, virgin paper is produced cheaper with the pulp that was made by the specific paper mill. Virgin paper contains no recycled content and is made directly from the pulp of trees or cotton. Materials recovered after the initial paper manufacturing process are considered recycled paper. Because that original standard was so vague, some “recycled papers” contained only mill scraps that would have been included in virgin paper anyway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>  Standards have recently been set to prevent companies from making it seem like they were selling recycled paper. The collection and recycling industries have fixated on the scraps of paper that is thrown away by customers daily in order to increase the amount of recycled paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recycled_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Recycled-65">[65]</a></sup>  Different paper mills are structured for different types of paper, and most “recovered office paper can be sent to a deinking mill”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conservatree_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conservatree-69">[69]</a></sup> A deinking mill serves as a step in the recycling paper process.  This type of mill detaches the ink from the paper fibers, along with any other excess materials which are also removed from the remaining paper. In the deinking mill, after all of the unwanted coatings of paper are stripped, the refurbished paper is sent to the paper machine. The old scraps are now constructed into new paper at the paper machine. Many papers mills have recycled business papers by transforming the old business papers into beneficial letters and envelopes. The production process for recycled paper is more costly than the well-developed paper mills that create paper with the use of trees.  This process in making recycled paper is also much more time-consuming. However, recycled paper has a multitude of benefits from an environmental perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>  “For all the state-of-the-art technology now incorporated into modern paper mills, the industry's underlying structure is still based upon a worldview that was transformative in the 19th-century but is out-of-date as the 21st century approaches”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conservatree_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conservatree-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pulp and paper is a heavily regulated industry in North America. U.S. and Canadian regulations are summarized below.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_and_water_pollution">Air and water pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Air and water pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>EPA first issued national wastewater standards, known as <a href="/wiki/Effluent_guidelines" title="Effluent guidelines">effluent guidelines</a>, for pulp and paper mills in 1974, pursuant to the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>. The agency established numeric limitations for several <a href="/wiki/Conventional_pollutant" title="Conventional pollutant">conventional pollutants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In 1982, EPA amended the regulations with numeric limitations for <a href="/wiki/Pentachlorophenol" title="Pentachlorophenol">pentachlorophenol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trichlorophenol" title="Trichlorophenol">trichlorophenol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Effluent limitations are implemented in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Pollutant_Discharge_Elimination_System" title="National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System">National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System</a> (NPDES) permits, which are renewed every five years. (<i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/United_States_regulation_of_point_source_water_pollution" title="United States regulation of point source water pollution">United States regulation of point source water pollution</a>.)
</p><p>EPA's 1998 "Cluster Rule" (CR) addressed additional toxic wastewater pollutants, and regulated hazardous air pollutant emissions as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-cluster_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-cluster-73">[73]</a></sup> Because both air and water regulations were addressed in the same <a href="/wiki/Rulemaking" title="Rulemaking">rulemaking</a> action, EPA made it possible for pulp and paper mills to select the best combination of air and water pollution prevention technologies rather than addressing one at a time. Some of the requirements and technologies were designed to reduce toxic air pollutants also reduced toxic wastewater pollutants and <i>vice versa</i>. EPA's intent in promulgating the CR was to provide a coordinated set of regulatory requirements, thereby improving clarity for industry as it worked to achieve compliance, and achieving a greater level of pollution prevention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gray-wb_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-gray-wb-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>The air emission regulations in the CR, a component of the <a href="/wiki/National_Emissions_Standards_for_Hazardous_Air_Pollutants" title="National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants">National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants</a> (NESHAP) under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, became effective in 2001. The regulations, also known as "Maximum Achievable Control Technology" (MACT) regulations, apply to mills that use chemical pulping and call for hazardous air pollutants to be reduced by 59% and for volatile organic carbon and particulate matter to be reduced by 49% and 37%, respectively. The wastewater regulations in the CR apply to mills that combine chlorine bleaching with kraft chemical pulping and aim to reduce <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> discharges by 96%, 96%, and 99%, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gray-wb_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-gray-wb-74">[74]</a></sup> EPA added numeric effluent limitations for 12 chlorinated phenolic pollutants and <a href="/wiki/Adsorbable_organic_halides" title="Adsorbable organic halides">adsorbable organic halides</a> (AOX). The regulation also requires the industry to implement <a href="/wiki/Best_management_practice_for_water_pollution" title="Best management practice for water pollution">best management practices</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Process_control" title="Process control">process control</a> monitoring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-cluster_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-cluster-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>All air emissions in the U.S. are regulated at the federal level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The Clean Air Act establishes <a href="/wiki/National_Ambient_Air_Quality_Standards" title="National Ambient Air Quality Standards">National Ambient Air Quality Standards</a> (NAAQS) for pollutants harmful to public health and the environment. Standards have been set for six principal pollutants: <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> (NO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (O<sub>3</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a> (PM) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> EPA revised the NESHAP regulations for the pulp and paper industry in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_management">Forest management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Forest management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Laws, regulations, and policies directing forest management on U.S. federal lands are well-documented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lacey_Act_of_1900" title="Lacey Act of 1900">Lacey Act of 1900</a> provides sanctions for <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Canada, federal and provincial environmental law is the primary source of regulations for pulp and paper mills. The following three listed Federal regulations are related to emissions to water:
</p>
<ol><li>Pulp and Paper Effluent Regulations: sets standards for the level of acute lethality to fish, biochemical oxygen demand and total suspended solids. Mills are also required to conduct environmental effects monitoring to determine the impact of their effluents on receiving waters and investigate the causes of, and solutions for, environmental effects associated with mill effluent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pulp and Paper Mill Effluent Chlorinated Dioxins and Furans Regulations: issued under the Canadian Environmental Protection Act and applies to polychlorinated dibenzo-para-dioxins and polychlorinated dibenzofurans in mill effluents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pulp and Paper Mill Defoamer and Wood Chip Regulation: regulates use of defoamers containing dibenzofuran or dibenzo-para-dioxin at pulp and paper mills using a chlorine bleaching process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>There are also regulations in place to control the releases of contaminants into the air, including particulate matter and ground-level ozone and these include the Canadian Ambient Air Quality Standards Regulations. At the federal level,  Environment and Climate Change Canada (ECCC) has a legislated, publicly accessible inventory of pollutant releases to air, water and land, as well as disposals and recycling, namely the National Pollutant Release Inventory, which companies are required to report each year. Operators of facilities that meet the reporting criteria are required to report facility greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to ECCC each year.
</p><p>Some provinces have their own regulatory frameworks to manage air quality such as the Ontario provincial standards, as well as to control GHG emissions including the British Columbia Carbon Tax, Alberta Climate Leadership Plan, and the Ontario and Quebec Cap and Trade Systems.
</p><p>In Canada, where 94% of the country’s forests are on public land, a framework of federal, provincial and territorial laws, regulations and policies enforces and guides sustainable forest management practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>“The Environmental, Health, and Safety (EHS) Guidelines are technical reference documents with general and industry specific examples of Good International Industry Practice (GIIP).”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
In simpler terms, the EHS is what helps develop the federal regulations on industries and companies that require factories that have the potential to cause a great amount of harm to society and the environment. These Guidelines for the <a href="/wiki/Environment,_health_and_safety" title="Environment, health and safety">environment, health and safety</a> list out the specific rules for the paper mill industries that explains what they need to follow in order to limit the pollution that is consequently distributed and by the mills.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_pulp_mills">Mechanical pulp mills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Mechanical pulp mills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Pulp_mill#The_mill" title="Pulp mill">Pulp mill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp#Bleaching_mechanical_pulps" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">Bleaching mechanical pulps</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">Wood pulp</a> produced primarily by grinding wood is known as "mechanical pulp" and is used mainly for <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">newsprint</a>. These mechanical processes use fewer chemicals than either kraft or sulfite mills. The primary source of pollution from these mills is organic material such as resin acids released from the wood when it is processed. Mechanical wood pulp is "<a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp#Bleaching_mechanical_pulps" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">brightened</a>," as opposed to bleached, using less toxic chemicals than are needed for chemical pulps.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inks">Inks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_paper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Inks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three main issues with the environmental impact of printing <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inks</a> is the use of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a> and non-renewable oils. Standards for the amount of heavy metals in ink have been set by some regulatory bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> There is a trend toward using <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oils</a> rather than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleum_oil" title="Petroleum oil">petroleum oils</a> in recent years due to a demand for better <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deinking" title="Deinking">Deinking</a> recycled paper pulp results in a waste slurry which may go to <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a>. De-inking at Cross Pointe's Miami, Ohio mill in the United States results in sludge weighing 22% of the weight of wastepaper recycled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s federal regulations for inks in the United States governed the use of toxic metals such as lead, arsenic, selenium, mercury, cadmium and hexavalent chromium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
