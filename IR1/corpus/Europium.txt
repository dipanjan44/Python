<p><b>Europium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Eu</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 63. It was isolated in 1901 and is named after the continent of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is a moderately hard, silvery <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> which readily oxidizes in air and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. Being a typical member of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, europium usually assumes the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> +3, but the oxidation state +2 is also common. All europium compounds with oxidation state +2 are slightly <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a>. Europium has no significant biological role and is relatively non-toxic compared to other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>. Most applications of europium exploit the phosphorescence of europium compounds. Europium is one of the least abundant elements in the universe; only about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6992500000000000000♠</span>5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−8</sup></span>% of all matter in the universe is europium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eu-Block.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1875" data-file-width="2827" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eu-Block.jpg/220px-Eu-Block.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eu-Block.jpg/330px-Eu-Block.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Eu-Block.jpg/440px-Eu-Block.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eu-Block.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>About 300 g of dendritic sublimated 99.998% pure europium handled in a glove box</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1374" data-file-width="1724" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg/220px-Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg/330px-Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg/440px-Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Europium_on_air_oxidized.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxidized europium, coated with yellow europium(II) carbonate</div></div></div>
<p>Europium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> metal with a hardness similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. It crystallizes in a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">body-centered cubic</a> lattice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-6">[6]</a></sup> Some properties of europium are strongly influenced by its half-filled <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell" title="Electron shell">electron shell</a>. Europium has the second lowest melting point and the lowest density of all lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Europium becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a> when it is cooled below 1.8 K and compressed to above 80 GPa. This is because europium is divalent in the metallic state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and is converted into the trivalent state by the applied pressure. In the divalent state, the strong local magnetic moment (J = <sup>7</sup>/<sub>2</sub>) suppresses the superconductivity, which is induced by eliminating this local moment (J = 0 in Eu<sup>3+</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europium is the most reactive <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth element</a>. It rapidly oxidizes in air, so that bulk oxidation of a centimeter-sized sample occurs within several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Its reactivity with water is comparable to that of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, and the reaction is
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Eu + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 Eu(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Because of the high reactivity, samples of solid europium rarely have the shiny appearance of the fresh metal, even when coated with a protective layer of mineral oil. Europium ignites in air at 150 to 180 °C to form <a href="/wiki/Europium(III)_oxide" title="Europium(III) oxide">europium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Eu + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Eu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Europium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form pale pink solutions of the hydrated Eu(III), which exist as a nonahydrate:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Eu + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + 18 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 [Eu(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 3 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h4><span id="Eu.28II.29_vs._Eu.28III.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Eu(II)_vs._Eu(III)">Eu(II) vs. Eu(III)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eu(II) vs. Eu(III)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although usually trivalent, europium readily forms divalent compounds. This behavior is unusual to most <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, which almost exclusively form compounds with an oxidation state of +3. The +2 state has an <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configuration</a> 4<i>f</i><sup>7</sup> because the half-filled <i>f</i>-shell gives more stability. In terms of size and <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination number</a>, europium(II) and <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>(II) are similar. For example, the sulfates of both barium and europium(II) are also highly insoluble in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Divalent europium is a mild reducing agent, oxidizing in air to form Eu(III) compounds. In anaerobic, and particularly geothermal conditions, the divalent form is sufficiently stable that it tends to be incorporated into minerals of calcium and the other alkaline earths. This ion-exchange process is the basis of the "negative <a href="/wiki/Europium_anomaly" title="Europium anomaly">europium anomaly</a>", the low europium content in many lanthanide minerals such as monazite, relative to the <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondritic</a> abundance. Bastnäsite tends to show less of a negative europium anomaly than does monazite, and hence is the major source of europium today. The development of easy methods to separate divalent europium from the other (trivalent) lanthanides made europium accessible even when present in low concentration, as it usually is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_europium" title="Isotopes of europium">Isotopes of europium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring europium is composed of 2 <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, <sup>151</sup>Eu and <sup>153</sup>Eu, with <sup>153</sup>Eu being the most abundant (52.2% <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">natural abundance</a>). While <sup>153</sup>Eu is stable, <sup>151</sup>Eu was recently found to be unstable to <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> with <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7026157788000000000♠</span>5<span style="margin-left:0.3em;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">+11<br/>−3</span></span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup> <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">years</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> giving about 1 alpha decay per two minutes in every kilogram of natural europium. This value is in reasonable agreement with theoretical predictions. Besides the natural radioisotope <sup>151</sup>Eu, 35 artificial radioisotopes have been characterized, the most stable being <sup>150</sup>Eu with a half-life of 36.9 years, <sup>152</sup>Eu with a half-life of 13.516 years, and <sup>154</sup>Eu with a half-life of 8.593 years. All the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives shorter than 4.7612 years, and the majority of these have half-lives shorter than 12.2 seconds. This element also has 8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, with the most stable being <sup>150m</sup>Eu (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=12.8 hours), <sup>152m1</sup>Eu (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=9.3116 hours) and <sup>152m2</sup>Eu (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=96 minutes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucleonica_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nucleonica-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> for isotopes lighter than <sup>153</sup>Eu is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, and the primary mode for heavier isotopes is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_minus_decay" title="Beta minus decay">beta minus decay</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> before <sup>153</sup>Eu are isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> (Sm) and the primary products after are isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> (Gd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucleonica_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nucleonica-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Europium is produced by nuclear fission, but the <a href="/wiki/Fission_product_yield" title="Fission product yield">fission product yields</a> of europium isotopes are low near the top of the mass range for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_products" title="Fission products">fission products</a>.
</p><p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, many isotopes, especially isotopes with odd mass numbers and neutron-poor isotopes like <sup>152</sup>Eu, have high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_cross-section" title="Neutron cross-section">cross sections</a> for <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a>, often high enough to be <a href="/wiki/Neutron_poison" title="Neutron poison">neutron poisons</a>.
</p><p><sup>151</sup>Eu is the <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarium-151" title="Samarium-151">samarium-151</a>, but since this has a long decay half-life and short mean time to neutron absorption, most <sup>151</sup>Sm instead ends up as <sup>152</sup>Sm.
</p><p><sup>152</sup>Eu (half-life 13.516 years) and <sup>154</sup>Eu (half-life 8.593 years) cannot be beta decay products because <sup>152</sup>Sm and <sup>154</sup>Sm are non-radioactive, but <sup>154</sup>Eu is the only long-lived "shielded" <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a>, other than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesium-134" title="Caesium-134"><sup>134</sup>Cs</a>, to have a fission yield of more than 2.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> fissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A larger amount of <sup>154</sup>Eu is produced by <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">neutron activation</a> of a significant portion of the non-radioactive <sup>153</sup>Eu; however, much of this is further converted to <sup>155</sup>Eu.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eu-155" title="Eu-155"><sup>155</sup>Eu</a> (half-life 4.7612 years) has a fission yield of 330 parts per million (ppm) for <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutrons</a>; most of it is transmuted to non-radioactive and nonabsorptive gadolinium-156 by the end of fuel <a href="/wiki/Burnup" title="Burnup">burnup</a>.
</p><p>Overall, europium is overshadowed by <a href="/wiki/Caesium-137" title="Caesium-137">caesium-137</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90">strontium-90</a> as a radiation hazard, and by samarium and others as a neutron poison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Mosambik,_O-Afrika.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1150" data-file-width="1300" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/220px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/330px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/440px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Mosambik,_O-Afrika.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monazite</div></div></div>
<p>Europium is not found in nature as a free element. Many minerals contain europium, with the most important sources being <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loparite-(Ce)" title="Loparite-(Ce)">loparite-(Ce)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-23">[23]</a></sup> No europium-dominant minerals are known yet, despite of a single find of a tiny possible Eu–O or Eu–O–C system phase in the Moon's regolith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Depletion or enrichment of europium in minerals relative to other rare-earth elements is known as the <a href="/wiki/Europium_anomaly" title="Europium anomaly">europium anomaly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Europium is commonly included in trace element studies in <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a> to understand the processes that form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous_rocks" title="Igneous rocks">igneous rocks</a> (rocks that cooled from <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>). The nature of the europium anomaly found helps reconstruct the relationships within a suite of igneous rocks.
</p><p>Divalent europium (Eu<sup>2+</sup>) in small amounts is the activator of the bright blue <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> of some samples of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> (CaF<sub>2</sub>). The reduction from Eu<sup>3+</sup> to Eu<sup>2+</sup> is induced by irradiation with energetic particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The most outstanding examples of this originated around <a href="/wiki/Weardale" title="Weardale">Weardale</a> and adjacent parts of northern England; it was the fluorite found here that <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> was named after in 1852, although it was not until much later that europium was determined to be the cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Europium is associated with the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_elements" title="Rare-earth elements">rare-earth elements</a> and is, therefore, mined together with them. Separation of the rare-earth elements is a step in the later processing. Rare-earth elements are found in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loparite-(Ce)" title="Loparite-(Ce)">loparite-(Ce)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> in mineable quantities. Bastnäsite is a group of related fluorocarbonates, Ln(CO<sub>3</sub>)(F,OH). Monazite is a group of related of orthophosphate minerals <span class="chemf nowrap">LnPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (Ln denotes a mixture of all the lanthanides except <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>), loparite-(Ce) is an oxide, and xenotime is an orthophosphate (Y,Yb,Er,...)PO<sub>4</sub>. Monazite also contains <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>, which complicates handling because thorium and its decay products are radioactive. For the extraction from the ore and the isolation of individual lanthanides, several methods have been developed. The choice of method is based on the concentration and composition of the ore and on the distribution of the individual lanthanides in the resulting concentrate. Roasting the ore and subsequent acidic and basic leaching is used mostly to produce a concentrate of lanthanides. If cerium is the dominant lanthanide, then it is converted from cerium(III) to cerium(IV) and then precipitated. Further separation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvent_extraction" title="Solvent extraction">solvent extractions</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_exchange_chromatography" title="Ion exchange chromatography">ion exchange chromatography</a> yields a fraction which is enriched in europium. This fraction is reduced with zinc, zinc/amalgam, electrolysis or other methods converting the europium(III) to europium(II). Europium(II) reacts in a way similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a> and therefore it can be precipitated as carbonate or is co-precipitated with barium sulfate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuptaL_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuptaL-32">[32]</a></sup> Europium metal is available through the electrolysis of a mixture of molten EuCl<sub>3</sub> and NaCl (or CaCl<sub>2</sub>) in a graphite cell, which serves as cathode, using graphite as anode. The other product is <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuptaL_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-GuptaL-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCoy_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCoy-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>A few large deposits produce or produced a significant amount of the world production. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayan_Obo" title="Bayan Obo">Bayan Obo</a> iron ore deposit contains significant amounts of bastnäsite and monazite and is, with an estimated 36 million tonnes of rare-earth element oxides, the largest known deposit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The mining operations at the Bayan Obo deposit made China the largest supplier of rare-earth elements in the 1990s. Only 0.2% of the rare-earth element content is europium. The second large source for rare-earth elements between 1965 and its closure in the late 1990s was the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Pass_rare_earth_mine" title="Mountain Pass rare earth mine">Mountain Pass rare earth mine</a>. The bastnäsite mined there is especially rich in the light rare-earth elements (La-Gd, Sc, and Y) and contains only 0.1% of europium. Another large source for rare-earth elements is the loparite found on the Kola peninsula. It contains besides niobium, tantalum and titanium up to 30% rare-earth elements and is the largest source for these elements in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirk_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kirk-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Europium_compounds" title="Category:Europium compounds">Category:Europium compounds</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eu-sulfate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="764" data-file-width="723" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Eu-sulfate.jpg/200px-Eu-sulfate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Eu-sulfate.jpg/300px-Eu-sulfate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Eu-sulfate.jpg/400px-Eu-sulfate.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eu-sulfate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Europium sulfate, Eu<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="704" data-file-width="704" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg/200px-Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg/300px-Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg/400px-Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eu-sulfate-luminescence.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Europium sulfate fluorescing red under ultraviolet light</div></div></div>
<p>Europium compounds tend to exist trivalent oxidation state under most conditions.  Commonly these compounds feature Eu(III) bound by 6–9 oxygenic ligands, typically water.  These compounds, the chlorides, sulfates, nitrates, are soluble in water or polar organic solvent.  Lipophilic europium complexes often feature acetylacetonate-like ligands, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eufod" title="Eufod">Eufod</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europium metal reacts with all the halogens:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Eu + 3 X<sub>2</sub> → 2 EuX<sub>3</sub> (X = F, Cl, Br, I)</dd></dl>
<p>This route gives white europium(III) fluoride (EuF<sub>3</sub>), yellow <a href="/wiki/Europium(III)_chloride" title="Europium(III) chloride">europium(III) chloride</a> (EuCl<sub>3</sub>), gray <a href="/wiki/Europium(III)_bromide" title="Europium(III) bromide">europium(III) bromide</a> (EuBr<sub>3</sub>), and colorless europium(III) iodide (EuI<sub>3</sub>). Europium also forms the corresponding dihalides: yellow-green europium(II) fluoride (EuF<sub>2</sub>), colorless europium(II) chloride (EuCl<sub>2</sub>), colorless <a href="/wiki/Europium(II)_bromide" title="Europium(II) bromide">europium(II) bromide</a> (EuBr<sub>2</sub>), and green europium(II) iodide (EuI<sub>2</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chalcogenides_and_pnictides">Chalcogenides and pnictides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chalcogenides and pnictides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Europium forms stable compounds with all of the chalcogens, but the heavier chalcogens (S, Se, and Te) stabilize the lower oxidation state. Three <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> are known: europium(II) oxide (EuO), <a href="/wiki/Europium(III)_oxide" title="Europium(III) oxide">europium(III) oxide</a> (Eu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-valency" title="Mixed-valency">mixed-valence</a> oxide Eu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, consisting of both Eu(II) and Eu(III).
Otherwise, the main chalcogenides are <a href="/wiki/Europium(II)_sulfide" title="Europium(II) sulfide">europium(II) sulfide</a> (EuS), europium(II) selenide (EuSe) and europium(II) telluride (EuTe): all three of these are black solids. EuS is prepared by sulfiding the oxide at temperatures sufficiently high to decompose the Eu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Eu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>S → 2 EuS + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O + S</dd></dl>
<p>The main <a href="/wiki/Nitride" title="Nitride">nitride</a> is europium(III) nitride (EuN).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although europium is present in most of the minerals containing the other rare elements, due to the difficulties in separating the elements it was not until the late 1800s that the element was isolated. <a href="/wiki/William_Crookes" title="William Crookes">William Crookes</a> observed the phosphorescent spectra of the rare elements including those eventually assigned to europium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Europium was first found in 1892 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a>, who obtained basic fractions from samarium-gadolinium concentrates which had spectral lines not accounted for by samarium or <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>. However, the discovery of europium is generally credited to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne-Anatole_Demar%C3%A7ay" title="Eugène-Anatole Demarçay">Eugène-Anatole Demarçay</a>, who suspected samples of the recently discovered element samarium were contaminated with an unknown element in 1896 and who was able to isolate it in 1901; he then named it <i>europium</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>When the europium-doped <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_orthovanadate" title="Yttrium orthovanadate">yttrium orthovanadate</a> red phosphor was discovered in the early 1960s, and understood to be about to cause a revolution in the color television industry, there was a scramble for the limited supply of europium on hand among the monazite processors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sri1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sri1-44">[44]</a></sup> as the typical europium content in monazite is about 0.05%. However, the Molycorp <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> deposit at the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Pass_rare_earth_mine" title="Mountain Pass rare earth mine">Mountain Pass rare earth mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, whose lanthanides had an unusually high europium content of 0.1%, was about to come on-line and provide sufficient europium to sustain the industry. Prior to europium, the color-TV red phosphor was very weak, and the other phosphor colors had to be muted, to maintain color balance. With the brilliant red europium phosphor, it was no longer necessary to mute the other colors, and a much brighter color TV picture was the result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sri1_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sri1-44">[44]</a></sup> Europium has continued to be in use in the TV industry ever since as well as in computer monitors. Californian bastnäsite now faces stiff competition from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayan_Obo" title="Bayan Obo">Bayan Obo</a>, China, with an even "richer" europium content of 0.2%.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Spedding" title="Frank Spedding">Frank Spedding</a>, celebrated for his development of the ion-exchange technology that revolutionized the rare-earth industry in the mid-1950s, once related the story of how<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> he was lecturing on the rare earths in the 1930s, when an elderly gentleman approached him with an offer of a gift of several pounds of europium oxide. This was an unheard-of quantity at the time, and Spedding did not take the man seriously. However, a package duly arrived in the mail, containing several pounds of genuine europium oxide. The elderly gentleman had turned out to be <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Newby_McCoy" title="Herbert Newby McCoy">Herbert Newby McCoy</a>, who had developed a famous method of europium purification involving redox chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCoy_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCoy-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aperture_Grille.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="353" data-file-width="353" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Aperture_Grille.jpg/220px-Aperture_Grille.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Aperture_Grille.jpg/330px-Aperture_Grille.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Aperture_Grille.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aperture_Grille.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Europium is one of the elements involved in emitting red light in CRT televisions.</div></div></div>
<p>Relative to most other elements, commercial applications for europium are few and rather specialized. Almost invariably, its phosphorescence is exploited, either in the +2 or +3 oxidation state.
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in some types of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> in <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> and other optoelectronic devices. Europium oxide (Eu<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is widely used as a red <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">television sets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamps" title="Fluorescent lamps">fluorescent lamps</a>, and as an activator for <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>-based phosphors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caro_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Color TV screens contain between 0.5 and 1 g of europium oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-49">[49]</a></sup> Whereas trivalent europium gives red phosphors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> the luminescence of divalent europium depends strongly on the composition of the host structure. UV to deep red luminescence can be achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The two classes of europium-based phosphor (red and blue), combined with the yellow/green <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a> phosphors give "white" light, the color temperature of which can be varied by altering the proportion or specific composition of the individual phosphors. This phosphor system is typically encountered in helical fluorescent light bulbs. Combining the same three classes is one way to make trichromatic systems in TV and computer screens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caro_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Caro-47">[47]</a></sup> Europium is also used in the manufacture of fluorescent glass. One of the more common persistent after-glow phosphors besides copper-doped zinc sulfide is europium-doped <a href="/wiki/Strontium_aluminate" title="Strontium aluminate">strontium aluminate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Europium fluorescence is used to interrogate biomolecular interactions in drug-discovery screens. It is also used in the anti-counterfeiting phosphors in <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> banknotes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>An application that has almost fallen out of use with the introduction of affordable superconducting magnets is the use of europium complexes, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eufod" title="Eufod">Eu(fod)<sub>3</sub></a>, as shift reagents in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a> spectroscopy. <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">Chiral</a> shift reagents, such as Eu(hfc)<sub>3</sub>, are still used to determine <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomeric</a> purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent (2015) application of europium is in quantum memory chips which can reliably store information for days at a time; these could allow sensitive quantum data to be stored to a hard disk-like device and shipped around the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>There are no clear indications that europium is particularly toxic compared to other <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>. Europium chloride, nitrate and oxide have been tested for toxicity: europium chloride shows an acute intraperitoneal LD<sub>50</sub> toxicity of 550 mg/kg and the acute oral  LD<sub>50</sub> toxicity is 5000 mg/kg. Europium nitrate shows a slightly higher intraperitoneal LD<sub>50</sub> toxicity of 320 mg/kg, while the oral toxicity is above 5000 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The metal dust presents a fire and explosion hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
