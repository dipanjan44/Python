<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide laser</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="1204" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg/400px-Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg/600px-Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg/800px-Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Dioxide_Laser_At_The_Laser_Effects_Test_Facility.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A test target bursts into flame upon irradiation by a continuous wave kilowatt-level carbon dioxide laser.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>carbon dioxide laser</b> (<b>CO<sub>2</sub> laser</b>) was one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Gas_laser" title="Gas laser">gas lasers</a> to be developed. It was invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kumar_Patel" title="Kumar Patel">Kumar Patel</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> in 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is still one of the most useful. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> lasers are the highest-power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuous_wave_laser" title="Continuous wave laser">continuous wave lasers</a> that are currently available. They are also quite efficient: the ratio of output power to <a href="/wiki/Laser_pumping" title="Laser pumping">pump</a> power can be as large as 20%.
The CO<sub>2</sub> laser produces a beam of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_light" title="Infrared light">infrared light</a> with the principal <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> bands centering on 9.4 and 10.6 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometers</a> (μm).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amplification">Amplification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Amplification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The <a href="/wiki/Active_laser_medium" title="Active laser medium">active laser medium</a> (laser gain/<a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplification</a> medium) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_discharge" title="Gas discharge">gas discharge</a> which is air- or water-cooled, depending on the power being applied. The filling gas within the discharge tube consists of around 10–20% <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), around 10–20% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), a few percent <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) and/or <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a> (Xe) (usually only used in a sealed tube), and the remainder of the gas mixture <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (He).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The specific proportions vary according to the particular laser.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Population_inversion" title="Population inversion">population inversion</a> in the laser is achieved by the following sequence: <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> impact excites vibrational motion of the nitrogen. Because nitrogen is a <a href="/wiki/Homonuclear_molecule" title="Homonuclear molecule">homonuclear molecule</a>, it cannot lose this energy by <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> emission, and its excited vibrational levels are therefore <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a> and relatively long-lived. Collisional energy transfer between the nitrogen and the carbon dioxide molecule causes vibrational excitation of the carbon dioxide, with sufficient efficiency to lead to the desired population inversion necessary for laser operation. The nitrogen molecules are left in a lower excited state. Their transition to ground state takes place by collision with cold helium atoms. The resulting hot helium atoms must be cooled in order to sustain the ability to produce a population inversion in the carbon dioxide molecules. In sealed lasers, this takes place as the helium atoms strike the walls of the container. In flow-through lasers, a continuous stream of CO<sub>2</sub> and nitrogen is excited by the plasma discharge and the hot gas mixture is exhausted from the resonator by pumps.
</p><p>Because the energy levels of molecular vibration and rotation are similar, the photons emitted due to transition between these levels have comparatively low energy, and longer wavelength, than visible and near-infrared light. The 9-12 μm wavelength of CO<sub>2</sub> lasers is useful because it falls into an important window for atmospheric transmission (up to 80% atmospheric transmission at this wavelength), and because many natural and synthetic materials have strong characteristic absorptions in this range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laserworld_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-laserworld-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Laser_construction" title="Laser construction">Laser construction</a></div>
<p>Because CO<sub>2</sub> lasers operate in the infrared, special materials are necessary for their construction. Typically, the <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a> are silvered, while windows and lenses are made of either <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc_selenide" title="Zinc selenide">zinc selenide</a>. For high power applications, gold mirrors and zinc selenide windows and lenses are preferred. There are also <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> windows and lenses in use. Diamond windows are extremely expensive, but their high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> and hardness make them useful in high-power applications and in dirty environments. Optical elements made of diamond can even be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_blasting" title="Sand blasting">sand blasted</a> without losing their optical properties. Historically, lenses and windows were made out of salt (either <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>). While the material was inexpensive, the lenses and windows degraded slowly with exposure to atmospheric moisture.
</p><p>The most basic form of a CO<sub>2</sub> laser consists of a gas discharge (with a mix close to that specified above) with a total <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflector</a> at one end, and an <a href="/wiki/Output_coupler" title="Output coupler">output coupler</a> (a partially reflecting mirror) at the output end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The CO<sub>2</sub> laser can be constructed to have continuous wave (CW) powers between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliwatt" title="Milliwatt">milliwatts</a> (mW) and hundreds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a> (kW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is also very easy to actively <a href="/wiki/Q-switching" title="Q-switching">Q-switch</a> a CO<sub>2</sub> laser by means of a rotating mirror or an electro-optic switch, giving rise to Q-switched peak powers of up to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">gigawatts</a> (GW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the laser transitions are actually on vibration-rotation bands of a linear triatomic molecule, the rotational structure of the P and R bands can be selected by a tuning element in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_cavity" title="Laser cavity">laser cavity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dispersive_prism" title="Dispersive prism">Prisms</a> are not practical as tuning elements because most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium_(optics)" title="Medium (optics)">media</a> that transmit in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-infrared" title="Mid-infrared">mid-infrared</a> absorb or scatter some of the light, so the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> tuning element is almost always a <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">diffraction grating</a>. By rotating the diffraction grating, a particular rotational line of the vibrational transition can be selected. The finest frequency selection may also be obtained through the use of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etalon" title="Etalon">etalon</a>. In practice, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopic_substitution" title="Isotopic substitution">isotopic substitution</a>, this means that a continuous comb of frequencies separated by around 1 cm<sup>−1</sup> (30 GHz) can be used that extend from 880 to 1090 cm<sup>−1</sup>. Such "line-tuneable" carbon dioxide lasers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> are principally of interest in research applications.
</p><p>One facet of output wavelength is due to the particular isotopes contained in the carbon dioxide molecule. Since various combinations of isotopes can produce wavelengths from 8.98 to 10.2 μm, precision laser fabricators must take this factor into account when selecting the gas for their products. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sharplan_40C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2490" data-file-width="1746" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Sharplan_40C.jpg/220px-Sharplan_40C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Sharplan_40C.jpg/330px-Sharplan_40C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Sharplan_40C.jpg/440px-Sharplan_40C.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sharplan_40C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A medical CO<sub>2</sub> laser</div></div></div>
<h3><span id="Industrial_.28cutting_and_welding.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_(cutting_and_welding)">Industrial (cutting and welding)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial (cutting and welding)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of the high power levels available (combined with reasonable cost for the laser), CO<sub>2</sub> lasers are frequently used in industrial applications for <a href="/wiki/Laser_cutting" title="Laser cutting">cutting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laser_beam_welding" title="Laser beam welding">welding</a>, while lower power level lasers are used for engraving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is also used in the additive manufacturing process of <a href="/wiki/Selective_laser_sintering" title="Selective laser sintering">Selective laser sintering</a> (SLS).
</p>
<h3><span id="Medical_.28soft-tissue_surgery.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_(soft-tissue_surgery)">Medical (soft-tissue surgery)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medical (soft-tissue surgery)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide lasers have become useful in surgical procedures because water (which makes up most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">biological tissue</a>) absorbs this frequency of light very well. Some examples of medical uses are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_applications#Medical" title="Laser applications">laser surgery</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_resurfacing" title="Laser resurfacing">skin resurfacing</a> ("laser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facelift" title="Facelift">facelifts</a>", which essentially consist of vaporizing the skin to promote collagen formation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> lasers may be used to treat certain skin conditions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hirsuties_papillaris_genitalis" title="Hirsuties papillaris genitalis">hirsuties papillaris genitalis</a> by removing bumps or podules. CO<sub>2</sub> lasers can be used to remove vocal fold lesions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal-fold_cyst" title="Vocal-fold cyst">vocal fold cysts</a>. Researchers in Israel are experimenting with using CO<sub>2</sub> lasers to weld human tissue, as an alternative to traditional <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">sutures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 10.6 μm CO<sub>2</sub> laser remains the best <a href="/wiki/Laser_surgery" title="Laser surgery">surgical laser</a> for the soft tissue where both cutting and <a href="/wiki/Hemostasis" title="Hemostasis">hemostasis</a> are achieved photo-thermally (radiantly).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> lasers can be used in place of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_scalpel" title="Laser scalpel">scalpel</a> for most procedures, and are even used in places a scalpel would not be used, in delicate areas where mechanical trauma could damage the surgical site. CO<sub>2</sub> lasers are the best suited for <a href="/wiki/Soft_tissue" title="Soft tissue">soft tissue</a> procedures in human and animal specialties, as compared to other laser <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>. Advantages include less bleeding, shorter surgery time, less risk of infection, and less post-op swelling. Applications include <a href="/wiki/Gynaecology" title="Gynaecology">gynecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oral_and_maxillofacial_surgery" title="Oral and maxillofacial surgery">oral and maxillofacial surgery</a>, and many others.
</p><p>The CO<sub>2</sub> laser at the 9.25 - 9.6 μm wavelength is sometimes used in dentistry for hard-tissue ablation. The hard-tissue is ablated at temperatures as high as 5,000° C, producing bright thermal radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_laser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The common plastic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poly_(methyl_methacrylate)" title="Poly (methyl methacrylate)">poly (methyl methacrylate)</a> (PMMA) absorbs IR light in the 2.8–25 µm wavelength band, so CO<sub>2</sub> lasers have been used in recent years for fabricating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfluidic_device" title="Microfluidic device">microfluidic devices</a> from it, with channel widths of a few hundred micrometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is quite transparent to infrared light, CO<sub>2</sub> lasers are also used for military <a href="/wiki/Rangefinder" title="Rangefinder">rangefinding</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIDAR" title="LIDAR">LIDAR</a> techniques.
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> lasers are used in <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silex_process" title="Silex process">Silex process</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enrichment_of_uranium" title="Enrichment of uranium">enrich</a> uranium.
</p><p>The Soviet <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> was designed to use a megawatt carbon-dioxide laser as an in-orbit weapon to destroy <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">SDI satellites</a>.
</p>
