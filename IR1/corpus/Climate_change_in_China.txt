<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in China</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="1371" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png/220px-Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png/330px-Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png/440px-Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_climate_agreement_chart.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green climate funds contributions of Paris Climate Agreement by county</div></div></div>
<p>China, since 2006, keeps emitting more CO<sub>2</sub> than any other country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> China ratified the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_parties_to_the_Kyoto_Protocol" title="List of parties to the Kyoto Protocol">non-Annex B party without binding targets</a>, and ratified the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> to fight <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">[6]</a></sup> As the world's largest coal producer and consumer country, China worked hard to change energy structure and experienced a decrease in coal consumption since 2013 to 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7">[7]</a></sup> However, China, the United States and India, the three biggest coal users, have increased coal mining in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-8">[8]</a></sup> The Chinese government has implemented several policies to control coal consumption, and boosted the usage of natural gas and electricity. Looking ahead, the construction and manufacturing industries of China will give way to the service industry, and the Chinese government will not set a higher goal for economic growth in 2018, thus the coal consumption may not experience continuous growth in the next few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The annual CO<small>2</small> emissions of China was 10150.82 million tonnes in 2016, followed by the United States (5311.69 million tonnes) and India (2430.8 million tonnes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-9">[9]</a></sup> The energy structure and human activities caused global warming and climate change, and China suffered from negative effects in agriculture, forest, water resource etc.
</p><p>China is implementing some policies to mitigate the bad effects of climate change, most of which are aiming to constrain coal consumption. The Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) of China set goals and committed to peak CO<small>2</small> emissions by 2030 in the latest, and increase the use of non-fossil energy carriers, taking up 20% of the total primary energy supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:10-10">[10]</a></sup> If China successfully reached NDC's targets, the GHG emissions level would be 12.8–14.3 GtCO2e in 2030, reducing 64% to 70% of emission intensity below 2005 levels. China has surpassed solar deployment and wind energy deployment targets for 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:11-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>China observed a ground average temperature increase of 0.24℃/decade from 1951 to 2017, exceeding the global ground average temperature increase rate. The average precipitation of China was 641.3 mm in 2017, 1.8% more than average precipitation of previous years. The sea level rise was 3.3mm/year from 1980 to 2017. There was an annual increase in concentrations of carbon dioxide from 1990 to 2016. The annual mean concentration of atmospheric carbon dioxide,  methane and nitrous oxide in the Wanliguan Station were 404.4 ppm, 1907 ppb and 329.7 ppb separately in 2016, slightly higher than the global mean concentration in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:14-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_consumption">Energy consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_power_in_China" title="Coal power in China">Coal power in China</a></div>
<p>China experienced an electricity increase in 2017 as the economy accelerate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to the Climate Data Explorer published by <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a>, China, European Union and the USA took up more than 50% of the global green house gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-14">[14]</a></sup> The energy industry has been the biggest contributor to green house gases emissions since the last decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the 2016 Chinese Statistical Yearbook published by <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Statistics_of_China" title="National Bureau of Statistics of China">China's National Bureau of Statistics</a>, China's energy consumption was 430,000 (10,000 tons of Standard Coal Equivalent), including 64% coal, 18.1% crude oil, 5.9% of natural gas, and 12.0% primary electricity and other energy in 2016. The percentage of coal has decreased since 2011, and the percentage of crude oil, natural gas and primary electricity and other energy have increased since 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:122_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:122-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_investigation">Public investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the Chinese citizen climate change recognition and understanding report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> conducted by China Climate Change Communication program, 94% interviewees agreed on carry out the Paris agreement, 96.8% interviewees supported international cooperation towards global climate change, and more than 70% interviewees were willing to purchase environmental friendly products. 98.7% interviewees agreed on carrying out climate change education at schools. The most concerned topic was the air pollution brought out by climate change. The investigation included 4025 samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The investigation showed that Chinese citizens agreed that they were experiencing climate change, which was caused by human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_climate_change">Effects of climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects of climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>China can suffer some of the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glacier retreat</a> and air pollution.
</p><p>The implications of climate change impose serious setbacks on global health and will hinder the economic development of various regions worldwide impacting countries on more than just the basic environmental scale.  As in the case of China, we will see the effects on a social and economic level.
</p><p>China’s first National Assessment of Global Climate Change, released recently by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science_and_Technology_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Science and Technology of the People's Republic of China">Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST)</a>, states that China already suffers from the environmental impacts of climate change: increase of surface and ocean temperature, rise of sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Qin Dahe,former head of China’s Meteorological Administration, has said that the temperatures in the Tibetan Plateau of China are rising four times faster than anywhere else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Rising sea level is an alarming trend because China has a very long and densely populated coastline, with some of the most economically developed cities such as Shanghai, Tianjin, and Guangzhou situated there. Chinese research has estimated that a one-meter rise in sea level would inundate 92,000 square kilometres of China’s coast, thereby displacing 67 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stonybrook.digication.com2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-stonybrook.digication.com2-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>There has also been an increased occurrence of climate-related disasters such as drought and flood and the amplitude is growing. They have grave consequences for productivity when they occur, and also create serious repercussions for natural environment and infrastructure. This threatens the lives of billions and aggravates poverty.
</p><p>Furthermore, climate change will worsen the uneven distribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_resources_in_China" title="Water resources in China">water resources in China</a>. Outstanding rises in temperature would exacerbate evapo-transpiration intensifying the risk of water shortage for agricultural production in the North. While because of the southern region’s over abundance in rainfall, most of its water is lost due to flooding. As the Chinese government faces challenges managing its expanding population, an increased demand for water to support the nation’s economic activity and people will burden the government. In essence, a water shortage is indeed a large concern for the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stonybrook.digication.com2_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-stonybrook.digication.com2-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lastly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_human_health" title="Effects of climate change on human health">climate change could endanger human health</a> by increasing outbreaks of disease and their transmission. After floods, for example, infectious diseases such as diarrhea and cholera are all far more prevalent. These effects would exacerbate the degradation of the ecologically fragile areas in which poor communities are concentrated pushing thousands back into poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The negative effects on China's agriculture caused by climate change have appeared. There was an increase in agricultural production instability, a severe damage caused by high temperature and drought, lower production and quality in prairie. In the near future, the climate change may cause negative influences, causing a reduction of output in wheat, rice and corn, and change agricultural distribution of production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:132_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:132-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_and_other_natural_ecosystems">Forest and other natural ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Forest and other natural ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate change increases forest belt limits and frequencies of pests and diseases, decreases frozen earth areas, and threatens to decrease glacial areas in the northwest China. The vulnerability of ecosystems may increase due to future climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:132_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-:132-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_resource_and_coastal_zone">Water resource and coastal zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Water resource and coastal zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate change decreased total water resources in north China while increased total water resources in south China. There were more floods, drought and extreme climates. There may be a big impact in the spacial and temporal distribution in China's water resources, increasing extreme weathers and natural disasters. Climate change caused the increase of the sea level, and threatened to impair the functions of the harbors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:132_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-:132-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some regions in China will be exposed to a 50 percent higher malaria transmission probability rate (Béguin et al., 2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WB2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-WB2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusion_of_IPCC">Conclusion of IPCC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conclusion of IPCC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> (2007) from 1900 to 2005 precipitation has declined in parts of southern Asia. By the 2050s freshwater availability including large river basins is projected to decrease in Asian regions. Coastal areas, specially the delta areas in Asia are projected to have increased flooding risk. Floods and droughts are expected to increase health concerns: diseases and mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_mitigation_methods">Climate change mitigation methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate change mitigation methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The People's Republic of China is an active participant in the climate change talks and other multilateral environmental negotiations, and claims to take environmental challenges seriously but is pushing for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">developed world</a> to help <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> to a greater extent. It is a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, although China is not required to reduce its carbon emissions under the terms of the present agreement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>China issued its first Climate Change Program in 2007, in response to its surpassing of the United States as the largest emitter of carbon dioxide emissions in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_national_carbon_trading_scheme" title="Chinese national carbon trading scheme">Chinese national carbon trading scheme</a> was later announced in November 2008 by the national government to enforce a compulsory <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission_trading" title="Carbon emission trading">carbon emission trading</a> scheme across the country's provinces as part of its strategy to create a "<a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">low carbon civilisation</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The scheme would allow provinces to earn money by investing in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture systems</a> in those regions that fail to invest in the technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, Premier <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a> promised to use an "iron hand" to make China more <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficient</a>. China has surpassed the rest of the world as the biggest investor in <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> technology. And it has dictated tough new energy standards for lighting and gas kilometrage for cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> With $34.6 billion invested in <a href="/wiki/Clean_technology" title="Clean technology">clean technology</a> in 2009, China is the world's leading investor in renewable energy technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> China produces more <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> each year than any other country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bradsher_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-bradsher-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coal is predicted to remain the most important power source in the near future but China has been seen as the world leader in <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">clean coal technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">Nuclear power</a> is planned to be rapidly expanded. By mid-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_neutron_reactor" title="Fast neutron reactor">fast neutron reactors</a> are seen as the main nuclear power technology which allows much more efficient use of fuel resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>China should push <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_cars" title="Electric cars">electric cars</a> to curb its dependence on imported <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> (oil) and foreign automobile technology, although they offer smaller cuts in carbon emissions than alternatives like <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid electric vehicles</a>, consulting firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Co" title="McKinsey &amp; Co">McKinsey &amp; Co</a> says.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2011 <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a> report predicted that Chinese CO2 emissions will peak around 2030. This because in many areas such as infrastructure, housing, commercial building, appliances per household, fertilizers, and cement production a maximum intensity will be reached and replacement will take the place of new demand. The 2030 emissions peak also became China's pledge at the Paris COP21 summit. Carbon emission intensity may decrease as policies become strengthened and more effectively implemented, including by more effective financial incentives, and as less carbon intensive energy supplies are deployed. In a "baseline" computer model CO2 emissions were predicted to peak in 2033; in an "Accelerated Improvement Scenario" they were predicted to peak in 2027.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debates">Debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_China&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Debate_over_China%27s_economic_responsibilities_for_climate_change_mitigation" title="Debate over China's economic responsibilities for climate change mitigation">Debate over China's economic responsibilities for climate change mitigation</a></div>
<p>Both internationally and within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>, there has been an ongoing debate over China's economic responsibilities for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>.
</p>
