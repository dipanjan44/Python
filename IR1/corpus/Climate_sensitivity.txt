<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate sensitivity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<p><b>Climate sensitivity</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_equilibrium" title="Radiative equilibrium">equilibrium</a> temperature change in response to changes of the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Therefore, climate sensitivity depends on the initial <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a>, but potentially can be accurately inferred from precise <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">palaeoclimate data</a>. Slow <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate feedbacks</a>, especially changes of ice sheet size and atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>, amplify the total Earth system sensitivity by an amount that depends on the time scale considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although climate sensitivity is usually used in the context of radiative forcing by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), it is thought of as a general property of the climate system: the change in surface air temperature (ΔT<sub>s</sub>) following a unit change in <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> (RF), and thus is expressed in units of °C/(W/m<sup>2</sup>). For this to be useful, the measure must be independent of the nature of the forcing (e.g. from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a>); to first order this is indeed found to be so<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>The climate sensitivity specifically due to CO<sub>2</sub> is often expressed as the temperature change in °C associated with a doubling of the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere</a>.
</p><p>For coupled atmosphere-ocean global climate models (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Coupled_model_intercomparison_project" title="Coupled model intercomparison project">CMIP5</a>) the climate sensitivity is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergent_property" title="Emergent property">emergent property</a>: it is not a model parameter, but rather a result of a combination of model physics and parameters. By contrast, simpler energy-balance models may have climate sensitivity as an explicit parameter.
</p><p><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \Delta T_{s}=\lambda \cdot RF}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</p><p>The terms represented in the equation relate radiative forcing (RF) to linear changes in global surface temperature change (ΔT<sub>s</sub>) via the climate sensitivity λ.
</p><p>It is also possible to estimate climate sensitivity from observations; however, this is difficult due to uncertainties in the forcing and temperature histories.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equilibrium_and_transient_climate_sensitivity">Equilibrium and transient climate sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Equilibrium and transient climate sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>equilibrium</b> climate sensitivity (ECS) refers to the equilibrium change in global mean near-surface air temperature that would result from a sustained doubling of the atmospheric (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">equivalent</a>) carbon dioxide concentration (ΔT<sub>x2</sub>). (Here it should be noted that the term "equilibrium," though widely used, is a misused; the climate system is far from the situation of a dynamic equilibrium, which requires equal and opposite fluxes on all paths; it is better characterized as a "steady state" or "near steady state." However the term "equilibrium" is widely used.) As estimated by the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> (<i>AR5</i>) "there is <i>high confidence</i> that ECS is <i>extremely unlikely</i> less than 1°C and <i>medium confidence</i> that the ECS is <i>likely</i> between 1.5°C and 4.5°C and <i>very unlikely</i> greater than 6°C."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This is a change from the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> (<i>AR4</i>), which said it was <i>likely to be in the range 2 to 4.5 °C with a best estimate of about 3 °C, and is very unlikely to be less than 1.5 °C. Values substantially higher than 4.5 °C cannot be excluded, but agreement of models with observations is not as good for those values</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">IPCC Third Assessment Report</a> (<i>TAR</i>) said it was "likely to be in the range of 1.5 to 4.5 °C".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_2001_climate_sensitivity_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_2001_climate_sensitivity-6">[6]</a></sup> Other estimates of climate sensitivity are discussed later on.
</p><p>A comprehensive model estimate of equilibrium sensitivity thus requires a very long model integration; fully equilibrating ocean temperatures requires integrations of thousands of model years, although it is possible to produce an approximate estimate more quickly using the method of Gregory et al. (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A measure requiring shorter integrations is the <a href="/wiki/Transient_climate_response_to_cumulative_carbon_emissions" title="Transient climate response to cumulative carbon emissions">transient climate response</a> (TCR) which is defined as the average temperature response over a twenty-year period centered at CO<sub>2</sub> doubling in a transient simulation with CO<sub>2</sub> increasing at 1% per year (compounded), i.e., 60 – 80 years following initiation of the increase in CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The transient response is lower than the equilibrium sensitivity because the deep ocean, which takes many centuries after a perturbation to reach a new steady state, continues to serve as a sink for heat from the upper ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the 50–100 year timescale, the climate response to forcing is likely to follow the TCR; for considerations of climate stabilization on the millennial time scale, the ECS is more pertinent.
</p><p>An estimate of the equilibrium climate sensitivity may be made from combining the transient climate sensitivity with the known properties of the ocean reservoirs and the surface heat fluxes; this is the <b>effective</b> climate sensitivity. This "may vary with forcing history and climate state".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_System_Sensitivity">Earth System Sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earth System Sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A less commonly used concept, the <b>Earth system sensitivity</b> (ESS), can be defined which includes the effects of slower feedbacks, such as the albedo change from melting the large ice sheets that covered much of the northern hemisphere during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a>. These extra feedbacks make the ESS larger than the ECS — possibly twice as large — but also mean that it may well not apply to current conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> ESS includes the 'whole' system, but omitting the carbon cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity_to_carbon_dioxide_forcing">Sensitivity to carbon dioxide forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sensitivity to carbon dioxide forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate sensitivity is often evaluated in terms of the change in equilibrium temperature due to radiative forcing due to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius#Greenhouse_effect" title="Svante Arrhenius">Arrhenius</a> relation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the radiative forcing (and hence the change in temperature) is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a> of the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>-absorbing gasses in the atmosphere. Thus, the sensitivity of temperature to gasses in the atmosphere (most notably <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>) is often expressed in terms of the change in temperature per doubling of the concentration of the gas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_forcing_due_to_doubled_CO2">Radiative forcing due to doubled CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Radiative forcing due to doubled CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> climate sensitivity has a component directly due to radiative forcing by CO<sub>2</sub>, and a further contribution arising from <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate feedbacks</a>, both positive and negative. "Without any feedbacks, a doubling of CO<sub>2</sub> (which amounts to a forcing of 3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>) would result in 1 °C <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which is easy to calculate and is undisputed. The remaining uncertainty is due entirely to feedbacks in the system, namely, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback#Role_of_water_vapor" title="Water vapor feedback">the water vapor feedback</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_feedback" title="Cloud feedback">cloud feedback</a>, and the lapse rate feedback";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rahmstorf2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-rahmstorf2008-15">[15]</a></sup> addition of these feedbacks leads to a value of the sensitivity to CO<sub>2</sub> doubling of approximately 3 °C ± 1.5 °C, which corresponds to a value of λ of 0.8 K/(W/m<sup>2</sup>).
</p><p>In the earlier 1979 NAS report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charney1979_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-charney1979-16">[16]</a></sup> (p. 7), the radiative forcing due to doubled CO<sub>2</sub> is estimated to be 4 W/m<sup>2</sup>, as calculated (for example) in <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">Ramanathan</a> et al. (1979).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2001 the IPCC adopted the revised value of 3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>, the difference attributed to a "stratospheric temperature adjustment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> More recently an intercomparison of radiative transfer codes (Collins et al., 2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> showed discrepancies among climate models and between climate models and more exact radiation codes in the forcing attributed to doubled CO<sub>2</sub> even in cloud-free sky; presumably the differences would be even greater if forcing were evaluated in the presence of clouds because of differences in the treatment of clouds in different models. Undoubtedly the difference in forcing attributed to doubled CO<sub>2</sub> in different climate models contributes to differences in apparent sensitivities of the models, although this effect is thought to be small relative to the intrinsic differences in sensitivities of the models themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus_estimates">Consensus estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consensus estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A committee on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">anthropogenic global warming</a> convened in 1979 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> and chaired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jule_Charney" title="Jule Charney">Jule Charney</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charney1979_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-charney1979-16">[16]</a></sup> estimated climate sensitivity to be 3 °C, plus or minus 1.5 °C. Only two sets of models were available; one, due to <a href="/wiki/Syukuro_Manabe" title="Syukuro Manabe">Syukuro Manabe</a>, exhibited a climate sensitivity of 2 °C, the other, due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a>, exhibited a climate sensitivity of 4 °C. "According to Manabe,
Charney chose 0.5 °C as a not-unreasonable margin of error, subtracted it from Manabe’s number, and added it to Hansen’s. Thus was born the 1.5 °C-to-4.5 °C range of likely climate sensitivity that has appeared in every greenhouse assessment since..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapter 4 of the "<a href="/wiki/Jule_Gregory_Charney#Charney_Report" title="Jule Gregory Charney">Charney report</a>" compares the predictions of the models: "We conclude that the predictions ... are basically consistent and mutually supporting. The differences in model results are relatively small and may be accounted for by differences in model characteristics and simplifying assumptions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charney1979_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-charney1979-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008 climatologist <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Rahmstorf" title="Stefan Rahmstorf">Stefan Rahmstorf</a> wrote, regarding the Charney report's original range of uncertainty: "At that time, this range was on very shaky ground. Since then, many vastly improved models have been developed by a number of climate research centers around the world. Current state-of-the-art climate models span a range of 2.6–4.1 °C, most clustering around 3 °C."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rahmstorf2008_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-rahmstorf2008-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 1990 <a href="/wiki/IPCC_First_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC First Assessment Report">IPCC First Assessment Report</a> estimated that equilibrium climate sensitivity to CO<sub>2</sub> doubling lay between 1.5 and 4.5 °C, with a "best guess in the light of current knowledge" of 2.5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This used models with strongly simplified representations of the ocean dynamics. The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_supplementary_report,_1992" title="IPCC supplementary report, 1992">IPCC supplementary report, 1992</a> which used full ocean <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">GCMs</a> nonetheless saw "no compelling reason to warrant changing" from this estimate <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">IPCC Second Assessment Report</a> found that "No strong reasons have emerged to change" these estimates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> with much of the uncertainty attributed to cloud processes. As noted above, the IPCC TAR retained the likely range 1.5 to 4.5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_2001_climate_sensitivity_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_2001_climate_sensitivity-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Authors of the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gerald_Meehl" title="Gerald Meehl">Meehl</a> <i>et al.</i>, 2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_2007_climate_sensitivity_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_2007_climate_sensitivity-25">[25]</a></sup> stated that confidence in estimates of equilibrium climate sensitivity had increased substantially since the TAR. AR4's assessment was based on a combination of several independent lines of evidence, including observed climate change and the strength of known "<a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">feedbacks</a>" simulated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">general circulation models</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_climate_sensitivity_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_climate_sensitivity-26">[26]</a></sup> IPCC authors concluded that the global mean equilibrium warming for doubling CO<sub>2</sub> (to a concentration of 560 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_notation" title="Parts per notation">ppmv</a>), or equilibrium climate sensitivity, very likely is greater than 1.5 °C (2.7 °F) and likely to lie in the range 2 to 4.5 °C (4 to 8.1 °F), with a most likely value of about 3 °C (5 °F). For fundamental physical reasons, as well as data limitations, the IPCC states a climate sensitivity higher than 4.5 °C (8.1 °F) cannot be ruled out, but that agreement for these values with observations and <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">"proxy" climate data</a> is generally worse compared to values in the 2 to 4.5 °C (4 to 8.1 °F) range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_climate_sensitivity_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa_climate_sensitivity-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The TAR uses the word "likely" in a qualitative sense to describe the likelihood of the 1.5 to 4.5 °C range being correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_2007_climate_sensitivity_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_2007_climate_sensitivity-25">[25]</a></sup> AR4, however, quantifies the probable range of climate sensitivity estimates:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_2007_uncertainty_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_2007_uncertainty-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>2–4.5 °C is "likely", = greater than 66% chance of being correct</li>
<li>less than 1.5 °C is "very unlikely" = less than 10%</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> stated: Equilibrium climate sensitivity is likely in the range 1.5 °C to 4.5 °C (high
confidence), extremely unlikely less than 1 °C (high confidence), and very unlikely greater than 6 °C (medium confidence).
</p><p>These are <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_probability" title="Bayesian probability">Bayesian</a> probabilities; although they are based on an expert assessment of the available evidence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_2007_uncertainty_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_2007_uncertainty-27">[27]</a></sup> every Bayesian procedure is an <a href="/wiki/Admissible_decision_rule" title="Admissible decision rule">admissible statistical method</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculations_of_CO2_sensitivity_from_observational_data">Calculations of CO<sub>2</sub> sensitivity from observational data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Calculations of CO2 sensitivity from observational data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_calculation_using_industrial-age_data">Sample calculation using industrial-age data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sample calculation using industrial-age data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rahmstorf (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rahmstorf2008_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-rahmstorf2008-15">[15]</a></sup> provides an informal example of how climate sensitivity might be estimated empirically, from which the following is modified. Denote the sensitivity, i.e. the equilibrium increase in global mean temperature including the effects of feedbacks due to a sustained forcing by doubled CO<sub>2</sub> (taken as 3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>), as <i>x</i> °C. If <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> were to experience an equilibrium temperature change of ΔT (°C) due to a sustained forcing of ΔF (W/m<sup>2</sup>), then one might say that <i>x</i>/(ΔT) = (3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>)/(ΔF), i.e. that <i>x</i> = ΔT * (3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>)/ΔF. The global temperature increase since the beginning of the industrial period (taken as 1750) is about 0.8 °C, and the radiative forcing due to CO<sub>2</sub> and other long-lived greenhouse gases (mainly methane, nitrous oxide, and chlorofluorocarbons) emitted since that time is about 2.6 W/m<sup>2</sup>. Neglecting other forcings and considering the temperature increase to be an equilibrium increase would lead to a sensitivity of about 1.1 °C. However, ΔF also contains contributions due to solar activity (+0.3 W/m<sup>2</sup>), aerosols (−1 W/m<sup>2</sup>), ozone (0.3 W/m<sup>2</sup>) and other lesser influences, bringing the total forcing over the industrial period to 1.6 W/m<sup>2</sup> according to best estimate of the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC AR4</a>, albeit with substantial uncertainty. Additionally the fact that the climate system is not at equilibrium must be accounted for; this is done by subtracting the planetary heat uptake rate H from the forcing; i.e., <i>x</i> = ΔT * (3.7 W/m<sup>2</sup>)/(ΔF-H). Taking planetary heat uptake rate as the rate of ocean heat uptake, estimated by the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC AR4</a> as 0.2 W/m<sup>2</sup>, yields a value for <i>x</i> of 2.1 °C. (All numbers are approximate and quite uncertain.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_calculation_using_ice-age_data">Sample calculation using ice-age data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sample calculation using ice-age data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, Farley wrote: "... examine the change in temperature and solar forcing between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> (ice age) and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> (no ice age) periods. The change in temperature, revealed in <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> samples, is 5 °C, while the change in solar forcing is 7.1 W/m<sup>2</sup>. The computed climate sensitivity is therefore 5/7.1 = 0.7 K(W/m<sup>2</sup>)<sup>−1</sup>. We can use this empirically derived climate sensitivity to predict the temperature rise from a forcing of 4 W/m<sup>2</sup>, arising from a doubling of the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> from pre-industrial levels. The result is a predicted temperature increase of 3 °C."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Based on analysis of uncertainties in total forcing, in Antarctic cooling, and in the ratio of global to Antarctic cooling of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a> relative to the present, Ganopolski and Schneider von Deimling (2008) infer a range of 1.3 to 6.8 °C for climate sensitivity determined by this approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>A lower figure was calculated in a 2011 <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> paper by Schmittner <i>et al.</i>, who combined temperature reconstructions of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> with climate model simulations to suggest a rate of global warming from doubling of atmospheric carbon dioxide of a median of 2.3 °C and uncertainty 1.7–2.6 °C (66% probability range), less than the earlier estimates of 2 to 4.5 °C as the 66% probability range. Schmittner et al. said their "results imply less probability of extreme climatic change than previously thought." Their work suggests that climate sensitivities &gt;6 °C "cannot be reconciled with paleoclimatic and geologic evidence, and hence should be assigned near-zero probability."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmittner11_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmittner11-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_observationally_based_estimates">Other observationally based estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other observationally based estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sherwood_B._Idso" title="Sherwood B. Idso">Idso</a> (1998)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-idso98_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-idso98-33">[33]</a></sup> calculated based on eight natural experiments a λ of 0.1 °C/(Wm<sup>−2</sup>) resulting in a climate sensitivity of only 0.4 °C for a doubling of the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere.
</p><p>Andronova and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schlesinger" title="Michael Schlesinger">Schlesinger</a> (2001) found that the climate sensitivity could lie between 1 and 10 °C, with a 54 percent likelihood that it lies outside the IPCC range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The exact range depends on which factors are most important during the instrumental period: "At present, the most likely scenario is one that includes anthropogenic sulfate aerosol forcing but not solar variation. Although the value of the climate sensitivity in that case is most uncertain, there is a 70 percent chance that it exceeds the maximum IPCC value. This is not good news," said Schlesinger.
</p><p>Forest, <i>et al.</i> (2002)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> using patterns of change and the MIT EMIC estimated a 95% confidence interval of 1.4–7.7 °C for the climate sensitivity, and a 30% probability that sensitivity was outside the 1.5 to 4.5 °C range.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_M._Gregory" title="Jonathan M. Gregory">Gregory</a>, <i>et al.</i> (2002)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> estimated a lower bound of 1.6 °C by estimating the change in Earth's radiation budget and comparing it to the global warming observed over the 20th century.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nir_Shaviv" title="Nir Shaviv">Shaviv</a> (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> carried out a similar analysis for 6 different time scales, ranging from the 11-yr solar cycle to the climate variations over geological time scales. He found a typical sensitivity of 0.54±0.12 K/(W m<sup>−2</sup>) or 2.1 °C (ranging between 1.6 °C and 2.5 °C at 99% confidence) if there is no cosmic-ray climate connection, or a typical sensitivity of 0.35±0.09 K/(W m<sup>−2</sup>) or 1.3 °C (between 1.0 °C and 1.7 °C at 99% confidence), if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">cosmic-ray climate link</a> is real. (Note Shaviv quotes a radiative forcing equivalent of 3.8 Wm<sup>−2</sup>. [ΔT<sub>x2</sub>=3.8 Wm<sup>−2</sup> λ].)
</p><p>Frame, <i>et al.</i> (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> noted that the range of the confidence limits is dependent on the nature of the prior assumptions made.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_Annan" title="James Annan">Annan</a> and Hargreaves (2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> presented an estimate that resulted from combining prior estimates based on analyses of paleoclimate, responses to volcanic eruptions, and the temperature change in response to forcings over the twentieth century. They also introduced a triad notation (L, C, H) to convey the probability distribution function (pdf) of the sensitivity, where the central value C indicates the maximum likelihood estimate in degrees Celsius and the outer values L and H represent the limits of the 95% confidence interval for a pdf, or 95% of the area under the curve for a likelihood function. In this notation their estimate of sensitivity was (1.7, 2.9, 4.9) °C.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Piers_Forster" title="Piers Forster">Forster</a> and Gregory (2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> presented a new independent estimate based on the slope of a plot of calculated greenhouse gas forcing minus top-of-atmosphere energy imbalance, as measured by satellite borne radiometers, versus global mean surface temperature. In the triad notation of Annan and Hargreaves their estimate of sensitivity was (1.0, 1.6, 4.1) °C.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dana_Royer" title="Dana Royer">Royer</a>, <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> determined climate sensitivity within a major part of the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a>. The range of values—1.5 °C minimum, 2.8 °C best estimate, and 6.2 °C maximum—is, given various uncertainties, consistent with sensitivities of current climate models and with other determinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_E._Schwartz" title="Stephen E. </math></span><img alt="C" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.766ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span> is the heat capacity of the climate system. His analysis yielded a time constant 5 yr, heat capacity 17 W yr m<sup>−2</sup> K<sup>−1</sup>, and sensitivity 0.30 K/(W m<sup>−2</sup>), corresponding to 1.1 °C for CO<sub>2</sub> doubling. This work was severely challenged, mainly on the grounds that the determination of time constant was flawed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In response Schwartz conceded<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> that the time constant was somewhat greater than he had determined, 8.5 yr, and that the sensitivity determined by his approach, revised to 0.51 K/(W m<sup>−2</sup>), was that of the upper compartment of the climate system, akin to a transient climate response (TCR 1.9 °C).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Lindzen</a> and Choi (2011) find the equilibrium climate sensitivity to be 0.7 C, implying a negative feedback of clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Skeie et al. (2013) use the Bayesian analysis of the OHC data and conclude that the equilibrium climate sensitivity is 1.8 C, far lower than previous best estimate relied upon by the IPCC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aldrin et al. (2012) use a simple deterministic climate model, modelling yearly hemispheric surface temperature and global ocean heat content as a function of historical radiative forcing and combine it with an empirical, stochastic model. By using a Bayesian framework they estimate the equilibrium climate sensitivity to be 1.98 C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aldrin_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aldrin-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lewis (2013) estimates by using the Bayesian framework that the equilibrium climate sensitivity is 1.6 K, with the likely range (90% confidence level) 1.2–2.2 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fasullo and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_E._Trenberth" title="Kevin E. Trenberth">Trenberth</a> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fasullo_2012_climate_sensitivity_estimate_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-fasullo_2012_climate_sensitivity_estimate-52">[52]</a></sup> assessed model estimates of climate sensitivity based on the ability of the models to reproduce observed relative <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>. The best performing models tended to project relatively high climate sensitivities around 4 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fasullo_2012_climate_sensitivity_estimate_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-fasullo_2012_climate_sensitivity_estimate-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Previdi et al. 2013 reviewed the 2×CO<sub>2</sub> Earth system sensitivity, and concluded it is higher if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice sheet</a> and the vegetation albedo feedback is included in addition to the fast feedbacks, being ∼4–6 °C, and higher still if climate–GHG feedbacks are also included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Previdi_et_al_2013_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Previdi_et_al_2013-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lewis and <a href="/wiki/Judith_Curry" title="Judith Curry">Curry</a> (2014) estimated that equilibrium climate sensitivity was 1.64 °C, based on the 1750-2011 time series and "the uncertainty ranges for forcing components" in the IPCC's Fifth Assessment Report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_reviews">Literature reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Literature reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A literature review by <a href="/wiki/Reto_Knutti" title="Reto Knutti">Knutti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_C._Hegerl" title="Gabriele C. Hegerl">Hegerl</a> (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> concluded, "various observations favour a climate sensitivity value of about 3 °C, with a likely range of about 2–4.5 °C. However, the physics of the response and uncertainties in forcing lead to difficulties in ruling out higher values." A subsequent literature survey by Knutti and others (2017)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> extended that range to 1.5–4.5 °C, but suggested that transient climate response may be a more important measure of climate sensitivity than equilibrium sensitivity.
</p><p>A review by <a href="/wiki/Piers_Forster" title="Piers Forster">Forster</a> (2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> concluded that recent attempts to diagnose equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) from changes in Earth’s energy budget point toward values of around 2 °C, but with tentative evidence that this underestimates the true ECS from a doubling of carbon dioxide.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_forcing_functions">Radiative forcing functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radiative forcing functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of different inputs can give rise to radiative forcing. In addition to the downwelling radiation due to the greenhouse effect, the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_First_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC First Assessment Report">IPCC First Scientific Assessment Report</a> listed solar radiation variability due to orbital changes, variability due to changes in solar irradiance, direct aerosol effects (<i>e.g.,</i> changes in albedo due to cloud cover), indirect aerosol effects, and surface characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity_to_solar_forcing">Sensitivity to solar forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_sensitivity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sensitivity to solar forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">Solar irradiance</a> is about 0.9 W/m<sup>2</sup> brighter during <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">solar maximum</a> than during <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">solar minimum</a>. Analysis by Camp and Tung shows that this correlates with a variation of ±0.1 °C in measured average global temperature between the peak and minimum of the 11-year solar cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solar-climate_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-solar-climate-59">[59]</a></sup> From this data (incorporating the Earth's albedo and the fact that the solar absorption cross-section is 1/4 of the surface area of the Earth), Tung, Zhou and Camp (2008) derive a transient sensitivity value of 0.69 to 0.97 °C/(W/m<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> This would correspond to a transient climate sensitivity to carbon dioxide doubling of 2.5 to 3.6 K, similar to the range of the current scientific consensus. However, they note that this is the transient response to a forcing with an 11-year cycle; due to lag effects, they estimate the equilibrium response to forcing would be about 1.5 times as high.
</p>
