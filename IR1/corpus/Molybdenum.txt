<p><b>Molybdenum</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Mo</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 42. The name is from <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">Neo-Latin</a> <i>molybdaenum</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">Μόλυβδος</span> <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration">molybdos</i></i>, meaning <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, since its ores were confused with lead ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCdescription_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRCdescription-6">[6]</a></sup> Molybdenum minerals have been known throughout history, but the element was discovered (in the sense of differentiating it as a new entity from the mineral salts of other metals) in 1778 by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a>. The metal was first isolated in 1781 by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jacob_Hjelm" title="Peter Jacob Hjelm">Peter Jacob Hjelm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum does not occur naturally as a <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">free metal</a> on Earth; it is found only in various <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> in minerals.  The free element, a silvery <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> with a gray cast, has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elements_by_melting_point" title="List of elements by melting point">sixth-highest</a> <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of any element. It readily forms hard, stable <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbides</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>, and for this reason most of world production of the element (about 80%) is used in <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> alloys, including high-strength alloys and <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a>.
</p><p>Most molybdenum compounds have low <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in water, but when molybdenum-bearing minerals contact <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and water, the resulting <a href="/wiki/Molybdate" title="Molybdate">molybdate</a> ion <span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> is quite soluble. Industrially, molybdenum <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> (about 14% of world production of the element) are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure" title="High-pressure">high-pressure</a> and high-temperature applications as <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysts</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Category:Molybdenum_enzymes" title="Category:Molybdenum enzymes">Molybdenum-bearing enzymes</a> are by far the most common bacterial catalysts for breaking the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> in atmospheric molecular <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> in the process of biological <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>. At least 50 molybdenum enzymes are now known in bacteria, plants, and animals, although only bacterial and cyanobacterial enzymes are involved in nitrogen fixation. These <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenases</a> contain molybdenum in a form different from other molybdenum enzymes, which all contain fully oxidized molybdenum in a <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor" title="Molybdenum cofactor">molybdenum cofactor</a>. These various molybdenum cofactor enzymes are vital to the organisms, and molybdenum is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_element" title="Essential element">essential element</a> for life in all higher <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a> organisms, though not in all bacteria.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its pure form, molybdenum is a silvery-grey metal with a <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 5.5, and a standard atomic weight of 95.95 g/mol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It has a <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of 2,623 °C (4,753 °F); of the naturally occurring elements, only <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> have higher melting points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCdescription_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRCdescription-6">[6]</a></sup> It has one of the lowest coefficients of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> among commercially used metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of molybdenum wires increases about 3 times, from about 10 to 30 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">GPa</a>, when their diameter decreases from ~50–100 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> to 10 nm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Molybdenum is a <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of 2.16 on the Pauling scale. It does not visibly react with oxygen or water at room temperature. Weak oxidation of molybdenum starts at 300 °C (572 °F); bulk oxidation occurs at temperatures above 600 °C, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_trioxide" title="Molybdenum trioxide">molybdenum trioxide</a>. Like many heavier transition metals, molybdenum shows little inclination to form a cation in aqueous solution, although the Mo<sup>3+</sup> cation is known under carefully controlled conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_molybdenum" title="Isotopes of molybdenum">Isotopes of molybdenum</a></div>
<p>There are 35 known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopes" title="Isotopes">isotopes</a> of molybdenum, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> from 83 to 117, as well as four metastable <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a>. Seven isotopes occur naturally, with atomic masses of 92, 94, 95, 96, 97, 98, and 100. Of these naturally occurring isotopes, only molybdenum-100 is unstable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum-98 is the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopic_abundance" title="Isotopic abundance">abundant</a> isotope, comprising 24.14% of all molybdenum. Molybdenum-100 has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of about 10<sup>19</sup> <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">y</a> and undergoes <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a> into <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>-100. Molybdenum isotopes with mass numbers from 111 to 117 all have half-lives of approximately 150 ns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCisotopes_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRCisotopes-14">[14]</a></sup> All unstable isotopes of molybdenum decay into isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCisotopes_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRCisotopes-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>As also noted below, the most common isotopic molybdenum application involves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molybdenum-99" title="Molybdenum-99">molybdenum-99</a>, which is a fission product. It is a parent radioisotope to the short-lived gamma-emitting daughter radioisotope <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m">technetium-99m</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> used in various imaging applications in medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-armstrong_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-armstrong-15">[15]</a></sup>
In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Delft_University_of_Technology" title="Delft University of Technology">Delft University of Technology</a> applied for a patent on the molybdenum-98-based production of molybdenum-99.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Molybdenum_compounds" title="Category:Molybdenum compounds">Category:Molybdenum compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Molybdenum forms chemical compounds in oxidation states from -II to +VI.  Higher oxidation states are more relevant to its terrestrial occurrence and its biological roles, mid-level oxidation states are often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_cluster" title="Metal cluster">metal clusters</a>, and very low oxidation states are typically associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organomolybdenum_compound" title="Organomolybdenum compound">organomolybdenum compounds</a>.  Mo and W chemistry shows strong similarities.  The relative rarity of molybdenum(III), for example, contrasts with the pervasiveness of the chromium(III) compounds. The highest oxidation state is seen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molybdenum(VI)_oxide" title="Molybdenum(VI) oxide">molybdenum(VI) oxide</a> (MoO<sub>3</sub>), whereas the normal sulfur compound is <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disulfide" title="Molybdenum disulfide">molybdenum disulfide</a> MoS<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>

<p>From the perspective of commerce, the most important compounds are molybdenum disulfide (<span class="chemf nowrap">MoS<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) and molybdenum trioxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>).  The black disulfide is the main mineral.  It is roasted in air to give the trioxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MoS<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 7 <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>  +  4 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The trioxide, which is volatile at high temperatures, is the precursor to virtually all other Mo compounds as well as alloys. Molybdenum has several <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a>, the most stable being +4 and +6 (bolded in the table at left).
</p><p>Molybdenum(VI) oxide is soluble in strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">alkaline</a> water, forming molybdates (MoO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>). Molybdates are weaker oxidants than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromates" title="Chromates">chromates</a>.  They tend to form structurally complex <a href="/wiki/Oxyanion" title="Oxyanion">oxyanions</a> by condensation at lower <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> values, such as [Mo<sub>7</sub>O<sub>24</sub>]<sup>6−</sup> and [Mo<sub>8</sub>O<sub>26</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>. Polymolybdates can incorporate other ions, forming <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">polyoxometalates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The dark-blue <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>-containing heteropolymolybdate P[Mo<sub>12</sub>O<sub>40</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> is used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet-visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> detection of phosphorus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The broad range of <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> of molybdenum is reflected in various molybdenum chlorides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum(II)_chloride" title="Molybdenum(II) chloride">Molybdenum(II) chloride</a> MoCl<sub>2</sub>, which exists as the hexamer Mo<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>12</sub> and the related dianion [Mo<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>14</sub>]<sup>2-</sup>.</li>
<li>Molybdenum(III) chloride MoCl<sub>3</sub>, a dark red solid, which converts to the anion trianionic complex [MoCl<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3-</sup>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_tetrachloride" title="Molybdenum tetrachloride">Molybdenum(IV) chloride</a> MoCl<sub>4</sub>, a black solid, which adopts a polymeric structure.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum(V)_chloride" title="Molybdenum(V) chloride">Molybdenum(V) chloride</a> MoCl<sub>5</sub> dark green solid that adopts a dimeric structure.</li></ul>
<p>Molybdenum(VI) chloride MoCl<sub>6</sub> is not known, although the <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_hexafluoride" title="Molybdenum hexafluoride">molybdenum hexafluoride</a> is well characterized.
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and some other transition metals, molybdenum forms <a href="/wiki/Quadruple_bond" title="Quadruple bond">quadruple bonds</a>, such as in Mo<sub>2</sub>(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>4</sub> and [Mo<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>8</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>, which also has a quadruple bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The oxidation state 0 is possible with carbon monoxide as ligand, such as in <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_hexacarbonyl" title="Molybdenum hexacarbonyl">molybdenum hexacarbonyl</a>, Mo(CO)<sub>6</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">Molybdenite</a>—the principal ore from which molybdenum is now extracted—was previously known as molybdena. Molybdena was confused with and often utilized as though it were <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>. Like graphite, molybdenite can be used to blacken a surface or as a solid lubricant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansdown1999_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lansdown1999-22">[22]</a></sup> Even when molybdena was distinguishable from graphite, it was still confused with the common <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> ore PbS (now called <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena</a>); the name comes from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">Μόλυβδος</span> <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">molybdos</i></i>, meaning <i>lead</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup> (The Greek word itself has been proposed as a <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_languages" title="Anatolian languages">Anatolian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luvian_language" title="Luvian language">Luvian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lydian_language" title="Lydian language">Lydian</a> languages).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melchert_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-melchert-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although (reportedly) molybdenum was deliberately alloyed with steel in one 14th-century Japanese sword (mfd. ca. 1330), that art was never employed widely and was later lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the West in 1754, <a href="/wiki/Bengt_Andersson_Qvist" title="Bengt Andersson Qvist">Bengt Andersson Qvist</a> examined a sample of molybdenite and determined that it did not contain lead and thus was not galena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanderkrogt_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanderkrogt-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1778 <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> stated firmly that molybdena was (indeed) neither galena nor graphite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elemental_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-elemental-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Instead, Scheele correctly proposed that molybdena was an ore of a distinct new element, named <i>molybdenum</i> for the mineral in which it resided, and from which it might be isolated. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jacob_Hjelm" title="Peter Jacob Hjelm">Peter Jacob Hjelm</a> successfully isolated molybdenum using <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a> in 1781.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the next century, molybdenum had no industrial use.  It was relatively scarce, the pure metal was difficult to extract, and the necessary techniques of metallurgy were immature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyt1921_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyt1921-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krupp1888_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krupp1888-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta1992_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta1992-32">[32]</a></sup> Early molybdenum steel alloys showed great promise of increased hardness, but efforts to manufacture the alloys on a large scale were hampered with inconsistent results, a tendency toward brittleness, and recrystallization. In 1906, <a href="/wiki/William_D._Coolidge" title="William D. Coolidge">William D. Coolidge</a> filed a patent for rendering molybdenum <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, leading to applications as a heating element for high-temperature furnaces and as a support for tungsten-filament light bulbs; oxide formation and degradation require that molybdenum be physically sealed or held in an inert gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1913, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_E._Elmore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frank E. Elmore (page does not exist)">Frank E. Elmore</a> developed a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Froth_flotation_process&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Froth flotation process (page does not exist)">froth flotation process</a> to recover <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite</a> from ores; flotation remains the primary isolation process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, demand for molybdenum spiked; it was used both in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicle_armor" title="Vehicle armor">armor plating</a> and as a substitute for tungsten in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_speed_steel" title="High speed steel">high speed steels</a>. Some British tanks were protected by 75 mm (3 in) <a href="/wiki/Mangalloy" title="Mangalloy">manganese steel</a> plating, but this proved to be ineffective. The manganese steel plates were replaced with much lighter 25 mm (1.0 in) molybdenum steel plates allowing for higher speed, greater maneuverability, and better protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup> The Germans also used molybdenum-doped <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> for heavy artillery, like in the super-heavy howitzer <a href="/wiki/Big_Bertha_(howitzer)" title="Big Bertha (howitzer)">Big Bertha</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> because traditional steel melts at the temperatures produced by the propellant of the <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">one ton</a> shell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> After the war, demand plummeted until metallurgical advances allowed extensive development of peacetime applications. In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, molybdenum again saw strategic importance as a substitute for tungsten in steel alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millholland1941_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millholland1941-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG"><img alt="Lustrous, silvery, flat, hexagonal crystals in roughly parallel layers sit flowerlike on a rough, translucent crystalline piece of quartz." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1387" data-file-width="1850" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG/220px-Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG/330px-Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG/440px-Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Molly_Hill_molybdenite.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">Molybdenite</a> on quartz</div></div></div>
<p>Molybdenum is the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">54th most abundant element in the Earth's crust</a> and the 25th most abundant element in its oceans, with an average of 10 parts per billion; it is the 42nd most abundant element in the Universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup> The Russian <a href="/wiki/Luna_24" title="Luna 24">Luna 24</a> mission discovered a molybdenum-bearing grain (1 × 0.6 µm) in a <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a> fragment taken from <a href="/wiki/Mare_Crisium" title="Mare Crisium">Mare Crisium</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The comparative rarity of molybdenum in the Earth's crust is offset by its concentration in a number of water-insoluble ores, often combined with sulfur in the same way as copper, with which it is often found. Though molybdenum is found in such <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> as <a href="/wiki/Wulfenite" title="Wulfenite">wulfenite</a> (PbMoO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Powellite" title="Powellite">powellite</a> (CaMoO<sub>4</sub>), the main commercial source is <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite</a> (Mo<a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a><sub>2</sub>). Molybdenum is mined as a principal ore and is also recovered as a byproduct of copper and tungsten mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCdescription_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRCdescription-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The world's production of molybdenum was 250,000 tonnes in 2011, the largest producers being China (94,000 t), the United States (64,000 t), Chile (38,000 t), Peru (18,000 t) and Mexico (12,000 t). The total reserves are estimated at 10 million tonnes, and are mostly concentrated in China (4.3 Mt), the US (2.7 Mt) and Chile (1.2 Mt). By continent, 93% of world molybdenum production is about evenly shared between North America, South America (mainly in Chile), and China. Europe and the rest of Asia (mostly Armenia, Russia, Iran and Mongolia) produce the remainder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molybdenum_world_production.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="750" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Molybdenum_world_production.svg/220px-Molybdenum_world_production.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Molybdenum_world_production.svg/330px-Molybdenum_world_production.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Molybdenum_world_production.svg/440px-Molybdenum_world_production.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Molybdenum_world_production.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World production trend</div></div></div>
<p>In molybdenite processing, the ore is first roasted in air at a temperature of 700 °C (1,292 °F).  The process gives gaseous sulfur dioxide and the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molybdenum(VI)_oxide" title="Molybdenum(VI) oxide">molybdenum(VI) oxide</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 MoS<sub>2</sub> + 7 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 MoO<sub>3</sub> +  4 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The oxidized ore is then usually extracted with aqueous ammonia to give ammonium molybdate:
</p>
<dl><dd>MoO<sub>3</sub> + 2 NH<sub>3</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  →  (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(MoO<sub>4</sub>)</dd></dl>
<p>Copper, an impurity in molybdenite, is less soluble in ammonia. To completely remove it from the solution, it is precipitated with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup>  Ammonium molybdate converts to <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_dimolybdate" title="Ammonium dimolybdate">ammonium dimolybdate</a>, which is isolated as a solid.  Heating this solid gives molybdenum trioxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>(NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Mo<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>   →   2 MoO<sub>3</sub>  +  2 NH<sub>3</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Crude trioxide can be further purified by sublimation at 1,100 °C (2,010 °F).
</p><p>Metallic molybdenum is produced by reduction of the oxide with hydrogen:
</p>
<dl><dd>MoO<sub>3</sub>   +  3 H<sub>2</sub>  →   Mo  +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The molybdenum for steel production is reduced by the <a href="/wiki/Aluminothermic_reaction" title="Aluminothermic reaction">aluminothermic reaction</a> with addition of iron to produce <a href="/wiki/Ferromolybdenum" title="Ferromolybdenum">ferromolybdenum</a>. A common form of ferromolybdenum contains 60% molybdenum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum had a value of approximately $30,000 per tonne as of August 2009. It maintained a price at or near $10,000 per tonne from 1997 through 2003, and reached a peak of $103,000 per tonne in June 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/London_Metal_Exchange" title="London Metal Exchange">London Metal Exchange</a> announced that molybdenum would be traded as a commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_molybdenum_mining">History of molybdenum mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History of molybdenum mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, the <a href="/wiki/Knaben" title="Knaben">Knaben</a> mine in southern Norway, opened in 1885, was the first dedicated molybdenum mine. It was closed in 1973 but was reopened in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and now produces 100,000 kilograms (98 long tons; 110 short tons) of molybdenum disulfide per year. Large mines in Colorado (such as the <a href="/wiki/Henderson_molybdenum_mine" title="Henderson molybdenum mine">Henderson mine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Climax_mine" title="Climax mine">Climax mine</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and in British Columbia yield molybdenite as their primary product, while many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porphyry_copper" title="Porphyry copper">porphyry copper</a> deposits such as the <a href="/wiki/Bingham_Canyon_Mine" title="Bingham Canyon Mine">Bingham Canyon Mine</a> in Utah and the <a href="/wiki/Chuquicamata" title="Chuquicamata">Chuquicamata</a> mine in northern Chile produce molybdenum as a byproduct of copper mining.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="491" data-file-width="600" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg/220px-Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg/330px-Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg/440px-Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plate_of_Molybdenum_Copper_.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plate of molybdenum copper alloy</div></div></div>
<p>About 86% of molybdenum produced is used in metallurgy, with the rest used in chemical applications. The estimated global use is structural steel 35%, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> 25%, chemicals 14%, tool &amp; high-speed steels 9%, <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> 6%, molybdenum elemental metal 6%, and <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum can withstand extreme temperatures without significantly expanding or softening, making it useful in environments of intense heat, including military armor, aircraft parts, electrical contacts, industrial motors, and filaments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-azom-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most high-strength steel <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/41xx_steel" title="41xx steel">41xx steels</a>) contain 0.25% to 8% molybdenum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCdescription_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRCdescription-6">[6]</a></sup> Even in these small portions, more than 43,000 tonnes of molybdenum are used each year in <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tool_steel" title="Tool steel">tool steels</a>, cast irons, and high-temperature superalloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum is also valued in steel alloys for its high <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance and <a href="/wiki/Weldability" title="Weldability">weldability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-40">[40]</a></sup> Molybdenum contributes corrosion resistance to type-300 stainless steels (specifically type-316) and especially so in the so-called super<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austenitic" title="Austenitic">austenitic</a> stainless steels (such as alloy <a href="/wiki/AL-6XN" title="AL-6XN">AL-6XN</a>, 254SMO and 1925hMo). Molybdenum increases lattice strain, thus increasing the energy required to dissolve iron atoms from the surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles"><span title="This text contradicts material elsewhere on this page. (September 2018)">contradictory</span></a></i>]</sup> Molybdenum is also used to enhance the corrosion resistance of ferritic (for example grade 444) and martensitic (for example 1.4122 and 1.4418) stainless steels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Because of its lower density and more stable price, molybdenum is sometimes used in place of tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup> An example is the 'M' series of high-speed steels such as M2, M4 and M42 as substitution for the 'T' steel series, which contain tungsten. Molybdenum can also be used as a flame-resistant coating for other metals. Although its melting point is 2,623 °C (4,753 °F), molybdenum rapidly oxidizes at temperatures above 760 °C (1,400 °F) making it better-suited for use in vacuum environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azom_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-azom-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>TZM (Mo (~99%), Ti (~0.5%), Zr (~0.08%) and some C) is a corrosion-resisting molybdenum superalloy that resists molten fluoride salts at temperatures above 1,300 °C (2,370 °F). It has about twice the strength of pure Mo, and is more ductile and more weldable, yet in tests it resisted corrosion of a standard eutectic salt (<a href="/wiki/FLiBe" title="FLiBe">FLiBe</a>) and salt vapors used in <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactors</a> for 1100 hours with so little corrosion that it was difficult to measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other molybdenum-based alloys that do not contain iron have only limited applications. For example, because of its resistance to molten zinc, both pure molybdenum and molybdenum-tungsten alloys (70%/30%) are used for piping, stirrers and pump impellers that come into contact with molten zinc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications_as_a_pure_element">Other applications as a pure element</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other applications as a pure element">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Molybdenum powder is used as a fertilizer for some plants, such as cauliflower<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Elemental molybdenum is used in NO, NO<sub>2</sub>, NO<sub>x</sub> analyzers in power plants for pollution controls. At 350 °C (662 °F), the element acts as a catalyst for NO<sub>2</sub>/NO<sub>x</sub> to form NO molecules for detection by infrared light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>Molybdenum anodes replace tungsten in certain low voltage X-ray sources for specialized uses such as <a href="/wiki/Mammography" title="Mammography">mammography</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>The radioactive isotope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molybdenum-99" title="Molybdenum-99">molybdenum-99</a> is used to generate <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m">technetium-99m</a>, used for medical imaging<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The isotope is handled and stored as the molybdate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span id="Compounds_.2814.25_of_global_use.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds_(14%_of_global_use)">Compounds (14% of global use)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Compounds (14% of global use)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disulfide" title="Molybdenum disulfide">Molybdenum disulfide</a> (MoS<sub>2</sub>) is used as a solid lubricant and a high-pressure high-temperature (HPHT) antiwear agent. It forms strong films on metallic surfaces and is a common additive to HPHT greases — in the event of a catastrophic grease failure, a thin layer of molybdenum prevents contact of the lubricated parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> It also has semiconducting properties with distinct advantages over traditional silicon or <a href="/wiki/Graphene" title="Graphene">graphene</a> in electronics applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorg-57">[57]</a></sup> MoS<sub>2</sub> is also used as a catalyst in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocracking" title="Hydrocracking">hydrocracking</a> of petroleum fractions containing nitrogen, sulfur and oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disilicide" title="Molybdenum disilicide">Molybdenum disilicide</a> (MoSi<sub>2</sub>) is an electrically conducting <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> with primary use in <a href="/wiki/Heating_element" title="Heating element">heating elements</a> operating at temperatures above 1500 °C in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_trioxide" title="Molybdenum trioxide">Molybdenum trioxide</a> (MoO<sub>3</sub>) is used as an adhesive between <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a> and metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elemental_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-elemental-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_molybdate" title="Lead molybdate">Lead molybdate</a> (wulfenite) co-precipitated with lead chromate and lead sulfate is a bright-orange pigment used with ceramics and plastics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>The molybdenum-based mixed oxides are versatile catalysts in the chemical industry. Some examples are the catalysts for the selective oxidation of propylene to acrolein and acrylic acid, the ammoxidation of propylene to acrylonitrile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Suitable catalysts and process for the direct selective oxidation of propane to acrylic acid are being researched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_heptamolybdate" title="Ammonium heptamolybdate">Ammonium heptamolybdate</a> is used in biological staining.</li>
<li>Molybdenum coated soda lime glass is used in CIGS <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phosphomolybdic_acid" title="Phosphomolybdic acid">Phosphomolybdic acid</a> is a stain used in thin layer chromatography</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mo-containing_enzymes">Mo-containing enzymes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mo-containing enzymes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Molybdenum is an essential element in most organisms. In fact a scarcity of molybdenum in the Earth's early oceans may have strongly influenced evolution of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic life</a> (which includes all plants and animals).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scottetal_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scottetal-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>At least 50 molybdenum-containing enzymes have been identified, mostly in bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> those enzymes include <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde_oxidase" title="Aldehyde oxidase">aldehyde oxidase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_oxidase" title="Sulfite oxidase">sulfite oxidase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_oxidase" title="Xanthine oxidase">xanthine oxidase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup> With one exception, Mo in proteins is bound by <a href="/wiki/Molybdopterin" title="Molybdopterin">molybdopterin</a> to give the <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor" title="Molybdenum cofactor">molybdenum cofactor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of function, molybdoenzymes catalyze the oxidation and sometimes reduction of certain small molecules in the process of regulating <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In some animals, and in humans, the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Xanthine" title="Xanthine">xanthine</a> to <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>, a process of <a href="/wiki/Purine" title="Purine">purine</a> <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a>, is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_oxidase" title="Xanthine oxidase">xanthine oxidase</a>, a molybdenum-containing enzyme. The activity of xanthine oxidase is directly proportional to the amount of molybdenum in the body. However, an extremely high concentration of molybdenum reverses the trend and can act as an inhibitor in both purine catabolism and other processes. Molybdenum concentration also affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, and growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOAoverview_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMOAoverview-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mo is as a component in most <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenases</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_metabolism_and_deficiency">Human metabolism and deficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Human metabolism and deficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Molybdenum is an essential trace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_element" title="Dietary element">dietary element</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Four mammalian Mo-dependent enzymes are known, all of them harboring a <a href="/wiki/Pterin" title="Pterin">pterin</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor" title="Molybdenum cofactor">molybdenum cofactor</a> (Moco) in their active site: <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_oxidase" title="Sulfite oxidase">sulfite oxidase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_oxidase" title="Xanthine oxidase">xanthine oxidoreductase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde_oxidase" title="Aldehyde oxidase">aldehyde oxidase</a>, and mitochondrial amidoxime reductase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>  People severely deficient in molybdenum have poorly functioning sulfite oxidase and are prone to toxic reactions to sulfites in foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The human body contains about 0.07 mg of molybdenum per kilogram of body weight,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> with higher concentrations in the liver and kidneys and in lower in the vertebrae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup> Molybdenum is also present within human <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">tooth enamel</a> and may help prevent its decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Acute toxicity has not been seen in humans, and the toxicity depends strongly on the chemical state. Studies on rats show a <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) as low as 180 mg/kg for some Mo compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-82">[82]</a></sup> Although human toxicity data is unavailable, animal studies have shown that chronic ingestion of more than 10 mg/day of molybdenum can cause diarrhea, growth retardation, <a href="/wiki/Infertility" title="Infertility">infertility</a>, low birth weight, and <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>; it can also affect the lungs, kidneys, and liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coug_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coug-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sodium_tungstate" title="Sodium tungstate">Sodium tungstate</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Competitive_inhibition" title="Competitive inhibition">competitive inhibitor</a> of molybdenum. Dietary tungsten reduces the concentration of molybdenum in tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Low soil concentration of molybdenum in a geographical band from northern China to Iran results in a general dietary <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_deficiency" title="Molybdenum deficiency">molybdenum deficiency</a>, and is associated with increased rates of <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">esophageal cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Compared to the United States, which has a greater supply of molybdenum in the soil, people living in those areas have about 16 times greater risk for <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">esophageal</a> <a href="/wiki/Squamous_cell_carcinoma" title="Squamous cell carcinoma">squamous cell carcinoma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molybdenum deficiency has also been reported as a consequence of non-molybdenum supplemented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_parenteral_nutrition" title="Total parenteral nutrition">total parenteral nutrition</a> (complete intravenous feeding) for long periods of time. It results in high blood levels of <a href="/wiki/Sulfite" title="Sulfite">sulfite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urate" title="Urate">urate</a>, in much the same way as <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor_deficiency" title="Molybdenum cofactor deficiency">molybdenum cofactor deficiency</a>. However (presumably since pure molybdenum deficiency from this cause occurs primarily in adults), the neurological consequences are not as marked as in cases of congenital cofactor deficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_diseases">Related diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Related diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A congenital <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor_deficiency" title="Molybdenum cofactor deficiency">molybdenum cofactor deficiency</a> disease, seen in infants, is an inability to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor" title="Molybdenum cofactor">molybdenum cofactor</a>, the heterocyclic molecule discussed above that binds molybdenum at the active site in all known human enzymes that use molybdenum.  The resulting deficiency results in high levels of <a href="/wiki/Sulfite" title="Sulfite">sulfite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urate" title="Urate">urate</a>, and neurological damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copper-molybdenum_antagonism">Copper-molybdenum antagonism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Copper-molybdenum antagonism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High levels of molybdenum can interfere with the body's uptake of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Copper_deficiency" title="Copper deficiency">copper deficiency</a>. Molybdenum prevents plasma proteins from binding to copper, and it also increases the amount of copper that is excreted in <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">Ruminants</a> that consume high levels of molybdenum suffer from <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, stunted growth, <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_hair_color#Aging_or_achromotrichia" title="Human hair color">achromotrichia</a> (loss of fur pigment). These symptoms can be alleviated by copper supplements, either dietary and injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suttle_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-suttle-91">[91]</a></sup> The effective copper deficiency can be aggravated by excess <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_38-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>Copper reduction or deficiency can also be deliberately induced for therapeutic purposes by the compound <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_tetrathiomolybdate" title="Ammonium tetrathiomolybdate">ammonium tetrathiomolybdate</a>, in which the bright red anion <b>tetrathiomolybdate</b> is the copper-chelating agent. Tetrathiomolybdate was first used therapeutically in the treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_toxicosis" title="Copper toxicosis">copper toxicosis</a> in animals. It was then introduced as a treatment in <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_disease" title="Wilson's disease">Wilson's disease</a>, a hereditary copper metabolism disorder in humans; it acts both by competing with copper absorption in the bowel and by increasing excretion. It has also been found to have an inhibitory effect on <a href="/wiki/Angiogenesis" title="Angiogenesis">angiogenesis</a>, potentially by inhibiting the membrane translocation process that is dependent on copper ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> This is a promising avenue for investigation of treatments for <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age-related_macular_degeneration" title="Age-related macular degeneration">age-related macular degeneration</a>, and other diseases that involve a pathologic proliferation of blood vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_recommendations">Dietary recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dietary recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000, the then U.S. Institute of Medicine (now the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Medicine" title="National Academy of Medicine">National Academy of Medicine</a>, NAM) updated its Estimated Average Requirements (EARs) and Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDAs) for molybdenum. If there is not sufficient information to establish EARs and RDAs, an estimate designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adequate_Intake" title="Adequate Intake">Adequate Intake</a> (AI) is used instead.
</p><p>An AI of 2 <a href="/wiki/Microgram" title="Microgram">micrograms</a> (μg) of molybdenum per day was established for infants up to 6 months of age, and 3 μg/day from 7 to 12 months of age, both for males and females. For older children and adults, the following daily RDAs have been established for molybdenum: 17 μg from 1 to 3 years of age, 22 μg from 4 to 8 years, 34 μg from 9 to 13 years, 43 μg from 14 to 18 years, and 45 μg for persons 19 years old and older. All these RDAs are valid for both sexes. <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">Pregnant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">lactating</a> females from 14 to 50 years of age have a higher daily RDA of 50 μg of molybdenum.
</p><p>As for safety, the NAM sets <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolerable_upper_intake_level" title="Tolerable upper intake level">tolerable upper intake levels</a> (ULs) for vitamins and minerals when evidence is sufficient. In the case of molybdenum, the UL is 2000 μg/day. Collectively the EARs, RDAs, AIs and ULs are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Dietary_Reference_Intake" title="Dietary Reference Intake">Dietary Reference Intakes</a> (DRIs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a> (EFSA) refers to the collective set of information as Dietary Reference Values, with Population Reference Intake (PRI) instead of RDA, and Average Requirement instead of EAR. AI and UL defined the same as in United States. For women and men ages 15 and older the AI is set at 65 μg/day. Pregnant and lactating women have the same AI. For children aged 1–14 years, the AIs increase with age from 15 to 45 μg/day. The adult AIs are higher than the U.S. RDAs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> but on the other hand, the European Food Safety Authority reviewed the same safety question and set its UL at 600 μg/day, which is much lower than the U.S. value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>For U.S. food and dietary supplement labeling purposes, the amount in a serving is expressed as a percent of Daily Value (%DV). For molybdenum labeling purposes 100% of the Daily Value was 75 μg, but as of May 27, 2016 it was revised to 45 μg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FedReg_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FedReg-99">[99]</a></sup> A table of the old and new adult Daily Values is provided at <a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">Reference Daily Intake</a>. The original deadline to be in compliance was July 28, 2018, but on September 29, 2017 the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) released a proposed rule that extended the deadline to January 1, 2020 for large companies and January 1, 2021 for small companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAdelay_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDAdelay-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_sources">Food sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Food sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Average daily intake varies between 120 and 240 μg/day, which is higher than dietary recommendations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coug_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coug-83">[83]</a></sup> Pork, lamb, and beef <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> each have approximately 1.5 parts per million of molybdenum. Other significant dietary sources include green beans, eggs, sunflower seeds, wheat flour, lentils, cucumbers, and cereal grain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molybdenum dusts and fumes, generated by mining or metalworking, can be toxic, especially if ingested (including dust trapped in the <a href="/wiki/Paranasal_sinuses" title="Paranasal sinuses">sinuses</a> and later swallowed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-82">[82]</a></sup> Low levels of prolonged exposure can cause irritation to the eyes and skin. Direct inhalation or ingestion of molybdenum and its oxides should be avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> regulations specify the maximum permissible molybdenum exposure in an 8-hour day as 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. Chronic exposure to 60 to 600 mg/m<sup>3</sup> can cause symptoms including fatigue, headaches and joint pains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> At levels of 5000 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, molybdenum is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
