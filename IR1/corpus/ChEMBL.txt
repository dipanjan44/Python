<p><b>ChEMBL</b> or <b>ChEMBLdb</b> is a manually curated <a href="/wiki/Chemical_database" title="Chemical database">chemical database</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biological_activity" title="Biological activity">bioactive</a> molecules with drug-like properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaulton_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaulton-1">[1]</a></sup>
It is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/European_Bioinformatics_Institute" title="European Bioinformatics Institute">European Bioinformatics Institute</a> (EBI), of the European Molecular Biology Laboratory (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EMBL" title="EMBL">EMBL</a>), based at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust_Genome_Campus" title="Wellcome Trust Genome Campus">Wellcome Trust Genome Campus</a>, Hinxton, UK.
</p><p>The database, originally known as StARlite, was developed by a biotechnology company called Inpharmatica Ltd. later acquired by <a href="/wiki/Galapagos_NV" title="Galapagos NV">Galapagos NV</a>. The data was acquired for EMBL in 2008 with an award from The <a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust</a>, resulting in the creation of the ChEMBL <a href="/wiki/Chemogenomics" title="Chemogenomics">chemogenomics</a> group at EMBL-EBI, led by John Overington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bender_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bender-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19194660_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19194660-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope_and_access">Scope and access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ChEMBL&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope and access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ChEMBL database contains compound bioactivity data against drug targets. Bioactivity is reported in Ki, Kd, IC50, and EC50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Data can be filtered and analyzed to develop compound screening libraries for lead identification during drug discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>ChEMBL version 2 (ChEMBL_02) was launched in January 2010, including 2.4 million <a href="/wiki/Bioassay" title="Bioassay">bioassay</a> measurements covering 622,824 compounds, including 24,000 natural products. This was obtained from curating over 34,000 publications across twelve <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_chemistry" title="Medicinal chemistry">medicinal chemistry</a> journals. ChEMBL's coverage of available bioactivity data has grown to become "the most comprehensive ever seen in a public database.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bender_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bender-2">[2]</a></sup> In October 2010 ChEMBL version 8 (ChEMBL_08) was launched, with over 2.97 million bioassay measurements covering 636,269 compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>ChEMBL_10 saw the addition of the <a href="/wiki/PubChem" title="PubChem">PubChem</a> confirmatory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assays" title="Assays">assays</a>, in order to integrate data that is comparable to the type and class of data contained within ChEMBL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>ChEMBLdb can be accessed via a web interface or downloaded by <a href="/wiki/File_Transfer_Protocol" title="File Transfer Protocol">File Transfer Protocol</a>. It is formatted in a manner amenable to computerized <a href="/wiki/Data_mining" title="Data mining">data mining</a>, and attempts to standardize activities between different publications, to enable comparative analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaulton_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gaulton-1">[1]</a></sup> ChEMBL is also integrated into other large-scale chemistry resources, including <a href="/wiki/PubChem" title="PubChem">PubChem</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ChemSpider" title="ChemSpider">ChemSpider</a> system of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">Royal Society of Chemistry</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_resources">Associated resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ChEMBL&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Associated resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the database, the ChEMBL group have developed tools and resources for data mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bellis_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bellis-8">[8]</a></sup> These include Kinase SARfari, an integrated chemogenomics workbench focussed on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinases" title="Kinases">kinases</a>. The system incorporates and links sequence, structure, compounds and <a href="/wiki/Screening_(medicine)" title="Screening (medicine)">screening data</a>.
</p><p>GPCR SARfari is a similar workbench focused on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPCR" title="GPCR">GPCRs</a>, and ChEMBL-Neglected Tropical Diseases (ChEMBL-NTD) is a repository for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Access_movement" title="Open Access movement">Open Access</a> primary screening and medicinal chemistry data directed at <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_diseases" title="Tropical diseases">tropical diseases</a> of the developing regions of the Africa, Asia, and the Americas. The primary purpose of ChEMBL-NTD is to provide a freely accessible and permanent archive and distribution centre for deposited data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bender_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bender-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>July 2012 saw the release of a new <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/chembl/malaria/" rel="nofollow">malaria data service</a>, sponsored by the Medicines for Malaria Venture (<a class="external text" href="http://www.mmv.org/" rel="nofollow">MMV</a>), aimed at researchers around the globe. The data in this service includes compounds from the Malaria Box screening set, as well as the other donated malaria data found in ChEMBL-NTD.
</p><p><a class="external text" href="http://chembl.blogspot.co.uk/2013/10/chembl-virtual-machine-aka-mychembl.html" rel="nofollow">myChEMBL</a>, the ChEMBL virtual machine, was released in October 2013 to allow users to access a complete and free, easy-to-install cheminformatics infrastructure.
</p><p>In December 2013, the operations of the SureChem patent informatics database were transferred to EMBL-EBI. In a portmanteau, SureChem was renamed SureChEMBL.
</p><p>2014 saw the introduction of the new resource <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/chembl/admesarfari" rel="nofollow">ADME SARfari</a> - a tool for predicting and comparing cross-species ADME targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
