<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">New Urbanism</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Neotraditionalism" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Neotraditional_(disambiguation)" title="Neotraditional (disambiguation)">Neotraditional</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seaside,_Florida.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2446" data-file-width="3683" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg/250px-Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg/375px-Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg/500px-Seaside%2C_Florida.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seaside,_Florida.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Seaside,_Florida" title="Seaside, Florida">Seaside, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>New Urbanism</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a> movement which promotes environmentally friendly habits by creating <a href="/wiki/Walkability" title="Walkability">walkable</a> neighborhoods containing a wide range of housing and job types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup> It arose in the United States in the early 1980s, and has gradually influenced many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_development" title="Real estate development">real estate development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>, and municipal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land-use" title="Land-use">land-use</a> strategies.
</p><p>New Urbanism is strongly influenced by urban design practices that were prominent until the rise of the automobile prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>; it encompasses ten basic principles such as traditional neighborhood design (TND) and <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">transit-oriented development</a> (TOD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These ideas can all be circled back to two concepts: building a sense of community and the development of ecological practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Celebration_fl.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1896" data-file-width="2448" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Celebration_fl.JPG/250px-Celebration_fl.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Celebration_fl.JPG/375px-Celebration_fl.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Celebration_fl.JPG/500px-Celebration_fl.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Celebration_fl.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Market Street, <a href="/wiki/Celebration,_Florida" title="Celebration, Florida">Celebration, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>The organizing body for New Urbanism is the <a class="external text" href="https://www.cnu.org/" rel="nofollow">Congress for the New Urbanism</a>, founded in 1993. Its foundational text is the <i>Charter of the New Urbanism</i>, which begins:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We advocate the restructuring of public policy and development practices to support the following principles: neighborhoods should be diverse in use and population; communities should be designed for the pedestrian and transit as well as the car; cities and towns should be shaped by physically defined and universally accessible public spaces and community institutions; urban places should be framed by architecture and landscape design that celebrate local history, climate, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, and building practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Charter_of_the_New_Urbanism_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Charter_of_the_New_Urbanism-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>New Urbanists support: <a href="/wiki/Regional_planning" title="Regional planning">regional planning</a> for open space; context-appropriate <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> and planning; adequate provision of infrastructure such as sporting facilities, libraries and community centres;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-5">[5]</a></sup> and the balanced development of jobs and housing. They believe their strategies can reduce traffic congestion by encouraging the population to ride bikes, walk, or take the train. They also hope that this set up will increase the supply of affordable housing and rein in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburban_sprawl" title="Suburban sprawl">suburban sprawl</a>. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charter_of_the_New_Urbanism" title="Charter of the New Urbanism">Charter of the New Urbanism</a></i> also covers issues such as <a href="/wiki/Historic_preservation" title="Historic preservation">historic preservation</a>, safe streets, <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a>, and the re-development of <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_land" title="Brownfield land">brownfield land</a>. The ten <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_of_Intelligent_Urbanism" title="Principles of Intelligent Urbanism">Principles of Intelligent Urbanism</a> also phrase guidelines for new urbanist approaches.
</p><p>Architecturally, new urbanist developments are often accompanied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Classical_Architecture" title="New Classical Architecture">New Classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">postmodern</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">vernacular</a> styles, although that is not always the case.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG/250px-Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG/375px-Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG/500px-Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Downtown_baldwin_park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Broad Street, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baldwin_Park,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baldwin Park, Florida (page does not exist)">Baldwin Park, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>Until the mid 20th century, cities were generally organized into and developed around mixed-use walkable neighborhoods. For most of human history this meant a city that was entirely walkable, although with the development of <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">mass transit</a> the reach of the city extended outward along transit lines, allowing for the growth of new pedestrian communities such as <a href="/wiki/Streetcar_suburb" title="Streetcar suburb">streetcar suburbs</a>. But with the advent of cheap automobiles and favorable government policies, attention began to shift away from cities and towards ways of growth more focused on the needs of the car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Specifically, after World War II <a href="/wiki/Urban_planner" title="Urban planner">urban planning</a> largely centered around the use of municipal <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> ordinances to segregate residential from commercial and industrial development, and focused on the construction of low-density single-family detached houses as the preferred housing format for the growing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_class_in_the_United_States" title="Middle class in the United States">middle class</a>. The physical separation of where people live from where they work, shop and frequently spend their recreational time, together with low housing density, which often drastically reduced population density relative to historical norms, made automobiles indispensable for practical transportation and contributed to the emergence of a culture of <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependency</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2635" data-file-width="2448" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG/250px-St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG/375px-St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG/500px-St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_petersburg_florida_downtown.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beach Drive, <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>This new system of development, with its rigorous separation of uses, arose after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and became known as "conventional suburban development"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or pejoratively as <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>. The majority of U.S. citizens now live in <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburban</a> communities built in the last fifty years, and automobile use per capita has soared.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post_office_in_celebration.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Post_office_in_celebration.JPG/250px-Post_office_in_celebration.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Post_office_in_celebration.JPG/375px-Post_office_in_celebration.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Post_office_in_celebration.JPG/500px-Post_office_in_celebration.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post_office_in_celebration.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celebration,_FL" title="Celebration, FL">Celebration, FL</a> Post Office, designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Michael_Graves" title="Michael Graves">Michael Graves</a></div></div></div>
<p>Although New Urbanism as an organized movement would only arise later, a number of activists and thinkers soon began to criticize the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist" title="Modernist">modernist</a> planning techniques being put into practice. Social philosopher and historian <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Mumford" title="Lewis Mumford">Lewis Mumford</a> criticized the "anti-urban" development of post-war America. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_and_Life_of_Great_American_Cities" title="The Death and Life of Great American Cities">The Death and Life of Great American Cities</a>,</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Jacobs" title="Jane Jacobs">Jane Jacobs</a> in the early 1960s, called for planners to reconsider the single-use housing projects, large car-dependent thoroughfares, and segregated commercial centers that had become the "norm". The French architect <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Spoerry" title="François Spoerry">François Spoerry</a> has developed in the 60's the concept of "soft architecture" that he applied to <a href="/wiki/Port_Grimaud" title="Port Grimaud">Port Grimaud</a>, a new marina in south of France. The success of this project had a considerable influence and led to many new projects of soft architecture like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Libert%C3%A9" title="Port Liberté">Port Liberté</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> or Le Plessis Robisson in France.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Rooted in these early dissenters, the ideas behind New Urbanism began to solidify in the 1970s and 80s with the urban visions and theoretical models for the reconstruction of the "European" city proposed by architect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Krier" title="Leon Krier">Leon Krier</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern language</a> theories of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Alexander" title="Christopher Alexander">Christopher Alexander</a>. The term "new urbanism" itself started being used in this context in the mid-1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but it wasn't until the early 1990s that it was commonly written as a proper noun capitalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1991, the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_(Sacramento,_California)" title="Local Government Commission (Sacramento, California)">Local Government Commission</a>, a private nonprofit group in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>, invited architects <a href="/wiki/Peter_Calthorpe" title="Peter Calthorpe">Peter Calthorpe</a>, Michael Corbett, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Duany" title="Andrés Duany">Andrés Duany</a>, Elizabeth Moule, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Plater-Zyberk" title="Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk">Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stefanos_Polyzoides" title="Stefanos Polyzoides">Stefanos Polyzoides</a>, and Daniel Solomon to develop a set of community principles for land use planning. Named the <i><a href="/wiki/Ahwahnee_Principles" title="Ahwahnee Principles">Ahwahnee Principles</a></i> (after <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ahwahnee_Hotel" title="Ahwahnee Hotel">Ahwahnee Hotel</a>), the commission presented the principles to about one hundred government officials in the fall of 1991, at its first Yosemite Conference for Local Elected Officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calthorpe, Duany, Moule, Plater-Zyberk, Polyzoides, and Solomon founded the Chicago-based Congress for the New Urbanism in 1993. The CNU has grown to more than three thousand members, and is the leading international organization promoting New Urbanist design principles. It holds annual Congresses in various U.S. cities.
</p><p>In 2009, co-founders Elizabeth Moule, Hank Dittmar, and Stefanos Polyzoides authored the Canons of Sustainable Architecture and Urbanism to clarify and detail the relationship between New Urbanism and sustainability. The Canons are "a set of operating principles for human settlement that reestablish the relationship between the art of building, the making of community, and the conservation of our natural world". They promote the use of passive heating and cooling solutions, the use of locally obtained materials, and in general, a "culture of permanence". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>New Urbanism is a broad movement that spans a number of different disciplines and geographic scales. And while the conventional approach to growth remains dominant, New Urbanist principles have become increasingly influential in the fields of planning, architecture, and public policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_elements">Defining elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defining elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="600" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg/250px-DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg/375px-DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg/500px-DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DSCN3187_prospectnewtown_e_600.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Prospect_New_Town" title="Prospect New Town">Prospect New Town</a> in <a href="/wiki/Longmont,_Colorado" title="Longmont, Colorado">Longmont, Colorado</a>, showing a mix of aggregate housing and traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detached_home" title="Detached home">detached homes</a></div></div></div>
<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Duany" title="Andrés Duany">Andrés Duany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Plater-Zyberk" title="Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk">Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk</a>, two of the founders of the Congress for the New Urbanism, observed mixed-use streetscapes with corner shops, front porches, and a diversity of well-crafted housing while living in one of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>. They and their colleagues observed patterns including the following:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Celebration_fl_park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Celebration_fl_park.JPG/250px-Celebration_fl_park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Celebration_fl_park.JPG/375px-Celebration_fl_park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Celebration_fl_park.JPG/500px-Celebration_fl_park.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Celebration_fl_park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A park in <a href="/wiki/Celebration,_Florida" title="Celebration, Florida">Celebration, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_King_St.,_New_Town.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="640" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg/220px-Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg/330px-Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg/440px-Great_King_St.%2C_New_Town.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_King_St.,_New_Town.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great King St, New Town, Edinburgh</div></div></div>
<ul><li>The neighborhood has a discernible center. This is often a square or a green and sometimes a busy or memorable street corner. A transit stop would be located at this center.</li>
<li>Most of the dwellings are within a five-minute walk of the center, an average of roughly 0.25 miles (0.40 km).</li>
<li>There are a variety of dwelling types — usually houses, <a href="/wiki/Terraced_house" title="Terraced house">rowhouses</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apartment_building" title="Apartment building">apartments</a> — so that younger and older people, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_(relationship)" title="Single (relationship)">singles</a> and families, the poor and the wealthy may find places to live.</li>
<li>At the edge of the neighborhood, there are shops and offices of sufficiently varied types to supply the weekly needs of a household.</li>
<li>A small ancillary building or <a href="/wiki/Garage_apartment" title="Garage apartment">garage apartment</a> is permitted within the backyard of each house. It may be used as a rental unit or place to work (for example, an office or craft workshop).</li>
<li>An elementary school is close enough so that most children can walk from their home.</li>
<li>There are small playgrounds accessible to every dwelling — not more than a tenth of a mile away.</li>
<li>Streets within the neighborhood form a connected network, which disperses traffic by providing a variety of pedestrian and vehicular routes to any destination.</li>
<li>The streets are relatively narrow and shaded by rows of trees. This slows traffic, creating an environment suitable for pedestrians and bicycles.</li>
<li>Buildings in the neighborhood center are placed close to the street, creating a well-defined outdoor room.</li>
<li>Parking lots and garage doors rarely front the street. Parking is relegated to the rear of buildings, usually accessed by alleys.</li>
<li>Certain prominent sites at the termination of street vistas or in the neighborhood center are reserved for civic buildings. These provide sites for community meetings, education, and religious or cultural activities.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:House_in_celebration.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/House_in_celebration.JPG/250px-House_in_celebration.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/House_in_celebration.JPG/375px-House_in_celebration.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/House_in_celebration.JPG/500px-House_in_celebration.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:House_in_celebration.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Revival" title="Mediterranean Revival">Mediterranean Revival</a> house in <a href="/wiki/Celebration,_Florida" title="Celebration, Florida">Celebration, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>Several terms are viewed either as synonymous, included in, or overlapping with the New Urbanism. The terms Neotraditional Development<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or Traditional Neighborhood Development are often associated with the New Urbanism. These terms generally refer to complete New Towns or new neighborhoods, often built in traditional architectural styles, as opposed to smaller infill and redevelopment projects. The term Traditional Urbanism has also been used to describe the New Urbanism by those who object to the "new" moniker. The term "Walkable Urbanism" was proposed as an alternative term by developer and professor Christopher Leinberger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Many debate whether <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_Growth" title="Smart Growth">Smart Growth</a> and the New Urbanism are the same or whether substantive differences exist between the two; overlap exists in membership and content between the two movements. Placemaking is another term that is often used to signify New Urbanist efforts or those of like-minded groups. The term Transit-Oriented Development is sometimes cited as being coined by prominent New Urbanist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Calthorpe" title="Peter Calthorpe">Peter Calthorpe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and is heavily promoted by New Urbanists. The term <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a> is sometimes associated with the New Urbanism as there has been an increasing focus on the environmental benefits of New Urbanism associated with the rise of the term sustainability in the 2000s, however, this has caused some confusion as the term is also used by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> to include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" title="Human development (humanity)">human development</a> issues (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing country</a>) that exceed the scope of <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land development</a> intended to be addressed by the New Urbanism or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_Urbanism" title="Sustainable Urbanism">Sustainable Urbanism</a>. The term "livability" or "livable communities" was popular under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_administration" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> though it dates back at least to the mid-1990s when the term was used by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_(Sacramento,_California)" title="Local Government Commission (Sacramento, California)">Local Government Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:House_in_baldwin_park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2448" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/House_in_baldwin_park.JPG/250px-House_in_baldwin_park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/House_in_baldwin_park.JPG/375px-House_in_baldwin_park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/House_in_baldwin_park.JPG/500px-House_in_baldwin_park.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:House_in_baldwin_park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a> style house in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baldwin_Park,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baldwin Park, Florida (page does not exist)">Baldwin Park, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>Planning magazine discussed the proliferation of "urbanisms" in an article in 2011 titled "A Short Guide to 60 of the Newest Urbanisms". <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Several New Urbanists have popularized terminology under the umbrella of the New Urbanism including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_Urbanism" title="Sustainable Urbanism">Sustainable Urbanism</a> and Tactical Urbanism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> (of which Guerrilla Urbanism can be viewed as a subset). The term Tactical Urbanism was coined by Frenchman Michel de Certau in 1968 and revived in 2011 by New Urbanist Mike Lydon and the co-authors of the Tactical Urbanism Guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2011 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andres_Duany" title="Andres Duany">Andres Duany</a> authored a book that used the term Agrarian Urbanism to describe an agriculturally-focused subset of New Urbanist town design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  In 2013 a group of New Urbanists led by CNU co-founder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andres_Duany" title="Andres Duany">Andres Duany</a> began a research project under the banner of Lean Urbanism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> which purported to provide a bridge between Tactical Urbanism and the New Urbanism.
</p><p>Other terms have surfaced in reaction to the New Urbanism intended to provide a contrast, alternative to, or a refinement of the New Urbanism. Some of these terms include Everyday Urbanism by Harvard Professor Margaret Crawford, John Chase, and John Kaliski,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_Urbanism" title="Ecological Urbanism">Ecological Urbanism</a>, and True Urbanism by architect Bernard Zyscovich. <a href="/wiki/Landscape_urbanism" title="Landscape urbanism">Landscape urbanism</a> was popularized by Charles Waldheim who explicitly defined it as in opposition to the New Urbanism in his lectures at Harvard University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Landscape Urbanism and its Discontents</i>, edited by Andres Duany and Emily Talen, specifically addressed the tension between these two views of <a href="/wiki/Urbanism" title="Urbanism">urbanism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Arth" title="Michael E. Arth">Michael E. Arth</a> promotes what he describes as a variant of the New Urbanism called the <a href="/wiki/New_pedestrianism" title="New pedestrianism">New Pedestrianism</a>, which is intended to be more pedestrian-oriented and traces its origins to a 1929 <a href="/wiki/Planned_community" title="Planned community">planned community</a> in <a href="/wiki/Radburn,_New_Jersey" title="Radburn, New Jersey">Radburn, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArthMichael_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArthMichael-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014,_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2723" data-file-width="4111" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG/250px-Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG/375px-Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG/500px-Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014%2C_1.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sankt_Eriksomr%C3%A5det_2014,_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New urbanist <i>Sankt Eriksområdet</i> quarter in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, built in the 1990s. (<a class="external text" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:S:t_Eriksomr%C3%A5det">More photos</a>)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bitola_2007.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="755" data-file-width="1144" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bitola_2007.JPG/250px-Bitola_2007.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bitola_2007.JPG/375px-Bitola_2007.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Bitola_2007.JPG/500px-Bitola_2007.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bitola_2007.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mixed use pedestrian-friendly street in <a href="/wiki/Bitola" title="Bitola">Bitola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Republic of Macedonia</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The primary organization promoting the New Urbanism in the United States is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_for_the_New_Urbanism" title="Congress for the New Urbanism">Congress for the New Urbanism</a> (CNU). The Congress for the New Urbanism is the leading organization promoting walkable, mixed-use neighborhood development, sustainable communities and healthier living conditions.  CNU members promote the principles of CNU's Charter and the hallmarks of New Urbanism, including:
</p>
<ul><li>Livable streets arranged in compact, walkable blocks.</li>
<li>A range of housing choices to serve people of diverse ages and income levels.</li>
<li>Schools, stores and other nearby destinations reachable by walking, bicycling or transit service.</li>
<li>An affirming, human-scaled public realm where appropriately designed buildings define and enliven streets and other public spaces.</li></ul>
<p>The CNU has met annually since 1993 when they held their first general meeting in <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria, Virginia</a>, with approximately one hundred attendees. By 2008 the Congress was drawing two to three thousand attendees to the annual meetings.
</p><p>The CNU began forming local and regional chapters circa 2004 with the founding of the New England and Florida Chapters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> By 2011 there were 16 official chapters and interest groups for 7 more.  As of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Canada hosts two full CNU Chapters, one in Ontario (CNU Ontario), and one in British Columbia (Cascadia) which also includes a portion of the north-west US states.
</p><p>While the CNU has international participation in Canada, sister organizations have been formed in other areas of the world including the Council for European Urbanism (CEU),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> the Movement for Israeli Urbanism (MIU) and the Australian Council for the New Urbanism.
</p><p>By 2002 chapters of Students for the New Urbanism began appearing at universities including the <a href="/wiki/Savannah_College_of_Art_and_Design" title="Savannah College of Art and Design">Savannah College of Art and Design</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Notre_Dame" title="University of Notre Dame">University of Notre Dame</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>. In 2003, a group of younger professionals and students met at the 11th Congress in Washington, D.C. and began developing a "Manifesto of the Next Generation of New Urbanists". The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Next_Generation_of_New_Urbanists" title="Next Generation of New Urbanists">Next Generation of New Urbanists</a> held their first major session the following year at the 12th meeting of the CNU in Chicago in 2004. The group has continued meeting annually as of  2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> with a focus on young professionals, students, new member issues, and ensuring the flow of fresh ideas and diverse viewpoints within the New Urbanism and the CNU. Spinoff projects of the Next Generation of the New Urbanists include the Living Urbanism publication first published in 2008 and the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactical_Urbanism" title="Tactical Urbanism">Tactical Urbanism</a> Guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The CNU has spawned publications and research groups. Publications include the <i>New Urban News</i> and the <i>New Town Paper</i>. Research groups have formed independent nonprofits to research individual topics such as the Form-Based Codes Institute, The National Charrette Institute and the Center for Applied Transect Studies.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> New Urbanist and European urbanism principles are practised and taught by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Prince%27s_Foundation_for_the_Built_Environment" title="The Prince's Foundation for the Built Environment">The Prince's Foundation for the Built Environment</a>. Other organisations promote New Urbanism as part of their remit, such as <a href="/wiki/International_Network_for_Traditional_Building,_Architecture_%26_Urbanism" title="International Network for Traditional Building, Architecture &amp; Urbanism">INTBAU</a>, A Vision of Europe, and others.
</p><p>The CNU and other national organizations have also formed partnerships with like-minded groups. Organizations under the banner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smart_Growth" title="Smart Growth">Smart Growth</a> also often work with the Congress for the New Urbanism. In addition the CNU has formed partnerships on specific projects such as working with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Green_Building_Council" title="United States Green Building Council">United States Green Building Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a> to develop the <a href="/wiki/LEED_for_Neighborhood_Development" title="LEED for Neighborhood Development">LEED for Neighborhood Development</a> standards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup> and with the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Transportation_Engineers" title="Institute of Transportation Engineers">Institute of Transportation Engineers</a> to develop a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Context_Sensitive_Solutions" title="Context Sensitive Solutions">Context Sensitive Solutions</a> (CSS) Design manual.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The New Urbanism Film Festival<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> was held in 2013 and 2014 in Los Angeles to highlight films and short films about the New Urbanism and related topics. The 2011 film <i><a href="/wiki/Urbanized" title="Urbanized">Urbanized</a></i> by Gary Hustwit featured then CNU Board Chair Ellen Dunham-Jones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and other urban thinkers on the international story of urbanization including the New Urbanist efforts in the United States.
</p><p>The 2004 <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_Suburbia" title="The End of Suburbia">The End of Suburbia: Oil Depletion and the Collapse of the American Dream</a></i> argues that the depletion of oil will result in the demise of the sprawl-type development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/New_Urban_Cowboy:_Toward_a_New_Pedestrianism" title="New Urban Cowboy: Toward a New Pedestrianism">New Urban Cowboy: Toward a New Pedestrianism</a></i>, a feature length 2008 documentary about urban designer <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Arth" title="Michael E. Arth">Michael E. Arth</a>, explains the principles of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Pedestrianism" title="New Pedestrianism">New Pedestrianism</a>, a more ecological and pedestrian-oriented version of New Urbanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArthMichael_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArthMichael-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  The film also gives a brief history of New Urbanism, and chronicles the rebuilding of an inner city slum into a model of New Pedestrianism and New Urbanism called The Garden District.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-globalize" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Globe icon." data-file-height="290" data-file-width="350" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" width="48"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this article <b>may not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You may <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a>, discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:New_Urbanism" title="Talk:New Urbanism">talk page</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">create a new article</a>, as appropriate.</span> <small><i>(February 2010)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>New Urbanism has drawn both praise and criticism from all parts of the <a href="/wiki/Political_spectrum" title="Political spectrum">political spectrum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It has been criticized both for being a <a href="/wiki/Social_engineering_(political_science)" title="Social engineering (political science)">social engineering</a> scheme and for failing to address social equity and for both restricting private enterprise and for being a deregulatory force in support of private sector developers.
</p><p>Journalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alex_Marshall_(Journalist)" title="Alex Marshall (Journalist)">Alex Marshall</a> has decried New Urbanism as essentially a marketing scheme that repackages conventional suburban sprawl behind a façade of nostalgic imagery and empty, aspirational slogans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In a 1996 article in <a href="/wiki/Metropolis_(architecture_magazine)" title="Metropolis (architecture magazine)">Metropolis Magazine</a>, Marshall denounced New Urbanism as "a grand fraud".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  The attack continued in numerous articles, including an opinion column in the <i>Washington Post</i> in September of the same year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and in Marshall's first book, <a class="external text" href="http://utpress.utexas.edu/index.php/books/marhow" rel="nofollow"><i>How Cities Work:  Suburbs, Sprawl, and the Roads Not Taken</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics have asserted that the effectiveness claimed for the New Urbanist solution of mixed income developments lacks statistical evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  Independent studies have supported the idea of addressing poverty through mixed-income developments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> but the argument that New Urbanism produces such diversity has been challenged from findings from one community in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some parties have criticized the New Urbanism for being too accommodating of motor vehicles and not going far enough to promote walking, cycling, and public transport. The Charter of the New Urbanism states that "communities should be designed for the pedestrian and transit as well as the car".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Charter_of_the_New_Urbanism_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Charter_of_the_New_Urbanism-4">[4]</a></sup> Some critics suggest that communities should exclude the car altogether in favor of <a href="/wiki/Car-free_movement" title="Car-free movement">car-free developments</a>.  Michael E Arth proposes <a href="/wiki/New_pedestrianism" title="New pedestrianism">new pedestrianism</a> as a way to further elevate the status of pedestrians by focusing on pedestrian-only paths. Steve Melia proposes the idea of "filtered permeability" (see <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(spatial_and_transport_planning)" title="Permeability (spatial and transport planning)">Permeability (spatial and transport planning)</a>) which increases the connectivity of the pedestrian and cycling network resulting in a time and convenience advantage over drivers while still limiting the connectivity of the vehicular network and thus maintaining the safety benefits of cul de sacs and horseshoe loops in resistance to property crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to critiques of a lack of evidence for the New Urbanism's claimed environmental benefits, a rating system for neighborhood environmental design, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LEED-ND" title="LEED-ND">LEED-ND</a>, was developed by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_for_the_New_Urbanism" title="Congress for the New Urbanism">Congress for the New Urbanism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> to quantify the sustainability of New Urbanist neighborhood design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> New Urbanist and board member of CNU, <a href="/wiki/Doug_Farr" title="Doug Farr">Doug Farr</a> has taken a step further and coined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_Urbanism" title="Sustainable Urbanism">Sustainable Urbanism</a>, which combines New Urbanism and LEED-ND to create walkable, transit-served urbanism with high performance buildings and infrastructure.
</p><p>New Urbanism has been criticized for being a form of centrally planned, large-scale development, "instead of allowing the initiative for construction to be taken by the final users themselves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It has been criticized for asserting universal principles of design instead of attending to local conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Examples_of_New_Urbanism" title="Examples of New Urbanism">Examples of New Urbanism</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Urbanism is having a growing influence on how and where metropolitan regions choose to grow. At least fourteen large-scale planning initiatives are based on the principles of linking transportation and land-use policies, and using the neighborhood as the fundamental building block of a region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a>, has adopted the most ambitious New Urbanist-based zoning code reform yet undertaken by a major U.S. city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>More than six hundred new towns, villages, and neighborhoods, following New Urbanist principles, have been planned or are currently under construction in the U.S. Hundreds of new, small-scale, urban and suburban infill projects are under way to reestablish walkable streets and blocks. In Maryland and several other states, New Urbanist principles are an integral part of <i><a href="/wiki/Smart_growth" title="Smart growth">smart growth</a></i> legislation.
</p><p>In the mid-1990s, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development" title="U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development">U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development</a> (HUD) adopted the principles of the New Urbanism in its multibillion-dollar program to rebuild public housing projects nationwide. New Urbanists have planned and developed hundreds of projects in infill locations. Most were driven by the private sector, but many, including HUD projects, used public money.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_Place_in_Memphis">University Place in Memphis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: University Place in Memphis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2010 University Place in Memphis became the second only <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a> (USGBC) LEED certified neighborhood.  LEED ND (neighborhood development) standards integrates principles of smart growth, urbanism and green building  and were developed through a collaboration between USGBC, Congress for the New Urbanism, and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a>.  University Place, developed by <a href="/wiki/McCormack_Baron_Salazar" title="McCormack Baron Salazar">McCormack Baron Salazar</a>, is a 405-unit, 30-acre, mixed-income, mixed use, multigenerational, <a href="/wiki/HOPE_VI" title="HOPE VI">HOPE VI</a> grant community that revitalized the severely distressed Lamar Terrace public housing site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cotton_District">The Cotton District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Cotton District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Cotton_District" title="The Cotton District">The Cotton District</a> in <a href="/wiki/Starkville,_Mississippi" title="Starkville, Mississippi">Starkville, Mississippi</a>, was the first New Urbanist development, begun in 1968 long before the New Urbanism movement was organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The District borders Mississippi State University, and consists mostly of residential rental units for college students along with restaurants, bars and retail. The Cotton District got its name because it is built in the vicinity of an old cotton mill.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seaside">Seaside</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Seaside">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seaside,_Florida" title="Seaside, Florida">Seaside, Florida</a>, the first fully New Urbanist town, began development in 1981 on eighty acres (324,000 m²) of <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> coastline. It was featured on the cover of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Monthly" title="Atlantic Monthly">Atlantic Monthly</a> in 1988, when only a few streets were completed, and has become internationally famous for its architecture, and the quality of its streets and public spaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Seaside is now a tourist destination and appeared in the 1998 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Truman_Show" title="The Truman Show">The Truman Show</a></i>. Lots sold for $15,000 in the early 1980s, and slightly over a decade later, the price had escalated to about $200,000. Today, most lots sell for more than a million dollars, and some houses top $5 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mueller_Community">Mueller Community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mueller Community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mueller_Community" title="Mueller Community">Mueller Community</a> is located on the 700 acre site of the former <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mueller_Municipal_Airport" title="Robert Mueller Municipal Airport">Robert Mueller Municipal Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>, which closed in 1999. Per the developer, the value of the Mueller development upon completion will be $1.3 billion, and will comprise 4.2 million square feet of non-residential development, 650,000 square feet of retail space, 4,600 homes, and 140 acres of open space. An estimated 10,000 permanent jobs within the development will have been created by the time it is complete. The Mueller Community also has more electric cars per capita than any other neighborhood in the United States – a fact partially attributable to an incentive program.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stapleton">Stapleton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Stapleton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The site of the former <a href="/wiki/Stapleton_International_Airport" title="Stapleton International Airport">Stapleton International Airport</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Colorado" title="Aurora, Colorado">Aurora, Colorado</a>, closed in 1995, is now being redeveloped by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_City_Enterprises" title="Forest City Enterprises">Forest City Enterprises</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAT_2003-10-18_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAT_2003-10-18-55">[55]</a></sup> Stapleton is expected to be home to at least 30,000 residents, six schools and 2 million square feet (180,000 m²) of retail. Construction began in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Northfield_Stapleton" title="Northfield Stapleton">Northfield Stapleton</a>, one of the development's major retail centers, recently opened.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="San_Antonio">San Antonio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: San Antonio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1997 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, as part of a new master plan, created new regulations called the Unified Development Code (UDC), largely influenced by New Urbanism. One feature of the UDC is six unique land development patterns that can be applied to certain districts: Conservation Development, Commercial Center Development, Office or Institutional Campus Development, Commercial Retrofit Development, Tradition Neighborhood Development, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_Oriented_Development" title="Transit Oriented Development">Transit Oriented Development</a>. Each district has specific standards and design regulation. The six development patterns were created to reflect existing development patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_House">Mountain House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mountain House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mountain_House,_San_Joaquin_County,_California" title="Mountain House, San Joaquin County, California">Mountain House</a>, one of the latest New Urbanist projects in the United States, is a new town located near <a href="/wiki/Tracy,_California" title="Tracy, California">Tracy, California</a>.  Construction started in 2001. Mountain House will consist of 12 villages, each with its own elementary school, park, and commercial area. In addition, a future <a href="/wiki/Train_station" title="Train station">train station</a>, transit center and bus system are planned for Mountain House.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesa_del_Sol">Mesa del Sol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mesa del Sol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mesa_del_Sol" title="Mesa del Sol">Mesa del Sol</a>, New Mexico—the largest New Urbanist project in the United States—was designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Peter_Calthorpe" title="Peter Calthorpe">Peter Calthorpe</a>, and is being developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_City_Enterprises" title="Forest City Enterprises">Forest City Enterprises</a>. Mesa del Sol may take five decades to reach full build-out, at which time it should have 38,000 residential units, housing a population of 100,000; a 1,400-acre (5.7 km<sup>2</sup>) industrial office park; four town centers; an urban center; and a <a href="/wiki/Downtown" title="Downtown">downtown</a> that would provide a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" title="Twin towns and sister cities">twin city</a> within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albuquerque" title="Albuquerque">Albuquerque</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="I.27On"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="I'On">I'On</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: I'On">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Located in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pleasant,_South_Carolina" title="Mount Pleasant, South Carolina">Mount Pleasant, South Carolina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I%27On" title="I'On">I'On</a> is a traditional neighborhood development, mixed with a new urbanism  styled architecture, reflecting on the building designs of the nearby downtown areas of <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>. Founded on April 30, 1995, I'On was designed by the town planning firms of Dover, Kohl &amp; Partners and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duany_Plater-Zyberk_%26_Company" title="Duany Plater-Zyberk &amp; Company">Duany Plater-Zyberk &amp; Company</a>, and currently holds over 750 single family homes. Features of the community include extensive sidewalks, shared <a href="/wiki/Public" title="Public">public</a> greens and parks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trails" title="Trails">trails</a> and a grid of narrow, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_calming" title="Traffic calming">traffic calming</a> streets. Most homes are required to have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Front_porch" title="Front porch">front porch</a> of not less than eight feet (2.46 m) in depth. Floor heights of 10 feet (3.1 m), raised foundations and smaller lot sizes give the community a <a href="/wiki/Urban_density" title="Urban density">dense</a>, vertical feel.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Haile_Plantation">Haile Plantation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Haile Plantation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Haile_Plantation,_Florida" title="Haile Plantation, Florida">Haile Plantation, Florida</a>, is a 2,600 household (1,700 acres (6.9 km<sup>2</sup>)) development of regional impact southwest of the city of Gainesville, within Alachua County. Haile Village Center is a traditional neighborhood center within the development. It was originally started in 1978 and completed in 2007. In addition to the 2,600 homes the neighborhood consists of two merchant centers (one a New England narrow street village and the other a chain grocery strip mall). There are also two public elementary schools and an 18-hole golf course.
</p>
<h4><span id="Celebration.2C_Florida"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebration,_Florida">Celebration, Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Celebration, Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In June 1996, the <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Walt Disney Company</a> unveiled its 5,000 acre (20 km²) town of <a href="/wiki/Celebration,_Florida" title="Celebration, Florida">Celebration</a>, near Orlando, Florida. Celebration opened its downtown in October 1996, relying heavily on the experiences of Seaside, whose downtown was nearly complete. Disney shuns the label New Urbanism, calling Celebration simply a "town".
</p><p>Celebration's Downtown has become one of the area's most popular tourist destinations making the community a showcase for New Urbanism as a prime example of the creation of a "sense of place".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jersey_City">Jersey City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Jersey City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The construction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hudson_Bergen_Light_Rail" title="Hudson Bergen Light Rail">Hudson Bergen Light Rail</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hudson_County,_New_Jersey" title="Hudson County, New Jersey">Hudson County, New Jersey</a> has spurred transit-oriented development. In <a href="/wiki/Jersey_City,_New_Jersey" title="Jersey City, New Jersey">Jersey City</a>, two projects are planned to transform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brownfield" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> sites, both of which have required remediation of <a href="/wiki/Toxic_waste" title="Toxic waste">toxic waste</a> by previous owners. <a href="/wiki/Bayfront,_Jersey_City" title="Bayfront, Jersey City">Bayfront</a>, once site of a <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">Honeywell</a> plant is a 100 acres (0.40 km<sup>2</sup>) site on the <a href="/wiki/Hackensack_River" title="Hackensack River">Hackensack River</a>, and is nearby the planned West Campus of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_City_University" title="New Jersey City University">New Jersey City University</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canal_Crossing,_Jersey_City" title="Canal Crossing, Jersey City">Canal Crossing</a>, named for the former <a href="/wiki/Morris_Canal" title="Morris Canal">Morris Canal</a>, was once partially owned by <a href="/wiki/PPG_Industries" title="PPG Industries">PPG Industries</a>, and is a 117 acres (0.47 km<sup>2</sup>) site west of <a href="/wiki/Liberty_State_Park" title="Liberty State Park">Liberty State Park</a>.

<h4><span id="Old_York_Village.2C_Chesterfield_Township.2C_New_Jersey"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_York_Village,_Chesterfield_Township,_New_Jersey">Old York Village, Chesterfield Township, New Jersey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Old York Village, Chesterfield Township, New Jersey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The sparsely developed agricultural <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_Township,_New_Jersey" title="Chesterfield Township, New Jersey">Township of Chesterfield</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> covers approximately 21.61 square miles (56.0 km<sup>2</sup>) and has made farmland preservation a priority since the 1970s.  Chesterfield has permanently preserved more than 7,000 acres (28 km<sup>2</sup>) of farmland through state and county programs and a township-wide transfer of development credits program that directs future growth to a designated "receiving area" known as Old York Village.  Old York Village is a neo-traditional, new urbanism town on 560 acres (2.3 km<sup>2</sup>) incorporating a variety of housing types, neighborhood commercial facilities, a new elementary school, civic uses, and active and passive open space areas with preserved agricultural land surrounding the planned village.  Construction began in the early 2000s and a significant percentage of the community is now complete. Old York Village was the winner of the American Planning Association National Outstanding Planning Award in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Civita">Civita</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Civita">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civita_(San_Diego,_California)" title="Civita (San Diego, California)">Civita</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation">transit</a>-oriented 230-acre master-planned village under development in the <a href="/wiki/Mission_Valley,_San_Diego" title="Mission Valley, San Diego">Mission Valley</a> area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" title="San Diego, California">San Diego, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Located on a former <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarry</a> site, the <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban</a>-style village is organized around a 19-acre community park that cascades down the terraced property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Civita development plans call for 60 to 70 acres of parks and open space, 4,780 residences (including approximately 478 <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordable</a> units), an approximately 480,000-square-foot <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_center_(retail)" title="Lifestyle center (retail)">retail center</a>, and 420,000 square feet for an office/business campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urbanland_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-urbanland-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leung_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-leung-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sudberry Properties, the developer of Civita, incorporated numerous <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> practices in the Civita design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In 2009, Civita achieved a Stage 1 Gold rating for the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Green_Building_Council" title="Green Building Council">Green Building Council’s</a> 2009 LEED-ND (Neighborhood Development) pilot and received the California Governor’s Environmental and Economic Leadership Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urbanland_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-urbanland-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In 2010, Civita was designated as a California Catalyst Community by the <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Housing_and_Community_Development" title="California Department of Housing and Community Development">California Department of Housing and Community Development</a> to support innovation and test sustainable strategies that reflect the interdependence of environmental, economic, and community health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Del_Mar_Station">Del Mar Station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Del Mar Station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Del_Mar_(Los_Angeles_Metro_station)" title="Del Mar (Los Angeles Metro station)">Del Mar (Los Angeles Metro station)</a>, which won a Congress for the New Urbanism Charter Award in 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">transit-oriented development</a> surrounding a prominent Metro Rail stop on the Gold Line, which connects Los Angeles and Pasadena. Located at the southern edge of downtown Pasadena, it serves as a gateway to the city with 347 apartments, out of which 15% are affordable units. Approximately 20,000 square feet of retail is linked with a network of public plazas, paseos and private courtyards. The 3.4-acre, $77 million project sits above a 1,200-car multi-level subterranean parking garage, with 600 spaces dedicated to transit. The light rail right of way, detailed as a public street, bisects the site. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Moule_%26_Polyzoides" title="Moule &amp; Polyzoides">Moule &amp; Polyzoides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Norfolk_VA_East_Beach">Norfolk VA East Beach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Norfolk VA East Beach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Norfolk, VA, East Beach. designed and built in the style of traditional Atlantic coastal villages. The Master Plan for East Beach was developed in the style of “New Urbanism” by world renowned TND master planners Duany Plater-Zyberk.  Newly constructed homes reflect traditional classic detail and proportion of Tidewater Virginia homes, and are built with materials that will withstand the test of time and forces of Mother Nature and the Chesapeake Bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Urbanism is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Urban_village" title="Urban village">Urban village</a> movement in Europe. They both occurred at similar times and share many of the same principles although urban villages has an emphasis on traditional city planning. In Europe many brown-field sites have been redeveloped since the 1980s following the models of the traditional city neighbourhoods rather than Modernist models. One well-publicized example is <a href="/wiki/Poundbury" title="Poundbury">Poundbury</a> in England, a suburban extension to the town of Dorchester, which was built on land owned by the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> under the overview of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a>. The original masterplan was designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Krier" title="Leon Krier">Leon Krier</a>. A report carried out after the first phase of construction found a high degree of satisfaction by residents, although the aspirations to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_dependency" title="Car dependency">car dependency</a> had not been successful. Rising house prices and a perceived premium have made the open market housing unaffordable for many local people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poundbury_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-poundbury-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Council for European Urbanism (CEU), formed in 2003, shares many of the same aims as the U.S.'s New Urbanists. CEU's Charter is a development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_for_the_New_Urbanism" title="Congress for the New Urbanism">Congress for the New Urbanism</a> Charter revised and reorganised to relate better to European conditions. An Australian organisation, Australian Council for New Urbanism has since 2001 run conferences and events to promote New Urbanism in that country. A <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Urban_Design_Protocol" title="New Zealand Urban Design Protocol">New Zealand Urban Design Protocol</a> was created by the Ministry for the Environment in 2005.
</p><p>There are many developments around the world that follow New Urbanist principles to a greater or lesser extent:
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Le_Plessis-Robinson" title="Le Plessis-Robinson">Le Plessis-Robinson</a>, a 21st-century example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_classical_architecture" title="New classical architecture">neo-traditionalism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> in the south-west of Paris. This city is in the process of transforming itself, destroying old modern blocklike buildings and replacing them with traditional buildings and houses in one of the biggest worldwide projects with Val d'Europe.  In 2008 the city was nominated best architectural project of the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Poundbury" title="Poundbury">Poundbury</a>, in Dorset, England, is a neotraditionalist urban extension focussed on high quality urban realm and the expression of traditional modes of urban or village life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tornagrain" title="Tornagrain">Tornagrain</a>, Between Inverness and Nairn, Scotland, The design is based on the architectural and planning traditions  of the Highlands and the rest of Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Europe" title="Val d'Europe">Val d'Europe</a>, east of Paris, France. <li><a href="/wiki/Jakriborg" title="Jakriborg">Jakriborg</a>, in Southern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, is a recent example of the New Urbanist movement.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brandevoort" title="Brandevoort">Brandevoort</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Helmond" title="Helmond">Helmond</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, is a new example of the New Urbanist movement.</li>
<li><i>Sankt Eriksområdet</i> quarter in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, built in the 1990s.</li>
<li>Other developments can be found at Heulebrug, part of <a href="/wiki/Knokke-Heist" title="Knokke-Heist">Knokke-Heist</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and Fonti di Matilde in <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Bartolomeo_(Reggio_Emilia)" title="it:San Bartolomeo (Reggio Emilia)">San Bartolomeo</a> (outside of <a href="/wiki/Reggio_Emilia" title="Reggio Emilia">Reggio Emilia</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kartanonkoski" title="Kartanonkoski">Kartanonkoski</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Vantaa" title="Vantaa">Vantaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, is the only example of neotraditional architecture in Finland implemented on a larger scale. The area has around 4000 inhabitants and its architecture has been mainly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Classicism" title="Nordic Classicism">Nordic Classicism</a>.</li></ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are several such developments in South Africa. The most notable is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melrose_Arch" title="Melrose Arch">Melrose Arch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_Point_(East_London)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Triple Point (East London) (page does not exist)">Triple Point</a> is a comparable mixed-use development in <a href="/wiki/East_London,_Eastern_Cape" title="East London, Eastern Cape">East London</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a> province. The development, announced in 2007, comprises 30 hectares. It is made up of three apartment complexes together with over 30 residential sites as well as 20,000 sq m of residential and office space. The development is valued at over R2 billion ($250 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> There have been cases where market forces of urban decay are confused with new urbanism in <a class="external text" href="https://www.africaurban.org/debunking-new-urbanism-harare/" rel="nofollow">African cities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> This has led to a form of suburban mixed-use development that does not promote walkability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Urbanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most new developments on the edges of Australia's major cities are master planned, often guided expressly by the principles of New Urbanism. The relationship between housing, activity centres, the transport network and key social infrastructure (sporting facilities, libraries, community centres etc.) is defined at structure planning stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Tullimbar Village, NSW Australia, is a new development which follows the principles of New Urbanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (December 2017)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<p>Another important factor or principle of New Urbanism that guides Australia's major cities is how good their foot circulation seems to be which is guided by the wayfinding systems that are implemented. Kenneth B. Hall, Jr. and Gerald A. Porterfield said in their book, "Community by Design," the way to gain good circulation is to take some thoughtful consideration to things like wayfinding, sight lines, transition, visual clues, and reference points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-85">[85]</a></sup> Circulation design should work to create an interesting and informative system that utilizes subtle elements as well as technical ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-85">[85]</a></sup> City of Port Philip, Australia,  is a good example of wayfinding where they have come up with a comprehensive pedestrian signage system, specifically for their local areas of St Kilda, South Melbourne and Port Melbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-87">[87]</a></sup> The city's wayfinding system consists of 26 individually designed panels that are placed on some major streets such as St Kilda and St Kilda East, linking St Kilda Junction and Balaclava Station to the foreshore via Fitzroy, Carlisle and Acland Streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-87">[87]</a></sup> City of Port Philip also created directional signage systems that makes use of the already existing street furniture such as trash cans to help provide for 130 directional indicators across Port Melbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
