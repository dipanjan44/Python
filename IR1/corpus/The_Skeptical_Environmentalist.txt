<p><i><b>The Skeptical Environmentalist: Measuring the Real State of the World</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i lang="da">Verdens sande tilstand</i>, literal translation: <i>The True State of the World</i>) is a book by Danish environmentalist author <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg" title="Bjørn Lomborg">Bjørn Lomborg</a>, controversial for its claims that <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>, declining <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> resources, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">species loss</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">water shortages</a>, certain aspects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and an assortment of other global <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_issues" title="List of environmental issues">environmental issues</a> are unsupported by statistical analysis of the relevant data. It was first <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">published</a> in <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> in 1998, while the English edition was published as a work in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the scope of the project, comprising the range of topics addressed, the diversity of data and sources employed, and the many types of conclusions and comments advanced, <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> does not fit easily into a particular scientific discipline or methodology. Although published by the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a> division of Cambridge University Press, the findings and conclusions were widely challenged on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a>. This interpretation of <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> as a work of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> generated much of the controversy and debate that surrounded the book.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_author">The author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg" title="Bjørn Lomborg">Bjørn Lomborg</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG/100px-Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG/150px-Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG/200px-Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg_small.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bjørn Lomborg</div></div></div>
<p>Prior to becoming the Director of the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Consensus_Center" title="Copenhagen Consensus Center">Copenhagen Consensus Center</a> and Adjunct Professor at the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Business_School" title="Copenhagen Business School">Copenhagen Business School</a>, Bjørn Lomborg was an Associate Professor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_Science" title="Political Science">Political Science</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Aarhus" title="University of Aarhus">University of Aarhus</a>.
</p><p>Some critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> focus on his lack of training or professional experience in the environmental sciences or <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. Supporters<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2012)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>argue his research is an appropriate application of his expertise in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a>, a standard analytical tool in policy assessment. His advocates further note that many of the scientists and environmentalists who criticized the book are not themselves environmental policy experts or experienced in cost-benefit research.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In numerous interviews, Lomborg ascribed his motivation for writing <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> to his personal convictions, making clear that he was a pro-environmentalist and <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> supporter. He has stated that he began his research as an attempt to counter what he saw as anti-<a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> arguments by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_Lincoln_Simon" title="Julian Lincoln Simon">Julian Lincoln Simon</a> in an article in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wired_magazine" title="Wired magazine">Wired</a></i>, but changed his mind after starting to analyze data. Lomborg describes the views he attributes to environmental campaigners as the "<a href="/wiki/Litany" title="Litany">Litany</a>", which he at one time claims to have affirmed, but purports to correct in his work.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The general analytical approach employed by Lomborg is based on cost-benefit analyses as employed in economics, social science, and the formulation and assessment of government policy. Much of Lomborg's examination of his Litany is based on statistical data analysis, therefore his work may be considered a work of that nature. Since it examines the costs and benefits of its many topics, it could be considered a work in economics, as categorized by its publisher. However, <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> is methodologically eclectic and cross-disciplinary, combining interpretation of data with assessments of the media and human behavior, evaluations of scientific theories, and other approaches, to arrive at its various conclusions.
</p><p>In arriving at the final work, Lomborg has used a similar approach in each of his work's main areas and subtopics. He progresses from the general to the specific, starting with a broad concern, such as <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> or energy, dividing it into subtopics (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>; <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">fossil fuel depletion</a>), and then identifying one or more widely held fears and their source (e.g. our air is growing increasingly toxic, by X measure, according to Y). From there, Lomborg chooses data that he considers to be the most reliable and reasonable available. He then analyzes that data to prove or disprove his selected proposition. In every case, his calculations find that the claim is not substantiated, and is either an exaggeration, or a completely reversed portrayal of an improving situation, rather than a deteriorating one. Having established what he calls "the true state of the world", for each topic and subtopic, Lomborg examines a variety of theories, technologies, implementation strategies and costs, and suggests alternative ways to improve not-so-dire situations, or advance in other areas not currently considered as pressing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">Contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Skeptical Environmentalist'</i>s subtitle refers to the <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_World_(book_series)" title="State of the World (book series)">State of the World</a></i> report, published annually since 1984 by the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a>. Lomborg designated the report "one of the best-researched and academically most ambitious environmental policy publications," but criticized it for using short-term trends to predict disastrous consequences, in cases where long-term trends would not support the same conclusions.
</p><p>In establishing its arguments, <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> examined a wide range of issues in the general area of environmental studies, including environmental economics and science, and came to an equally broad set of conclusions and recommendations. Lomborg's work directly challenged popular examples of green concerns by interpreting data from some 3,000 assembled sources. The author suggested that environmentalists diverted potentially beneficial resources to less deserving environmental issues in ways that were economically damaging.  Much of the book's <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a> and integrity have been subject to criticism which argue that Lomborg distorted the fields of research he covers. Support for the book was staunch as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GristSkepticalLook_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GristSkepticalLook-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Litany"><i>The Litany</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Litany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"The Litany" comprises very diverse areas where, Lomborg claims, overly pessimistic claims are made and bad policies are implemented as a result. He cites accepted mainstream sources, like the United States government, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> agencies and others, preferring global long-term data over regional and short-term statistics.
</p><p><i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> is arranged around four major themes:
</p>
<ol><li>Human prosperity from an economic and demographic point of view</li>
<li>Human prosperity from an ecological point of view</li>
<li>Pollution as a threat to human prosperity</li>
<li>Future threats to human prosperity</li></ol>
<p>Lomborg's main argument is that the vast majority of environmental problems—such as pollution, water shortages, deforestation, and species loss, as well as population growth, hunger, and AIDS—are area-specific and highly correlated with poverty. Therefore, challenges to human prosperity are essentially logistical matters, and can be solved largely through economic and social development. Concerning problems that are more pressing at the global level, such as the depletion of fossil fuels and global warming, Lomborg argues that these issues are often overstated and that recommended policies are often inappropriate if assessed against alternatives.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1._Human_prosperity_from_an_economic_and_demographic_point_of_view">1. Human prosperity from an economic and demographic point of view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1. Human prosperity from an economic and demographic point of view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lomborg analyzes three major themes: <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>, food and <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a>, and prosperity, finding that life expectancy and health levels have <i>dramatically</i> improved over the past centuries, even though several regions of the world remain threatened, in particular by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>. He dismisses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a>' theory that increases in the world's population lead to widespread hunger. On the contrary, Lomborg claims that food is widespread, and humanity's daily intake of calories is increasing, and will continue to rise until hunger's eradication, thanks to technological improvements in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. However, Lomborg notes that <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in particular still produces too little sustenance, an effect he attributes to the continent's dismal economic and political systems. Concerning prosperity, Lomborg argues that wealth, as measured by per capita GDP, should not be the only judging criterion. He points to improvements in education, safety, leisure, and ever more widespread access to consumer goods as signs that prosperity is increasing in most parts of the world.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2._Human_prosperity_from_an_ecological_point_of_view">2. Human prosperity from an ecological point of view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2. Human prosperity from an ecological point of view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In this section, Lomborg looks at the world's natural resources and draws a conclusion that contrasts starkly to that of the well known report <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>. First, he analyzes food once more, this time from an ecological perspective, and again claims that most food products are not threatened by human growth. An exception, however, is fish, which continues to be depleted. As a partial solution, Lomborg presents fish farms, which cause a less disruptive impact on the world's oceans. Next, Lomborg looks at forests. He finds no indication of widespread <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, and notes that even the Amazon still retains more than 80% of its 1978 tree cover. Lomborg points out that in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, deforestation is linked to poverty and poor economic conditions, so he proposes that economic growth is the best means to tackle the loss of forests.
Concerning energy, Lomborg asserts that oil is not being depleted as fast as is claimed, and that improvements of technology will provide people with fossil fuels for years to come. The author further asserts that many alternatives already exist, and that with time they will replace fossil fuels as an energy source. Concerning other resources, such as metals, Lomborg suggests that based on their price history they are not in short supply. Examining the challenge of collecting sufficient amounts of water, Lomborg says that wars will probably not erupt over water because fighting such wars is not cost-effective (one week of war with the Palestinians, for instance, would cost <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> more than five desalination plants, according to an Israeli officer). Lomborg emphasizes the need for better water management, as water is distributed unequally around the world.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="3._Pollution_as_a_threat_to_human_prosperity">3. Pollution as a threat to human prosperity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 3. Pollution as a threat to human prosperity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lomborg considers pollution from different angles. He notes that air pollution in wealthy nations has steadily decreased in recent decades. He finds that air pollution levels are highly linked to economic development, with moderately developed countries polluting most. Again, Lomborg argues that faster growth in emerging countries would help them reduce their air pollution levels. Lomborg suggests that devoting resources to reduce the levels of specific air pollutants would provide the greatest health benefits and save the largest number of lives (per amount of money spent), continuing an already decades-long improvement in air quality in most developed countries.  Concerning <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, Lomborg notes again that it is connected with economic progress. He also notes that water pollution in major Western rivers decreased rapidly after the use of sewage systems became widespread. Concerning <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>, Lomborg notes once again that fears are overblown, as the entire waste produced by the United States in the 21st century could fit into a square 100 feet thick and 28 km along each side, or 0.009% of the total surface of the United States.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="4._Future_threats_to_human_prosperity">4. Future threats to human prosperity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 4. Future threats to human prosperity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In this last section, Lomborg puts forward his main assertion: based on a cost-benefit analysis, the environmental threats to human prosperity are overstated and much of policy response is misguided.  As an example, Lomborg cites worries about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> and their link to <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>. He argues that such concerns are vastly exaggerated in the public perception, as alcohol and coffee are the foods that create by far the greatest risk of cancer, as opposed to vegetables that have been sprayed with pesticides. Furthermore, if pesticides were not used on fruit and vegetables, their cost would rise, and consequently their consumption would go down, which would cause cancer rates to increase. He goes on to criticize the fear of a vertiginous decline in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, proposing that 0.7% of species have gone extinct in the last 50 years (as compared to a maximum of 50%, as claimed by some biologists). While Lomborg admits that extinctions are a problem, he asserts that they are not the catastrophe claimed by some, and have little effect on human prosperity.
</p><p>Lomborg's most contentious assertion, however, involves <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. From the outset, Lomborg "accepts the reality of man-made global warming" though he refers to a number of uncertainties in the computer simulations of climate change and some aspects of data collection.  His main contention involves not the science of global warming but the politics and the policy response to scientific findings. Lomborg points out that, given the amount of greenhouse gas reduction required to combat global warming, the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> is grossly insufficient. He argues that the economic costs of legislative restrictions that aim to slow or reverse global warming are far higher than the alternative of international coordination. Moreover, he asserts that the cost of combating global warming would be disproportionately shouldered by  developing countries. Lomborg proposes that since the Kyoto agreement limits economic activities, developing countries that suffer from pollution and poverty most, will be perpetually handicapped economically.
</p><p>Lomborg proposes that the importance of global warming in terms of policy priority is low compared to other policy issues such as fighting poverty, disease and aiding poor countries, which has direct and more immediate impact both in terms of welfare and the environment. He therefore suggests that a global cost-benefit analysis be undertaken before deciding on future measures. The <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Consensus" title="Copenhagen Consensus">Copenhagen Consensus</a> that Lomborg later organized concluded that combating global warming does have a benefit but its priority compared to other issues is "poor" (ranked 13th) and three projects addressing climate change (optimal carbon tax, the Kyoto protocol and value-at-risk carbon tax), are the least cost-efficient of its proposals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions">Conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lomborg concludes his book by once again reviewing the Litany, and noting that the real state of the world is much better than the Litany claims. According to Lomborg, this discrepancy poses a problem, as it focuses public attention on relatively unimportant issues, while ignoring those that are paramount. In the worst case, <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> argues, the global community is pressured to adopt inappropriate policies which have adverse effects on humanity, wasting resources that could be put to better use in aiding poor countries or fighting diseases such as AIDS. Lomborg thus urges us to look at what he calls the true problems of the world, since solving those will also solve the Litany.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> was controversial even before its English-language release, with anti-publication efforts launched against Cambridge University Press. Once in the public arena, the book elicited strong reactions in scientific circles and in the mainstream media. Opinion was largely polarized. Environmental groups were generally critical.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-publication_pressures">Anti-publication pressures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anti-publication pressures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dr. Chris Harrison (Publishing Director of social science publishing for <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a>), anticipating the level of controversy a book like <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> would likely provoke, took extra care with the book's peer-review process. Instead of choosing candidates from the usual list of social science referees, Cambridge University Press chose from a list provided by their environmental science publishing program. Four were chosen:  a <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> scientist, an expert in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a> development, a specialist on the economics of climate change (whose credentials include reviewing publications for the UN's <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC)) and a "pure" economist.  All four members of Cambridge's initial review panel agreed that the book should be published.
</p><p>While criticism of the book was to be expected, the publisher was apparently surprised by the pressure brought against it to not publish <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i>. The complaints of some critics included demands that Cambridge convene a special panel to review the book in order to identify errors (despite existing pre-publication <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a>), that Cambridge transfer their publishing rights to a "non-scholarly publishing house" and that they review their own policies to prevent publication of any book described as "essentially a political tract" in the future.
</p><p>In the article, entitled "Peer review, politics and pluralism", Dr. Harrison noted that "many of the critical reviews of <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> went beyond the usual unpicking of a thesis and concentrated instead on the role of the publisher in publishing the book at all. The post tray and e-mail inbox of editors and senior managers at the press bore witness to a concerted campaign to persuade Cambridge to renounce the book."  He went on to describe complaints from <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a> who feared the book would be "abused by corporate interests". Cambridge University Press felt it necessary to issue a formal, written statement, in order to "explain the editorial decisions that led not just to publishing the book but also to Cambridge's resistance to concerted pressure to withdraw it from the market." With these complaints and the publication of a <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> issue regarding the book (described below), Cambridge stated, in response to those who claimed the book lacked <i>peer-review credentials</i>, "it would be quite wrong to abandon an author who had satisfied the requirements of our peer-review system."
</p><p>Cambridge took the additional step of inviting submissions of publishing proposals for books which offered an opposing argument to Lomborg's but noted that they had, to the best of Chris Harrison's knowledge, seen no attempt by any of the critics to submit such a proposal.  This is seen by some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to suggest that criticism of the book was political rather than academic. Subsequent to Cambridge's unequivocal assertion that <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> had been subject to peer-review, Harrison noted that
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>we were surprised and disappointed to see the critics' letter being quoted in an issue of <i>Time</i> magazine (2 September 2002)... in which the authors repeated their charge that the book had not been peer-reviewed despite the assurances to the contrary that they had by then received by the press... It has become part of the anti-Lomborg folklore that this book bypassed the usual Cambridge peer-review process...  This is a charge that is repeated in many of the public and private attacks in the press, and it is unfounded.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Cambridge University Press maintained their position and the book was published.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_material_and_methods">Criticism of the material and methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism of the material and methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The January 2002 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> contained, under the heading "Misleading Math about the Earth", a set of essays by several scientists, which claim that Lomborg and <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> misrepresent both scientific evidence and scientific opinion.  The magazine then refused Lomborg's request to print a lengthy point-by-point rebuttal in his own defence, on the grounds that the 32 pages would have taken a disproportionate share of the month's installment. <i>Scientific American</i> allowed Lomborg a one-page defense in the May 2002 edition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and then attempted to remove Lomborg's publication of his complete response online, citing a copyright violation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After receiving much criticism, the magazine published his complete rebuttal on its website,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> along with the counter rebuttals of <a href="/wiki/John_Rennie_(editor)" title="John Rennie (editor)">John Rennie</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Holdren" title="John Holdren">John P. Holdren</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> also published a harsh review of Lomborg's book, in which <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Pimm" title="Stuart Pimm">Stuart Pimm</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_for_Environmental_Research_and_Conservation" title="Center for Environmental Research and Conservation">Center for Environmental Research and Conservation</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Harvey_(biologist)" title="Jeffrey Harvey (biologist)">Jeff Harvey</a> of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Netherlands_Institute_of_Ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Netherlands Institute of Ecology (page does not exist)">Netherlands Institute of Ecology</a> wrote: <i>"the text employs the strategy of those who, for example, argue that gay men aren't dying of AIDS, that Jews weren't singled out by the Nazis for extermination, and so on."</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Lomborg has also been criticized for using <a href="/wiki/Straw_man" title="Straw man">straw man</a> arguments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with charges that his Litany of environmental doom-mongering does not accurately represent the mainstream views of the contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_movement" title="Green movement">green movement</a>.
</p><p>The "separately written expert reviews" further detail the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expert_opinion" title="Expert opinion">expert opinions</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gleick" title="Peter Gleick">Peter Gleick</a>'s assessment, for example, states:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>There is nothing original or unique in Lomborg's book. Many of his criticisms have appeared in... previous works—and even in the work of environmental scientists themselves. What is new, perhaps, is the scope and variety of the errors he makes.</dd></dl>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerry_Mahlman" title="Jerry Mahlman">Jerry Mahlman</a>'s appraisal of the chapter he was asked to evaluate, states:
</p>
<dl><dd>I found some aspects of this chapter to be interesting, challenging, and logical. For example, the author's characterizations of the degree of difficulty in actually doing something meaningful about climate change through mitigation  and coping/adaptation are perceptive and valuable. In principle, such characterizations could provide a foundation for more meaningful policy planning on this difficult problem.  Unfortunately, the author's lack of rigor and consistency on these larger issues is likely to negate any real respect for his insights.</dd></dl>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Pimentel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Pimentel (page does not exist)">David Pimentel</a>, who was repeatedly criticized in the book, also wrote a critical review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_media_handling">Criticism of media handling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism of media handling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One critical article, "The Skeptical Environmentalist: A Case Study in the Manufacture of News",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> attributes this media success to its initial, influential supporters:
</p>
<dl><dd>"News of the pending book first appeared in the UK in early June of 2001 when a <i>Sunday Times</i> article by Nayab Chohan featured an advanced report of claims made by Lomborg that London's air was cleaner than at any time since 1585.  Headlined "Cleanest London Air for 400 Years," the publicity hook was both local and timely, as the tail end of the article linked the book's questioning of the Kyoto climate change protocol to U.S. president George W. Bush's visit the same week to Europe, and Bush's controversial opposition to the treaty. The<i> Times </i>followed up the report the next day with a news article further detailing the book's Kyoto protocol angle."</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>"With <i>The Times</i> reports, Lomborg and his claims had made the Anglo media agenda. As is typically the case, other media outlets followed the reporting of the elite newspaper. Articles pegging the claims of <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> to Bush's European visit ran later that week in the U.K's <i>The Express</i> and <i>Daily Telegraph</i>, and Canada's <i>Toronto Star</i>."</dd></dl>
<p>The media was criticized for the biased selection of reviewers and not informing readers of reviewers' background. Richard C. Bell, writing for Worldwatch noted that the Wall Street Journal, "instead of seeking scientists with a critical perspective," like many publications "put out reviews by people who were closely associated with Lomborg", with the Journal soliciting a review from the Competitive Enterprise Institute's Ronald Bailey, someone "who had earlier written a book called The True State of the World, from which much of Lomborg's claims were taken." Bell also criticized the Washington Post, whose Sunday Book World assigned the book review to <a href="/wiki/Denis_Dutton" title="Denis Dutton">Denis Dutton</a>, identified as "a professor of philosophy who lectures on the dangers of pseudoscience at the science faculties of the University of Canterbury in New Zealand", and as the editor of the web site <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts_and_Letters_Daily" title="Arts and Letters Daily">Arts and Letters Daily</a>. Bell noted that:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The Post did not tell its readers that Dutton's web site features links to the Global Climate Coalition, an anti-Kyoto consortium of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_Lobby" title="Energy Lobby">oil and coal businesses</a>, and to the messages of Julian Simon --the man whose <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">denial</a> that global warming was occurring apparently gave Lomborg the idea for his book in the first place. It was hardly surprising that Dutton anointed Lomborg's book as 'the most significant work on the environment since the appearance of its polar opposite, Rachel Carson's Silent Spring, in 1962. It's a magnificent achievement.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>"</p></blockquote>
<h4><span id="The_.22unrealistic.22_critique"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='The_"unrealistic"_critique'>The "unrealistic" critique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title='Edit section: The "unrealistic" critique'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some critics of <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> took issue not with the statistical investigation of Lomborg's Litany, but with the suggestions and conclusions for which they were the foundation. This line of criticism considered the book as a contribution to the policy debate over environment rather than the work of natural science. In a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> column from August 23, 2001, veteran BBC environmental correspondent <a href="/wiki/Alex_Kirby_(journalist)" title="Alex Kirby (journalist)">Alex Kirby</a> wrote:
</p>
<dl><dd>"I am neither a statistician nor a scientist, and I lack the skill to judge Lomborg's reworkings of the statistics of conventional wisdom. But I am worried that on virtually every topic he touches, he reaches conclusions radically different from almost everybody else. That seems to suggest that most scientists are wrong, short-sighted, naïve, interested only in securing research funds, or deliberately dancing to the campaigners' tune. Most I know are honest, intelligent and competent. So it beggars belief to suppose that Professor Lomborg is the only one in step, every single time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Kirby's first concern was not with the extensive research and statistical analysis, but the conclusions drawn from them:
</p>
<dl><dd>"What really riles me about his book is that it is so damnably reasonable. In the rational world that Bjørn Lomborg thinks we all inhabit, we would manage problems sensibly, one by one...But the real world is messier, more unpredictable - and more impatient."</dd></dl>
<p>On September 5, 2001, at a Lomborg book reading in England, British environmentalist author <a href="/wiki/Mark_Lynas" title="Mark Lynas">Mark Lynas</a> threw a cream pie in Lomborg's face. In a September 9, 2001, article, "Why I pied Lomborg", Lynas stated:
</p>
<dl><dd>"Lomborg specialises in presenting the reader with false choices - such as the assertion that money not spent on preventing climate change could be spent on bringing clean water to the developing world, thereby saving more lives per dollar of expenditure. Of course, in the real world, these are not the kind of choices we are faced with. Why not take the $60 billion from George Bush's stupid Son of Star Wars program and use that cash to save lives in Ethiopia? Because in a world where political choices are not made democratically at a global level, but by a small number of rich countries and corporations, the poor and the environment are never going to be a priority."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The December 12, 2001 issue of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grist_Magazine" title="Grist Magazine">Grist</a></i> devoted an issue to <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GristSkepticalLook_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-GristSkepticalLook-4">[4]</a></sup> with a series of essays from various scientists challenging individual sections. A separate article examining the book's overall approach took issue with the framing of Lomborg's conclusions:
</p>
<dl><dd>"Lomborg begins by making the entirely reasonable point that accurate information is critical to informed decision-making. If information is skewed to paint a bleaker environmental picture than is justified by reality, as he claims, then we will in turn skew our limited resources in favor of the environment and away from other important causes. ... Then Lomborg proceeds to weigh the causes championed by the environmental movement against a deliberately circumscribed universe of other possible "good causes." It is up to us, he says, to make responsible decisions about whether to protect the environment or "boost Medicaid, increase funding to the arts, or cut taxes. ... The worse they can make this state appear, the easier it is for them to convince us we need to spend more money on the environment rather on hospitals, kindergartens, etc." A few pages later he again claims that the purpose of the Litany is to cause us to prioritize the environment over "hospitals, child day care, etc." ... But who is really failing to consider how our money is spent? As Lomborg notes, "We will never have enough money," and therefore, "Prioritization is absolutely essential." Why, then, does he weigh the environment only against hospitals and childcare, rather than against, say, industry subsidies and defense spending?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Addressing the apparent difficulty of scientists opposing <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> in criticizing the book strictly on the basis of statistics and challenging the conclusions about areas of environmental sciences that were drawn from them, Lynas contends:
</p>
<dl><dd>"One of the biggest problems facing the environmental community in analyzing Lomborg’s book is that his work, as flawed as it is, has clearly been very time-consuming and meticulous. In a busy and under funded world, few people have the time or background knowledge to plow though 3,000 footnotes checking his sources. It is impressively interdisciplinary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support">Support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Influential UK newsweekly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> weighed in at the start with heavy support, publishing an advance essay by Lomborg in which he detailed his Litany, and following up with a highly favorable review and supportive coverage. It stated that "This is one of the most valuable books on public policy—not merely environmental policy— to have been written for the intelligent general reader in the past ten years...<i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> is a triumph."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the general media, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> stated that "The primary target of the book, a substantial work of analysis with almost 3,000 footnotes, are statements made by environmental organizations like the Worldwatch Institute, the World Wildlife Fund and Greenpeace." The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> deemed Lomborg's work "a superbly documented and readable book.". A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> review claimed that "Bjørn Lomborg's good news about the environment is bad news for Green ideologues. His richly informative, lucid book is now the place from which environmental policy decisions must be argued. In fact,<i> The Skeptical Environmentalist </i>is the most significant work on the environment since the appearance of its polar opposite, Rachel Carson's <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i>, in 1962. It's a magnificent achievement." <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> wrote that "Lomborg pulls off the remarkable feat of welding the techno-optimism of the Internet age with a lefty's concern for the fate of the planet."
</p><p>In March 2003 the <i>New York Law School Law Review</i> published<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> an examination of the critical reviews of <i>Skeptical Environmentalist</i> from the <i>Scientific American</i>, <i>Nature</i> and <i>Science</i> magazines by Professor of Law David Shoenbrod and then Senior Law Student Christi Wilson of New York Law School. The authors take the perspective of a court faced with an argument against hearing an expert witness in order to evaluate whether Lomborg was credible as an expert, and whether his testimony is valid to his expertise. They classify the types of criticisms leveled at Lomborg and his arguments, and proceed to evaluate each of the reasons given for disqualifying Lomborg. They conclude that a court should accept Lomborg as a credible expert in the field of statistics, and that his testimony was appropriately restricted to his area of expertise. Of course, Professor Shoenbrod and Wilson note, Mr. Lomborg's factual conclusions may not be correct, nor his policy proposals effective, but his criticisms should be addressed, not merely dismissed out of hand.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Concerned_Scientists" title="Union of Concerned Scientists">Union of Concerned Scientists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Committees_on_Scientific_Dishonesty" title="Danish Committees on Scientific Dishonesty">Danish Committees on Scientific Dishonesty</a> raised concern about the responses of certain sections of the scientific community to a peer reviewed book published under the category of environmental economics. The groups worried that the receptions to Lomborg were a politicization of science by scientists.  This unease was reflected in the involvement of the Union of Concerned Scientists and Danish Committees on Scientific Dishonesty in "When scientists politicize science: making sense of controversy over The Skeptical Environmentalist",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> where Roger A. Pielke argued:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The use of science by scientists as a means of negotiating for desired political outcomes – the politicization of science by scientists – threatens the development of effective policies in contested issues. By tying themselves to politics, rather than policy, scientists necessarily restrict their value and the value of their science.
</p></blockquote>
<p>In "Green with Ideology - The hidden agenda behind the "scientific" attacks on Bjørn Lomborg’s controversial book, The Skeptical Environmentalist", <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Bailey" title="Ronald Bailey">Ronald Bailey</a> stated that "The bitter anti-Lomborg campaign reveals the hidden crisis of what we might call ideological environmentalism." He further wrote:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Skeptical Environmentalist obviously should be held to high standards of accuracy, but to insist that it read like a scientific paper is both specious and disingenuous. The book is essentially a response to such popular environmentalist tracts as the State of the World report and the reams of misinformation disseminated by Greenpeace, Friends of the Earth, the Union of Concerned Scientists, The Ecologist, the Turning Point Project, Grist, Wild Earth, and the rest of the sprawling eco-media propaganda complex.
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_of_scientific_dishonesty">Accusations of scientific dishonesty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Accusations of scientific dishonesty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the publication of <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i>, Lomborg was accused of scientific dishonesty. Several environmental scientists brought a total of three complaints against Lomborg to the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Committees_on_Scientific_Dishonesty" title="Danish Committees on Scientific Dishonesty">Danish Committees on Scientific Dishonesty</a> (DCSD), a body under Denmark's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science,_Technology_and_Innovation_of_Denmark" title="Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation of Denmark">Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation</a>. Lomborg was asked whether he regarded the book as a "debate" publication, and thereby not under the purview of the DCSD, or as a scientific work; he chose the latter, clearing the way for the inquiry that followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icg2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-icg2008-24">[24]</a></sup> The charges claimed that <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> contained deliberately misleading data and flawed conclusions. Due to the similarity of the complaints, the DCSD decided to proceed on the three cases under one investigation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DCSD_investigation">DCSD investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: DCSD investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 6, 2003, a mixed DCSD ruling was released, in which the Committees decided that <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> was scientifically dishonest, but Lomborg was innocent of wrongdoing due to a lack of expertise in the relevant fields:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>"Objectively speaking, the publication of the work under consideration is deemed to fall within the concept of scientific dishonesty. ...In view of the subjective requirements made in terms of intent or gross negligence, however, Lomborg's publication cannot fall within the bounds of this characterization. Conversely, the publication is deemed clearly contrary to the standards of good scientific practice."</dd></dl>
<p>The DCSD cited <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> for:
</p>
<ul><li>Fabrication of data;</li>
<li>Selective discarding of unwanted results (selective citation);</li>
<li>Deliberately <a href="/wiki/Systemic_bias" title="Systemic bias">misleading use of statistical methods</a>;</li>
<li>Distorted interpretation of conclusions;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plagiarism" title="Plagiarism">Plagiarism</a>;</li>
<li>Deliberate misinterpretation of others' results.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MSTI_review_and_response">MSTI review and response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: MSTI review and response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 13, 2003, Lomborg filed a complaint against the DCSD's decision with the Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation (MSTI), which oversees the group.
</p><p>On December 17, 2003, the Ministry found that the DCSD had made a number of procedural errors, including:
</p>
<ul><li>Not using a precise standard for deciding "good scientific practice" in the social sciences;</li>
<li>Defining "objective scientific dishonesty" in a way unclear in determining whether "distortion of statistical data" had to be deliberate or not;</li>
<li>Not properly documenting that <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i> was a scientific publication on which they had the right to intervene in the first place;</li>
<li>Not providing specific statements on actual errors.</li></ul>
<p>The Ministry remitted the case to the DCSD. In doing so the Ministry indicated that it regarded the DCSD's previous findings of scientific dishonesty in regard to the book as invalid. The Ministry also instructed the DCSD to decide whether to reinvestigate. On March 12, 2004, the Committee formally decided not to act further on the complaints, reasoning that renewed scrutiny would, in all likelihood, result in the same conclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icg2008_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-icg2008-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Response_of_the_scientific_community">Response of the scientific community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Response of the scientific community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original DCSD decision about Lomborg provoked a <a href="/wiki/Petition" title="Petition">petition</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> among Danish academics from 308 scientists, many from the social sciences, who criticised the DCSD's investigative methods.
</p><p>Another group of Danish scientists collected signatures in support of the DCSD. The 640 signatures in this second petition came almost exclusively from the medical and <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a>, and included <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel laureate</a> in Chemistry <a href="/wiki/Jens_Christian_Skou" title="Jens Christian Skou">Jens Christian Skou</a>, former university <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kjeld_M%C3%B8llg%C3%A5rd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kjeld Møllgård (page does not exist)">Kjeld Møllgård</a>, and professor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Harremo%C3%ABs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Poul Harremoës (page does not exist)">Poul Harremoës</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Denmark" title="Technical University of Denmark">Technical University of Denmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continued_debate_and_criticism">Continued debate and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Continued debate and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A group of scientists published an article in 2005 in the <i>Journal of Information Ethics</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> in which they concluded that most criticism against Lomborg was unjustified, and that the scientific community had misused their authority to suppress the author.
</p>
<h3><span id="K.C3.A5re_Fog"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kåre_Fog">Kåre Fog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Skeptical_Environmentalist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Kåre Fog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The claim that allegations against Lomborg were unsubstantiated was challenged in the next issue of <i>Journal of Information Ethics</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> by Kåre Fog, one of the original DCSD petitioners. Fog reasserted his contention that, despite the ministry's decision, most of the accusations against Lomborg were valid, and rejected what he called "the Galileo hypothesis", which portrays Lomborg as a brave young man confronting an entrenched opposition.
</p><p>Fog has established a curated catalogue of criticisms against Lomborg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> which includes a section for each page of every <i>Skeptical Environmentalist</i> chapter. Fog enumerates and details what he believes to be flaws and errors in Lomborg's work. He explicitly indicates if particular mistakes may have been made deliberately by Lomborg, in order to mislead. According to Fog, since none of his denunciations of Lomborg's work have been proven false, the suspicion that Lomborg has misled deliberately is maintained. Lomborg has written a full text published online as <b>Godehetens Pris</b> (Danish) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> that goes through the main allegations put forward by Fog and others.
</p>
