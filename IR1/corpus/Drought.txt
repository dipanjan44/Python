<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Drought</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drought.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3504" data-file-width="2336" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Drought.jpg/170px-Drought.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Drought.jpg/255px-Drought.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Drought.jpg/340px-Drought.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drought.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contraction/Desiccation cracks in dry earth (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonoran_desert" title="Sonoran desert">Sonoran desert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>drought</b>  is a period of below-average precipitation in a given region, resulting in prolonged shortages in the water supply, whether atmospheric, <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">ground water</a>. A drought can last for months or years, or may be declared after as few as 15 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It can have a substantial impact on the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> of the affected region<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and harm to the local <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Annual dry seasons in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> significantly increase the chances of a drought developing and subsequent bush fires.  Periods of heat can significantly worsen drought conditions by hastening evaporation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>.
</p><p>Many plant species, such as those in the family Cactaceae (or <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cacti</a>), have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drought_tolerant" title="Drought tolerant">drought tolerance</a> adaptations like reduced leaf area and waxy cuticles to enhance their ability to tolerate drought. Some others survive dry periods as buried seeds. Semi-permanent drought produces arid biomes such as deserts and grasslands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Prolonged droughts have caused <a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">mass migrations</a> and humanitarian crisis.  Most arid ecosystems have inherently low productivity. The most prolonged drought ever in the world in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a> occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (400 Years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_drought">Causes of drought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes of drought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation_deficiency">Precipitation deficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precipitation deficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">Precipitation</a></div>
<p>Mechanisms of producing precipitation include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective" title="Convective">convective</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic</a> rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Convective processes involve strong vertical motions that can cause the overturning of the atmosphere in that location within an hour and cause heavy precipitation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convection_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-convection-8">[8]</a></sup> while stratiform processes involve weaker upward motions and less intense precipitation over a longer duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Precipitation can be divided into three categories, based on whether it falls as liquid water, liquid water that freezes on contact with the surface, or ice. Droughts occur mainly in areas where normal levels of rainfall are, in themselves, low. If these factors do not support precipitation volumes sufficiently to reach the surface over a sufficient time, the result is a drought.  Drought can be triggered by a high level of reflected sunlight and above average prevalence of high <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">pressure systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> carrying continental, rather than oceanic air masses, and ridges of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">high pressure areas</a> aloft can prevent or restrict the developing of thunderstorm activity or rainfall over one certain region.  Once a region is within drought, feedback mechanisms such as local arid air,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> hot conditions which can promote warm core ridging,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and minimal evapotranspiration can worsen drought conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_season">Dry season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dry season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">Dry season</a></div>
<p>Within the tropics, distinct, <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a> and dry <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> emerge due to the movement of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">Monsoon trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-12">[12]</a></sup> The dry season greatly increases drought occurrence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and is characterized by its low humidity, with watering holes and rivers drying up.  Because of the lack of these watering holes, many grazing animals are forced to migrate due to the lack of water and feed to more fertile spots. Examples of such animals are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zebras" title="Zebras">zebras</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elephants" title="Elephants">elephants</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wildebeest" title="Wildebeest">wildebeest</a>. Because of the lack of water in the plants, bushfires are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Since water vapor becomes more energetic with increasing temperature, more water vapor is required to increase relative humidity values to 100% at higher temperatures (or to get the temperature to fall to the dew point).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Periods of warmth quicken the pace of fruit and vegetable production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> increase evaporation and transpiration from plants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and worsen drought conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="El_Ni.C3.B1o"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="El_Niño">El Niño</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: El Niño">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:El_Nino_regional_impacts.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="539" data-file-width="486" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/El_Nino_regional_impacts.png/220px-El_Nino_regional_impacts.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/El_Nino_regional_impacts.png/330px-El_Nino_regional_impacts.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/El_Nino_regional_impacts.png/440px-El_Nino_regional_impacts.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:El_Nino_regional_impacts.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regional impacts of warm ENSO episodes (<a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a></div>
<p>Drier and hotter weather occurs in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> Basin, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> during El Niño events.  Winters during the El Niño are warmer and drier than average conditions in the Northwest, northern Midwest, and northern Mideast United States, so those regions experience reduced snowfalls.  Conditions are also drier than normal from December to February in south-central Africa, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>.  Direct effects of El Niño resulting in drier conditions occur in parts of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Australia" title="Northern Australia">Northern Australia</a>, increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_fire" title="Bush fire">bush fires</a>, worsening <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>, and decreasing air quality dramatically. Drier-than-normal conditions are also in general observed in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, inland <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, inland <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> from June to August.  As warm water spreads from the west Pacific and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the east Pacific, it causes extensive drought in the western Pacific. Singapore experienced the driest February in 2014 since records began in 1869, with only 6.3 mm of rain falling in the month and temperatures hitting as high as 35 °C on 26 February. The years 1968 and 2005 had the next driest Februaries, when 8.4 mm of rain fell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion_and_human_activities">Erosion and human activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Erosion and human activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">Aeolian processes</a></div>
<p>Human activity can directly trigger exacerbating factors such as over farming, excessive <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galilee_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galilee-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> adversely impact the ability of the land to capture and hold water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  In arid climates, the main source of erosion is wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erosion_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erosion-23">[23]</a></sup>  Erosion can be the result of material movement by the wind.  The wind can cause small particles to be lifted and therefore moved to another region (deflation).  Suspended particles within the wind may impact on solid objects causing erosion by abrasion (ecological succession). Wind erosion generally occurs in areas with little or no vegetation, often in areas where there is insufficient rainfall to support vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fields_outside_benambra.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="924" data-file-width="2250" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/220px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/330px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/440px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fields_outside_benambra.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fields outside <a href="/wiki/Benambra,_Victoria" title="Benambra, Victoria">Benambra, Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> suffering from drought conditions.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">Loess</a> is a homogeneous, typically nonstratified, porous, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friable" title="Friable">friable</a>, slightly coherent, often calcareous, fine-grained, <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silty</a>, pale yellow or buff, windblown (<a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">Aeolian</a>) <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It generally occurs as a widespread blanket deposit that covers areas of hundreds of square kilometers and tens of meters thick. Loess often stands in either steep or vertical faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Loess tends to develop into highly rich soils. Under appropriate climatic conditions, areas with loess are among the most agriculturally productive in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Loess deposits are geologically unstable by nature, and will erode very readily. Therefore, windbreaks (such as big trees and bushes) are often planted by farmers to reduce the wind erosion of loess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erosion_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Erosion-23">[23]</a></sup>  Wind erosion is much more severe in arid areas and during times of drought. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>, it is estimated that soil loss due to wind erosion can be as much as 6100 times greater in drought years than in wet years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatic_changes">Climatic changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climatic changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a></div>
<p>Activities resulting in global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> are expected to trigger droughts with a <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">substantial impact on agriculture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> throughout the world, and especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nation" title="Developing nation">developing nations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Overall, global warming will result in increased world rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Along with drought in some areas, flooding and erosion will increase in others. Paradoxically, some proposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">solutions to global warming</a> that focus on more active techniques, <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">solar radiation management</a> through the use of a <a href="/wiki/Space_sunshade" title="Space sunshade">space sunshade</a> for one, may also carry with them increased chances of drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewScientist_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewScientist-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_drought">Types of drought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types of drought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a drought persists, the conditions surrounding it gradually worsen and its impact on the local population gradually increases. People tend to define droughts in three main ways:
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorological</a> drought is brought about when there is a prolonged time with less than average precipitation. Meteorological drought usually precedes the other kinds of drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAAsurprise_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAAsurprise-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agricultural</a> droughts affect crop production or the ecology of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a>. This condition can also arise independently from any change in precipitation levels when <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> conditions and erosion triggered by poorly planned agricultural endeavors cause a shortfall in water available to the crops. However, in a traditional drought, it is caused by an extended period of below average precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">Hydrological</a> drought is brought about when the water reserves available in sources such as <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a> fall below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical" title="Statistical">statistical</a> <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">average</a>. Hydrological drought tends to show up more slowly because it involves stored water that is used but not replenished. Like an agricultural drought, this can be triggered by more than just a loss of rainfall. For instance, around 2007 <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> was awarded a large amount of money by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> to restore water that had been diverted to other nations from the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> under <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Similar circumstances also place their largest lake, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Balkhash" title="Lake Balkhash">Balkhash</a>, at risk of completely drying out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences_of_drought">Consequences of drought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Consequences of drought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="475" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg/220px-Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg/330px-Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg/440px-Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mongolian_Gazelle_dead_of_drought.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_gazelle" title="Mongolian gazelle">Mongolian gazelle</a> dead due to drought.</div></div></div><p> One can divide the effects of droughts and water shortages into three groups: environmental, economic and social.
</p><ul><li>In the case of environmental effects: lower surface and subterranean water-levels, lower flow-levels (with a decrease below the minimum leading to direct danger for amphibian life), increased pollution of surface water, the drying out of wetlands, more and larger fires, higher deflation intensity, <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>, worse health of trees and the appearance of pests and dendroid diseases.</li>
<li>Economic losses include lower agricultural, forests, game and fishing output, higher food-production costs, lower energy-production levels in hydro plants, losses caused by depleted water tourism and transport revenue, problems with water supply for the energy sector and for technological processes in metallurgy, mining, the chemical, paper, wood, foodstuff industries etc., disruption of water supplies for municipal economies.</li>
<li>Social costs include the negative effect on the health of people directly exposed to this phenomenon (excessive heat waves), possible limitation of water supplies, increased pollution levels, high food-costs, stress caused by failed harvests, etc. This explains why droughts and fresh water shortages operate as a factor which increases the gap between developed and developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Effects vary according to vulnerability. For example, subsistence farmers are more likely to migrate during drought because they do not have alternative food-sources. Areas with populations that depend on water sources as a major food-source are more vulnerable to famine.
</p><p>Drought can also reduce water quality,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> because lower water-flows reduce dilution of pollutants and increase <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> of remaining water-sources. Common consequences of drought include:
</p>
<ul><li>Diminished <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop growth or yield productions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> for <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_bowl" title="Dust bowl">Dust bowls</a>, themselves a sign of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, which further erode the <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_storms" title="Dust storms">Dust storms</a>, when drought hits an area suffering from desertification and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">Famine</a> due to lack of water for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">Habitat</a> damage, affecting both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregion" title="Terrestrial ecoregion">terrestrial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic</a> wildlife<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">Hunger</a> – drought provides too little water to support food crops.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a> and related diseases</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">Mass migration</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Internally_displaced_person" title="Internally displaced person">internal displacement</a> and international <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a></li>
<li>Reduced <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity production</a> due to reduced water-flow through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_energy" title="Hydroelectric energy">hydroelectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dams</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>Shortages of water for <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> users<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">Snake</a> migration, which results in snake-bites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>Social <a href="/wiki/Unrest" title="Unrest">unrest</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/War" title="War">War</a> over natural resources, including water and food</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildfires" title="Wildfires">Wildfires</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bushfire" title="Bushfire">bushfires</a>, become more common during times of drought and may cause human deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>Exposure and oxidation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_sulfate_soils" title="Acid sulfate soils">acid sulfate soils</a> due to falling surface- and ground-water levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MosleyA2014_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-MosleyA2014-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MosleyB2014_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-MosleyB2014-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MosleyC2014_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-MosleyC2014-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyanotoxin" title="Cyanotoxin">Cyanotoxin</a> accumulation within food chains and water supply (some of which are among the most potent toxins known to science) can cause cancer with low exposure over the long term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> High levels of <a href="/wiki/Microcystin" title="Microcystin">microcystin</a> appeared in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> salt-water shellfish and fresh-water supplies throughout the state of California in 2016.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Globally">Globally</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Globally">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drought is a normal, recurring feature of the climate in most parts of the world. It is among the earliest documented climatic events, present in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a> and tied to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> story of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_(Hebrew_Bible)" title="Joseph (Hebrew Bible)">Joseph</a>'s arrival in and the later <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">Exodus</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Hunter-gatherer migrations in 9,500 BC Chile have been linked to the phenomenon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> as has the exodus of early humans <a href="/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans" title="Recent African origin of modern humans">out of Africa</a> and into the rest of the world around 135,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas,_South_Dakota_1936.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg/220px-Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg/330px-Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg/440px-Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas%2C_South_Dakota_1936.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dust_Bowl_-_Dallas,_South_Dakota_1936.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A South Dakota farm during the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a>, 1936</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_droughts" title="List of droughts">List of droughts</a></div>
<p>Well-known historical droughts include:
</p>
<ul><li>1540 Central Europe, said to be the “worst drought of the millennium” with eleven months without rain and temperatures of five to seven °C above the average of the 20<sup>th</sup> century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>1900 India killing between 250,000 and 3.25 million.</li>
<li>1921–22 Soviet Union in which over 5 million perished from starvation due to drought</li>
<li>1928–30 Northwest China resulting in over 3 million deaths by famine.</li>
<li>1936 and 1941 Sichuan Province China resulting in 5 million and 2.5 million deaths respectively.</li>
<li>The 1997–2009 <a href="/wiki/2000s_Australian_drought" title="2000s Australian drought">Millennium Drought</a> in Australia led to a water supply crisis across much of the country.  As a result, many desalination plants were built for the first time (<a href="/wiki/List_of_desalination_plants_in_Australia" title="List of desalination plants in Australia">see list</a>).</li>
<li>In 2006, Sichuan Province China experienced its worst drought in modern times with nearly 8 million people and over 7 million cattle facing water shortages.</li>
<li>12-year drought that was devastating southwest Western Australia, southeast South Australia, Victoria and northern Tasmania was "very severe and without historical precedent".</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="925" data-file-width="2000" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png/220px-Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png/330px-Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png/440px-Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sahel_Map-Africa_rough.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Affected areas in the western <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> belt during the <a href="/wiki/2012_Sahel_drought" title="2012 Sahel drought">2012 drought</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Darfur conflict in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, also affecting <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, was fueled by decades of drought; combination of drought, <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a> are among the causes of the Darfur conflict, because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> <a href="/wiki/Baggara" title="Baggara">Baggara</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomads" title="Nomads">nomads</a> searching for water have to take their livestock further south, to land mainly occupied by non-Arab farming people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately 2.4 billion people live in the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> of the Himalayan rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> could experience floods followed by droughts in coming decades. <a href="/wiki/Drought_in_India" title="Drought in India">Drought in India</a> affecting the Ganges is of particular concern, as it provides <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> and agricultural <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> for more than 500 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The west coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, which gets much of its water from glaciers in mountain ranges such as the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, also would be affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka,_India,_2012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="536" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg/220px-Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg/330px-Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg/440px-Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka%2C_India%2C_2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drought_affected_area_in_Karnataka,_India,_2012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drought affected area in <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in 2012.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, parts of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> experienced the worst drought in 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A 23 July 2006 article reported <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Research_Center" title="Woods Hole Research Center">Woods Hole Research Center</a> results showing that the forest in its present form could survive only three years of drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Scientists at the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Amazonian_Research" title="National Institute of Amazonian Research">National Institute of Amazonian Research</a> argue in the article that this drought response, coupled with the effects of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> on regional climate, are pushing the rainforest towards a "<a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping point</a>" where it would irreversibly start to die. It concludes that the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> is on the brink of being turned into <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> or <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, with catastrophic consequences for the world's climate. According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>, the combination of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and deforestation increases the drying effect of dead trees that fuels forest fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="566" data-file-width="540" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg/220px-ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg/330px-ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg/440px-ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ShrinkingLakeChad-1973-1997-EO.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Chad" title="Lake Chad">Lake Chad</a> in a 2001 satellite image. The lake has shrunk by 95% since the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>By far the largest part of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is <a href="/wiki/Deserts_of_Australia" title="Deserts of Australia">desert</a> or semi-arid lands commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">outback</a>. A 2005 study by Australian and American researchers investigated the desertification of the interior, and suggested that one explanation was related to <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> settlers who arrived about 50,000 years ago. Regular burning by these settlers could have prevented <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a> from reaching interior Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In June 2008 it became known that an expert panel had warned of long term, maybe irreversible, severe ecological damage for the whole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray-Darling_basin" title="Murray-Darling basin">Murray-Darling basin</a> if it did not receive sufficient water by October 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Australia could experience more severe droughts and they could become more frequent in the future, a government-commissioned report said on July 6, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Australian environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Tim_Flannery" title="Tim Flannery">Tim Flannery</a>, predicted that unless it made drastic changes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> could become the world’s first <a href="/wiki/Ghost_town" title="Ghost town">ghost metropolis</a>, an abandoned city with no more water to sustain its population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>  The long Australian <a href="/wiki/2000s_Australian_drought" title="2000s Australian drought">Millennial drought</a> broke in 2010.
</p><p>Recurring droughts leading to <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> have created grave ecological catastrophes, prompting food shortages in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1983%E2%80%9385_famine_in_Ethiopia" title="1983–85 famine in Ethiopia">1984–85</a>, <a href="/wiki/2006_Horn_of_Africa_food_crisis" title="2006 Horn of Africa food crisis">2006</a> and <a href="/wiki/2011_East_Africa_drought" title="2011 East Africa drought">2011</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODI-77">[77]</a></sup> During the 2011 drought, an estimated 50,000 to 150,000 people were reported to have died,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> though these figures and the extent of the crisis are disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mafhscbafro_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mafhscbafro-79">[79]</a></sup> In February 2012, the UN announced that the crisis was over due to a scaling up of relief efforts and a bumper harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unsfisiobrr_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unsfisiobrr-80">[80]</a></sup> Aid agencies subsequently shifted their emphasis to recovery efforts, including digging irrigation canals and distributing plant seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unsfisiobrr_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unsfisiobrr-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, a severe <a href="/wiki/2012_Sahel_drought" title="2012 Sahel drought">drought</a> struck the western <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist_Relief_%26_Development_Fund_(MRDF)" title="Methodist Relief &amp; Development Fund (MRDF)">Methodist Relief &amp; Development Fund (MRDF)</a> reported that more than 10 million people in the region were at risk of famine due to a month-long heat wave that was hovering over <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>. A fund of about £20,000 was distributed to the drought-hit countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Protection.2C_mitigation_and_relief"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection,_mitigation_and_relief">Protection, mitigation and relief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protection, mitigation and relief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_(etc).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg/220px-Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg/330px-Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg/440px-Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_%28etc%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Huntington_Desert_Garden_Cactus_(etc).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Succulent plants are well-adapted to survive long periods of drought.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="300" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg/170px-FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg/255px-FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_917_-_Photograph_by_Angel_Santiago_taken_on_04-03-1998_in_Marshall_Islands.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water distribution on <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Agriculturally, people can effectively mitigate much of the impact of drought through irrigation and crop rotation. Failure to develop adequate drought mitigation strategies carries a grave human cost in the modern era, exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">ever-increasing</a> population densities.  President Roosevelt on April 27, 1935, signed documents creating the Soil Conservation Service (SCS)—now the Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS).  Models of the law were sent to each state where they were enacted.  These were the first enduring practical programs to curtail future susceptibility to drought, creating agencies that first began to stress soil conservation measures to protect farm lands today.  It was not until the 1950s that there was an importance placed on water conservation was put into the existing laws (NRCS 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="716" data-file-width="720" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/220px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/330px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/440px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerosols over the Amazon each September for four burning seasons (2005 through 2008) during the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> drought. The aerosol scale (yellow to dark reddish-brown) indicates the relative amount of particles that absorb sunlight.</div></div></div>
<p>Strategies for drought protection, mitigation or relief include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">Dams</a> – many dams and their associated reservoirs supply additional water in times of drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">Cloud seeding</a> – a form of intentional weather modification to induce rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>  This remains a hotly debated topic, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Research_Council_(United_States)" title="National Research Council (United States)">United States National Research Council</a> released a report in 2004 stating that to date, there is still no convincing scientific proof of the efficacy of intentional weather modification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">Desalination</a> – use of sea water for irrigation or consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></li>
<li>Drought monitoring – Continuous observation of rainfall levels and comparisons with current usage levels can help prevent man-made drought. For instance, analysis of water usage in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> has revealed that their <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> (underground water level) is put at grave risk by over-use to fertilize their <a href="/wiki/Khat" title="Khat">Khat</a> crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Careful monitoring of moisture levels can also help predict increased risk for wildfires, using such metrics as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keetch-Byram_Drought_Index" title="Keetch-Byram Drought Index">Keetch-Byram Drought Index</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-49">[49]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmer_Drought_Index" title="Palmer Drought Index">Palmer Drought Index</a>.</li>
<li>Land use – Carefully planned <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a> can help to minimize <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and allow farmers to plant less water-dependent crops in drier years.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Outdoor_water-use_restriction" title="Outdoor water-use restriction">Outdoor water-use restriction</a> – Regulating the use of sprinklers, hoses or buckets on outdoor plants, filling pools, and other water-intensive home maintenance tasks.  <a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">Xeriscaping</a> yards can significantly reduce unnecessary water use by residents of towns and cities.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a> – Collection and storage of rainwater from roofs or other suitable catchments.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycled_water" title="Recycled water">Recycled water</a> – Former wastewater (sewage) that has been treated and purified for reuse.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transvasement" title="Transvasement">Transvasement</a> – Building canals or redirecting rivers as massive attempts at <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> in drought-prone areas.</li></ul>
