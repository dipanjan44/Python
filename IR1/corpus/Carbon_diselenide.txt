</tbody></table><p><b>Carbon diselenide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the chemical formula CSe<sub>2</sub>. It is a yellow-orange oily liquid with pungent odor. It is an analogue of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> (CS<sub>2</sub>). This light-sensitive compound is insoluble in water and soluble in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvents" title="Organic solvents">organic solvents</a>.
</p><h2><span id="Synthesis.2C_structure_and_reactions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis,_structure_and_reactions">Synthesis, structure and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_diselenide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis, structure and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon diselenide is a linear molecule with D<sub>∞h</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">symmetry</a>. It is produced by reacting <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> powder with <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> vapor near 550 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fackler_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fackler-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Se + CH<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub> → CSe<sub>2</sub> + 2 HCl</dd></dl>
<p>It was first reported by Grimm and Metzger, who prepared it by treating <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_selenide" title="Hydrogen selenide">hydrogen selenide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> in a hot tube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a>, carbon diselenide polymerizes under high pressure. The structure of the polymer is thought to be a head-to-head structure with a <a href="/wiki/Backbone_chain" title="Backbone chain">backbone</a> in the form of –[Se–C(=Se)–C(=Se)–Se]–.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
The polymer is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> with a room-temperature conductivity of 50 S/cm.
</p><p>In addition, carbon diselenide is a precursor to tetraselenafulvalenes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the selenium analogue of <a href="/wiki/Tetrathiafulvalene" title="Tetrathiafulvalene">tetrathiafulvalene</a>, which can be further used to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Conductive_polymer" title="Conductive polymer">organic conductors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_superconductor" title="Organic superconductor">organic superconductors</a>.
</p><p>Carbon diselenide reacts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_amine" title="Secondary amine">secondary amines</a> to give dialkydiselenocarbamates:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fackler_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fackler-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Et<sub>2</sub>NH + CSe<sub>2</sub> → (Et<sub>2</sub>NH<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>)(Et<sub>2</sub>NCSe<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>)</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_diselenide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon diselenide has high vapor pressure. It has a moderate <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> and presents an inhalation hazard. It may be dangerous due to its easy <a href="/wiki/Membrane_transport" title="Membrane transport">membrane transport</a>. It decomposes slowly in storage (about 1% per month at –30 °C). When obtained commercially, its cost is high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mixed with air, carbon diselenide releases an extremely offensive odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its smell forced an evacuation of a nearby village when it was first synthesized in 1936. The stench can be neutralized by oxidizing with <a href="/wiki/Bleach" title="Bleach">bleach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of the odor, synthetic pathways have been developed to avoid its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patent1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-patent1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
