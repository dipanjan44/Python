<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_policy">Climate policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_Strategy_2001">Climate Strategy 2001</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate Strategy 2001">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Finnish national renewable energy program was done in 1999 and it was accepted as the national climate strategy in 2001. It included targets for the renewable energy but no limit in the use of the fossil and nuclear energy. The target can be compared to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_Directive" title="EU Directive">EU Directive</a> 2001/77/EU that also promoted renewable energy in the electricity production.
</p><p>The government ordered the evaluation report, published in February 2003, from the Electrowatt-Ekono Oy that was part of <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B6yry" title="Pöyry">Pöyry</a>. Pöyry is a national and international company having tradition in the development and consulting of the forest industry. According to this evaluation report Finland’s national target was to increase during 1995-2010:
</p>
<ul><li>Renewables of primary energy +36 TWh (achieved 1995-2001: 17 TWh)</li>
<li>Renewables of electricity +8,35 TWh (achieved 1995-2001: 3,1 TWh)</li></ul>
<p>By fuel:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">Bioenergy</a> +33 TWh (achieved 1995-2001 16 TWh)</li>
<li>Bioelectricity +6,2 TWh (achieved 1995–2001: 2,8 TWh)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">Hydropower</a> +1 TWh  (achieved 1995–2010, 23 TWh)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">Wind power</a> +1.1 TWh (494 MW) (achieved 1995-2001 59 GWh 32 MW)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">Solar energy</a> 50 GWh warming 50 GWh electricity 40 MW capacity (achieved 1995-2001 2 GWh 1 GWh 1,5 MW)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">Heat pumps</a> 1 TWh (achieved 1995-2001 250 GWh)</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Finland.E2.80.99s_National_Climate_Change_Adaptation_Plan_2022"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland’s_National_Climate_Change_Adaptation_Plan_2022">Finland’s National Climate Change Adaptation Plan 2022</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Finland’s National Climate Change Adaptation Plan 2022">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nature and society are strongly impacted by the global climate change and adaptations are needed. For this reason Finland implemented a National Climate Change Adaptation Plan for 2022 which aims to handle the risks associated with climate change and adapt to changes in climate for the Finnish society. The goals of the plan are: A) Adaptation has to be integrated into the planning and activities in different sectors and their stakeholders; B) Stakeholders need to have access to climate change assessment and management methods and C) Research and development, communication and education need to improve the adaptive capacity of the society, developed innovative solutions and improved citizens’ awareness on climate change adaptation. The aim of the National Climate Change Adaptation Plan is to establish the adaptation for the climate change until 2022. The National Climate Change Adaptation Plan implements the EU Strategy on Adaptation to Climate Change within Finland..<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions">Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Finland" title="Energy in Finland">Energy in Finland</a></div>
<p>Finland is a member of the European Union. The EU aims in the <a href="/wiki/2014_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> a legally binding 40% drop in emissions by 2030 against carbon output in 1990 as baseline. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Mr. Heikki Simola <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Association_for_Nature_Conservation" title="Finnish Association for Nature Conservation">Finnish Association for Nature Conservation</a> Finnish forest management has made Finnish forest and mire ecosystems as  a considerable net source of carbon into the atmosphere for decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, Finland's carbon dioxide emissions amounted to 58.8 million tonnes; 12.5 million tonnes less than the amount in 1990. However, this figure had resulted from a 6 per cent increase from 2015; nevertheless, it is still 18 per cent lower than in 1990. The largest factors explaining the growth in emissions between 2015-16 were the increase in coal consumption and the decline in the proportion of biofuels used in transport. Emissions grew in some sectors; these sectors include energy, where it went up by eight per cent (or 3.3 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent); industrial processes, where product use increased by four per cent (or 0.3 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent). Emissions from agriculture grew by one per cent (or 0.04 million tonnes of CO2). Emissions from transport rose by 1.5 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent, and from the production of electricity and heat, they rose by 1.4 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Year 2017 climate gas emissions compared to 1990 were in Helsinki - 24%, Espoo + 8 %, Vantaa +2% and Kauniainen -9 %. Ca 80 % of emissions weree from heating and transport. Summary report does not include emissions from air travel and construction nor verify that these emissions were indifferent to climate change. Summary report does not specify domestic industry emissions abroad. E.g. <a href="/wiki/Fortum" title="Fortum">Fortum</a> acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Uniper" title="Uniper">Uniper</a> Germany may increase this company total emissions so meaningfully that foreign emission have importance to climate change. Fortum headquarters is in Espoo Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contribution_abroad">Contribution abroad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contribution abroad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to biologist Sesse Koivisto, whose husband contributed in the <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_Zoo" title="Helsinki Zoo">Helsinki Zoo</a>, Finnish forest company <a href="/wiki/UPM_(company)" title="UPM (company)">UPM</a> plant is an ecological  risk in Uruguay. In November 2017 UPM aims to build the biggest cellulose plant in the world in <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>. The plant will use planted <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> forests as raw material. According to   Sesse Koivisto  eucalyptus use a lot of water and will contaminate the soil. Concerns include following: Is there risk that endemic forest area will decline directly or indirectly because of the new eucalyptus areas? How does the eucalyptus <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a> of nature? Will they promote <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_energy">Nuclear energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nuclear energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Kyoto agreement Sweden was permitted lower emission decline targets based on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_phase-out" title="Nuclear power phase-out">Nuclear power phase-out</a>. Respectively Finnish emission cut obligations may be increased based on higher nuclear dependency.  
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fortum" title="Fortum">Fortum</a> is half state owned energy company. Fortum energy strategy is large investments in the nuclear energy in Finland, Sweden and Russia. According to <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a> Fortum aims to invest 15 per cent in a controversial Finnish nuclear power plant to be built by <a href="/wiki/Rosatom" title="Rosatom">Rosatom</a>, the Russian state-owned energy company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Fortum has saved no funds to invest in the new <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> forms. Until end of 2014 Finnish governments have given no obligations in the new renewables for companies, industry or municipals. Russia had interest to build and share own a nuclear plant in Finland in 2014 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine" title="2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine">2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2014_Crimean_crisis" title="2014 Crimean crisis">2014 Crimean crisis</a>. Unlike Finland most other European countries demanded to decline energy dependency from Russia. 
</p><p>The Finnish governments support nuclear energy by allocating majority of the risks, accident costs and nuclear waste costs to the tax payers. Finnish nuclear energy is informed to be risk free. According to Helsingin Sanomat reporting same assurance had been given in  Japan before the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster</a>.  
</p><p>Finland was among the top five greenhouse gas emitters in 2001: The <b>consumption emissions per capita of greenhouse gases in 2001</b> of the top 5 countries were <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">US</a> 29 tonnes, <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Australia" title="Energy in Australia">Australia</a> 21 tonnes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_Canada" title="Energy in Canada">Canada</a> 20 tonnes, <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Switzerland" title="Energy in Switzerland">Switzerland</a> 18 tonnes and <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Finland" title="Energy in Finland">Finland</a> 18 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2010-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>European Union aims in 2014 demanding targets to decline emissions 40% from 1990 level to 2030. In Finnish traffic this goal demands decline from (Mtn CO2) 12.48 to 7.4. As linear reduction this objective is  annual decline in value of 0.30 from the top year emission 13.36 in 2010. This objective equals maximum emission levels of 12,16 (2014) and 11,56 (2016). Finnish traffic warming emissions (million tonnes CO2) were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>1990 - 12,48</dd>
<dd>2008 - 13,42</dd>
<dd>2009 - 12,75</dd>
<dd>2010 - 13,36</dd>
<dd>2011 - 13,23</dd>
<dd>2012 – 12,68</dd></dl>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Katainen_Cabinet" title="Katainen Cabinet">Katainen Cabinet</a> minister <a href="/wiki/Merja_Kyll%C3%B6nen" title="Merja Kyllönen">Merja Kyllönen</a> asked a leader of the multinational oil and gas company <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a> representative as head of the committee to give recommendations for the future traffic policy in Finland.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jyrki_Katainen" title="Jyrki Katainen">Jyrki Katainen</a> suggested in December 2014 EU to fund in Finnland a <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas_terminal" title="Liquefied natural gas terminal">liquefied natural gas terminal</a> while in respect to climate change challenge and ongoing <a href="/wiki/2014_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> Katainen neglected initiatives in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_phase-out" title="Fossil-fuel phase-out">fossil-fuel phase-out</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_growth">Population growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Population growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>World population grew from 1990 to 2017 in value of 42 %. If population grows, decline of emissions is likely to be more demanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Population growth in the Finnish biggest cities from 1990 to 2017 was: <a href="/wiki/Espoo" title="Espoo">Espoo</a> 62%, <a href="/wiki/Oulu" title="Oulu">Oulu</a> 48 %, <a href="/wiki/Vantaa" title="Vantaa">Vantaa</a> 44% <a href="/wiki/Tampere" title="Tampere">Tampere</a> 34 % ja <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> 31 %.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> &gt; International climate change emission statistics ignore the construction emissions which may distort development. There are empty houses in the smaller cities but more and more new houses are built in the big cities. No restriction of population growth in the Finnish cities exist, like sterilization of men in the big cities with one child or two children.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agreements_and_law">Agreements and law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Agreements and law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_European_Union" title="Climate change in the European Union">Climate change in the European Union</a></div>
<p>Finland is a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> and thus the EU directives are binding in Finland. Finland has approved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a>. Finnish government has approved that human indused greenhouse gases cause the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Despite this the most harmful use of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> as energy has been financially promoted by Finnish government since 2005. Regarding the climate change expenses, the Polluter Pay -principle has been neglected in Finland at least until 2011.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_and_peat">Coal and peat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Coal and peat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Peat_energy_in_Finland" title="Peat energy in Finland">Peat energy in Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal_in_Finland" title="Coal in Finland">Coal in Finland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coal_in_Europe" title="Coal in Europe">Coal in Europe</a></div>
<p>CO2 emissions from <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> were 15% and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and peat 39% of total <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> emissions in Finland in 2006.
</p><p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a> peatlands are the main carbon storage and their protection is one of the main issues in the climate change mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEPBali_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNEPBali-13">[13]</a></sup> Peat land drainage destroys the habitat of many species, and heavily fuels climate change. <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a> is the most harmful energy source for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> in Finland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In conflict with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">IEA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> reports Finnish Ministry of Trade and Industry claim peat to be renewable energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, it is undisputed fact that peat is formed during 10,000 years in favourable conditions. Finland has ditched majority of its wetlands. The Finnish peat companies have had activity also abroad like in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. According to IEA country report the Finnish subsidies for peat in 2007-2010 undermined the goal to reduce CO2 emissions and counteracted The European Union emissions trading scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The state owned research institute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VTT" title="VTT">VTT</a> director Satu Helynen had close connection with the peat industry in 2010. She proposed for the government to exclude <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> for peat in 2010. Moreover, she tried to suppress all the second opinions of her collegees in VTT in conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a> and research ethics. After this conflict came public VTT wrote new directions following: "<i>Scientists should prevent all criticism of the content of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VTT" title="VTT">VTT</a> publications publicly after the publications.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The annual Finnish mean temperature has risen 2.3 °C since the middle of the 19th century. Warming has been greatest in early winter, nearly 5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The month of July 2018 in Finland had the highest-ever temperatures recorded by the Finnish Meteorological Institute. (FMI) FMI was founded in 1838.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature">Nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate change brings new southern species to the <a href="/wiki/%C3%85land_Islands" title="Åland Islands">Åland Islands</a>. In 2012 was found a fly known only in England, Denmark, the Czech Republic and Hungary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather_events"><a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a> events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Extreme weather events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Summer 2010 storms (Asta 29.-30.7 Vera 4.8, Lahja 7.8 and Sylvi 8.8) caused widespread damages. Insurance companies paid 81.6 million € for the storm damages. Trees fell 9.1 million m<sup>3</sup>. 480 000 persons had power line brakes, max 6 weeks. 35 000 km of the power line was damaged. Compensations costs were over 10 million € for the power customers. Other power company costs included 18 million € for the repair and 4 million $ for extra investments. Co-operation between the responsible organizations and persons did not run effectively after the storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Storm <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Dagmar" title="Cyclone Dagmar">Tapani</a> hit Finland on St Stephen's Day 26.12.2011. The previous storm of this type in Finland was Janika in November 2001. A highest momentary gust on land was 31.5 m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  On 27.12.2011 at highest over 300,000 homes had no electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finland received a heat wave above + 30 °C in June 2013 at same period as the <a href="/wiki/2013_European_floods" title="2013 European floods">2013 European floods</a> hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, autumn storm Eino resulted in over 200,000 homes becoming dark in Finland, ca 10% of families. Maximum wind speed was 27.3 m/s in land and 32.9 m/s in see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_by_region">Climate change by region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Climate change by region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Uusimaa">Uusimaa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Uusimaa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Uusimaa target is to be carbon zero in 2050. Lohja, Raasepori, Siuntio and Hanko have target to decline the emissions 80% from 2007 to 2030. In 2013 Uusimaa emissions were close to year 1995 level and in Uusimaa there was no decline in emissions since year 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Finland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Finland, and especially the northern rural parts of the country, has a variety of nature-based tourism attractions, such as down-hill skiing, snowmobiling and dog sledding. Since the 1980´s, the christmas tourism has emerged in Finnish Lapland, as an image of that being a winter wonderland has developed, creating job opportunities but also a tourism which success is depending on cold temperatures and snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The revenue of the Finnish tourism industry was 16,2 billion USD in 2015, and is expected to reach 18,6 billion USD in 2020. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>About 97% of the finnish population takes part in recreational activities, and about 40% participates in nature-based tourism. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to climate change projections, the crucial changes in temperature will occur during the winter where the predicted warming until 2040 is 1,2-5 C. This change in temperature will decrease the number of days where temperature is below 0 C, and scientists predict that the date of the first frost will occur 15-30 days later than today, while the last frost day will occur 15-30 days earlier than today. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>The number of days with snow cover is predicted to decrease to 40-60 days at the end of the 21st century where the greatest decrease will happen in the south-western parts of Finland, compared to today´s annual number of 120 (in southern Finland) - 240 (in northern Finland) days with snow cover. The winter precipitation is predicted to increase, which in short term may lead increasing levels of snow in the northern and central parts of Finland, but long-term, it might lead to a diminished snow cover in the entire country. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>With warmer temperatures, winter precipitation may fall as rain to a greater extent than at present day, which could decrease the snow cover depth and lead to icy surfaces, hindering movement and change the aesthetics of the landscape. A decrease in snow and ice and changes in the quality of the snow, is considered a threat for many nature-based tourism activities, but a shorter winter season is, however, not a threat for all entrepreneurs, such as those arranging downhill skiing. The entrepreneurs arranging snowmobiling and dog sledding, are considered vulnerable since their activities require large areas and plenty of snow. The concept of Finland being a winter wonderland and the home of Santa Claus, has developed a dependency of snowy landscapes among entrepreneurs, building great expectations among tourists. A change in snow quality and a delay in the season where snow is abundant, may lead to a decrease in number of tourists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
