<p><b>Dolomite</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small> </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_mineral" title="Carbonate mineral">carbonate mineral</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a>, ideally <span class="nowrap">CaMg(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.</span> The term is also used for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rock</a> composed mostly of the mineral dolomite. An alternative name sometimes used for the dolomitic rock type is <a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostone</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dolomite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most probably the mineral dolomite was first described by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a> in 1768.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1791, it was described as a rock by the French <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">naturalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a> <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9odat_Gratet_de_Dolomieu" title="Déodat Gratet de Dolomieu">Déodat Gratet de Dolomieu</a> (1750–1801), first in buildings of the old city of Rome, and later as samples collected in the mountains now known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolomite_Alps" title="Dolomite Alps">Dolomite Alps</a> of northern Italy. <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Th%C3%A9odore_de_Saussure" title="Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure">Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure</a> first named the mineral (after Dolomieu) in March 1792.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dolomite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mineral dolomite crystallizes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal" title="Trigonal">trigonal-rhombohedral</a> system. It forms white, tan, gray, or pink crystals. Dolomite is a double carbonate, having an alternating structural arrangement of calcium and magnesium ions. Unless it is in fine powder form, it does not rapidly dissolve or effervesce (fizz) in cold dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> as <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Crystal_twinning" title="Crystal twinning">Crystal twinning</a> is common.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">Solid solution</a> exists between dolomite, the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-dominant <a href="/wiki/Ankerite" title="Ankerite">ankerite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>-dominant <a href="/wiki/Kutnohorite" title="Kutnohorite">kutnohorite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">[9]</a></sup> Small amounts of iron in the structure give the crystals a yellow to brown tint. Manganese substitutes in the structure also up to about three percent MnO. A high manganese content gives the crystals a rosy pink color. <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> also substitute in the structure for magnesium. The mineral dolomite is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Huntite" title="Huntite">huntite</a> <span class="nowrap">Mg<sub>3</sub>Ca(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub></span>.
</p><p>Because dolomite can be dissolved by slightly acidic water, areas of dolomite are important as <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and contribute to <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> terrain formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dolomite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern dolomite formation has been found to occur under <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> conditions in <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a> saline <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, namely, Lagoa Vermelha and Brejo do Espinho. It is often thought that dolomite will develop only with the help of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfate-reducing bacteria">sulfate-reducing bacteria</a> (e.g. <i>Desulfovibrio brasiliensis</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, low-temperature dolomite may occur in natural environments rich in organic matter and microbial cell surfaces. This occurs as a result of magnesium complexation by carboxyl groups associated with organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2821" data-file-width="3628" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg/220px-Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg/330px-Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg/440px-Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dolomite-Magn%C3%A9site-_Navarre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dolomite (white) with magnesite (yellowish) from Spain</div></div></div>

<p>Vast deposits of dolomite are present in the geological record, but the mineral is relatively rare in modern environments. Reproducible, inorganic low-temperature syntheses of dolomite and <a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a> were published for the first time in 1999. Those laboratory experiments showed how the initial precipitation of a metastable "precursor" (such as magnesium calcite) will change gradually into more and more of the stable phase (such as dolomite or magnesite) during periodical intervals of dissolution and re-precipitation. The general principle governing the course of this irreversible <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemical</a> reaction has been coined "breaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostwald%27s_step_rule" title="Ostwald's step rule">Ostwald's step rule</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is some evidence for a biogenic occurrence of dolomite. One example is that of the formation of dolomite in the <a href="/wiki/Urinary_bladder" title="Urinary bladder">urinary bladder</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalmatian_(dog)" title="Dalmatian (dog)">Dalmatian</a> dog, possibly as the result of an illness or infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dolomite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dolomite is used as an ornamental stone, a concrete aggregate, and a source of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Pidgeon_process" title="Pidgeon process">Pidgeon process</a> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>. It is an important <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">reservoir</a> rock, and serves as the host rock for large strata-bound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley-Type" title="Mississippi Valley-Type">Mississippi Valley-Type</a> (MVT) <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> deposits of <a href="/wiki/Base_metal" title="Base metal">base metals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. Where <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> is uncommon or too costly, dolomite is sometimes used in its place as a <a href="/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy)" title="Flux (metallurgy)">flux</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> of iron and steel. Large quantities of processed dolomite are used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Float_glass" title="Float glass">float glass</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a>, dolomite and dolomitic limestone are added to soils and soilless potting mixes as a pH buffer and as a magnesium source.
</p><p>Dolomite is also used as the substrate in marine (saltwater) aquariums to help buffer changes in pH of the water.
</p><p>
Calcined dolomite is also used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> for destruction of <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> at high temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="536" data-file-width="750" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg/220px-Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg/330px-Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg/440px-Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chalcopyrite-Dolomite-44427.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dolomite (light pink) with <a href="/wiki/Chalcopyrite" title="Chalcopyrite">chalcopyrite</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tri-state_district" title="Tri-state district">Tri-state district</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_County,_Kansas" title="Cherokee County, Kansas">Cherokee County, Kansas</a> (size: 11.4×7.2×4.6 cm)</div></div></div><p>Particle physics researchers like to build <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">particle detectors</a> under layers of dolomite to enable the detectors to detect the highest possible number of exotic particles. Because dolomite contains relatively minor quantities of radioactive materials, it can insulate against interference from <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> without adding to <a href="/wiki/Background_radiation" title="Background radiation">background radiation</a> levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dolomite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
