<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Supersaturation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">State of a solution that contains more solute than can be dissolved at equilibrium </div>
<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">

<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/supersaturation" title="wiktionary:supersaturation">supersaturation</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Supersaturation</b> is a state of a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> that contains more of the dissolved material than could be dissolved by the <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> under normal circumstances. It can also refer to a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> of a compound that has a higher (partial) pressure than the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> of that compound.
</p>

These devices detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particles" title="Particles">particles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> by creating a supersaturated layer of <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condenses" title="Condenses">condenses</a> into cloud tracks when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particles" title="Particles">particles</a> pass through.</div></div></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supersaturation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Special conditions need to be met in order to generate a supersaturated <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. One of the easiest ways to do this relies on the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> dependence of <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>. As a general rule, the more heat is added to a system, the more soluble a substance becomes. (There are exceptions where the opposite is true). Therefore, at high temperatures, more solute can be dissolved than at lower temperatures. If this solution were to be suddenly cooled at a rate faster than the rate of precipitation, the solution will become supersaturated until the solute precipitates to the temperature-determined saturation point. The <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> or crystallization of the solute takes longer than the actual cooling time because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> need to meet up and form the precipitate without being knocked apart by water. Thus, the larger the molecule, the longer it will take to crystallize due to the principles of <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>.
</p><p><br/>
The condition of supersaturation does not necessarily have to be reached through the manipulation of heat. The <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>

</p><p>suggests that <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> can also be changed to force a system into a supersaturated state. If the volume of solvent is decreased, the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of the solute can be above the saturation point and thus create a supersaturated solution. The decrease in volume is most commonly generated through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. Similarly, an increase in pressure can drive a solution to a supersaturated state. All three of these mechanisms rely on the fact that the conditions of the solution can be changed quicker than the solute can precipitate or crystallize out.
</p>
<h2><span id="Phase_change_.28crystallization_and_condensation.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_change_(crystallization_and_condensation)">Phase change (crystallization and condensation)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supersaturation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phase change (crystallization and condensation)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supersaturated solutions will also undergo crystallization under specific conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In a normal solution, once the maximum amount of solute is dissolved, adding more solute would either cause the dissolved solute to precipitate out and/or for the solute to not dissolve at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Similarly, there are cases wherein solubility of a saturated solution is decreased by manipulating temperature, pressure, or volume but a supersaturated state does not occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In these cases, the solute will simply precipitate out. This is because a supersaturated solution is in a higher energy state than a saturated solution.
</p><p>A supersaturated solution of gases in a liquid may form bubbles if suitable nucleation sites exist. Supersaturation may be defined as a sum of all gas partial pressures in the liquid which exceeds the ambient pressure in the liquid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg/220px-A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg/330px-A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg/440px-A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_Supersaturated_Solution.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A chemistry lab practiced in class by sophomores in high school to learn more about solvents; this image is the final result of the lab, a supersaturated solution that has crystallized as it cooled to normal temperature</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">Crystallization</a> will occur to allow the solution to reach a lower energy state.(Keep in mind that this process can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a> comes in the form of a <i>nuclei crystal</i> being added to the liquid solution (or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensation_nuclei" title="Condensation nuclei">condensation nuclei</a> when the solution is gaseous). This nuclei can be either added from another source, which is known as seeding, or can spontaneously form within the solution due partly to ion and molecule interactions. This process is known as primary nucleation. It is necessary for the nuclei to be identical to the solute that is crystallizing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This will allow for the dissolved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> to build up on the nuclei and then each other in the process of crystal growth or secondary nucleation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There are a multitude of factors that will affect the rate and order of magnitude with which crystallization proceeds as well as the difference in formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystallites" title="Crystallites">crystallites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_crystals" title="Single crystals">single crystals</a>.
</p><p>A crystallization phase diagram shows where undersaturation, saturation, and supersaturation occur at certain concentrations. Concentrations below the solubility curve result in an undersaturation solution. Saturation occurs when the concentrations are on the solubility curve. If the concentrations are above the solubility curve, the solution is considered supersaturated. There are three mechanisms with which supersaturation occurs: precipitation, nucleation, and metastable. In the precipitation zone, the molecules in a solution are in excess and will separate from the solution to form amorphous aggregates. The excess of molecules aggregate to form a crystalline structure when in the nucleation zone. In the metastable zone, the solution takes time to nucleate. In order to grow crystals while in the metastable zone, the conditions would require the formation of one nucleus while in the nucleation zone, just past the metastable region. The supersaturated solution can then return to the metastable region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supersaturation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supersaturation has been a frequent topic of research throughout history. Early studies of these solutions were normally conducted with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a>, also known as Glauber’s Salt, due to the stability of the crystal and the rising role it had in industry. Through the use of this salt, an important scientific discovery was made by Jean-Baptiste Ziz, a botanist from <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mayence</a>, in 1809.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His experiments allowed him to conclude that the crystallization of a supersaturated solution does not simply come from its agitation, (the previous belief) but from solid matter entering and acting as a “starting” site for crystals to form, now called nuclei sites. Expanding upon this, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac" title="Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac">Gay-Lussac</a> brought attention to the <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a> of salt ions and the characteristics of the container having an impact on the supersaturation state. He was also able to expand upon the number of salts with which a supersaturated solution can be obtained. Later Henri Löwel came to the conclusion that both nuclei of the solution and the walls of the container have a catalyzing effect on the solution that cause crystallization. Explaining and providing a model for this phenomenon has been a task taken on by more recent research. Désiré Gernez contributed to this research by discovering that nuclei must be of the same salt that is being crystallized in order to yield crystallization.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supersaturation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supersaturation is a widely encountered phenomenon both found in environmental processes and exploited in commercial manufacturing. For example, <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, the sweet nectar-derived food source, is a supersaturated solution of sugars. Nectar itself is a sugary solution below the point of saturation. Once bees harvest the nectar, they fan it rapidly with their wings to force evaporation. This forces the solution into a supersaturated state, creating honey. This explains why honey crystallizes; the solution is simply returning to its saturated state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2070" data-file-width="3665" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg/220px-Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg/330px-Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg/440px-Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rock-Candy-Sticks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The supersaturation of sugar in water allows for <a href="/wiki/Rock_candy" title="Rock candy">rock candy</a> to form.</div></div></div>
<p>Certain candies are made by crystallizing supersaturated solutions of sugar. To make <a href="/wiki/Rock_candy" title="Rock candy">rock candy</a>, manufacturers can raise a solvent to a high temperature, add sugar to reach a high concentration, and then lower the temperature. If a string or stick is present in the solution as it cools, the crystallization will occur on that solid and create a candy. This is the same principle that leads maple syrup to crystallize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The carbonation of water is also reliant upon the behavior of supersaturated solutions. In this case, the solution is supersaturated with a gas. To create sodas and seltzer water, carbon dioxide gas is forced to dissolve in water beyond its saturation point. This is done by applying high amounts of pressure to the gas in the presence of water followed by sealing the system in an airtight manner.
</p><p>The characteristics of supersaturation have practical applications in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>. By creating a supersaturated solution of a certain drug, it can be ingested in liquid form. The drug can be made driven into a supersaturated state through any normal mechanism and then prevented from precipitating out by adding precipitation inhibitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Drugs in this state are referred to as "supersaturating drug delivery services," or "SDDS."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Oral consumption of a drug in this form is simple and allows for the measurement of very precise dosages. Primarily, it provides a means for drugs with very low solubility to be made into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous_solutions" title="Aqueous solutions">aqueous solutions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition, some drugs can undergo supersaturation inside the body despite being ingested in a crystalline form <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>. This phenomenon is known as <a href="/wiki/In_vivo_supersaturation" title="In vivo supersaturation">in vivo supersaturation</a>.
</p><p>The identification of supersaturated solutions can be used as a tool for marine ecologists to study the activity of organisms and populations. Photosynthetic organisms release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%E2%82%82" title="O₂">O<sub>2</sub></a> gas into the water. Thus, an area of the ocean supersaturated with O<sub>2</sub> gas can likely determined to be rich with photosynthetic activity. Though some O<sub>2</sub> will naturally be found in the ocean due to simple physical chemical properties, upwards of 70% of all oxygen gas found in supersaturated regions can be attributed to photosynthetic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The study of supersaturation is also relevant to atmospheric studies. Since the 1940s, the presence of supersaturation in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> has been known. When water is supersaturated in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, the formation of ice lattices is frequently observed. In a state of saturation, the water particles will not form ice under tropospheric conditions. It is not enough for molecules of water to form an ice lattice at saturation pressures; they require a surface to condense on to or conglomerations of liquid water molecules of water to freeze. For these reasons, relative humidities over ice in the atmosphere can be found above 100%, meaning supersaturation has occurred. Supersaturation of water is actually very common in the upper troposphere, occurring between 20% and 40% of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This can be determined using satellite data from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Infrared_Sounder" title="Atmospheric Infrared Sounder">Atmospheric Infrared Sounder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Evidence of supersaturation in the troposphere can be seen in the contrails of airplanes and rocket ships, which need to reach a humidity above that of ice saturation in order to form.
</p>
