<p>The <b>effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></b> are the environmental and social changes caused (directly or indirectly) by human emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. There is <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">a scientific consensus that climate change is occurring</a>, and that human activities are the primary driver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many impacts of climate change have already been observed, including <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glacier retreat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cramer_observed_effects_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cramer_observed_effects-2">[2]</a></sup> changes in the timing of seasonal events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cramer_observed_effects_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cramer_observed_effects-2">[2]</a></sup> (e.g., earlier flowering of plants),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and changes in agricultural productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cramer_observed_effects_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-cramer_observed_effects-2">[2]</a></sup> 
Anthropogenic forcing has likely contributed to some of the observed changes, including <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, changes in climate <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extremes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">declines in Arctic sea ice</a> extent and glacier retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Future effects of climate change will vary depending on <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">climate change policies</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adaptation_and_mitigation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-adaptation_and_mitigation-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_development" title="Social development">social development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The two main policies to address climate change are reducing human greenhouse gas emissions (<a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">adapting to the impacts</a> of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_change_policy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate_change_policy-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Geoengineering</a> is another policy option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_change_policy_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-climate_change_policy-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Near-term climate change policies could significantly affect long-term climate change impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adaptation_and_mitigation_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-adaptation_and_mitigation-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Stringent mitigation policies might be able to limit global warming (in 2100) to around 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> or below, relative to pre-industrial levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Without mitigation, increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_demand" title="Energy demand">energy demand</a> and extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> might lead to global warming of around 4 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Higher magnitudes of global warming would be more difficult to adapt to,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and would increase the risk of negative impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change</a></div>
<p>In this article, "<a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>" means a change in climate that persists over a sustained period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_definition_of_climate_change_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_definition_of_climate_change-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> defines this time period as 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_definition_of_climate_change_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_definition_of_climate_change-15">[15]</a></sup> Examples of climate change include increases in global surface temperature (global warming), changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> patterns, and changes in the frequency of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events. Changes in climate may be due to natural causes, e.g., changes in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>'s output, or due to human activities, e.g., changing the composition of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_climate_change_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_climate_change-17">[17]</a></sup> Any human-induced changes in climate will occur against a background of natural climatic variations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_climate_change_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_climate_change-17">[17]</a></sup> and of variations in human activity such as population growth on shores or in arid areas which increase or decrease climate vulnerability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureTaboo_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatureTaboo-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, the term "anthropogenic forcing" refers to the influence exerted on a habitat or chemical environment by humans, as opposed to a natural process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Detection" is the process of demonstrating that climate has changed in some defined <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical</a> sense, without providing a reason for that change. Detection does not imply attribution of the detected change to a particular cause. <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">"Attribution" of causes of climate change</a> is the process of establishing the most likely causes for the detected change with some defined level of confidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Detection and attribution may also be applied to observed changes in physical, ecological and social systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_changes">Temperature changes</span></h2>

<p>This article breaks down some of the impacts of climate change according to different levels of future global warming.  This way of describing impacts has, for instance, been used in the IPCC (<a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>) Assessment Reports on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-table_of_key_vulnerabilities_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-table_of_key_vulnerabilities-23">[23]</a></sup> The instrumental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperature record</a> shows global warming of around 0.6 °C during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects-24">[24]</a></sup> In a study carried out by David R. Easterling et al., trends were observed over a period of time. “It is clear from the observed record that there has been an increase in the global mean temperature of about 0.6℃ since the start of the 20th century and that this increase is associated with a stronger warming in daily minimum temperatures than in maximums leading to a reduction in the diurnal temperature range.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SRES_emissions_scenarios">SRES emissions scenarios</span></h3>
<p>The future level of global warming is uncertain, but a wide range of estimates (projections) have been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview_of_GHG_emissions_scenarios_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_GHG_emissions_scenarios-26">[26]</a></sup> The IPCC's "<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a>" scenarios have been frequently used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model#Projections_of_future_climate_change" title="Global climate model">projections of future climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22–24</span></sup>  The SRES scenarios are "<a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#Baselines" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">baseline</a>" (or "reference") scenarios, which means that they do not take into account any current or future measures to limit GHG emissions (e.g., the UNFCCC's <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">Cancún agreements</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>  Emissions projections of the SRES scenarios are broadly comparable in range to the baseline emissions scenarios that have been developed by the scientific community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview_of_GHG_emissions_scenarios_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_GHG_emissions_scenarios-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a>, changes in future global mean temperature were projected using the six SRES "marker" emissions scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Table_of_global_warming_projections_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Table_of_global_warming_projections-30">[30]</a></sup>  Emissions projections for the six SRES "marker" scenarios are representative of the full set of forty SRES scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  For the lowest emissions SRES marker scenario ("B1" – see the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a> article for details on this scenario), the best estimate for global mean temperature is an increase of 1.8 °C (3.2 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Table_of_global_warming_projections_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Table_of_global_warming_projections-30">[30]</a></sup> by the end of the 21st century. This projection is relative to global temperatures at the end of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The "likely" range (greater than 66% probability, based on expert judgement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup> for the SRES B1 marker scenario is 1.1–2.9 °C (2.0–5.2 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Table_of_global_warming_projections_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Table_of_global_warming_projections-30">[30]</a></sup>  For the highest emissions SRES marker scenario (A1FI), the best estimate for global mean temperature increase is 4.0 °C (7.2 °F), with a "likely" range of 2.4–6.4 °C (4.3–11.5 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Table_of_global_warming_projections_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Table_of_global_warming_projections-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The range in temperature projections partly reflects (1) the choice of <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Emissions_scenarios" title="Economics of global warming">emissions scenario</a>, and (2) the "<a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22–24</span></sup> For (1), different scenarios make different assumptions of future social and economic development (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_projection" title="Population projection">population level</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy" title="Energy policy">energy policies</a>), which in turn affects projections of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22–24</span></sup> The projected magnitude of warming by 2100 is closely related to the level of cumulative emissions over the 21st century (i.e. total emissions between 2000–2100).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep_cumulative_emissions_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-unep_cumulative_emissions-34">[34]</a></sup> The higher the cumulative emissions over this time period, the greater the level of warming is projected to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep_cumulative_emissions_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-unep_cumulative_emissions-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>(2) reflects uncertainty in the response of the climate system to past and future GHG emissions, which is measured by the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22–24</span></sup> Higher estimates of climate sensitivity lead to greater projected warming, while lower estimates of climate sensitivity lead to less projected warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the next several millennia, projections suggest that global warming could be irreversible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_irreversible_climate_change_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_irreversible_climate_change-36">[36]</a></sup> Even if emissions were drastically reduced, global temperatures would remain close to their highest level for at least 1,000 years (see the later section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Irreversibilities" title="Effects of global warming">irreversibilities</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Scientists have used various "proxy" data to assess past changes in Earth's climate (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_proxy_climate_data_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_proxy_climate_data-40">[40]</a></sup> Sources of proxy data include historical records (such as <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmers</a>' logs), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_pollen" title="Fossil pollen">fossil pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, and ocean and lake <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_proxy_climate_data_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_proxy_climate_data-40">[40]</a></sup> Analysis of these data suggest that recent warming is unusual in the past 400 years, possibly longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> By the end of the 21st century, temperatures may increase to a level not experienced since the mid-<a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>, around 3 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jansen_pliocene_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-jansen_pliocene-42">[42]</a></sup> At that time, models suggest that mean global temperatures were about 2–3 °C warmer than pre-industrial temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jansen_pliocene_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-jansen_pliocene-42">[42]</a></sup> Even a 2 °C rise above the pre-industrial level would be outside the range of temperatures experienced by human <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_climate_history_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_climate_history-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A broad range of evidence shows that the climate system has warmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Evidence of global warming is shown in the graphs opposite. Some of the graphs show a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trend_estimation" title="Trend estimation">trend</a>, e.g., increasing temperature over land and the ocean, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea level rise</a>. Other graphs show a negative trend, e.g., decreased <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, and declining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent. Evidence of warming is also apparent in living <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Observed_impacts_on_biological_systems" title="Effects of global warming">(biological) systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenzweig_observed_impacts_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-rosenzweig_observed_impacts-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Human activities have contributed to a number of the observed changes in climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hegerl_climate_change_attribution_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-hegerl_climate_change_attribution-48">[48]</a></sup>  This contribution has principally been through the burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, which has led to an increase in the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>  Another human influence on the climate are <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> emissions, which are a precursor to the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate_aerosols" title="Sulfate aerosols">sulfate aerosols</a> in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Human-induced warming could lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irreversible_effects_of_global_warming" title="Irreversible effects of global warming">large-scale, irreversible, and/or abrupt changes</a> in physical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> An example of this is the melting of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, which contributes to sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnitudes_of_impact_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-magnitudes_of_impact-53">[53]</a></sup>  The probability of warming having unforeseen consequences increases with the rate, magnitude, and duration of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_weather">Effects on weather</span></h3>
<p>Observations show that there have been changes in weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> As climate changes, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probabilities" title="Probabilities">probabilities</a> of certain types of weather events are affected.
</p>

<p>Changes have been observed in the amount, intensity, frequency, and type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>18</span></sup> Widespread increases in heavy precipitation have occurred, even in places where total rain amounts have decreased. With medium confidence (see <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Footnotes" title="Effects of global warming">footnote 1</a>), IPCC (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> concluded that human influences had contributed to an increase in heavy precipitation events at the global scale.
</p><p>Projections of future changes in precipitation show overall increases in the global average, but with substantial shifts in where and how precipitation falls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_exec_sum_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_exec_sum-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup> Projections suggest a reduction in rainfall in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>, and an increase in precipitation in subpolar latitudes and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_region" title="Equatorial region">equatorial regions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfdl_rainfall_projections_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-gfdl_rainfall_projections-57">[57]</a></sup> In other words, regions which are dry at present will in general become even drier, while regions that are currently wet will in general become even wetter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfdl_rainfall_projections_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-gfdl_rainfall_projections-57">[57]</a></sup> This projection does not apply to every locale, and in some cases can be modified by local conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfdl_rainfall_projections_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-gfdl_rainfall_projections-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather">Extreme weather</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Global_warming" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone § Global warming</a></div>
<p>Over most land areas since the 1950s, it is very likely that there have been fewer or warmer cold days and nights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> Hot days and nights have also very likely become warmer or more frequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> Human activities have very likely contributed to these trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> There may have been changes in other climate extremes (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>) but these changes are more difficult to identify.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Projections suggest changes in the frequency and intensity of some extreme weather events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> Confidence in projections varies over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-5"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Near-term projections (2016–2035)</dt></dl>
<p>Some changes (e.g., more frequent hot days) will probably be evident in the near term, while other near-term changes (e.g., more intense droughts and tropical cyclones) are more uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-6"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Long-term projections (2081–2100)</dt></dl>
<p>Future climate change will be associated with more very hot days and fewer very cold days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-7"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> The frequency, length and intensity of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> will very likely increase over most land areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-8"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup> Higher growth in anthropogenic GHG emissions will be associated with larger increases in the frequency and severity of temperature extremes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assuming high growth in GHG emissions (IPCC scenario <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">RCP8.5</a>), presently dry regions may be affected by an increase in the risk of drought and reductions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" title="Soil moisture">soil moisture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Over most of the mid-latitude land masses and wet tropical regions, extreme precipitation events will very likely become more intense and frequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes_59-9"><a href="#cite_note-ar5_wg1_climate_extremes-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Heat waves</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> boosts the probability of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, like heat waves, far more than it boosts more moderate events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the last 30-40 years, heat waves with high humidity have became more frequent and severe. Extremely hot nights have doubled in frequency. The area in which extremely hot summers are observed, has increased 50-100 fold. These changes are not explained by natural variability, and attributed by climate scientists to the influence of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthropogenic" title="wikt:anthropogenic">anthropogenic</a> climate change.  Heat waves with high humidity pose a big risk to human health while heat waves with low humidity lead to dry conditions that increase  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildfires" title="Wildfires">wildfires</a>. The mortality from extreme heat is larger than the mortality from hurricanes, lightning, tornadoes, floods, and earthquakes together<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heat_Waves:_The_Details_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heat_Waves:_The_Details-65">[65]</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/2018_heat_wave" title="2018 heat wave">2018 heat wave</a>.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<dl><dt>Tropical cyclones</dt></dl>
<p>At the global scale, the frequency of tropical cyclones will probably decrease or be unchanged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christensen_2013_cyclones_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-christensen_2013_cyclones-66">[66]</a></sup> Global mean tropical cyclone maximum wind speed and precipitation rates will likely increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christensen_2013_cyclones_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-christensen_2013_cyclones-66">[66]</a></sup> Changes in tropical cyclones will probably vary by region, but these variations are uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-christensen_2013_cyclones_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-christensen_2013_cyclones-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Effects of climate extremes</dt></dl>
<p>The impacts of extreme events on the environment and human society will vary. Some impacts will be beneficial—e.g., fewer cold extremes will probably lead to fewer cold deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-67">[67]</a></sup> Overall, however, impacts will probably be mostly negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_synthesis_reasons_for_concern_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_synthesis_reasons_for_concern-69">[69]</a></sup>
A rise in temperature will cause the glaciers to melt, when water heats up, it expands, both of these factors contribute to a rise in sea levels which will put people living in lowland areas, for example The Netherlands in danger.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryosphere">Cryosphere</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png"><img alt="refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="650" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/220px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/330px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/440px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of the change in thickness of mountain glaciers since 1970. Thinning in orange and red, thickening in blue.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg"><img alt="2012 Arctic sea ice extent" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="1440" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/220px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/330px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/440px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map that shows ice concentration on 16 September 2012, along with the extent of the previous record low (yellow line) and the mid-September median extent (black line) setting a new record low that was 18 percent smaller than the previous record and nearly 50 percent smaller than the long-term (1979–2000) average.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> is made up of areas of the Earth which are covered by snow or ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Observed changes in the cryosphere include declines in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> the widespread retreat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_glaciers" title="Alpine glaciers">alpine glaciers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and reduced snow cover in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solomon <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_arctic_sea_ice_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_arctic_sea_ice-74">[74]</a></sup> assessed the potential impacts of climate change on <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summertime</a> Arctic sea ice extent. Assuming high growth in greenhouse gas emissions (<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES A2</a>), some models projected that Arctic sea ice in the summer could largely disappear by the end of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_arctic_sea_ice_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_arctic_sea_ice-74">[74]</a></sup> More recent projections suggest that the Arctic summers could be ice-free (defined as ice extent less than 1 million square km) as early as 2025–2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 21st century, glaciers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and snow cover are projected to continue their widespread retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In the western mountains of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, increasing temperatures and changes in precipitation are projected to lead to reduced <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Snowpack is the seasonal accumulation of slow-melting snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a> could contribute to sea level rise, especially over long time-scales (see the section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets" title="Effects of global warming">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnitudes_of_impact_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-magnitudes_of_impact-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Changes in the cryosphere are projected to have social impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> For example, in some regions, glacier retreat could increase the risk of reductions in seasonal water availability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Barnett <i>et al.</i> (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> estimated that more than one-sixth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_population" title="World's population">world's population</a> rely on glaciers and snowpack for their water supply.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_oceans" title="Effects of global warming on oceans">Effects of global warming on oceans</a></div>
<p>The role of the oceans in global warming is complex. The oceans serve as a sink for carbon dioxide, taking up much that would otherwise remain in the atmosphere, but increased levels of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> have led to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>. Furthermore, as the temperature of the oceans increases, they become less able to absorb excess <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. The ocean have also acted as a sink in absorbing extra heat from the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statecl_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-statecl-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> The increase in ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_content" title="Heat content">heat content</a> is much larger than any other store of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in the Earth’s heat balance over the two periods 1961 to 2003 and 1993 to 2003, and accounts for more than 90% of the possible increase in heat content of the Earth system during these periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>Global warming is projected to have a number of effects on the oceans.  Ongoing effects include rising sea levels due to thermal expansion and melting of glaciers and ice sheets, and warming of the ocean surface, leading to increased temperature stratification. Other possible effects include large-scale changes in ocean circulation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidification">Acidification</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans,_1880-2012_(US_EPA).png"><img between the 1880s and the most recent decade (2003–2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_epa_2012_aragonite_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_epa_2012_aragonite-85">[85]</a></sup> Historical modeling suggests that since the 1880s, increased CO<sub>2</sub> has led to lower <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> saturation levels (less availability of minerals) in the oceans around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_epa_2012_aragonite_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-us_epa_2012_aragonite-85">[85]</a></sup> The largest decreases in aragonite saturation have occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_epa_2012_aragonite_85-2"><a href="#cite_note-us_epa_2012_aragonite-85">[85]</a></sup> However, decreases in cold areas may be of greater concern because colder waters typically have lower aragonite levels to begin with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_epa_2012_aragonite_85-3"><a href="#cite_note-us_epa_2012_aragonite-85">[85]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>About one-third of the carbon dioxide emitted by human activity has already been taken up by the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc_2008b_ocean_acidification_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc_2008b_ocean_acidification-86">[86]</a></sup>  As carbon dioxide dissolves in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_water" title="Sea water">sea water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> is formed, which has the effect of acidifying the ocean, measured as a change in <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. The uptake of human carbon emissions since the year 1750 has led to an average decrease in pH of 0.1 units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_ocean_acidification_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_ocean_acidification-87">[87]</a></sup>  Projections using the SRES emissions scenarios suggest a further reduction in average global surface ocean pH of between 0.14 and 0.35 units over the 21st century.
</p><p>The effects of ocean acidification on the marine <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> have yet to be documented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_ocean_acidification_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_ocean_acidification-87">[87]</a></sup>  Laboratory experiments suggest beneficial effects for a few species, with potentially highly detrimental effects for a substantial number of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc_2008b_ocean_acidification_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrc_2008b_ocean_acidification-86">[86]</a></sup>  With medium confidence, Fischlin <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> projected that future ocean acidification and climate change would impair a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> and shallow <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a> marine organisms that use <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> to make their shells or skeletons, such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and marine <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pteropoda" title="Pteropoda">pteropods</a>), with significant impacts particularly in the Southern Ocean.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_depletion">Oxygen depletion</span></h4>
<p>The amount of oxygen dissolved in the oceans may decline, with adverse consequences for ocean life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_rise">Sea level rise</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">Current sea level rise</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_sea_level" title="Future sea level">Future sea level</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level,_1870-2008_(US_EPA).png"><img alt="refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="717" data-file-width="670" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png/220px-Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png/330px-Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png/440px-Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level%2C_1870-2008_%28US_EPA%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trends_in_global_average_absolute_sea_level,_1870-2008_(US_EPA).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trends in global average absolute sea level, 1870–2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>There is strong evidence that global sea level rose gradually over the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_5.1_is_sea_level_rising_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_5.1_is_sea_level_rising-92">[92]</a></sup>  With high confidence, Bindoff <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>  concluded that between the mid-19th and mid-20th centuries, the rate of sea level rise increased. Authors of the IPCC Fourth Assessment Synthesis Report (IPCC AR4 SYR, 2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects-24">[24]</a></sup>  reported that between the years 1961 and 2003, global average sea level rose at an average rate of 1.8 mm per year (mm/yr), with a range of 1.3–2.3 mm/yr. Between 1993 and 2003, the rate increased above the previous period to 3.1 mm/yr (range of 2.4–3.8 mm/yr). Authors of IPCC AR4 SYR (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Observed_changes_in_climate_and_their_effects-24">[24]</a></sup> were uncertain whether the increase in rate from 1993 to 2003 was due to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Internal_forcing_mechanisms" title="Climate change">natural variations</a> in sea level over the time period, or whether it reflected an increase in the underlying long-term trend.
</p><p>There are two main factors that have contributed to observed sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_5.1_is_sea_level_rising_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_5.1_is_sea_level_rising-92">[92]</a></sup>  The first is <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a>: as ocean water warms, it expands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>  The second is from the contribution of land-based ice due to increased melting. The major store of water on land is found in glaciers and ice sheets. Anthropogenic forces very likely (greater than 90% probability, based on expert judgement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup>  contributed to sea level rise during the latter half of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hegerl_climate_change_attribution_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-hegerl_climate_change_attribution-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a widespread consensus that substantial long-term sea level rise will continue for centuries to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
In their Fourth Assessment Report, the IPCC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> projected sea level rise to the end of the 21st century using the SRES emissions scenarios. Across the six SRES marker scenarios, sea level was projected to rise by 18 to 59 cm (7.1 to 23.2 in), relative to sea level at the end of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Thermal expansion is the largest component in these projections, contributing 70–75% of the central estimate for all scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>  Due to a lack of scientific understanding, this sea level rise estimate does not include all of the possible contributions of ice sheets (see the section on <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets" title="Effects of global warming">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</a>).
</p><p>An assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a> on climate change was published in 2010 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> (US NRC, 2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise-99">[99]</a></sup> NRC (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise-99">[99]</a></sup> described the projections in AR4 (i.e. those cited in the above paragraph) as "conservative", and summarized the results of more recent studies. Cited studies suggested a great deal of uncertainty in projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise_99-2"><a href="#cite_note-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise-99">[99]</a></sup> A range of projections suggested possible sea level rise by the end of the 21st century of between 0.56 and 2 m, relative to sea levels at the end of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise_99-3"><a href="#cite_note-NRC_2010_projections_of_sea_level_rise-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_temperature_rise">Ocean temperature rise</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heat_content700m2000myr.png"><img alt="Global ocean heat content" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="566" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Heat_content700m2000myr.png/220px-Heat_content700m2000myr.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Heat_content700m2000myr.png/330px-Heat_content700m2000myr.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Heat_content700m2000myr.png/440px-Heat_content700m2000myr.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heat_content700m2000myr.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global ocean heat content from 1955–2012</div></div></div>
<p>From 1961 to 2003, the global ocean temperature has risen by 0.10 °C from the surface to a depth of 700 m. There is variability both year-to-year and over longer time scales, with global ocean heat content observations showing high rates of warming for 1991–2003, but some cooling from 2003 to 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> The temperature of the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> rose by 0.17 °C (0.31 °F) between the 1950s and the 1980s, nearly twice the rate for the world's oceans as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> As well as having effects on ecosystems (e.g. by melting sea ice, affecting algae that grow on its underside), warming reduces the ocean's ability to absorb <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  It is likely (greater than 66% probability, based on expert judgement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup> that anthropogenic forcing contributed to the general warming observed in the upper several hundred metres of the ocean during the latter half of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hegerl_climate_change_attribution_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-hegerl_climate_change_attribution-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Regional effects of global warming vary in nature. Some are the result of a generalised global change, such as rising temperature, resulting in local effects, such as melting ice. In other cases, a change may be related to a change in a particular ocean current or weather system. In such cases, the regional effect may be disproportionate and will not necessarily follow the global trend.
</p><p>There are three major ways in which global warming will make changes to regional climate: melting or forming ice, changing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a> (of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>) and changing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents in the oceans</a> and air flows in the atmosphere. The coast can also be considered a region, and will suffer severe impacts from <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, small islands and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">megadeltas</a> are regions that are likely to be especially affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_especially_affected_regions_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_especially_affected_regions-103">[103]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Low-latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">less-developed</a> regions are at most risk of experiencing negative impacts due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_distribution_of_impacts_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_distribution_of_impacts-104">[104]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">Developed countries</a> are also vulnerable to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_regional_vulnerabilities_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_regional_vulnerabilities-105">[105]</a></sup> For example, developed countries will be negatively affected by increases in the severity and frequency of some <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_regional_vulnerabilities_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_regional_vulnerabilities-105">[105]</a></sup> In all regions, some people can be particularly at risk from climate change, such as the <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poor</a>, young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children" title="Children">children</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elderly" title="Elderly">elderly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_especially_affected_regions_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_especially_affected_regions-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_distribution_of_impacts_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_distribution_of_impacts-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>Projections of future climate changes at the regional scale do not hold as high a level of scientific confidence as projections made at the global scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc_2008_climate_change_brochure_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc_2008_climate_change_brochure-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup> It is, however, expected that future warming will follow a similar geographical pattern to that seen already, with greatest warming over land and high northern <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, and least over the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> Nearly all land areas will very likely warm more than the global average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_table_of_robust_findings_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_table_of_robust_findings-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_systems">Social systems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans" title="Effects of climate change on humans">Effects of climate change on humans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change,_industry_and_society" title="Climate change, industry and society">Climate change, industry and society</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_gender" title="Climate change and gender">Climate change and gender</a></div>
<p>The impacts of climate change can be thought of in terms of sensitivity and vulnerability. "Sensitivity" is the degree to which a particular system or sector might be affected, positively or negatively, by climate change and/or <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Causes" title="Climate change">climate variability</a>. "Vulnerability" is the degree to which a particular system or sector might be adversely affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sensitivity of human society to climate change varies. Sectors sensitive to climate change include water resources, coastal zones, human settlements, and human health. Industries sensitive to climate change include <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_and_climate_change" title="Fisheries and climate change">fisheries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Climate change will impact agriculture and food production around the world due to: the effects of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere, higher temperatures, altered precipitation and <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> regimes, increased frequency of extreme events, and modified <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> In general, low-latitude areas are at most risk of having decreased crop yields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2007, the effects of regional climate change on agriculture have been small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenzweig_observed_impacts_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-rosenzweig_observed_impacts-47">[47]</a></sup> Changes in crop <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a> provide important evidence of the response to recent regional climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Phenology is the study of natural phenomena that recur periodically, and how these phenomena relate to climate and seasonal changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> A significant advance in phenology has been observed for agriculture and forestry in large parts of the Northern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenzweig_observed_impacts_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-rosenzweig_observed_impacts-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>With low to medium confidence, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-table_of_key_vulnerabilities_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-table_of_key_vulnerabilities-23">[23]</a></sup> projected that for about a 1 to 3 °C increase in global mean temperature (by the years 2090–2100, relative to average temperatures in the years 1990–2000), there would be productivity decreases for some <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a> in low latitudes, and productivity increases in high latitudes. With medium confidence, global production potential was projected to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-table_of_key_vulnerabilities_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-table_of_key_vulnerabilities-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>increase up to around 3 °C,</li>
<li>very likely decrease above about 3 °C.</li></ul>
<p>Most of the studies on global agriculture assessed by Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture-115">[115]</a></sup> had not incorporated a number of critical factors, including changes in extreme events, or the spread of pests and diseases. Studies had also not considered the development of specific practices or technologies to aid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming#Agricultural_production" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture_115-2"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_climate_change_and_agriculture-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>The graphs opposite show the projected effects of climate change on selected crop yields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2011_text_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2011_text-119">[119]</a></sup> Actual changes in yields may be above or below these central estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2011_text_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2011_text-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>The projections above can be expressed relative to pre-industrial (1750) temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> 0.6 °C of warming is estimated to have occurred between 1750 and 1990–2000. Add 0.6 °C to the above projections to convert them from a 1990–2000 to pre-industrial baseline.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_security">Food security</span></h5>
<p>Easterling <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> assessed studies that made quantitative projections of climate change impacts on <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>. It was noted that these projections were highly uncertain and had limitations. However, the assessed studies suggested a number of fairly robust findings. The first was that climate change would likely increase the number of people at risk of <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> compared with reference scenarios with no climate change. Climate change impacts depended strongly on projected future social and economic development. Additionally, the magnitude of climate change impacts was projected to be smaller compared to the impact of social and economic development. In 2006, the global estimate for the number of people <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">undernourished</a> was 820 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> Under the SRES A1, B1, and B2 scenarios (see the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios#Scenario_families" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES article</a> for information on each scenario group), projections for the year 2080 showed a reduction in the number of people undernourished of about 560–700 million people, with a global total of undernourished people of 100–240 million in 2080. By contrast, the SRES A2 scenario showed only a small decrease in the risk of hunger from 2006 levels. The smaller reduction under A2 was attributed to the higher projected future population level in this scenario.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Droughts_and_agriculture">Droughts and agriculture</span></h4>
<p>Some evidence suggests that droughts have been occurring more frequently because of global warming and they are expected to become more frequent and intense in Africa, southern Europe, the Middle East, most of the Americas, Australia, and Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> However, other research suggests that there has been little change in drought over the past 60 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> Their impacts are aggravated because of increased water demand, population growth, urban expansion, and environmental protection efforts in many areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> Droughts result in crop failures and the loss of pasture grazing land for livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">Effects of global warming on human health</a></div>
<p>Human beings are exposed to climate change through changing weather patterns (temperature, precipitation, sea-level rise and more frequent extreme events) and indirectly through changes in water, air and food quality and changes in ecosystems, agriculture, industry and settlements and the economy (Confalonieri <i>et al.</i>, 2007:393).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-67">[67]</a></sup> According to an assessment of the scientific literature by Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007:393),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-67">[67]</a></sup> the effects of climate change to date have been small, but are projected to progressively increase in all countries and regions.
</p><p>A study by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO, 2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>  estimated the effect of climate change on human health. Not all of the effects of climate change were included in their estimates, for example, the effects of more frequent and extreme storms were excluded. Climate change was estimated to have been responsible for 3% of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrhoea" title="Diarrhoea">diarrhoea</a>, 3% of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, and 3.8% of <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a> deaths worldwide in 2004. Total attributable mortality was about 0.2% of deaths in 2004; of these, 85% were child deaths.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections_2">Projections</span></h4>
<p>With high confidence, authors of the IPCC AR4 Synthesis report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_Synthesis_Report_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_Synthesis_Report-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>48</span></sup> projected that climate change would bring some benefits in temperate areas, such as fewer deaths from cold exposure, and some mixed effects such as changes in range and transmission potential of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Benefits were projected to be outweighed by negative health effects of rising temperatures, especially in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>.
</p><p>With very high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_67-3"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>393</span></sup> concluded that economic development was an important component of possible adaptation to climate change. Economic growth on its own, however, was not judged to be sufficient to insulate the world's population from disease and injury due to climate change. Future vulnerability to climate change will depend not only on the extent of social and economic change, but also on how the benefits and costs of change are distributed in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Future_vulnerability_to_climate_change_health_impacts_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Future_vulnerability_to_climate_change_health_impacts-129">[129]</a></sup> For example, in the 19th century, rapid <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a> lead to a plummeting in population health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Future_vulnerability_to_climate_change_health_impacts_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-Future_vulnerability_to_climate_change_health_impacts-129">[129]</a></sup> Other factors important in determining the health of populations include <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, the availability of health services, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public-health</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_67-4"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>393</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_resources">Water resources</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Water_security" title="Water security">Water security</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="959" data-file-width="649" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/220px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/330px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/440px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Precipitation during the 20th century and up through 2008 during global warming, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> estimating an observed trend over that period of 1.87% global precipitation increase per century.</div></div></div>
<p>A number of climate-related trends have been observed that affect <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>. These include changes in precipitation, the crysosphere and <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface waters</a> (e.g., changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_flows" title="River flows">river flows</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> Observed and projected impacts of climate change on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> systems and their management are mainly due to changes in temperature, sea level and precipitation variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freshwater_impacts_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-freshwater_impacts-131">[131]</a></sup> Sea level rise will extend areas of <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, resulting in a decrease in freshwater availability for humans and ecosystems in coastal areas. In an assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a>, Kundzewicz <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freshwater_impacts_131-1"><a href="#cite_note-freshwater_impacts-131">[131]</a></sup> concluded, with high confidence, that:
</p>
<ul><li>the negative impacts of climate change on freshwater systems outweigh the benefits. All of the regions assessed in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> (<a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">Polar regions</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>), and small islands) showed an overall net negative impact of climate change on water resources and freshwater ecosystems.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">Semi-arid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> areas are particularly exposed to the impacts of climate change on freshwater. With very high confidence, it was judged that many of these areas, e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, and north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, would suffer a decrease in water resources due to climate change.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_and_conflict">Migration and conflict</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">Environmental migrant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_security" title="Climate security">Climate security</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">General circulation models</a> project that the future climate change will bring wetter coasts, drier mid-continent areas, and further sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sar_migration_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-sar_migration-132">[132]</a></sup> Such changes could result in the gravest effects of climate change through <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">human migration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> Millions might be displaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoreline_erosion" title="Shoreline erosion">shoreline erosions</a>, river and coastal <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, or severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>.
</p><p>Migration related to climate change is likely to be predominantly from <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> to towns and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cities" title="Cities">cities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sar_migration_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-sar_migration-132">[132]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>407</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wdr_migration_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-wdr_migration-134">[134]</a></sup> In the short term climate stress is likely to add incrementally to existing migration patterns rather than generating entirely new flows of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wdr_migration_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-wdr_migration-134">[134]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>110</span></sup>
</p><p>It has been argued that environmental degradation, loss of access to resources (e.g., water resources),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> and resulting human migration could become a source of political and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_conflict" title="Military conflict">military conflict</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_migration_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_migration-136">[136]</a></sup> Factors other than climate change may, however, be more important in affecting conflict. For example, Wilbanks <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> suggested that major environmentally influenced conflicts in Africa were more to do with the relative abundance of resources, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamonds</a>, than with resource scarcity. Scott <i>et al.</i> (2001) placed only low confidence in predictions of increased conflict due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_migration_136-1"><a href="#cite_note-tar_migration-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2013 study found that significant climatic changes were associated with a higher risk of conflict worldwide, and predicted that "amplified rates of human conflict could represent a large and critical social impact of anthropogenic climate change in both low- and high-income countries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> Similarly, a 2014 study found that higher temperatures were associated with a greater likelihood of violent crime, and predicted that global warming would cause millions of such crimes in the United States alone during the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> A 2018 study in the journal <i>Nature Climate Change</i> found that previous studies on the relationship between climate change and conflict suffered from <a href="/wiki/Sampling_bias" title="Sampling bias">sampling bias</a> and other methodological problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>
</p><p>Military planners are concerned that global warming is a "threat multiplier".  "Whether it is poverty, food and <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a>, diseases, economic instability, or threat of natural disasters, the broad range of changing climatic conditions may be far reaching.  These challenges may threaten stability in much of the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>  For example, the onset of <a href="/wiki/Arab_Spring" title="Arab Spring">Arab Spring</a> in December 2010 is partly the result of a spike in wheat prices following crop losses from the <a href="/wiki/2010_Northern_Hemisphere_heat_waves" title="2010 Northern Hemisphere heat waves">2010 Russian heat wave</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_impacts">Aggregate impacts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Economic impacts of climate change">Economic impacts of climate change</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aggregate_data" title="Aggregate data">Aggregating</a> impacts adds up the total impact of climate change across sectors and/or regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> Examples of aggregate measures include <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Distribution_of_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">economic cost</a> (e.g., changes in <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Marginal_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">social cost of carbon</a>), changes in ecosystems (e.g., changes over land area from one type of vegetation to another),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> human health impacts, and the number of people affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> Aggregate measures such as economic cost require researchers to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_judgement" title="Value judgement">value judgements</a> over the importance of impacts occurring in different regions and at different times.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts">Observed impacts</span></h4>
<p>Global losses reveal rapidly rising costs due to extreme weather-related events since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomics" title="Socioeconomics">Socio-economic</a> factors have contributed to the observed trend of global losses, e.g., population growth, increased <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disaster_losses_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-disaster_losses-148">[148]</a></sup> Part of the growth is also related to regional climatic factors, e.g., changes in precipitation and flooding events. It is difficult to quantify the relative impact of socio-economic factors and climate change on the observed trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disaster_losses_148-1"><a href="#cite_note-disaster_losses-148">[148]</a></sup> The trend does, however, suggest increasing vulnerability of social systems to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disaster_losses_148-2"><a href="#cite_note-disaster_losses-148">[148]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_impacts">Projected impacts</span></h4>
<p>The total economic impacts from climate change are highly uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> With medium confidence, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_estimate_of_climate_change_impacts_on_GDP_151-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_estimate_of_climate_change_impacts_on_GDP-151">[151]</a></sup>  concluded that world GDP would change by plus or minus a few percent for a small increase in global mean temperature (up to around 2 °C relative to the 1990 temperature level). Most studies assessed by Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_estimate_of_climate_change_impacts_on_GDP_151-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_estimate_of_climate_change_impacts_on_GDP-151">[151]</a></sup> projected losses in world GDP for a medium increase in global mean temperature (above 2–3 °C relative to the 1990 temperature level),  with increasing losses for greater temperature increases. This assessment is consistent with the findings of more recent studies, as reviewed by Hitz and Smith (2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_of_reasons_for_concern_152-0"><a href="#cite_note-update_of_reasons_for_concern-152">[152]</a></sup>
</p><p>Economic impacts are expected to vary regionally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_of_reasons_for_concern_152-1"><a href="#cite_note-update_of_reasons_for_concern-152">[152]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> For a medium increase in global mean temperature (2–3 °C of warming, relative to the average temperature between 1990–2000), market sectors in low-latitude and less-developed areas might experience net costs due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-table_of_key_vulnerabilities_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-table_of_key_vulnerabilities-23">[23]</a></sup> On the other hand, market sectors in high-latitude and developed regions might experience net benefits for this level of warming. A global mean temperature increase above about 2–3 °C (relative to 1990–2000) would very likely result in market sectors across all regions experiencing either declines in net benefits or rises in net costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnitudes_of_impact_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-magnitudes_of_impact-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aggregate impacts have also been quantified in non-economic terms. For example, climate change over the 21st century is likely to adversely affect hundreds of millions of people through increased coastal flooding, reductions in water supplies, increased malnutrition and increased health impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_synthesis_reasons_for_concern_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_synthesis_reasons_for_concern-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_systems">Biological systems</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems" title="Climate change and ecosystems">Climate change and ecosystems</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_global_warming" title="Extinction risk from global warming">Extinction risk from global warming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_climate_change_on_plant_biodiversity" title="Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity">Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_terrestrial_animals" title="Effects of climate change on terrestrial animals">Effects of climate change on terrestrial animals</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_marine_mammals" title="Effects of climate change on marine mammals">Effects of climate change on marine mammals</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts_on_biological_systems">Observed impacts on biological systems</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_(low-res).png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2267" data-file-width="1725" height="460" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png/350px-A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png/525px-A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png/700px-A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_%28low-res%29.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_vast_array_of_physical_and_biological_systems_across_the_Earth_are_being_affected_by_human-induced_global_warming_(low-res).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A vast array of physical and biological systems across the Earth are being affected by human-induced global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_briefs_154-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_briefs-154">[154]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>With very high confidence, Rosenzweig <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that recent warming had strongly affected natural biological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenzweig_observed_impacts_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-rosenzweig_observed_impacts-47">[47]</a></sup>  Hundreds of studies have documented responses of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> to the climate changes that have already occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup>  For example, in the Northern Hemisphere, species are almost uniformly moving their ranges northward and up in elevation in search of cooler temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc_2008b_observed_ecosystem_impacts_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc_2008b_observed_ecosystem_impacts-156">[156]</a></sup>  Humans are very likely causing changes in regional temperatures to which plants and animals are responding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc_2008b_observed_ecosystem_impacts_156-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrc_2008b_observed_ecosystem_impacts-156">[156]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_impacts_on_biological_systems">Projected impacts on biological systems</span></h3>
<p>By the year 2100, ecosystems will be exposed to atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> levels substantially higher than in the past 650,000 years, and global temperatures at least among the highest of those experienced in the past 740,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fischlin_executive_summary_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-fischlin_executive_summary-157">[157]</a></sup> Significant disruptions of ecosystems are projected to increase with future climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_synthesis_ecosystems_158-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_synthesis_ecosystems-158">[158]</a></sup>  Examples of disruptions include disturbances such as fire, drought, pest <a href="/wiki/Infestation" title="Infestation">infestation</a>, invasion of species, storms, and <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> events. The stresses caused by climate change, added to other stresses on ecological systems (e.g., land conversion, <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvesting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>), threaten substantial damage to or complete loss of some unique ecosystems, and <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of some critically endangered species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_synthesis_ecosystems_158-1"><a href="#cite_note-tar_synthesis_ecosystems-158">[158]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change has been estimated to be a major driver of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a> in cool <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a> forests, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">mediterranean-climate</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_tundra" title="Arctic tundra">Arctic tundra</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_ecosystem_impacts_160-0"><a href="#cite_note-global_ecosystem_impacts-160">[160]</a></sup> In other ecosystems, <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land-use</a> change may be a stronger driver of biodiversity loss at least in the near-term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_ecosystem_impacts_160-1"><a href="#cite_note-global_ecosystem_impacts-160">[160]</a></sup> Beyond the year 2050, climate change may be the major driver for biodiversity loss globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_ecosystem_impacts_160-2"><a href="#cite_note-global_ecosystem_impacts-160">[160]</a></sup>
</p><p>A literature assessment by Fischlin <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fischlin_executive_summary_157-1"><a href="#cite_note-fischlin_executive_summary-157">[157]</a></sup> included a quantitative estimate of the number of species at increased risk of extinction due to climate change. With medium confidence, it was projected that approximately 20 to 30% of plant and animal species assessed so far (in an unbiased <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(statistics)" title="Sampling (statistics)">sample</a>) would likely be at increasingly high risk of extinction should global mean temperatures exceed a warming of 2 to 3 °C above pre-industrial temperature levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fischlin_executive_summary_157-2"><a href="#cite_note-fischlin_executive_summary-157">[157]</a></sup>  The uncertainties in this estimate, however, are large: for a rise of about 2 °C the percentage may be as low as 10%, or for about 3 °C, as high as 40%, and depending on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a> (all living <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> of an area, the <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> considered as a unit)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup>  the range is between 1% and 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_ecosystem_impacts_160-3"><a href="#cite_note-global_ecosystem_impacts-160">[160]</a></sup>  As global average temperature exceeds <a href="/wiki/4_Degrees_and_Beyond_International_Climate_Conference" title="4 Degrees and Beyond International Climate Conference">4 °C</a> above pre-industrial levels, model projections suggested that there could be significant extinctions (40–70% of species that were assessed) around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_ecosystem_impacts_160-4"><a href="#cite_note-global_ecosystem_impacts-160">[160]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assessing whether future changes in ecosystems will be beneficial or detrimental is largely based on how ecosystems are valued by human society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup>  For increases in global average temperature exceeding 1.5 to 2.5 °C (relative to global temperatures over the years 1980–1999)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup>  and in concomitant atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> concentrations, projected changes in ecosystems will have predominantly negative consequences for biodiversity and ecosystems goods and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">services</a>, e.g., water and food supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abrupt_or_irreversible_changes">Abrupt or irreversible changes</span></h2>
<p>Physical, ecological and social systems may respond in an abrupt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a> or irregular way to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singularities_165-0"><a href="#cite_note-singularities-165">[165]</a></sup> This is as opposed to a smooth or regular response. A quantitative entity behaves "irregularly" when its <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">dynamics</a> are <a href="/wiki/Continuous_function" title="Continuous function">discontinuous</a> (i.e., not smooth), nondifferentiable, unbounded, wildly varying, or otherwise ill-defined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singularities_165-1"><a href="#cite_note-singularities-165">[165]</a></sup> Such behaviour is often termed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_singularity" title="Mathematical singularity">singular</a>". Irregular behaviour in Earth systems may give rise to certain thresholds, which, when crossed, may lead to a large change in the system.
</p><p>Some singularities could potentially lead to severe impacts at regional or global scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_et_al_singularities_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-white_et_al_singularities-166">[166]</a></sup>  Examples of  "large-scale" singularities are discussed in the articles on <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">runaway climate change</a>. It is possible that human-induced climate change could trigger large-scale singularities, but the probabilities of triggering such events are, for the most part,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup>  poorly understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_et_al_singularities_166-1"><a href="#cite_note-white_et_al_singularities-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>With low to medium confidence, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singularities_165-2"><a href="#cite_note-singularities-165">[165]</a></sup>  concluded that a rapid warming of more than 3 °C above 1990 levels would exceed thresholds that would lead to large-scale discontinuities in the climate system. Since the assessment by Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001), improved scientific understanding provides more guidance for two large-scale singularities: the role of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> feedbacks in future climate change (discussed below in the section on biogeochemical cycles) and the melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_of_reasons_for_concern_152-2"><a href="#cite_note-update_of_reasons_for_concern-152">[152]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeochemical_cycles">Biogeochemical cycles</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a></div>
<p>Climate change may have an effect on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> in an interactive "feedback" process. A feedback exists where an initial process triggers changes in a second process that in turn influences the initial process.  A positive feedback intensifies the original process, and a negative feedback reduces it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_Synthesis_Report_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_Synthesis_Report-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>78</span></sup>  Models suggest that the interaction of the climate system and the carbon cycle is one where the feedback effect is positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change_168-0"><a href="#cite_note-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change-168">[168]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>792</span></sup>
</p><p>Using the A2 SRES emissions scenario, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change_168-1"><a href="#cite_note-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change-168">[168]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>789</span></sup> found that this effect led to additional warming by the years 2090–2100 (relative to the 1990–2000) of 0.1–1.5 °C.  This estimate was made with high confidence. The climate projections made in the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report summarized earlier of 1.1–6.4 °C account for this feedback effect. On the other hand, with medium confidence, Schneider <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change_168-2"><a href="#cite_note-Assessing_key_vulnerabilities_and_the_risk_from_climate_change-168">[168]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>789</span></sup>  commented that additional releases of GHGs were possible from permafrost, peat lands, wetlands, and large stores of marine hydrates at high latitudes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland_and_West_Antarctic_Ice_sheets">Greenland and West Antarctic Ice sheets</span></h3>
<p>With medium confidence, authors of AR4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-magnitudes_of_impact_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-magnitudes_of_impact-53">[53]</a></sup> concluded that with a global average temperature increase of 1–4 °C (relative to temperatures over the years 1990–2000), at least a partial deglaciation of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a>, and possibly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheets</a> would occur. The estimated timescale for partial deglaciation was centuries to millennia, and would contribute 4 to 6 metres (13 to 20 ft) or more to sea level rise over this period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="1100" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/320px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/480px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/640px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This map shows the general location and direction of the warm surface (red) and cold deep water (blue) currents of the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">Salinity</a> is represented by color in units of the Practical Salinity Scale. Low values (blue) are less saline, while high values (orange) are more saline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is an important component of the Earth's climate system, characterized by a northward flow of warm, salty water in the upper layers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic</a> and a southward flow of colder water in the deep Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_170-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> The AMOC is equivalently known as the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> (THC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_of_reasons_for_concern_152-3"><a href="#cite_note-update_of_reasons_for_concern-152">[152]</a></sup> Potential impacts associated with MOC changes include reduced warming or (in the case of abrupt change) absolute cooling of northern high-latitude areas near <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and north-western Europe, an increased warming of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> high-latitudes, tropical drying, as well as changes to <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a>, terrestrial vegetation, oceanic <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> uptake, oceanic oxygen concentrations, and shifts in fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup>  According to an assessment by the US <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="Climate Change Science Program">Climate Change Science Program</a> (CCSP, 2008b),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_170-1"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>  it is very likely (greater than 90% probability, based on expert judgement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_170-2"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> that the strength of the AMOC will decrease over the course of the 21st century. Warming is still expected to occur over most of the European region downstream of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> in response to increasing GHGs, as well as over <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Although it is very unlikely (less than 10% probability, based on expert judgement)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_170-3"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> that the AMOC will collapse in the 21st century, the potential consequences of such a collapse could be severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_170-4"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversibilities">Irreversibilities</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Commitment_to_radiative_forcing">Commitment to radiative forcing</span></h4>
<p>Emissions of GHGs are a potentially irreversible commitment to sustained <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_irreversible_radiative_forcing_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_irreversible_radiative_forcing-172">[172]</a></sup> The contribution of a GHG to radiative forcing depends on the gas's ability to trap <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> (heat) radiation, the concentration of the gas in the atmosphere, and the length of time the gas resides in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albritton_irreversible_radiative_forcing_172-1"><a href="#cite_note-albritton_irreversible_radiative_forcing-172">[172]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is the most important anthropogenic GHG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_2007_causes_of_climate_change_173-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_2007_causes_of_climate_change-173">[173]</a></sup>  While more than half of the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emitted is currently removed from the atmosphere within a century, some fraction (about 20%) of emitted <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> remains in the atmosphere for many thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup>  Consequently, <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emitted today is potentially an irreversible commitment to sustained radiative forcing over thousands of years.
</p><p>This commitment may not be truly irreversible should techniques be developed to remove <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> or other GHGs directly from the atmosphere, or to block sunlight to induce cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_irreversible_climate_change_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_irreversible_climate_change-36">[36]</a></sup>  Techniques of this sort are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a>. Little is known about the effectiveness, costs or potential <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">side-effects</a> of geoengineering options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker_geoengineering_175-0"><a href="#cite_note-barker_geoengineering-175">[175]</a></sup>  Some geoengineering options, such as blocking sunlight, would not prevent further <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker_geoengineering_175-1"><a href="#cite_note-barker_geoengineering-175">[175]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreversible_impacts">Irreversible impacts</span></h4>
<p>Human-induced climate change may lead to irreversible impacts on physical, biological, and social systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup>  There are a number of examples of climate change impacts that may be irreversible, at least over the timescale of many human generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_irreversibilities_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_irreversibilities-177">[177]</a></sup>  These include the large-scale singularities described above – changes in carbon cycle feedbacks, the melting of the Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets, and changes to the AMOC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_irreversibilities_177-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_irreversibilities-177">[177]</a></sup> In biological systems, the extinction of species would be an irreversible impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_irreversibilities_177-2"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_irreversibilities-177">[177]</a></sup> In social systems, unique <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a> may be lost due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_irreversibilities_177-3"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_irreversibilities-177">[177]</a></sup> For example, humans living on <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atoll</a> islands face risks due to sea-level rise, sea-surface warming, and increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_data_and_tools">NASA data and tools</span></h3>
<p>NASA has released public data and tools to predict how temperature and rainfall patterns worldwide may change through to the year 2100 caused by increasing carbon dioxide in Earth’s atmosphere. The dataset shows projected changes worldwide on a regional level simulated by 21 climate models. The data can be viewed on a daily timescale for individual cities and towns and may be used to conduct climate risk assessments to predict the local and global effects of weather dangers, for example droughts, floods, heat waves and declines in agriculture productivity, and help plan responses to global warming effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footnotes">Footnotes</span></h2>
<p><sup>1</sup> The TAR<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uncertainty_in_the_TAR_190-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uncertainty_in_the_TAR-190">[190]</a></sup>  and AR4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup>  that are referred to in this article use specific and quantitative language to describe uncertainty. This language is intended to provide an indication of the level of confidence that IPCC authors have about a particular finding. The qualitative language used to describe uncertainty has a quantitative scale associated with it.  The quantitative values for qualitative terms are intended to ensure that confidence levels are interpreted correctly. This is because qualitative statements, e.g., using the word "likely", can be interpreted differently in quantitative terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44</span></sup>
</p><p>Confidence levels used in the TAR:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Very High = 95% or greater</li>
<li>High = 67–95%</li>
<li>Medium = 33–67%</li>
<li>Low = 5–33%</li>
<li>Very Low = 5% or less</li></ul>
<p>Confidence statements made in AR4 are listed below:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Very high confidence: At least 9 out of 10 chance of being correct</li>
<li>High confidence: About 8 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Medium confidence: About 5 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Low confidence: About 2 out of 10 chance " " "</li>
<li>Very low confidence: Less than a 1 out of 10 chance " " "</li></ul>
<p>IPCC (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> uses the following terms: "very low", "low", "medium", "high", and "very high confidence". Unlike the TAR and AR4, the scale is qualitative rather than quantitative.
</p><p>The quantitative values used by IPCC authors are "subjective" probabilities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uncertainty_in_the_TAR_190-1"><a href="#cite_note-Uncertainty_in_the_TAR-190">[190]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup>  also known as "personalist" or "<a href="/wiki/Bayesian_probability" title="Bayesian probability">Bayesian</a>" probabilities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty_195-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty-195">[195]</a></sup>  and reflect the expert judgement of IPCC authors. In this formulation, probability is not only a function of an event, but also the state of information that is available to the person making the assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty_195-1"><a href="#cite_note-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty-195">[195]</a></sup> In this framework, assigned probabilities may change as more or different information becomes available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty_195-2"><a href="#cite_note-US_CCSP_report_on_uncertainty-195">[195]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC also uses another scale to describe the likelihood of a particular event occurring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-6"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup> This is different from the confidence scales described above, and it is possible to assign confidence values to statements of likelihood. For example, the judgement that an event is improbable (e.g., rolling a <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> twice and getting a six both times) may be assigned a high level of scientific confidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-7"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup> Also, the probability that an event has an even chance of occurring (e.g., a tossed <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coin</a> coming up heads) may also be assigned a high level of confidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty_33-8"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_ar4_uncertainty-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
