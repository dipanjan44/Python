<p>On June 1, 2017, United States President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> announced that the U.S. would cease all participation in the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">2015 Paris Agreement</a> on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>. Trump stated that "The Paris accord will undermine (the U.S.) economy," and "puts (the U.S.) at a permanent disadvantage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During the presidential campaign, Trump had pledged to withdraw from the pact, saying a withdrawal would help American businesses and workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hill1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-3">[3]</a></sup> Trump stated that the withdrawal would be in accordance with his <a href="/wiki/America_First_(policy)" title="America First (policy)">America First</a> policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In accordance with <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement#Withdrawal_from_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Article 28 of the Paris Agreement</a>, the earliest possible effective withdrawal date by the United States cannot be before November 4, 2020, four years after the Agreement came into effect in the United States and one day after the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2020" title="United States presidential election, 2020">2020 U.S. presidential election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT060117_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT060117-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> The White House later clarified that the U.S. will abide by the four-year exit process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PPG-6">[6]</a></sup> Until the withdrawal takes effect, the United States may be obligated to maintain its commitments under the Agreement, such as the requirement to continue reporting its emissions to the United Nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT060117_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT060117-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>While celebrated by some members of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> international reactions to the withdrawal were overwhelmingly negative from across the political spectrum, and the decision received substantial criticism from religious organizations, businesses, political leaders of all parties, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a>, and scientists and citizens from the United States and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etherington1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-etherington1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following Trump's announcement, the governors of several U.S. states formed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Climate_Alliance" title="United States Climate Alliance">United States Climate Alliance</a> to continue to advance the objectives of the Paris Agreement at the state level despite the federal withdrawal. As of February 22, 2018, 16 states and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> have joined the alliance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and similar commitments have also been expressed by other state governors, mayors, and businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT060117_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT060117-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Paris Agreement was an addition to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC), initially agreed to by all 195 countries present at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in December of that year, including the United States then under the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Barack_Obama" title="Presidency of Barack Obama">presidency of Barack Obama</a>. Due to the status of the United States and China as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">greatest emitters of carbon dioxide</a>, Obama's support and his <a href="/wiki/China%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="China–United States relations">cooperation with China</a> were seen as major factors leading to the convention's early success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main aim of the Agreement is to hold the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels", predominantly by reducing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions. The agreement differs from the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, the last widely adopted amendment to the UNFCCC, in that no annexes are established to lessen responsibility of developing nations. Rather, emissions targets for each nation were separately negotiated and are to be voluntarily enforced, leading United States officials to regard the Paris Agreement as an <a href="/wiki/Executive_agreement" title="Executive agreement">executive agreement</a> rather than a legally binding <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a>. This removed the requirement for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> to ratify the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In April 2016, the United States became a signatory to the Paris Agreement, and accepted it by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">executive order</a> in September 2016. President Obama committed the United States to contributing US$3 billion to the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Fund has set itself a goal of raising $100 billion a year by 2020.
</p><p>Article 28 of the agreement enables parties to withdraw from the agreement after sending a withdrawal notification to the depositary, but notice can be given no earlier than three years after the agreement goes into force for the country.  Withdrawal is effective one year after the depositary is notified.  Alternatively, the Agreement stipulates that withdrawal from the UNFCCC, under which the Paris Agreement was adopted, would also withdraw the state from the Paris Agreement.  The conditions for withdrawal from the UNFCCC are the same as for the Paris Agreement.
</p><p>On November 8, 2016, four days after the Paris Agreement <a href="/wiki/Coming_into_force" title="Coming into force">entered into force</a> in the United States, Donald Trump of the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> was <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">elected</a> President of the United States. Many conservative Republicans dispute the level of human involvement in climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donald_Trump_and_global_warming" title="Donald Trump and global warming">Trump is a climate change sceptic</a>, who in 2012 tweeted that he believed the concept of global warming was created by China in order to impair American competitiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump_presidential_campaign,_2016" title="Donald Trump presidential campaign, 2016">Trump's 2016 election campaign</a>, Trump promised to revitalize the coal industry, which he claimed has been hampered by environmental regulations. It has been argued that this contributed to the support he enjoyed from crucial <a href="/wiki/Swing_state" title="Swing state">swing states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His opposition to climate change mitigation was unchanged in the first months of <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Donald_Trump" title="Presidency of Donald Trump">his presidency</a>, in which he issued an <a href="/wiki/List_of_executive_actions_by_Donald_Trump" title="List of executive actions by Donald Trump">executive order</a> to reverse Obama's <a href="/wiki/Clean_Power_Plan" title="Clean Power Plan">Clean Power Plan</a> and other environmental regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In April 2017, a group of 20 <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">members of the European Parliament</a> from the right-wing <a href="/wiki/Alternative_for_Germany" title="Alternative for Germany">Alternative for Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a>, and other parties sent a letter to Trump on urging him to withdraw from the Paris Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEP_letter_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEP_letter-21">[21]</a></sup> On May 25, 2017, 22 Republican Senators, including Senate Majority Leader <a href="/wiki/Mitch_McConnell" title="Mitch McConnell">Mitch McConnell</a>, sent a two-page letter to Trump urging him to withdraw the U.S. from the Paris agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APSenatorsUrge_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-APSenatorsUrge-22">[22]</a></sup> The letter was drafted by Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Barrasso" title="John Barrasso">John Barrasso</a>, the chairman of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Environment_and_Public_Works" title="United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works">Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works</a>, and Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Inhofe" title="Jim Inhofe">Jim Inhofe</a>, known for his longtime <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">climate change denial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthyGambino_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthyGambino-23">[23]</a></sup> Most of signatories to the letter were elected from states reliant on the burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> (coal, oil and gas);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APSenatorsUrge_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-APSenatorsUrge-22">[22]</a></sup> the group of 22 senators had collectively received more than $10 million in campaign contributions from fossil-fuels companies in the previous three election cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCarthyGambino_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthyGambino-23">[23]</a></sup> Earlier the same week, a group of 40 Democratic Senators sent Trump a letter urging him to keep America in the Paris Agreement, writing that "a withdrawal would hurt America's credibility and influence on the world stage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APSenatorsUrge_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-APSenatorsUrge-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both support for the move and opposition to it were reported among Trump's cabinet and advisers: Secretary of Energy <a href="/wiki/Rick_Perry" title="Rick Perry">Rick Perry</a>, Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Rex_Tillerson" title="Rex Tillerson">Rex Tillerson</a>, economic advisor <a href="/wiki/Gary_Cohn_(investment_banker)" title="Gary Cohn (investment banker)">Gary Cohn</a>, and advisor and son-in-law <a href="/wiki/Jared_Kushner" title="Jared Kushner">Jared Kushner</a> reportedly wanted the United States to remain committed to the agreement, while White House Advisor <a href="/wiki/Steve_Bannon" title="Steve Bannon">Steve Bannon</a>, White House Counsel <a href="/wiki/Don_McGahn" title="Don McGahn">Don McGahn</a>, and EPA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Scott_Pruitt" title="Scott Pruitt">Scott Pruitt</a> wanted the United States to abandon it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/43rd_G7_summit" title="43rd G7 summit">G7 summit in late May 2017</a>, Trump was the only <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Seven" title="Group of Seven">G7</a> member not to reconfirm commitment to the Paris Agreement. German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>, one of the other leaders present, was publicly unimpressed with Trump's refusal to cooperate on climate change mitigation, which was seen to damage <a href="/wiki/Germany%E2%80%93United_States_relations" title="Germany–United States relations">Germany–United States relations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The communique issued at the conclusion of the summit stated that the United States "is not in a position to join the consensus" of the other G7 countries on policies regarding climate change and the Paris Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In a televised announcement from the White House Rose Garden on June 1, 2017, Trump said, "In order to fulfill my solemn duty to protect the United States and its citizens, the United States will withdraw from the Paris climate accord," adding "The bottom line is that the Paris accord is very unfair at the highest level to the United States." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hill1-2">[2]</a></sup> He claimed that the agreement, if implemented, would cost the United States $3 trillion in lost GDP and 6.5 million jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-3">[3]</a></sup> He added that it would "undermine our economy, hamstring our workers," and "effectively decapitate our coal industry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-PPG-6">[6]</a></sup> He said he was open to renegotiating the arrangement or negotiating a new one, but European and UN leaders said the pact "cannot be renegotiated at the request of a single party".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-28">[28]</a></sup> Trump also criticized the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a>, calling it a scheme to redistribute wealth from rich to poor countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The White House said that Trump will end the implementation of carbon reduction targets set by former President Barack Obama<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-28">[28]</a></sup> and that the withdrawal will be done in accordance with the years-long exit process spelled out in the accord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-PPG-6">[6]</a></sup> On September 16, 2017, a European official said the Trump administration had appeared to soften its stance on withdrawing the agreement. The White House told the press that it had not changed its position on the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Examinations of Trump's speech by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> pointed to numerous fallacies, including, but not limited to, claims that the U.S., under the Paris Agreement, was forbidden to build coal power plants; that a difference of 0.2 degrees Celsius is insignificant in <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>; that U.S. contributions to the Green Climate Fund were paid out of the U.S. defense budget; projections that the U.S. is on course to become the "cleanest" nation on earth; and Trump's reiterated claim of personal support for environmental causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In accordance with <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement#Withdrawal_from_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Article 28 of the Paris Agreement</a>, the earliest possible effective withdrawal date by the United States cannot be before November 4, 2020, four years after the Agreement came into effect in the United States and coincidentally one day after the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2020" title="United States presidential election, 2020">2020 U.S. presidential election</a>. Until the withdrawal takes effect, the United States may be obligated to maintain its commitments under the Agreement, including the requirement to continue reporting its emissions to the United Nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT060117_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT060117-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> According to a memo obtained by <a href="/wiki/HuffPost" title="HuffPost">HuffPost</a> believed to be written by US State Department legal office, any "attempts to withdraw from the Paris Agreement outside of the above-described withdrawal provisions would be inconsistent with international law and would not be accepted internationally."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 4, 2017, the Trump administration formally outlined its intention of the withdrawal in an official notice delivered to the United Nations as depositary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UScom_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-UScom-37">[37]</a></sup> In a separate statement, the State Department said it will continue participating in international climate change negotiations, including talks aimed at implementing the climate deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the withdrawal takes effect, the U.S. will be the only UNFCCC member states who is not a signatory to the Paris Agreement.  At the time of the original withdrawal announcement, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> were also not participants; however, both Syria and Nicaragua have since ratified the agreement, leaving the U.S. the only UNFCCC member state intending to not be a party to the Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Luke Kemp of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a>'s Fenner School of Environment and Society wrote in a commentary for <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> that "withdrawal is unlikely to change US emissions" since "the greenhouse gas emissions of the US are divorced from international legal obligations." However, he added that it could hamper climate change mitigation efforts if the U.S. stops contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a>. Kemp said the effect of a U.S. withdrawal could be either good or bad for the Paris agreement, since "a rogue US can cause more damage inside rather than outside of the agreement." Finally, "A withdrawal could also make the US into a climate pariah and provide a unique opportunity for China and the EU to take control of the climate regime and significantly boost their international reputations and <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">soft power</a>." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_economic_impact">Potential economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Potential economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The German car industry expressed concerns about its ability to remain competitive in light of the United States decision to withdraw. The president of the German auto industry lobby group <a href="/wiki/Verband_der_Automobilindustrie" title="Verband der Automobilindustrie">VDA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Wissmann" title="Matthias Wissmann">Matthias Wissmann</a> said, "The regrettable announcement by the USA makes it inevitable that Europe must facilitate a cost efficient and economically feasible climate policy to remain internationally competitive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the larger auto and aviation companies had already invested billions into reducing emissions and were unlikely to change course. <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, the largest automobile manufacturer in the United States, immediately pointed out: "Our position on climate change has not changed ... we publicly advocate for climate action," and reiterated its support for various climate pledges. Analyst Rebecca Lindland also pointed out that manufacturers of automobiles were under no specific restrictions under the Accord and that nothing had changed. Even if Trump loosened other restrictions on the car industry that allowed for the production of less environmental cars, such cars still needed to conform to standards before they could be exported to other continents or even certain states. Jason Bordoff, energy-policy expert at Columbia University, agreed that withdrawing would make no difference to the economy, arguing that it would be determined by market conditions like the price of oil and gas. At the same time, airlines have been spending billions on seeking more fuel efficient ways to fly anyway –fuel is an airline's second-biggest expense after labor and so using less fuel (which means less emissions) is in their financial interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Kabir Nanda and Varad Pande, senior consultant and partner at <a href="/wiki/Dalberg_Global_Development_Advisors" title="Dalberg Global Development Advisors">Dahlberg</a>, respectively, argued that despite the US withdrawal the American private sector was still committed to renewable energy and technology. Also noted was the fact that solar energy had become cheaper than coal in an increasing number of countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petitions">Petitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Petitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Petitions were launched across states in order to persuade state governors to join the Paris Agreement or have Trump reverse the planned withdrawal, which included a "<a href="#ExtLinks">ParisMyState</a>" and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MoveOn.org" title="MoveOn.org">MoveOn</a> petition that has received over 535,000 signatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientists_and_environmentalists">Scientists and environmentalists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scientists and environmentalists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Piers_Forster" title="Piers Forster">Piers Forster</a>, the director of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>' Priestley International Centre for Climate, called the decision to withdraw "a sad day for <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_policy" title="Evidence-based policy">evidence-based policy</a>" and expressed hope that individual Americans, businesses and states would nevertheless choose to <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">decarbonize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceReaction_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceReaction-49">[49]</a></sup> Climate scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dave_Reay" title="Dave Reay">Dave Reay</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> said that "The United States will come to rue this day."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceReaction_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceReaction-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/University_Corporation_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="University Corporation for Atmospheric Research">University Corporation for Atmospheric Research</a> (UCAR), in a statement by its president <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Busalacchi_Jr." title="Antonio Busalacchi Jr.">Antonio Busalacchi Jr.</a>, said that the decision to withdraw "does not mean that climate change will go away" and warned that "the heightened potential for increased greenhouse gas emissions poses a substantial threat to our communities, businesses, and military."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceReaction_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceReaction-49">[49]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Information_Technology_and_Innovation_Foundation" title="Information Technology and Innovation Foundation">Information Technology and Innovation Foundation</a> called the decision to withdraw "very discouraging" and said that it would diminish confidence in international climate change efforts; the technology think tank called for federal efforts on "the smart grid, energy storage, carbon capture and sequestration, and advanced nuclear and solar power" and warned that "Without a smart, aggressive clean-energy innovation strategy, the world will not avert the worst effects of climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceReaction_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceReaction-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canadian academic and environmental activist <a href="/wiki/David_Suzuki" title="David Suzuki">David Suzuki</a> stated, "Trump just passed on the best deal the planet has ever seen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Navroz Dubash of the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Policy_Research" title="Centre for Policy Research">Centre for Policy Research</a> in New Delhi expressed bafflement at Trump's move, citing the declining costs of renewable energy sources and the increasing difficulty of obtaining investment for fossil-fuel projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Environmental scientist and risk assessor Dana Nuccitelli stated that it "now seems inevitable that the history books will view <a href="/wiki/Historical_rankings_of_presidents_of_the_United_States" title="Historical rankings of presidents of the United States">Trump as America's worst-ever president</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian1-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Ward_(communications_director)" title="Bob Ward (communications director)">Bob Ward</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Grantham_Research_Institute_on_Climate_Change_and_the_Environment" title="Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment">Grantham Research Institute</a> also described Trump's speech as "confused nonsense".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> criticized Trump, saying that he "will cause avoidable environmental damage to our beautiful planet, endangering the natural world, for us and our children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmental groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a>, condemned Trump's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> American environmentalist and writer <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a>, the founder of the climate change action group <a href="/wiki/350.org" title="350.org">350.org</a>, called the move "a stupid and reckless decision — our nation's dumbest act since <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">launching the war in Iraq</a>." McKibben wrote that Trump's decision to withdraw amounted "to a thorough repudiation of two of the civilizing forces on our planet: diplomacy and science." He called upon U.S. states and cities to "double down" on commitments to <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_political_response">Domestic political response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Domestic political response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Republicans">Republicans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Republicans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Republicans gave mixed reviews of Trump's decision to withdraw. Vice president <a href="/wiki/Mike_Pence" title="Mike Pence">Mike Pence</a> stated that Trump administration "demonstrated real leadership" by pulling out United States of the international accords which he called "a transfer of wealth from the most powerful economy in the world to other countries around the planet". He also stated that he doesn't understand why Democrats and <a href="/wiki/Liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Liberalism in the United States">liberals in the United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left</a> around the world care about climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sampathkumar_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sampathkumar-59">[59]</a></sup> Senate Majority Leader <a href="/wiki/Mitch_McConnell" title="Mitch McConnell">Mitch McConnell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Speaker_of_the_House" title="United States Speaker of the House">Speaker of the House</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ryan" title="Paul Ryan">Paul Ryan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counselor_to_the_President" title="Counselor to the President">Counselor to the President</a> <a href="/wiki/Kellyanne_Conway" title="Kellyanne Conway">Kellyanne Conway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Administrator_of_the_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency">Administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency</a> <a href="/wiki/Scott_Pruitt" title="Scott Pruitt">Scott Pruitt</a> praised the decision as a victory for America's middle class, workers, businesses and coal miners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sampathkumar_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sampathkumar-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-politico1_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-politico1-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Texas_Attorney_General" title="Texas Attorney General">Texas Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ken_Paxton" title="Ken Paxton">Ken Paxton</a> described Trump's decision as "courageous" and said that it lifted a burden from the American taxpayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian1_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian1-52">[52]</a></sup> However, Republican Senator <a href="/wiki/Susan_Collins" title="Susan Collins">Susan Collins</a> of Maine was critical of the decision, stating that she was disappointed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">Governor of California</a> <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a> issued a video address describing Trump's decision as a retrograde step.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian1_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian1-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Democrats">Democrats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Democrats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Former President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> wrote: "Walking away from Paris treaty is a mistake. Climate change is real. We owe our children more. Protecting our future also creates more jobs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian1_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian1-52">[52]</a></sup> Former President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> said of Trump's decision: "Even as this Administration joins a small handful of nations that reject the future, I'm confident that our states, cities, and businesses will step up and do even more to lead the way, and help protect for future generations the one planet we've got."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Former vice-president <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> said he believes the move imperils American security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his withdrawal speech, President Trump stated: "I was elected to represent the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, not Paris." The incumbent Mayor of Pittsburgh, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Peduto" title="Bill Peduto">Bill Peduto</a>, immediately acknowledged on Twitter with a reminder that 80% of his city's voters favored <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> during the 2016 presidential election, and wrote: "As the Mayor of Pittsburgh, I can assure you that we will follow the guidelines of the Paris Agreement for our people, our economy and future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Senate Democratic Leader <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Schumer" title="Chuck Schumer">Chuck Schumer</a> condemned the withdrawal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._states">U.S. states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: U.S. states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Climate_Alliance" title="United States Climate Alliance">United States Climate Alliance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Climate-alliance.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="928" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Climate-alliance.svg/220px-Climate-alliance.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Climate-alliance.svg/330px-Climate-alliance.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Climate-alliance.svg/440px-Climate-alliance.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Climate-alliance.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #000080; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> United States Climate Alliance</div><div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #007f80; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> Other areas whose officials have expressed support for the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a></div></div></div></div>
<p>In an immediate response to the withdrawal, the governors of California, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and Washington founded the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Climate_Alliance" title="United States Climate Alliance">United States Climate Alliance</a>, pledging to uphold the Paris Agreement within their borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waPo06012017_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-waPo06012017-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>  By the evening of June 1, 2017, Colorado, Connecticut, Hawaii, Oregon, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Vermont and Virginia declared their intention to join with United States Climate Alliance members in reaching Paris Agreement goals. Governors of other states also expressed interest in upholding the Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> As of July 2017 the alliance included 13 states plus <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alliance_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-alliance-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_response">International response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: International response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Following Trump's announcement, <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shell_Oil_Company" title="Shell Oil Company">Shell</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> reaffirmed their support for the Paris Agreement and for measures to tackle climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_166-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> pledged $15 million to the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change Executive Secretariat, explaining: "Americans will honor and fulfill the Paris Agreement by leading from the bottom up – and there isn't anything Washington can do to stop us".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> Shortly following Trump's announcement, thirty city mayors, three state governors, more than eighty university presidents and the leaders of more than a hundred businesses joined Bloomberg in opening negotiations with the United Nations to submit a plan for limiting American climate-change emissions in accord with the Paris Agreement guidelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Blankfein" title="Lloyd Blankfein">Lloyd Blankfein</a> described Trump's decision as "a setback for the environment and for the U.S.'s leadership position in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_166-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-166">[166]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Immelt" title="Jeff Immelt">Jeff Immelt</a> stated that "climate change is real".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple tech company executives—including Google CEO <a href="/wiki/Sundar_Pichai" title="Sundar Pichai">Sundar Pichai</a>, Microsoft President and Chief Legal Officer Brad Smith, Apple CEO <a href="/wiki/Tim_Cook" title="Tim Cook">Tim Cook</a>, Facebook CEO <a href="/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg" title="Mark Zuckerberg">Mark Zuckerberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> CEO Jeff Immelt—condemned the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup> Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Satya_Nadella" title="Satya Nadella">Satya Nadella</a> said Microsoft believes that "climate change is an urgent issue that demands global action." Google's <a href="/wiki/Sundar_Pichai" title="Sundar Pichai">Sundar Pichai</a> tweeted "Disappointed with today's decision. Google will keep working hard for a cleaner, more prosperous future for all". Facebook's <a href="/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg" title="Mark Zuckerberg">Mark Zuckerberg</a> said "Withdrawing from the Paris climate agreement is bad for the environment, bad for the economy, and it puts our children's future at risk."
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Resignations_from_presidential_advisory_boards_in_protest">Resignations from presidential advisory boards in protest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Resignations from presidential advisory boards in protest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Two business leaders resigned from Trump advisory boards in protest to his decision to withdraw. <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/Tesla,_Inc." title="Tesla, Inc.">Tesla, Inc.</a> and <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>, resigned from the two presidential advisory councils on which he had sat. Musk stated: "Climate change is real. Leaving Paris is not good for America or the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Iger" title="Robert Iger">Robert Iger</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a>, also resigned, saying "As a matter of principle, I've resigned from the President's Council over the Paris Agreement withdrawal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._public_opinion">U.S. public opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: U.S. public opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Paris Agreement is broadly popular among Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-177">[177]</a></sup> A national poll by the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Council_on_Global_Affairs" title="Chicago Council on Global Affairs">Chicago Council on Global Affairs</a> conducted in June 2016  found that 71 percent of American adults favored U.S. participation in the Paris Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup> Similar, a November 2016 poll conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Program_on_Climate_Change_Communication" title="Yale Program on Climate Change Communication">Yale Program on Climate Change Communication</a> found that 69 percent of U.S. registered voters favored U.S. participation in the Paris Agreement, while just 13 percent were opposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> Trump's decision to withdraw the U.S. from the accord was seen as an attempt to appeal to his <a href="/wiki/Base_(politics)" title="Base (politics)">base</a>, even at the risk of alienating Democrats and independent voters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_177-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-177">[177]</a></sup> This strategy diverged from the typical approach taken by most U.S. presidents, who historically have sought to appeal to the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_177-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-177">[177]</a></sup> A <i>New York Times</i> analysis described the move as "a daring and risky strategy" taken by "the first president in the history of polling to govern without the support of a majority of the public from the start of his tenure," adding: "In effect, Mr. Trump is doubling down on presiding as a minority president, betting that when the time comes, his fervent supporters will matter more, especially clustered in key Midwest states."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_177-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-177">[177]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>/ABC News <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">public opinion</a> survey of American adults, conducted from June 2–4, 2017, found that 59 percent opposed Trump's decision to withdraw the U.S. from the Paris Agreement, and just 28 percent supported it. Asked about the effect of withdrawal on the U.S. economy, 42 percent said it would hurt the economy; 32 percent believed it would help the economy; and 20 percent believed that it would make no difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClementDavisPoll_181-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClementDavisPoll-181">[181]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPoPollDetails_182-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPoPollDetails-182">[182]</a></sup> The poll showed a sharp division among partisan lines: 67 percent of Republicans supported Trump's decision, but just 22 percent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(voter)" title="Independent (voter)">independents</a> and 8 percent of Democrats supported it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClementDavisPoll_181-1"><a href="#cite_note-ClementDavisPoll-181">[181]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic">Domestic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Domestic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>, in an editorial, stated that "There was no greatness in the decision he rendered Thursday, just the heightened prospect of a climate-stricken globe left behind for future generations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> called it "disgraceful" and stated that Trump "knows nothing or cares little about the science underlying the stark warnings of environmental disruption."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup>
</p><p>For their edition of June 2, 2017, the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i> revived their famous 1975 "Ford to City: Drop Dead" cover with a photo of Trump and the words "Trump to World: Drop Dead".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate-1jun2017_185-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-1jun2017-185">[185]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Times" title="Tampa Bay Times">Tampa Bay Times</a></i> criticized the move, writing that it would especially endanger coastal states such as Florida, which are already suffering from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rising_sea_level" title="Rising sea level">rising sea levels</a>, which damage property and infrastructure and harm the drinking water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Free_Press" title="Detroit Free Press">Detroit Free Press</a></i> stated that "President Donald Trump has betrayed the future of our children, our grandchildren, and our planet".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P." title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg</a> stated that "Under Trump, the U.S. has already become an irresponsible role model."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_San_Diego_Union-Tribune" title="The San Diego Union-Tribune">The San Diego Union-Tribune</a></i> stated that "President Trump is ushering in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Century" title="Chinese Century">Chinese Century</a>" and called it the worst decision of Trump's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup>
</p><p>A piece by commentator <a href="/wiki/Erick_Erickson" title="Erick Erickson">Erick Erickson</a> published by <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a> described the withdrawal from the Paris Agreement as the correct move, for the reason that "climate change is [not] an issue worth caring much about".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-190"><a href="#cite_note-190">[190]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_E._Schoen" title="Douglas E. Schoen">Douglas E. Schoen</a>, also writing for Fox, contrarily said that a withdrawal from the Paris Agreement "only hastens America's retreat from global political and economic leadership".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign">Foreign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Foreign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Foreign coverage of Trump's announcement was overwhelmingly unfavorable.  A lead in the British newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> said that the decision would be unlikely to stunt the growth of renewable energy, and suggested that "a much more likely casualty of Trump's choice is the US economy he claims to be protecting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup> The British newspaper <i>The Independent</i> noted the "tension between myth and reality" in Trump's withdrawal speech.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> The German tabloid <i><a href="/wiki/Berliner_Kurier" title="Berliner Kurier">Berliner Kurier</a></i> ran a headline "Erde an Trump: Fuck You!" ("Earth to Trump: Fuck You!").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup>
</p><p>China's <a href="/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency" title="Xinhua News Agency">Xinhua</a> state news agency called the withdrawal a "global setback."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> said that
</p>
<blockquote><p>In the long catalogue of destructive things that Donald Trump has inflicted on the United States and the world, pulling out of the most important global attempt to slow the impact of climate change must go down as the worst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protests">Protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Protest_(35042111665).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg/220px-Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg/330px-Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg/440px-Paris_Protest_%2835042111665%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Protest_(35042111665).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Protesters in Washington, D.C., the day of the announcement</div></div></div>
<p>Protesters gathered at the White House gates on the day of the announcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehill-1jun2017-1_198-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehill-1jun2017-1-198">[198]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nye" title="Bill Nye">Bill Nye</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Science_communication" title="Science communication">science communicator</a> and television personality known for making scientific concepts more accessible to the general public, was one of the protesters in attendance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehill-1jun2017-2_199-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehill-1jun2017-2-199">[199]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/John_A._Wilson_Building" title="John A. Wilson Building">John A. Wilson Building</a> in D.C. was lit in green in protest of the decision,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcwash-1jun2017_200-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcwash-1jun2017-200">[200]</a></sup> as were <a href="/wiki/One_World_Trade_Center" title="One World Trade Center">One World Trade Center</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosciuszko_Bridge" title="Kosciuszko Bridge">Kosciuszko Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Hall" title="New York City Hall">New York City Hall</a> in New York City, <a href="/wiki/Boston_City_Hall" title="Boston City Hall">Boston City Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montreal_City_Hall" title="Montreal City Hall">Montreal City Hall</a>, the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_de_Ville,_Paris" title="Hôtel de Ville, Paris">Hôtel de Ville</a> in Paris, and the <a href="/wiki/Monumento_a_la_Revoluci%C3%B3n" title="Monumento a la Revolución">Monumento a la Revolución</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Angel_of_Independence" title="Angel of Independence">Angel of Independence</a> in Mexico City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn-1jun2017_201-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-1jun2017-201">[201]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo-2jun2017_202-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-2jun2017-202">[202]</a></sup> Protests also occurred in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcny-1jun2017_203-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcny-1jun2017-203">[203]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami-1jun2017_204-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami-1jun2017-204">[204]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sd-1jun2017_205-0"><a href="#cite_note-sd-1jun2017-205">[205]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_New_York" title="Syracuse, New York">Syracuse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cny-1jun2017_206-0"><a href="#cite_note-cny-1jun2017-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
