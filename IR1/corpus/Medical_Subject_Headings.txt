<p><b>Medical Subject Headings</b> (<b>MeSH</b>) is a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Controlled_vocabulary" title="Controlled vocabulary">controlled vocabulary</a> for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexing</a> journal articles and books in the life sciences; it serves as a <a href="/wiki/Thesaurus_(information_retrieval)" title="Thesaurus (information retrieval)">thesaurus</a> that facilitates searching. Created and updated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">United States National Library of Medicine</a> (NLM), it is used by the <a href="/wiki/MEDLINE" title="MEDLINE">MEDLINE</a>/<a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a> article database and by NLM's catalog of book holdings. MeSH is also used by <a href="/wiki/ClinicalTrials.gov" title="ClinicalTrials.gov">ClinicalTrials.gov</a> registry to classify which diseases are studied by trials registered in ClinicalTrials.gov.
</p><p>MeSH was introduced in 1960, with the NLM's own <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">index catalogue</a> and the subject headings of the <a href="/wiki/Index_Medicus" title="Index Medicus">Quarterly Cumulative Index Medicus</a> (1940 edition) as precursors. The yearly printed version of MeSH was discontinued in 2007 and MeSH is now available online only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup> It can be browsed and downloaded free of charge through <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>. Originally in English, MeSH has been translated into numerous other languages and allows retrieval of documents from different languages.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2009 version of MeSH contains a total of 25,186 <i>subject headings</i>, also known as <i>descriptors</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-factsheet_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-2">[2]</a></sup> Most of these are accompanied by a short description or definition, links to related descriptors, and a list of synonyms or very similar terms (known as <i>entry terms</i>). This additional information and the hierarchical structure (see below) make the MeSH essentially a <a href="/wiki/Thesaurus_(information_retrieval)" title="Thesaurus (information retrieval)">thesaurus</a>, rather than a plain subject headings list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptor_hierarchy">Descriptor hierarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descriptor hierarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>descriptors</i> or <i>subject headings</i> are arranged in a hierarchy. A given descriptor may appear at several locations in the hierarchical tree. The tree locations carry systematic labels known as <i>tree numbers</i>, and consequently one descriptor can carry several tree numbers. For example, the descriptor "Digestive System Neoplasms" has the tree numbers C06.301 and C04.588.274; C stands for Diseases, C06 for Digestive System Diseases and C06.301 for Digestive System Neoplasms; C04 for Neoplasms, C04.588 for Neoplasms By Site, and C04.588.274 also for Digestive System Neoplasms. The tree numbers of a given descriptor are subject to change as MeSH is updated. Every descriptor also carries a unique alphanumerical ID that will not change.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions">Descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most subject headings come with a short description or definition. See the <a class="external text" href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/sites/entrez?Db=mesh&amp;Cmd=ShowDetailView&amp;TermToSearch=68003924&amp;ordinalpos=3&amp;itool=EntrezSystem2.PEntrez.Mesh.Mesh_ResultsPanel.Mesh_RVDocSum" rel="nofollow">MeSH description for diabetes type 2</a> as an example. The explanatory text is written by the MeSH team based on their standard sources if not otherwise stated. References are mostly encyclopaedias and standard textbooks of the subject areas. References for specific statements in the descriptions are not given, instead readers are referred to the <a class="external text" href="https://www.nlm.nih.gov/mesh/intro_biblio2007.html" rel="nofollow">bibliography</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifiers">Qualifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Qualifiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the descriptor hierarchy, MeSH contains a small number of standard <i>qualifiers</i> (also known as <i>subheadings</i>), which can be added to descriptors to narrow down the topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, "Measles" is a descriptor and "epidemiology" is a qualifier; "Measles/epidemiology" describes the subheading of epidemiological articles about Measles. The "epidemiology" qualifier can be added to all other disease descriptors. Not all descriptor/qualifier combinations are allowed since some of them may be meaningless. In all there are 83 different qualifiers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supplements">Supplements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Supplements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the descriptors, MeSH also contains some 139,000 <i>supplementary concept records</i>. These do not belong to the controlled vocabulary as such; instead they enlarge the thesaurus and contain links to the closest fitting descriptor to be used in a MEDLINE search. Many of these records describe chemical substances.
</p>
<h2><span id="Use_in_Medline.2FPubMed"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Medline/PubMed">Use in Medline/PubMed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use in Medline/PubMed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In MEDLINE/PubMed, every journal article is indexed with about 10–15 subject headings, subheadings and supplementary concept records, with some of them designated as <i>major</i> and marked with an asterisk, indicating the article's major topics. When performing a MEDLINE search via PubMed, entry terms are automatically translated into (i.e. mapped to) the corresponding descriptors with a good degree of reliability; it is recommended to check the 'Details tab' in PubMed to see how a search formulation was translated. By default, a search for a descriptor will include all the descriptors in the hierarchy below the given one.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_ClinicalTrials.gov">Use in ClinicalTrials.gov</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_Subject_Headings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use in ClinicalTrials.gov">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In ClinicalTrials.gov, each trial has keywords that describe the trial. The ClinicalTrials.gov team assigns each trial two sets of MeSH terms. One  set for the conditions studied by the trial and another for the set of interventions used in the trial. The XML file that can be downloaded for each trial contains these MeSH keywords. The XML file also has a comment that says: "the assignment of MeSH keywords is done by imperfect algorithm".
</p>
