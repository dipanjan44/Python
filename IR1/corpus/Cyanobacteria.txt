<p><b>Cyanobacteria</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɪər/: 'ear' in 'near'">ɪər</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also known as <b>Cyanophyta</b>, are a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylum_(biology)" title="Phylum (biology)">phylum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that obtain their energy through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucmp-cyanobacteria-lh_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucmp-cyanobacteria-lh-4">[4]</a></sup> and are the only photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> able to produce <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The name "cyanobacteria" comes from the color of the bacteria (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">κυανός</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek" title="Romanization of Greek">translit.</a> </small><i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek-language transliteration">kyanós</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a> </small>'blue').<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Cyanobacteria, which are prokaryotes, are also called <b>"blue-green algae"</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucmp-cyanobacteria-lh_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ucmp-cyanobacteria-lh-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncbi-taxonomy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncbi-taxonomy-8">[8]</a></sup> though the term "<a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>" in modern usage is restricted to <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allaby_92_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allaby_92-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophic</a> prokaryotes, cyanobacteria have internal membranes. These are flattened sacs called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thylakoids" title="Thylakoids">thylakoids</a> where photosynthesis is performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">Phototrophic</a> eukaryotes perform photosynthesis by <a href="/wiki/Plastid" title="Plastid">plastids</a> that may have their ancestry in cyanobacteria, acquired long ago via a process called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiosis" title="Endosymbiosis">endosymbiosis</a>. These endosymbiotic cyanobacteria in eukaryotes may have evolved or differentiated into specialized <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etioplast" title="Etioplast">etioplasts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leucoplast" title="Leucoplast">leucoplasts</a>.
</p><p>By producing and releasing oxygen (as a byproduct of photosynthesis), cyanobacteria are thought to have converted the early oxygen-poor, <a href="/wiki/Reducing_atmosphere" title="Reducing atmosphere">reducing atmosphere</a> into an <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizing</a> one, causing the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a> and the "rusting of the Earth",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitton2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitton2012-12">[12]</a></sup> which dramatically changed the composition of the Earth's life forms and led to the near-extinction of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic organisms</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cyanobacteria are a group of photosynthetic bacteria, some of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen-fixing" title="Nitrogen-fixing">nitrogen-fixing</a>, that live in a wide variety of moist soils and water either freely or in a symbiotic relationship with plants or <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>-forming <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> (as in the lichen genus <i><a href="/wiki/Peltigera" title="Peltigera">Peltigera</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They range from <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular</a> to <a href="/wiki/Filamentation" title="Filamentation">filamentous</a> and include <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonial</a> species. Colonies may form filaments, sheets, or even hollow spheres. Some filamentous species can differentiate into several different <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> types: <b>vegetative cells</b> – the normal, photosynthetic cells that are formed under favorable growing conditions; <b><a href="/wiki/Akinete" title="Akinete">akinetes</a></b> – climate-resistant spores that may form when environmental conditions become harsh; and thick-walled <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterocysts" title="Heterocysts">heterocysts</a></b> – which contain the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenase</a>, vital for <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golden_1998_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golden_1998-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fay_1992_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fay_1992-16">[16]</a></sup> in an anaerobic environment due to its sensitivity to oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fay_1992_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fay_1992-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_fixation">Nitrogen fixation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nitrogen fixation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some cyanobacteria can fix atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> in anaerobic conditions by means of specialized cells called <a href="/wiki/Heterocyst" title="Heterocyst">heterocysts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golden_1998_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Golden_1998-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fay_1992_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fay_1992-16">[16]</a></sup> Heterocysts may also form under the appropriate environmental conditions (anoxic) when fixed nitrogen is scarce. Heterocyst-forming species are specialized for nitrogen fixation and are able to fix nitrogen gas into <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrites" title="Nitrites">nitrites</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrates" title="Nitrates">nitrates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></a>), which can be absorbed by plants and converted to protein and nucleic acids (atmospheric nitrogen is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioavailable" title="Bioavailable">bioavailable</a> to plants, except for those having endosymbiotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen-fixing_bacteria" title="Nitrogen-fixing bacteria">nitrogen-fixing bacteria</a>, especially the <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a> family, among others).
</p><p>Free-living cyanobacteria are present in the water of <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">rice paddies</a>, and cyanobacteria can be found growing as <a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">epiphytes</a> on the surfaces of the green alga, <i><a href="/wiki/Chara_(alga)" title="Chara (alga)">Chara</a></i>, where they may fix nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Cyanobacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Anabaena" title="Anabaena">Anabaena</a></i> (a symbiont of the aquatic fern <i><a href="/wiki/Azolla" title="Azolla">Azolla</a></i>), can provide rice plantations with <a href="/wiki/Biofertilizer" title="Biofertilizer">biofertilizer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azolla-Anabaena_as_a_Biofertilizer_for_Rice_Paddy_Fields_in_the_Po_Valley,_a_Temperate_Rice_Area_in_Northern_Italy_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Azolla-Anabaena_as_a_Biofertilizer_for_Rice_Paddy_Fields_in_the_Po_Valley,_a_Temperate_Rice_Area_in_Northern_Italy-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:267px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="984" data-file-width="1375" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg/265px-CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg/398px-CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg/530px-CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg 2x" width="265"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CyanobacteriaColl1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colonies of <i><a href="/wiki/Nostoc_pruniforme" title="Nostoc pruniforme">Nostoc pruniforme</a></i></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:221px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Clyndrospermum_(Cyanobacteria).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2329" data-file-width="2329" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG/219px-Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG/329px-Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG/438px-Clyndrospermum_%28Cyanobacteria%29.JPG 2x" width="219"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Clyndrospermum_(Cyanobacteria).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Cylindrospermum" title="Cylindrospermum">Cylindrospermum</a></i> sp.</div></div></div>
<p>Many cyanobacteria form motile filaments of cells, called <a href="/wiki/Hormogonium" title="Hormogonium">hormogonia</a>, that travel away from the main biomass to bud and form new colonies elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The cells in a hormogonium are often thinner than in the vegetative state, and the cells on either end of the motile chain may be tapered. To break away from the parent colony, a hormogonium often must tear apart a weaker cell in a filament, called a necridium.
</p><p>Each individual cell (each single cyanobacterium) typically has a thick, gelatinous <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> They lack <a href="/wiki/Flagellum" title="Flagellum">flagella</a>, but hormogonia of some species can move about by <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_gliding" title="Bacterial gliding">gliding</a> along surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Many of the multicellular filamentous forms of <i><a href="/wiki/Oscillatoria" title="Oscillatoria">Oscillatoria</a></i> are capable of a waving motion; the filament oscillates back and forth. In water columns, some cyanobacteria float by forming <a href="/wiki/Gas_vesicle" title="Gas vesicle">gas vesicles</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> These vesicles are not <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> as such. They are not bounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_membranes" title="Lipid membranes">lipid membranes</a>, but by a protein sheath.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5661" data-file-width="4118" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg/170px-2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg/255px-2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg/340px-2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2010_Filamentous_Cyanobacteria_Bloom_near_Fiji.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cyanobacterial bloom near <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a></div></div></div>
<p>Cyanobacteria can be found in almost every terrestrial and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_habitat" title="Aquatic habitat">aquatic habitat</a>—<a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, damp soil, temporarily moistened rocks in <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>, bare rock and soil, and even <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> rocks. They can occur as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planktonic" title="Planktonic">planktonic</a> cells or form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phototrophic_biofilms" title="Phototrophic biofilms">phototrophic biofilms</a>. They are found in almost every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endolithic_ecosystem" title="Endolithic ecosystem">endolithic ecosystem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A few are <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiont" title="Endosymbiont">endosymbionts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>, plants, various <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a> and provide energy for the <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a>. Some live in the fur of <a href="/wiki/Sloth" title="Sloth">sloths</a>, providing a form of <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aquatic cyanobacteria are known for their extensive and highly visible <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">blooms</a> that can form in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> and marine environments. The blooms can have the appearance of blue-green paint or scum. These blooms can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a>, and frequently lead to the closure of recreational waters when spotted. <a href="/wiki/Marine_bacteriophage" title="Marine bacteriophage">Marine bacteriophages</a> are significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasites" title="Parasites">parasites</a> of unicellular marine cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cyanobacteria growth is favoured in ponds and lakes where waters are calm and have less turbulent mixing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JOHNK_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-JOHNK-27">[27]</a></sup> Their life cycles are disrupted when the water naturally or artificially mixes from churning currents caused by the flowing water of streams or the churning water of fountains. For this reason blooms of cyanobacteria seldom occur in rivers unless the water is flowing slowly. Growth is also favored at higher temperatures, making increasing water temperature as a result of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> more problematic. At higher temperatures <a href="/wiki/Microcystis" title="Microcystis">Microcystis</a> species are able to outcompete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatoms" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_algae" title="Green algae">green algae</a>. This is a concern because of the production of toxins produced by Microcystis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JOHNK_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-JOHNK-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Based on environmental trends, models and observations suggest cyanobacteria will likely increase their dominance in aquatic environments. This can lead to serious consequences, particularly the contamination of sources of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>. Cyanobacteria can interfere with <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a> in various ways, primarily by plugging filters (often large beds of sand and similar media), and by producing <a href="/wiki/Cyanotoxin" title="Cyanotoxin">cyanotoxins</a> which have the potential to cause serious illness if consumed. Consequences may also lie within fisheries and waste management practices. Anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>, rising temperatures, vertical stratification and increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide" title="Atmospheric carbon dioxide">atmospheric carbon dioxide</a> are contributors to cyanobacteria increasing dominance of aquatic ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cyanobacteria have been found to play an important role in terrestrial habitats. It has been widely reported that cyanobacteria <a href="/wiki/Soil_crust" title="Soil crust">soil crusts</a> help to stabilize soil to prevent <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and retain water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> An example of a cyanobacterial species that does so is <i>Microcoleus vaginatus</i>. <i>M. vaginatus</i> stabilizes soil using a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a> sheath that binds to sand particles and absorbs water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of these organisms contribute significantly to global ecology and the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_cycle" title="Oxygen cycle">oxygen cycle</a>. The tiny marine cyanobacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Prochlorococcus" title="Prochlorococcus">Prochlorococcus</a></i> was discovered in 1986 and accounts for more than half of the photosynthesis of the open ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Many cyanobacteria even display the <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a> that were once thought to exist only in eukaryotic cells (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_circadian_rhythms" title="Bacterial circadian rhythms">bacterial circadian rhythms</a>).
</p>
<blockquote><p> "Cyanobacteria are arguably the most successful group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> on earth. They are the most genetically diverse; they occupy a broad range of habitats across all latitudes, widespread in freshwater, marine, and terrestrial ecosystems, and they are found in the most extreme niches such as hot springs, salt works, and hypersaline bays. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoautotrophic" title="Photoautotrophic">Photoautotrophic</a>, oxygen-producing cyanobacteria created the conditions in the planet's early atmosphere that directed the evolution of aerobic metabolism and eukaryotic photosynthesis. Cyanobacteria fulfill vital ecological functions in the world's oceans, being important contributors to global carbon and nitrogen budgets." – Stewart and Falconer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WalshSmith2011_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-WalshSmith2011-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photosynthesis">Photosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While contemporary cyanobacteria are linked to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_kingdom" title="Plant kingdom">plant kingdom</a> as descendants of the progenitor of the endosymbiotic <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a>, there are several features which are unique to this group.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_fixation">Carbon fixation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon fixation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cyanobacteria use the energy of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> to drive <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, a process where the energy of light is used to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> from carbon dioxide. Because they are aquatic organisms, they typically employ several strategies which are collectively known as a "carbon concentrating mechanism" to aid in the acquisition of inorganic carbon (CO<sub>2</sub> or <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a>). Among the more specific strategies is the widespread prevalence of the bacterial microcompartments known as <a href="/wiki/Carboxysome" title="Carboxysome">carboxysomes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KerfeldHeinhorst2010_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-KerfeldHeinhorst2010-33">[33]</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosahedral</a> structures are composed of hexameric shell proteins that assemble into cage-like structures that can be several hundreds of nanometers in diameter. It is believed that these structures tether the CO<sub>2</sub>-fixing enzyme, <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a>, to the interior of the shell, as well as the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a>, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic_channeling" title="Metabolic channeling">metabolic channeling</a> to enhance the local CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations and thus increase the efficiency of the RuBisCO enzyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LongBadger2007_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-LongBadger2007-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electron_transport">Electron transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Electron transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Purple_bacteria" title="Purple bacteria">purple bacteria</a> and other bacteria performing <a href="/wiki/Anoxygenic_photosynthesis" title="Anoxygenic photosynthesis">anoxygenic photosynthesis</a>, thylakoid membranes of cyanobacteria are not continuous with the plasma membrane but are separate compartments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vothknecht&amp;Westhoff20012_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vothknecht&amp;Westhoff20012-35">[35]</a></sup> The photosynthetic machinery is embedded in the <a href="/wiki/Thylakoid" title="Thylakoid">thylakoid</a> membranes, with <a href="/wiki/Phycobilisome" title="Phycobilisome">phycobilisomes</a> acting as <a href="/wiki/Light-harvesting_complex" title="Light-harvesting complex">light-harvesting antennae</a> attached to the membrane, giving the green pigmentation observed (with wavelengths from 450 nm to 660 nm) in most cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobiechowska-Sasim_2014_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sobiechowska-Sasim_2014-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="floatright"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cyanobacterium-inline.svg" title="Diagram of a typical cyanobacterial cell"><img alt="Diagram of a typical cyanobacterial cell" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="540" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cyanobacterium-inline.svg/500px-Cyanobacterium-inline.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cyanobacterium-inline.svg/750px-Cyanobacterium-inline.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cyanobacterium-inline.svg/1000px-Cyanobacterium-inline.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div>
<p>While most of the high-energy <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> derived from water are used by the cyanobacterial cells for their own needs, a fraction of these electrons may be donated to the external environment via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrogenic" title="Electrogenic">electrogenic</a> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pisciotta_JM,_Zou_Y,_Baskakov_IV_2010_e108212_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pisciotta_JM,_Zou_Y,_Baskakov_IV_2010_e108212-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiration">Respiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Respiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">Respiration</a> in cyanobacteria can occur in the thylakoid membrane alongside photosynthesis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vermaas_2001_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vermaas_2001-38">[38]</a></sup> with their photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport</a> sharing the same compartment as the components of respiratory electron transport. While the goal of photosynthesis is to store energy by building carbohydrates from CO<sub>2</sub>, respiration is the reverse of this, with carbohydrates turned back into CO<sub>2</sub> accompanying energy release.
</p><p>Cyanobacteria appear to separate these two processes with their plasma membrane containing only components of the respiratory chain, while the thylakoid membrane hosts an interlinked respiratory and photosynthetic electron transport chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vermaas_2001_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vermaas_2001-38">[38]</a></sup> Cyanobacteria use electrons from <a href="/wiki/Succinate_dehydrogenase" title="Succinate dehydrogenase">succinate dehydrogenase</a> rather than from <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADPH</a> for respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vermaas_2001_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vermaas_2001-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electron_transport_chain">Electron transport chain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electron transport chain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many cyanobacteria are able to reduce nitrogen and carbon dioxide under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_cellular_respiration" title="Aerobic cellular respiration">aerobic</a> conditions, a fact that may be responsible for their evolutionary and ecological success. The water-oxidizing photosynthesis is accomplished by coupling the activity of <a href="/wiki/Photosystem" title="Photosystem">photosystem</a> (PS) II and I (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z-scheme" title="Z-scheme">Z-scheme</a>). In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Green_sulfur_bacteria" title="Green sulfur bacteria">green sulfur bacteria</a> which only use one photosystem, the use of water as an electron donor is energetically demanding, requiring two photosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klatt_2016_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klatt_2016-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Attached to the thylakoid membrane, <a href="/wiki/Phycobilisome" title="Phycobilisome">phycobilisomes</a> act as <a href="/wiki/Light-harvesting_complex" title="Light-harvesting complex">light-harvesting antennae</a> for the photosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The phycobilisome components (<a href="/wiki/Phycobiliprotein" title="Phycobiliprotein">phycobiliproteins</a>) are responsible for the blue-green pigmentation of most cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The variations on this theme are due mainly to <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phycoerythrin" title="Phycoerythrin">phycoerythrins</a> that give the cells their red-brownish coloration. In some cyanobacteria, the color of light influences the composition of the phycobilisomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In green light, the cells accumulate more phycoerythrin, whereas in red light they produce more <a href="/wiki/Phycocyanin" title="Phycocyanin">phycocyanin</a>. Thus, the bacteria appear green in red light and red in green light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This process of complementary chromatic adaptation is a way for the cells to maximize the use of available light for photosynthesis.
</p><p>A few genera lack phycobilisomes and have <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll_b" title="Chlorophyll b">chlorophyll b</a> instead (<i><a href="/wiki/Prochloron" title="Prochloron">Prochloron</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Prochlorococcus" title="Prochlorococcus">Prochlorococcus</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prochlorothrix&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prochlorothrix (page does not exist)">Prochlorothrix</a></i>). These were originally grouped together as the <a href="/wiki/Prochlorophyta" title="Prochlorophyta">prochlorophytes</a> or chloroxybacteria, but appear to have developed in several different lines of cyanobacteria. For this reason, they are now considered as part of the cyanobacterial group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In general, photosynthesis in cyanobacteria uses water as an <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">electron donor</a> and produces <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as a byproduct, though some may also use <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_Y,_Jørgensen_BB,_Revsbech_NP,_Poplawski_R_19862_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_Y,_Jørgensen_BB,_Revsbech_NP,_Poplawski_R_19862-47">[47]</a></sup> a process which occurs among other photosynthetic bacteria such as the <a href="/wiki/Purple_sulfur_bacteria" title="Purple sulfur bacteria">purple sulfur bacteria</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> is reduced to form <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The large amounts of oxygen in the atmosphere are considered to have been first created by the activities of ancient cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Och_2012_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Och_2012-49">[49]</a></sup> They are often found as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbiont" title="Symbiont">symbionts</a> with a number of other groups of organisms such as fungi (lichens), <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pteridophyte" title="Pteridophyte">pteridophytes</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Azolla" title="Azolla">Azolla</a></i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperms</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Gunnera" title="Gunnera">Gunnera</a></i>), etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are some groups capable of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophic" title="Heterotrophic">heterotrophic</a> growth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CarrWhitton1973_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-CarrWhitton1973-51">[51]</a></sup> while others are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitic" title="Parasitic">parasitic</a>, causing diseases in invertebrates or algae (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Black_band_disease" title="Black band disease">black band disease</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_chloroplasts">Relationship to chloroplasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Relationship to chloroplasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast#Chloroplast_lineages_and_evolution" title="Chloroplast">Chloroplast § Chloroplast lineages and evolution</a></div>
<p>Primary <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> are cell <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> found in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukarya" title="Eukarya">eukaryotic</a> lineages, where they are specialized in performing the <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. They are known to have evolved from cyanobacteria through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endosymbiosis" title="Endosymbiosis">endosymbiosis</a>, i.e. after the engulfment of a cell by another. In this case, a photosynthesizing cyanobacteria that was engulfed in some ancient eukaryotic cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-55">[55]</a></sup> After some years of debate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> it is now generally accepted that the three major groups of primary endosymbiotic eukaryotes (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Viridiplantae" title="Viridiplantae">green plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhodophytes" title="Rhodophytes">red algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glaucophyte" title="Glaucophyte">glaucophytes</a>) form one large <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyletic group</a> called <a href="/wiki/Archaeplastida" title="Archaeplastida">Archaeplastida</a>, which evolved after one unique endosymbiotic event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodríguez-Ezpeleta_N_2005_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodríguez-Ezpeleta_N_2005-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ponce-Toledo_RI_2016_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ponce-Toledo_RI_2016-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphological</a> similarity between chloroplasts and cyanobacteria was first reported by German botanist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andreas_Schimper" title="Andreas Schimper">Andreas Schimper</a> in the 19th century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schimper_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schimper-61">[61]</a></sup> Chloroplasts are only found in <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_biology_of_the_cell—chloroplasts_and_photosynthesis_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_biology_of_the_cell—chloroplasts_and_photosynthesis-62">[62]</a></sup> thus paving the way for Russian biologist <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Mereschkowski" title="Konstantin Mereschkowski">Konstantin Mereschkowski</a> to suggest the symbiogenic origin of the plastid in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Margulis" title="Lynn Margulis">Lynn Margulis</a> brought this hypothesis back to attention more than 60 years later<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> but it was not until supplementary data started to accumulate that the idea became fully accepted. The cyanobacterial origin of plastids is now supported by various pieces of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodríguez-Ezpeleta_N_2005_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rodríguez-Ezpeleta_N_2005-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ponce-Toledo_RI_2016_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ponce-Toledo_RI_2016-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> biochemical<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and structural evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The fact that another independent and more recent primary endosymbiosis event has been described between a cyanobacterium and a separate eukaryote lineage (the <a href="/wiki/Rhizaria" title="Rhizaria">rhizarian</a> <i>Paulinella chromatophora</i>) also gives credibility to the endosymbiotic origin of the plastid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to this primary endosymbiosis, many eukaryotic lineages have been subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_endosymbiosis" title="Secondary endosymbiosis">secondary</a> or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_endosymbiotic_events" title="Tertiary endosymbiotic events">tertiary endosymbiotic events</a>, that is the "<a href="/wiki/Matryoshka_doll" title="Matryoshka doll">Matryoshka</a>-like" engulfment by a eukaryote of another plastid-bearing eukaryote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within this evolutionary context, it is noteworthy that, as far as we can tell, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygenic_photosynthesis" title="Oxygenic photosynthesis">oxygenic photosynthesis</a> only evolved once (in cyanobacteria), and all other photosynthetic eukaryotes (including all plants and algae) have acquired this ability from them. In other words, all the oxygen that makes the atmosphere breathable for <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic organisms</a> originally comes from cyanobacteria or their later descendants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_repair">DNA repair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: DNA repair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cyanobacteria are challenged by environmental stresses and internally generated <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen species</a> that cause <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring)" title="DNA damage (naturally occurring)">DNA damages</a>.  Cyanobacteria possess numerous <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i>-like <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">DNA repair</a> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid27881980_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid27881980-73">[73]</a></sup>  Several DNA repair genes are highly conserved in cyanobacteria, even in small <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a>, suggesting that core DNA repair processes such as <a href="/wiki/Homologous_recombination" title="Homologous recombination">recombinational repair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide_excision_repair" title="Nucleotide excision repair">nucleotide excision repair</a> and methyl-directed <a href="/wiki/DNA_mismatch_repair" title="DNA mismatch repair">DNA mismatch repair</a> are common among cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid27881980_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid27881980-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_genetic_transformation">Natural genetic transformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Natural genetic transformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cyanobacteria are capable of natural genetic <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)" title="Transformation (genetics)">transformation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4209657_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4209657-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16592896_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16592896-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>  Natural genetic transformation is the genetic alteration of a cell resulting from the direct uptake and incorporation of exogenous DNA from its surroundings. For bacterial transformation to take place, the recipient bacteria must be in a state of <a href="/wiki/Natural_competence" title="Natural competence">competence</a>, which may occur in nature as a response to conditions such as starvation, high cell density or exposure to DNA damaging agents. In chromosomal transformation, homologous transforming DNA can be integrated into the recipient genome by <a href="/wiki/Homologous_recombination" title="Homologous recombination">homologous recombination</a>, and this process appears to be an adaptation for <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">repairing DNA damage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid29111273_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid29111273-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_taxonomy" title="Bacterial taxonomy">Bacterial taxonomy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Haeckel_arbol_bn.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3307" data-file-width="2128" height="342" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haeckel_arbol_bn.png/220px-Haeckel_arbol_bn.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haeckel_arbol_bn.png/330px-Haeckel_arbol_bn.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Haeckel_arbol_bn.png/440px-Haeckel_arbol_bn.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Haeckel_arbol_bn.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tree of Life in <i>Generelle Morphologie der Organismen</i> (1866). Note the location of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Nostoc" title="Nostoc">Nostoc</a></i> with algae and not with bacteria (kingdom "Monera")</div></div></div>
<p>Historically, bacteria were first classified as plants constituting the class Schizomycetes, which along with the Schizophyceae (blue-green algae/Cyanobacteria) formed the phylum Schizophyta,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nageli_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-nageli-78">[78]</a></sup> then in the phylum <a href="/wiki/Monera" title="Monera">Monera</a> in the kingdom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protista" title="Protista">Protista</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haeckel" title="Haeckel">Haeckel</a> in 1866, comprising <i>Protogens, Protamaeba, Vampyrella, Protomonae</i>, and <i>Vibrio</i>, but not <i>Nostoc</i> and other cyanobacteria, which were classified with algae,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
later reclassified as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokaryotes" title="Prokaryotes">Prokaryotes</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Chatton" title="Édouard Chatton">Chatton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cyanobacteria were traditionally classified by morphology into five sections, referred to by the numerals I–V. The first three – <a href="/wiki/Chroococcales" title="Chroococcales">Chroococcales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pleurocapsales" title="Pleurocapsales">Pleurocapsales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oscillatoriales" title="Oscillatoriales">Oscillatoriales</a> – are not supported by phylogenetic studies. The latter two – <a href="/wiki/Nostocales" title="Nostocales">Nostocales</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stigonematales" title="Stigonematales">Stigonematales</a> – are monophyletic, and make up the heterocystous cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gugger_2004_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gugger_2004-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>The members of Chroococales are unicellular and usually aggregate in colonies. The classic taxonomic criterion has been the cell morphology and the plane of cell division. In Pleurocapsales, the cells have the ability to form internal spores (baeocytes). The rest of the sections include filamentous species. In Oscillatoriales, the cells are uniseriately arranged and do not form specialized cells (akinetes and heterocysts).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In Nostocales and Stigonematales, the cells have the ability to develop heterocysts in certain conditions. Stigonematales, unlike Nostocales, include species with truly branched trichomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gugger_2004_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gugger_2004-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most taxa included in the phylum or division Cyanobacteria have not yet been validly published<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_of_Bacteria" title="International Code of Nomenclature of Bacteria">Bacteriological Code</a>, except:
</p>
<ul><li>The classes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chroobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chroobacteria (page does not exist)">Chroobacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormogoneae" title="Hormogoneae">Hormogoneae</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloeobacteria" title="Gloeobacteria">Gloeobacteria</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_history">Earth history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Earth history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stromatolites" title="Stromatolites">Stromatolites</a> are layered biochemical <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(geology)" title="Accretion (geology)">accretionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structures</a> formed in shallow water by the trapping, binding, and cementation of sedimentary grains by <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mats</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, especially cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stromatolites.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="550" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/150px-Stromatolites.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/225px-Stromatolites.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Stromatolites.jpg/300px-Stromatolites.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stromatolites.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stromatolites" title="Stromatolites">Stromatolites</a> left behind by cyanobacteria are the oldest known fossils of life on Earth. This one-billion-year-old fossil is from <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a> in Montana.</div></div></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a>, stromatolite communities of microorganisms grew in most marine and non-marine environments in the photic zone. After the Cambrian explosion of marine animals, grazing on the stromatolite mats by herbivores greatly reduced the occurrence of the stromatolites in marine environments. Since then, they are found mostly in hypersaline conditions where grazing invertebrates cannot live (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Shark_Bay" title="Shark Bay">Shark Bay</a>, Western Australia). Stromatolites provide ancient records of life on Earth by fossil remains which might date from more than 3.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annus#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Annus">Ga</a> ago, but this is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PiP_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-PiP-86">[86]</a></sup> As of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the oldest undisputed evidence of cyanobacteria is from 2.1 Ga ago, but there is some evidence for them as far back as 2.7 Ga ago. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> concentrations in the atmosphere remained around or below 1% of today's level until 2.4 Ga ago (the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a>). The rise in oxygen may have caused a fall in the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a>, and triggered the <a href="/wiki/Huronian_glaciation" title="Huronian glaciation">Huronian glaciation</a> from around 2.4 to 2.1 Ga ago. In this way, cyanobacteria may have killed off much of the other bacteria of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lane-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oncolite" title="Oncolite">Oncolites</a> are <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_structures" title="Sedimentary structures">sedimentary structures</a> composed of oncoids, which are layered structures formed by cyanobacterial growth. Oncolites are similar to stromatolites, but instead of forming columns, they form approximately spherical structures that were not attached to the underlying substrate as they formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corsetti2003_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corsetti2003-88">[88]</a></sup> The oncoids often form around a central nucleus, such as a shell fragment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> structure is deposited by encrusting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbes</a>. Oncolites are indicators of warm waters in the <a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">photic zone</a>, but are also known in contemporary freshwater environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> These structures rarely exceed 10 cm in diameter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotechnology_and_applications">Biotechnology and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biotechnology and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The unicellular cyanobacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Synechocystis" title="Synechocystis">Synechocystis</a></i> sp. PCC6803 was the third prokaryote and first photosynthetic organism whose <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> was completely <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">sequenced</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> It continues to be an important model organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Cyanothece" title="Cyanothece">Cyanothece</a></i> ATCC 51142 is an important <a href="/wiki/Diazotroph" title="Diazotroph">diazotrophic</a> model organism. The smallest genomes have been found in <i><a href="/wiki/Prochlorococcus" title="Prochlorococcus">Prochlorococcus</a></i> spp. (1.7 Mb)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> and the largest in <i><a href="/wiki/Nostoc_punctiforme" title="Nostoc punctiforme">Nostoc punctiforme</a></i> (9 Mb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Those of <i><a href="/wiki/Calothrix" title="Calothrix">Calothrix</a></i> spp. are estimated at 12–15 Mb,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> as large as <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg/150px-OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg/225px-OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg/300px-OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OncolitesAlamoBreccia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Oncolite" title="Oncolite">Oncolites</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Devonian" title="Late Devonian">Late Devonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Alamo_bolide_impact" title="Alamo bolide impact">Alamo bolide impact</a> in Nevada</div></div></div>
<p>Recent research has suggested the potential application of cyanobacteria to the generation of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> by converting sunlight into electricity. Internal photosynthetic pathways can be coupled to chemical mediators that transfer electrons to external electrodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> Currently, efforts are underway to commercialize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algae-based_fuels" title="Algae-based fuels">algae-based fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pisciotta_JM,_Zou_Y,_Baskakov_IV_2010_e108212_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pisciotta_JM,_Zou_Y,_Baskakov_IV_2010_e108212-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1299" data-file-width="2000" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg/150px-Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg/225px-Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg/300px-Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue-green_algae_cultured_in_specific_media.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cyanobacteria cultured in specific media: Cyanobacteria can be helpful in agriculture as they have the ability to fix atmospheric nitrogen in soil.</div></div></div>
<p>Researchers from a company called <a href="/wiki/Algenol" title="Algenol">Algenol</a> have cultured genetically modified cyanobacteria in sea water inside a clear plastic enclosure so they first make sugar (pyruvate) from CO<sub>2</sub> and the water via photosynthesis. Then, the bacteria secrete ethanol from the cell into the salt water. As the day progresses, and the solar radiation intensifies, ethanol concentrations build up and the ethanol itself evaporates onto the roof of the enclosure. As the sun recedes, evaporated ethanol and water condense into droplets, which run along the plastic walls and into ethanol collectors, from where it is extracted from the enclosure with the water and ethanol separated outside the enclosure. As of March 2013, Algenol was claiming to have tested its technology in Florida and to have achieved yields of 9,000 US gallons per acre per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> This could potentially meet US demands for ethanol in gasoline in 2025, assuming a B30 blend, from an area of around half the size of California’s San Bernardino County, requiring less than one-tenth of the area than ethanol from other biomass, such as corn, and only very limited amounts of fresh water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cyanobacteria may possess the ability to produce substances that could one day serve as anti-inflammatory agents and combat bacterial infections in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spirulina's extracted blue color is used as a natural food coloring in gum and candy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers from several space agencies argue that cyanobacteria could be used for producing goods for human consumption in future manned outposts on Mars, by transforming materials available on this planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verseux2015_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verseux2015-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_risks">Health risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Health risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyanotoxin" title="Cyanotoxin">Cyanotoxin</a></div>
<p>Cyanobacteria can produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurotoxins" title="Neurotoxins">neurotoxins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytotoxins" title="Cytotoxins">cytotoxins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endotoxins" title="Endotoxins">endotoxins</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatotoxins" title="Hepatotoxins">hepatotoxins</a> (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Microcystin" title="Microcystin">microcystin</a>-producing bacteria genus <a href="/wiki/Microcystis" title="Microcystis">microcystis</a>), which are collectively known as <a href="/wiki/Cyanotoxin" title="Cyanotoxin">cyanotoxins</a>.
</p><p>Specific toxins include, <a href="/wiki/Anatoxin-a" title="Anatoxin-a">anatoxin-a</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatoxin-as" title="Anatoxin-as">anatoxin-as</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aplysiatoxin" title="Aplysiatoxin">aplysiatoxin</a>, cyanopeptolin, <a href="/wiki/Cylindrospermopsin" title="Cylindrospermopsin">cylindrospermopsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domoic_acid" title="Domoic acid">domoic acid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodularin_R" title="Nodularin R">nodularin R</a> (from <i><a href="/wiki/Nodularia" title="Nodularia">Nodularia</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Neosaxitoxin" title="Neosaxitoxin">neosaxitoxin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saxitoxin" title="Saxitoxin">saxitoxin</a>. Cyanobacteria reproduce explosively under certain conditions. This results in <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal blooms</a>, which can become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harmful_algal_blooms" title="Harmful algal blooms">harmful to other species</a>, and pose a danger to humans and animals, if the cyanobacteria involved produce toxins. Several cases of human poisoning have been documented, but a lack of knowledge prevents an accurate assessment of the risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc-sc1_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-hc-sc1-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent studies suggest that significant exposure to high levels of cyanobacteria producing toxins such as <a href="/wiki/Beta-Methylamino-L-alanine" title="Beta-Methylamino-L-alanine">BMAA</a> can cause <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a> (ALS).
People living within half a mile of cyanobacterially contaminated lakes have had a 2.3 times greater risk of developing ALS than the rest of the population; people around New Hampshire's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Mascoma" title="Lake Mascoma">Lake Mascoma</a> had an up to 25 times greater risk of ALS than the expected incidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
BMAA from desert crusts found throughout Qatar might have contributed to higher rates of ALS in Gulf War veterans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc-sc1_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-hc-sc1-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_control">Chemical control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chemical control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several chemicals can eliminate cyanobacterial blooms from water-based systems. They include: <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hypochlorite" title="Calcium hypochlorite">calcium hypochlorite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate" title="Copper(II) sulfate">copper sulphate</a>, cupricide, and <a href="/wiki/Simazine" title="Simazine">simazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup> The calcium hypochlorite amount needed varies depending on the cyanobacteria bloom, and treatment is needed periodically. According to the Department of Agriculture Australia, a rate of 12 g of 70% material in 1000 l of water is often effective to treat a bloom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup> Copper sulfate is also used commonly, but no longer recommended by the Australian Department of Agriculture, as it kills livestock, crustaceans, and fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-2"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup> Culpricide is a chelated copper product that eliminates blooms with lower toxicity risks than copper sulfate. Dosage recommendations vary from 190 ml to 4.8 l per 1000 m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-3"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup> Ferric alum treatments at the rate of 50 mg/l will reduce algae blooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-4"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Simazine, which is also a herbicide, will continue to kill blooms for several days after an application. Simazine is marketed at different strengths (25, 50, and 90%), the recommended amount needed for one cubic meter of water per product is 25% product 8 ml; 50% product 4 ml; or 90% product 2.2 ml.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluegreenalgae2006_110-5"><a href="#cite_note-bluegreenalgae2006-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dietary_supplementation">Dietary supplementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyanobacteria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Dietary supplementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spirulina_tablets.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1194" data-file-width="1449" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Spirulina_tablets.jpg/150px-Spirulina_tablets.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Spirulina_tablets.jpg/225px-Spirulina_tablets.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Spirulina_tablets.jpg/300px-Spirulina_tablets.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spirulina_tablets.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Spirulina_(dietary_supplement)" title="Spirulina (dietary supplement)">Spirulina</a> tablets</div></div></div>
<p>Some cyanobacteria are sold as food, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Aphanizomenon_flos-aquae" title="Aphanizomenon flos-aquae">Aphanizomenon flos-aquae</a></i> and <i>Arthrospira platensis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Spirulina_(dietary_supplement)" title="Spirulina (dietary supplement)">Spirulina</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the associated toxins which many of the members of this phylum produce, some microalgae also contain substances of high biological value, such as polyunsaturated fatty acids, amino acids, proteins, pigments, antioxidants, vitamins, and minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Edible blue-green algae reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines by inhibiting NF-κB pathway in macrophages and splenocytes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Sulfate polysaccharides exhibit immunomodulatory, antitumor, antithrombotic, anticoagulant, anti-mutagenic, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and even antiviral activity against HIV, herpes, and hepatitis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p>
