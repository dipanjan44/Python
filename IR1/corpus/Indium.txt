<p><b>Indium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>In</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 49. It is a <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metal</a> that makes up 0.21 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> of the Earth's crust. Very soft and malleable, indium has a melting point higher than <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, but lower than <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>. Chemically, indium is similar to gallium and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>, and it is largely intermediate between the two in terms of its properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ph.D.Lide2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ph.D.Lide2010-6">[6]</a></sup> Indium was discovered in 1863 by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Reich" title="Ferdinand Reich">Ferdinand Reich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hieronymous_Theodor_Richter" title="Hieronymous Theodor Richter">Hieronymous Theodor Richter</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscopic methods</a>. They named it for the indigo blue line in its spectrum. Indium was isolated the next year.
</p><p>Indium is a minor component in <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a> ores and is produced as a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> refinement. It is most notably used in the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_industry" title="Semiconductor industry">semiconductor industry</a>, in low-melting-point metal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">alloys</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Solder#Alloying_element_roles" title="Solder">solders</a>, in soft-metal high-vacuum seals, and in the production of transparent conductive coatings of <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> (ITO) on glass.
</p><p>Indium has no biological role, though its compounds are somewhat toxic when injected into the bloodstream. Most occupational exposure is through ingestion, from which indium compounds are not absorbed well, and inhalation, from which they are moderately absorbed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indium_wetting_glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1013" data-file-width="1372" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Indium_wetting_glass.jpg/220px-Indium_wetting_glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Indium_wetting_glass.jpg/330px-Indium_wetting_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Indium_wetting_glass.jpg/440px-Indium_wetting_glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indium_wetting_glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Indium wetting the glass surface of a test tube</div></div></div>
<p>Indium is a silvery-<a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>, highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metal</a> with a bright <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">luster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InProcess_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-InProcess-7">[7]</a></sup> It is so soft (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> 1.2) that like sodium, it can be cut with a knife. It also leaves a visible line on paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binder_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Binder-8">[8]</a></sup> It is a member of <a href="/wiki/Boron_group" title="Boron group">group 13</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> and its properties are mostly intermediate between its vertical neighbours <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>. Like <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, a high-pitched <a href="/wiki/Tin_cry" title="Tin cry">cry</a> is heard when indium is bent – a crackling sound due to <a href="/wiki/Crystal_twinning" title="Crystal twinning">crystal twinning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InProcess_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-InProcess-7">[7]</a></sup> Like <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, indium is able to <a href="/wiki/Wetting" title="Wetting">wet</a> glass. Like both, indium has a low <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>, 156.60 °C (313.88 °F); higher than its lighter homologue, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, but lower than its heavier homologue, <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>, and lower than <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lange-9">[9]</a></sup> The boiling point is 2072 °C (3762 °F), higher than that of thallium, but lower than gallium, conversely to the general trend of melting points, but similarly to the trends down the other post-transition metal groups because of the weakness of the metallic bonding with few electrons delocalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The density of indium, 7.31 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, is also greater than gallium, but lower than thallium. Below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature" title="Critical temperature">critical temperature</a>, 3.41 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>, indium becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductor</a>. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, indium crystallizes in the face-centered <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal crystal system</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> <i>I</i>4/<i>mmm</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_parameter" title="Lattice parameter">lattice parameters</a>: <i>a</i> = 325 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picometer" title="Picometer">pm</a>, <i>c</i> = 495 pm):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lange-9">[9]</a></sup> this is a slightly distorted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centered_cubic" title="Face-centered cubic">face-centered cubic</a> structure, where each indium atom has four neighbours at 324 pm distance and eight neighbours slightly further (336 pm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood252_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood252-11">[11]</a></sup> Indium displays a ductile viscoplastic response, found to be size-independent in tension and compression. However it does have a <a href="/wiki/Size_effect_on_structural_strength" title="Size effect on structural strength">size effect</a> in bending and indentation, associated to a length-scale of order 50–100 µm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> significantly large when compared with other metals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Indium has 49 electrons, with an electronic configuration of [<a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">Kr</a>]4d<sup>10</sup>5s<sup>2</sup>5p<sup>1</sup>. In compounds, indium most commonly donates the three outermost electrons to become indium(III), In<sup>3+</sup>.  In some cases, the pair of 5s-electrons are not donated, resulting in indium(I), In<sup>+</sup>. The stabilization of the <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">monovalent</a> state is attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Inert_pair_effect" title="Inert pair effect">inert pair effect</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry" title="Relativistic quantum chemistry">relativistic effects</a> stabilize the 5s-orbital, observed in heavier elements.  Thallium (indium's heavier <a href="/wiki/Homology_(chemistry)" title="Homology (chemistry)">homolog</a>) shows an even stronger effect, causing <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> to thallium(I) to be more probable than to thallium(III),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> whereas gallium (indium's lighter homolog) commonly shows only the +3 oxidation state. Thus, although thallium(III) is a moderately strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>, indium(III) is not, and many indium(I) compounds are powerful <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-14">[14]</a></sup> While the energy required to include the s-electrons in chemical bonding is lowest for indium among the group 13 metals, bond energies decrease down the group so that by indium, the energy released in forming two additional bonds and attaining the +3 state is not always enough to outweigh the energy needed to involve the 5s-electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood256_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood256-15">[15]</a></sup> Indium(I) oxide and hydroxide are more basic and indium(III) oxide and hydroxide are more acidic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood256_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood256-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of standard electrode potentials, depending on the reaction under study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> are reported for indium, reflecting the decreased stability of the +3 oxidation state:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood252_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood252-11">[11]</a></sup>

<p>Indium metal does not react with water, but it is oxidized by stronger oxidizing agents such as <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> to give indium(III) compounds. It does not form a <a href="/wiki/Boride" title="Boride">boride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicide" title="Silicide">silicide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbide</a>, and the hydride <a href="/wiki/Indium_trihydride" title="Indium trihydride">InH<sub>3</sub></a> has at best a transitory existence in <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethereal</a> solutions at low temperatures, being unstable enough to spontaneously polymerize without coordination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-14">[14]</a></sup> Indium is rather basic in aqueous solution, showing only slight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> characteristics, and unlike its lighter homologs aluminium and gallium, it is insoluble in aqueous alkaline solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood255_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood255-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_indium" title="Isotopes of indium">Isotopes of indium</a></div>
<p>Indium has 39 known isotopes, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> from 97 to 135. Only two isotopes occur naturally as <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a>: indium-113, the only stable isotope, and indium-115, which has a half-life of 4.41<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">14</span></sup> years, four orders of magnitude greater than the age of the universe and nearly 30,000 times greater than that of <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_thorium" title="Isotopes of thorium">natural thorium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-18">[18]</a></sup> The half-life of <sup>115</sup>In is very long because the beta decay to <sup>115</sup><a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">Sn</a> is <a href="/wiki/Selection_rule" title="Selection rule">spin-forbidden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Indium-115 makes up 95.7% of all indium.  Indium is one of three known elements (the others being <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>) of which the stable isotope is less abundant in nature than the long-lived primordial radioisotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stablest <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">artificial</a> isotope is indium-111, with a half-life of approximately 2.8 days. All other isotopes have half-lives shorter than 5 hours. Indium also has 47 meta states, among which indium-114m1 (half-life about 49.51 days) is the most stable, more stable than the ground state of any indium isotope other than the primordial.  All decay by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomeric_transition" title="Isomeric transition">isomeric transition</a>. The indium isotopes lighter than <sup>115</sup>In predominantly decay through <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> isotopes, while the other indium isotopes from <sup>115</sup>In and greater predominantly decay through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-minus_decay" title="Beta-minus decay">beta-minus decay</a> to form tin isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Indium_compounds" title="Category:Indium compounds">Category:Indium compounds</a>.</div>
<h3><span id="Indium.28III.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indium(III)">Indium(III)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indium(III)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kristallstruktur_Chrom(III)-chlorid.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="1040" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png/220px-Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png/330px-Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png/440px-Kristallstruktur_Chrom%28III%29-chlorid.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kristallstruktur_Chrom(III)-chlorid.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indium_trichloride" title="Indium trichloride">InCl<sub>3</sub></a> <i>(structure pictured)</i> is a common compound of indium.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indium(III)_oxide" title="Indium(III) oxide">Indium(III) oxide</a>, In<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, forms when indium metal is burned in air or when the hydroxide or nitrate is heated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-downs-21">[21]</a></sup>  In<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> adopts a structure like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> and is amphoteric, that is able to react with both acids and bases. Indium reacts with water to reproduce soluble <a href="/wiki/Indium(III)_hydroxide" title="Indium(III) hydroxide">indium(III) hydroxide</a>, which is also amphoteric; with alkalis to produce indates(III); and with acids to produce indium(III) salts:
</p>
<dl><dd>In(OH)<sub>3</sub>  +  3 HCl  →  InCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The analogous sesquichalcogenides with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> are also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood286_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood286-22">[22]</a></sup> Indium forms the expected <a href="/wiki/Indium_halides" title="Indium halides">trihalides</a>. Chlorination, bromination, and iodination of In produce colorless <a href="/wiki/Indium(III)_chloride" title="Indium(III) chloride">InCl<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Indium(III)_bromide" title="Indium(III) bromide">InBr<sub>3</sub></a>, and yellow InI<sub>3</sub>. The compounds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acids</a>, somewhat akin to the better known aluminium trihalides.  Again like the related aluminium compound, InF<sub>3</sub> is polymeric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood263_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood263-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Direct reaction of indium with the <a href="/wiki/Pnictogen" title="Pnictogen">pnictogens</a> produces the gray or semimetallic III–V <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>. Many of them slowly decompose in moist air, necessitating careful storage of semiconductor compounds to prevent contact with the atmosphere. Indium nitride is readily attacked by acids and alkalis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood288_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood288-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Indium.28I.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indium(I)">Indium(I)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Indium(I)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Indium(I) compounds are not common. The chloride, <a href="/wiki/Indium(I)_bromide" title="Indium(I) bromide">bromide</a>, and iodide are deeply colored, unlike the parent trihalides from which they are prepared. The fluoride is known only as an unstable gaseous compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood270_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood270-25">[25]</a></sup> Indium(I) oxide black powder is produced when indium(III) oxide decomposes upon heating to 700 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-downs-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_oxidation_states">Other oxidation states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other oxidation states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Less frequently, indium forms compounds in oxidation state +2 and even fractional oxidation states.  Usually such materials feature In–In bonding, most notably in the <a href="/wiki/Indium_halides" title="Indium halides">halides</a> In<sub>2</sub>X<sub>4</sub> and [In<sub>2</sub>X<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can82_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-can82-26">[26]</a></sup> and various subchalcogenides such as In<sub>4</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood287_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood287-27">[27]</a></sup> Several other compounds are known to combine indium(I) and indium(III), such as In<sup>I</sup><sub>6</sub>(In<sup>III</sup>Cl<sub>6</sub>)Cl<sub>3</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In<sup>I</sup><sub>5</sub>(In<sup>III</sup>Br<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(In<sup>III</sup>Br<sub>6</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In<sup>I</sup>In<sup>III</sup>Br<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-can82_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-can82-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organoindium_compounds">Organoindium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organoindium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Organoindium compounds feature In–C bonds.  Most are In(III) derivatives, but <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienylindium(I)" title="Cyclopentadienylindium(I)">cyclopentadienylindium(I)</a> is an exception. It was the first known organoindium(I) compound,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and is polymeric, consisting of zigzag chains of alternating indium atoms and <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">cyclopentadienyl complexes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Perhaps the best-known organoindium compound is <a href="/wiki/Trimethylindium" title="Trimethylindium">trimethylindium</a>, In(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, used to prepare certain semiconducting materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1863, the German chemists <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Reich" title="Ferdinand Reich">Ferdinand Reich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hieronymous_Theodor_Richter" title="Hieronymous Theodor Richter">Hieronymous Theodor Richter</a> were testing ores from the mines around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freiberg,_Saxony" title="Freiberg, Saxony">Freiberg, Saxony</a>. They dissolved the minerals <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">arsenopyrite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and distilled raw <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a>. Reich, who was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color-blind" title="Color-blind">color-blind</a>, employed Richter as an assistant for detecting the colored spectral lines. Knowing that ores from that region sometimes contain <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>, they searched for the green thallium emission spectrum lines. Instead, they found a bright blue line. Because that blue line did not match any known element, they hypothesized a new element was present in the minerals. They named the element indium, from the <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> color seen in its spectrum, after the Latin <i>indicum</i>, meaning 'of India'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venetskii_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venetskii-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood244_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood244-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Richter went on to isolate the metal in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  An ingot of 0.5 kg (1.1 lb) was presented at the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1867)" title="Exposition Universelle (1867)">World Fair</a> 1867.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchSch_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchSch-39">[39]</a></sup>  Reich and Richter later fell out when the latter claimed to be the sole discoverer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg"><img alt="yellow squares with red and blue arrows" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="740" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg/400px-S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg/600px-S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg/800px-S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S-process-elem-Ag-to-Sb.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The s-process acting in the range from <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> to <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a></div></div></div>
<p>Indium is created by the long-lasting (up to thousands of years) <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a> (slow neutron capture) in low-to-medium-mass stars (which range in mass between 0.6 and 10 <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>). When a silver-109 atom (the isotope that comprises approximately half of all silver in existence) catches a neutron, it undergoes a <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> to become cadmium-110. Capturing further neutrons, it becomes cadmium-115, which decays to indium-115 by another <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. This explains why the radioactive isotope is more abundant than the stable one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The stable indium isotope, indium-113, is one of the <a href="/wiki/P-nuclei" title="P-nuclei">p-nuclei</a>, the origin of which is not fully understood; although indium-113 is known to be made directly in the s- and <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-processes</a> (rapid neutron capture), and also as the daughter of very long-lived cadmium-113, which has a half-life of about eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrillion" title="Quadrillion">quadrillion</a> years, this cannot account for all indium-113.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s-contrib_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-s-contrib-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r-contrib_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-r-contrib-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indium is the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">68th most abundant element in Earth's crust</a> at approximately 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a>. This is similar to the crustal abundance of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>. It very rarely forms its own minerals, or occurs in elemental form. Fewer than 10 indium minerals such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roquesite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roquesite (page does not exist)">roquesite</a> (CuInS<sub>2</sub>) are known, and none occur at sufficient concentrations for economic extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43">[43]</a></sup> Instead, indium is usually a trace constituent of more common ore minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalcopyrite" title="Chalcopyrite">chalcopyrite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> From these, it can be extracted as a <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> during smelting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup> While the enrichment of indium in these deposits is high relative to its crustal abundance, it is insufficient, at current prices, to support extraction of indium as the main product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Different estimates exist of the amounts of indium contained within the ores of other metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2007_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2007-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> However, these amounts are not extractable without mining of the host materials (see Production and availability). Thus, the availability of indium is fundamentally determined by the <i>rate</i> at which these ores are extracted, and not their absolute amount. This is an aspect that is often forgotten in the current debate, e.g. by the Graedel group at Yale in their criticality assessments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> explaining the paradoxically low depletion times some studies cite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_availability">Production and availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Production and availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indium_world_production.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="750" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Indium_world_production.svg/220px-Indium_world_production.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Indium_world_production.svg/330px-Indium_world_production.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Indium_world_production.svg/440px-Indium_world_production.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indium_world_production.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World production trend<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Indium is produced exclusively as a <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> during the processing of the ores of other metals. Its main source material are sulfidic zinc ores, where it is mostly hosted by sphalerite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup> Minor amounts are probably also extracted from sulfidic copper ores. During the <a href="/wiki/Zinc_smelting" title="Zinc smelting">roast-leach-electrowinning process of zinc smelting</a>, indium accumulates in the iron-rich residues. From these, it can be extracted in different ways. It may also be recovered directly from the process solutions. Further purification is done by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood247_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood247-52">[52]</a></sup> The exact process varies with the mode of operation of the smelter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InProcess_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-InProcess-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Its by-product status means that indium production is constrained by the amount of sulfidic zinc (and copper) ores extracted each year. Therefore, its availability needs to be discussed in terms of supply potential. The supply potential of a by-product is defined as that amount which is economically extractable from its host materials <i>per year</i> under current market conditions (i.e. technology and price).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Reserves and resources are not relevant for by-products, since they <i>cannot</i> be extracted independently from the main-products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup> Recent estimates put the supply potential of indium at a minimum of 1,300 t/yr from sulfidic zinc ores and 20 t/yr from sulfidic copper ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_46-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46">[46]</a></sup> These figures are significantly greater than current production (655 t in 2016).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-54">[54]</a></sup> Thus, major future increases in the by-product production of indium will be possible without significant increases in production costs or price. The average indium price in 2016 was <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span>240/kg, down from <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span>705/kg in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>China is a leading producer of indium (290 tonnes in 2016), followed by South Korea (195 t), Japan (70 t) and Canada (65 t).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-54">[54]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Teck_Resources" title="Teck Resources">Teck Resources</a> refinery in <a href="/wiki/Trail,_British_Columbia" title="Trail, British Columbia">Trail, British Columbia</a>, is a large single-source indium producer, with an output of 32.5 tonnes in 2005, 41.8 tonnes in 2004 and 36.1 tonnes in 2003.
</p><p>The primary consumption of indium worldwide is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">LCD</a> production. Demand rose rapidly from the late 1990s to 2010 with the popularity of LCD computer monitors and television sets, which now account for 50% of indium consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Increased manufacturing efficiency and recycling (especially in Japan) maintain a balance between demand and supply. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a>, indium's end-of-life recycling rate is less than 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2011_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2011-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3010" data-file-width="1993" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg/220px-Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg/330px-Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg/440px-Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dell_axim_LCD_under_microscope.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A magnified image of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TFT_LCD" title="TFT LCD">LCD</a> screen showing RGB pixels. Individual transistors are seen as white dots in the bottom part.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1924, indium was found to have a valued property of stabilizing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-ferrous_metals" title="Non-ferrous metals">non-ferrous metals</a>, and that became the first significant use for the element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dd_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-dd-58">[58]</a></sup> The first large-scale application for indium was coating <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearings</a> in high-performance <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> engines during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, to protect against damage and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>; this is no longer a major use of the element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood247_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood247-52">[52]</a></sup>  New uses were found in <a href="/wiki/Fusible_alloy" title="Fusible alloy">fusible alloys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">solders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>. In the 1950s, tiny beads of indium were used for the emitters and collectors of PNP <a href="/wiki/Alloy-junction_transistor" title="Alloy-junction transistor">alloy-junction transistors</a>. In the middle and late 1980s, the development of indium phosphide <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> thin films for <a href="/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display" title="Liquid-crystal display">liquid-crystal displays</a> (LCD) aroused much interest. By 1992, the thin-film application had become the largest end use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSYB2007_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSYB2007-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downs_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Downs-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indium(III) oxide and <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> (ITO) are used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">transparent</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">conductive</a> coating on <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> substrates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electroluminescent" title="Electroluminescent">electroluminescent</a> panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Indium tin oxide is used as a light filter in <a href="/wiki/Sodium-vapor_lamp#Low-pressure_sodium" title="Sodium-vapor lamp">low-pressure sodium-vapor lamps</a>. The infrared radiation is reflected back into the lamp, which increases the temperature within the tube and improves the performance of the lamp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downs_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Downs-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indium has many <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>-related applications. Some indium compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Indium_antimonide" title="Indium antimonide">indium antimonide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indium_phosphide" title="Indium phosphide">indium phosphide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> are <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> with useful properties: one precursor is usually <a href="/wiki/Trimethylindium" title="Trimethylindium">trimethylindium</a> (TMI), which is also used as the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in II–VI <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_semiconductor" title="Compound semiconductor">compound semiconductors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> InAs and InSb are used for low-temperature transistors and InP for high-temperature transistors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood247_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood247-52">[52]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_semiconductor" title="Compound semiconductor">compound semiconductors</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGaN" title="InGaN">InGaN</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGaP" title="InGaP">InGaP</a> are used in <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs) and laser diodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Indium is used in <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> as the semiconductor <a href="/wiki/Copper_indium_gallium_selenide" title="Copper indium gallium selenide">copper indium gallium selenide</a> (CIGS), also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIGS_solar_cell" title="CIGS solar cell">CIGS solar cells</a>, a type of second-generation <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_solar_cell" title="Thin-film solar cell">thin-film solar cell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Indium is used in PNP <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_junction_transistor" title="Bipolar junction transistor">bipolar junction transistors</a> with <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>: when soldered at low temperature, indium does not stress the germanium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood247_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood247-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indium_wire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Indium_wire.jpg/220px-Indium_wire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Indium_wire.jpg/330px-Indium_wire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Indium_wire.jpg/440px-Indium_wire.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indium_wire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ductile indium wire</div></div></div>
<p>Indium wire is used as a <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_seal" title="Cryogenic seal">vacuum seal</a> and a thermal conductor in <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high_vacuum" title="Ultra-high vacuum">ultra-high-vacuum</a> applications, in such manufacturing applications as <a href="/wiki/Gasket" title="Gasket">gaskets</a> that deform to fill gaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Indium is an ingredient in the gallium–indium–tin alloy <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">galinstan</a>, which is liquid at room temperature and replaces <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> in some <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Other alloys of indium with <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, which have higher but still low melting points (between 50 and 100 °C), are used in <a href="/wiki/Fire_sprinkler_system" title="Fire sprinkler system">fire sprinkler systems</a> and heat regulators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood247_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood247-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indium is one of many substitutes for mercury in alkaline batteries to prevent the zinc from corroding and releasing hydrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Indium is added to some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_amalgam" title="Dental amalgam">dental amalgam</a> alloys to decrease the surface tension of the mercury and allow for less mercury and easier amalgamation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indium's high neutron-capture cross-section for thermal neutrons makes it suitable for use in <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> for nuclear reactors, typically in an alloy of 80% silver, 15% indium, and 5% cadmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In nuclear engineering, the (n,n') reactions of <sup>113</sup>In and <sup>115</sup>In are used to determine magnitudes of neutron fluxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Indium has no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary_element" title="Dietary element">metabolic</a> role in any organism. In a similar way to aluminium salts, indium(III) ions can be toxic to the kidney when given by injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toxic_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-toxic-73">[73]</a></sup> Indium tin oxide and indium phosphide harm the pulmonary and immune systems, predominantly through ionic indium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> though hydrated indium oxide is more than forty times as toxic when injected, measured by the quantity of indium introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toxic_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-toxic-73">[73]</a></sup> Radioactive indium-111 (in very small amounts on a chemical basis) is used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> tests, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiotracer" title="Radiotracer">radiotracer</a> to follow the movement of labeled proteins and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indium_leukocyte_imaging" title="Indium leukocyte imaging">white blood cells</a> in the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indium-111_Radiochemical_Indium_Chloride_Solution_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indium-111_Radiochemical_Indium_Chloride_Solution-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Indium compounds are mostly not absorbed upon ingestion and are only moderately absorbed on inhalation; they tend to be stored temporarily in the <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a> before being excreted, and the <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> of indium is about two weeks in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to indium in the workplace by inhalation, ingestion, skin contact, and eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an eight-hour workday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
