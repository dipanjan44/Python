<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change feedback</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">Runaway climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">Abrupt climate change</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_Dangerous_Climate_Change" title="Avoiding Dangerous Climate Change">Avoiding Dangerous Climate Change</a></div>

(2008) Global warming will increase permafrost and peatland thaw, which can result in collapse of plateau surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Climate change feedback</b> is important in the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> because feedback processes may amplify or diminish the effect of each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a>, and so play an important part in determining the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a> and future <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a>. <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">Feedback</a> in general is the process in which changing one quantity changes a second quantity, and the change in the second quantity in turn changes the first. <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">Positive feedback</a> amplifies the change in the first quantity while <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> reduces it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "forcing" means a change which may "push" the climate system in the direction of warming or cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> An example of a climate forcing is increased atmospheric concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. By definition, forcings are external to the climate system while feedbacks are internal; in essence, feedbacks represent the internal processes of the system. Some feedbacks may act in relative isolation to the rest of the climate system; others may be tightly coupled; hence it may be difficult to tell just how much a particular process contributes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Forcings, feedbacks and the dynamics of the climate system determine how much and how fast the climate changes. The main positive feedback in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is the tendency of warming to increase the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere, which in turn leads to further warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The main negative feedback comes from the <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">Stefan–Boltzmann law</a>, the amount of heat radiated from the Earth into space changes with the fourth power of the temperature of Earth's surface and atmosphere.
</p><p>Some observed and potential <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> are positive feedbacks, which contribute directly to further global warming. The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>'s (IPCC) <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report</a> states that "Anthropogenic warming could lead to some effects that are abrupt or irreversible, depending upon the rate and magnitude of the climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive">Positive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Positive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon_feedback" title="Soil carbon feedback">Soil carbon feedback</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_cycle_feedbacks">Carbon cycle feedbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon cycle feedbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">Carbon cycle</a></div>
<p>There have been predictions, and some evidence, that global warming might cause loss of carbon from terrestrial ecosystems, leading to an increase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_CO2" title="Atmospheric CO2">atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub></a> levels. Several climate models indicate that global warming through the 21st century could be accelerated by the response of the terrestrial carbon cycle to such warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> All 11 models in the <a href="/wiki/C4MIP" title="C4MIP">C4MIP</a> study found that a larger fraction of anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> will stay airborne if climate change is accounted for. By the end of the twenty-first century, this additional CO<sub>2</sub> varied between 20 and 200 ppm for the two extreme models, the majority of the models lying between 50 and 100 ppm. The higher CO<sub>2</sub> levels led to an additional climate warming ranging between 0.1° and 1.5 °C. However, there was still a large uncertainty on the magnitude of these sensitivities. Eight models attributed most of the changes to the land, while three attributed it to the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The strongest feedbacks in these cases are due to increased respiration of carbon from soils throughout the high latitude <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">boreal forests</a> of the Northern Hemisphere. One model in particular (<a href="/wiki/HadCM3" title="HadCM3">HadCM3</a>) indicates a secondary carbon cycle feedback due to the loss of much of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> in response to significantly reduced precipitation over tropical South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> While models disagree on the strength of any terrestrial carbon cycle feedback, they each suggest any such feedback would accelerate global warming.
</p><p>Observations show that soils in the U.K have been losing carbon at the rate of four million tonnes a year for the past 25 years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> according to a paper in Nature by Bellamy et al. in September 2005, who note that these results are unlikely to be explained by land use changes. Results such as this rely on a dense sampling network and thus are not available on a global scale. Extrapolating to all of the United Kingdom, they estimate annual losses of 13 million tons per year. This is as much as the annual reductions in carbon dioxide emissions achieved by the UK under the Kyoto Treaty (12.7 million tons of carbon per year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has also been suggested (by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Freeman_(scientist)" title="Chris Freeman (scientist)">Chris Freeman</a>) that the release of <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_organic_carbon" title="Dissolved organic carbon">dissolved organic carbon</a> (DOC) from <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> into water courses (from which it would in turn enter the atmosphere) constitutes a positive feedback for global warming. The carbon currently stored in peatlands (390–455 gigatonnes, one-third of the total land-based carbon store) is over half the amount of carbon already in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman2001-12">[12]</a></sup> DOC levels in water courses are observably rising; Freeman's hypothesis is that, not elevated temperatures, but elevated levels of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> are responsible, through stimulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_productivity" title="Primary productivity">primary productivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman2004_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman2004-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor2004_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Connor2004-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tree deaths are believed to be increasing as a result of climate change, which is a positive feedback effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arctic_methane_release">Arctic methane release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arctic methane release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a></div>
<p>Warming is also the triggering variable for the release of carbon (potentially as methane) in the arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Methane released from thawing <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> such as the frozen <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a> on the sea floor, creates a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Western Siberia is the world's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_bog" title="Peat bog">peat bog</a>, a one million square kilometer region of <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> peat bog that was formed 11,000 years ago at the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The melting of its permafrost is likely to lead to the release, over decades, of large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. As much as 70,000 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of methane, an extremely effective greenhouse gas, might be released over the next few decades, creating an additional source of greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Similar melting has been observed in eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Lawrence et al. (2008) suggest that a rapid melting of Arctic sea ice may start a feedback loop that rapidly melts Arctic permafrost, triggering further warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_release_from_hydrates">Methane release from hydrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methane release from hydrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">Clathrate gun hypothesis</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">Methane clathrate</a>, also called methane hydrate, is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(properties)" title="Water (properties)">water</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> that contains a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> within its <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> structure. Extremely large deposits of methane clathrate have been found under sediments on the sea and ocean floors of Earth. The sudden release of large amounts of natural gas from methane clathrate deposits, in a <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect#Positive_feedback_and_runaway_global_warming" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway global warming</a> event, has been hypothesized as a cause of past and possibly future climate changes. The release of this trapped methane is a potential major outcome of a rise in temperature; it is thought that this might increase the global temperature by an additional 5° in itself, as methane is much more powerful as a greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide. The theory also predicts this will greatly affect available oxygen content of the atmosphere. This theory has been proposed to explain the most severe mass extinction event on earth known as the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, and also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> climate change event. In 2008, a research expedition for the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> detected levels of methane up to 100 times above normal in the Siberian Arctic, likely being released by methane clathrates being released by holes in a frozen 'lid' of seabed <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, around the outfall of the <a href="/wiki/Lena_River" title="Lena River">Lena River</a> and the area between the <a href="/wiki/Laptev_Sea" title="Laptev Sea">Laptev Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Siberian_Sea" title="East Siberian Sea">East Siberian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abrupt_increases_in_atmospheric_methane">Abrupt increases in atmospheric methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abrupt increases in atmospheric methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">Literature</a> assessments by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and the US <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="Climate Change Science Program">Climate Change Science Program</a> (CCSP) have considered the possibility of future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model#Projections_of_future_climate_change" title="Global climate model">projected climate change</a> leading to a rapid increase in atmospheric methane. The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">IPCC Third Assessment Report</a>, published in 2001, looked at possible rapid increases in methane due either to reductions in the atmospheric chemical sink or from the release of buried methane reservoirs. In both cases, it was judged that such a release would be "exceptionally unlikely"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
(less than a 1% chance, based on expert judgement).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
The CCSP assessment, published in 2008, concluded that an abrupt release of methane into the atmosphere appeared "very unlikely"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp-28">[28]</a></sup>
(less than 10% probability, based on expert judgement).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
The CCSP assessment, however, noted that climate change would "very likely" (greater than 90% probability, based on expert judgement) accelerate the pace of persistent emissions from both hydrate sources and wetlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition">Decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">Decomposition</a></div>
<p>Organic matter stored in permafrost generates heat as it decomposes in response to the permafrost melting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heimann2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heimann2008-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_decomposition">Peat decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Peat decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a>, occurring naturally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_bog" title="Peat bog">peat bogs</a>, is a store of carbon significant on a global scale.  When peat dries it <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Decomposes (page does not exist)">decomposes</a>, and may additionally burn.  <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">Water table</a> adjustment due to global warming may cause significant excursions of carbon from peat bogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  This may be released as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, which can exacerbate the feedback effect, due to its high <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainforest_drying">Rainforest drying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rainforest drying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainforests" title="Rainforests">Rainforests</a>, most notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforests" title="Tropical rainforests">tropical rainforests</a>, are particularly vulnerable to global warming.  There are a number of effects which may occur, but two are particularly concerning.  Firstly, the drier vegetation may cause total collapse of the rainforest <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> would tend to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Caatinga" title="Caatinga">caatinga</a> ecosystems.  Further, even tropical rainforests ecosystems which do not collapse entirely may lose significant proportions of their stored carbon as a result of drying, due to changes in vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_fires">Forest fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forest fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems#Forests" title="Climate change and ecosystems">Climate_change_and_ecosystems § Forests</a></div>
<p>The IPCC Fourth Assessment Report predicts that many mid-latitude regions, such as Mediterranean Europe, will experience decreased rainfall and an increased risk of drought, which in turn would allow forest fires to occur on larger scale, and more regularly. This releases more stored carbon into the atmosphere than the carbon cycle can naturally re-absorb, as well as reducing the overall forest area on the planet, creating a positive feedback loop. Part of that feedback loop is more rapid growth of replacement forests and a northward migration of forests as northern latitudes become more suitable climates for sustaining forests. There is a question of whether the burning of renewable fuels such as forests should be counted as contributing to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  Cook &amp; Vizy also found that forest fires were likely in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>, eventually resulting in a transition to <a href="/wiki/Caatinga" title="Caatinga">Caatinga</a> vegetation in the Eastern Amazon region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Desertification">Desertification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Desertification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">Desertification</a> is a consequence of global warming in some environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  Desert soils contain little <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>, and support little vegetation.  As a result, transition to desert ecosystems is typically associated with excursions of carbon.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling_results">Modelling results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modelling results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The global warming projections contained in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC's Fourth Assessment Report</a> (AR4) include carbon cycle feedbacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections-38">[38]</a></sup> Authors of AR4, however, noted that scientific understanding of carbon cycle feedbacks was poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Projections in AR4 were based on a range of greenhouse gas emissions scenarios, and suggested warming between the late 20th and late 21st century of 1.1 to 6.4 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections-38">[38]</a></sup> This is the "likely" range (greater than 66% probability), based on the expert judgement of the IPCC's authors. Authors noted that the lower end of the "likely" range appeared to be better constrained than the upper end of the "likely" range, in part due to carbon cycle feedbacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_2007_global_warming_projections-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> has commented that more research is needed to model the effects of carbon cycle feedbacks in climate change projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Isaken <i>et al.</i> (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> considered how future methane release from the Arctic might contribute to global warming. Their study suggested that if global <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> were to increase by a factor of 2.5 to 5.2 above (then) current emissions, the indirect contribution to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> would be about 250% and 400% respectively, of the forcing that can be directly attributed to methane. This amplification of methane warming is due to projected changes in atmospheric chemistry.
</p><p>Schaefer <i>et al.</i> (2011)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSDIC_permafrost_study_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSDIC_permafrost_study-42">[42]</a></sup> considered how carbon released from permafrost might contribute to global warming. Their study projected changes in permafrost based on a medium greenhouse gas emissions scenario (<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a> A1B). According to the study, by 2200, the permafrost feedback might contribute 190 (+/- 64) gigatons of carbon cumulatively to the atmosphere. Schaefer <i>et al.</i> (2011) commented that this estimate may be low.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_climate_policy">Implications for climate policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Implications for climate policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Uncertainty over climate change feedbacks has implications for climate policy. For instance, uncertainty over carbon cycle feedbacks may affect targets for reducing greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Emissions targets are often based on a target stabilization level of atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations, or on a target for limiting global warming to a particular magnitude. Both of these targets (concentrations or temperatures) require an understanding of future changes in the carbon cycle. If models incorrectly project future changes in the carbon cycle, then concentration or temperature targets could be missed. For example, if models underestimate the amount of carbon released into the atmosphere due to positive feedbacks (e.g., due to melting permafrost), then they may also underestimate the extent of emissions reductions necessary to meet a concentration or temperature target.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_feedback">Cloud feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cloud feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cloud_feedback" title="Cloud feedback">Cloud feedback</a></div>
<p>Warming is expected to change the distribution and type of clouds. Seen from below, clouds emit infrared radiation back to the surface, and so exert a warming effect; seen from above, clouds reflect sunlight and emit infrared radiation to space, and so exert a cooling effect. Whether the net effect is warming or cooling depends on details such as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">type</a> and altitude of the cloud. High clouds tend to trap more heat and therefore have a positive feedback, low clouds normally reflect more sunlight so they have a negative feedback. These details were poorly observed before the advent of satellite data and are difficult to represent in climate models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SodenHeld2005_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-SodenHeld2005-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_release">Gas release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Gas release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a></div>
<p>Release of gases of biological origin may be affected by global warming, but research into such effects is at an early stage. Some of these gases, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> released from <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, directly affect climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> released from oceans, have indirect effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice-albedo_feedback">Ice-albedo feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ice-albedo feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">Arctic shrinkage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">Ice-albedo feedback</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="952" data-file-width="1452" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png/220px-Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png/330px-Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png/440px-Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Ice_MeltPonds.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial photograph showing a section of sea ice. The lighter blue areas are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melt_ponds" title="Melt ponds">melt ponds</a> and the darkest areas are open water, both have a lower albedo than the white sea ice. The melting ice contributes to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>.</div></div></div><p> When ice melts, land or open water takes its place. Both land and open water are on average less reflective than ice and thus absorb more solar radiation. This causes more warming, which in turn causes more melting, and this cycle continues.  During times of <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">global cooling</a>, additional ice increases the reflectivity which reduces the absorption of solar radiation which results in more cooling in a continuing cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StockerEtAl2001_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-StockerEtAl2001-47">[47]</a></sup>  Considered a faster feedback mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pubs.giss.nasa.gov_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.giss.nasa.gov-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seaice-1870-part-2009.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="840" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/220px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/330px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/440px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seaice-1870-part-2009.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1870–2009 Northern hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Measurement_of_sea_ice#Sea_ice_extent" title="Measurement of sea ice">sea ice extent</a> in million square kilometers. Blue shading indicates the pre-satellite era; data then is less reliable. In particular, the near-constant level extent in Autumn up to 1940 reflects lack of data rather than a real lack of variation.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">Albedo</a> change is also the main reason why <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> predict polar temperatures in the northern hemisphere to rise up to twice as much as those of the rest of the world, in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Polar_amplification" title="Polar amplification">polar amplification</a>. In September 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_ice_pack" title="Arctic ice pack">Arctic sea ice area</a> reached about half the size of the average summer minimum area between 1979 and 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Also in September 2007, Arctic sea ice retreated far enough for the Northwest Passage to become navigable to shipping for the first time in recorded history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The record losses of 2007 and 2008 may, however, be temporary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
Mark Serreze of the US <a href="/wiki/National_Snow_and_Ice_Data_Center" title="National Snow and Ice Data Center">National Snow and Ice Data Center</a> views 2030 as a "reasonable estimate" for when the summertime Arctic ice cap might be ice-free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Polar_amplification" title="Polar amplification">polar amplification</a> of global warming is not predicted to occur in the southern hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The Antarctic sea ice reached its greatest extent on record since the beginning of observation in 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> but the gain in ice in the south is exceeded by the loss in the north. The trend for global sea ice, northern hemisphere and southern hemisphere combined is clearly a decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice loss may have internal feedback processes, as melting of ice over land can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eustatic_sea_level_rise" title="Eustatic sea level rise">eustatic sea level rise</a>, potentially causing instability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_shelves" title="Ice shelves">ice shelves</a> and inundating coastal ice masses, such as glacier tongues.  Further, a potential feedback cycle exists due to earthquakes caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isostatic_rebound" title="Isostatic rebound">isostatic rebound</a> further destabilising ice shelves, glaciers and ice caps.
</p><p>The ice-albedo in some sub-arctic forests is also changing, as stands of <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larch</a> (which shed their needles in winter, allowing sunlight to reflect off the snow in spring and fall) are being replaced by <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> trees (which retain their dark needles all year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_vapor_feedback">Water vapor feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Water vapor feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback" title="Water vapor feedback">Water vapor feedback</a></div>
<p>If the atmospheres are warmed, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_vapor_pressure" title="Saturation vapor pressure">saturation vapor pressure</a> increases, and the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere will tend to increase. Since water vapor is a greenhouse gas, the increase in water vapor content makes the atmosphere warm further; this warming causes the atmosphere to hold still more water vapor (a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>), and so on until other processes stop the feedback loop. The result is a much larger greenhouse effect than that due to CO<sub>2</sub> alone. Although this feedback process causes an increase in the absolute moisture content of the air, the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> stays nearly constant or even decreases slightly because the air is warmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SodenHeld2005_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-SodenHeld2005-44">[44]</a></sup>  Climate models incorporate this feedback. Water vapor feedback is strongly positive, with most evidence supporting a magnitude of 1.5 to 2.0 W/m<sup>2</sup>/K, sufficient  to roughly double the warming that would otherwise occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Water vapor feedback is considered a faster feedback mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pubs.giss.nasa.gov_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.giss.nasa.gov-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative">Negative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Negative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blackbody_radiation">Blackbody radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Blackbody radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the temperature of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> increases, the emission of infrared radiation back into space increases with the fourth power of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperature</a> according to Stefan–Boltzmann law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This increases the amount of outgoing radiation as the Earth warms. The impact of this negative feedback effect is included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a> summarized by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a>. This is also called the <i>Planck feedback</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_cycle">Carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Le_Chatelier.27s_principle"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Le_Chatelier's_principle">Le Chatelier's principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Le Chatelier's principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier's principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>, the chemical equilibrium of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> will shift in response to anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions. The primary driver of this is the ocean, which absorbs anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> via the so-called <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">solubility pump</a>. At present this accounts for only about one third of the current emissions, but ultimately most (~75%) of the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by human activities will dissolve in the ocean over a period of centuries: "A better approximation of the lifetime of fossil fuel CO<sub>2</sub> for public discussion might be 300 years, plus 25% that lasts forever".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However, the rate at which the ocean will take it up in the future is less certain, and will be affected by <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratification</a> induced by warming and, potentially, changes in the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_weathering">Chemical weathering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chemical weathering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">Chemical weathering</a> over the geological long term acts to remove CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere. With current <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, weathering is increasing, demonstrating significant feedbacks between climate and Earth surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">Biosequestration</a> also captures and stores CO<sub>2</sub> by biological processes.  The formation of <a href="/wiki/Biomineralization" title="Biomineralization">shells</a> by organisms in the ocean, over a very long time, removes CO<sub>2</sub> from the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>  The complete conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to limestone takes thousands to hundreds of thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Net_Primary_Productivity">Net Primary Productivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Net Primary Productivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Net_primary_productivity" title="Net primary productivity">Net primary productivity</a> changes in response to increased CO<sub>2</sub>, as plants photosynthesis increased in response to increasing concentrations.  However, this effect is swamped by other changes in the biosphere due to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lapse_rate">Lapse rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Lapse rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">Lapse rate</a></div>
<p>The atmosphere's temperature decreases with height in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. Since emission of infrared radiation varies with temperature, <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">longwave radiation</a> escaping to space from the relatively cold upper atmosphere is less than that emitted toward the ground from the lower atmosphere. Thus, the strength of the greenhouse effect depends on the atmosphere's rate of temperature decrease with height. Both theory and climate models indicate that global warming will reduce the rate of temperature decrease with height, producing a negative <i>lapse rate feedback</i> that weakens the greenhouse effect. Measurements of the rate of temperature change with height are very sensitive to small errors in observations, making it difficult to establish whether the models agree with observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRC2003_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRC2003-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
