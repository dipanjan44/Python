<p><b>Charles David Keeling</b> (April 20, 1928 – June 20, 2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> was an American scientist whose recording of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaheleHanaHou_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-KaheleHanaHou-3">[3]</a></sup>  first alerted the world to the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> contribution to the "<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a> measures the progressive buildup of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas, in the atmosphere.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_early_career">Early life and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_David_Keeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Keeling was born in <a href="/wiki/Scranton,_Pennsylvania" title="Scranton, Pennsylvania">Scranton, Pennsylvania</a> to Ralph Keeling and Grace L Keeling (née Sherberne). His father, an investment banker, excited interests of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> in a 5-year-old Charles, while his mother instilled a lifelong love of music. He graduated with a degree in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois</a> in 1948. Charles Keeling earned a PhD in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> from <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_University" title="Northwestern University">Northwestern University</a> in 1953 under <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Dole" title="Malcolm Dole">Malcolm Dole</a>, a polymer chemist. Most of Dole's graduates were going straight into the oil industry; Keeling "had trouble seeing the future this way" and had become interested in <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, for which he had acquired most of the undergraduate coursework during his PhD. Keeling had applied for postdoctoral positions as a chemist almost exclusively to geology departments "west of the continental divide." He received an offer from <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Brown" title="Harrison Brown">Harrison Brown</a> who had recently started a <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> department at <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>.  He was a postdoctoral fellow in <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> there until he joined <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a> in 1956, and was appointed professor of <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> there in 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>At <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">Caltech</a> he developed the first instrument to measure carbon dioxide in atmospheric samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT01_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT01-5">[5]</a></sup>  Keeling camped at <a href="/wiki/Big_Sur" title="Big Sur">Big Sur</a> where he used his new device to measure the level of carbon dioxide and found that it had risen since the 19th century.
</p>
<h2><span id="Work_with_Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography.2C_1958.E2.80.932005"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_with_Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography,_1958–2005">Work with Scripps Institution of Oceanography, 1958–2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_David_Keeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work with Scripps Institution of Oceanography, 1958–2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="708" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/280px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/420px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/560px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations measured at <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a>: The <i><a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Keeling worked at the Scripps Institution for 43 years during which time he published many influential papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Revelle" title="Roger Revelle">Roger Revelle</a>, the Director of <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a>, based at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Jolla,_California" title="La Jolla, California">La Jolla, California</a>, persuaded Keeling to continue his work there. Revelle was also one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> (IGY) in 1957–58 and Keeling received IGY funding to establish a base on <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a> in Hawaii, two miles (3,000 m) above sea level.
</p><p>Keeling started collecting carbon dioxide samples at the base in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaheleHanaHou_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-KaheleHanaHou-3">[3]</a></sup>  By 1960, he had established that there are strong seasonal variations in carbon dioxide levels with peak levels reached in the late northern hemisphere winter.  A reduction in carbon dioxide followed during spring and early summer each year as plant growth increased in the land-rich northern hemisphere. In 1961, Keeling produced data showing that carbon dioxide levels were rising steadily in what became known as the "<a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a>".
</p><p>In the early 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> stopped supporting his research, calling the outcome "routine". Despite this lack of interest, the Foundation used Keeling's research in its warning in 1963 of rapidly increasing amounts of heat-trapping gases. A 1965 report from <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">President Johnson's</a> <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Science_Advisory_Committee" title="President's Science Advisory Committee">Science Advisory Committee</a> similarly warned of the dangers of extra heat-trapping gases, which cause the temperature of the Earth to rise.
</p><p>The data collection started by Keeling and continued at Mauna Loa is the longest continuous record of atmospheric carbon dioxide in the world and is considered a reliable indicator of the global trend in the mid-level <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. Keeling's research showed that the atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide grew from 315 parts per million (ppm) in 1958 to 380 (ppm) in 2005, with increases correlated to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> emissions. There has also been an increase in seasonal variation in samples from the late 20th century and early 21st century.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_David_Keeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Keeling was an enthusiastic outdoorsman who made many hiking and camping trips to the Western mountains, particularly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cascade_Mountains" title="Cascade Mountains">Cascade Mountains</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_state" title="Washington state">Washington state</a>. He was an active member of the <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(United_States)" title="The Wilderness Society (United States)">Wilderness Society</a> for much of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WS_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-WS-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Keeling married Louise Barthold in 1955. They had five children, one of whom (<a href="/wiki/Ralph_Keeling" title="Ralph Keeling">Ralph Keeling</a>) followed in his father's footsteps and is currently a climate scientist at <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a>.  Charles Keeling was also an accomplished classical pianist who almost chose a career in music. Keeling was a founding director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_San_Diego" title="University of California San Diego">University of California San Diego</a> Madrigal Singers. He was also general chairman of the citizens committee which drafted the <a href="/wiki/Del_Mar,_California" title="Del Mar, California">Del Mar</a> General Plan (or "Community Plan") in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>He died of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> in 2005, aged 77.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_David_Keeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Second Half Century Award of the <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a>, 1981</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ewing_Medal" title="Maurice Ewing Medal">Maurice Ewing Medal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a>, 1991</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blue_Planet_Prize" title="Blue Planet Prize">Blue Planet Prize</a> from the Science Council of Japan and the Asahi Glass Foundation, 1993</li>
<li>At a <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> ceremony held in July 1997, Keeling was presented with a "special achievement award" from <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>. Keeling was honored "for 40 years of outstanding scientific research associated with monitoring of atmospheric carbon dioxide in connection with <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a>".</li>
<li>In 2002, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> presented Keeling with the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a>, the highest US award for scientific research lifetime achievement.</li>
<li>Keeling received the <a href="/wiki/Tyler_Prize_for_Environmental_Achievement" title="Tyler Prize for Environmental Achievement">Tyler Prize for Environmental Achievement</a> in 2005.</li>
<li>The Keeling Curve is "engraved in bronze on a building at Mauna Loa and carved into a wall at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in Washington." It was also a chart on the wall in a classroom at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> where Dr. Revelle had moved to teach in the 1960s and where among others, student <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> would see and "marvel" at it. In 2006, Gore would feature the graph in the <a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth_(book)" title="An Inconvenient Truth (book)">book</a> and movie <i><a href="/wiki/An_Inconvenient_Truth" title="An Inconvenient Truth">An Inconvenient Truth</a></i> and for that work with climate change go on to win, with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT01_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT01-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Revelle_College" title="Revelle College">Revelle College</a> apartments at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_San_Diego" title="University of California San Diego">University of California San Diego</a>, which were designed to emphasize environmental awareness and minimize ecological impact, are named the Charles David Keeling Apartments in his honor.</li>
<li>Since 2014 Keeling's life and work has been the subject of a one-man play, <i>Dr Keeling's Curve</i>, written by George Shea and performed by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Farrell" title="Mike Farrell">Mike Farrell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span id="Memberships.2Ffellowships"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Memberships/fellowships">Memberships/fellowships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_David_Keeling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Memberships/fellowships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Keeling was a Guggenheim fellow at the Meteorological Institute, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Stockholm" title="University of Stockholm">University of Stockholm</a> (1961–62); a guest professor at the Second Physical Institute of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University of Heidelberg</a> (1969–70) and the Physical Institute of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bern" title="University of Bern">University of Bern</a> (1979–80).
</p><p>He was a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>, the American Geophysical Union, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a>, and a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.
</p><p>He was a member of the commission on global pollution of the International Association of Meteorology, and scientific director of the Central CO<sub>2</sub> Calibration Laboratory of the World Meteorological Organization.
</p>
