<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> include its effects on human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The observed and projected increased frequency and severity of climate related impacts will further exacerbate the effects on human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  This article describes some of those effects on individuals and populations.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_disease">Impact on disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Impact on disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_vascular_disease">Impact on vascular disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact on vascular disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erythromelalgia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="611" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Erythromelalgia.jpg/220px-Erythromelalgia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Erythromelalgia.jpg/330px-Erythromelalgia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Erythromelalgia.jpg/440px-Erythromelalgia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erythromelalgia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Erythromelalgia" title="Erythromelalgia">Erythromelalgia</a> in a 77-year-old woman</div></div></div>
<p>A good example of the impact of global warming on health can be seen in the disease <a href="/wiki/Erythromelalgia" title="Erythromelalgia">erythromelalgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lui_-_2015_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lui_-_2015-5">[5]</a></sup>  This is a vascular disease that is commonly triggered by the involvement of change in temperature, which leads to syndromes including (first and second degree) burning pain, increased temperature, erythema and swelling, of mainly the hands and feet that are affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lui_-_2015_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lui_-_2015-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a Chinese study, epidemic Erythromelalgia appears quite common in southern China, most likely due to a sharp decline in temperature following by a rapid increase of temperature and the effects this has on the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lui_-_2015_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lui_-_2015-5">[5]</a></sup>  The acral small superficial arteries intensely constrict and dilate during the sharp decline of temperature, whereas a sharp increase of temperature, the intense expansion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> irritate the nerve endings around, and thus lead to symptoms including burning pain, increased temperature, erythema and swelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lui_-_2015_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lui_-_2015-5">[5]</a></sup>
As climate change proceeds, more Erythromelalgia outbreaks may occur because of the extreme weather events that are projected to increase in coming decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lui_-_2015_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lui_-_2015-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_infectious_diseases">Impact on infectious diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on infectious diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warming oceans and a changing climate are resulting in extreme weather patterns which have brought about an increase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a>—both new and re-emerging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerging_Infections_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emerging_Infections-7">[7]</a></sup>  These extreme weather patterns are creating extended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainy_seasons" title="Rainy seasons">rainy seasons</a> in some areas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobering_Predictions_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sobering_Predictions-8">[8]</a></sup> and extended periods of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> in others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-9">[9]</a></sup> as well as introducing new climates to different regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-9">[9]</a></sup> These extended seasons are creating climates that are able to sustain <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> for longer periods of time, allowing them to multiply rapidly, and also creating climates that are allowing the introduction and survival of new vectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_warmer_and_wetter_climates">Impact of warmer and wetter climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact of warmer and wetter climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Mosquito-borne diseases are probably the greatest threat to humans as they include <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_filariasis" title="Lymphatic filariasis">elephantiasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rift_Valley_fever" title="Rift Valley fever">Rift Valley fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosquitoes-Borne_Disease_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mosquitoes-Borne_Disease-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elephantiasis_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-elephantiasis-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_change_&amp;_health_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_change_&amp;_health-12">[12]</a></sup> Studies are showing higher prevalence of these diseases in areas that have experienced extreme flooding and drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mosquitoes-Borne_Disease_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mosquitoes-Borne_Disease-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elephantiasis_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-elephantiasis-11">[11]</a></sup>   Flooding creates more standing water for mosquitoes to breed; as well,  shown that these vectors are able to feed more and grow faster in warmer climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> As the climate warms over the oceans and coastal regions, warmer temperatures are also creeping up to higher elevations allowing mosquitoes to survive in areas they had never been able to before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> As the climate continues to warm there is a risk that malaria will make a return to the developed world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ticks" title="Ticks">Ticks</a> are also thriving in the warmer temperatures allowing them to feed and grow at a faster rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ticks_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ticks-13">[13]</a></sup>   The <a href="/wiki/Ixodes" title="Ixodes">black legged tick</a>, a carrier of <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>, when not feeding, spends its time burrowed in soil absorbing moisture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobering_Predictions_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sobering_Predictions-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_and_Lyme_Disease_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_and_Lyme_Disease-14">[14]</a></sup>  Ticks die when the climate either becomes too cold or when the climate becomes too dry, causing the ticks to dry out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobering_Predictions_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sobering_Predictions-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_and_Lyme_Disease_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_and_Lyme_Disease-14">[14]</a></sup> The natural environmental controls that used to keep the tick populations in check are disappearing, and warmer and wetter climates are allowing the ticks to breed and grow at an alarming rate, resulting in an increase in Lyme disease, both in existing areas and in areas where it has not been seen before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobering_Predictions_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sobering_Predictions-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ticks_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ticks-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another impact that the warming global temperature has had is on the frequency and severity of heat waves. In addition to dehydration and heat stroke, these heat waves have also resulted in epidemics of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). Recent studies have shown that prolonged heat exposure, physical exertion, and dehydration are sufficient factors to developing CKD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These cases are occurring across the world congruently with heat stress nephropathy.
</p><p>Other diseases on the rise due to extreme weather include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hantavirus" title="Hantavirus">hantavirus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hantavirus_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hantavirus-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elephantiasis_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-elephantiasis-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_change_&amp;_health_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_change_&amp;_health-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Onchocerciasis" title="Onchocerciasis">onchocerciasis</a> (river blindness),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_change_&amp;_health_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_change_&amp;_health-12">[12]</a></sup>  and tuberculosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerging_Infections_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emerging_Infections-7">[7]</a></sup> It also  causes the rise in hay fever, as when the weather gets warmer there is a rise in pollen levels in the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Projected increases in temperature would make parts of southwest Asia uninhabitable, when temperature combined with high humidity reaches a <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet-bulb temperature</a> of 35 °C, the threshold for a fit human to survive in well-ventilated conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Warmer temperatures may also lead an increase in aggression levels. Research has shows links between higher temperatures and increased aggressive and criminal behaviour. Which can be seen by the rise in the rate of criminality during the warmer summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_of_warmer_oceans">Impact of warmer oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impact of warmer oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The warming oceans are becoming a breeding ground for toxic algae blooms (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_tides" title="Red tides">red tides</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_change_&amp;_health_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_change_&amp;_health-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mozambique_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mozambique-20">[20]</a></sup> As the nitrogen and phosphorus levels in the oceans increase, the cholera bacteria that lives within <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> emerge from their dormant state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mozambique_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mozambique-20">[20]</a></sup>  The changing winds and changing ocean currents push the zooplankton toward the coastline, carrying the cholera bacteria, which then contaminate drinking water, causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholera_outbreaks" title="Cholera outbreaks">cholera outbreaks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mozambique_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mozambique-20">[20]</a></sup> As flooding increases there is also an increase in cholera epidemics as the flood waters that are carrying the bacteria are infiltrating the drinking water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Health_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Health-21">[21]</a></sup>   El Nino has also been linked with cholera outbreaks because this weather pattern warms the shoreline waters, causing the cholera bacteria to multiply rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mozambique_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mozambique-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Health_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Health-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaria">Malaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Malaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">Malaria</a> is a mosquito-borne parasitic disease that infects humans and other animals caused by microorganisms in the <a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a> family. It begins with a bite from an infected female mosquito, which introduces the parasite through its saliva and into the infected host’s circulatory system. It then travels through the bloodstream into the liver where it can mature and reproduce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet-22">[22]</a></sup> The disease causes symptoms that typically include fever, headache, shaking chills, anemia, and in severe cases can progress to coma or death.
</p><p>'About 3.2 billion people – nearly half of the world's population – are at risk of malaria. In 2015, there were roughly 214 million malaria cases and an estimated 438,000 malaria deaths.' <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate is an influential driving force of vector-borne diseases such as malaria. Malaria is especially susceptible to the effects of climate change because mosquitoes lack the mechanisms to regulate their internal temperature. This implies that there is a limited range of climatic conditions within which the pathogen (malaria) and vector (a mosquito) can survive, reproduce and infect hosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infectious_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-infectious-24">[24]</a></sup> Vector-borne diseases, such as malaria, have distinctive characteristics that determine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogenicity" title="Pathogenicity">pathogenicity</a>. These include the survival and reproduction rate of the vector, the level of vector activity (i.e. the biting or feeding rate), and the development and reproduction rate of the pathogen within the vector or host.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infectious_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-infectious-24">[24]</a></sup> Changes in climate factors substantially affect reproduction, development, distribution and seasonal transmissions of malaria.
</p><p>Mosquitoes have a small window for preferential conditions for breeding and maturation. The ultimate breeding and maturing temperature for mosquitoes range from sixteen to eighteen degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commonwealth_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-commonwealth-25">[25]</a></sup> If the temperature is decreased by two degrees, most of the insects will succumb to death. This is why malaria is unsustainable in places with cool winters. If a climate with an average of approximately 16 degrees Celsius experiences an increase of about two degrees, the mature bugs and the larvae flourish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> Female mosquitoes will need more food (human/animal blood) to sustain life and to stimulate production of eggs. This increases the chance of spread of malaria due to more human contact and a higher number of the blood sucking insects surviving and living longer. Mosquitoes are also highly sensitive to changes in precipitation and humidity. Increased precipitation can increase mosquito population indirectly by expanding larval habitat and food supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infectious2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-infectious2-26">[26]</a></sup> These prime temperatures are creating large breeding grounds for the insects and places for the larvae to mature. Increased temperature is causing snow to melt and stagnant pools of water to become more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> Bugs that are already carrying the disease are more likely to multiply and infect other mosquitoes causing a dangerous spread of the deadly disease.
</p><p>Climate change has a direct impact on people’s health in places where Malaria is not prevalent. In communities of higher altitudes in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, people are at higher risk for developing malaria in recent years because of an increase temperature. Mosquitos are sensitive to temperature changes and the warming of their environment will boost their rates of production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A fluctuation of two or three degrees is creating exceptional breeding grounds for mosquitoes, for larvae to grow and mature mosquitoes carrying the virus to infect people that have never been exposed before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> This is a severe problem because people in these communities have never been exposed to this disease causing an increased risk for complications from malaria such as cerebral malaria (a type of malaria that causes mental disability, paralysis and has a high mortality rate) and death by the disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> Residents of these communities are being hit hard by malaria because they are unfamiliar with it; they do not know the signs and symptoms and have little to no immunity.
</p><p>The population at risk of malaria in the absence of climate change is projected to double between 1990 and 2080 to 8820 million, however; unmitigated climate change would, by the 2080s, further increase the population at risk of malaria by another 257 to 323 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goklany_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goklany-28">[28]</a></sup> Therefore, reducing the effects of climate change in the present would reduce the total by about 3.5%, saving tens of thousands of lives worldwide.
</p><p>If there is a slight discrepancy in the normal temperature, the perfect conditions for the insects to multiply are created. People that have never been infected before are unknowingly at risk for this deadly disease and do not have the immunity to combat it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup> An increase in temperature has the potential to cause a widespread <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> of the disease that has the capacity to wipe out entire populations of people. It is important to track the prevalence, species and number of insects carrying the disease as well as the number of humans infected in countries and places that have never seen malaria before. It is simple for the slightest of fluctuation in temperature to cause a catastrophic epidemic that has the possibility to end the lives of many innocent and unsuspecting people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-commonwealth_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-commonwealth-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dengue_fever">Dengue fever</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dengue fever">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">Dengue fever</a> is an infectious disease caused by <a href="/wiki/Dengue_virus" title="Dengue virus">dengue viruses</a> known to be in the tropical regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue-29">[29]</a></sup>   It is transmitted by the mosquito <a href="/wiki/Aedes" title="Aedes">Aedes</a>, or A. aegypti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cases of dengue fever have increased dramatically since the 1970s and it continues to become more prevalent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue-29">[29]</a></sup> The greater incidence of this disease is believed to be due to a combination of urbanization, population growth, increased international travel, and global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The same trends also led to the spread of different serotypes of the disease to new areas, and to the emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengue_hemorrhagic_fever" title="Dengue hemorrhagic fever">dengue hemorrhagic fever</a>. There are four different types of viruses in dengue fever. If someone is infected with one type of dengue virus, he or she will have permanent immunity to that type of dengue virus, but will have short term immunity to the other type of dengue fever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dengue_and_Sever_Dengue-29">[29]</a></sup> Some of the symptoms of dengue fever are fever, headache, muscle and joint pains and skin rash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denguefever_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-denguefever-32">[32]</a></sup> There is no vaccine for dengue fever right now and there is no true treatment to get rid of it, but there are treatments to assist with some of the work of dengue, such as the use of oral or intravenous fluids for rehydration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denguefever_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-denguefever-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dengue fever used to be considered a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_disease" title="Tropical disease">tropical disease</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> is causing dengue fever to spread. Dengue fever is transmitted by certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquitoes" title="Mosquitoes">mosquitoes</a>, which have been spreading further and further north. This is because some of the climate changes that are occurring are increased heat, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> which create prime breeding grounds for mosquitoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The hotter and wetter a climate is, the faster the mosquitoes can mature and the faster the disease can develop. Another influence is the changing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Nino" title="El Nino">El Nino</a> effects that are affecting the climate to change in different areas of the world, causing dengue fever to be able to spread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many things that can be done, both on a governmental level and on an individual basis. One improvement would be having a better system of detecting when dengue outbreaks may happen. This can be done by monitoring environments, such as temperatures, rainfall and humidity that would be attractive to these types of mosquitoes and help them to flourish. Another useful plan is to educate the public by letting them know when a dengue outbreak is occurring and what they can do to protect themselves. For example, people should create a living environment that is not attractive to mosquitoes (no standing water), dress in appropriate clothing (light colours, long sleeves), and wear insect repellent.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tick_Borne_Disease">Tick Borne Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tick Borne Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A high humidity of greater than 85% is the best condition for a tick to start and finish its life cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Studies have indicated that temperature and vapor play a significant role in determining the range for tick population. More specifically, maximum temperature has been found to play the most influential variable in sustaining tick populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup> Higher temperatures augment both hatching and developmental rates while hindering overall survival. Temperature is so important to overall survival that an average monthly minimum temperature of below -7°C in the winter can prevent an area from maintaining established populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effect of climate on the tick life cycle is one of the more difficult projections to make in relation to climate and vector-borne disease. Unlike other vectors, tick life cycles span multiple seasons as they mature from larva to nymph to adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Further, infection and spread of diseases such as Lyme disease happens across the multiple stages adding additional variables to consider. Infection of ticks happen in the larval/nymph stage (after the first blood meal) when they are exposed to borrelia burgdorferi (the spirochete responsible for Lyme disease), but transmission to humans doesn’t occur until the adult stages. 
</p><p>The expansion of tick populations are concurrent with global climatic change. Species distribution models of recent years indicate that the deer tick, known as <i>I. scapularis,</i> is pushing its distribution to higher latitudes of the the Northeastern United States and Canada, as well as pushing and maintaining populations in the South Central and Northern Midwest regions of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Climate models project further expansion of tick habit north into Canada as progressing Northwest from the Northeastern United States. Additionally, however, tick populations are expected to retreat from the Southeastern coast of the U.S., but this has not yet been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It’s estimated that coinciding with this expansion, increased average temperatures may double tick populations by 2020 as well as bring an earlier start to the tick exposure season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tick populations are not only spreading wider, but moving to higher elevations. In Colorado, the Rocky Mountain wood tick known as <i>D. andersoni</i> is found along the front range and is want to feed, and consequently infect, human populations with tularemia (<i>Francisella tularensis</i>), Rocky Mountain spotted fever (<i>Rickettsia rickettsii</i>), and Colorado tick fever (CTF virus). A case study testing climatic interaction affecting tick vector (<i>D. andersoni</i>) populations in Larimer County, Colorado indicated that an estimated increase of 1.2–2.0 °C in summer temperatures would increase tick populations moving 100m upwards in elevation, increasing the range and susceptibility of tick-borne illnesses along the front range.
</p><p>Initial symptoms of tick-borne infections are generally quite similar to that of other viral illnesses. This includes fever, headache, fatigue, and general malaise. This group of diseases can further be difficult to distinguish early on in the disease process due to these general symptoms in addition to most people (reported around 75%) not realizing they have been bitten or exposed to the tick vector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Unique to early Lyme disease is the development of the classic erythema migrans skin rash, also known as the “bull’s eye” or “target” rash, which occurs in about 80% of people diagnosed with Lyme disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-43">[43]</a></sup> This symptom can be an important distinguishing factor helping to make the diagnosis early. If Lyme disease is unrecognized, misdiagnosed, or improperly treated it can lead to much more severe and serious consequences with the spread of the spirochete to joints, heart, and nervous system causing arthritis, carditis, cranial nerve palsies or encephalopathy and cognitive dysfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Regardless of the specific diagnosis (Lyme, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, Colorado Tick Fever, Babesiosis etc) the key to management and prevention of sequelae is early identification of disease and initiation of appropriate antibiotic therapy. With regard to the effects of a warming world and the expansion of tick populations to previously unexposed areas, adaptive keys to prevention will include expansion of health care infrastructure and pharmacologic availability, as well as education of people and providers as to the risks of disease and preventative measures they can take.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the face of these expanding threats, strong collaboration between government officials and environmental scientists is necessary for advancing preventative and reactive response measures. Without acknowledging the climate changes that make environments more habitable for disease carriers, policy and infrastructure will lag behind vector borne disease spread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The human cost associated with denying climate change science is one that concerns many governments. In the United States, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) is conducting a grant program called Building Resilience Against Climate Effects (BRACE) which details a 5 step process for combating climate effects like tick borne disease spread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> As is the case when responding to other vectors and effects of climate change, vulnerable populations including children and the elderly will need to be prioritized by any intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Productive policies in the U.S. and the world need to accurately model changes in vector populations as well as the burden of disease, educate the public on ways to mitigate infection, and prepare health systems for the increasing disease load.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_mental_health">Impact on mental health<span id="Mental_health"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impact on mental health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the physical health impacts of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> are well known, the impact on <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> has only begun to be recognized in the last decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> According to 2011 in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Psychologist" title="American Psychologist">American Psychologist</a></i> Clayton &amp; Doherty, concluded that global climate change is bound to have substantial negative impacts on mental health and wellbeing, effects which will primarily be felt by vulnerable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Populations" title="Populations">populations</a> and those with pre-existing serious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_illness" title="Mental illness">mental illness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Research done by Berry, Bowen, and Kjellstrom in 2008 found that climate change exposes populations to <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">trauma</a>, which negatively impacts mental health in very serious ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup>
Both the Clayton study and the Berry study identify three classes of psychological impacts from global climate change: direct, indirect, and psychosocial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup> The Clayton study claims that in order to appreciate these impacts on psychological wellbeing, a basic understanding of certain aspects is required. One must recognize the multiple meanings and cultural narratives associated with climate change, as well as how climate change, global phenomena like increased population, are interrelated. Climate change does not impact everyone equally; those of lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> and social status are at greater risk and experience more devastating impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> A 2018 study of CDC data connected temperature rise to increased numbers of suicides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The study revealed that hotter days could increase suicide rates and could cause approximately 26,000 more suicides in the U.S. by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_impacts">Direct impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Direct impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Direct impacts on mental health happen when a <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> experiences <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> and changed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-3"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> 
Direct impacts like landscape changes, impaired <a href="/wiki/Place_attachment" title="Place attachment">place attachment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_trauma" title="Psychological trauma">psychological trauma</a> are all immediate and localized problems resulting from extreme weather events and environmental changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-4"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Extreme weather events cause negative changes to landscape and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. This leads to communities facing economic aspects, especially for communities that use agriculture as a main source of income. After economic fall, communities face loss of livelihoods and <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. Many communities will also face isolation, alienation, <a href="/wiki/Grief" title="Grief">grief</a>, bereavement, and displacement from these effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup> Individuals will have an increased rate of <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> and emotional stress. The rate of effects on mental health increases in already-vulnerable communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup> Clayton reinforces that the more powerful the extreme weather event, and the more frequent these weather events are, the more damage is done to the mental health of the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-5"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup>
Some of the extreme weather events responsible for these mental health changes include wildfires,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup> earthquakes, hurricanes, fires, floods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup> and extreme heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldhealth_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldhealth-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_impacts">Indirect impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Indirect impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Indirect impacts on mental health occur via impacts on physical health and community wellbeing. Physical health and mental health have a reciprocal relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-4"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup> If the physical health of an individual is negatively impacted, the decline in mental health will soon follow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-5"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup> These impacts are more gradual and cumulative. They are threats to emotional wellbeing through concern and uncertainly about future risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-6"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> They are also large-scale community and social effects, like conflicts related to <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">migration</a> and subsequent shortages or adjustment after a disaster. Extreme weather events play a major role here; their impacts can be indirect, not just direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-6"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup> This is due to the effect on physical health from extreme weather events. Each extreme weather event effects humans in different ways, but they all lead to the decline of mental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-7"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a> indirectly causes mental health issues through physical health issues. The World Health Organization presents the fact that high extreme heat is directly related to certain ailments like <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">respiratory disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>. One piece of their evidence is that in summer 2003, during Europe’s big heat wave, there were 70,000 recorded deaths related to the heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldhealth_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-worldhealth-55">[55]</a></sup> Heat exhaustion also occurs during extreme heat. As climate change continues, heat will continue to rise and these problems will exacerbate. These physical problems lead to mental health problems. As physical health worsens and is less curable, mental stability starts to deteriorate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-8"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>As extreme heat makes landscapes dry, nature is more prone to fire. Research shows that rising heat due to climate change has caused an increase in fires around the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">Burns</a> and smoke inhalation from the increasing number of fires lead to a decline in physical health, which leads to mental health problems. Deaths of family and friends cause individuals to suffer from stress and other conditions. Many suffering from loss of family and friends will internalize their emotions, feel extreme guilt and helplessness, and become paranoid. Others will develop fear of future loss and have an overall displacement of feelings that could last for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowlby_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-bowlby-57">[57]</a></sup> Anderson published research in the American Psychological Association that shows the increase in murders in the United States directly correlates with the temperature increase. For every one-degree <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>, there will be nine more murders in the country, which leads to an additional 24,000 murders or assaults per year in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also an increased risk in <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> in communities that suffer from extreme weather events. Studies show that suicide rates increase after extreme weather events. This is evidence for the decline in mental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-epstein-59">[59]</a></sup> The increased suicide risk has been demonstrated in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> has resulted in <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crop</a> failures and despair to the Australian countryside. After the event, farmers were left with almost nothing. They were forced to sell their belongings, reduce their stock, and borrow large sums of money to plant crops at the start of the next season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-epstein-59">[59]</a></sup> These consequences have caused a growing increase in <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a>, and suicide. More than one hundred farmers in the Australian countryside had committed suicide by 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-epstein-59">[59]</a></sup> An individual’s suicide often leads to mental health problems of loved ones. They face issues like those who have lost loved ones due to fire: grief, sadness, anger, <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoia</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowlby_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-bowlby-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some impacts pertaining to mental health are even more gradual and cumulative than the others, like <a href="/wiki/Social_relation" title="Social relation">social interaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">media</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-7"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> The social interaction between communities and within communities is greatly affected by migration. Communities choose to migrate, or are forced to migrate, due to stressors on limited resources. This is worsened by extreme weather events caused by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup> Common mental health conditions associated indirectly from these extreme weather events include <a href="/wiki/Acute_stress_reaction" title="Acute stress reaction">acute traumatic stress</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-traumatic_stress_disorder" title="Post-traumatic stress disorder">post-traumatic stress disorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complicated_grief" title="Complicated grief">complicated grief</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anxiety_disorders" title="Anxiety disorders">anxiety disorders</a>, sleep difficulties, and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dysfunction" title="Sexual dysfunction">sexual dysfunction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">Drug abuse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcohol abuse</a> are also common aftereffects, and can lead to both physical and mental issues, <a href="/wiki/Addiction" title="Addiction">addiction</a> and substance reliance being the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-4"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effects of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, a past extreme weather event in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, lead to a variety of mental health problems due to the destruction of resources<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-epstein-59">[59]</a></sup> Many people impacted by Hurricane Katrina were left <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a>, disenfranchised, stressed, and suffering physical illness. This strain on the <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> system decreased access and availability of medical resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-epstein-59">[59]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">climate change adaptation</a> measures may prevent the need for displacement. However, some communities may be unable to implement adaptation strategies, and this will create added stress, further exacerbating already existing mental health issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-5"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup>  Extreme weather events and population displacement lead to limited availability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medications" title="Medications">medications</a>, one of the primary resources required to meet psychological and physical needs of those affected by such events. Less medication and medical resources means fewer people can get the help they need to recover. Slowed recovery and lack of recovery worsen overall mental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-6"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_impacts">Psychological impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Psychological impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Psychological impacts are the effects that heat, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, migrations, and climate-related conflicts have on social life and community life. This includes post-disaster adjustment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-8"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Most of these effects are indirect instead of direct, but Clayton and Berry place them in a separate category because they deal with the relationships within a community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berry_51-9"><a href="#cite_note-berry-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-9"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Many of the results are from how people use and occupy territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-10"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Human migration of large communities causes discord within those communities because the already scarce resources are even more limited during migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portier_54-7"><a href="#cite_note-portier-54">[54]</a></sup> Agriculture and aquaculture are severely impacted by the extreme weather events of climate change, the suitability of territory being the most notable kind of change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-11"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> During and after migration, the geographical distribution of populations is altered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-12"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Children and parents may be separated at these times. The early separation of kids from their parents can cause symptoms of grieving, depression, and detachment in both the young and old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowlby_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-bowlby-57">[57]</a></sup> The loss in resources can also lead to inter-community violence and aggression. Two groups may fight over remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>. A community may choose to migrate to find better resources, and encroach on another community’s territory, either accidentally or purposefully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayton_50-13"><a href="#cite_note-clayton-50">[50]</a></sup> Civil unrest can occur when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governments" title="Governments">governments</a> fail to adequately protect communities against the extreme weather events that cause these effects. When this happens, individuals lose confidence and trust in their government. A loss in trust can be the beginning of oncoming mental health problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowlby_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-bowlby-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The disruption of a community when they are forced to relocate results in the deterioration of geographic and social connections. This leads to grief, anxiety, and an overall sense of loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_and_permafrost">Climate change and permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate change and permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> is an important part of our environment and plays an important role in maintaining the stability of many ecosystems around the world.  
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_sustainability">Soil sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Soil sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Permafrost is integral to soil stability in arctic regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Task_Force_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Task_Force-62">[62]</a></sup> Melting permafrost causes the surrounding soil to become unstable and settle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Task_Force_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Task_Force-62">[62]</a></sup> Settlement of surface soil associated with melting permafrost leads to significant infrastructure instability and damage to roads, bridges, buildings, homes, pipelines and airstrips in affected areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Task_Force_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-Task_Force-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_natural_resources">Impact on natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Impact on natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drinking_water">Drinking water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Drinking water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In rural Africa and the Middle East, when droughts dry up the regular water supply, rural and impoverished families are forced to resort to drinking the dirty, sediment-and-parasite-laden water that sits in puddles and small pools on the surface of the earth. Many are aware of the presence of contamination, but will drink from these sources nonetheless in order to avoid dying of dehydration. It has been estimated that up to 80% of human illness in the world can be attributed to contaminated water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>When there is an adequate amount of drinking water, humans drink from different sources than their livestock. However, when drought occurs and drinking water slowly disappears, catchment areas such as streams and depressions in the ground where water gathers are often shared between people and the livestock they depend on for financial and nutritional support, and this is when humans can fall seriously ill. Although some diseases that are transferred to humans can be prevented by boiling the water, many people, living on just a litre or two of water per day, refuse to boil, as it loses a certain percentage of the water to steam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sharing of water between livestock and humans is one of the most common factors in the transmission of non-tuberulosis mycobacteria (NTM). NTM is carried in cattle and pig feces, and if this contaminates the drinking water supply, it can result in pulmonary disease, disseminated disease or localized lesions in humans with both compromised and competent immune systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>  During drought, water supplies are even more susceptible to harmful algal blooms and microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>  Algal blooms increase water turbidity, suffocating aquatic plants, and can deplete oxygen, killing fish. Some kinds of blue-green algae create neurotoxins, hepatoxins, cytotoxins or endotoxins that can cause serious and sometimes fatal neurological, liver and digestive diseases in humans. Cyanobacteria grow best in warmer temperatures (especially above 25 degrees Celsius), and so areas of the world that are experiencing general warming as a result of climate change are also experiencing harmful algal blooms more frequently and for longer periods of time. During times of intense precipitation (such as during the “wet season” in much of the tropical and sub-tropical world, including Australia and Panama), nutrients that cyanobacteria depend on are carried from groundwater and the earth’s surface into bodies of water. As drought begins and these bodies gradually dry up, the nutrients are concentrated, providing the perfect opportunity for algal blooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh_water">Fresh water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fresh water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the climate warms, it changes the nature of global rainfall, evaporation, snow, stream flow and other factors that affect water supply and quality. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">Freshwater</a> resources are highly sensitive to variations in weather and climate. Climate change is projected to affect water availability.  In areas where the amount of water in rivers and streams depends on snow melting, warmer temperatures increase the fraction of precipitation falling as rain rather than as snow, causing the annual spring peak in water runoff to occur earlier in the year. This can lead to an increased likelihood of winter <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> and reduced late summer river flows. Rising sea levels cause saltwater to enter into fresh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underground_water" title="Underground water">underground water</a> and freshwater streams. This reduces the amount of freshwater available for drinking and farming. Warmer water temperatures also affect water quality and accelerate <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_livestock">Impact on livestock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Impact on livestock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate change is beginning to lead the global population into a food shortage, greatly affecting our <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> supply. Although the change in our climate is causing us to lose food, these sources are also contributing to climate change, essentially, creating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loop</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">Greenhouse gases</a>, specifically from livestock, are one of the leading sources furthering global warming; these emissions, which drastically effect climatic change, are also beginning to harm our livestock in ways we could never imagine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_effects">Greenhouse gas effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Our agricultural food system is responsible for a significant amount of the greenhouse-gas emissions that are produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>, it makes up between, at least, 10-12% of the emissions, and when there are changes in land due to the agriculture, it can even rise as high as 17%. More specifically, emissions from farms, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, are the main culprits, and can be held accountable for up to half of the greenhouse-gases produced by the overall food industry, or 80% of all emissions just within agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>The types of farm animals, as well as the food they supply can be put into two categories: <a href="/wiki/Monogastric" title="Monogastric">monogastric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminant</a>. Typically, beef and dairy, in other words, ruminant products, rank high in greenhouse-gas emissions; monogastric, or pigs and poultry-related foods, are low. The consumption of the monogastric types, therefore, yield less emissions. This is due to the fact that these types of animals have a higher feed-conversion efficiency, and also do not produce any methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>As lower-income countries begin, and continue, to develop, the necessity for a consistent meat supply will increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-3"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-impact-73">[73]</a></sup> This means the cattle population will be required to grow in order to keep up with the demand, producing the highest possible rate of greenhouse-gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-4"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many strategies that can be used to help soften the effects, and the further production of greenhouse-gas emissions. Some of these strategies include a higher efficiency in livestock farming, which includes management, as well as technology; a more effective process of managing manure; a lower dependence upon fossil-fuels and nonrenewable resources; a variation in the animals' eating and drinking duration, time and location; and a cutback in both the production and consumption of animal-sourced foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategies_72-5"><a href="#cite_note-strategies-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-impact-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-who-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_stress">Heat stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Heat stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_stress" title="Heat stress">Heat stress</a> on livestock has a devastating effect on not only their growth and reproduction, but their food intake and production of dairy and meat. Cattle require a temperature range of 5-15 degrees Celsius, but upwards to 25 °C, to live comfortably, and once climate change increases the temperature, the chance of these changes occurring increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-impact-73">[73]</a></sup> Once the high temperatures hit, the livestock struggle to keep up their metabolism, resulting in decreased food intake, lowered activity rate, and a drop in weight. This causes a decline in livestock productivity and can be detrimental to the farmers and consumers. Obviously, the location and species of the livestock varies and therefore the effects of heat vary between them. This is noted in livestock at a higher elevation and in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, of which have a generally increased effect from climate change. Livestock in a higher elevation are very vulnerable to high heat and are not well adapted to those changes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_plant_based_food">Impact on plant based food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Impact on plant based food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> has many potential impacts on the production of food crops—from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_scarcity" title="Food scarcity">food scarcity</a> and nutrient deficiency to possible increased food production because of elevated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</a> levels—all of which directly affect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_health" title="Human health">human health</a>. Part of this variability in possible outcomes is from the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_model" title="Climate change model">climate change models</a> used to project potential impacts; each model takes into account different factors and so come out with a slightly different result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup> A second problem comes from the fact that projections are made based on historical data which is not necessarily helpful in accurate forecasting as changes are occurring exponentially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup> As such, there are many different possible impacts—both positive and negative—that may result from climate change affecting global regions in different ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_scarcity">Food scarcity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Food scarcity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food scarcity is a major key for many populations and is one of the prominent concerns with the changing climate. Currently, 1/6 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_population" title="Global population">global population</a> are without adequate food supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup> By 2050, the global population is projected to reach 9 billion requiring global food productions to increase by 50% to meet population demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup> In short, food scarcity is a growing concern that, according to many researchers, is projected to worsen with climate change because of a number of factors including <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events and an increase in <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather">Extreme weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Extreme weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_temperatures">Rising temperatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Rising temperatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> changes and weather patterns become more extreme, areas which were historically good for farmland will no longer be as amicable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Connor-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sindhu_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sindhu-83">[83]</a></sup> The current prediction is for temperature increase and precipitation decrease for major <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid</a> regions (<a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwest_United_States" title="Southwest United States">Southwest United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-Connor-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello-84">[84]</a></sup> In addition, crop yields in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_regions" title="Tropical regions">tropical regions</a> will be negatively affected by the projected moderate increase in temperature (1-2 °C) expected to occur during the first half of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana-85">[85]</a></sup> During the second half of the century, further warming is projected to decrease <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crop</a> yields in all regions including <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">Northern United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello-84">[84]</a></sup> Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staple_crop" title="Staple crop">staple crops</a> are extremely sensitive to heat and when temperatures rise over 36 °C, soybean seedlings are killed and corn pollen loses its vitality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-86">[86]</a></sup> Scientists project that an annual increase of 1 °C will in turn decrease wheat, rice and corn yields by 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_84-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer_&amp;_Shah_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fischer_&amp;_Shah-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are, however, some positive possible aspects to climate change as well. The projected increase in temperature during the first half of the century (1-3 °C) is expected to benefit crop and pasture yields in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_region" title="Temperate region">temperate regions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_&amp;_Van_der_Velde_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello_&amp;_Van_der_Velde-88">[88]</a></sup> This will lead to higher winter temperatures and more frost-free days in these regions; resulting in a longer <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a>, increased thermal resources and accelerated maturation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-79">[79]</a></sup> If the climate scenario results in mild and wet weather, some areas and crops will suffer, but many may benefit from this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drought_and_flood">Drought and flood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Drought and flood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Extreme weather conditions continue to decrease crop yields in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droughts" title="Droughts">droughts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods">floods</a>. While these weather events are becoming more common, there is still uncertainty and therefore a lack of preparedness as to when and where they will take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristjanson_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kristjanson-89">[89]</a></sup> In extreme cases, floods destroy crops, disrupting agricultural activities and rendering workers jobless and eliminating food supply. On the opposite end of the spectrum, droughts can also wipe out crops. It is estimated that 35-50% of the world’s crops are at risk of drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drought_in_Australia" title="Drought in Australia">Australia</a> has been experiencing severe, recurrent droughts for a number of years, bringing serious despair to its farmers. The country’s rates of <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a> are increasing and as of 2007, more than one hundred farmers had committed suicide as their thirsty crops slipped away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> Drought is even more disastrous in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">developing world</a>, exacerbating the pre-existing <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and fostering <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>Droughts can cause farmers to rely more heavily on <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>; this has downsides for both the individual farmers and the consumers. The equipment is expensive to install and some farmers may not have the financial ability to purchase it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Connor_82-2"><a href="#cite_note-Connor-82">[82]</a></sup> The water itself must come from somewhere and if the area has been in a drought for any length of time, the rivers may be dry and the water must be transported from further distances. With 70% of “blue water” currently being used for global agriculture, any need over and above this could potentiate a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">water crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, water is used to flood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_fields" title="Rice fields">rice fields</a> to control the weed population; with the projection of less precipitation for this area, this <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">historical method</a> of weed control will no longer be possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rod10_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rod10-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>With more costs to the farmer, some will no longer find it financially feasible to farm. <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> employs the majority of the population in most low-income countries and increased costs can result in worker layoffs or pay cuts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup> Other farmers will respond by raising their food prices; a cost which is directly passed on to the consumer and impacts the affordability of food. Some farms do not export their goods and their function is to feed a direct family or community; without that food, people will not have enough to eat. This results in decreased production, increased food prices, and potential starvation in parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial">Financial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Financial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some research suggests that initially climate change will help developing nations because some regions will be experiencing more negative climate change effects which will result in increased demand for food leading to higher prices and increased wages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup> However, many of the projected <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">climate scenarios</a> suggest a huge financial burden. For example, the <a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">heat wave that passed through Europe in 2003</a> cost 13 billion euros in uninsured agriculture losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana-85">[85]</a></sup> In addition, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Nino" title="El Nino">El Nino</a> weather conditions, the chance of the Australian farmer’s income falling below average increased by 75%, greatly impacting the country’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana_85-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tubiello_&amp;_Soussana-85">[85]</a></sup> The agriculture industry in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> makes up 52% of their employment and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Canadian Prairies</a> supply 51% of Canadian agriculture; any changes in the production of food crops from these areas could have profound effects on the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sindhu_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sindhu-83">[83]</a></sup> This could negatively affect the affordability of food and the subsequent health of the population.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pests_and_pathogens">Pests and pathogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Pests and pathogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, CO<sub>2</sub> levels are 40% higher than they were in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-industrial_agriculture" title="Pre-industrial agriculture">pre-industrial</a> times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> This diminishes nutritional content for both human and insect consumption. Studies have shown that when CO<sub>2</sub> levels rise, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> leaves are less nutritious; therefore plant-eating beetles have to eat more to get their required <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> In addition, soybeans are less capable of defending themselves against the predatory insects under high CO<sub>2</sub>. The CO<sub>2</sub> diminishes the plant’s <a href="/wiki/Jasmonic_acid" title="Jasmonic acid">jasmonic acid</a> production, an insect-killing poison that is excreted when the plant senses it’s being attacked. Without this protection, beetles are able to eat the soybean leaves freely, resulting in a lower crop yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> This is not a problem unique to soybeans, and many plant species’ defense mechanisms are impaired in a high CO<sub>2</sub> environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, pathogens take 10-16% of the global harvest and this level is likely to rise as plants are at an ever-increasing risk of exposure to pests and pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup> Historically, cold temperatures at night and in the winter months would kill off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insects" title="Insects">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>. The warmer, wetter winters are promoting fungal plant diseases like <a href="/wiki/Soybean_rust" title="Soybean rust">soybean rust</a> to travel northward. Soybean rust is a vicious plant pathogen that can kill off entire fields in a matter of days, devastating farmers and costing billions in agricultural losses. Another example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_Pine_Beetle" title="Mountain Pine Beetle">Mountain Pine Beetle</a> epidemic in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BC,_Canada" title="BC, Canada">BC, Canada</a> which killed millions of pine trees because the winters were not cold enough to slow or kill the growing beetle larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-9"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> The increasing incidence of flooding and heavy rains also promotes the growth of various other plant pests and diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rod11_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rod11-91">[91]</a></sup> On the opposite end of the spectrum, drought conditions favour different kinds of pests like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphids" title="Aphids">aphids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiteflies" title="Whiteflies">whiteflies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locusts" title="Locusts">locusts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-10"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>The competitive balance between plants and pests has been relatively stable for the past century, but with the rapidly shifting climate, there is a change in this balance which often favours the more biologically diverse <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Monocropping" title="Monocropping">monocrops</a> most farms consist of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rod11_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rod11-91">[91]</a></sup> Currently, weeds claim about one tenth of global crop yields annually as there are about eight to ten weed species in a field competing with crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-11"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> Characteristics of weeds such as their <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a>, cross-breeding ability, and fast-growth rates put them at an advantage in changing climates as these characteristics allow them to adapt readily in comparison to most farm's uniform crops, and give them a biological advantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein_77-12"><a href="#cite_note-Ferber_&amp;_Epstein-77">[77]</a></sup> There is also a shift in the distribution of pests as the altered climate makes areas previously uninhabitable more uninviting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomson_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-86">[86]</a></sup> Finally, with the increased CO<sub>2</sub> levels, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicides" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a> will lose their efficiency which in turn increases the tolerance of weeds to herbicides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rod11_91-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rod11-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_nutrition">Impact on nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Impact on nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>	Another area of concern is the effect of climate change on the nutritional content of food for human consumption. Studies show that increasing atmospheric levels of CO<sub>2</sub> have an unfavourable effect on the nutrients in plants. As the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> concentration in the plant’s tissues increase, there is a corresponding decrease in the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. Of significant concern is the <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> content of plants, which also decreases in relation to elevating carbon content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Effects_of_CO2_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Effects_of_CO2-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>Irakli Loladze explains that the lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_nutrients" title="Essential nutrients">essential nutrients</a> in crops contributes the problem of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micronutrient_malnutrition" title="Micronutrient malnutrition">micronutrient malnutrition</a> in society, commonly known as “hidden hunger”; despite adequate caloric intake, the body still is not nutritionally satisfied and therefore continues to be “hungry”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loladze_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loladze-93">[93]</a></sup> This problem is aggravated by the rising cost of food, resulting in a global shift towards diets which are less expensive, but high in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calories" title="Calories">calories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and animal products. This results in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undernutrition" title="Undernutrition">undernutrition</a> and an increase in <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> and diet-related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_diseases" title="Chronic diseases">chronic diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-4"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loladze_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loladze-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>Countries worldwide are already impacted by deficiencies in micronutrients and are seeing the effects in the health of their populations. <a href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency" title="Iron deficiency">Iron deficiency</a> affects more than 3.5 billion people; increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" title="Maternal mortality">maternal mortality</a> and hindering <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_development" title="Cognitive development">cognitive development</a> in children, leading to education losses. <a href="/wiki/Iodine_deficiency" title="Iodine deficiency">Iodine deficiency</a> leads to ailments like <a href="/wiki/Goitre" title="Goitre">goitre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cretinism" title="Cretinism">cretinism</a> and is a problem in at least 130 different countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loladze_93-2"><a href="#cite_note-Loladze-93">[93]</a></sup> Even though these deficiencies are invisible, they have great potential to impact human health on a global scale.
</p><p>It must also be noted that small increases in CO<sub>2</sub> levels can cause a CO<sub>2</sub> fertilization effect where the growth and reproduction abilities of <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C<sub>3</sub></a> plants such as soybeans and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> are actually enhanced by 10-20% in laboratory experiments. This does not take into account, however, the additional burden of pests, pathogens, nutrients and water affecting the crop yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Effects_of_CO2_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-Effects_of_CO2-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_and_mitigation_strategies">Adaptation and mitigation strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Adaptation and mitigation strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While researchers acknowledge there are possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benefits_of_global_warming" title="Benefits of global warming">benefits of global warming</a>, most agree that the negative consequences of climate change will outweigh any potential benefits and instead the shifting climate will result in more benefits to developed countries and more detriments to developing countries; exacerbating the discrepancy between wealthy and impoverished nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischer_&amp;_Shah_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fischer_&amp;_Shah-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory-94">[94]</a></sup>  By thoughtful and proactive efforts, climate change can be mitigated by addressing these issues with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_approach" title="Multidisciplinary approach">multidisciplinary approach</a> that works on a <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">global</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">national</a> and <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> basis that recognizes the uniqueness of each country’s situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-5"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sindhu_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sindhu-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a study of <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>’s smallholder farms, impacts of climate change on agriculture are already being seen there resulting in changes to farming practices such as <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercropping</a>, crop, soil, land, water and livestock management systems, and introduction of new technologies and seed varieties by some of the farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kristjanson_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kristjanson-89">[89]</a></sup> Some other suggestions such as eliminating <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> and household food waste, encouraging diverse and vegetable-rich diets, and providing global access to foods (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_aid" title="Food aid">food aid</a> programs) have been suggested as ways to adapt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-6"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup> Many researchers agree that agricultural innovation is essential to addressing the potential issues of climate change. This includes better management of soil, water-saving technology, matching crops to environments, introducing different crop varieties, crop rotations, appropriate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> use, and supporting community-based adaptation strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kul_78-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kul-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-7"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sindhu_83-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sindhu-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rod11_91-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rod11-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFPRI_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFPRI-95">[95]</a></sup>  On a government and global level, research and investments into <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_productivity" title="Agricultural productivity">agricultural productivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> must be done to get a better picture of the issues involved and the best methods to address them. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_policies" title="Government policies">Government policies</a> and programs must provide environmentally sensitive government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsidies" title="Subsidies">subsidies</a>, educational campaigns and economic incentives as well as funds, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> and safety nets for vulnerable populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertel_76-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hertel-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-8"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_81-6"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sindhu_83-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sindhu-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFPRI_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFPRI-95">[95]</a></sup>  In addition, providing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_warning_systems" title="Early warning systems">early warning systems</a>, and accurate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_forecasts" title="Weather forecasts">weather forecasts</a> to poor or remote areas will allow for better preparation; by using and sharing the available technology, the global issue of climate change can be addressed and mitigated by the global community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beddington_80-9"><a href="#cite_note-Beddington-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans_and_human_health">Oceans and human health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Oceans and human health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps one of the most recent adverse <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of climate change</a> to be explored is that of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>. Our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a> cover approximately 71 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface and support a diverse range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>, which are home to over 50 percent of all the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-96">[96]</a></sup> Oceans regulate <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> as well as providing <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a> for a vast variety of species, humans included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-96">[96]</a></sup> Covering such an extensive part of the planet has allowed the oceans to absorb a large portion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven99_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven99-97">[97]</a></sup> This process is part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> in which the fluxes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</a> in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> are described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Humans have drastically added to the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</a> in the atmosphere through the burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> and the process of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. Oceans work as a sink absorbing excess <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>). As the oceans absorb <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) it breaks down into <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, a mild acid, this neutralizes the normally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> ocean water. As a result, the pH in the oceans is declining. In the research surrounding global climate change we are only just beginning to realize that our oceans can sequester a finite amount of CO<sub>2</sub> before we start seeing impacts on marine life that could lead to devastating losses. Acidification of our oceans has the potential to drastically alter life as we know it - from <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> patterns and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_scarcity" title="Food scarcity">food scarcity</a> to a loss of millions of species from the planet - all of these consequences hold the potential to directly affect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_health" title="Human health">human health</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coral">Coral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Coral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With degradation of protective coral reefs through acidic erosion, bleaching and death, salt water is able to infiltrate fresh ground water supplies that large populations depend on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atoll_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-atoll-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-child_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-child-100">[100]</a></sup> Nowhere is this more evident than atoll islands. These islands possess limited freshwater supplies, namely ground water lenses and rain fall. When the protective coral reefs surrounding them erodes due to higher temperatures and acidic water chemistry, salt water is able to infiltrate the lens and contaminate the drinking water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atoll_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-atoll-99">[99]</a></sup> In coastal Bangladesh it has been demonstrated that seasonal hypertension in pregnant women is connected with such phenomenon due to high sodium intake from drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-child_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-child-100">[100]</a></sup> Reef erosion, coupled with sea level rise, tends to flood low-lying areas more frequently during storm surges and weather events. Warming ocean waters generate larger and more devastating weather events that can decimate coastal populations especially without the protection of coral reefs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_health">Human health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Human health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The health of our oceans has a direct effect on the health humans. According to Small and Nicholls, they estimated that 1.2 billion people worldwide, lived in the near-coastal region (within 100 km and 100m of the shoreline).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-settle_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-settle-101">[101]</a></sup> This data was collected in 1990 and therefore is a conservative estimate in modern terms. In the U.S. alone 53% of the population lives within 50 miles of the coastal shoreline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grass_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-grass-102">[102]</a></sup> Humans rely heavily on oceans for food, employment, recreation, weather patterns and transportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guest_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-guest-103">[103]</a></sup>  In the U.S. alone the lands adjacent to the oceans contribute over $1 trillion annually through these various activities not to mention pharmaceutical and medicinal discoveries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guest_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-guest-103">[103]</a></sup> In all, the oceans are very important for our survival as a species.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_safety">Food safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Food safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Our insatiable appetite for seafood of all types has led to overfishing and has already significantly strained marine food stocks to the point of collapse in many cases. With seafood being a major protein source for so much of the population, there are inherent health risks associated with global warming. As mentioned above increased agricultural runoff and warmer water temperature allows for eutrophication of ocean waters. This increased growth of algae and phytoplankton in turn can have dire consequences. These algal blooms can emit toxic substances that can be harmful to humans if consumed. Organisms, such as shellfish, marine crustaceans and even fish, feed on or near these infected blooms, ingest the toxins and can be consumed unknowingly by humans. One of these toxin producing algae is Pseudo-nitzschia fraudulenta. This species produces a substance called <a href="/wiki/Domoic_acid" title="Domoic acid">domoic acid</a> which is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Amnesic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Amnesic shellfish poisoning">amnesic shellfish poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pseudo_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-pseudo-104">[104]</a></sup>  The toxicity of this species has been shown to increase with greater CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations associated with ocean acidification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pseudo_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-pseudo-104">[104]</a></sup>  Some of the more common illnesses reported from harmful algal blooms include; <a href="/wiki/Ciguatera_fish_poisoning" title="Ciguatera fish poisoning">Ciguatera fish poisoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paralytic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Paralytic shellfish poisoning">paralytic shellfish poisoning</a>, azaspiracid shellfish poisoning, <a href="/wiki/Diarrhetic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Diarrhetic shellfish poisoning">diarrhetic shellfish poisoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Neurotoxic shellfish poisoning">neurotoxic shellfish poisoning</a> and the above-mentioned amnesic shellfish poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pseudo_104-2"><a href="#cite_note-pseudo-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather_events">Extreme weather events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Extreme weather events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Infectious disease often accompanies <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, such as floods, earthquakes and drought. These local epidemics occur due to loss of infrastructure, such as hospitals and sanitation services, but also because of changes in local ecology and environment. For example, malaria outbreaks have been strongly associated with the El Niño cycles of a number of countries (India and Venezuela, for example). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> can lead to drastic, though temporary, changes in the environment such as temperature fluctuations and <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash floods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup> Because of global warming there has been a marked trend towards more variable and anomalous weather. This has led to an increase in the number and severity of extreme weather events. This trend towards more variability and fluctuation is perhaps more important, in terms of its impact on human health, than that of a gradual and long-term trend towards higher average temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drought">Drought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Drought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arguably one of the worst effects that drought has directly on human health is the destruction of food supply. Farmers who depend on weather to water their crops lose tons of crops per year due to drought. Plant growth is severely stunted without adequate water, and plant resistance mechanisms to fungi and insects weaken like human immune systems. The expression of genes is altered by increased temperatures, which can also affect a plant’s resistance mechanisms. One example is wheat, which has the ability to express genes that make it resistant to leaf and stem rusts, and to the Hessian fly; its resistance declines with increasing temperatures.
A number of other factors associated with lack of water may actually attract pestilent insects, as well- some studies have shown that many insects are attracted to yellow hues, including the yellowing leaves of drought-stressed plants.
During times of mild drought is when conditions are most suitable to insect infestation in crops; once the plants become too weakened, they lack the nutrients necessary to keep the insects healthy. This means that even a relatively short, mild drought may cause enormous damage- even though the drought on its own may not be enough to kill a significant portion of the crops, once the plants become weakened, they are at higher risk of becoming infested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>The results of the loss of crop yields affect everyone, but they can be felt most by the poorest people in the world. As supplies of corn, flour and vegetables decline, world food prices are driven up. Malnutrition rates in poor areas of the world skyrocket, and with this, dozens of associated diseases and health problems. Immune function decreases, so mortality rates due to infectious and other diseases climb. For those whose incomes were affected by droughts (namely agriculturalists and pastoralists), and for those who can barely afford the increased food prices, the cost to see a doctor or visit a clinic can simply be out of reach. Without treatment, some of these diseases can hinder one’s ability to work, decreasing future opportunities for income and perpetuating the vicious cycle of poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Floods">Floods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Floods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">Health</a> concerns around the world can be linked to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods">floods</a>. With the increase in temperatures worldwide due to climate change the increase in flooding is unavoidable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods">Floods</a> have short and long term negative implications to peoples' health and well being. Short term implications include <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortalities</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injuries" title="Injuries">injuries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases" title="Diseases">diseases</a>, while long term implications include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-communicable_diseases" title="Non-communicable diseases">non-communicable diseases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychosocial" title="Psychosocial">psychosocial</a> health aspects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mortalities are not uncommon when it comes to floods. The Countries with lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incomes" title="Incomes">incomes</a> are more likely to have more <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">fatalities</a>, because of the lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resources" title="Resources">resources</a> they have and the supplies to prepare for a flood. This does depend on the type and properties of the flood. For example, if there is a <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flood</a> it would not matter how prepared you are. Fatalities connected directly to floods are usually caused by <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning</a>; the waters in a flood are very deep and have strong <a href="/wiki/Current_(fluid)" title="Current (fluid)">currents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaths" title="Deaths">Deaths</a> do not just occur from drowning, deaths are connected with <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> and any other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illness" title="Illness">illness</a> that needs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_supplies" title="Medical supplies">medical supplies</a> that cannot be delivered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-2"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup> Due to flooding mud, grit or sand particles can be deposited into the lakes and rivers. These particles cause the water to become dirty and this becomes a problem as the dirty water leads to water related diseases. For example, cholera and guinea worm disease are caused by dirty water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Injuries can lead to an excessive amount of morbidity when a flood occurs. Victims who already have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_illness" title="Chronic illness">chronic illness</a> and then sustain a non-fatal injury are put at a higher risk for that non-fatal injury to become <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">fatal</a>. Injuries are not isolated to just those who were directly in the flood, <a href="/wiki/Rescue" title="Rescue">rescue</a> teams and even people delivering supplies can sustain an injury. Injuries can occur anytime during the flood process; before, during and after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-3"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>  Before the flood people are trying to <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation" title="Emergency evacuation">evacuate</a> as fast as they can, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_accidents" title="Motor vehicle accidents">motor vehicle accidents</a>, in this case, are a primary source of injuries obtained post flood. During floods accidents occur with falling <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">debris</a> or any of the many fast moving objects in the water. After the flood rescue attempts are where large numbers injuries can occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-4"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communicable_diseases" title="Communicable diseases">Communicable diseases</a> are increased due to many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that are being transported by the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. In floods where there are many fatalities in the water there is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygienic" title="Hygienic">hygienic</a> problem with the handling of bodies, due to the <a href="/wiki/Panic" title="Panic">panic</a> stricken mode that comes over a town in distress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-5"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>  There are many water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contaminated" title="Contaminated">contaminated</a> diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">hepatitis A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_E" title="Hepatitis E">hepatitis E</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrheal_diseases" title="Diarrheal diseases">diarrheal diseases</a>, to mention a few. There are certain diseases that are directly correlated with floods they include any <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a> and any <a href="/wiki/Wound" title="Wound">wound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throat" title="Throat">throat</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ear_infection" title="Ear infection">ear infection</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_disease" title="Gastrointestinal disease">Gastrointestinal disease</a> and diarrheal diseases are very common due to a lack of clean water during a flood. Most of clean water supplies are contaminated when flooding occurs. Hepatitis A and E are common because of the lack of <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> in the water and in living quarters depending on where the flood is and how prepared the <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> is for a flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-6"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory_diseases" title="Respiratory diseases">Respiratory diseases</a> are a common after the disaster has occurred. This depends on the amount of <a href="/wiki/Water_damage" title="Water damage">water damage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a> that grows after an incident. <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">Vector</a> borne diseases increase as well due to the increase in still water after the floods have settled. The diseases that are vector borne are  <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengue" title="Dengue">dengue</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-7"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>Non-communicable diseases are a long-term effect of floods. They are either caused by a flood or they are worsened by a flood; they include <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lung_disease" title="Lung disease">lung disease</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>. Floods have a huge impact on victims' psychosocial <a href="/wiki/Integrity" title="Integrity">integrity</a>. People suffer from a wide variety of losses and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(psychological)" title="Stress (psychological)">stress</a>. One of the most treated illness in long-term health problems are <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a> caused by the flood and all the <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a> that flows with one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alderman_109-8"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricanes">Hurricanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Hurricanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another result of the warming oceans are stronger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, which will wreak more havoc on land, and in the oceans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Storms_and_Sickness_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Storms_and_Sickness-111">[111]</a></sup> and create more opportunities for vectors to breed and infectious diseases to flourish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobering_Predictions_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sobering_Predictions-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a> also means stronger winds. These winds can carry vectors tens of thousands of kilometers, resulting in an introduction of new infectious agents to regions that have never seen them before, making the humans in these regions even more susceptible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Mosquito's_Bite_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-The_Mosquito's_Bite-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_melting">Glacial melting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Glacial melting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> is a mass of ice that has originated from snow that has been compacted via pressure and have definite lateral limits and movements in definite directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-112">[112]</a></sup> They are found in areas where the temperatures do not get warm enough to melt annual snow accumulation, thus resulting in many layers of snow piling up over many years, creating the pressure needed to make a glacier.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_change" title="Global climate change">Global climate change</a> and fluctuation is causing an increasingly exponential melting of Earth’s glaciers.  These melting glaciers have many <a href="/wiki/Social" title="Social">social</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> consequences that directly or indirectly impact the health and well-being of humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlove_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orlove-113">[113]</a></sup>  The recession of glaciers change <a href="/wiki/Sea_salt" title="Sea salt">sea salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>, and temperature ratios in the ocean which changes currents, weather patterns, and marine life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-2"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>  The melt also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_levels" title="Ocean levels">increases ocean levels</a> and decreases the <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">availability of water</a> for human consumption, agriculture, and hydroelectricity.  This aggravates and increases the likelihood of issues such as sanitation,  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_hunger" title="World hunger">world hunger</a>, population shifts, and <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">catastrophic weather</a> such as flooding, drought, and worldwide temperature fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-3"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>“Glacier mass-balances show consistent decreases over the last century in most regions of the world and retreat may be accelerating in many locations" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyurgerov_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dyurgerov-114">[114]</a></sup> with an average loss of ten meters per year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlove_113-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orlove-113">[113]</a></sup> nearly twice as fast as ten years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>  Glaciers currently cover ~10% of the Earth’s surface, or ~15 million km² and holds ~75% of Earth’s fresh water supply. Glacial retreat first gained the attention of alpinists and the tourist industry shortly after 1940 – when the globe warmed ~0.5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-112">[112]</a></sup> Even with 62 years of awareness, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> is just becoming an issue for some parts of society. Over this time period the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_glaciers" title="Alpine glaciers">cirque and steep alpine glaciers</a> were able to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acclimatize" title="Acclimatize">acclimatize</a> to the new temperatures posed by climate change; large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valley_glacier" title="Valley glacier">valley glaciers</a> have not yet made this adjustment. This means the large valley glaciers are rapidly retreating, as their mass is attempting to achieve equilibrium with the current climate.  If regional <a href="/wiki/Snow_line" title="Snow line">snow lines</a> stay constant, then the glaciers remain constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_112-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-112">[112]</a></sup> Today this is clearly not the case as global warming is causing mountain snow lines to rapidly retreat.  Even the United States’ famous <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a> is receding.  More than two-thirds of its glaciers have disappeared and it is expected for them to be nonexistent in the park by the year 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p><p>Glacial melt will affect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_wetland" title="Coastal wetland">low-lying coastal wetlands</a> via <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, change key drivers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresh-water_ecosystem" title="Fresh-water ecosystem">fresh-water ecosystems</a>, shift the timing of snow packs, and alter the unique character of associated fresh water streams off of snow pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jenkins_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins-117">[117]</a></sup>  It has also been stated that the sea level will rise 28–43 cm by 2100;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jenkins_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins-117">[117]</a></sup> if all the ice on Earth melts, it is predicted that the ocean level will increase 75 meters, destroying many coastal cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-4"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>  In addition, the freshwater swaps in northern areas are already affected by the intrusion of salt water. “Sea level rise will cause a change of state from freshwater to marine or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuarine" title="Estuarine">estuarine ecosystems</a>, radically altering the composition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biotic_community" title="Biotic community">biotic communities</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jenkins_117-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Not only are glaciers causing a rise in sea level, they are causing an increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño Southern Oscillation</a> (ESNO) and global temperature itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_112-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-112">[112]</a></sup>  Glacier loss adds to global heat rise through a decrease in what is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>.  As more ice melts, there is less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_reflectance" title="Solar reflectance">solar reflectivity</a> and less heat is reflected away from the Earth, causing more heat to be absorbed, and retained in the atmosphere and soil <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-5"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup> In addition to the El Niño events, glacial melt is contributing to the rapid turnover of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chinn_112-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chinn-112">[112]</a></sup> and ocean salt content by diluting the ocean water and slowing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_conveyor_belt" title="Atlantic conveyor belt">Atlantic conveyor belt's</a> usually swift dive because of a top layer of buoyant, cold, fresh water that slows the flow of warm water to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-6"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fifty percent of the world’s fresh water consumption is dependent glacial runoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_116-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hall-116">[116]</a></sup>  Earth's glaciers are expected to melt within the next forty years, greatly decreasing fresh water flow in the hotter times of the year, causing everyone to depend on rainwater, resulting in large shortages and fluctuations in fresh water availability which largely effects agriculture, power supply, and human health and well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-7"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>  Many power sources and a large portion of agriculture rely on glacial runoff in the late summer.  “In many parts of the world, disappearing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_glacier" title="Mountain glacier">mountain glaciers</a> and droughts will make fresh, clean water for drinking, bathing, and other necessary human (and livestock) uses scarce" and a valuable commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_105-8"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_stress_2">Heat stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Heat stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The upper limit for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_stress" title="Heat stress">heat stress</a> humans can adapt to is called into question with a 7 °C temperature rise, quantified by the <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet-bulb temperature</a>, regions of Earth would lose their habitability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_deforestation_in_the_African_Highlands">Effects of deforestation in the African Highlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Effects of deforestation in the African Highlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2136" data-file-width="2848" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg/250px-Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg/375px-Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg/500px-Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ethiopian_highlands_01_mod.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Highlands" title="Ethiopian Highlands">Ethiopian Highlands</a></div></div></div>
<p>Environmental changes such as deforestation could increase local temperatures in the highlands thus could enhance the vectorial capacity of the <a href="/wiki/Anopheles" title="Anopheles">anopheles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Afrane_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Afrane-119">[119]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anopheles" title="Anopheles">Anopheles</a> mosquitoes are responsible for the transmission of a number of diseases in the world, such as, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_filariasis" title="Lymphatic filariasis">lymphatic filariasis</a> and viruses that can cause such ailments, like the <a href="/wiki/O%27nyong%27nyong_virus" title="O'nyong'nyong virus">O'nyong'nyong virus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Afrane_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Afrane-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Environmental changes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_variability" title="Climate variability">climate variability</a>, and climate change are such factors that could affect biology and ecology of Anophelse <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_transmission" title="Disease transmission">disease transmission</a> potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Afrane_119-2"><a href="#cite_note-Afrane-119">[119]</a></sup> Climate change is expected to lead to latitudinal and altitudinal temperature increases. Global warming projections indicate that the best estimate of surface air warming for a “high scenario” is 4 C, with a likely range of 2.4-6.4 C by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> A temperature increase of this size would alter the biology and the ecology of many mosquito vectors and the dynamics of the diseases they transmit such as malaria. Anopheles mosquitoes in highland areas are to experience a larger shift in their metabolic rate due to the climate change. This climate change is due to the deforestation in the highland areas where these mosquitoes dwell. When temperature rises, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larvae" title="Larvae">larvae</a> take a shorter time to mature <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> and, consequently, there is a greater capacity to produce more offspring. In turn this could potentially lead to an increase in malaria transmission when infected humans are available.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1470" data-file-width="1000" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png/150px-Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png/225px-Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png/300px-Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> is directly linked with a decrease in plant <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muhammad_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-muhammad-122">[122]</a></sup>   This decrease in biodiversity has several implications for human health.  One such implication is the loss of <a href="/wiki/Herbalism" title="Herbalism">medicinal plants</a>.  The use of plants for medicinal purposes is extensive, with ~70 to 80% of individuals worldwide relying solely on plant-based medicine as their primary source of healthcare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton-123">[123]</a></sup>   This dependency on plants for medicinal purposes is especially rife in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> that only consume 15% of manufactured pharmaceutical drugs, many of which are fake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamilton_123-1"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton-123">[123]</a></sup> Local knowledge surrounding medicinal plants is useful for screening for new herbal medicines that may be useful for treating disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirsanjari_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-mirsanjari-124">[124]</a></sup>  Villages and communities which reside continually in a single geographic area over time, create, transmit and apply widespread information surrounding the medicinal resources in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirsanjari_124-1"><a href="#cite_note-mirsanjari-124">[124]</a></sup> Formal scientific methods have been useful in identifying the active ingredients used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnopharmacy" title="Ethnopharmacy">ethnopharmacy</a> and applying them to modern medicines.  However, it is important that medicinal resources are managed appropriately as they become globally traded in order to prevent <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">species endangerment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirsanjari_124-2"><a href="#cite_note-mirsanjari-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction_of_indigenous_groups">Extinction of indigenous groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Extinction of indigenous groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deforestation is also a primary cause of dislocation and in some cases, extinction of indigenous people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurance_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laurance-125">[125]</a></sup>  The Malaysian state <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a> is an example where rampant deforestation has overrun many <a href="/wiki/Dayak_people" title="Dayak people">Dayak</a> groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurance_125-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laurance-125">[125]</a></sup>  The indigenous Sarawakians relied on shifting agriculture, hunting and gathering in order to sustain their relatively low population density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaur_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaur-126">[126]</a></sup>  With the advent of modern logging technology the Sarawak forests entered 'mainstream' economic development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurance_125-2"><a href="#cite_note-Laurance-125">[125]</a></sup> This has led to massive forced evacuations and relocation of the Dayak people causing a loss of their traditions and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goroh_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goroh-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Mountain_pine_beetle.2C_forest_ecosystems_and_forest_fires"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountain_pine_beetle,_forest_ecosystems_and_forest_fires">Mountain pine beetle, forest ecosystems and forest fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Mountain pine beetle, forest ecosystems and forest fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dendroctonus_ponderosae.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Dendroctonus_ponderosae.jpg/200px-Dendroctonus_ponderosae.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e3/Dendroctonus_ponderosae.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dendroctonus_ponderosae.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adult mountain pine beetle</div></div></div>
<p>Climate change and the associated changing weather patterns occurring worldwide have a direct effect on biology, population ecology, and the population of eruptive insects, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pine_beetle" title="Mountain pine beetle">mountain pine beetle</a> (MPB). This is because temperature is a factor which determines insect development and population success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_Pine_Beetle" title="Mountain Pine Beetle">Mountain Pine Beetle</a> are a species native to Western <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Prior to climatic and temperature changes, the mountain pine beetle predominately lived and attacked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lodgepole_pine" title="Lodgepole pine">lodgepole</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponderosa_Pine" title="Ponderosa Pine">ponderosa</a> pine trees at lower elevations, as the higher elevation <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascades</a> were too cold for their survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> Under normal seasonal freezing weather conditions in the lower elevations, the forest ecosystems that pine beetles inhabit are kept in a balance by factors such as tree defense mechanisms, beetle defense mechanisms, and freezing temperatures. It is a simple relationship between a <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">host</a> (the forest), an <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">agent</a> (the beetle) and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> (the weather &amp; temperature).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> However, as climate change causes mountain areas to become warmer and drier, pine beetles have more power to infest and destroy the forest ecosystems, such as the whitebark pine forests of the Rockies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-2"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> This is a forest so important to forest ecosystems that it is called the “rooftop of the rockies”. Climate change has led to a threatening pine beetle <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a>, causing them to spread far beyond their native habitat. This leads to <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> changes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">forest fires</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floods" title="Floods">floods</a> and hazards to human <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-3"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>The whitebark pine ecosystem in these high elevations plays many essential roles, providing support to plant and animal life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-4"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup>  They provide food for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grizzly_bears" title="Grizzly bears">grizzly bears</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squirrels" title="Squirrels">squirrels</a>, as well as shelter and breeding grounds for <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>; protects <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">watersheds</a> by sending water to parched foothills and plains; serves as a reservoir by dispensing supplies of water from melted snowpacks that are trapped beneath the shaded areas; and creates new soil which allows for growth of other trees and plant species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-5"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Without these pines, animals do not have adequate food, water, or shelter, and the reproductive life cycle, as well as quality of life, is affected as a consequence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-6"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Normally, the pine beetle cannot survive in these frigid temperatures and high elevation of the Rocky Mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-7"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> However, warmer temperatures means that the pine beetle can now survive and attack these forests, as it no longer is cold enough to freeze and kill the beetle at such elevations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-8"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Increased temperatures also allow the pine beetle to increase their life cycle by 100%: it only takes a single year instead of two for the pine beetle to develop. As the Rockies have not adapted to deal with pine beetle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infestations" title="Infestations">infestations</a>, they lack the defenses to fight the beetles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-9"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Warmer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_patterns" title="Weather patterns">weather patterns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, and beetle defense mechanisms together dries out <a href="/wiki/Sap" title="Sap">sap</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pine_trees" title="Pine trees">pine trees</a>, which is the main mechanism of defense that trees have against the beetle, as it drowns the beetles and their eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-10"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> This makes it easier for the beetle to infest and release chemicals into the tree, luring other beetles in an attempt to overcome the weakened defense system of the pine tree. As a consequence, the host (forest) becomes more vulnerable to the disease-causing agent (the beetle).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-11"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>The whitebark forests of the Rockies are not the only forests that have been affected by the mountain pine beetle. Due to temperature changes and wind patterns, the pine beetle has now spread through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Divide" title="Continental Divide">Continental Divide</a> of the Rockies and has invaded the fragile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, known as the “lungs of the Earth”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-12"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> These forests are imperative for producing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and removing <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. But as the forests become infested and die, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is released into the environment, and contributes even more to a warming climate. Ecosystems and humans rely on the supply of oxygen in the environment, and threats to this boreal forest results in severe consequences to our planet and human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-13"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> In a forest ravaged by pine beetle, the dead logs and kindle which can easily be ignited by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">Forest fires</a> present dangers to the environment, human health and the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-14"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup>  They are detrimental to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, releasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> compounds as they burn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-15"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup> Due to human induced <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and climate change, along with the pine beetle pandemic, the strength of forest ecosystems decrease. The infestations and resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases" title="Diseases">diseases</a> can indirectly, but seriously, effect human health. As droughts and temperature increases continue, so does the frequency of devastating forest fires, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> infestations, forest diebacks, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, habitat loss, animal endangerment and threats to safe drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinebeetle_129-16"><a href="#cite_note-pinebeetle-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smoke_from_wildfires">Smoke from wildfires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Smoke from wildfires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort,_Utah.jpg"><img alt="Flat expanse of brown grasses and some green trees with black and some gray smoke and visible flames in the distance." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg/240px-Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg/360px-Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg/480px-Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort%2C_Utah.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wildfire_near_Cedar_Fort,_Utah.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A surface fire in the western desert of <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, U.S.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> increases <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> potential and activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> leads to a warmer ground temperature and its effects include earlier snowmelt dates, drier than expected <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, increased number of potential fire days, increased occurrence of summer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droughts" title="Droughts">droughts</a>, and a prolonged <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westerling_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-westerling-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p>Warming <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> temperatures increase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammability</a> of materials that make up the forest floors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westerling_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-westerling-132">[132]</a></sup> Warmer temperatures cause dehydration of these materials, which prevents <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> from soaking up and dampening fires.  Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> from wildfires can exacerbate climate change by releasing atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>, which modify <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> patterns.
</p><p>Wood smoke from wildfires produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a> that has damaging effects to human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naeher_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-naeher-133">[133]</a></sup>  The primary pollutants in wood smoke are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westerling_132-2"><a href="#cite_note-westerling-132">[132]</a></sup>  Through the destruction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">forests</a> and human-designed <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> smoke releases other toxic and carcinogenic compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein2_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-epstein2-134">[134]</a></sup>  These pollutants damage human health by evading the <a href="/wiki/Mucociliary_clearance" title="Mucociliary clearance">mucociliary clearance</a> system and depositing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_respiratory_tract" title="Upper respiratory tract">upper respiratory tract</a>, where they exert toxic effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westerling_132-3"><a href="#cite_note-westerling-132">[132]</a></sup> Research by Naeher and colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naeher_133-1"><a href="#cite_note-naeher-133">[133]</a></sup> found that physician visits for respiratory diseases increased by 45-80% during wildfire activity in urban British Columbia.
</p><p>The health effects of wildfire smoke exposure include exacerbation and development of respiratory illness such as <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disorder" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder">chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder</a>; increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesothelioma" title="Mesothelioma">mesothelioma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>; increased airway hyper-responsiveness; changes in levels of inflammatory mediators and coafulation factors; and <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract_infection" title="Respiratory tract infection">respiratory tract infection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naeher_133-2"><a href="#cite_note-naeher-133">[133]</a></sup>  It may also have intrauterine effects on fetal development, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Low_birth_weight" title="Low birth weight">low birth weight</a> newborns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>  Because wildfire smoke travels and is often not isolated to a single geographic region, the health effects are widespread among populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epstein2_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-epstein2-134">[134]</a></sup>
The suppression of wild fires also takes up a large amount of a country’s <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> which directly affects the country’s economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellison_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-ellison-136">[136]</a></sup>  In the United States, it was reported that approximately $6 million was spent between 2004-2008 to suppress wildfires in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellison_136-1"><a href="#cite_note-ellison-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Displacement.2Fmigration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Displacement/migration">Displacement/migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Displacement/migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">Environmental migrant</a></div>
<p>Climate change causes displacement of people in several ways, the most obvious—and dramatic—being through the increased number and severity of weather-related disasters which destroy homes and habitats causing people to seek shelter or livelihoods elsewhere. Slow onset phenomena, including effects of climate change such as <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rising_sea_levels" title="Rising sea levels">rising sea levels</a> gradually erode livelihoods and force communities to abandon traditional homelands for more accommodating environments. This is currently happening in areas of Africa’s <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>, the semi-arid belt that spans the continent just below its northern deserts. Deteriorating environments triggered by climate change can also lead to increased conflict over resources which in turn can displace people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p><p>Extreme environmental events are increasingly recognized as a key driver of migration across the world. According to the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, more than 42 million people were displaced in Asia and the Pacific during 2010 and 2011, more than twice the population of Sri Lanka. This figure includes those displaced by storms, floods, and heat and cold waves. Still others were displaced drought and sea-level rise. Most of those compelled to leave their homes eventually returned when conditions improved, but an undetermined number became migrants, usually within their country, but also across national borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogumil_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-bogumil-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p><p>Asia and the Pacific is the global area most prone to natural disasters, both in terms of the absolute number of disasters and of populations affected. It is highly exposed to climate impacts, and is home to highly vulnerable population groups, who are disproportionately poor and marginalized. A recent Asian Development Bank report highlights “environmental hot spots” that are particular risk of flooding, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a>, and water stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beta_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-beta-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>To reduce migration compelled by worsening environmental conditions, and to strengthen resilience of at-risk communities, governments should adopt policies and commit financing to social protection, livelihoods development, basic urban infrastructure
development, and disaster risk management. Though every effort should be made to ensure that people can stay where they live, it is also important to recognize that migration can also be a way for people to cope with environmental changes. If properly managed, and efforts made to protect the rights of migrants, migration can provide substantial benefits to both origin and destination areas, as well as to the migrants themselves. However, migrants – particularly low-skilled ones – are among the most vulnerable people in society and are often denied basic protections and access to services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beta_139-1"><a href="#cite_note-beta-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>The links between the gradual environmental degradation of climate change and displacement are complex: as the decision to migrate is taken at the household level, it is difficult to measure the respective influence of climate change in these decisions with regard to other influencing factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a> options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bogumil_138-1"><a href="#cite_note-bogumil-138">[138]</a></sup> This situates the debate on <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">environmental migration</a> in a highly contested field: the use of the term 'environmental refugee', although commonly used in some contexts, is disrecommended by agencies such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNHCR" title="UNHCR">UNHCR</a> who argue that the term 'refugee' has a strict legal definition which does not apply to environmental migrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNHCR_on_environmental_migration_terminology_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNHCR_on_environmental_migration_terminology-140">[140]</a></sup> Neither the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="UN Framework Convention on Climate Change">UN Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> nor the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, an international agreement on climate change, includes any provisions concerning specific assistance or protection for those who will be directly affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p>
