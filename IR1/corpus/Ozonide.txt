<p><b>Ozonide</b> is the unstable, reactive <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> analog of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> or any of several classes of <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxide</a> compounds similar formed by the reaction of ozone with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsaturated_compound" title="Unsaturated compound">unsaturated compound</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionic_ozonides">Ionic ozonides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozonide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ionic ozonides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inorganic ozonides<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> are dark red <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a> containing the reactive <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> anion. The anion has the <a href="/wiki/Bent_molecular_geometry" title="Bent molecular geometry">bent shape</a> of the ozone molecule.
</p><p>Inorganic ozonides are formed by burning <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, or by treating the <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a> hydroxide with ozone; if potassium is left undisturbed in air for years it accumulates a covering of superoxide and ozonide. They are very sensitive explosives that have to be handled at low temperatures in an atmosphere consisting of an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert gas</a>. Lithium and sodium ozonide are extremely unstable and must be prepared by low-temperature ion exchange starting from CsO<sub>3</sub>. Sodium ozonide, <span class="chemf nowrap">NaO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which is prone to decomposition into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_Hydroxide" title="Sodium Hydroxide">NaOH</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_superoxide" title="Sodium superoxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">NaO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a>, was previously thought to be impossible to obtain in pure form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, with the help of <a href="/wiki/Cryptand" title="Cryptand">cryptands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methylamine" title="Methylamine">methylamine</a>, pure <span class="chemf nowrap">NaO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> may be obtained as red crystals isostructural to <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite"><span class="chemf nowrap">NaNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ionic ozonides are being investigated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as sources of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oxygen_generator" title="Chemical oxygen generator">chemical oxygen generators</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tetramethylammonium" title="Tetramethylammonium">Tetramethylammonium</a> ozonide, which can be made by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metathesis_reaction" title="Metathesis reaction">metathesis reaction</a> with caesium ozonide in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_ammonia" title="Liquid ammonia">liquid ammonia</a>, is stable up to 348K:
</p>
<dl><dd>CsO<sub>3</sub> + [(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>N][O<sub>2</sub>] → CsO<sub>2</sub> + [(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>N][O<sub>3</sub>]</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Covalent_singly_bonded_structures">Covalent singly bonded structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozonide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Covalent singly bonded structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phosphite ozonides, (RO)<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub>, are used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Singlet_oxygen" title="Singlet oxygen">singlet oxygen</a>. They are made by ozonizing a <a href="/wiki/Phosphite_ester" title="Phosphite ester">phosphite ester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> at low temperatures, and decompose to yield singlet oxygen and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphate_ester" title="Phosphate ester">phosphate ester</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>(RO)<sub>3</sub>P + O<sub>3</sub> → (RO)<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub></li>
<li>(RO)<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub> → (RO)<sub>3</sub>PO + <sup>1</sup>O<sub>2</sub></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molozonides">Molozonides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozonide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Molozonides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Organic ozonides are called <a href="/wiki/Molozonide" title="Molozonide">molozonides</a> and are typically formed by the addition reaction between ozone and <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. They are more explosive cousins of the <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxides</a> and as such are rarely isolated during the course of the ozonolysis reaction sequence. Molozonides are unstable and rapidly convert to the <b>trioxolane</b> ring structure with a five-membered C–O–O–C–O ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They usually appear in the form of foul-smelling oily liquids, and rapidly decompose in the presence of water to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> compounds: <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxides</a>.
</p>
