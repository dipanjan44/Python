<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Overdrafting</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about groundwater extraction. For financial uses of the term, see <a href="/wiki/Overdraft" title="Overdraft">Overdraft</a>.</div>
<p><b>Overdrafting</b> is the process of extracting <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> beyond the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> yield of the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>.
</p><p>There are two sets of yields, safe yield and sustainable yield. Safe yield is the amount of water that can be taken out of the ground without there being any undesirable results. Sustainable yield is extraction that takes into account both recharge rate and surface water impacts
</p><p>There are two types of aquifers. The first is confined aquifers. This is when the aquifer has an overbearing layer called aquitard, which contains materials that does not allow for penetration for groundwater extraction. The second is unconfined aquifers. This is when the aquifer does not have a aquitard over its layer and the groundwater is able to be penetrated for extraction. Extracting groundwater from unconfined aquifers is like borrowing the water, it has to be recharged at a proper amount. If recharge is not done so in proper amounts there can be many impacts. Recharge may happen through artificial recharge and natural recharge..<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural process of recharge is done through percolation of surface water. Artificial process of recharging the aquifer is through means of pumping reclaimed water from wastewater management projects directly into the aquifer. An example is the Orange County Water District in the State of California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This organization take waste water, treats it to a proper level, and then systematically pumps it back into the aquifers for artificial recharge.
</p><p>When groundwater is extracted the water is primarily pulled from the aquifer which creates a cone depression around the well. When drafting of water continues the cone of depression increases in width. The increase in width leads to the negative impacts caused by overdrafting. Impacts include aiding in the drop of water table, land subsidence, and loss of surface water reaching the streams. In extream cases the supply of water to naturally recharge the aquifers is pulled directly from streams and rivers, leading to depletion of water levels in streams and rivers. The depletion of water in rivers and streams has an effect on not only the wildlife, but also humans who might be using the water for other purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since every groundwater basin <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">recharges</a> at a different rate depending upon <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetative_cover" title="Vegetative cover">vegetative cover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a> practises, the quantity of groundwater that can be safely pumped varies greatly among regions of the world and even within provinces.  Some aquifers require a very long time to recharge and thus the process of overdrafting can have consequences of effectively drying up certain sub-surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_supplies" title="Water supplies">water supplies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Groundwater-related_subsidence" title="Groundwater-related subsidence">Subsidence</a> occurs when excessive groundwater is extracted from rocks that support more weight when saturated. This can lead to a capacity reduction in the aquifer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">Groundwater</a> is the fresh water that can be found underground; it is also one of the largest sources. Groundwater depletion can be comparable to ¨money in a bank¨,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The primary cause of groundwater depletion is pumping or the excessive pulling up of groundwater from underground aquifers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accelerated_decline_in_subterranean_reservoirs">Accelerated decline in subterranean reservoirs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overdrafting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Accelerated decline in subterranean reservoirs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a 2013 report by research hydrologist, Leonard F. Konikow,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KonikowUSGCjan2013_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-KonikowUSGCjan2013-8">[8]</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (USGS), the depletion of the <a href="/wiki/Ogallala_Aquifer" title="Ogallala Aquifer">Ogallala Aquifer</a> between 2001–2008, inclusive, is about 32 percent of the cumulative depletion during the entire 20th century (Konikow 2013:22)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KonikowUSGCjan2013_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-KonikowUSGCjan2013-8">[8]</a></sup> In the United States, the biggest users of water from aquifers include agricultural <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_drilling" title="Oil drilling">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal extraction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-depletion_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-depletion-9">[9]</a></sup>"Cumulative total groundwater depletion in the United States accelerated in the late 1940s and continued at an almost steady linear rate through the end of the century. In addition to widely recognized environmental consequences, groundwater depletion also adversely impacts the long-term sustainability of groundwater supplies to help meet the Nation’s water needs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KonikowUSGCjan2013_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-KonikowUSGCjan2013-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to another USGS study of withdrawals from 66 major US aquifers, the three greatest uses of water extracted from aquifers were <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> (<a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>) (68%), public <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> (19%), and self-supplied industrial (4%). The remaining about 8% of groundwater withdrawals were for “self-supplied domestic, <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">thermoelectric power</a> uses.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_on_the_environment">Impacts on the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overdrafting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impacts on the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> of overdrafting includes:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_subsidence" title="Land subsidence">Land subsidence</a> - the collapse of land from lack of support (from the water that is being depleted). The first recorded case of land subsidence was in the 1940s. Land subsidence can be as little as a local land collapsing or as large as an entire region's land being lowerd. The subsidence can lead to infrastructural and ecosystem damage.</li>
<li>Lowering of the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a>, which makes water harder to reach streams and rivers.</li>
<li>Reduction of water volume in streams and lakes because their supply of water is being diminished by surface water recharging the aquifers.
<ul><li>Animals that depend on streams and lakes for food and water and <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> will be affected</li></ul></li>
<li>Deteriorating <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_prize" title="Water prize">cost of water</a> to the consumer rises. This is due to the water table lowering so more energy is needed to pump further down, and to cover that pumping companies need more profit.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">Crop production decrease</a> from lack of water( 60% of US irrigation relies on groundwater so this is a large loss)</li>
<li>Groundwater depletion also throws off the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_climate">Effects on climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overdrafting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects on climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aquifer <a href="/wiki/Drawdown_(hydrology)" title="Drawdown (hydrology)">drawdown</a> or overdrafting and the pumping of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_water" title="Fossil water">fossil water</a> may be a contributing factor to sea-level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By increasing the amount of <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> available to fall as precipitation, severe weather events are more likely to occur. To some extent moisture in the atmosphere accelerates the probability of a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> event. The <a href="/wiki/Correlation_coefficient" title="Correlation coefficient">correlation coefficient</a> is not yet scientifically determined.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Socio-economic_effects">Socio-economic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overdrafting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Socio-economic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scores of countries are overpumping aquifers as they struggle to satisfy their growing water needs, including each of the big three <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> producers— <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. These three, along with a number of other countries where water tables are falling, are home to more than half the world’s people.
</p><p>Water is intrinsic to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_growth" title="Biological growth">biological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, and overdraft limits its available supply. According to <a href="/wiki/Liebig%27s_law_of_the_minimum" title="Liebig's law of the minimum">Liebig's law of the minimum</a>, growth is therefore impeded. Deeper <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a> must be <a href="/wiki/Well_drilling" title="Well drilling">drilled</a> as the water table drops, which can become expensive. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy needed</a> to extract a given volume of water increases with the amount the aquifer has been depleted. The deeper the water is extracted from the worse the quality of the water becomes, which increases the cost of filtration. <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_intrusion" title="Saltwater intrusion">Saltwater intrusion</a> is another consequence of overdrafting, leading to a reduction in <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_solutions">Possible solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overdrafting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Possible solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Artificial <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">recharge</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup>- Since recharge is the natural replenishment of water, <i>artificial</i> recharge is the artificial or man made replenishment of groundwater. This is the more aesthetically pleasing option though there is a limited amount of water that can be used for replenishing which is a problem.</li>
<li>Decreased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">water use</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup> - This can be used in combination with the solution above. Consumptive use refers to the water that is naturally taken from the system (Ex; <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> ). So decreasing that use is what needs to happen in areas where recharge alone will not work. It requires such things as switching to less water-intensive crops.</li></ul>
