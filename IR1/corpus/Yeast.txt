<p><b>Yeasts</b> are <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">single-celled</a> <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> classified as members of the <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a>. The first yeast originated hundreds of millions of years ago, and 1,500 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are currently identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid26447128_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid26447128-3">[3]</a></sup> They are estimated to constitute 1% of all described fungal species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurtzman2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurtzman2-4">[4]</a></sup> Yeasts are unicellular organisms which evolved from multicellular ancestors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yong_2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yong_2012-5">[5]</a></sup> with some species having the ability to develop <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_organism" title="Multicellular organism">multicellular</a> characteristics by forming strings of connected budding cells known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudohyphae" title="Pseudohyphae">pseudohyphae</a> or false hyphae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurtzman1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurtzman1-6">[6]</a></sup> Yeast sizes vary greatly, depending on species and environment, typically measuring 3–4 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a> in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, although some yeasts can grow to 40 µm in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-7">[7]</a></sup> Most yeasts reproduce <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexually</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>, and many do so by the asymmetric division process known as <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a>.
</p><p>Yeasts, with their single-celled growth habit, can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">molds</a>, which grow <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphae</a>. Fungal species that can take both forms (depending on temperature or other conditions) are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimorphic_fungi" title="Dimorphic fungi">dimorphic fungi</a> ("dimorphic" means "having two forms").
</p><p>By <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a>, the yeast species <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> converts <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohols</a> – for thousands of years the carbon dioxide has been used in <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a> and the alcohol in alcoholic beverages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legras2007_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legras2007-8">[8]</a></sup> It is also a centrally important <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a> research, and is one of the most thoroughly researched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> microorganisms. Researchers have used it to gather information about the biology of the eukaryotic cell and ultimately human biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ostergaard_2000_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ostergaard_2000-9">[9]</a></sup> Other species of yeasts, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">Candida albicans</a></i>, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunistic_pathogen" title="Opportunistic pathogen">opportunistic pathogens</a> and can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_infection" title="Yeast infection">infections</a> in humans. Yeasts have recently been used to generate electricity in <a href="/wiki/Microbial_fuel_cell" title="Microbial fuel cell">microbial fuel cells</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef3-10">[10]</a></sup> and produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> industry.
</p><p>Yeasts do not form a single <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> grouping. The term "yeast" is often taken as a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a> for <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurtzman1994_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurtzman1994-11">[11]</a></sup> but the phylogenetic diversity of yeasts is shown by their placement in two separate <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phyla</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Ascomycota" title="Ascomycota">Ascomycota</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Basidiomycota" title="Basidiomycota">Basidiomycota</a>. The budding yeasts ("true yeasts") are classified in the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Saccharomycetales" title="Saccharomycetales">Saccharomycetales</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef2-12">[12]</a></sup> within the phylum Ascomycota.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_wine" title="History of wine">History of wine</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_beer" title="History of beer">History of beer</a></div>
<p>The word "yeast" comes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i>gist</i>, <i>gyst</i>, and from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto_Indo-European_language" title="Proto Indo-European language">Indo-European</a> root <i>yes-</i>, meaning "boil", "foam", or "bubble".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bartleby2000_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bartleby2000-13">[13]</a></sup> Yeast microbes are probably one of the earliest domesticated organisms. Archaeologists digging in Egyptian ruins found early grinding stones and baking chambers for yeast-raised bread, as well as drawings of 4,000-year-old bakeries and breweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1680, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_(ethnic_group)" title="Dutch (ethnic group)">Dutch</a> naturalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anton_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Anton van Leeuwenhoek">Anton van Leeuwenhoek</a> first <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopically</a> observed yeast, but at the time did not consider them to be <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">living organisms</a>, but rather globular structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef11_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef11-15">[15]</a></sup> Researchers were doubtful whether yeasts were algae or fungi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ainsworth_1976_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ainsworth_1976-16">[16]</a></sup> but in 1837 <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Theodor Schwann</a> recognized them as fungi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwann_1837_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwann_1837-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett_2004_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett_2004-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1857, French microbiologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> proved in the paper "<i>Mémoire sur la fermentation alcoolique</i>" that alcoholic fermentation was conducted by living yeasts and not by a chemical catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett2003_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett2003-19">[19]</a></sup> Pasteur showed that by bubbling oxygen into the yeast broth, <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">cell growth</a> could be increased, but fermentation was inhibited – an observation later called the "<a href="/wiki/Pasteur_effect" title="Pasteur effect">Pasteur effect</a>".
</p><p>By the late 18th century, two yeast strains used in brewing had been identified: <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> (top-fermenting yeast) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_carlsbergensis" title="Saccharomyces carlsbergensis">S. carlsbergensis</a></i> (bottom-fermenting yeast). <i>S. cerevisiae</i> has been sold commercially by the Dutch for bread-making since 1780; while, around 1800, the Germans started producing <i>S. cerevisiae</i> in the form of cream. In 1825, a method was developed to remove the liquid so the yeast could be prepared as solid blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klieger2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klieger2004-20">[20]</a></sup> The industrial production of yeast blocks was enhanced by the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Filter_press" title="Filter press">filter press</a> in 1867. In 1872, Baron Max de Springer developed a manufacturing process to create granulated yeast, a technique that was used until the first World War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlLe_Comité_des_Fabricants_de_levure —_COFALEC —_représente_les_fabricants_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlLe_Comité_des_Fabricants_de_levure_—_COFALEC_—_représente_les_fabricants-21">[21]</a></sup> In the United States, naturally occurring airborne yeasts were used almost exclusively until commercial yeast was marketed at the <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Exposition" title="Centennial Exposition">Centennial Exposition</a> in 1876 in Philadelphia, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_L._Fleischmann" title="Charles L. Fleischmann">Charles L. Fleischmann</a> exhibited the product and a process to use it, as well as serving the resultant baked bread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snodgrass2004_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Snodgrass2004-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition_and_growth">Nutrition and growth</span></h2>
<p>Yeasts are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoorganotroph" title="Chemoorganotroph">chemoorganotrophs</a>, as they use <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> as a source of energy and do not require sunlight to grow. Carbon is obtained mostly from <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">hexose</a> sugars, such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, or disaccharides such as <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a>. Some species can metabolize <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">pentose</a> sugars such as ribose,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett1975_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett1975-23">[23]</a></sup> alcohols, and <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid" title="Organic acid">organic acids</a>. Yeast species either require oxygen for aerobic <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a> (<a href="/wiki/Obligate_aerobe" title="Obligate aerobe">obligate aerobes</a>) or are anaerobic, but also have aerobic methods of energy production (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobe" title="Facultative anaerobe">facultative anaerobes</a>). Unlike <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, no known yeast species grow only anaerobically (<a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">obligate anaerobes</a>). Most yeasts grow best in a neutral or slightly acidic pH environment.
</p><p>Yeasts vary in regard to the temperature range in which they grow best. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Leucosporidium_frigidum" title="Leucosporidium frigidum">Leucosporidium frigidum</a></i> grows at −2 to 20 °C (28 to 68 °F), <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_telluris" title="Saccharomyces telluris">Saccharomyces telluris</a></i> at 5 to 35 °C (41 to 95 °F), and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Candida_slooffi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Candida slooffi (page does not exist)">Candida slooffi</a></i> at 28 to 45 °C (82 to 113 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arthur1976_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arthur1976-24">[24]</a></sup> The cells can survive freezing under certain conditions, with viability decreasing over time.
</p><p>In general, yeasts are grown in the laboratory on solid <a href="/wiki/Growth_medium" title="Growth medium">growth media</a> or in liquid <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">broths</a>. Common media used for the cultivation of yeasts include <a href="/wiki/Potato_dextrose_agar" title="Potato dextrose agar">potato dextrose agar</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potato_dextrose_broth" title="Potato dextrose broth">potato dextrose broth</a>, Wallerstein Laboratories nutrient <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a>, yeast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptone" title="Peptone">peptone</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrose" title="Dextrose">dextrose</a> agar, and yeast mould agar or broth. Home brewers who cultivate yeast frequently use dried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malt_extract" title="Malt extract">malt extract</a> and agar as a solid growth medium. The <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cycloheximide" title="Cycloheximide">cycloheximide</a> is sometimes added to yeast growth media to inhibit the growth of <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces" title="Saccharomyces">Saccharomyces</a></i> yeasts and select for wild/indigenous yeast species. This will change the yeast process.
</p><p>The appearance of a white, thready yeast, commonly known as kahm yeast, is often a byproduct of the lactofermentation (or pickling) of certain vegetables, usually the result of exposure to air. Although harmless, it can give pickled vegetables a bad flavor and must be removed regularly during fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaufmann2002_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaufmann2002-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span></h2>
<p>Yeasts are very common in the environment, and are often isolated from sugar-rich materials. Examples include naturally occurring yeasts on the skins of fruits and berries (such as grapes, apples, or <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peaches</a>), and exudates from plants (such as plant saps or cacti). Some yeasts are found in association with soil and insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suh2005_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suh2005-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slavikova2003_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slavikova2003-27">[27]</a></sup> The ecological function and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> of yeasts are relatively unknown compared to those of other <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herrera2002_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera2002-28">[28]</a></sup> Yeasts, including <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">Candida albicans</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodotorula" title="Rhodotorula">Rhodotorula</a> rubra</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torulopsis" title="Torulopsis">Torulopsis</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trichosporon_cutaneum" title="Trichosporon cutaneum">Trichosporon cutaneum</a></i>, have been found living in between people's toes as part of their <a href="/wiki/Skin_flora" title="Skin flora">skin flora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oyeka2002_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oyeka2002-29">[29]</a></sup> Yeasts are also present in the <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a> of mammals and some insects<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martini1992_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martini1992-30">[30]</a></sup> and even deep-sea environments host an array of yeasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bass2007_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bass2007-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>An Indian study of seven <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">bee</a> species and 9 plant species found 45 species from 16 genera colonise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nectaries" title="Nectaries">nectaries</a> of flowers and honey stomachs of bees. Most were members of the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candida_(genus)" title="Candida (genus)">Candida</a></i>; the most common species in honey stomachs was <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dekkera_intermedia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dekkera intermedia (page does not exist)">Dekkera intermedia</a></i> and in flower nectaries, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_blankii" title="Candida blankii">Candida blankii</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandhu1985_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sandhu1985-33">[33]</a></sup> Yeast colonising nectaries of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stinking_hellebore" title="Stinking hellebore">stinking hellebore</a> have been found to raise the temperature of the flower, which may aid in attracting pollinators by increasing the evaporation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herrera2002_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera2002-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barley2010_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barley2010-34">[34]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Black_yeast" title="Black yeast">black yeast</a> has been recorded as a partner in a complex relationship between <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant-fungus_mutualism" title="Ant-fungus mutualism">mutualistic fungus</a>, a fungal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasite</a> of the fungus and a bacterium that kills the parasite. The yeast has a negative effect on the bacteria that normally produce antibiotics to kill the parasite, so may affect the ants' health by allowing the parasite to spread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Little2008_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Little2008-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain strains of some species of yeasts produce proteins called yeast killer toxins that allow them to eliminate competing strains. (See main article on <a href="/wiki/Killer_yeast" title="Killer yeast">killer yeast</a>.) This can cause problems for winemaking but could potentially also be used to advantage by using killer toxin-producing strains to make the wine. Yeast killer toxins may also have medical applications in treating yeast infections (see "Pathogenic yeasts" section below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magliani_2005_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magliani_2005-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_lifecycle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="698" data-file-width="1029" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Yeast_lifecycle.svg/250px-Yeast_lifecycle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Yeast_lifecycle.svg/375px-Yeast_lifecycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Yeast_lifecycle.svg/500px-Yeast_lifecycle.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_lifecycle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The yeast cell's life cycle: <div><ol><li>Budding</li><li>Conjugation</li><li>Spore</li></ol></div></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mating_of_yeast" title="Mating of yeast">Mating of yeast</a></div>
<p>Yeasts, like all fungi, may have <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual</a> reproductive cycles. The most common mode of vegetative growth in yeast is asexual reproduction by <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balasubramanian_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balasubramanian-37">[37]</a></sup> Here, a small bud (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Bleb_(cell_biology)" title="Bleb (cell biology)">bleb</a>), or daughter cell, is formed on the parent cell. The <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a> of the parent cell splits into a daughter nucleus and migrates into the daughter cell. The bud continues to grow until it separates from the parent cell, forming a new cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yeong2005_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yeong2005-38">[38]</a></sup> The daughter cell produced during the budding process is generally smaller than the mother cell. Some yeasts, including <i><a href="/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces_pombe" title="Schizosaccharomyces pombe">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</a></i>, reproduce by <a href="/wiki/Fission_(biology)" title="Fission (biology)">fission</a> instead of budding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balasubramanian_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balasubramanian-37">[37]</a></sup> thereby creating two identically sized daughter cells.
</p><p>In general, under high-stress conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> starvation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> cells will die; under the same conditions, however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> cells can undergo sporulation, entering sexual reproduction (<a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>) and producing a variety of haploid <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a>, which can go on to <a href="/wiki/Mating_of_yeast" title="Mating of yeast">mate</a> (conjugate), reforming the diploid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neiman2005_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neiman2005-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The haploid fission yeast <i>Schizosaccharomyces pombe</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Facultative" title="Facultative">facultative</a> sexual microorganism that can undergo mating when nutrients are limiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid26447128_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid26447128-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davey_1998_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davey_1998-40">[40]</a></sup> Exposure of <i>S. pombe</i> to hydrogen peroxide, an agent that causes oxidative stress leading to oxidative DNA damage, strongly induces mating and the formation of meiotic spores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_1989_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein_1989-41">[41]</a></sup> The budding yeast <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> reproduces by mitosis as diploid cells when nutrients are abundant, but when starved, this yeast undergoes meiosis to form haploid spores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herskowitz_1988_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herskowitz_1988-42">[42]</a></sup> Haploid cells may then reproduce asexually by mitosis. Katz Ezov et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz_2010_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katz_2010-43">[43]</a></sup> presented evidence that in natural <i>S. cerevisiae</i> populations clonal reproduction and selfing (in the form of intratetrad mating) predominate. In nature, mating of haploid cells to form diploid cells is most often between members of the same clonal population and <a href="/wiki/Outcrossing" title="Outcrossing">out-crossing</a> is uncommon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruderfer_2006_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruderfer_2006-44">[44]</a></sup> Analysis of the ancestry of natural <i>S. cerevisiae</i> strains led to the conclusion that out-crossing occurs only about once every 50,000 cell divisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruderfer_2006_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruderfer_2006-44">[44]</a></sup> These observations suggest that the possible long-term benefits of outcrossing (e.g. generation of diversity) are likely to be insufficient for generally maintaining sex from one generation to the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernstein_2013_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernstein_2013-45">[45]</a></sup> Rather, a short-term benefit, such as recombinational repair during meiosis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birdsell_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birdsell-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> may be the key to the maintenance of sex in <i>S. cerevisiae</i>.
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Pucciniomycotina" title="Pucciniomycotina">pucciniomycete</a> yeasts, in particular species of <i><a href="/wiki/Sporidiobolus" title="Sporidiobolus">Sporidiobolus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sporobolomyces" title="Sporobolomyces">Sporobolomyces</a></i>, produce aerially dispersed, asexual <a href="/wiki/Ballistospore" title="Ballistospore">ballistoconidia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bai2002_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bai2002-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<p>The useful physiological properties of yeast have led to their use in the field of <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">Fermentation</a> of sugars by yeast is the oldest and largest application of this technology. Many types of yeasts are used for making many foods: <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">baker's yeast</a> in bread production, brewer's yeast in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer_fermentation" title="Beer fermentation">beer fermentation</a>, and yeast in wine fermentation and for <a href="/wiki/Xylitol" title="Xylitol">xylitol</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen_2010_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen_2010-49">[49]</a></sup> So-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_rice_yeast" title="Red rice yeast">red rice yeast</a> is actually a <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monascus_purpureus" title="Monascus purpureus">Monascus purpureus</a></i>. Yeasts include some of the most widely used <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organisms</a> for <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Botstein_2011_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Botstein_2011-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcoholic_beverages">Alcoholic beverages</span></h3>
<p>Alcoholic beverages are defined as <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">beverages</a> that contain <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH). This ethanol is almost always produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> by certain species of yeasts under anaerobic or low-oxygen conditions. Beverages such as mead, wine, beer, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">distilled spirits</a> all use yeast at some stage of their production. A distilled beverage is a beverage containing ethanol that has been purified by <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>. Carbohydrate-containing plant material is fermented by yeast, producing a dilute solution of ethanol in the process. Spirits such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiskey" title="Whiskey">whiskey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a> are prepared by distilling these dilute solutions of ethanol. Components other than ethanol are collected in the condensate, including water, <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, and other alcohols, which (in addition to that provided by the oak in which it may be aged) account for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavour" title="Flavour">flavour</a> of the beverage.
</p><p><span id="top-fermenting"></span><span id="bottom-fermenting"></span>

<p>Brewing yeasts may be classed as "top-cropping" (or "top-fermenting") and "bottom-cropping" (or "bottom-fermenting").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Top-cropping yeasts are so called because they form a foam at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> during fermentation. An example of a top-cropping yeast is <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, sometimes called an "ale yeast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson2010_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson2010-52">[52]</a></sup> Bottom-cropping yeasts are typically used to produce <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">lager</a>-type beers, though they can also produce <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a>-type beers. These yeasts ferment well at low temperatures. An example of bottom-cropping yeast is <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></i>, formerly known as <i>S. carlsbergensis</i>.
</p><p>Decades ago,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (March 2018)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> taxonomists reclassified <i>S. carlsbergensis</i> (uvarum) as a member of <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, noting that the only distinct difference between the two is metabolic. Lager strains of <i>S. cerevisiae</i> secrete an enzyme called melibiase, allowing them to hydrolyse <a href="/wiki/Melibiose" title="Melibiose">melibiose</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a>, into more fermentable <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a>. Top- and bottom-cropping and cold- and warm-fermenting distinctions are largely generalizations used by laypersons to communicate to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common top-cropping brewer's yeast, <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, is the same species as the common baking yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmendolaRees2002_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmendolaRees2002-54">[54]</a></sup> Brewer's yeast is also very rich in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_mineral" title="Essential mineral">essential minerals</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_vitamin" title="B vitamin">B vitamins</a> (except B<sub>12</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlUMMC_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlUMMC-55">[55]</a></sup> However, baking and brewing yeasts typically belong to different strains, cultivated to favour different characteristics: baking yeast strains are more aggressive, to carbonate <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">dough</a> in the shortest amount of time possible; brewing yeast strains act slower but tend to produce fewer off-flavours and tolerate higher alcohol concentrations (with some strains, up to 22%).
</p><p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dekkera/Brettanomyces" title="Dekkera/Brettanomyces">Dekkera/Brettanomyces</a></i> is a genus of yeast known for its important role in the production of '<a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">lambic</a>' and specialty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sour_ale" title="Sour ale">sour ales</a>, along with the secondary conditioning of a particular Belgian <a href="/wiki/Trappist_beer" title="Trappist beer">Trappist beer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanderhaegen2003_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanderhaegen2003-56">[56]</a></sup> The taxonomy of the genus <i>Brettanomyces</i> has been debated since its early discovery and has seen many reclassifications over the years. Early classification was based on a few species that reproduced asexually (anamorph form) through multipolar budding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Custers1940_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Custers1940-57">[57]</a></sup> Shortly after, the formation of ascospores was observed and the genus <i>Dekkera</i>, which reproduces sexually (teleomorph form), was introduced as part of the taxonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VanderWalt1984_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-VanderWalt1984-58">[58]</a></sup> The current taxonomy includes five species within the genera of <i>Dekkera/Brettanomyces</i>. Those are the anamorphs <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_bruxellensis" title="Brettanomyces bruxellensis">Brettanomyces bruxellensis</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_anomalus" title="Brettanomyces anomalus">Brettanomyces anomalus</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brettanomyces_custersianus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brettanomyces custersianus (page does not exist)">Brettanomyces custersianus</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brettanomyces_naardenensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brettanomyces naardenensis (page does not exist)">Brettanomyces naardenensis</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brettanomyces_nanus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brettanomyces nanus (page does not exist)">Brettanomyces nanus</a></i>, with teleomorphs existing for the first two species, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dekkera_bruxellensis" title="Dekkera bruxellensis">Dekkera bruxellensis</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dekkera_anomala&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dekkera anomala (page does not exist)">Dekkera anomala</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oelofse2008_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oelofse2008-59">[59]</a></sup> The distinction between <i>Dekkera</i> and <i>Brettanomyces</i> is arguable, with Oelofse et al. (2008) citing Loureiro and Malfeito-Ferreira from 2006 when they affirmed that current molecular DNA detection techniques have uncovered no variance between the anamorph and teleomorph states. Over the past decade, <i>Brettanomyces</i> spp. have seen an increasing use in the craft-brewing sector of the industry, with a handful of breweries having produced beers that were primarily fermented with pure cultures of <i>Brettanomyces</i> spp. This has occurred out of experimentation, as very little information exists regarding pure culture fermentative capabilities and the aromatic compounds produced by various strains. <i>Dekkera</i>/<i>Brettanomyces</i> spp. have been the subjects of numerous studies conducted over the past century, although a majority of the recent research has focused on enhancing the knowledge of the wine industry. Recent research on eight <i>Brettanomyces</i> strains available in the brewing industry focused on strain-specific fermentations and identified the major compounds produced during pure culture anaerobic fermentation in wort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yakobson2010_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yakobson2010-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine">Wine</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Yeast_in_winemaking" title="Yeast in winemaking">Yeast in winemaking</a></div>
<p>Yeast is used in <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">winemaking</a>, where it converts the sugars present (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Grape_juice" title="Grape juice">grape juice</a> (<a href="/wiki/Must" title="Must">must</a>) into ethanol. Yeast is normally already present on grape skins. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">Fermentation</a> can be done with this endogenous "wild yeast",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ross_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ross-61">[61]</a></sup> but this procedure gives unpredictable results, which depend upon the exact types of yeast species present. For this reason, a pure yeast culture is usually added to the must; this yeast quickly dominates the fermentation. The wild yeasts are repressed, which ensures a reliable and predictable fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most added wine yeasts are strains of <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, though not all strains of the species are suitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-62">[62]</a></sup> Different <i>S. cerevisiae</i> yeast strains have differing physiological and fermentative properties, therefore the actual strain of yeast selected can have a direct impact on the finished wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn-63">[63]</a></sup> Significant research has been undertaken into the development of novel wine yeast strains that produce atypical flavour profiles or increased complexity in wines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef14_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef14-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBryde_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBryde-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>The growth of some yeasts, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Zygosaccharomyces" title="Zygosaccharomyces">Zygosaccharomyces</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces" title="Brettanomyces">Brettanomyces</a></i>, in wine can result in <a href="/wiki/Wine_fault" title="Wine fault">wine faults</a> and subsequent spoilage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loureiro2003_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loureiro2003-66">[66]</a></sup> <i>Brettanomyces</i> produces an array of <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolites</a> when growing in wine, some of which are volatile <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenolic</a> compounds. Together, these compounds are often referred to as "<i>Brettanomyces</i> character", and are often described as "<a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a>" or "barnyard" type aromas. <i>Brettanomyces</i> is a significant contributor to wine faults within the wine industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef15_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef15-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a>, Canada, have found a new strain of yeast that has reduced <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>. The amines in <a href="/wiki/Wine_color" title="Wine color">red wine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">Chardonnay</a> produce off-flavors and cause headaches and hypertension in some people. About 30% of people are sensitive to biogenic amines, such as <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baking">Baking</span></h3>

Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(April 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">Baker's yeast</a></div>
<p>Yeast, the most common one being <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, is used in baking as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a>, where it converts the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">food</a>/fermentable sugars present in dough into the gas <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. This causes the dough to expand or rise as gas forms pockets or bubbles. When the dough is baked, the yeast dies and the air pockets "set", giving the baked product a soft and spongy texture. The use of potatoes, water from potato boiling, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, or sugar in a bread dough accelerates the growth of yeasts. Most yeasts used in baking are of the same species common in alcoholic fermentation. In addition, <i>Saccharomyces exiguus</i> (also known as <i>S. minor</i>), a wild yeast found on plants, fruits, and grains, is occasionally used for baking. In breadmaking, the yeast initially respires aerobically, producing carbon dioxide and water. When the oxygen is depleted, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> begins, producing ethanol as a waste product; however, this evaporates during baking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreLandecker1996p533-534_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-MooreLandecker1996p533-534-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1330" data-file-width="2000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/220px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/330px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/440px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A block of compressed fresh yeast</div></div></div>
<p>It is not known when yeast was first used to bake bread. The first records that show this use came from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legras2007_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Legras2007-8">[8]</a></sup> Researchers speculate a mixture of flour meal and water was left longer than usual on a warm day and the yeasts that occur in natural contaminants of the <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> caused it to ferment before baking. The resulting bread would have been lighter and tastier than the normal flat, hard cake.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_yeast.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/220px-Dry_yeast.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/330px-Dry_yeast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/440px-Dry_yeast.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_yeast.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Active dried yeast, a granulated form in which yeast is commercially sold</div></div></div>
<p>Today, there are several retailers of baker's yeast; one of the earlier developments in North America is <a href="/wiki/Fleischmann%27s_Yeast" title="Fleischmann's Yeast">Fleischmann's Yeast</a>, in 1868. During World War II, Fleischmann's developed a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/granulate" title="wikt:granulate">granulated</a> active dry yeast which did not require refrigeration, had a longer <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a> than fresh yeast, and rose twice as fast. Baker's yeast is also sold as a fresh yeast compressed into a square "cake". This form perishes quickly, so must be used soon after production. A weak solution of water and sugar can be used to determine whether yeast is expired. In the solution, active yeast will foam and bubble as it ferments the sugar into ethanol and carbon dioxide. Some recipes refer to this as <a href="/wiki/Proofing_(baking_technique)" title="Proofing (baking technique)">proofing</a> the yeast, as it "proves" (tests) the viability of the yeast before the other ingredients are added. When a <a href="/wiki/Sourdough" title="Sourdough">sourdough</a> starter is used, flour and water are added instead of sugar; this is referred to as proofing the <a href="/wiki/Sponge_and_dough" title="Sponge and dough">sponge</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="'Reliable sources needed for the whole paragraph, also maybe some irrelevant information for an WP article' (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>When yeast is used for making bread, it is mixed with <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>, salt, and warm water or milk. The dough is <a href="/wiki/Kneading" title="Kneading">kneaded</a> until it is smooth, and then left to rise, sometimes until it has doubled in size. The dough is then shaped into loaves. Some bread doughs are knocked back after one rising and left to rise again (this is called <a href="/wiki/Proofing_(baking_technique)" title="Proofing (baking technique)">dough proofing</a>) and then baked. A longer rising time gives a better flavour, but the yeast can fail to raise the bread in the final stages if it is left for too long initially.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioremediation">Bioremediation</span></h3>
<p>Some yeasts can find potential application in the field of <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a>. One such yeast, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarrowia_lipolytica" title="Yarrowia lipolytica">Yarrowia lipolytica</a></i>, is known to degrade <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> mill <a href="/wiki/Effluent" title="Effluent">effluent</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> (an explosive material), and other <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>, fats and oils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zinjard_2014_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zinjard_2014-70">[70]</a></sup> It can also tolerate high concentrations of salt and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bankar2009rev_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bankar2009rev-71">[71]</a></sup> and is being investigated for its potential as a heavy metal <a href="/wiki/Biosorption" title="Biosorption">biosorbent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bankar2009_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bankar2009-72">[72]</a></sup> <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> has potential to bioremediate toxic pollutants like <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> from industrial effluent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soares_2012_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soares_2012-73">[73]</a></sup> Bronze statues are known to be degraded by certain species of yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cappitelli_2008_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cappitelli_2008-74">[74]</a></sup> Different yeasts from Brazilian gold mines <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulate</a> free and <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">complexed</a> silver ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_2006_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh_2006-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_ethanol_production">Industrial ethanol production</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel#Bioalcohols" title="Biofuel">Bioethanol</a></div>
<p>The ability of yeast to convert sugar into ethanol has been harnessed by the biotechnology industry to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>. The process starts by milling a feedstock, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_corn" title="Field corn">field corn</a>, or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereal_grain" title="Cereal grain">cereal grains</a>, and then adding dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, or fungal alpha <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a> enzymes, to break down the starches into complex sugars. A glucoamylase is then added to break the complex sugars down into simple sugars. After this, yeasts are added to convert the simple sugars to ethanol, which is then distilled off to obtain ethanol up to 96% in purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef4_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef4-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Saccharomyces</i> yeasts have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically_engineered" title="Genetically engineered">genetically engineered</a> to ferment <a href="/wiki/Xylose" title="Xylose">xylose</a>, one of the major fermentable sugars present in <a href="/wiki/Lignocellulosic_biomass" title="Lignocellulosic biomass">cellulosic biomasses</a>, such as agriculture residues, paper wastes, and wood chips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEM_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-AEM-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ho1998_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ho1998-78">[78]</a></sup> Such a development means ethanol can be efficiently produced from more inexpensive feedstocks, making <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a> fuel a more competitively priced alternative to gasoline fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madhavan_2012_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madhavan_2012-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonalcoholic_beverages">Nonalcoholic beverages</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kombucha_jar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1298" data-file-width="1146" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Kombucha_jar.jpg/220px-Kombucha_jar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Kombucha_jar.jpg/330px-Kombucha_jar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Kombucha_jar.jpg/440px-Kombucha_jar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kombucha_jar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <i><a href="/wiki/Kombucha" title="Kombucha">kombucha</a></i> culture fermenting in a jar</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kombuchacultsm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="424" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Kombuchacultsm.jpg/220px-Kombuchacultsm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Kombuchacultsm.jpg/330px-Kombuchacultsm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Kombuchacultsm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kombuchacultsm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yeast and bacteria in kombucha at 400×</div></div></div>
<p>A number of sweet <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">carbonated beverages</a> can be produced by the same methods as beer, except the fermentation is stopped sooner, producing carbon dioxide, but only trace amounts of alcohol, leaving a significant amount of residual sugar in the drink.
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">Root beer</a></b>, originally made by Native Americans, commercialized in the United States by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Elmer_Hires" title="Charles Elmer Hires">Charles Elmer Hires</a> and especially popular during <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kvass" title="Kvass">Kvass</a></b>, a fermented drink made from <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, popular in Eastern Europe. It has a recognizable, but low alcoholic content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_2013_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2013-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kombucha" title="Kombucha">Kombucha</a></b>, a fermented sweetened tea. Yeast in <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a> with <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid_bacteria" title="Acetic acid bacteria">acetic acid bacteria</a> is used in its preparation. Species of yeasts found in the tea can vary, and may include: <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_bruxellensis" title="Brettanomyces bruxellensis">Brettanomyces bruxellensis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_stellata" title="Candida stellata">Candida stellata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces_pombe" title="Schizosaccharomyces pombe">Schizosaccharomyces pombe</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Torulaspora_delbrueckii" title="Torulaspora delbrueckii">Torulaspora delbrueckii</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Zygosaccharomyces_bailii" title="Zygosaccharomyces bailii">Zygosaccharomyces bailii</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teoh2004_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Teoh2004-81">[81]</a></sup> Also popular in Eastern Europe and some <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">former Soviet republics</a> under the name <i>chajnyj grib</i> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Чайный гриб</span>), which means "tea mushroom".</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kefir" title="Kefir">Kefir</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Kumis" title="Kumis">kumis</a></b> are made by fermenting milk with yeast and bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Oliveira_Leite_2013_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Oliveira_Leite_2013-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mauby" title="Mauby">Mauby</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">mabí</i>), made by fermenting sugar with the wild yeasts naturally present on the bark of the <i><a href="/wiki/Colubrina_elliptica" title="Colubrina elliptica">Colubrina elliptica</a></i> tree, popular in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_supplements">Nutritional supplements</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tibicos" title="Tibicos">Tibicos</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Advert" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>contains content that is written like <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_a_soapbox_or_means_of_promotion" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">an advertisement</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Yeast&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by removing <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Spam" title="Wikipedia:Spam">promotional content</a> and inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:External_links" title="Wikipedia:External links">external links</a>, and by adding encyclopedic content written from a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutral point of view</a>.</span> <small><i>(October 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Yeast is used in nutritional supplements, especially those marketed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegan" title="Vegan">vegans</a>. It is often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Nutritional_yeast" title="Nutritional yeast">nutritional yeast</a>" when sold as a dietary supplement. Nutritional yeast is a deactivated yeast, usually <i>S.  cerevisiae</i>. It is naturally low in fat and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> as well as an excellent source of protein and vitamins, especially most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_B" title="Vitamin B">B-complex</a> vitamins<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thaler_2014_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thaler_2014-83">[83]</a></sup> (contrary to some claims, it contains little or no vitamin B<sub>12</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlUMMC_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-urlUMMC-55">[55]</a></sup> ), as well as other minerals and <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a> required for growth. Some brands of nutritional yeast, though not all, are fortified with <a href="/wiki/Cyanocobalamin" title="Cyanocobalamin">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a>, which is produced separately by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duyff_2012_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duyff_2012-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1920, the <a href="/wiki/Fleischmann%27s_Yeast" title="Fleischmann's Yeast">Fleischmann Yeast Company</a> began to promote yeast cakes in a "Yeast for Health" campaign. They initially emphasized yeast as a source of vitamins, good for skin and digestion. Their later advertising claimed a much broader range of health benefits, and was censured as misleading by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Fad" title="Fad">fad</a> for yeast cakes lasted until the late 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yeast_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yeast-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nutritional yeast has a nutty, cheesy flavor and is often used as an ingredient in cheese substitutes. Another popular use is as a topping for popcorn. It can also be used in mashed and fried potatoes, as well as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrambled_egg" title="Scrambled egg">scrambled eggs</a>. It comes in the form of flakes, or as a yellow powder similar in texture to <a href="/wiki/Cornmeal" title="Cornmeal">cornmeal</a>. In Australia, it is sometimes sold as "savoury yeast flakes". Though "nutritional yeast" usually refers to commercial products, inadequately fed prisoners have used "home-grown" yeast to prevent vitamin deficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef8_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef8-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Probiotics">Probiotics</span></h3>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Probiotic" title="Probiotic">probiotic</a> supplements use the yeast <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_boulardii" title="Saccharomyces boulardii">S. boulardii</a></i> to maintain and restore the natural flora in the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>. <i>S. boulardii</i> has been shown to reduce the symptoms of acute <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinleyici_2012_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dinleyici_2012-87">[87]</a></sup> reduce the chance of infection by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clostridium_difficile_(bacteria)" title="Clostridium difficile (bacteria)">Clostridium difficile</a></i> (often identified simply as C. difficile or C. diff),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_2012_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson_2012-88">[88]</a></sup> reduce bowel movements in diarrhea-predominant <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">IBS</a> patients,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dai_2013_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dai_2013-89">[89]</a></sup> and reduce the incidence of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic-associated_diarrhea" title="Antibiotic-associated diarrhea">antibiotic</a>-, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Travelers%27_diarrhea" title="Travelers' diarrhea">traveler's</a>-, and <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>-associated diarrheas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McFarland_2010_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-McFarland_2010-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquarium_hobby">Aquarium hobby</span></h3>
<p>Yeast is often used by <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquarium</a> hobbyists to generate carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) to nourish plants in <a href="/wiki/Aquascaping" title="Aquascaping">planted aquaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedersen2007_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pedersen2007-91">[91]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> levels from yeast are more difficult to regulate than those from pressurized CO<sub>2</sub> systems. However, the low cost of yeast makes it a widely used alternative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedersen2007_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pedersen2007-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Yeast extract is the common name for various forms of processed yeast products that are used as <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additives</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavour" title="Flavour">flavours</a>. They are often used in the same way that <a href="/wiki/Monosodium_glutamate" title="Monosodium glutamate">monosodium glutamate</a> (MSG) is used and, like MSG, often contain free <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The general method for making yeast extract for food products such as <a href="/wiki/Vegemite" title="Vegemite">Vegemite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marmite" title="Marmite">Marmite</a> on a commercial scale is to add salt to a suspension of yeast, making the solution hypertonic, which leads to the cells' shrivelling up. This triggers <a href="/wiki/Autolysis_(biology)" title="Autolysis (biology)">autolysis</a>, wherein the yeast's <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestive</a> enzymes break their own <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> down into simpler compounds, a process of self-destruction. The dying yeast cells are then heated to complete their breakdown, after which the husks (yeast with thick cell walls that would give poor texture) are separated. Yeast autolysates are used in <a href="/wiki/Vegemite" title="Vegemite">Vegemite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Promite" title="Promite">Promite</a> (Australia); <a href="/wiki/Marmite" title="Marmite">Marmite</a> (the United Kingdom); the unrelated <a href="/wiki/Marmite_(New_Zealand)" title="Marmite (New Zealand)">Marmite</a> (New Zealand); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitam-R" title="Vitam-R">Vitam-R</a> (Germany); and <a href="/wiki/Cenovis" title="Cenovis">Cenovis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_research">Scientific research</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_cell_english.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="296" data-file-width="382" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yeast_cell_english.svg/220px-Yeast_cell_english.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yeast_cell_english.svg/330px-Yeast_cell_english.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Yeast_cell_english.svg/440px-Yeast_cell_english.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_cell_english.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing a yeast cell</div></div></div>
<p>Several yeasts, in particular <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S._cerevisiae" title="S. cerevisiae">S. cerevisiae</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S._pombe" title="S. pombe">S. pombe</a></i>, have been widely used in genetics and cell biology, largely because they are simple <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> cells, serving as a model for all eukaryotes, including humans, for the study of fundamental cellular processes such as the <a href="/wiki/Cell_cycle" title="Cell cycle">cell cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">DNA replication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a>, and metabolism. Also, yeasts are easily manipulated and cultured in the laboratory, which has allowed for the development of powerful standard techniques, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_two-hybrid" title="Yeast two-hybrid">yeast two-hybrid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brückner_2009_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brückner_2009-93">[93]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_genetic_array" title="Synthetic genetic array">synthetic genetic array</a> analysis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tong_2006_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tong_2006-94">[94]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrad_(genetics)" title="Tetrad (genetics)">tetrad analysis</a>. Many proteins important in human biology were first discovered by studying their <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologues</a> in yeast; these proteins include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_cycle_protein" title="Cell cycle protein">cell cycle proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">signaling proteins</a>, and protein-processing <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conn_2007_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conn_2007-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 24 April 1996, <i>S. cerevisiae</i> was announced to be the first eukaryote to have its <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>, consisting of 12 million <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairs</a>, fully sequenced as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genome_Project" title="Genome Project">Genome Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams1996_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams1996-96">[96]</a></sup> At the time, it was the most complex organism to have its full genome sequenced, and the work seven years and the involvement of more than 100 laboratories to accomplish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-accessexcellence_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-accessexcellence-97">[97]</a></sup> The second yeast species to have its genome sequenced was <i>Schizosaccharomyces pombe</i>, which was completed in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood2002_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood2002-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinert2002_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reinert2002-99">[99]</a></sup> It was the sixth eukaryotic genome sequenced and consists of 13.8 million base pairs. As of 2014, over 50 yeast species have had their genomes sequenced and published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Piskur_2014_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Piskur_2014-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>Genomic and functional gene annotation of the two major yeast models can be accessed via their respective <a href="/wiki/Model_organism_databases" title="Model organism databases">model organism databases</a>: <a class="external text" href="https://www.yeastgenome.org/" rel="nofollow">SGD</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.pombase.org" rel="nofollow">PomBase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_engineered_biofactories">Genetically engineered biofactories</span></h3>
<p>Various yeast species have been genetically engineered to efficiently produce various drugs, a technique called <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_engineering" title="Metabolic engineering">metabolic engineering</a>. <i>S. cerevisiae</i> is easy to genetically engineer; its physiology, metabolism and genetics are well known, and it is amenable for use in harsh industrial conditions. A wide variety of chemical in different classes can be produced by engineered yeast, including <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenolics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoprenoid" title="Isoprenoid">isoprenoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polyketide" title="Polyketide">polyketides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqui_2012_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqui_2012-103">[103]</a></sup> About 20% of <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceuticals</a> are produced in <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, including <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_serum_albumin" title="Human serum albumin">human serum albumin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nielsen_2012_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nielsen_2012-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogenic_yeasts">Pathogenic yeasts</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C_albicans_en.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/C_albicans_en.jpg/250px-C_albicans_en.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/C_albicans_en.jpg/375px-C_albicans_en.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/C_albicans_en.jpg/500px-C_albicans_en.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C_albicans_en.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photomicrograph" title="Photomicrograph">photomicrograph</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">Candida albicans</a></i> showing hyphal outgrowth and other morphological characteristics</div></div></div>
<p>Some species of yeast are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunistic_pathogen" title="Opportunistic pathogen">opportunistic pathogens</a> that can cause infection in people with compromised <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune systems</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptococcus_neoformans" title="Cryptococcus neoformans">Cryptococcus neoformans</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptococcus_gattii" title="Cryptococcus gattii">Cryptococcus gattii</a></i> are significant pathogens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunocompromised" title="Immunocompromised">immunocompromised</a> people. They are the species primarily responsible for <a href="/wiki/Cryptococcosis" title="Cryptococcosis">cryptococcosis</a>, a fungal disease that occurs in about one million <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> patients, causing over 600,000 deaths annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cogliati_2013_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cogliati_2013-105">[105]</a></sup> The cells of these yeast are surrounded by a rigid <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a> capsule, which helps to prevent them from being recognised and engulfed by <a href="/wiki/White_blood_cell" title="White blood cell">white blood cells</a> in the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Meara_2012_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Meara_2012-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>Yeasts of the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candida_(genus)" title="Candida (genus)">Candida</a></i>, another group of opportunistic pathogens, cause <a href="/wiki/Oral_candidiasis" title="Oral candidiasis">oral</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaginal_infection" title="Vaginal infection">vaginal infections</a> in humans, known as <a href="/wiki/Candidiasis" title="Candidiasis">candidiasis</a>. <i>Candida</i> is commonly found as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commensal" title="Commensal">commensal</a> yeast in the <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a> of humans and other warm-blooded animals. However, sometimes these same strains can become pathogenic. The yeast cells sprout a <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphal</a> outgrowth, which locally penetrates the <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucosal membrane</a>, causing irritation and shedding of the tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YeastRef9_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-YeastRef9-107">[107]</a></sup> The pathogenic yeasts of candidiasis in probable descending order of <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a> for humans are: <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_albicans" title="Candida albicans">C. albicans</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_tropicalis" title="Candida tropicalis">C. tropicalis</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candida_stellatoidea" title="Candida stellatoidea">C. stellatoidea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_glabrata" title="Candida glabrata">C. glabrata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_krusei" title="Candida krusei">C. krusei</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_parapsilosis" title="Candida parapsilosis">C. parapsilosis</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candida_guilliermondii" title="Candida guilliermondii">C. guilliermondii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_viswanathii" title="Candida viswanathii">C. viswanathii</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Candida_lusitaniae" title="Candida lusitaniae">C. lusitaniae</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodotorula" title="Rhodotorula">Rhodotorula mucilaginosa</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurley1987_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurley1987-108">[108]</a></sup> <i>Candida glabrata</i> is the second most common <i>Candida</i> pathogen after <i>C. albicans</i>, causing infections of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urogenital_tract" title="Urogenital tract">urogenital tract</a>, and of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodstream" title="Bloodstream">bloodstream</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candidemia" title="Candidemia">candidemia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunke_2013_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brunke_2013-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_spoilage">Food spoilage</span></h2>
<p>Yeasts are able to grow in foods with a low pH (5.0 or lower) and in the presence of sugars, organic acids, and other easily metabolized carbon sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurtzman3_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurtzman3-110">[110]</a></sup> During their growth, yeasts metabolize some food components and produce metabolic end products. This causes the physical, chemical, and sensible properties of a food to change, and the food is spoiled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleet_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fleet-111">[111]</a></sup> The growth of yeast within food products is often seen on their surfaces, as in cheeses or meats, or by the fermentation of sugars in beverages, such as juices, and semiliquid products, such as <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrups</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jam" title="Jam">jams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurtzman3_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kurtzman3-110">[110]</a></sup> The yeast of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Zygosaccharomyces" title="Zygosaccharomyces">Zygosaccharomyces</a></i> have had a long history as spoilage yeasts within the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>. This is mainly because these species can grow in the presence of high sucrose, ethanol, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorbic_acid" title="Sorbic acid">sorbic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzoic_acid" title="Benzoic acid">benzoic acid</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> concentrations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loureiro2003_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loureiro2003-66">[66]</a></sup> representing some of the commonly used <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">food preservation</a> methods. <a href="/wiki/Methylene_blue" title="Methylene blue">Methylene blue</a> is used to test for the presence of live yeast cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downes2001_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Downes2001-112">[112]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Oenology" title="Oenology">oenology</a>, the major spoilage yeast is <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_bruxellensis" title="Brettanomyces bruxellensis">Brettanomyces bruxellensis</a></i>.
</p>
