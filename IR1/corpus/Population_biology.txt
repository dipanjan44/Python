<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Population biology</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Population biology</b> is an interdisciplinary field combining the areas of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Population biology draws on tools from <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Systematics" title="Systematics">systematics</a>. Population biologists study allele frequency changes (<a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>) within populations of the same species (<a href="/wiki/Population_genetics" title="Population genetics">population genetics</a>), and interactions between populations of different species (<a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>).
</p>
