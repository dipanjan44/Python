<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Halocarbon</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Halocarbon</b> compounds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a> in which one or more <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> are linked by <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> with one or more <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> – <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group</a> 17</span>) resulting in the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organofluorine_compound" title="Organofluorine compound">organofluorine compounds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organochlorine_compound" title="Organochlorine compound">organochlorine compounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organobromine_compound" title="Organobromine compound">organobromine compounds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organoiodine_compound" title="Organoiodine compound">organoiodine compounds</a>. Chlorine halocarbons are the most common and are called <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">organochlorides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many synthetic organic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymers" title="Polymers">polymers</a>, and a few natural ones, contain halogen atoms; they are known as <i>halogenated</i> compounds or <i>organohalogens</i>. Organochlorides are the most common industrially used organohalides, although the other organohalides are used commonly in organic synthesis. Except for extremely rare cases, organohalides are not produced biologically, but many pharmaceuticals are organohalides. Notably, many pharmaceuticals such as <a href="/wiki/Fluoxetine" title="Fluoxetine">Prozac</a> have trifluoromethyl groups.
</p><p>For information on inorganic halide chemistry, see <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halide</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_families">Chemical families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Organohalogen-chlorides.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="127" data-file-width="491" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Organohalogen-chlorides.png/300px-Organohalogen-chlorides.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Organohalogen-chlorides.png/450px-Organohalogen-chlorides.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Organohalogen-chlorides.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Organohalogen-chlorides.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Examples of organohalogens-chlorides</div></div></div>
<p>Halocarbons are typically classified in the same ways as the similarly <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">structured</a> <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> that have <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> occupying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular" title="Molecular">molecular</a> sites of the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> in halocarbons. Among the chemical families are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">haloalkanes</a>—compounds with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> linked by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">single bonds</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haloalkene" title="Haloalkene">haloalkenes</a>—compounds with one or more <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a> between <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haloaromatic" title="Haloaromatic">haloaromatics</a>—compounds with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbons</a> linked in one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_ring" title="Aromatic ring">aromatic rings</a> with a delocalised donut shaped pi cloud.</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> in halocarbon <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> are often called "<a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituents</a>," as though those atoms had been substituted for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms. However halocarbons are prepared in many ways that do not involve direct substitution of <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogens</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_context">History and context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few halocarbons are produced in massive amounts by microorganisms. For example, several million tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_bromide" title="Methyl bromide">methyl bromide</a> are estimated to be produced by marine organisms annually. Most of the halocarbons encountered in everyday life – solvents, medicines, plastics – are man-made. The first synthesis of halocarbons was achieved in the early 1800s. Production began accelerating when their useful properties as solvents and anesthetics were discovered. Development of plastics and synthetic elastomers has led to greatly expanded scale of production. A substantial percentage of drugs are halocarbons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_halocarbons">Natural halocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural halocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A large amount of the naturally occurring halocarbons are created by wood fire, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxine" title="Dioxine">dioxine</a> for example, or volcanic activities. A second large source are marine algae which produce several chlorinated <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> containing compounds. There are several thousand complex halocarbons known, produced mainly by marine species. Although chlorine compounds are the majority of the discovered compounds, bromides, iodides and fluorides have also been found. The <a href="/wiki/Tyrian_purple" title="Tyrian purple">tyrian purple</a>, which is a dibromoindigo, is representative of the bromides, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroxine" title="Thyroxine">thyroxine</a> secreted from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_gland" title="Thyroid gland">thyroid gland</a>, is an iodide, and the highly toxic <a href="/wiki/Fluoroacetic_acid" title="Fluoroacetic acid">fluoroacetate</a> is one of the rare organofluorides. These three representatives, thyroxine from humans, tyrian purple from snails and fluoroacetate from plants, also show that unrelated species use halocarbons for several purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble98_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble98-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble99_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble99-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble02_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble02-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Organoiodine_compounds.2C_including_biological_derivatives"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Organoiodine_compounds,_including_biological_derivatives">Organoiodine compounds, including biological derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organoiodine compounds, including biological derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoiodine_compounds" title="Organoiodine compounds">Organoiodine compounds</a></div>
<p>Organoiodine compounds, called <b>organic iodides</b>, are similar in structure to organochlorine and organobromine compounds, but the C-I bond is weaker. Many organic iodides are known, but few are of major industrial importance. Iodide compounds are mainly produced as nutritional supplements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroxin" title="Thyroxin">thyroxin</a> hormones are essential for human health, hence the usefulness of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodized_salt" title="Iodized salt">iodized salt</a>.
</p><p>Six mg of iodide a day can be used to treat patients with <a href="/wiki/Hyperthyroidism" title="Hyperthyroidism">hyperthyroidism</a> due to its ability to inhibit the organification process in thyroid hormone synthesis, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolff-Chaikoff_effect" title="Wolff-Chaikoff effect">Wolff-Chaikoff effect</a>. Prior to 1940, iodides were the predominant antithyroid agents. In large doses, iodides inhibit <a href="/wiki/Proteolysis" title="Proteolysis">proteolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thyroglobulin" title="Thyroglobulin">thyroglobulin</a>, which permits TH to be synthesized and stored in <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloid</a>, but not released into the bloodstream.
</p><p>This treatment is seldom used today as a stand-alone therapy despite the rapid improvement of patients immediately following administration. The major disadvantage of iodide treatment lies in the fact that excessive stores of TH accumulate, slowing the onset of action of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thioamides" title="Thioamides">thioamides</a> (TH synthesis blockers). In addition, the functionality of iodides fades after the initial treatment period. An "escape from block" is also a concern, as extra stored TH may spike following discontinuation of treatment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first halocarbon commercially used was <a href="/wiki/Tyrian_purple" title="Tyrian purple">Tyrian purple</a> a natural organobromide of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murex_brandaris" title="Murex brandaris">Murex brandaris</a></i> marine snail.
</p><p>Common uses for halocarbons have been as <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>, fire-resistant oils, ingredients of <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a> and sealants, electrically insulating coatings, <a href="/wiki/Plasticizer" title="Plasticizer">plasticizers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>. Many halocarbons have specialized uses in industry. One halocarbon, <a href="/wiki/Sucralose" title="Sucralose">sucralose</a>, is a sweetener.
</p><p>Before they became strictly regulated, the general public often encountered <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">haloalkanes</a> as paint and cleaning solvents such as <a href="/wiki/1,1,1-Trichloroethane" title="1,1,1-Trichloroethane">trichloroethane</a> (1,1,1-trichloroethane) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> (tetrachloromethane), pesticides like <a href="/wiki/1,2-Dibromoethane" title="1,2-Dibromoethane">1,2-dibromoethane</a> (EDB, ethylene dibromide), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerants" title="Refrigerants">refrigerants</a> like <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a>-22 (<a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">duPont</a> trademark for chlorodifluoromethane). Some haloalkanes are still widely used for industrial cleaning, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylene_chloride" title="Methylene chloride">methylene chloride</a> (dichloromethane), and as refrigerants, such as R-134a (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane" title="1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane">1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane</a>).
</p><p>Haloalkenes have also been used as <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perchloroethylene" title="Perchloroethylene">perchloroethylene</a> (Perc, tetrachloroethene), widespread in dry cleaning, and <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> (TCE, 1,1,2-trichloroethene). Other haloalkenes have been chemical building blocks of plastics such as <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> ("vinyl" or PVC, polymerized chloroethene) and Teflon (<a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">duPont</a> trademark for polymerized tetrafluoroethene, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PTFE" title="PTFE">PTFE</a>).
</p><p>Haloaromatics include the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aroclor" title="Aroclor">Aroclors</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_Company" title="Monsanto Company">Monsanto Company</a> trademark for <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a>, PCBs), once widely used in power transformers and capacitors and in building caulk, the former <a href="/wiki/Halowax" title="Halowax">Halowaxes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Union_Carbide" title="Union Carbide">Union Carbide</a> trademark for <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_naphthalene" title="Polychlorinated naphthalene">polychlorinated naphthalenes</a>, PCNs), once used for electrical insulation, and the <a href="/wiki/Chlorobenzene" title="Chlorobenzene">chlorobenzenes</a> and their derivatives, used for <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfectants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> such as dichloro-diphenyl-trichloroethane (<a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>, 1,1,1-trichloro-2,2-bis(p-chlorophenyl)ethane), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicides" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4-D" title="2,4-D">2,4-D</a> (2,4-dichlorophenoxyacetic acid), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Askarel" title="Askarel">askarel</a> <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectrics</a> (mixed with PCBs, no longer used in most countries), and chemical feedstocks.
</p><p>A few halocarbons, including acid halides like <a href="/wiki/Acetyl_chloride" title="Acetyl chloride">acetyl chloride</a>, are highly <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactive</a>; these are rarely found outside chemical processing. The widespread uses of halocarbons were often driven by observations that most of them were more stable than other substances. They may be less affected by acids or alkalis; they may not burn as readily; they may not be attacked by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">molds</a>; or they may not be affected as much by sun exposure.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stability of halocarbons tended to encourage beliefs that they were mostly harmless, although in the mid-1920s physicians reported workers in <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_naphthalene" title="Polychlorinated naphthalene">polychlorinated naphthalene</a> manufacturing suffering from <a href="/wiki/Chloracne" title="Chloracne">chloracne</a> (<a href="#CITEREFTeleky1927">Teleky 1927</a>), and by the late 1930s it was known that workers exposed to <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_naphthalene" title="Polychlorinated naphthalene">PCNs</a> could die from <a href="/wiki/Liver_disease" title="Liver disease">liver disease</a> (<a href="#CITEREFFlinnJarvik1936">Flinn &amp; Jarvik 1936</a>) and that <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> would kill <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitos</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> (<a href="#CITEREFMüller1948">Müller 1948</a>). By the 1950s, there had been several reports and investigations of workplace hazards. In 1956, for example, after testing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a> oils containing <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">PCBs</a>, the U.S. Navy found that skin contact caused fatal <a href="/wiki/Liver_disease" title="Liver disease">liver disease</a> in animals and rejected them as "too toxic for use in a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a>" (<a href="#CITEREFOwens_v._Monsanto2001">Owens v. Monsanto 2001</a>).
</p><p>In 1962 a book by U.S. biologist <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a> (<a href="#CITEREFCarson1962">Carson 1962</a>) started a storm of concerns about environmental <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, first focused on <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, some of them also halocarbons. These concerns were amplified when in 1966 Swedish chemist Soren Jensen reported widespread residues of <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">PCBs</a> among Arctic and sub-Arctic fish and birds (<a href="#CITEREFJensen1966">Jensen 1966</a>). In 1974, Mexican chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mario_Molina" title="Mario Molina">Mario Molina</a> and U.S. chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sherwood_Rowland" title="Sherwood Rowland">Sherwood Rowland</a> predicted that common halocarbon <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs), would accumulate in the upper <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and destroy protective <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> (<a href="#CITEREFMolinaRowland1974">Molina &amp; Rowland 1974</a>). Within a few years, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> depletion was being observed above <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, leading to bans on production and use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> in many countries. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) said halocarbons were a direct cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1970s there have been longstanding, unresolved controversies over potential health hazards of <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> (TCE) and other halocarbon <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> that had been widely used for industrial cleaning (<a href="#CITEREFAnderson_v._Grace1986">Anderson v. Grace 1986</a>) (<a href="#CITEREFScottCogliano2000">Scott &amp; Cogliano 2000</a>) (<a href="#CITEREFU.S._National_Academies_of_Science2004">U.S. National Academies of Science 2004</a>) (<a href="#CITEREFUnited_States2004">United States 2004</a>). More recently <a href="/wiki/Perfluorooctanoic_acid" title="Perfluorooctanoic acid">perfluorooctanoic acid</a> (PFOA), a precursor in the most common manufacturing process for Teflon and also used to make coatings for fabrics and food packaging, became a health and environmental concern starting in 2006 (<a href="#CITEREFUnited_States2010_(begun_in_2006)">United States &amp; 2010 (begun in 2006)</a>), suggesting that halocarbons, though thought to be among the most inert, may also present hazards.
</p><p>Halocarbons, including those that might not be hazards in themselves, can present <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">waste disposal</a> issues. Because they do not readily degrade in natural environments, halocarbons tend to accumulate. <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">Incineration</a> and accidental fires can create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> byproducts such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisons</a> like halogenated <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furans</a>. Species of Desulfitobacterium are being investigated for their potential in the <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> of halogenic organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
