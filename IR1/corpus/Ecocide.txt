<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecocide</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Ecocide</b>, or <b>ecocatastrophe</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><i>,</i> is the extensive damage to, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystem_destruction" title="Ecosystem destruction">destruction</a> of or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss">loss</a> of ecosystem(s) of a given territory, whether by human agency or by other, to such an extent that peaceful enjoyment by the inhabitants of that territory has been or will be severely diminished. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_ecocide">Anthropogenic ecocide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic ecocide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term ecocide is more recently used to refer to the destructive <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">impact of humanity on its own natural environment</a>.  As a group of complex organisms we are committing ecocide through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsustainable" title="Unsustainable">unsustainable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_exploitation" title="Resource exploitation">exploitation of the planet's resources</a>. The geological era we are living in, known as the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">anthropocene</a>, is so named because the activities of the human species are influencing the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">natural state</a> in a way never seen before. The most notable example is that of the atmosphere which is being transformed through the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">emission of gases</a> from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> use : <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a> etc. The ecocide we are witnessing is a symptom of the disregard and reward for accounting for the damage being caused. U.S. environmental theorist and activist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Patrick_Hossay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Patrick Hossay (page does not exist)">Patrick Hossay</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unsustainable:_A_Primer_for_Global_Environmental_and_Social_Justice_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unsustainable:_A_Primer_for_Global_Environmental_and_Social_Justice-2">[2]</a></sup> argues that the human species is committing ecocide, via industrial civilization's effects on the global environment. Much of the modern environmental movement stems from this belief as a precept.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecocide_as_a_proposed_international_crime">Ecocide as a proposed international crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecocide as a proposed international crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of ecocide as an international <a href="/wiki/Environmental_crime" title="Environmental crime">crime</a> originated in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010 it was proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Rome_Statute_of_the_International_Criminal_Court" title="Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court">Rome Statute</a> be amended to include the international crime of Ecocide. The proposal was submitted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_International_Law_Commission" title="United Nations International Law Commission">United Nations International Law Commission</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> who are '<b>‘'</b>mandated to promote the progressive development of international law and its codification’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The definition proposed includes provisions for both individual and state responsibility and would be a strict liability crime(including both intent and negligence). It would create a duty of care in events of naturally occurring ecocide as well as creating criminal responsibility for human caused ecocide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severity_of_environmental_harm">Severity of environmental harm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Severity of environmental harm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To be considered an ecocide under the proposed law, an environmental harm would need to be widespread, long lasting or severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This is the parameter based disjunctive test, as already set out under the 1977 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_Environmental_Modification" title="United Nations Convention on Environmental Modification">United Nations Convention on Environmental Modification</a>, which specifies the terms ‘widespread’, ‘long-lasting’ or ‘severe’ as: 
</p>
<ol><li><i>widespread: encompassing an area on the scale of several hundred square kilometers; and/or</i></li>
<li><i>long-lasting: lasting for a period of months, or approximately a season; and/or</i></li>
<li><i>severe: involving serious or significant disruption or harm to human life, natural and economic resources or other assets.</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="620" data-file-width="620" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ambox_wikify.svg/40px-Ambox_wikify.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ambox_wikify.svg/60px-Ambox_wikify.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ambox_wikify.svg/80px-Ambox_wikify.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs more <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Linking" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Linking">links to other articles</a> to help <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:BUILD" title="Wikipedia:BUILD">integrate it into the encyclopedia</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by adding links <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Only_make_links_that_are_relevant_to_the_context" title="Wikipedia:Only make links that are relevant to the context">that are relevant to the context</a> within the existing text.</span> <small><i>(June 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><i>1970</i>
</p><p>The word was recorded at the Conference on War and National Responsibility in Washington 1970, where <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Galston" title="Arthur Galston">Arthur Galston</a> proposed a new international agreement to ban ecocide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Galston was a US biologist who identified the defoliant effects of a chemical later developed into Agent Orange. Subsequently a bioethicist, he was the first in 1970 to name massive damage and destruction of ecosystems as an ecocide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In an <i><a href="/wiki/Obiter_dictum" title="Obiter dictum">obiter dictum</a></i> in the 1970 Barcelona Traction case judgement, the International Court of Justice identified a category of international obligations called <i><a href="/wiki/Erga_omnes" title="Erga omnes">erga omnes</a></i>, namely obligations owed by states to the international community as a whole, intended to protect and promote the basic values and common interests of all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1972</i>
</p><p>In 1972 at the United Nations <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stockholm_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="Stockholm Conference on the Human Environment">Stockholm Conference on the Human Environment</a> which adopted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stockholm_Declaration" title="Stockholm Declaration">Stockholm Declaration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olof_Palme" title="Olof Palme">Olof Palme</a> the Prime Minister of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, in his opening speech spoke explicitly of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnam_war" title="Vietnam war">Vietnam war</a> as an ecocide and it was discussed in the unofficial events running parallel to the official UN Stockholm Conference on Human Environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folkrorelser_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-folkrorelser-12">[12]</a></sup> Others, including Indira Gandhi from India and Tang Ke, the leader of the Chinese delegation, also denounced the war in human and environmental terms. They too called for ecocide to be an international crime<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. A Working Group on Crimes Against the Environment was formed at the conference, and a draft Ecocide Convention was submitted into the United Nations in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dai Dong, a branch of the International Fellowship of Reconciliation sponsored a Convention on Ecocidal War which took place in Stockholm, Sweden. The Convention brought together many people including experts Richard A. Falk, expert on the international law of war crimes and Robert Jay Lifton, a psychohistorian. The Convention called for a United Nations Convention on Ecocidal Warfare, which would amongst other matters seek to define and condemn ecocide as an international crime of war. Richard A. Falk drafted an Ecocide Convention in 1973, explicitly stating at the outset to recognise "that man has consciously and unconsciously inflicted irreparable damage to the environment in times of war and peace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Westing's view was that the element of intent did not always apply. "Intent may not only be impossible to establish without admission but, I believe, it is essentially irrelevant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1978</i>
</p><p>Draft Code of Crimes Against the Peace and Security of Mankind discussions commence. At the same time, State responsibility and international crimes are discussed and drafted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ILC 1978 Yearbook's 'Draft articles on State Responsibility and International Crime' included: "an international crime (which) may result, inter alia, from: (d) a serious breach of an international obligation of essential importance for the safeguarding and preservation of the human environment, such as those prohibiting massive pollution of the atmosphere or of the seas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Supporters who spoke out in favour of a crime of ecocide included Romania and the Holy See,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup> Austria, Poland, Rwanda, Congo and Oman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1985</i>
</p><p>Ecocide as a crime continued to be addressed. The Whitaker Report, commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Sub-Commission_on_the_Promotion_and_Protection_of_Human_Rights" title="Sub-Commission on the Promotion and Protection of Human Rights">Sub-Commission on the Promotion and Protection of Human Rights</a> on the question of the prevention and punishment of the crime of genocide was prepared by then <a href="/wiki/Special_Rapporteur" title="Special Rapporteur">Special Rapporteur</a>, Benjamin Whitaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The report contained a passage that "some members of the Sub-Commission have, however, proposed that the definition of genocide should be broadened to include cultural genocide or "ethnocide", and also "ecocide": adverse alterations, often irreparable, to the environment - for example through nuclear explosions, chemical weapons, serious pollution and acid rain, or destruction of the rain forest - which threaten the existence of entire populations, whether deliberately or with criminal negligence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1987</i>
</p><p>Discussion of international crimes continued in the International Law Commission, where it was proposed that "the list of international crimes include "ecocide", as a reflection of the need to safeguard and preserve the environment, as well as the first use of nuclear weapons, <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_aggression&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Economic aggression (page does not exist)">economic aggression</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mercenarism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mercenarism (page does not exist)">mercenarism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1991</i>
</p><p>The ILC 'Draft Code of Crimes Against the Peace and Security of Mankind' of 1991 contained 12 crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal.un.org_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-legal.un.org-22">[22]</a></sup> One of those was 'wilful damage to the environment (Article 26)'.
</p><p><i>1993</i>
</p><p>As of 29 March 1993, the Secretary-General had received 23 replies from Member States and one reply from a non-member State. They were: Australia, Austria, Belarus, Belgium, Brazil, Bulgaria, Costa Rica, Ecuador, Greece, Netherlands, the Nordic countries (Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, Sweden), Paraguay, Poland, Senegal, Sudan, Turkey, UK, USA, Uruguay and Switzerland. Many objections were raised, for summarised commentary see the 1993 ILC Yearbook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Only
three countries, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom and the United States of
America, opposed the inclusion of an environmental crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The issue of adding a high test of intent (‘wilful’) was of concern: Austria commented: "Since perpetrators of this crime are usually acting out of a profit motive, intent should not be a condition for liability to punishment." Belgium and Uruguay also took the position that no element of intent was necessary for the crime of severe damage to the environment (Article 26).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1996</i>
</p><p>In 1996, Canadian/Australian lawyer Mark Gray published his proposal for an international crime of ecocide, based on established international environmental and human rights law. He demonstrated that states, and arguably individuals and organisations, causing or permitting harm to the natural environment on a massive scale breach a duty of care owed to humanity in general. He proposed that such breaches, where deliberate, reckless or negligent, be identified as ecocide where they entail serious, and extensive or lasting, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">ecological damage</a>; international consequences; and <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Meanwhile, in the ILC, ‘wilful and severe damage to the environment’ (Article 26) had been tasked to a working-group: "The Commission further decided that consultations would continue as regards [Article 26] …the Commission decided … to establish a working group that would meet … to examine the possibility of covering in the draft Code the issue of wilful and severe damage to the environment ...  the Commission decided by a vote to refer to the Drafting Committee only the text prepared by the working group for inclusion of wilful and severe damage to the environment as a war crime.
"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>1998</i>
</p><p>The final Draft Code was used as inspiration for the Rome Statute at the United Nations United Nations Diplomatic Conference of Plenipotentiaries on the Establishment of an International Criminal Court, which was held in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Rome Statute was the founding document of the International Criminal Court (ICC), to be used when a state is either unwilling or unable to bring their own prosecutions for international crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecocide was not included in the Rome Statute as a separate crime, but featured in relation to a war-crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">[30]</a></sup>  The test for this war crime was narrower than previous proposed tests.  Under the Environmental Modification Convention 1977 (ENMOD) the test for war-time environmental destruction is ‘widespread, long-term <u>or</u> severe’,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">[8]</a></sup> whereas Article 8(2)(b) of the Rome Statute 1998 modified the ENMOD test with the change of one word to ‘widespread, long-term <u>and</u> severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">[30]</a></sup>’ Article 8(2)(b) limited environmental harm to circumstances when "Intentionally launching an attack in the knowledge that such attack will cause incidental loss of life or injury to civilians or damage to civilian objects or widespread, long-term and severe damage to the natural environment which would be clearly excessive in relation to the concrete and direct overall military advantage anticipated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>2010</i>
</p><p>The proposal for the crime of Ecocide was submitted to the United Nations by a private party. In March 2010, British "earth lawyer" Polly Higgins submitted to the United Nations an amendment to the Rome Statute, proposing that “ecocide” be legally recognised as the fifth international <a href="/wiki/Crime_against_peace" title="Crime against peace">Crime against peace</a>. The Rome Statute currently acknowledges four crimes against peace: genocide; crimes against humanity; war crimes; and the crime of aggression. Each of these crimes affects human victims. While Higgins’ proposed definition of ecocide attends to inhabitants’ “peaceful enjoyment”, the victim the amendment is primarily promising to protect is not human but environmental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>2011</i>
</p><p>A mock Ecocide Act was drafted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and then tested in the UK Supreme Court via a mock trial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Group" title="Hamilton Group">Hamilton Group</a>.
</p><p><i>2012</i>
</p><p>A concept paper on the Law of Ecocide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> was sent out to governments In June 2012 the idea of making ecocide a crime was presented<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> to legislators and judges from around the world at the World Congress on Justice Governance and Law for Environmental Sustainability,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> held in Mangaratiba before the Rio +20 Earth Summit, the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development. Making ecocide an international crime was voted as one of the top twenty solutions to achieving sustainable development at the <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Congress" title="World Youth Congress">World Youth Congress</a> in Rio de Janeiro in June 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2012 a range of experts gathered at the international conference Environmental Crime: Current and Emerging Threats<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> held in Rome at the UN Food and Agricultural Organization Headquarters hosted by the United Nations Interregional Crime and Justice Research Institute(UNICRI) in cooperation with United Nations Environmental Programme (UNEP) and the Ministry of the Environment (Italy). The conference recognized that environmental crime is an important new form of transnational organized crime in need a greater response. One of the outcomes was that UNEP and UNICRI head up a study into the definition of environmental crime, new environmental crime and give due consideration to the history of making ecocide an international crime once again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>2013 - 2014</i>
</p><p><i>European citizen Initiative for criminalising ecocide</i>
</p><p>On January 22, 2013, a committee of eleven citizens from nine EU countries officially launched the "European Citizens Initiative "End Ecocide in Europe".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The European Citizens' Initiative,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> or ECI, is a tool created by the Lisbon Treaty to promote participative and direct democracy. The ECI is a way for EU citizens to propose new or suggest amendments to legislation directly to the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> which is the institution proposing new EU laws. This initiative aimed at criminalising ecocide, the extensive damage and destruction of ecosystems, including the denial of market access for products based on ecocide to the EU and investments in activities causing ecocide. Three MEPs, Keith Taylor, Eva Joly, and Jo Leinen, publicly gave the first signatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  The initiative did not collect the 1 million signatures needed, but was discussed in the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Existing_domestic_ecocide_laws">Existing domestic ecocide laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecocide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Existing domestic ecocide laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ten countries have codified ecocide as a crime during peacetime. Those countries follow closely the ILC Draft articles definition of "An individual who wilfully causes or orders the causing of widespread, long-term and severe damage to the natural environment shall, on conviction thereof, be sentenced [to...]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal.un.org_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-legal.un.org-22">[22]</a></sup> Although there are Laws of Ecocide in place, the effectiveness of these laws depends on a number of factors including the enforcement of the law, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_judiciary" title="Independent judiciary">independent judiciary</a> and respect for the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rule of law</a>. Many of the countries with national laws of ecocide in place are ranked very highly for corruption and low for respect for the rule of law by <a href="/wiki/Transparency_International" title="Transparency International">Transparency International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
