<p><b>Chlorine dioxide</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the formula ClO<sub>2</sub>.  This yellowish-green <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> crystallizes as bright orange crystals at −59 °C. As one of several <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_oxide" title="Chlorine oxide">oxides of chlorine</a>, it is a potent and useful <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> used in water treatment and in bleaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="175" data-file-width="200" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg/170px-Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg/255px-Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg/340px-Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graphical_comparison_of_bonds.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of three-electron bond to the conventional covalent bond</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ClO2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="134" data-file-width="561" height="41" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ClO2.svg/170px-ClO2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ClO2.svg/255px-ClO2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ClO2.svg/340px-ClO2.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ClO2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The two resonance structures</div></div></div>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is a neutral <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chlorine compound</a>. It is very different from elemental chlorine, both in its chemical structure and in its behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vogt,_H._2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vogt,_H._2010-5">[5]</a></sup>  One of the most important qualities of chlorine dioxide is its high water solubility, especially in cold water.  Chlorine dioxide does not hydrolyze when it enters water; it remains a dissolved gas in solution.  Chlorine dioxide is approximately 10 times more soluble in water than chlorine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vogt,_H._2010_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vogt,_H._2010-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The molecule ClO<sub>2</sub> has an odd number of <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a>, and therefore, it is a <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a>. Its electronic structure has long baffled chemists because none of the possible <a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis structures</a> is very satisfactory. In 1933, L. O. Brockway proposed a structure that involved a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three-electron_bond" title="Three-electron bond">three-electron bond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Chemist <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a> further developed this idea and arrived at two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonance_structure" title="Resonance structure">resonance structures</a> involving a double bond on one side and a single bond plus three-electron bond on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In Pauling's view the latter combination should represent a bond that is slightly <i>weaker</i> than the double bond. In <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital_theory" title="Molecular orbital theory">molecular orbital theory</a> this idea is commonplace if the third electron is placed in an anti-bonding orbital. Later work has confirmed that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HOMO" title="HOMO">highest occupied molecular orbital</a> is indeed an incompletely-filled antibonding orbital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is a compound that can decompose extremely violently when separated from diluting substances. As a result, preparation methods that involve producing solutions of it without going through a gas-phase stage are often preferred. Arranging handling in a safe manner is essential.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation_of_chlorite">Oxidation of chlorite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxidation of chlorite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the laboratory, ClO<sub>2</sub> can be prepared by oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorite" title="Sodium chlorite">sodium chlorite</a> with chlorine:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaClO<sub>2</sub>  +   Cl<sub>2</sub>  → 2 ClO<sub>2</sub>  + 2 NaCl</dd></dl>
<p>Traditionally, chlorine dioxide for <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfection</a> applications has been made from sodium <a href="/wiki/Chlorite" title="Chlorite">chlorite</a> or the sodium chlorite – hypochlorite method:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaClO<sub>2</sub> + 2 HCl + NaOCl → 2 ClO<sub>2</sub> + 3 NaCl + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>or the sodium chlorite – hydrochloric acid method:
</p>
<dl><dd>5 NaClO<sub>2</sub> + 4 HCl → 5 NaCl + 4 ClO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>or the chlorite – sulfuric acid method:
8ClO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> + 4H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 4ClO<sub>2</sub> + 2HClO<sub>3</sub> + 4SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O + 2HCl
</p><p>All three sodium chlorite chemistries can produce chlorine dioxide with high chlorite conversion yield, but unlike the other processes the chlorite-sulfuric acid method produces completely chlorine-free chlorine dioxide, although it suffers from the requirement of 25% more chlorite to produce an equivalent amount of chlorine dioxide. Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> may be efficiently used in small-scale applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogt2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-vogt2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_of_chlorate">Reduction of chlorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reduction of chlorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the laboratory, chlorine dioxide can be prepared also by reaction of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">potassium chlorate</a> with oxalic acid:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 KClO<sub>3</sub>  + 2 H<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>  →  K<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>  + 2 ClO<sub>2</sub>  + 2 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 KClO<sub>3</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>  + 2 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>  → 2 KHSO<sub>4</sub>  + 2 ClO<sub>2</sub>  + 2 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Over 95% of the chlorine dioxide produced in the world today is made by reduction of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a>, for use in <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">pulp bleaching</a>. It is produced with high efficiency in a strong acid solution with a suitable <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogt2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-vogt2010-10">[10]</a></sup> Modern technologies are based on methanol or hydrogen peroxide, as these chemistries allow the best economy and do not co-produce elemental chlorine. The overall reaction can be written as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>chlorate + acid + reducing agent → chlorine dioxide + by-products</dd></dl>
<p>As a typical example, the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> in a single reactor is believed to proceed through the following pathway:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + HOCl</dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> → 2<span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">HOCl</span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></dd></dl>
<p>which gives the overall reaction  2<span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 2<span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + 4<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> → 2<span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> .
</p><p>The commercially more important production route uses methanol as the reducing agent and sulfuric acid for the acidity. Two advantages of not using the chloride-based processes are that there is no formation of elemental chlorine, and that <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a>, a valuable chemical for the pulp mill, is a side-product. These methanol-based processes provide high efficiency and can be made very safe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogt2010_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-vogt2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The variant process using the chlorate, hydrogen peroxide and sulfuric acid has been increasingly used since 1999 for water treatment and other small-scale <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfection</a> applications, since it produce a chlorine-free product at high efficiency.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_processes">Other processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Very pure<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> chlorine dioxide can also be produced by electrolysis of a chlorite solution:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-white-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaClO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O  →  2 ClO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaOH + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>High-purity chlorine dioxide gas (7.7% in air or nitrogen) can be produced by the gas–solid method, which reacts dilute chlorine gas with solid sodium chlorite:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-white_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-white-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaClO<sub>2</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub> →  2 ClO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaCl</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handling_properties">Handling properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Handling properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At gas-phase concentrations greater than 30% volume in air at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Temperature_and_Pressure" title="Standard Temperature and Pressure">STP</a> (more correctly: at partial pressures above 10 kPa<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogt2010_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-vogt2010-10">[10]</a></sup>), ClO<sub>2</sub> may explosively decompose into <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The decomposition can be initiated by light, hot spots, chemical reaction, or pressure shock.  Thus, chlorine dioxide gas is never handled in concentrated form, but is almost always handled as a dissolved gas in water in a concentration range of 0.5 to 10 grams per liter. Its solubility increases at lower temperatures, thus it is common to use chilled water (5 °C, or 41 °F) when storing at concentrations above 3 grams per liter. In many countries, such as the United States, chlorine dioxide gas may not be transported at any concentration and is almost always produced at the application site using a chlorine dioxide generator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogt2010_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-vogt2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In some countries,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> chlorine dioxide solutions below 3 grams per liter in concentration may be transported by land, however, they are relatively unstable and deteriorate quickly.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is used for <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleaching of wood pulp</a> and for the <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfection</a> (called <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">chlorination</a>) of municipal drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4-1</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-block2001_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-block2001-15">[15]</a></sup>  As a disinfectant it is effective even at low concentrations because of its unique qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vogt,_H._2010_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vogt,_H._2010-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bleaching">Bleaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bleaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is sometimes used for <a href="/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp" title="Bleaching of wood pulp">bleaching of wood pulp</a> in combination with chlorine, but it is used alone in ECF (elemental chlorine-free) bleaching sequences. It is used at moderately acidic pH (3.5 to 6). The use of chlorine dioxide minimizes the amount of <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">organochlorine</a> compounds produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eero_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-eero-16">[16]</a></sup> Chlorine dioxide (ECF technology) currently is the most important bleaching method worldwide. About 95% of all bleached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kraft_pulp" title="Kraft pulp">kraft pulp</a> is made using chlorine dioxide in ECF bleaching sequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide is also used to <a href="/wiki/Bleach" title="Bleach">bleach</a> <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_chlorination">Water chlorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Water chlorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">Water chlorination</a></div> <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorine_Dioxide_Tablets#Chemical_disinfection" title="Chlorine Dioxide Tablets">Chlorine Dioxide Tablets § Chemical disinfection</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls,_New_York" title="Niagara Falls, New York">Niagara Falls, New York</a>, water treatment plant first used chlorine dioxide for <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> treatment in 1944 for <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> destruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–17</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-block2001_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-block2001-15">[15]</a></sup> Chlorine dioxide was introduced as a drinking water disinfectant on a large scale in 1956, when <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, Belgium, changed from chlorine to chlorine dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-block2001_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-block2001-15">[15]</a></sup> Its most common use in water treatment is as a pre-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidant" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a> prior to chlorination of drinking water to destroy natural water impurities that would otherwise produce <a href="/wiki/Trihalomethane" title="Trihalomethane">trihalomethanes</a> on exposure to free chlorine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volk2002_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-volk2002-20">[20]</a></sup> Trihalomethanes are suspect carcinogenic disinfection by-products<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> associated with chlorination of naturally occurring organics in the raw water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volk2002_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-volk2002-20">[20]</a></sup>  Chlorine dioxide is also superior to chlorine when operating above <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> 7,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–33</span></sup> in the presence of ammonia and amines<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and/or for the control of biofilms in water distribution systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volk2002_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-volk2002-20">[20]</a></sup> Chlorine dioxide is used in many industrial water treatment applications as a <a href="/wiki/Biocide" title="Biocide">biocide</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooling_towers" title="Cooling towers">cooling towers</a>, process water, and food processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide is less corrosive than chlorine and superior for the control of <a href="/wiki/Legionella" title="Legionella">legionella</a> bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-block2001_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-block2001-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
Chlorine dioxide is superior to some other secondary water disinfection methods in that chlorine dioxide: 1) is an EPA registered biocide, 2) is not negatively impacted by pH, 3) does not lose efficacy over time (the bacteria will not grow resistant to it) and 4) is not negatively impacted by silica and phosphate, which are commonly used potable water corrosion inhibitors.
</p><p>It is more effective as a disinfectant than chlorine in most circumstances against waterborne pathogenic agents such as <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> – including the <a href="/wiki/Microbial_cyst" title="Microbial cyst">cysts</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" title="Giardia lamblia">Giardia</a></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oocyst" title="Oocyst">oocysts</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidiosis" title="Cryptosporidiosis">Cryptosporidium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4-20–4-21</span></sup>
</p><p>The use of chlorine dioxide in water treatment leads to the formation of the by-product chlorite, which is currently limited to a maximum of 1 ppm in drinking water in the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–33</span></sup>  This EPA standard limits the use of chlorine dioxide in the USA to relatively high-quality water<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=" (December 2014)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup>, or water that is to be treated with iron-based coagulants (iron can reduce chlorite to chloride).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide has many applications as an oxidizer or disinfectant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vogt,_H._2010_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vogt,_H._2010-5">[5]</a></sup> Chlorine dioxide can be used for air disinfection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and was the principal agent used in the decontamination of buildings in the United States after the <a href="/wiki/2001_anthrax_attacks" title="2001 anthrax attacks">2001 anthrax attacks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> After the disaster of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and the surrounding Gulf Coast, chlorine dioxide has been used to eradicate dangerous <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a> from houses inundated by the flood water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  Because of its unique qualities, chlorine dioxide is an effective disinfectant even at low concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_disinfection_uses">Other disinfection uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other disinfection uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometimes chlorine dioxide is used as a fumigant treatment to "sanitize" fruits such as blueberries, raspberries, and strawberries that develop molds and yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide is used for the disinfection of <a href="/wiki/Endoscopy" title="Endoscopy">endoscopes</a>, such as under the trade name Tristel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It is also available in a "trio" consisting of a preceding "pre-clean" with <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactant</a> and a succeeding "rinse" with deionised water and low-level antioxidant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide also is used for control of <a href="/wiki/Zebra_mussel" title="Zebra mussel">zebra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quagga_mussel" title="Quagga mussel">quagga mussels</a> in water intakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–34</span></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine dioxide also was shown to be effective in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bed_bug_(insect)" title="Bed bug (insect)">bedbug</a> eradication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudomedicine">Pseudomedicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pseudomedicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Miracle_Mineral_Supplement" title="Miracle Mineral Supplement">Miracle Mineral Supplement</a></div>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is fraudulently marketed as a magic cure for a range of diseases from brain cancer to AIDS. Chlorine dioxide is relabelled to a variety of brand names including but not limited to MMS, Miracle Mineral Solution and CD protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> There is no scientific basis for chlorine dioxide's medical properties and FDA has warned against its usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is used as an oxidant for phenol destruction in waste water streams and for odor control in the air scrubbers of animal byproduct (rendering) plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1999_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-epa1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–34</span></sup> It is also available for use as a deodorant for cars and boats, in chlorine dioxide generating packages that are activated by water and left in the boat or car overnight.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_issues_in_water_and_supplements">Safety issues in water and supplements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorine_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Safety issues in water and supplements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chlorine dioxide is toxic, hence limits on exposure to it are needed to ensure its safe use. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> has set a maximum level of 0.8 mg/L for chlorine dioxide in drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA), an agency of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">United States Department of Labor</a>, has set an 8-hour <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> of 0.1 ppm in air (0.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milligram" title="Milligram">mg</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_meter" title="Cubic meter">m<sup>3</sup></a>) for people working with chlorine dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 30, 2010, and again on October 1, 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="United States Food and Drug Administration">United States Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) warned against the use of the product "<a href="/wiki/Miracle_Mineral_Supplement" title="Miracle Mineral Supplement">Miracle Mineral Supplement</a>", or "MMS", which when made up according to instructions produces chlorine dioxide. MMS has been marketed as a treatment for a variety of conditions, including HIV, cancer, autism, and acne.  The FDA warnings informed consumers that MMS can cause serious harm to health and stated that it has received numerous reports of nausea, diarrhea, severe vomiting, and life-threatening low blood pressure caused by dehydration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
