<p><b>Samarium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Sm</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 62. It is a moderately hard silvery <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that slowly oxidizes in air. Being a typical member of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, samarium usually assumes the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> +3. Compounds of samarium(II) are also known, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Monoxide" title="Monoxide">monoxide</a> SmO, <a href="/wiki/Samarium_monochalcogenides" title="Samarium monochalcogenides">monochalcogenides</a> SmS, SmSe and SmTe, as well as <a href="/wiki/Samarium(II)_iodide" title="Samarium(II) iodide">samarium(II) iodide</a>. The last compound is a common <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a>. Samarium has no significant biological role but is only slightly toxic.
</p><p>Samarium was discovered in 1879 by the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Paul-%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a> and named after the mineral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarskite" title="Samarskite">samarskite</a> from which it was isolated. The mineral itself was earlier named after a Russian mine official, Colonel <a href="/wiki/Vassili_Samarsky-Bykhovets" title="Vassili Samarsky-Bykhovets">Vassili Samarsky-Bykhovets</a>, who thereby became the first person to have a chemical element named after him, albeit indirectly. Although classified as a <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth element</a>, samarium is the 40th most abundant element in the Earth's crust and is more common than such metals as <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>. Samarium occurs with concentration up to 2.8% in several minerals including <a href="/wiki/Cerite" title="Cerite">cerite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>, samarskite, <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, the last two being the most common commercial sources of the element. These minerals are mostly found in China, the United States, Brazil, India, Sri Lanka and Australia; China is by far the world leader in samarium mining and production.
</p><p>The major commercial application of samarium is in <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93cobalt_magnet" title="Samarium–cobalt magnet">samarium–cobalt magnets</a>, which have <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">permanent magnetization</a> second only to <a href="/wiki/Neodymium_magnet" title="Neodymium magnet">neodymium magnets</a>; however, samarium compounds can withstand significantly higher temperatures, above 700 °C (1,292 °F), without losing their magnetic properties, due to the alloy's higher <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie point</a>. The radioactive <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarium-153" title="Samarium-153">samarium-153</a> is the active component of the drug <a href="/wiki/Samarium_(153Sm)_lexidronam" title="Samarium (153Sm) lexidronam">samarium (<sup>153</sup>Sm) lexidronam</a> (Quadramet), which kills cancer cells in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osteosarcoma" title="Osteosarcoma">osteosarcoma</a>. Another isotope, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarium-149" title="Samarium-149">samarium-149</a>, is a strong <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> absorber and is therefore added to the <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. It is also formed as a decay product during the reactor operation and is one of the important factors considered in the reactor design and operation. Other applications of samarium include <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93neodymium_dating" title="Samarium–neodymium dating">radioactive dating</a> and an <a href="/wiki/X-ray_laser" title="X-ray laser">X-ray laser</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Samarium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth metal</a> having a hardness and density similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>. With the boiling point of 1794 °C, samarium is the third most volatile lanthanide after <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>; this property facilitates separation of samarium from the mineral ore. At ambient conditions, samarium normally assumes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhombohedral" title="Rhombohedral">rhombohedral</a> structure (α form). Upon heating to 731 °C, its crystal symmetry changes into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonally close-packed</a> (<i>hcp</i>), however the transition temperature depends on the metal purity. Further heating to 922 °C transforms the metal into a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">body-centered cubic</a> (<i>bcc</i>) phase. Heating to 300 °C combined with compression to 40 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">kbar</a> results in a double-hexagonally close-packed structure (<i>dhcp</i>). Applying higher pressure of the order of hundreds or thousands of kilobars induces a series of phase transformations, in particular with a <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal</a> phase appearing at about 900 kbar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sm_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sm-4">[4]</a></sup> In one study, the <i>dhcp</i> phase could be produced without compression, using a nonequilibrium annealing regime with a rapid temperature change between about 400 and 700 °C, confirming the transient character of this samarium phase. Also, thin films of samarium obtained by vapor deposition may contain the <i>hcp</i> or <i>dhcp</i> phases at ambient conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sm_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sm-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium (and its <a href="/wiki/Sesquioxide" title="Sesquioxide">sesquioxide</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a> at room temperature. Their corresponding effective magnetic moments, below 2<a href="/wiki/Bohr_magneton" title="Bohr magneton">µ<sub>B</sub></a>, are the 3rd lowest among the lanthanides (and their oxides) after lanthanum and lutetium. The metal transforms to an <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetic</a> state upon cooling to 14.8 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Individual samarium atoms can be isolated by encapsulating them into <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerene</a> molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They can also be doped between the C<sub>60</sub> molecules in the <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerene</a> solid, rendering it <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductive</a> at temperatures below 8 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Samarium doping of <a href="/wiki/Iron-based_superconductor" title="Iron-based superconductor">iron-based superconductors</a> – the most recent class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductor" title="High-temperature superconductor">high-temperature superconductors</a> – allows enhancing their transition temperature to 56 K, which is the highest value achieved so far in this series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wu2008_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wu2008-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Freshly prepared samarium has a silvery luster. In air, it slowly oxidizes at room temperature and spontaneously ignites at 150 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup> Even when stored under <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a>, samarium gradually oxidizes and develops a grayish-yellow powder of the <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> mixture at the surface. The metallic appearance of a sample can be preserved by sealing it under an inert gas such as <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>.
</p><p>Samarium is quite electropositive and reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form samarium hydroxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-we_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-we-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Sm (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Sm(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Samarium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the yellow<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1243_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1243-13">[13]</a></sup> to pale green Sm(III) ions, which exist as [Sm(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-we_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-we-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Sm (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Sm<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Samarium is one of the few lanthanides that exhibit the oxidation state +2. The Sm<sup>2+</sup> ions are blood-red in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1248_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1248-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides">Oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most stable oxide of samarium is the <a href="/wiki/Sesquioxide" title="Sesquioxide">sesquioxide</a> Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. As many other samarium compounds, it exists in several crystalline phases. The trigonal form is obtained by slow cooling from the melt. The melting point of Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is rather high (2345 °C) and therefore melting is usually achieved not by direct heating, but with <a href="/wiki/Induction_heating" title="Induction heating">induction heating</a>, through a radio-frequency coil. The Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> crystals of monoclinic symmetry can be grown by the flame fusion method (<a href="/wiki/Verneuil_process" title="Verneuil process">Verneuil process</a>) from the Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> powder, that yields cylindrical boules up to several centimeters long and about one centimeter in diameter. The boules are transparent when pure and defect-free and are orange otherwise. Heating the metastable trigonal Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to 1900 °C converts it to the more stable monoclinic phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smo_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-smo-17">[17]</a></sup> Cubic Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has also been described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smo2_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-smo2-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium is one of the few lanthanides that form a monoxide, SmO. This lustrous golden-yellow compound was obtained by reducing Sm<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with samarium metal at elevated temperature (1000 °C) and pressure above 50 kbar; lowering the pressure resulted in an incomplete reaction. SmO has the cubic rock-salt lattice structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smox_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-smox-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1239_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1239-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chalcogenides">Chalcogenides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chalcogenides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Samarium_monochalcogenides" title="Samarium monochalcogenides">Samarium monochalcogenides</a></div>
<p>Samarium forms trivalent <a href="/wiki/Samarium(III)_sulfide" title="Samarium(III) sulfide">sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenide" title="Selenide">selenide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telluride_(chemistry)" title="Telluride (chemistry)">telluride</a>. Divalent chalcogenides SmS, SmSe and SmTe with cubic rock-salt crystal structure are also known. They are remarkable by converting from semiconducting to metallic state at room temperature upon application of pressure. Whereas the transition is continuous and occurs at about 20–30 kbar in SmSe and SmTe, it is abrupt in SmS and requires only 6.5 kbar. This effect results in spectacular color change in SmS from black to golden yellow when its crystals of films are scratched or polished. The transition does not change lattice symmetry, but there is a sharp decrease (~15%) in the crystal volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-37">[37]</a></sup> It shows hysteresis, that is when the pressure is released, SmS returns to the semiconducting state at much lower pressure of about 0.4 kbar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Samarium metal reacts with all the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>, forming trihalides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1236_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1236-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Sm (s) + 3 X<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 SmX<sub>3</sub> (s) (X = F, Cl, Br or I)</dd></dl>
<p>Their further reduction with samarium, lithium or sodium metals at elevated temperatures (about 700–900 °C) yields dihalides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smcl2_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-smcl2-29">[29]</a></sup> The diiodide can also be prepared by heating SmI<sub>3</sub>, or by reacting the metal with <a href="/wiki/1,2-Diiodoethane" title="1,2-Diiodoethane">1,2-diiodoethane</a> in anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> at room temperature:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1240_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1240-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Sm (s) + ICH<sub>2</sub>-CH<sub>2</sub>I → SmI<sub>2</sub> + CH<sub>2</sub>=CH<sub>2</sub>.</dd></dl>
<p>In addition to dihalides, the reduction also produces numerous <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric</a> samarium halides with a well-defined crystal structure, such as Sm<sub>3</sub>F<sub>7</sub>, Sm<sub>14</sub>F<sub>33</sub>, Sm<sub>27</sub>F<sub>64</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smf2_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-smf2-28">[28]</a></sup> Sm<sub>11</sub>Br<sub>24</sub>, Sm<sub>5</sub>Br<sub>11</sub> and Sm<sub>6</sub>Br<sub>13</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>As reflected in the table above, samarium halides change their crystal structures when one type of halide atoms is substituted for another, which is an uncommon behavior for most elements (e.g. actinides). Many halides have two major crystal phases for one composition, one being significantly more stable and another being metastable. The latter is formed upon compression or heating, followed by quenching to ambient conditions. For example, compressing the usual monoclinic samarium diiodide and releasing the pressure results in a PbCl<sub>2</sub>-type orthorhombic structure (density 5.90 g/cm<sup>3</sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and similar treatment results in a new phase of samarium triiodide (density 5.97 g/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Borides">Borides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Borides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">Sintering</a> powders of samarium oxide and boron, in vacuum, yields a powder containing several samarium boride phases, and their volume ratio can be controlled through the mixing proportion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smb6b_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-smb6b-44">[44]</a></sup> The powder can be converted into larger crystals of a certain samarium boride using <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">arc melting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zone_melting" title="Zone melting">zone melting</a> techniques, relying on the different melting/crystallization temperature of SmB<sub>6</sub> (2580 °C), SmB<sub>4</sub> (about 2300 °C) and SmB<sub>66</sub> (2150 °C). All these materials are hard, brittle, dark-gray solids with the hardness increasing with the boron content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smb6_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-smb6-24">[24]</a></sup> Samarium diboride is too volatile to be produced with these methods and requires high pressure (about 65 kbar) and low temperatures between 1140 and 1240 °C to stabilize its growth. Increasing the temperature results in the preferential formations of SmB<sub>6</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smb2_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-smb2-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Samarium_hexaboride">Samarium hexaboride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Samarium hexaboride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Samarium hexaboride is a typical intermediate-valence compound where samarium is present both as Sm<sup>2+</sup> and Sm<sup>3+</sup> ions at the ratio 3:7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smb6b_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-smb6b-44">[44]</a></sup> It belongs to a class of <a href="/wiki/Kondo_insulator" title="Kondo insulator">Kondo insulators</a>, that is at high temperatures (above 50 K), its properties are typical of a Kondo metal, with metallic electrical conductivity characterized by strong electron scattering, whereas at low temperatures, it behaves as a non-magnetic insulator with a narrow <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of about 4–14 meV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The cooling-induced metal-insulator transition in SmB<sub>6</sub> is accompanied by a sharp increase in the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a>, peaking at about 15 K. The reason for this increase is that electrons themselves do not contribute to the thermal conductivity at low temperatures, which is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">phonons</a>, but the decrease in electron concentration reduced the rate of electron-phonon scattering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>New research seems to show that it may be a <a href="/wiki/Topological_insulator" title="Topological insulator">topological insulator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_inorganic_compounds">Other inorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other inorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samarium-sulfate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="669" data-file-width="673" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Samarium-sulfate.jpg/170px-Samarium-sulfate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Samarium-sulfate.jpg/255px-Samarium-sulfate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Samarium-sulfate.jpg/340px-Samarium-sulfate.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Samarium-sulfate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Samarium sulfate, Sm<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub></div></div></div>
<p>Samarium <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbides</a> are prepared by melting a graphite-metal mixture in an inert atmosphere. After the synthesis, they are unstable in air and are studied also under inert atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smc_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-smc-26">[26]</a></sup> Samarium monophosphide SmP is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> with the bandgap of 1.10 eV, the same as in <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, and high electrical conductivity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" title="N-type semiconductor">n-type</a>. It can be prepared by annealing at 1100 °C an evacuated quartz ampoule containing mixed powders of phosphorus and samarium. Phosphorus is highly volatile at high temperatures and may explode, thus the heating rate has to be kept well below 1 °C/min.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smp_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-smp-34">[34]</a></sup> Similar procedure is adopted for the monarsenide SmAs, but the synthesis temperature is higher at 1800 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smas_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-smas-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous crystalline binary compounds are known for samarium and one of the group-14, 15 or 16 element X, where X is Si, Ge, Sn, Pb, Sb or Te, and metallic alloys of samarium form another large group. They are all prepared by annealing mixed powders of the corresponding elements. Many of the resulting compounds are non-stoichiometric and have nominal compositions Sm<sub>a</sub>X<sub>b</sub>, where the b/a ratio varies between 0.5 and 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organometallic_compounds">Organometallic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organometallic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Samarium forms a <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadiene" title="Cyclopentadiene">cyclopentadienide</a> Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and its chloroderivatives Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Cl and Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)Cl<sub>2</sub>. They are prepared by reacting samarium trichloride with NaC<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub> in <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a>. Contrary to cyclopentadienides of most other lanthanides, in Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub> some C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub> rings bridge each other by forming ring vertexes η<sup>1</sup> or edges η<sup>2</sup> toward another neighboring samarium atom, thereby creating polymeric chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1248_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-g1248-14">[14]</a></sup> The chloroderivative Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Cl has a dimer structure, which is more accurately expressed as (η<sup>5</sup>-C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Sm(µ-Cl)<sub>2</sub>(η<sup>5</sup>-C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>. There, the chlorine bridges can be replaced, for instance, by iodine, hydrogen or nitrogen atoms or by CN groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1249_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-g1249-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>The (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sup>−</sup> ion in samarium cyclopentadienides can be replaced by the indenide (C<sub>9</sub>H<sub>7</sub>)<sup>−</sup> or <a href="/wiki/Cyclooctatetraene" title="Cyclooctatetraene">cyclooctatetraenide</a> (C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>)<sup>2−</sup> ring, resulting in Sm(C<sub>9</sub>H<sub>7</sub>)<sub>3</sub> or KSm(η<sup>8</sup>-C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>)<sub>2</sub>. The latter compound has a similar structure to that of <a href="/wiki/Uranocene" title="Uranocene">uranocene</a>. There is also a cyclopentadienide of divalent samarium, Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub> – a solid that sublimates at about 85 °C. Contrary to <a href="/wiki/Ferrocene" title="Ferrocene">ferrocene</a>, the C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub> rings in Sm(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub> are not parallel but are tilted by 40°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1249_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-g1249-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">Alkyls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryls</a> of samarium are obtained through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metathesis_reaction" title="Metathesis reaction">metathesis reaction</a> in tetrahydrofuran or <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1249_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-g1249-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SmCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 LiR → SmR<sub>3</sub> + 3 LiCl</dd>
<dd>Sm(OR)<sub>3</sub> + 3 LiCH(SiMe<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> → Sm{CH(SiMe<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>}<sub>3</sub> + 3 LiOR</dd></dl>
<p>Here R is a hydrocarbon group and Me stands for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_samarium" title="Isotopes of samarium">Isotopes of samarium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring samarium has a <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactivity</a> of 128 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">Bq</a>/g. It is composed of four stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: <sup>144</sup>Sm, <sup>150</sup>Sm, <sup>152</sup>Sm and <sup>154</sup>Sm, and three extremely long-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a>, <sup>147</sup>Sm (half-life <i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 1.06<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">11</span></sup> years), <sup>148</sup>Sm (7<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup> years) and <sup>149</sup>Sm (&gt;2<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup> years), with <sup>152</sup>Sm being the most abundant (<a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">natural abundance</a> 26.75%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-55">[55]</a></sup> <sup>149</sup>Sm is listed by various sources either as stable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> or radioactive isotope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The long-lived isotopes,<sup>146</sup>Sm, <sup>147</sup>Sm, and <sup>148</sup>Sm, primarily decay by emission of <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha particles</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>. Lighter unstable isotopes of samarium primarily decay by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>, while heavier ones convert through <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> of <sup>147</sup>Sm to <sup>143</sup>Nd with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 1.06<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">11</span></sup> years serve for <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93neodymium_dating" title="Samarium–neodymium dating">samarium–neodymium dating</a>.
</p><p>The half-lives of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarium-151" title="Samarium-151"><sup>151</sup>Sm</a> and <sup>145</sup>Sm are 90 years and 340 days, respectively. All the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotopes" title="Radioisotopes">radioisotopes</a> have half-lives that are less than 2 days, and the majority of these have half-lives that are less than 48 seconds. Samarium also has five <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> with the most stable being <sup>141m</sup>Sm (<a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> 22.6 minutes), <sup>143m1</sup>Sm (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 66 seconds) and <sup>139m</sup>Sm (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 10.7 seconds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="200" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran.jpg/170px-Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a>, the discoverer of samarium</div></div></div>
<p>Detection of samarium and related elements was announced by several scientists in the second half of the 19th century; however, most sources give the priority to the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-59">[59]</a></sup> Boisbaudran isolated samarium oxide and/or hydroxide in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1879 from the mineral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarskite" title="Samarskite">samarskite</a> ((Y,Ce,U,Fe)<sub>3</sub>(Nb,Ta,Ti)<sub>5</sub>O<sub>16</sub>) and identified a new element in it via sharp optical absorption lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup> The Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Marc_Delafontaine" title="Marc Delafontaine">Marc Delafontaine</a> announced a new element <i><a href="/wiki/Decipium" title="Decipium">decipium</a></i> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">decipiens</i> meaning "deceptive, misleading") in 1878,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> but later in 1880–1881 demonstrated that it was a mixture of several elements, one being identical to the Boisbaudran's samarium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-iupac-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Although samarskite was first found in the remote Russian region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urals" title="Urals">Urals</a>, by the late 1870s its deposits had been located in other places making the mineral available to many researchers. In particular, it was found that the samarium isolated by Boisbaudran was also impure and contained comparable amount of <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>. The pure element was produced only in 1901 by <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne-Anatole_Demar%C3%A7ay" title="Eugène-Anatole Demarçay">Eugène-Anatole Demarçay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-van-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Boisbaudran named his element <i>samaria</i> after the mineral samarskite, which in turn honored <a href="/wiki/Vassili_Samarsky-Bykhovets" title="Vassili Samarsky-Bykhovets">Vassili Samarsky-Bykhovets</a> (1803–1870). Samarsky-Bykhovets, as the Chief of Staff of the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> Corps of Mining Engineers, had granted access for two German mineralogists, the brothers <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Rose" title="Gustav Rose">Gustav Rose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rose" title="Heinrich Rose">Heinrich Rose</a>, to study the mineral samples from the Urals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bse_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-bse-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In this sense samarium was the first chemical element to be named after a person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-van-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSC_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-68">[68]</a></sup> Later the name <i>samaria</i> used by Boisbaudran was transformed into <i>samarium</i>, to conform with other element names, and samaria nowadays is sometimes used to refer to samarium oxide, by analogy with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">yttria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconia" title="Zirconia">zirconia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceria" title="Ceria">ceria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holmium(III)_oxide" title="Holmium(III) oxide">holmia</a>, etc. The symbol <i>Sm</i> was suggested for samarium; however an alternative <i>Sa</i> was frequently used instead until the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-van-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to the advent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> separation technology in the 1950s, samarium had no commercial uses in pure form. However, a by-product of the fractional crystallization purification of neodymium was a mixture of samarium and gadolinium that acquired the name of "Lindsay Mix" after the company that made it. This material is thought to have been used for nuclear <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> in some early nuclear reactors. Nowadays, a similar commodity product has the name "samarium-europium-<a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>" (SEG) concentrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSC_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSC-68">[68]</a></sup> It is prepared by solvent extraction from the mixed <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> isolated from bastnäsite (or monazite). Since the heavier lanthanides have the greater affinity for the solvent used, they are easily extracted from the bulk using relatively small proportions of solvent. Not all rare-earth producers who process bastnäsite do so on a large enough scale to continue onward with the separation of the components of SEG, which typically makes up only one or two percent of the original ore. Such producers will therefore be making SEG with a view to marketing it to the specialized processors. In this manner, the valuable europium content of the ore is rescued for use in <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a> manufacture. Samarium purification follows the removal of the europium. As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, being in oversupply, samarium oxide is less expensive on a commercial scale than its relative abundance in the ore might suggest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-price_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-price-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samarskite-fresh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="1400" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Samarskite-fresh.jpg/220px-Samarskite-fresh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Samarskite-fresh.jpg/330px-Samarskite-fresh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Samarskite-fresh.jpg/440px-Samarskite-fresh.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Samarskite-fresh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Samarskite</div></div></div>
<p>With the average concentration of about 8 parts per million (ppm), samarium is the 40th most abundant element in the Earth's crust. It is the fifth most abundant lanthanide and is more common than elements such as tin. Samarium concentration in soils varies between 2 and 23 ppm, and oceans contain about 0.5–0.8 parts per trillion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup> Distribution of samarium in soils strongly depends on its chemical state and is very inhomogeneous: in sandy soils, samarium concentration is about 200 times higher at the surface of soil particles than in the water trapped between them, and this ratio can exceed 1,000 in clays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA2_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-LA2-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium is not found free in nature, but, like other rare earth elements, is contained in many minerals, including <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerite" title="Cerite">cerite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarskite" title="Samarskite">samarskite</a>; monazite (in which samarium occurs at concentrations of up to 2.8%)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup> and bastnäsite are mostly used as commercial sources. World resources of samarium are estimated at two million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>; they are mostly located in China, US, Brazil, India, Sri Lanka and Australia, and the annual production is about 700 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup> Country production reports are usually given for all rare-earth metals combined. By far, China has the largest production with 120,000 tonnes mined per year; it is followed by the US (about 5,000 tonnes)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA2_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-LA2-71">[71]</a></sup> and India (2,700 tonnes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Samarium is usually sold as oxide, which at the price of about 30 USD/kg is one of the cheapest lanthanide oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-price_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-price-70">[70]</a></sup> Whereas <a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">mischmetal</a> – a mixture of rare earth metals containing about 1% of samarium – has long been used, relatively pure samarium has been isolated only recently, through <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> processes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvent_extraction" title="Solvent extraction">solvent extraction</a> techniques, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Electrochemical_deposition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Electrochemical deposition (page does not exist)">electrochemical deposition</a>. The metal is often prepared by electrolysis of a molten mixture of <a href="/wiki/Samarium(III)_chloride" title="Samarium(III) chloride">samarium(III) chloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>. Samarium can also be obtained by reducing its oxide with <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>. The product is then distilled to separate samarium (boiling point 1794 °C) and lanthanum (b.p. 3464 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Domination of samarium in minerals is unique. Minerals with essential (dominant) samarium include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monazite-(Sm)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monazite-(Sm) (page does not exist)">monazite-(Sm)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florencite-(Sm)" title="Florencite-(Sm)">florencite-(Sm)</a>. They are very rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium-151 is produced in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> with the yield of about
0.4% of the total number of fission events. It is also synthesized upon <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a> by samarium-149, which is added to the <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> of nuclear reactors. Consequently, samarium-151 is present in spent <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> and radioactive waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA2_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-LA2-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Samariumiodide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="294" data-file-width="282" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Samariumiodide.jpg/220px-Samariumiodide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/99/Samariumiodide.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Samariumiodide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier reaction</a> using SmI<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<p>One of the most important applications of samarium is in <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93cobalt_magnet" title="Samarium–cobalt magnet">samarium–cobalt magnets</a>, which have a nominal composition of SmCo<sub>5</sub> or Sm<sub>2</sub>Co<sub>17</sub>. They have high permanent magnetization, which is about 10,000 times that of iron and is second only to that of <a href="/wiki/Neodymium_magnet" title="Neodymium magnet">neodymium magnets</a>. However, samarium-based magnets have higher resistance to demagnetization, as they are stable to temperatures above 700 °C (cf. 300–400 °C for neodymium magnets). These magnets are found in small motors, headphones, and high-end magnetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pick_up_(music_technology)" title="Pick up (music technology)">pickups</a> for guitars and related musical instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, they are used in the motors of a <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar-powered</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_aircraft" title="Electric aircraft">electric aircraft</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Challenger" title="Solar Challenger">Solar Challenger</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Vintage_Noiseless" title="Vintage Noiseless">Samarium Cobalt Noiseless</a> electric guitar and bass pickups.
</p><p>Another important application of samarium and its compounds is as catalyst and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reagent" title="Chemical reagent">chemical reagent</a>. Samarium catalysts assist decomposition of plastics, dechlorination of pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs), as well as the dehydration and <a href="/wiki/Dehydrogenation" title="Dehydrogenation">dehydrogenation</a> of ethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_trifluoromethanesulfonates" title="Lanthanide trifluoromethanesulfonates">Samarium(III) triflate</a> (Sm(OTf)<sub>3</sub>, that is Sm(CF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>), is one of the most efficient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> catalysts for a halogen-promoted <a href="/wiki/Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_reaction" title="Friedel–Crafts reaction">Friedel–Crafts reaction</a> with alkenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Samarium(II)_iodide" title="Samarium(II) iodide">Samarium(II) iodide</a> is a very common reducing and coupling agent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, for example in the <a href="/wiki/Desulfonylation_reactions" title="Desulfonylation reactions">desulfonylation reactions</a>; <a href="/wiki/Annulation" title="Annulation">annulation</a>; <a href="/wiki/Danishefsky_Taxol_total_synthesis" title="Danishefsky Taxol total synthesis">Danishefsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwajima_Taxol_total_synthesis" title="Kuwajima Taxol total synthesis">Kuwajima</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mukaiyama_Taxol_total_synthesis" title="Mukaiyama Taxol total synthesis">Mukaiyama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holton_Taxol_total_synthesis" title="Holton Taxol total synthesis">Holton Taxol total syntheses</a>; <a href="/wiki/Strychnine_total_synthesis" title="Strychnine total synthesis">strychnine total synthesis</a>; <a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier reaction</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Reductions_with_samarium(II)_iodide" title="Reductions with samarium(II) iodide">reductions with samarium(II) iodide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>In its usual oxidized form, samarium is added to ceramics and glasses where it increases absorption of infrared light. As a (minor) part of <a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">mischmetal</a>, samarium is found in "<a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a>" ignition device of many <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">lighters</a> and torches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="132" data-file-width="262" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg/220px-153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg/330px-153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg/440px-153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:153Sm-lexidronam_structure.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chemical structure of <a href="/wiki/Samarium_(153Sm)_lexidronam" title="Samarium (153Sm) lexidronam">Sm-EDTMP</a></div></div></div>
<p>Radioactive samarium-153 is a beta emitter with a half-life of 46.3 hours. It is used to kill cancer cells in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Osteosarcoma" title="Osteosarcoma">osteosarcoma</a>. For this purpose, samarium-153 is <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelated</a> with ethylene diamine tetramethylene phosphonate (<a href="/wiki/EDTMP" title="EDTMP">EDTMP</a>) and injected intravenously. The chelation prevents accumulation of radioactive samarium in the body that would result in excessive irradiation and generation of new cancer cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup> The corresponding drug has several names including <a href="/wiki/Samarium_(153Sm)_lexidronam" title="Samarium (153Sm) lexidronam">samarium (<sup>153</sup>Sm) lexidronam</a>; its <a href="/wiki/Trade_name" title="Trade name">trade name</a> is Quadramet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium-149 has high cross-section for <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a> (41,000 <a href="/wiki/Barn_(unit)" title="Barn (unit)">barns</a>) and is therefore used in the control rods of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. Its advantage compared to competing materials, such as boron and cadmium, is stability of absorption – most of the fusion and decay products of samarium-149 are other isotopes of samarium that are also good neutron absorbers. For example, the cross section of samarium-151 is 15,000 barns, it is on the order of hundreds of barns for <sup>150</sup>Sm, <sup>152</sup>Sm, and <sup>153</sup>Sm, and is 6,800 barns for natural (mixed-isotope) samarium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA2_71-3"><a href="#cite_note-LA2-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Among the decay products in a nuclear reactor, samarium-149 is regarded as the second most important for the reactor design and operation after <a href="/wiki/Xenon-135" title="Xenon-135">xenon-135</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Samarium hexaboride, abbreviated SmB<sub>6</sub>, has recently been shown to be a <a href="/wiki/Topological_insulator" title="Topological insulator">topological insulator</a> with potential applications to <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computing" title="Quantum computing">quantum computing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorgsamarium_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorgsamarium-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-commercial_and_potential_applications">Non-commercial and potential applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samarium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Non-commercial and potential applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Samarium-doped <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a> crystals were used as an active medium in one of the first <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a> designed and constructed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_P._Sorokin" title="Peter P. Sorokin">Peter Sorokin</a> (co-inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Dye_laser" title="Dye laser">dye laser</a>) and Mirek Stevenson at <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> research labs in early 1961. This samarium laser emitted pulses of red light at 708.5 nm. It had to be cooled by liquid helium and thus did not find practical applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another samarium-based laser became the first saturated <a href="/wiki/X-ray_laser" title="X-ray laser">X-ray laser</a> operating at wavelengths shorter than 10 nanometers. It provided 50-picosecond pulses at 7.3 and 6.8 nm suitable for applications in <a href="/wiki/Holography" title="Holography">holography</a>, high-resolution <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a> of biological specimens, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deflectometry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deflectometry (page does not exist)">deflectometry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">interferometry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a> of dense plasmas related to confinement fusion and <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>. Saturated operation meant that the maximum possible power was extracted from the lasing medium, resulting in the high peak energy of 0.3 mJ. The active medium was samarium plasma produced by irradiating samarium-coated glass with a pulsed infrared <a href="/wiki/Nd:YAG_laser" title="Nd:YAG laser">Nd-glass laser</a> (wavelength ~1.05 µm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>The change in electrical resistivity in <a href="/wiki/Samarium_monochalcogenides" title="Samarium monochalcogenides">samarium monochalcogenides</a> can be used in a pressure sensor or in a memory device triggered between a low-resistance and high-resistance state by external pressure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and such devices are being developed commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Samarium monosulfide also generates electric voltage upon moderate heating to about 150 °C that can be applied in <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_generator" title="Thermoelectric generator">thermoelectric power converters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>The analysis of relative concentrations of samarium and neodymium isotopes <sup>147</sup>Sm, <sup>144</sup>Nd, and <sup>143</sup>Nd allows the determination of the age and origin of rocks and meteorites in <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93neodymium_dating" title="Samarium–neodymium dating">samarium–neodymium dating</a>. Both elements are lanthanides and have very similar physical and chemical properties. Therefore, Sm–Nd dating is either insensitive to partitioning of the marker elements during various geological processes, or such partitioning can well be understood and modeled from the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radii</a> of the involved elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sm<sup>3+</sup> ion is a potential <a href="/wiki/Activator_(phosphor)" title="Activator (phosphor)">activator</a> for use in warm-white light emitting diodes. It offers high <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">luminous efficacy</a> due to the narrow emission bands, however, the generally low <a href="/wiki/Quantum_efficiency" title="Quantum efficiency">quantum efficiency</a> and insufficient absorption in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet#Subtypes" title="Ultraviolet">UV-A</a> to blue spectral region hinders commercial application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years it has been demonstrated that nanocrystalline BaFCl:Sm<sup>3+</sup> as prepared by a co-precipitation can serve as a very efficient x-ray storage phosphor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> The co-precipitation leads to nanocrystallites of the order of 100-200 nm in size and their sensitivity as x-ray storage phosphors is increased an astounding ∼500,000 times because of the specific arrangements and density of defect centres in comparison with microcrystalline samples prepared by sintering at high temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> The mechanism is based on the reduction of Sm<sup>3+</sup> to Sm<sup>2+</sup> by trapping electrons that are created upon exposure to ionizing radiation in the BaFCl host. The <sup>5</sup> D<sub>J</sub>-<sup>7</sup> F<sub>J</sub> f-f luminescence lines can be very efficiently excited via the parity allowed 4f<sup>6</sup> →4f<sup>5</sup> 5d transition at around 417 nm. The latter wavelength is ideal for efficient excitation by blue-violet laser diodes as the transition is electric dipole allowed and thus relatively intense (400 l/(mol⋅cm)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
The phosphor has potential applications in personal dosimetry, dosimetry and imaging in radiotherapy, and medical imaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Samarium salts stimulate metabolism, but it is unclear whether this is the effect of samarium or other lanthanides present with it. The total amount of samarium in adults is about 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrograms" title="Micrograms">µg</a>, mostly in liver and kidneys and with about 8 µg/L being dissolved in the blood. Samarium is not absorbed by plants to a measurable concentration and therefore is normally not a part of human diet. However, a few plants and vegetables may contain up to 1 part per million of samarium. Insoluble salts of samarium are non-toxic and the soluble ones are only slightly toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>When ingested, only about 0.05% of samarium salts is absorbed into the bloodstream and the remainder is excreted. From the blood, about 45% goes to the liver and 45% is deposited on the surface of the bones where it remains for about 10 years; the balance 10% is excreted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA2_71-4"><a href="#cite_note-LA2-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
