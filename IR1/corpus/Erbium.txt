<p><b>Erbium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Er</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 68. A silvery-white solid metal when artificially isolated, natural erbium is always found in chemical combination with other elements. It is a <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a>, originally found in the <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> mine in <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, from which it got its name.
</p><p>Erbium's principal uses involve its pink-colored Er<sup>3+</sup> ions, which have optical fluorescent properties particularly useful in certain laser applications. Erbium-doped glasses or crystals can be used as optical amplification media, where Er<sup>3+</sup> ions are optically pumped at around 980 or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994148000000000000♠</span>1480 nm</span> and then radiate light at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994153000000000000♠</span>1530 nm</span> in stimulated emission. This process results in an unusually mechanically simple <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> <a href="/wiki/Optical_amplifier" title="Optical amplifier">optical amplifier</a> for signals transmitted by fiber optics. The <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994155000000000000♠</span>1550 nm</span> wavelength is especially important for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_communications" title="Optical communications">optical communications</a> because standard single mode <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_fibers" title="Optical fibers">optical fibers</a> have minimal loss at this particular wavelength.
</p><p>In addition to optical fiber amplifier-lasers, a large variety of medical applications (i.e. dermatology, dentistry) rely on the erbium ion's <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994294000000000000♠</span>2940 nm</span> emission (see <a href="/wiki/Er:YAG_laser" title="Er:YAG laser">Er:YAG laser</a>) when lit at another wavelength, which is highly absorbed in water in tissues, making its effect very superficial. Such shallow tissue deposition of laser energy is helpful in <a href="/wiki/Laser_surgery" title="Laser surgery">laser surgery</a>, and for the efficient production of steam which produces enamel ablation by common types of <a href="/wiki/Dental_laser" title="Dental laser">dental laser</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erbium(III)chloride_sunlight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="280" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Erbium%28III%29chloride_sunlight.jpg/220px-Erbium%28III%29chloride_sunlight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0f/Erbium%28III%29chloride_sunlight.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erbium(III)chloride_sunlight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Erbium(III)chloride in sunlight, showing some pink fluorescence of Er<sup>+3</sup> from natural ultraviolet.</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">trivalent</a> element, pure erbium <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> is malleable (or easily shaped), soft yet stable in air, and does not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidize</a> as quickly as some other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metals" title="Rare-earth metals">rare-earth metals</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> are rose-colored, and the element has characteristic sharp <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_spectra" title="Absorption spectra">absorption spectra</a> bands in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, and near <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>. Otherwise it looks much like the other rare earths. Its <a href="/wiki/Sesquioxide" title="Sesquioxide">sesquioxide</a> is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a>. Erbium's properties are to a degree dictated by the kind and amount of impurities present. Erbium does not play any known biological role, but is thought to be able to stimulate <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Erbium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> below 19 K, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> between 19 and 80 K and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> above 80 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Erbium can form propeller-shaped atomic clusters Er<sub>3</sub>N, where the distance between the erbium atoms is 0.35 nm. Those clusters can be isolated by encapsulating them into <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerene</a> molecules, as confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">transmission electron microscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Erbium metal tarnishes slowly in air and burns readily to form <a href="/wiki/Erbium(III)_oxide" title="Erbium(III) oxide">erbium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Er + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Er<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Erbium is quite electropositive and reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form erbium hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Er (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Er(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Erbium metal reacts with all the halogens:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Er (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 ErF<sub>3</sub> (s) [pink]</dd>
<dd>2 Er (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 ErCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [violet]</dd>
<dd>2 Er (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 ErBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [violet]</dd>
<dd>2 Er (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 ErI<sub>3</sub> (s) [violet]</dd></dl>
<p>Erbium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing hydrated Er(III) ions, which exist as rose red [Er(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> hydration complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Er (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Er<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Erbium (for <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a>, a village in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>) was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> in 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Mosander was working with a sample of what was thought to be the single metal oxide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">yttria</a>, derived from the mineral <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>. He discovered that the sample contained at least two metal oxides in addition to pure yttria, which he named "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terbia" title="Terbia">terbia</a>" after the village of Ytterby where the gadolinite had been found. Mosander was not certain of the purity of the oxides and later tests confirmed his uncertainty. Not only did the "yttria" contain yttrium, erbium, and terbium; in the ensuing years, chemists, geologists and spectroscopists discovered five additional elements: <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  Erbia and terbia, however, were confused at this time. A spectroscopist mistakenly switched the names of the two elements during spectroscopy.  After 1860, terbia was renamed erbia and after 1877 what had been known as erbia was renamed terbia. Fairly pure Er<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub> was independently isolated in 1905 by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a>. Reasonably pure erbium metal was not produced until 1934 when <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wilhelm_Klemm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wilhelm Klemm (page does not exist)">Wilhelm Klemm</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Heinrich_Bommer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Heinrich Bommer (page does not exist)">Heinrich Bommer</a> reduced the <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> vapor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was only in the 1990s that the price for Chinese-derived erbium oxide became low enough for erbium to be considered for use as a colorant in art glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MonaziteUSGOV.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="270" data-file-width="360" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/MonaziteUSGOV.jpg/220px-MonaziteUSGOV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/MonaziteUSGOV.jpg/330px-MonaziteUSGOV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/MonaziteUSGOV.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MonaziteUSGOV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monazite sand</div></div></div>
<p>The concentration of erbium in the Earth crust is about 2.8 mg/kg and in the sea water 0.9 ng/L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-12">[12]</a></sup> This concentration is enough to make erbium about 45th in <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">elemental abundance in the Earth's crust</a>.
</p><p>Like other rare earths, this element is never found as a free element in nature but is found bound in <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> sand ores. It has historically been very difficult and expensive to separate rare earths from each other in their ores but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> chromatography methods<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> developed in the late 20th century have greatly brought down the cost of production of all rare-earth metals and their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>.
</p><p>The principal commercial sources of erbium are from the minerals <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>, and most recently, the ion adsorption clays of southern China; in consequence, China has now become the principal global supplier of this element. In the high-yttrium versions of these ore concentrates, yttrium is about two-thirds of the total by weight, and erbia is about 4–5%. When the concentrate is dissolved in acid, the erbia liberates enough erbium ion to impart a distinct and characteristic pink color to the solution. This color behavior is similar to what Mosander and the other early workers in the lanthanides would have seen in their extracts from the gadolinite minerals of Ytterby.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crushed minerals are attacked by hydrochloric or <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> that transforms insoluble rare-earth oxides into soluble chlorides or sulfates. The acidic filtrates are partially neutralized with caustic soda (sodium hydroxide) to pH 3–4. <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Thorium</a> precipitates out of solution as hydroxide and is removed. After that the solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths into their insoluble <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalates</a>. The oxalates are converted to oxides by annealing. The oxides are dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> that excludes one of the main components, <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>, whose oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub>. The solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_nitrate" title="Magnesium nitrate">magnesium nitrate</a> to produce a crystallized mixture of <a href="/wiki/Double_salt" title="Double salt">double salts</a> of rare-earth metals. The salts are separated by <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>. In this process, rare-earth ions are sorbed onto suitable ion-exchange resin by exchange with hydrogen, ammonium or cupric ions present in the resin. The rare earth ions are then selectively washed out by suitable complexing agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-12">[12]</a></sup> Erbium metal is obtained from its oxide or salts by heating with <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003172315000000000♠</span>1450 °C</span> under argon atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erbium-glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="596" data-file-width="596" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Erbium-glass.jpg/220px-Erbium-glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Erbium-glass.jpg/330px-Erbium-glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Erbium-glass.jpg/440px-Erbium-glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erbium-glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Erbium-colored glass</div></div></div>
<p>Erbium's everyday uses are varied. It is commonly used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(photography)" title="Filter (photography)">photographic filter</a>, and because of its resilience it is useful as a metallurgical additive. Other uses:
</p>
<ul><li>Used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a> technology in neutron-absorbing <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>When added to <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a>, erbium lowers hardness and improves workability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbium_oxide" title="Erbium oxide">Erbium oxide</a> has a pink color, and is sometimes used as a colorant for <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic zirconia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>. The glass is then often used in <a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">sunglasses</a> and cheap <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>Erbium-doped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_fibers" title="Optical fibers">optical silica-glass fibers</a> are the active element in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbium-doped_fiber_amplifier" title="Erbium-doped fiber amplifier">erbium-doped fiber amplifiers</a> (EDFAs), which are widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_communications" title="Optical communications">optical communications</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The same fibers can be used to create fiber <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lasers" title="Lasers">lasers</a>. In order to work efficiently, erbium-doped fiber is usually co-doped with glass modifiers/homogenizers, often aluminum or phosphorus.  These dopants help prevent clustering of Er-ions and transfer the energy more efficiently between the Er ions and the signal. Co-doping of optical fiber with Er and Yb is used in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=High-power_Er/Yb_fiber_lasers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="High-power Er/Yb fiber lasers (page does not exist)">high-power Er/Yb fiber lasers</a>. Erbium can also be used in <a href="/wiki/Erbium-doped_waveguide_amplifier" title="Erbium-doped waveguide amplifier">erbium-doped waveguide amplifiers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>An erbium-<a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> alloy Er<sub>3</sub>Ni has an unusually high specific heat capacity at liquid-helium temperatures and is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryocoolers" title="Cryocoolers">cryocoolers</a>; a mixture of 65% Er<sub>3</sub><a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">Co</a> and 35% Er<sub>0.9</sub><a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">Yb</a><sub>0.1</sub>Ni by volume improves the specific heat capacity even more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>A large variety of medical applications (i.e. dermatology, dentistry) utilize erbium ion's <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994294000000000000♠</span>2940 nm</span> emission (see <a href="/wiki/Er:YAG_laser" title="Er:YAG laser">Er:YAG laser</a>), which is highly absorbed in water (<a href="/wiki/Attenuation_coefficient" title="Attenuation coefficient">absorption coefficient</a> about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006120000000000000♠</span>12<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span>/cm</span>). Such shallow tissue deposition of laser energy is necessary for laser surgery, and the efficient production of steam for laser enamel ablation in dentistry.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Erbium does not have a biological role, but erbium salts can stimulate <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. Humans consume 1 milligram of erbium a year on average. The highest concentration of erbium in humans is in the <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, but there is also erbium in the human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidneys" title="Kidneys">kidneys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Erbium is slightly toxic if ingested, but erbium compounds are not toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-3">[3]</a></sup> Metallic erbium in dust form presents a fire and explosion hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
