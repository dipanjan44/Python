<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Urban heat island</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-globalize" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Globe icon." data-file-height="290" data-file-width="350" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" width="48"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this article <b>deal primarily with the United States and do not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You may <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a>, discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Urban_heat_island" title="Talk:Urban heat island">talk page</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">create a new article</a>, as appropriate.</span> <small><i>(August 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2000" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png/300px-HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png/450px-HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png/600px-HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HeatIsland_Kanto_en.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, an example of an urban heat island. Normal temperatures of Tokyo go up more than those of the surrounding area.</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>urban heat island</b> (<b>UHI</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban area</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> that is significantly warmer than its surrounding <a href="/wiki/Rural_area" title="Rural area">rural areas</a> due to human activities.  The <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> difference usually is larger at night than during the day, and is most apparent when <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> are weak. UHI is most noticeable during the <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>. The main cause of the urban heat island effect is from the modification of land surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">Waste heat</a> generated by energy usage is a secondary contributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liyan2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-liyan2012-3">[3]</a></sup> As a population center grows, it tends to expand its area and increase its average temperature. The less-used term <b>heat island</b> refers to any area, populated or not, which is consistently hotter than the surrounding area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Monthly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">rainfall</a> is greater downwind of cities, partially due to the UHI. Increases in heat within urban centers increases the length of <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing seasons</a>, and decreases the occurrence of weak <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. The UHI decreases <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> by increasing the production of pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, and decreases water quality as warmer waters flow into area streams and put stress on their <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.
</p><p>Not all cities have a distinct urban heat island. Mitigation of the urban heat island effect can be accomplished through the use of <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Reflective_surfaces_(climate_engineering)" title="Reflective surfaces (climate engineering)">use of lighter-colored surfaces</a> in urban areas, which reflect more sunlight and absorb less heat.
</p><p>There are concerns raised about possible contribution from urban heat islands to global warming. Research on China<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> indicates that urban heat island effect contributes to climate warming by about 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On the other hand, one 1999 comparison between urban and rural areas proposed that the urban heat island effects have little influence on global mean temperature trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Many studies reveal increases in the severity of the effect with the progress of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phenomenon was first investigated and described by <a href="/wiki/Luke_Howard" title="Luke Howard">Luke Howard</a> in the 1810s, although he was not the one to name the phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newyork_heat_island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1084" data-file-width="540" height="442" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Newyork_heat_island.jpg/220px-Newyork_heat_island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Newyork_heat_island.jpg/330px-Newyork_heat_island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Newyork_heat_island.jpg/440px-Newyork_heat_island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Newyork_heat_island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thermal (top) and vegetation (bottom) locations around <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> via infrared satellite imagery. A comparison of the images shows that where vegetation is dense, temperatures are cooler.</div></div></div>
<p>There are several causes of an urban heat island (UHI); for example, dark surfaces absorb significantly more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>, which causes urban concentrations of roads and buildings to heat more than suburban and rural areas during the day;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-1">[1]</a></sup> materials commonly used in urban areas for pavement and roofs, such as <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>, have significantly different thermal bulk properties (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">heat capacity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a>) and surface radiative properties (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a>) than the surrounding rural areas. This causes a change in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">energy budget</a> of the urban area, often leading to higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okeljnljn_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Okeljnljn-10">[10]</a></sup> Another major reason is the lack of <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> (for example, through lack of vegetation) in urban areas. The U.S. Forest Service found in 2018 that cities in the United States are losing 36 million trees each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> With a decreased amount of vegetation, cities also lose the shade and cooling effect of trees, and the removal of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other causes of a UHI are due to geometric effects. The tall buildings within many urban areas provide multiple surfaces for the reflection and absorption of sunlight, increasing the efficiency with which urban areas are heated. This is called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_canyon" title="Urban canyon">urban canyon effect</a>". Another effect of buildings is the blocking of wind, which also inhibits cooling by <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> and prevents pollutants from dissipating. Waste heat from automobiles, air conditioning, industry, and other sources also contributes to the UHI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liyan2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-liyan2012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  High levels of pollution in urban areas can also increase the UHI, as many forms of pollution change the radiative properties of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okeljnljn_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Okeljnljn-10">[10]</a></sup> As UHI raises the temperature of cities, it also increases the concentration of ozone, a greenhouse gas whose production accelerates with an increase in temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some cities exhibit a heat island effect, largest at night. Seasonally, UHI shows up both in summer and winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  The typical temperature difference is several degrees between the center of the city and surrounding fields.   The difference in temperature between an inner city and its surrounding suburbs is frequently mentioned in weather reports, as in "68 °F (20 °C) downtown, 64 °F (18 °C) in the suburbs". "The annual mean air temperature of a city with 1 million people or more can be 1.8–5.4 °F (1.0–3.0 °C) warmer than its surroundings. In the evening, the difference can be as high as 22 °F (12 °C)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. EPA webpage makes this extreme claim (22F/12C) with a footnote citation to an online scientific paper that nowhere mentions the claim. EPA has been alerted and is investigating. Below, the lead sentence of the Mitigation section cites more-realistic numbers (9F/5C). (August 2015)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The UHI can be defined as either the air temperature difference (the canopy UHI) or the surface temperature difference (surface UHI) between the urban and the rural area. These two show slightly different diurnal and seasonal variability and have different causes <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakra_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakra-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diurnal_behavior">Diurnal behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diurnal behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> stated that "it is well-known that compared to non-urban areas urban heat islands raise night-time temperatures more than daytime temperatures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grida.no_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-grida.no-21">[21]</a></sup>  For example, <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> is 0.2 °C <span class="nowrap">(0.4 °F)</span> cooler for daily maxima and 2.9 °C <span class="nowrap">(5.2 °F)</span> warmer for minima than a nearby rural station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  A description of the very first report of the UHI by <a href="/wiki/Luke_Howard" title="Luke Howard">Luke Howard</a> in the late 1810s said that the urban center of London was warmer at night than the surrounding countryside by 3.7 °F <span class="nowrap">(2.1 °C)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Though the warmer air temperature within the UHI is generally most apparent at night, urban heat islands exhibit significant and somewhat paradoxical diurnal behavior. The air temperature difference between the UHI and the surrounding environment is large at night and small during the day. The opposite is true for skin temperatures of the urban landscape within the UHI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roth-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout the daytime, particularly when the skies are free of clouds, urban surfaces are warmed by the absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>. Surfaces in the urban areas tend to warm faster than those of the surrounding rural areas. By virtue of their high <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacities</a>, urban surfaces act as a giant reservoir of heat energy. For example, concrete can hold roughly 2,000 times as much heat as an equivalent volume of air. As a result, the large daytime surface temperature within the UHI is easily seen via thermal remote sensing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-25">[25]</a></sup>  As is often the case with daytime heating, this warming also has the effect of generating <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> winds within the urban <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a>. It is theorized that, due to the atmospheric mixing that results, the air temperature perturbation within the UHI is generally minimal or nonexistent during the day, though the surface temperatures can reach extremely high levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cam_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cam-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>At night, the situation reverses. The absence of solar heating causes the atmospheric convection to decrease, and the urban boundary layer begins to stabilize. If enough stabilization occurs, an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion layer</a> is formed. This traps urban air near the surface, and keeping surface air warm from the still-warm urban surfaces, forming the nighttime warmer air temperatures within the UHI. Other than the heat retention properties of urban areas, the nighttime maximum in urban canyons could also be due to the blocking of "sky view" during cooling: surfaces lose heat at night principally by radiation to the comparatively cool sky, and this is blocked by the buildings in an urban area. Radiative cooling is more dominant when wind speed is low and the sky is cloudless, and indeed the UHI is found to be largest at night in these conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_behavior">Seasonal behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seasonal behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(December 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The urban heat island temperature difference is not only usually larger at night than during the day, but also larger in winter than in summer. This is particularly so in regions where snow is pervasive, as cities generally retain snow for much shorter time periods than the surrounding countryside (due to higher capacity to retain heat, as well as human-activity such as plowing). This decreases the albedo of the city and thereby magnifies the heating effect. Higher wind speeds in rural areas, particularly in winter, can also function to make them cooler than urban areas. Regions with distinct wet and dry seasons will exhibit a larger urban heat island effect during the dry season. The <a href="/wiki/Time_constant" title="Time constant">thermal time constant</a> of moist soil is much higher than that of dry soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As a result, moist rural soils will cool slower than dry rural soils and act to minimize the nocturnal temperature difference between urban and rural regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction">Prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If a city or town has a good system of taking weather observations the UHI can be measured directly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> An alternative is to use a complex simulation of the location to calculate the UHI, or to use an approximate empirical method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Such models allow the UHI to be included in estimates of future temperatures rises within cities due to climate change.
</p><p>Leonard O. Myrup published the first comprehensive numerical treatment to predict the effects of the urban heat island (UHI) in 1969.  His paper surveys UHI and criticizes then-existing theories as being excessively qualitative. A general purpose, numerical energy budget model is described and applied to the urban atmosphere. Calculations for several special cases as well as a sensitivity analysis are presented. The model is found to predict the correct order of magnitude of the urban temperature excess. The heat island effect is found to be the net result of several competing physical processes. In general, reduced evaporation in the city center and the thermal properties of the city building and paving materials are the dominant parameters. It is suggested that such a model could be used in engineering calculations to improve the climate of existing and future cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_animals">Impact on animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Impact on animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">Ant</a> colonies in urban heat islands have an increased heat tolerance at no cost to cold tolerance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Species that are good at colonizing can utilize conditions provided by urban heat islands to thrive in regions outside of their normal range.  Examples of this include <a href="/wiki/Grey-headed_flying_fox" title="Grey-headed flying fox">grey-headed flying fox</a> (<i>Pteropus poliocephalus</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Common_house_gecko" title="Common house gecko">common house gecko</a> (<i>Hemidactylus frenatus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  Grey-headed flying foxes, found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melbourne,_Australia" title="Melbourne, Australia">Melbourne, Australia</a>, colonized urban habitats following increase in temperatures there. Increased temperatures, causing warmer winter conditions, made the city more similar in climate to the  more northerly wildland habitat of the species.
</p><p>With attempts to mitigate and manage urban heat islands, temperature changes and availability of food and water are reduced.  With temperate climates, urban heat islands will extend the growing season, therefore altering breeding strategies of inhabiting species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  This can be seen the best in the effects that urban heat islands have on water temperature.  With the temperature of the nearby buildings sometimes reaching over 50 degrees different from the near-surface air temperature, precipitation will warm rapidly, causing runoff into nearby streams, lakes and rivers (or other bodies of water) to provide excessive thermal pollution.  The increase in the thermal pollution has the ability to increase water temperature by 20 to 30 degrees.  This increase will cause the fish species inhabiting the body of water to undergo thermal stress and shock due to the rapid change in temperature to their climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urban heat islands caused by cities have altered the <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  Selective pressures like temporal variation in food, predation and water are relaxed causing for a new set of selective forces to roll out.  For example, within urban habitats, insects are more abundant than in rural areas.  Insects are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectotherms" title="Ectotherms">ectotherms</a>. This means that they depend on the temperature of the environment to control their body temperature, making for the warmer climates of the city perfect for their ability to thrive.  A study done in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a> conducted on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Parthenolecanium_quercifex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Parthenolecanium quercifex (page does not exist)">Parthenolecanium quercifex</a> (oak scales), showed that this particular species preferred warmer climates and were therefore found in higher abundance in the urban habitats than on <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees in rural habitats.  Over time of living in urban habitats, they have adapted to thrive in warmer climates than in cooler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of non-native species is heavily dependent on the amount of human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  An example of this can be seen in the populations of <a href="/wiki/American_cliff_swallow" title="American cliff swallow">cliff swallows</a> seen taking nests under the eaves of homes in urban habitats.  They make their homes using the shelter provided  by the humans in the upper regions of homes, allowing for an influx in their populations due to added protection and reduced predator numbers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_impacts_on_weather_and_climate">Other impacts on weather and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other impacts on weather and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aside from the effect on temperature, UHIs can produce secondary effects on local meteorology, including the altering of local wind patterns, the development of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, and the rates of precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  The extra heat provided by the UHI leads to greater upward motion, which can induce additional shower and thunderstorm activity. In addition, the UHI creates during the day a local low pressure area where relatively moist air from its rural surroundings converges, possibly leading to more favorable conditions for cloud formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hee_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hee-42">[42]</a></sup> Rainfall rates downwind of cities are increased between 48% and 116%. Partly as a result of this warming, monthly rainfall is about 28% greater between 20 miles (32 km) to 40 miles (64 km) downwind of cities, compared with upwind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  Some cities show a total precipitation increase of 51%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research has been done in a few areas suggesting that metropolitan areas are less susceptible to weak tornadoes due to the turbulent mixing caused by the warmth of the urban heat island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornado_project_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-tornado_project-45">[45]</a></sup> Using satellite images, researchers discovered that city climates have a noticeable influence on plant growing seasons up to 10 kilometers (6.2 miles) away from a city's edges. Growing seasons in 70 cities in eastern North America were about 15 days longer in urban areas compared to rural areas outside of a city's influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlanta_thermal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="379" data-file-width="540" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Atlanta_thermal.jpg/220px-Atlanta_thermal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Atlanta_thermal.jpg/330px-Atlanta_thermal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Atlanta_thermal.jpg/440px-Atlanta_thermal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlanta_thermal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, showing temperature distribution, with blue showing cool temperatures, red warm, and hot areas appear white.</div></div></div>
<p>UHIs have the potential to directly influence the health and welfare of urban residents. Within the United States alone, an average of 1,000 people die each year due to extreme heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Changnon_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Changnon-47">[47]</a></sup>  As UHIs are characterized by increased temperature, they can potentially increase the magnitude and duration of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> within cities. Research has found that the mortality rate during a heat wave increases exponentially with the maximum temperature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buechley_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buechley-48">[48]</a></sup> an effect that is exacerbated by the UHI. The nighttime effect of UHIs can be particularly harmful during a heat wave, as it deprives urban residents of the cool relief found in rural areas during the night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clarke_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research in the United States suggests that the relationship between extreme temperature and mortality varies by location. Heat is more likely to increase the risk of mortality in cities in the northern part of the country than in the southern regions of the country. For example, when <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, or <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>  experience unusually hot summertime temperatures, elevated levels of illness and death are predicted. In contrast, parts of the country that are mild to hot year-round have a lower public health risk from excessive heat. Research shows that residents of southern cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampa" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>, tend to be acclimated to hot weather conditions and therefore less vulnerable to heat related deaths. However, as a whole, people in the United States appear to be adapting to hotter temperatures further north each decade. However, this might be due to better infrastructure, more modern building design, and better public awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increased temperatures have been reported to cause <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_exhaustion" title="Heat exhaustion">heat exhaustion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_syncope" title="Heat syncope">heat syncope</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heat_cramps" title="Heat cramps">heat cramps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> Some studies have also looked at how severe heat stroke can lead to permanent damage to organ systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> This damage can increase the risk of early mortality because the damage can cause severe impairment in organ function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> Other complications of heat stroke include <a href="/wiki/Acute_respiratory_distress_syndrome" title="Acute respiratory distress syndrome">respiratory distress syndrome</a> in adults and <a href="/wiki/Disseminated_intravascular_coagulation" title="Disseminated intravascular coagulation">disseminated intravascular coagulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> Some researchers have noted that any compromise to the human body's ability to <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulate</a> would in theory increase risk of mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> This includes illnesses that may affect a person's mobility, awareness, or behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-4"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> Researchers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> have noted that individuals with cognitive health issues (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>) are more at risk when faced with high temperatures and "need to take extra care"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-5"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> as cognitive performance has been shown to be differentially affected<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cognitive_Performance_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cognitive_Performance-53">[53]</a></sup> by heat.  People with diabetes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-6"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> are overweight,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> have sleep deprivation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> or have cardiovascular/cerebrovascular conditions should avoid too much heat exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-7"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> Some common medications that have an effect on thermoregulation can also increase the risk of mortality. Specific examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticholinergics" title="Anticholinergics">anticholinergics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-8"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diuretics" title="Diuretics">diuretics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review1_51-9"><a href="#cite_note-Review1-51">[51]</a></sup> phenotiazines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-5"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbiturates" title="Barbiturates">barbiturates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-6"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> Not only health, but heat can also affect behavior. A U.S. study suggests that heat can make people more irritable and aggressive, noting that violent crimes increased by 4.58 out of 100,000 for every one degree increase in temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>A researcher found that high UHI intensity correlates with increased concentrations of air pollutants that gathered at night, which can affect the next day's <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air quality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-54">[54]</a></sup> These pollutants include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulate matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-7"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup> The production of these pollutants combined with the higher temperatures in UHIs can quicken the production of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-54">[54]</a></sup> Ozone at surface level is considered to be a harmful pollutant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-54">[54]</a></sup> Studies suggest that increased temperatures in UHIs can increase polluted days but also note that other factors (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">air pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud cover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a>) can also have an effect on pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_54-4"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> notes that it "is difficult to make valid projections of heat-related illness and death under varying climate change scenarios" and that "heat–related deaths are preventable, as evidenced by the decline of all-cause mortality during heat events over the past 35 years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-55">[55]</a></sup> However, some studies suggest the possibility that health impacts from UHIs are disproportionate because the impacts can be unevenly distributed based on a variety of factors (e.g. age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_52-8"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_article_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz_article-56">[56]</a></sup> ethnicity and socioeconomic status<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harlan_article_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harlan_article-57">[57]</a></sup>). This raises the possibility of health impacts from UHIs being an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> issue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inequality_of_tree_canopy_cover">Inequality of tree canopy cover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Inequality of tree canopy cover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt>Relationship between neighborhood income and tree canopy cover</dt></dl>
<p>In recent years, researchers have discovered a strong correlation between neighborhood income and tree canopy cover. In 2010, researchers at Auburn University and University of Southern California found that the presence of trees are "highly responsive to changes in [neighborhood] income."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demand_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Demand-58">[58]</a></sup>  Low-income neighborhoods tend to have significantly fewer trees than neighborhoods with higher incomes. They described this unequal distribution of trees as a demand for "luxury," rather than "necessity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Income_Inequality_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Income_Inequality-59">[59]</a></sup>  According to the study, "for every 1 percent increase in per capita income, demand for forest cover increased by 1.76 percent. But when income dropped by the same amount, demand decreased by 1.26 percent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Income_Inequality_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Income_Inequality-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Trees are a necessary feature in combating the urban heat island effect because they reduce air temperatures by 10 °F or 5.5 °C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and surface temperatures by up to 20-45 °F or 11-25 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Researchers hypothesized that less-well-off neighborhoods do not have the financial resources to plant and maintain trees. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affluent" title="Affluent">Affluent</a> neighborhoods can afford more trees, on "both public and private property."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Part of this is also that wealthier homeowners and communities can afford more land, which can be kept open as <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">green space</a>, whereas poorer ones are often rentals, where landowners try to maximize their <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profit</a> by putting as much <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">density</a> as possible on their land.
</p><p>Additionally, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blogging" title="Blogging">blogging</a> sites overlay <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> provided by <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> data to confirm or debunk the aforementioned research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_nearby_water_bodies">Impact on nearby water bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Impact on nearby water bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UHIs also impair water quality. Hot pavement and rooftop surfaces transfer their excess heat to stormwater, which then drains into storm sewers and raises water temperatures as it is released into streams, rivers, ponds, and lakes. Additionally, increased urban water body temperatures lead to a decrease in diversity in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In August 2001, rains over <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Rapids,_Iowa" title="Cedar Rapids, Iowa">Cedar Rapids, Iowa</a>, led to a 10.5C (18.9F) rise in the nearby stream within one hour, which led to a fish kill. Since the temperature of the rain was comparatively cool, it could be attributed to the hot pavement of the city. Similar events have been documented across the American Midwest, as well as Oregon and California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Rapid temperature changes can be stressful to aquatic ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">Permeable pavements</a> may mitigate these effects by percolating water through the pavement into subsurface storage areas where it can be dissipate through absorption and evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_energy_usage">Impact on energy usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact on energy usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="974" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fb/NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg/250px-NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fb/NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg/375px-NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fb/NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg/500px-NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA_SLC_Urban_Heat_Island_Effect_Roof.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Images of Salt Lake City, Utah, show positive correlation between white reflective roofs and cooler temperatures. Image A depicts an aerial view of <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a>, Utah, site of 865,000-square-foot (80,400 m<sup>2</sup>) white reflective roof. Image B is a thermal infrared image of same area, showing hot (red and yellow) and cool (green and blue) spots. The reflective vinyl roof, not absorbing solar radiation, is shown in blue surrounded by other hot spots.</div></div></div><p>Another consequence of urban heat islands is the increased energy required for <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> in cities that are in comparatively hot climates. The Heat Island Group estimates that the heat island effect costs <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">US$</a>100 million per year in energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Conversely, those that are in cold climates such as <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow, Russia</a> would have less demand for heating. However, through the implementation of heat island reduction strategies, significant annual net energy savings have been calculated for northern locations such as Chicago, Salt Lake City, and Toronto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG/220px-20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG/330px-20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG/440px-20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20080708_Chicago_City_Hall_Green_Roof.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green roof of <a href="/wiki/Chicago_City_Hall" title="Chicago City Hall">City Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The temperature difference between urban areas and the surrounding suburban or rural areas can be as much as 5 °C (9.0 °F). Nearly 40 percent of that increase is due to the prevalence of dark roofs, with the remainder coming from dark-colored pavement and the declining presence of vegetation. The heat island effect can be counteracted slightly by using white or reflective materials to build houses, roofs, pavements, and roads, thus increasing the overall <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Relative to remedying the other sources of the problem, replacing dark roofing requires the least amount of investment for the most immediate return. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cool_roof" title="Cool roof">cool roof</a> made from a reflective material such as vinyl reflects at least 75 percent of the sun's rays, and emit at least 70 percent of the solar radiation absorbed by the building envelope. Asphalt built-up roofs (BUR), by comparison, reflect 6 percent to 26 percent of solar radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using light-colored concrete has proven effective in reflecting up to 50% more light than asphalt and reducing ambient temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_2_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave_2-72">[72]</a></sup> A low albedo value, characteristic of black asphalt, absorbs a large percentage of solar heat creating warmer near-surface temperatures. Paving with light-colored concrete, in addition to replacing asphalt with light-colored concrete, communities may be able to lower average temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldchange_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldchange-73">[73]</a></sup> However, research into the interaction between reflective pavements and buildings has found that, unless the nearby buildings are fitted with reflective glass, solar radiation reflected off light-colored pavements can increase building temperatures, increasing air conditioning demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yaghoobian_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yaghoobian-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>A second option is to increase the amount of well-watered vegetation. These two options can be combined with the implementation of <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a>. Green roofs are excellent insulators during the warm weather months and the plants cool the surrounding environment. Air quality is improved as the plants absorb carbon dioxide with concomitant production of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The city of New York determined that the cooling potential per area was highest for street trees, followed by living roofs, light covered surface, and open space planting. From the standpoint of cost effectiveness, light surfaces, light roofs, and curbside planting have lower costs per temperature reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>A hypothetical "cool communities" program in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> has projected that urban temperatures could be reduced by approximately 3 °C (5 °F) after planting ten million trees, reroofing five million homes, and painting one-quarter of the roads at an estimated cost of US$1 billion, giving estimated annual benefits of US$170 million from reduced air-conditioning costs and US$360 million in smog related health savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heat_Islands_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heat_Islands-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mitigation strategies include:
</p>
<ul><li><b>White roofs</b>: Painting rooftops white has become a common strategy to reduce the heat island effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-79">[79]</a></sup> In cities, there are many dark colored surfaces that absorb the heat of the sun in turn lowering the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-79">[79]</a></sup> White rooftops allow high solar reflectance and high solar emittance, increasing the albedo of the city or area the effect is occurring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-79">[79]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Green roofs</b>: Green roofs are another method of decreasing the urban heat island effect. Green roofery is the practice of having vegetation on a roof; such as having trees or a garden. The plants that are on the roof  increase the albedo and decreases the urban heat island effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_79-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-79">[79]</a></sup> This method has been studied and criticized for the fact that green roofs are affected by climatic conditions, green roof variables are hard to measure, and are very complex systems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_79-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-79">[79]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Planting trees in cities</b>: Planting trees around the city can be another way of increasing albedo and decreasing the urban heat island effect. Trees absorb <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and provide shade. It is recommended to plant <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> trees because they can provide many benefits such as more shade in the summer and not blocking warmth of winter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Green_parking_lot" title="Green parking lot">Green parking lots</a>:</b> Green parking lots use surfaces other than asphalt and vegetation to limit the impact urban heat island effect.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Mitigation_policies.2C_measures_and_other_strategies"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_policies,_measures_and_other_strategies">Mitigation policies, measures and other strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mitigation policies, measures and other strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="AB32_scoping_plan">AB32 scoping plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: AB32 scoping plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AB32 required the California Air Resources Board to create a scoping plan. This plan is California's approach on how to carry out their goal of  combatting climate change by reducing greenhouse emissions by 2020 to levels from the 1990s. The scoping plan had four primary programs, advanced clean cars, cap and trade, renewables portfolio standard and <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">low-carbon fuel standard</a> all geared toward increased energy efficiency. The plan has main strategies to reduce green house gases such as having monetary incentives, regulations and voluntary actions. Every five years the scoping plan is updated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>The advanced clean car rules program</b> was made to reduce tail pipe emissions. The Air Resources Board approved the program to control emissions for newer models from the year 2017 to 2025. Some of their goals by 2025 are to have more environmentally superior cars to be available in different models and different types of cars. New automobiles will emit 34 percent fewer global warming gases and 75 percent fewer smog-forming emissions. And if fully implemented consumers can save an average of $6,000 over the life of the car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>The renewable portfolio standard</b> mandates to increase <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> from a variety of sources such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> and wind. Investor-owned utilities, community choice aggregators and electric service providers are required to increase <a href="/wiki/Procurement" title="Procurement">procurement</a> to 33% by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Low carbon fuel standards</b> is administered by the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> and attempts to make wider choice of cleaner fuels to Californians. Producers of petroleum-based fuels are required to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_intensity" title="Carbon intensity">carbon intensity</a> of their products to 10 percent in 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Cap and trade</b> is designed to reduce the effects of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> by setting a cap on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> released into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. The cap will decline approximately three percent each year in 2013. The trading will create incentives to reduce the effects of climate change in California communities by reducing greenhouse gases through investments in clean technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><b>Clean Air Act</b>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a> has initiated several air quality requirements that help reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground-level_ozone" title="Ground-level ozone">ground-level ozone</a> that leads to urban heat islands. In the Clean Air Act, one of the EPA's chief policies, there are certain regulations that are put in place to ensure the state's emissions stay below a certain level. Included in the Clean Air Act, all states must set forth a <a href="/wiki/State_Implementation_Plan" title="State Implementation Plan">State Implementation Plan</a> (SIP) which is designed to guarantee all states meet a central air quality standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa.gov_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>State implementation plans and policies</b>
</p>
<ul><li><b>The Emerging and Voluntary Measures Policy</b> allows a state to add unconventional forms of heat island mitigation. This can include removing <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> after it has already been emitted into air, water, or soil. These measures are not implemented into law, but they do make it possible for certain parties to voluntarily become more efficient. The purpose of this policy is for all polluting sources to follow by example and use the most successful forms of mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><b>The Guidance on State Implementation Plan Credits for Emissions Reductions from Electric-Sector Energy Efficiency or Renewable Energy Measures</b> is an educational tool for states to create an up-to-date and well-organized SIP. It allows states to include plans that meet the guidelines or plans that exceed expectations. Based on the success of their SIP, some states can have their plans incorporated into other SIPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><b>The Bundled Measures Policy</b> authorizes different factions within the state to collaborate on mitigation projects. This policy takes a more of a community-based approach by adding several groups for the purpose of multiple perspectives and inventive approaches. The Bundled Measures Policy is one method that generates co-benefits for both parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> In example, if a partaking business were to add cool roofs, there will be a reduction in greenhouse gases which is beneficial for the environment as well as the need for excess energy which is beneficial for the business.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation_of_policies">Implementation of policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Implementation of policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Seattle Green Factor, a multifaceted system for urban <a href="/wiki/Landscaping" title="Landscaping">landscaping</a>, has seen much success in the mitigation of urban heat islands. The program focuses on areas that are prone to high pollution, such as business districts. There are strict guidelines for any new <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> that exceeds roughly 20 parking spaces, and this platform helps developers physically see their levels of pollution while trying different methods of construction to figure out the most effective course of action. Seattle has correspondingly produced a "score sheet" for cities to use in their city planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa.gov_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa.gov-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AB32_and_urban_heat_islands">AB32 and urban heat islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: AB32 and urban heat islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Urban heat islands increase demand for energy consumption during the summer when temperatures rise. As a result of increased energy consumption, there is an increase in air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions. This policy focuses on lowering greenhouse emissions, which contributes to lowering the heat island effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EPA_Compendium_of_Strategies">EPA Compendium of Strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: EPA Compendium of Strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This compendium focuses on a variety of issues dealing with urban heat islands. They describe how <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_heat_islands" title="Urban heat islands">urban heat islands</a> are created, who is affected, and how people can make a difference to reduce temperature. It also shows examples of policies and voluntary actions by state and local governments to reduce the effect of urban heat islands<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incentives">Incentives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Incentives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Sacramento_Municipal_Utility_District" title="Sacramento Municipal Utility District">Sacramento Municipal Utility District</a></b> (SMUD) and the Sacramento Tree Foundation have partnered to provide the city of Sacramento shade trees for free. The program allows citizens to receive trees from four to seven feet tall. They also give them fertilizer, and delivery, all at no cost. They encourage citizens to plant their trees to benefit their home by reducing air conditioning costs. Approximately more than 450,000 shade trees have been planted in the Sacramento area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>The Eco-Roof Incentive Program</b>: In Canada, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grants" title="Grants">grants</a> are distributed throughout Toronto for installing green and cool roofs on residential and commercial buildings. This will reduce usage of energy and lower green house gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Tree vitalize</b>: This program is a partnership with multiple entities that focuses on helping restore tree cover in the city, it also educates citizens about the positive effects of trees on climate change and the urban heat island effect. And another goal they have is to build capacity among local governments to understand, protect and restore their urban trees. Because there is a need for educating citizens about the maintenance of trees, Treevitalize provides nine hours of classroom and field training to community residents.  The classes cover a variety of topics such as tree identification, pruning, tree biology, and proper species selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-94">[94]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weatherization">Weatherization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Weatherization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> Weatherization Assistance Program helps low income recipients by covering their heating bills and helping the families to make their homes energy efficient. In addition, this program allows states to also use the funds to install cooling efficiency measures such as shading devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>

<ul><li><b>Tree Utah</b>: a statewide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organization</a> is dedicated to educating communities about the environmental and social benefits provided by trees. They are also committed to planting thousands of trees throughout the state of Utah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_protection_ordinances">Tree protection ordinances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Tree protection ordinances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>A variety of local governments have implemented tree and landscape ordinances, which will help communities by providing shade during summer. Tree protection is an ordinance that does not allow someone to <a href="/wiki/Prune" title="Prune">prune</a> or remove trees without a city permit. An example is the city of <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale, California</a>: Through the Indigenous Tree Ordinance, the city of Glendale protects the following species of trees, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_sycamore" title="California sycamore">California sycamore</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_live_oak" title="Coast live oak">coast live oak</a>, mesa oak, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valley_oak" title="Valley oak">valley oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scrub_oak" title="Scrub oak">scrub oak</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_bay" title="California bay">California bay</a>. Anyone who is planning on removing or <a href="/wiki/Pruning" title="Pruning">trimming</a> the trees has to obtain an indigenous tree permit. Within the permit they have to provide detailed information about the number of trees affected, trunk diameter and the health of the tree itself. They also have to submit photographs of the site, and a site plan sketch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></li>
<li>Another example is the city of <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley</a>, California. The tree protection ordinance prohibits the removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_live_oak" title="Coast live oak">coast live oak</a> trees and any excessive <a href="/wiki/Pruning" title="Pruning">pruning</a> that can cause harm to the tree is also prohibited. The only exception is if the tree is poses a danger to life or limb and danger to the property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></li>
<li>The city of <a href="/wiki/Visalia,_California" title="Visalia, California">Visalia, California</a>, has implemented a street tree ordinance intended to promote and regulate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planting" title="Planting">planting</a>, maintenance, and protection of street trees within the city. Their <a href="/wiki/Local_ordinance" title="Local ordinance">ordinance</a> does not allow street trees to be altered, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pruned" title="Pruned">pruned</a> or removed. Street trees are also protected during construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></li></ul>

By having many trees, we can cool the city heat by approximately 10 degrees to 20 degrees, which will help reducing ozone and helping communities that are mostly affected by the effects of climate change and urban heat islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_building_programs">Green building programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Green building programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Voluntary green building programs have been promoting the mitigation of the heat island effect for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> For example, one of the ways for a site to earn points under the US Green Building Council's (USGBC) <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) Green Building Rating System is to take action that reduces heat islands, minimizing impacts on microclimates and human and wildlife habitats. Credits associated with reflective roofing or planted roofs can help a building achieve LEED certification. Buildings also receive credits by providing shade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Similarly, The <a class="external text" href="http://www.thegbi.org/" rel="nofollow">Green Building Initiative</a> (GBI)'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Globes" title="Green Globes">Green Globes</a> program awards points to sites that take measures to decrease a building's energy consumption and reduce the heat island effect. As many as 10 points may be awarded to sites with roof coverage from vegetation, highly reflective materials, or a combination of the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_analysis">Cost analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Cost analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Every year in the U.S. 15% of energy goes towards the <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> of buildings in these urban heat islands. According to Rosenfeld et al., "the air conditioning demand has risen 10% within the last 40 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld,_Arthur_H._1998_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld,_Arthur_H._1998-109">[109]</a></sup> Home and business owners alike can benefit from building a cool community. A decrease in energy usage directly correlates to cost efficiency. Areas with substantial <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> and reflective surface materials used for roofs of houses, pavement, and roads are proven to be more effective and cost efficient.
</p><p>In a case study of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Basin" title="Los Angeles Basin">Los Angeles Basin</a>, simulations showed that even when trees are not strategically placed in these urban heat islands, they can still aid in minimization of pollutants and energy reduction. It is estimated that with this wide-scale implementation, the city of Los Angeles can annually save $100M with most of the savings coming from cool roofs, lighter colored pavement, and the planting of trees. With a citywide implementation, added benefits from the lowering smog-level would result in at least one billion dollars of saving per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld,_Arthur_H._1998_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld,_Arthur_H._1998-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cost efficiency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_roofs" title="Green roofs">green roofs</a> is quite high because of several reasons. According to Carter, "A conventional roof is estimated to be $83.78/m<sup>2</sup> while a green roof was estimated at $158.82/m<sup>2</sup>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> For one, green roofs have over double the lifespan of a conventional roof, effectively decelerating the amount of roof replacements every year. In addition to roof-life, green roofs add <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> management reducing fees for utilities. The cost for green roofs is more in the beginning, but over a period of time, their efficiency provides financial as well as health benefits.
</p><p>In Capital E Analysis' conclusions of the financial benefits of green buildings, it was determined that green roofs successfully lowered energy usage and raised health benefits. For every square foot of green roof used in one study the savings amounted to $5.80 energy-wise. There were also savings seen in the emissions, water, and maintenance categories. Overall, the savings amounted to $52.90-$71.30 on average while the cost of going green totaled -$3.00-$5.00.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because some parts of some cities may be hotter than their surroundings, concerns have been raised that the effects of <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> might be misinterpreted as an increase in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global temperature</a>. Such effects are removed by <a href="/wiki/Homogenization_(climate)" title="Homogenization (climate)">homogenization</a> from the raw climate record by comparing urban stations with surrounding stations. While the "heat island" warming is an important local effect, there is no evidence that it biases <i>trends</i> in the homogenized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_temperature_record" title="Historical temperature record">historical temperature record</a>. For example, urban and rural trends are very similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grida.no_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-grida.no-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Third_Assessment_Report_from_the_IPCC"></span>
The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">Third Assessment Report from the IPCC</a> says:
</p>
<blockquote><p>However, over the Northern Hemisphere land areas where urban heat islands are most apparent, both the trends of lower-<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">tropospheric</a> temperature and surface air temperature show no significant differences. In fact, the lower-tropospheric temperatures warm at a slightly greater rate over North America (about 0.28°C/decade using satellite data) than do the surface temperatures (0.27°C/decade), although again the difference is not statistically significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grida.no_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-grida.no-21">[21]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p><span id="Ground_temperature_measurements"></span>
Ground temperature measurements, like most weather observations, are logged by location. Their siting predates the massive sprawl, roadbuilding programs, and high- and medium-rise expansions which contribute to the UHI. More importantly, station logs allow sites in question to be filtered easily from data sets. Doing so, the presence of heat islands is visible, but overall trends change in magnitude, not direction.
The effects of the urban heat island may be overstated. One study stated, "Contrary to generally accepted wisdom, no statistically significant impact of <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> could be found in annual temperatures." This was done by using satellite-based night-light detection of urban areas, and more thorough homogenisation of the time series (with corrections, for example, for the tendency of surrounding rural stations to be slightly higher in elevation, and thus cooler, than urban areas). If its conclusion is accepted, then it is necessary to "unravel the mystery of how a global temperature time series created partly from urban <a href="/wiki/In_situ#Earth_and_atmospheric_sciences" title="In situ">in situ</a> stations could show no contamination from urban warming."  The main conclusion is that <a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">microscale</a> and local-scale impacts dominate the <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> impact of the urban heat island. Many sections of towns may be warmer than rural sites, but <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observations</a> are likely to be made in park "cool islands."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peterson_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all cities show a warming relative to their rural surroundings. After trends were adjusted in urban <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> around the world to match rural stations in their regions, in an effort to homogenise the temperature record, in 42 percent of cases, cities were getting <i>cooler</i> relative to their surroundings rather than warmer. One reason is that urban areas are heterogeneous, and weather stations are often sited in "cool islands" – parks, for example – within urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Studies_in_2004_and_2006"></span>
Studies in 2004 and 2006 attempted to test the urban heat island theory, by comparing temperature readings taken on calm nights with those taken on windy nights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker2004_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker2004-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>  If the urban heat island theory is correct then instruments should have recorded a bigger temperature rise for calm nights than for windy ones, because wind blows excess heat away from cities and away from the measuring instruments. There was no difference between the calm and windy nights, and one study said that "we show that, globally, temperatures over land have risen as much on windy nights as on calm nights, indicating that the observed overall warming is not a consequence of urban development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker2004b_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker2004b-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black2004_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black2004-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="skeptics"></span>
A view often held by those who reject the evidence for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is that much of the temperature increase seen in land based thermometers could be due to an increase in urbanization and the siting of measurement stations in urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black2004_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-Black2004-117">[117]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Ross_McKitrick" title="Ross McKitrick">Ross McKitrick</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_J._Michaels" title="Patrick J. Michaels">Patrick J. Michaels</a> conducted a statistical study of surface-temperature data <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">regressed</a> against socioeconomic indicators, and concluded that about half of the observed warming trend (for 1979–2002) could be accounted for by the residual UHI effects in the corrected temperature data set they studied—which had already been processed to remove the (modeled) UHI contribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> Critics of this paper, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gavin_A._Schmidt" title="Gavin A. Schmidt">Gavin A. Schmidt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> have said the results can be explained away as an artifact of spatial <a href="/wiki/Autocorrelation" title="Autocorrelation">autocorrelation</a>. McKittrick and Nicolas Nierenberg stated further that "the evidence for contamination of climatic data is robust across numerous data sets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>The preliminary results of an independent assessment carried out by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Earth_Surface_Temperature" title="Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature">Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature</a> group, and made available to the public in October 2011, found that among other scientific concerns raised by skeptics, the urban heat island effect did not bias the results obtained by NOAA, the Hadley Centre and NASA's GISS. The Berkeley Earth group also confirmed that over the past 50 years the land surface warmed by 0.911 °C, and their results closely matched those obtained from earlier studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature1011_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature1011-122">[122]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily1011_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily1011-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian1011_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian1011-124">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcBlack_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcBlack-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1011_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1011-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Climate_Change_2007"></span>
<i>Climate Change 2007</i>, the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report from the IPCC</a> states the following.
</p>
<blockquote><p>Studies that have looked at hemispheric and global scales conclude that any urban-related trend is an order of magnitude smaller than decadal and longer time-scale trends evident in the series (e.g., Jones et al., 1990; Peterson et al., 1999). This result could partly be attributed to the omission from the gridded data set of a small number of sites (&lt;1%) with clear urban-related warming trends. In a worldwide set of about 270 stations, Parker (2004, 2006) noted that warming trends in night minimum temperatures over the period 1950 to 2000 were not enhanced on calm nights, which would be the time most likely to be affected by urban warming. Thus, the global land warming trend discussed is very unlikely to be influenced significantly by increasing urbanisation (Parker, 2006). ... Accordingly, this assessment adds the same level of urban warming uncertainty as in the TAR: 0.006°C per decade since 1900 for land, and 0.002°C per decade since 1900 for blended land with ocean, as ocean UHI is zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p><span id="2014_study"></span>
A 2014 study published in the <a href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America</a> looks at the potential of large-scale urban adaptation to counteract the effects of long-term global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The researchers calculate that without any adaptive urban design, by 2100 the expansion of existing U.S. cities into regional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalopolis_(city_type)" title="Megalopolis (city type)">megalopolises</a> could raise near-surface temperatures between 1 and 2 degrees Celsius over large regions, "a significant fraction of the 21st-century greenhouse gas-induced climate change simulated by global climate models." Large-scale adaptive design could completely offset this increase, however. For example, the temperature increase in California was calculated to be as high as 1.31 degrees Celsius, but a 100% deployment of "cool roofs" would result in a temperature drop of 1.47 degrees Celsius—more than the increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_cold_island">Urban cold island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_heat_island&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Urban cold island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ironically, the same urban area that is hotter in the day, can be colder than surrounding rural areas at ground level at night, leading to a new term <i>urban cold island</i>.   Snow cover in rural areas, for example, insulates plants.  This was an unexpected discovery when studying the response of plants to urban environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p>
