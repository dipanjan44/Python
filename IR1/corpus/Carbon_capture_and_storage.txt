<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon capture and storage</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_utilization" title="Carbon capture and utilization">Carbon capture and utilization</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="530" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/350px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/525px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/700px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic showing both terrestrial and geological sequestration of carbon dioxide emissions from a coal-fired plant</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon capture and storage</b> (<b>CCS</b>) (or <b>carbon capture and sequestration</b> or <b>carbon control and sequestration</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the process of capturing waste <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) from large <a href="/wiki/Point_source_pollution" title="Point source pollution">point sources</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">fossil fuel power plants</a>, transporting it to a storage site, and depositing it where it will not enter the atmosphere, normally an underground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(stratigraphy)" title="Formation (stratigraphy)">geological formation</a>. The aim is to prevent the release of large quantities of CO<sub>2</sub> into the atmosphere (from fossil fuel use in power generation and other industries). It is a potential means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigating</a> the contribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_emissions" title="Fossil fuel emissions">fossil fuel emissions</a> to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Although CO<sub>2</sub> has been injected into geological formations for several decades for various purposes, including <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>, the long term storage of CO<sub>2</sub> is a relatively new concept. The first commercial example was the <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Another example is <a href="/wiki/SaskPower" title="SaskPower">SaskPower's</a> <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Dam_Power_Station" title="Boundary Dam Power Station">Boundary Dam</a>. 'CCS' can also be used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">scrubbing</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> from ambient air as a <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a> technique.
</p><p>An integrated pilot-scale CCS power plant was to begin operating in September 2008 in the eastern German power plant <a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe</a> run by utility <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a>, to test the technological feasibility and economic efficiency. CCS applied to a modern conventional power plant could reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions to the atmosphere by approximately 80–90% compared to a plant without CCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> estimates that the economic potential of CCS could be between 10% and 55% of the total carbon mitigation effort until year 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide can be captured out of air or fossil fuel power plant <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> using <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon scrubbing</a>), membrane gas separation, or adsorption technologies. Amines are the leading carbon scrubbing technology.
Capturing and compressing CO<sub>2</sub> may increase the energy needs of a coal-fired CCS plant by 25–40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup> These and other system costs are estimated to increase the cost per watt-hour energy produced by 21–91% for fossil fuel power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup> Applying the technology to existing plants would be more expensive, especially if they are far from a sequestration site. A 2005 industry report suggests that with successful research, development and deployment (RD&amp;D), sequestered coal-based electricity generation in 2025 may cost less than unsequestered coal-based electricity generation today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Storage of the CO<sub>2</sub> is envisaged either in deep geological formations, or in the form of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>. Deep ocean storage is not currently considered feasible due to the associated effect of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenfacts.org_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenfacts.org-7">[7]</a></sup> Geological formations are currently considered the most promising sequestration sites. The <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Technology_Laboratory" title="National Energy Technology Laboratory">National Energy Technology Laboratory</a> (NETL) reported that North America has enough storage capacity for more than 900 years worth of carbon dioxide at current production rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A general problem is that long term predictions about submarine or underground storage security are very difficult and uncertain, and there is still the risk that CO<sub>2</sub> might leak into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture">Capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">Carbon dioxide scrubber</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_air_capture" title="Carbon dioxide air capture">carbon dioxide air capture</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_coal" title="Clean coal">Clean coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">Amine gas treating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Membrane_gas_separation" title="Membrane gas separation">Membrane gas separation</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">Metal-organic framework</a></div>
<p>Capturing CO<sub>2</sub> is most effective at point sources, such as large fossil fuel or biomass energy facilities, industries with major CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_processing" title="Natural gas processing">natural gas processing</a>, synthetic fuel plants and fossil fuel-based <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a> plants. Extracting CO<sub>2</sub> from air is also possible,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> although the far lower concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in air compared to combustion sources presents significant engineering challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organisms that produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">fermentation</a> generate cool, essentially pure CO<sub>2</sub> that can be pumped underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Fermentation produces slightly less CO<sub>2</sub> than ethanol by weight.
</p><p>Flue gas from the combustion of coal in oxygen has a large concentration of CO2, about 10-15% CO<sub>2</sub>  whereas natural gas power plant <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> is about 5–10% CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">[13]</a></sup> Therefore, it is more energy and cost efficient to capture CO2 from coal-fired power plants.
Impurities in CO<sub>2</sub> streams, like sulfurs and water, could have a significant effect on their phase behaviour and could pose a significant threat of increased corrosion of pipeline and well materials. In instances where CO<sub>2</sub> impurities exist, especially with air capture, a scrubbing separation process would be needed to initially clean the flue gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to the Wallula Energy Resource Center in Washington state, by gasifying coal, it is possible to capture approximately 65% of carbon dioxide embedded in it and sequester it in a solid form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Broadly, three different configurations of technologies for capture exist: post-combustion, pre-combustion, and oxyfuel combustion:
</p>
<ul><li>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post_combustion_capture" title="Post combustion capture">post combustion capture</a></i>, the CO<sub>2</sub> is removed after combustion of the fossil fuel — this is the scheme that would be applied to fossil-fuel burning power plants. Here, carbon dioxide is captured from <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">power stations</a> or other large point sources. The technology is well understood and is currently used in other industrial applications, although not at the same scale as might be required in a commercial scale power station. Post combustion capture is most popular in research because existing fossil fuel power plants can be retrofitted to include CCS technology in this configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>The technology for <i>pre-combustion</i> is widely applied in fertilizer, chemical, gaseous fuel (H<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>), and power production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In these cases, the fossil fuel is partially oxidized, for instance in a <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasifier</a>. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> (CO and H<sub>2</sub>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">shifted</a> into CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>. The resulting CO<sub>2</sub> can be captured from a relatively pure exhaust stream. The H<sub>2</sub> can now be used as fuel; the carbon dioxide is removed before combustion takes place. There are several advantages and disadvantages when compared to conventional post combustion carbon dioxide capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> is removed after combustion of fossil fuels, but before the flue gas is expanded to atmospheric pressure. This scheme is applied to new fossil fuel burning power plants, or to existing plants where re-powering is an option. The capture before expansion, i.e. from pressurized gas, is standard in almost all industrial CO<sub>2</sub> capture processes, at the same scale as will be required for utility power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_combustion" title="Oxy-fuel combustion">oxy-fuel combustion</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the fuel is burned in oxygen instead of air. To limit the resulting flame temperatures to levels common during conventional combustion, cooled flue gas is recirculated and injected into the combustion chamber. The flue gas consists of mainly carbon dioxide and water vapour, the latter of which is condensed through cooling. The result is an almost pure carbon dioxide stream that can be transported to the sequestration site and stored. Power plant processes based on oxyfuel combustion are sometimes referred to as "zero emission" cycles, because the CO<sub>2</sub> stored is not a fraction removed from the flue gas stream (as in the cases of pre- and post-combustion capture) but the flue gas stream itself. A certain fraction of the CO<sub>2</sub> generated during combustion will inevitably end up in the condensed water. To warrant the label "zero emission" the water would thus have to be treated or disposed of appropriately. The technique is promising, but the initial air separation step demands a lot of energy.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_separation_technologies">CO<sub>2</sub> separation technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CO2 separation technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide can be separated out of air or flue gas with <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Membrane_gas_separation" title="Membrane gas separation">membrane gas separation</a> technologies. Absorption, or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon scrubbing</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amines" title="Amines">amines</a> is currently the dominant capture technology. Membrane and adsorption technologies are still in the developmental research stages, initiating primary pilot plants in the near future. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-Organic_Framework" title="Metal-Organic Framework">Metal-Organic Frameworks</a> (MOFs) are a novel class of materials that offer promise for carbon capture using adsorption technologies.
</p><p>Carbon dioxide adsorbs to a MOF through <a href="/wiki/Physisorption" title="Physisorption">physisorption</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorption</a> based on the porosity and <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_and_specificity" title="Sensitivity and specificity">selectivity</a> of the MOF leaving behind a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a> poor gas stream that is more environmentally friendly. The carbon dioxide is then stripped off the MOF using temperature swing adsorption (TSA) or pressure swing adsorption (PSA) so the MOF can be reused. Adsorbents and absorbents require regeneration steps where the CO<sub>2</sub> is removed from the sorbent or solution that collected it out of the flue gas in order for the sorbent or solution to be reused. Monoethanolamine (MEA) solutions, the leading amine for capturing CO<sub>2</sub>, have a heat capacity between 3-4 J/g K since they are mostly water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Higher heat capacities add to the energy penalty in the solvent regeneration step. Thus, to optimize a MOF for carbon capture, low heat capacities and heats of adsorption are desired. Additionally, high working capacity and high selectivity are desirable in order to capture as much CO<sub>2</sub> as possible from the flue gas. However, there is an energy trade off with selectivity and energy expenditure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> As the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> captured increases, the energy, and therefore cost, required to regenerate increases. A large drawback of using MOFs for CCS is the limitations imposed by their chemical and thermal stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Current research is looking to optimize MOF properties for CCS, but it has proven difficult to find these optimizations that also result in a stable MOF. Metal reservoirs are also a limiting factor to the potential success of MOFs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Capture is attributed to about two thirds of the total cost of CCS, making it limit the wide-scale deployment of CCS technologies. To optimize a CO<sub>2</sub> capture process would significantly increase the feasibility of CCS since the transport and storage steps of CCS are rather mature technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>An alternate method under development is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_looping_combustion" title="Chemical looping combustion">chemical looping combustion</a> (CLC). Chemical looping uses a metal oxide as a solid oxygen carrier. Metal oxide particles react with a solid, liquid or gaseous fuel in a <a href="/wiki/Fluidization" title="Fluidization">fluidized bed</a> combustor, producing solid metal particles and a mixture of carbon dioxide and water vapor.  The water vapor is condensed, leaving pure carbon dioxide, which can then be sequestered.  The solid metal particles are circulated to another fluidized bed where they react with air, producing heat and regenerating metal oxide particles that are recirculated to the fluidized bed combustor. A variant of chemical looping is <a href="/wiki/Calcium_looping" title="Calcium looping">calcium looping</a>, which uses the alternating carbonation and then calcination of a <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> based carrier as a means of capturing CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_air_capture">Direct air capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Direct air capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Direct air capture refers to the process of removing CO<sub>2</sub> directly from the ambient air (as opposed to from point sources). Combining direct air capture with carbon storage could act as a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> technology and as such would constitute a form of <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a> if deployed at large scale.
</p><p>A few engineering proposals have been made for such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_air_capture" title="Direct air capture">direct air capture</a>, but work in this area is still in its infancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewDAC2016_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewDAC2016-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A pilot plant has operated in British Columbia, Canada since 2015. An economic study of this plant in 2018 estimated the cost at US$94-$232 per tonne of atmospheric CO2 removed. This estimate has decreased compared to a 2011 study that estimated that direct air capture would cost $600 per tonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the specific chemical processes that are being explored, three stand out: causticization with alkali and alkali-earth hydroxides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
and organic−inorganic hybrid sorbents consisting of amines supported in porous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbents" title="Adsorbents">adsorbents</a>.
</p><p>Given that CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere is highly diluted compared to point-source CO<sub>2</sub> capture, capture costs are estimated to be higher. Once costs and incentives for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a> rise higher later this century, however, they might become attractive for dealing with emissions from diffuse sources such as automobiles and aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Global Research Technologies demonstrated a pre-prototype of air capture technology in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After capture, the CO<sub>2</sub> would have to be transported to suitable storage sites. This would most likely be done by pipeline, which is generally the cheapest form of transport for large volume of CO2. In 2008, there were approximately 5,800 km of CO<sub>2</sub> pipelines in the United States, used to transport CO<sub>2</sub> to oil production fields where it is then injected into older fields to extract oil. The injection of CO<sub>2</sub> to produce oil is generally called <i><a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, there are several pilot programs in various stages to test the long-term storage of CO<sub>2</sub> in non-oil producing geologic formations.
</p><p>According to the Congressional Research Service, "There are important unanswered questions about pipeline network requirements, economic regulation, utility cost recovery, regulatory classification of CO<sub>2</sub> itself, and pipeline safety. Furthermore, because CO<sub>2</sub> pipelines for enhanced oil recovery are already in use today, policy decisions affecting CO<sub>2</sub> pipelines take on an urgency that is unrecognized by many. Federal classification of CO<sub>2</sub> as both a commodity (by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a>) and as a pollutant (by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>) could potentially create an immediate conflict which may need to be addressed not only for the sake of future CCS implementation, but also to ensure consistency of future CCS with CO<sub>2</sub> pipeline operations today."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ships could also be utilized for transport where pipelines are not feasible. These methods are currently used for transporting CO<sub>2</sub> for other applications.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequestration">Sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">Carbon sequestration</a></div>
<p>Various forms have been conceived for permanent storage of CO<sub>2</sub>. These forms include gaseous storage in various deep geological formations (including saline formations and exhausted gas fields), and solid storage by reaction of CO<sub>2</sub> with metal <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> to produce stable <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_storage">Geological storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geological storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also known as <i>geo-sequestration</i>, this method involves injecting carbon dioxide, generally in <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> form, directly into underground geological formations. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">Oil fields</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_field" title="Gas field">gas fields</a>, saline formations, unmineable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_seam" title="Coal seam">coal seams</a>, and saline-filled basalt formations have been suggested as storage sites. Various physical (e.g., highly impermeable <a href="/wiki/Caprock" title="Caprock">caprock</a>) and geochemical trapping mechanisms would prevent the CO<sub>2</sub> from escaping to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy_Institute_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Institute-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> is sometimes injected into declining oil fields to <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">increase oil recovery</a>. Approximately 30 to 50 million metric tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> are injected annually in the United States into declining oil fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  This option is attractive because the geology of hydrocarbon reservoirs is generally well understood and storage costs may be partly offset by the sale of additional oil that is recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Disadvantages of old oil fields are their geographic distribution and their limited capacity, as well as the fact that subsequent burning of the additional oil recovered will offset much or all of the reduction in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unmineable coal seams can be used to store CO<sub>2</sub> because the CO<sub>2</sub> molecules attach to the surface of coal. The technical feasibility, however, depends on the permeability of the coal bed. In the process of absorption the coal releases previously absorbed <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and the methane can be recovered (<a href="/wiki/Enhanced_coal_bed_methane_recovery" title="Enhanced coal bed methane recovery">enhanced coal bed methane recovery</a>). The sale of the methane can be used to offset a portion of the cost of the CO<sub>2</sub> storage. Burning the resultant methane, however, would negate some of the benefit of sequestering the original CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Saline formations contain highly mineralized brines, and have so far been considered of no benefit to humans. Saline aquifers have been used for storage of chemical waste in a few cases. The main advantage of saline aquifers is their large potential storage volume and their common occurrence. The major disadvantage of saline aquifers is that relatively little is known about them, especially compared to oil fields. To keep the cost of storage acceptable, the geophysical exploration may be limited, resulting in larger uncertainty about the aquifer structure. Unlike storage in oil fields or coal beds, no side product will offset the storage cost. Leakage of CO<sub>2</sub> back into the atmosphere may be a problem in saline aquifer storage. Current research shows, however, that <i>trapping mechanisms</i> such as structural trapping, residual trapping, solubility trapping and mineral trapping could immobilize the CO<sub>2</sub> underground and reduce the risk of leakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy_Institute_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_Institute-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_oil_recovery">Enhanced oil recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enhanced oil recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">Enhanced oil recovery</a> (EOR) is a generic term for techniques used to increase the amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> that can be extracted from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil field</a>. In carbon capture and sequestration enhanced oil recovery (CCS EOR), carbon dioxide is injected into an oil field to recover oil that is often never recovered using more traditional methods.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">Crude oil</a> development and production in U.S. oil reservoirs can include up to three distinct phases: primary, secondary, and tertiary (or enhanced) recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> During primary recovery only about 10 percent of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">reservoir's</a> original oil in place is typically produced. Secondary recovery techniques extend a field's productive life generally by injecting water or gas to displace oil and drive it to a production <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellbore" title="Wellbore">wellbore</a>, resulting in the recovery of 20 to 40 percent of the original oil in place. However, with much of the easy-to-produce oil already recovered from U.S. oil fields, producers have attempted several tertiary, or enhanced oil recovery, techniques that offer prospects for ultimately producing 30 to 60 percent, or more, of the reservoir's original oil in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>An example of a project that will use CCS EOR is the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. Due to the Kemper Project's close proximity to oil fields, the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproduct" title="Byproduct">byproduct</a> from producing <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> will be transported to the neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_fields" title="Oil fields">oil fields</a> for enhanced oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_storage">Ocean storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ocean storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Framework_Convention_On_Climate_Change">Framework Convention On Climate Change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Framework Convention On Climate Change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Held in 1992, the results from this climate convention are the most widely agreed upon for addressing the problem of climate change and the use of sinks of greenhouse gases. Ocean storage of CO<sub>2</sub> has been pointed out a viable option of mitigating the levels of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. However, the convention also states that there should be precautionary measures taken to mitigate any detriments to the environment. Therefore, with this convention, ocean storage may prove to be a way to store carbon dioxide efficiently, especially in places where it is cost effective such as Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit.edu_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-mit.edu-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="London_Convention">London Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: London Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Taken place in 1972 with more than 70 members, the result of the London Convention is that it prohibits all ocean dumping without the direct approval of a national authority. In 1991, an addendum also prohibited the dumping of all radioactive and industrial waste, making it unclear and debated to today whether or not carbon dioxide falls under industrial waste. The London Convention also applies to sea-faring vessels such as ships, planes, and offshore drilling platforms. If these types of vessels want to dump carbon dioxide into the ocean, they must get permission from their respective national authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit.edu_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-mit.edu-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1996 Protocol to the London Convention went even further by prohibiting carbon dioxide storage that comes from sea-based storage. In 1997, the Joint Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine environmental Protection stated that until there was 2/3 majority vote, any dumping of carbon dioxide from ships was in direct violation of the London Convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit.edu_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-mit.edu-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="OSPAR_Convention">OSPAR Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: OSPAR Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Also occurring in 1992, this convention was set up to protect the North East Atlantic Ocean. The convention was more certain in its results from the meeting. The first is that it prohibited carbon dioxide from going to a storage site through a petroleum platform and also prohibited transport by ship. It did however, allow transport of carbon dioxide through a pipeline from land to a site that did not require the use of a platform used for gas exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, it was suggested that CO<sub>2</sub> could be stored in the oceans, but this would only exacerbate <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> and has been made illegal under specific regulations. Ocean storage is no longer considered feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenfacts.org_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenfacts.org-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_storage">Mineral storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mineral storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In this process, CO<sub>2</sub> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermically</a> reacted with available metal oxides, which in turn produces stable carbonates (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a>). This process occurs naturally over many years and is responsible for a great amount of surface <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>.  The idea of using <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> has been promoted by the geochemist Prof. Schuiling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olivine_weathering_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olivine_weathering-47">[47]</a></sup> The reaction rate can be made faster, for example, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> or by reacting at higher temperatures and/or pressures, or by pre-treatment of the minerals, although this method can require additional energy. The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> estimates that a power plant equipped with CCS using mineral storage will need 60–180% more energy than a power plant without CCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The economics of mineral carbonation at scale are now being tested in a world-first pilot plant project based in Newcastle, Australia. New techniques for mineral activation and reaction have been developed the GreenMag Group and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Newcastle_(Australia)" title="University of Newcastle (Australia)">University of Newcastle</a> and funded by the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and Australian Governments to be operational by 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.dpi.nsw.gov.au_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.dpi.nsw.gov.au-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009 it was reported that scientists had mapped 6,000 square miles (16,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of rock formations in the U.S. that could be used to store 500 years' worth of U.S. carbon dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> A study on mineral sequestration in the US states:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Carbon sequestration by reacting naturally occurring Mg and Ca containing minerals with CO<sub>2</sub> to form carbonates has many unique advantages. Most notabl[e] is the fact that carbonates have a lower energy state than CO<sub>2</sub>, which is why mineral carbonation is thermodynamically favorable and occurs naturally (e.g., the weathering of rock over geologic time periods). Secondly, the raw materials such as magnesium based minerals are abundant. Finally, the produced carbonates are unarguably stable and thus re-release of CO<sub>2</sub> into the atmosphere is not an issue. However, conventional carbonation pathways are slow under ambient temperatures and pressures. The significant challenge being addressed by this effort is to identify an industrially and environmentally viable carbonation route that will allow mineral sequestration to be implemented with acceptable economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p></blockquote>

<p>Ultramafic mine tailings are a readily available source of fine-grained metal oxides that can act as artificial carbon sinks to reduce net greenhouse gas emissions in the mining industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Accelerating passive CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration via mineral carbonation may be achieved through microbial processes that enhance mineral dissolution and carbonate precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_requirements">Energy requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Energy requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The energy requirements of sequestration processes may be significant. In one paper, sequestration consumed 25 percent of the plant's rated 600-megawatt output capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>After adding CO<sub>2</sub> capture and compression, the capacity of the coal-fired power plant is reduced to 457 MW.</i></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leakage">Leakage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Leakage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tml15-16_Nc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="1277" height="79" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/220px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/330px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/440px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tml15-16_Nc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> as it appeared fewer than two weeks after the eruption; August 29, 1986.</div></div></div>
<p>A major concern with CCS is whether leakage of stored CO<sub>2</sub> will compromise CCS as a climate change mitigation option. For well-selected, designed and managed geological storage sites, IPCC estimates that risks are comparable to those associated with current hydrocarbon activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> However, this finding is contested due to a lack of experience in such long term storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> could be trapped for millions of years, and although some leakage occurs upwards through the soil, well selected storage sites are likely to retain over 99% of the injected CO<sub>2</sub> over 1000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Leakage through the injection pipe is a greater risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the injection pipe is usually protected with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-return_valve" title="Non-return valve">non-return valves</a> to prevent release on a power outage, there is still a risk that the pipe itself could tear and leak due to the pressure. The <a href="/wiki/Berkel_en_Rodenrijs" title="Berkel en Rodenrijs">Berkel en Rodenrijs</a> incident in December 2008 was an example, where a modest release of CO<sub>2</sub> from a pipeline under a bridge resulted in the deaths of some ducks sheltering there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eenden_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-eenden-62">[62]</a></sup> In order to measure accidental carbon releases more accurately and decrease the risk of fatalities through this type of leakage, the implementation of CO<sub>2</sub> alert meters around the project perimeter has been proposed. Malfunction of a carbon dioxide industrial fire suppression system in a large warehouse released CO<sub>2</sub> and 14 citizens collapsed on the nearby public road. A release of CO<sub>2</sub> from a salt mine killed a person at distance of 300 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986 a large leakage of naturally sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> rose from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> and asphyxiated 1,700 people. While the carbon had been sequestered naturally, some point to the event as evidence for the potentially catastrophic effects of sequestering carbon artificially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-64">[64]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> disaster resulted from a volcanic event, which very suddenly released as much as a cubic kilometre of CO<sub>2</sub> gas from a pool of naturally occurring CO<sub>2</sub> under the lake in a deep narrow valley. The location of this pool of CO<sub>2</sub> is not a place where man can inject or store CO<sub>2</sub>, and this pool was not known about nor monitored until after the occurrence of the natural disaster.
</p><p>For ocean storage, the retention of CO<sub>2</sub> would depend on the depth. The IPCC estimates 30–85% of the sequestered carbon dioxide would be retained after 500 years for depths 1000–3000 m. Mineral storage is not regarded as having any risks of leakage. The IPCC recommends that limits be set to the amount of leakage that can take place. This might rule out deep ocean storage as an option.
</p><p>At the conditions of the deeper oceans, (about 400 bar or 40 MPa, 280 K) water–CO<sub>2</sub>(l) mixing is <i>very</i> low (where carbonate formation/acidification is the rate limiting step), but the formation of water-CO<sub>2</sub> hydrates, a kind of solid water cage that surrounds the CO<sub>2</sub>, is favorable.
</p><p>To further investigate the safety of CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration, Norway's <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner gas field</a> can be studied, as it is the oldest plant that stores CO<sub>2</sub> on an industrial scale. According to an environmental assessment of the gas field which was conducted after ten years of operation, the author affirmed that geosequestration of CO<sub>2</sub> was the most definite form of permanent <i>geological</i> storage of CO<sub>2</sub>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Available geological information shows absence of major tectonic events after the deposition of the Utsira formation [saline reservoir]. This implies that the geological environment is tectonically stable and a site suitable for carbon dioxide storage. The solubility trapping [is] the most permanent and secure form of geological storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In March 2009 StatoilHydro issued a study showing the slow spread of CO<sub>2</sub> in the formation after more than 10 years operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy-pedia_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Energy-pedia-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Phase I of the <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weyburn,_Saskatchewan" title="Weyburn, Saskatchewan">Weyburn, Saskatchewan</a>, Canada has determined that the likelihood of stored CO<sub>2</sub> release is less than one percent in 5,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A January 2011 report, however, claimed <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project#Claims_of_leaking" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">evidence of leakage</a> in land above that project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> This report was strongly refuted by the IEAGHG Weyburn-Midale CO<sub>2</sub> Monitoring and Storage Project, which issued an eight-page analysis of the study, claiming that it showed no evidence of leakage from the reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The liability of potential leak(s) is one of the largest barriers to large-scale CCS. To assess and reduce such liability, the leakage of stored gasses, particularly <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, into the atmosphere may be detected via atmospheric gas monitoring, and can be quantified directly via the <a href="/wiki/Eddy_covariance" title="Eddy covariance">eddy covariance</a> flux measurements,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring_geological_sequestration_sites">Monitoring geological sequestration sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Monitoring geological sequestration sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to detect carbon dioxide leaks and the effectiveness of geological sequestration sites, different monitoring techniques can be employed to verify that the sequestered carbon stays trapped below the surface in the intended reservoir. Leakage due to injection at improper locations or conditions could result in carbon dioxide being released back into the atmosphere. It is important to be able to detect leaks with enough warning to put a stop to it, and to be able to quantify the amount of carbon that has leaked for purposes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_and_trade" title="Cap and trade">cap and trade</a> policies, evaluation of environmental impact of leaked carbon, as well as accounting for the total loss and cost of the process. To quantify the amount of carbon dioxide released, should a leak occur, or to closely watch stored CO<sub>2</sub>, there are several monitoring methods that can be done at both the surface and subsurface levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-main_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-main-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsurface_monitoring">Subsurface monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Subsurface monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In subsurface monitoring, there are direct and indirect methods to determine the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> in the reservoir. A direct method would be drilling deep enough to collect a fluid sample. This drilling can be difficult and expensive due to the physical properties of the rock. It also only provides data at a specific location. Indirect methods would be to send sound or electromagnetic waves down to the reservoir where it is then reflected back up to be interpreted. This approach is also expensive but it provides data over a much larger region; it does however lack precision. Both direct and indirect monitoring can be done intermittently or continuously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-main_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-main-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seismic_monitoring">Seismic monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Seismic monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">Seismic monitoring</a> is a type of indirect subsurface monitoring. It is done by creating vibrational waves either at the surface using a <a href="/wiki/Seismic_vibrator" title="Seismic vibrator">vibroseis truck</a>, or inside a well using <a href="/wiki/Rotating_unbalance" title="Rotating unbalance">spinning eccentric mass</a>.  These vibrational waves then propagate through the geological layers and reflect back creating patterns that are read and interpreted by seismometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> It can identify migration pathways of the CO<sub>2</sub> plume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Two examples of monitoring geological sequestration sites using seismic monitoring are the <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner sequestration project</a> and the Frio CO<sub>2</sub> Injection test. Although this method can confirm the presence of CO<sub>2</sub> in a given region, it cannot determine the specifics of the environment or concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_monitoring">Surface monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Surface monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eddy_covariance" title="Eddy covariance">Eddy covariance</a> is a surface monitoring technique that measures the flux of CO<sub>2</sub> from the ground's surface. It involves measuring CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations as well as vertical wind velocities using an anemometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> This provides a measure of the total vertical flux of CO2. Eddy covariance towers could potentially detect leaks, however, the natural carbon cycle, such as photosynthesis and the respiration of plants, would have to be accounted for and a baseline CO<sub>2</sub> cycle would have to be developed for the location of monitoring. An example of Eddy covariance techniques used to monitor carbon sequestration sites is the <a class="external text" href="http://escholarship.org/uc/item/397007sh" rel="nofollow">Shallow Release test</a>. Another similar approach is utilizing accumulation chambers. These chambers are sealed to the ground with an inlet and outlet flow stream connected to a gas analyzer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-main_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-main-73">[73]</a></sup> This also measures the vertical flux of CO<sub>2</sub>. The disadvantage of accumulation chambers is its inability to monitor a large region which is necessary in detecting CO<sub>2</sub> leaks over the entire sequestration site.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="InSAR_monitoring">InSAR monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: InSAR monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InSAR" title="InSAR">InSAR</a> monitoring is another type of surface monitoring. It involves a satellite sending signals down to the Earth's surface where it is reflected back to the satellite's receiver. From this, the satellite is able to measure the distance to that point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insar_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-insar-77">[77]</a></sup> In CCS, the injection of CO<sub>2</sub> in deep sublayers of geological sites creates high pressures. These high pressured, fluid filled layers affect those above and below it resulting in a change of the surface landscape. In areas of stored CO<sub>2</sub>, the ground's surface often rises due to the high pressures originating in the deep subsurface layers. These changes in elevation of the Earth's surface corresponds to a change in the distance from the inSAR satellite which is then detectable and measurable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-insar_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-insar-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Carbon_capture_and_utilization_.28CCU.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_utilization_(CCU)">Carbon capture and utilization (CCU)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and utilization (CCU)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral_fuel" title="Carbon neutral fuel">Carbon neutral fuel</a></div>
<p>Carbon capture and use may offer a response to the global challenge of significantly reducing greenhouse gas emissions from major stationary (industrial) emitters in the near to medium term,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Given that it does not result in geological storage of carbon dioxide, it represents a different technological category from CCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Technologies under development, such as Bio CCS Algal Synthesis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalccsinstitute.com_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-globalccsinstitute.com-79">[79]</a></sup> utilises pre-smokestack CO<sub>2</sub> (such as from a coal-fired power station) as a useful feedstock input to the production of oil-rich algae in solar membranes to produce oil for plastics and transport fuel (including aviation fuel), and nutritious stock-feed for farm animal production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalccsinstitute.com_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-globalccsinstitute.com-79">[79]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> and other captured greenhouse gases are injected into the membranes containing waste water and select strains of algae causing, together with sunlight or UV light, an oil rich biomass that doubles in mass every 24 hours<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>The Bio CCS Algal Synthesis process is based on earth science photosynthesis: the technology is entirely retrofittable and collocated with the emitter, and the capital outlays may offer a return upon investment due to the high value commodities produced (oil for plastics, fuel and feed).
</p><p>Bio CCS Algal Synthesis test facilities were being trialed at Australia's three largest coal-fired power stations (Tarong, Queensland; Eraring, NSW; Loy Yang, Victoria) using piped pre-emission smokestack CO<sub>2</sub> (and other greenhouse gases) as feedstock to grow oil-rich algal biomass in enclosed membranes for the production of plastics, transport fuel and nutritious animal feed.
</p><p>Another potentially useful way of dealing with industrial sources of CO<sub>2</sub> is to convert it into hydrocarbons where it can be stored or reused as fuel or to make plastics. There are a number of projects investigating this possibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci2645_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsci2645-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">Carbon dioxide scrubbing</a> variants exist based on <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a> which can be used to create liquid fuels, though this process requires a great deal of energy input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Although the creation of fuel from atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> does not result in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> as carbon dioxide is re-released when the fuel is burned. Therefore, synfuels do not represent a <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a> technique. Nevertheless, they are potentially useful as net-zero-carbon fuel.
</p><p>Other uses are the production of stable carbonates from silicates (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a>). These processes are still under research and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_step_methods:_methanol">Single step methods: methanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Single step methods: methanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A proven process to produce a hydrocarbon is to make <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>.  Methanol is easily synthesized from CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>.  Based on this fact the idea of a <a href="/wiki/Methanol_economy" title="Methanol economy">methanol economy</a> was born.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_step_methods:_hydrocarbons">Single step methods: hydrocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Single step methods: hydrocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the department of Industrial Chemistry and Engineering of Materials at the University of Messina, Italy, there is a project to develop a system which works like a fuel-cell in reverse, whereby a catalyst is used that enables sunlight to split water into hydrogen ions and oxygen gas. The ions cross a membrane where they react with the CO<sub>2</sub> to create hydrocarbons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_step_methods">Two step methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Two step methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If CO<sub>2</sub> is heated to 2400 °C, it splits into carbon monoxide (CO) and oxygen. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a> can then be used to convert the CO into hydrocarbons. The required temperature can be achieved by using a chamber containing a mirror to focus sunlight on the gas. Rival teams are developing such chambers, at Solarec and at <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a>, both based in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> According to Sandia these chambers could provide enough fuel to power 100% of domestic vehicles using 5800 km<sup>2</sup>; unlike biofuels this would not take fertile land away from crops but would be land that is not being used for anything else. <a href="/wiki/James_May" title="James May">James May</a>, the British TV presenter, visited a demonstration plant in a programme in his <a href="/wiki/James_May%27s_Big_Ideas" title="James May's Big Ideas">Big Ideas</a> series.
</p>

<h4><span id="In_Salah_CO2_Injection_.E2.80.94_Algeria"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Salah_CO2_Injection_—_Algeria">In Salah CO<sub>2</sub> Injection — <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: In Salah CO2 Injection — Algeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/In_Salah" title="In Salah">In Salah</a> is a fully operational onshore gas field with CO<sub>2</sub> injection. CO<sub>2</sub> is separated from produced gas and reinjected in the producing hydrocarbon reservoir zones. Since 2004, about 1 Mt/a of CO<sub>2</sub> has been captured during <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> extraction and injected into the Krechba geologic formation at a depth of 1,800m. The Krechba formation is expected to store 17Mt CO<sub>2</sub> over the life of the project.
</p><p>Injection suspended in 2011 due to concerns about the integrity of the seal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Sleipner_CO2_Injection_.E2.80.94_Norway"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleipner_CO2_Injection_—_Norway">Sleipner CO<sub>2</sub> Injection — Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Sleipner CO2 Injection — Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner gas field</a></div>
<p>Sleipner<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy-pedia_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Energy-pedia-66">[66]</a></sup> is a fully operational offshore gas field with CO<sub>2</sub> injection initiated in 1996. CO<sub>2</sub> is separated from produced gas and reinjected in the Utsira saline aquifer (800–1000 m below ocean floor) above the hydrocarbon reservoir zones. This aquifer extends much further north from the Sleipner facility at its southern extreme. The large size of the reservoir accounts for why 600 billion tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> are expected to be stored, long after the Sleipner natural gas project has ended.
</p>
<h4><span id="Sn.C3.B8hvit_CO2_Injection_.E2.80.94_Norway"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Snøhvit_CO2_Injection_—_Norway">Snøhvit CO<sub>2</sub> Injection — Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Snøhvit CO2 Injection — Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Snøhvit is a fully operational offshore gas field with CO<sub>2</sub> injection. The LNG plant is located onshore. CO<sub>2</sub> is necessarily separated to produce <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (LNG) and then CO<sub>2</sub> is injected in a saline aquifer below the hydrocarbon reservoir zones offshore at a rate of 700,000 t/a into the Tubåen <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> formation 2,600 m under the seabed for storage. This formation was closed April 2011, and injection started in the Stø-formation where produced gas is taken. Produced CO<sub>2</sub> is increasing, therefore separation capacity may limit production before end 2015 when a new formation will be drilled for CO<sub>2</sub>-injection only. (Teknisk Ukeblad nr. 30, 2013, tu.no)
</p>
<h4><span id="Great_Plains_Synfuel_Plant_and_Weyburn-Midale_Project_.E2.80.94_Canada"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Plains_Synfuel_Plant_and_Weyburn-Midale_Project_—_Canada">Great Plains Synfuel Plant and Weyburn-Midale Project — Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Great Plains Synfuel Plant and Weyburn-Midale Project — Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Weyburn-Midale is a <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a> operation that produces synthetic natural gas and various petrochemicals from coal. This project captures about 2.8 Mt/a of CO<sub>2</sub> from its coal gasification plant located in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, US, transported by pipeline 320 km across the Canada–US border and injects it into depleting oil fields in Saskatchewan where it is used for enhanced oil recovery (EOR).
</p>
<h4><span id="Shute_Creek_Gas_Processing_Facility_.E2.80.94_US"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shute_Creek_Gas_Processing_Facility_—_US">Shute Creek Gas Processing Facility — US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Shute Creek Gas Processing Facility — US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Around 7 million tonnes per annum of carbon dioxide are recovered from <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a>'s Shute Creek gas processing plant in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, and transported by pipeline to various oil fields for enhanced oil recovery. This project has been operational since 1986.
</p>
<h4><span id="Enid_Fertilizer_.E2.80.94_US"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Enid_Fertilizer_—_US">Enid Fertilizer — US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Enid Fertilizer — US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Enid Fertilizer plant sends 675,000 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> to be used for EOR. The pipeline and wells are operated separately by Anadarko Petroleum.

<p>CO<sub>2</sub> from Mitchell, Gray Ranch, Puckett, and Turrell gas processing plants is transported via the Val Verde and CRC pipelines<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. <h4><span id="Century_Plant_.E2.80.94_US"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Century_Plant_—_US">Century Plant — US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Century Plant — US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Occidental_Petroleum" title="Occidental Petroleum">Occidental Petroleum</a>, along with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sandridge_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sandridge Energy (page does not exist)">Sandridge Energy</a>, is operating a West Texas hydrocarbon gas processing plant and related pipeline infrastructure that provides CO<sub>2</sub> for use in <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">EOR</a>. With a total CO<sub>2</sub> capture capacity of 8.5 Mt/a expected in 2012, the Century plant would be the largest single industrial source CO<sub>2</sub> capture facility in North America.
</p>
<h4><span id="Duke_Energy_East_Bend_Station_.E2.80.94_US"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Duke_Energy_East_Bend_Station_—_US">Duke Energy East Bend Station  — US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Duke Energy East Bend Station — US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Researchers at the Center for Applied Energy Research of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kentucky" title="University of Kentucky">University of Kentucky</a> are currently developing the algae-mediated conversion of coal-fired power plant flue gas to drop-in hydrocarbon fuels. Through their work, these researchers have proven that the carbon dioxide within <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_plants" title="Coal-fired power plants">coal-fired power plants</a> can be captured using algae, which can be subsequently harvested and utilized, e.g. as a feedstock for the production of drop-in hydrocarbon fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Petra_Nova_.E2.80.94_US"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Petra_Nova_—_US">Petra Nova  — US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Petra Nova — US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Petra_Nova" title="Petra Nova">Petra Nova</a> project is a billion dollar endeavour taken upon by NRG Energy and JP Nippon to retrofit their jointly owned W.A Parish coal-fired power plant with post-combustion carbon capture. The plant, which is located in Thompsons, Texas (just outside of Houston), entered commercial service in 1977, and carbon capture began operation on January 10, 2017. The plant generates 240 MW and 90% of the CO2 (or 1.4 million tonnes) is captured per year. The carbon dioxide captured (99% purity) from the power plant is compressed and piped about 82 miles to West Ranch Oil Field, Texas, where it will be used for enhanced oil recovery. The field has a capacity of 60 million barrels of oil and is expected to increase oil production by a factor of 506. This project is expected to run for at least another 20 years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The federal government in the 2008 and 2009 budgets has invested approximately $1.4 billion in Carbon Capture and Storage development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alberta">Alberta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Alberta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Alberta has committed $170 million in 2013/2014 – and a total of $1.3 billion over 15 years – to fund two large-scale CCS projects that will help reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from tar sands refining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> In 2010 a grant agreement was signed with the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Carbon_Trunk_Line" title="Alberta Carbon Trunk Line">Alberta Carbon Trunk Line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> The second is the Quest Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The Quest project uses amine absorption to capture CO<sub>2</sub> from the Scotford Steam Methane Reformer units. Approximately 1.2 Mtonne CO<sub>2</sub> per year is captured and transported through 64 km of onshore pipeline into a saline aquifer in the Cambrian Basal Sands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quest_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Project_Annual_Summary_Report_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quest_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Project_Annual_Summary_Report-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Quest_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Project">Quest Carbon Capture and Storage Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Quest Carbon Capture and Storage Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quest_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Project" title="Quest Carbon Capture and Storage Project">Quest Carbon Capture and Storage Project</a> captures and stores underground one million tonnes of CO2 emissions per year. The capture unit is located at the Scotford Upgrader in Alberta, Canada, where hydrogen is produced to <a href="/wiki/Upgrader" title="Upgrader">upgrade</a> bitumen from <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a> into synthetic crude oil.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Weyburn_Project">Weyburn Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Weyburn Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The objective of the <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project</a> is to increase oil production and extend the oil field’s lifetime through Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) by injecting the captured CO2 from the Dakota Gasification Company's Great Plains Synfuels Plant in North Dakota and SaskPower’s coal-fired Boundary Dam Power Station in Saskatchewan, Canada into the Weyburn oilfields.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Columbia">British Columbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: British Columbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spectra_Energy" title="Spectra Energy">Spectra Energy</a>'s Fort Nelson Project is proposed but still needs to secure funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canadian_CCS_Projects_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_CCS_Projects-97">[97]</a></sup> The total project cost is estimated to be US $12.5 Million. The source of CO<sub>2</sub> will be from the Fort Nelson Natural Gas Processing Plant and will be transported 15 km via an onshore pipeline to middle Devonian carbonate rock that is between 6500 and 7000 feet deep. The process will capture 2.2 Megatonne CO<sub>2</sub> per year using an amine process in its pre-combustion capture. Injections and MVA Operations have already occurred (2014) and is projected to startup in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spectra_Energy's_Fort_Nelson_CCS_Project_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spectra_Energy's_Fort_Nelson_CCS_Project-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Saskatchewan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Led by the provincially owned utility, SaskPower, one of the world's first and largest full production carbon capture facilities is operating at the coal fired <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Dam_Power_Station" title="Boundary Dam Power Station">Boundary Dam Power Station</a>. With an initial investment of $1.5 to $1.6 billion. Of the 90% of CO2 emissions captured, about a half of this will be sold and permanently sequestered in enhanced oil recovery. The remainder is released into the atmosphere during capturing, and processing in the oil field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> The project started in May 2011 and became operational in October 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> The post-combustion full flue gas capture process was to capture 1 million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canadian_CCS_Projects_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_CCS_Projects-97">[97]</a></sup> Since opening serious design issues were found in the carbon capture system, resulting in maintenance problems that led to operating only 40% of the time. In 2016 the contracts to sell CO2 were renegotiated to reflect the reduced sales.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Netherlands, a 68-megawatt oxyfuel plant ("Zero Emission Power Plant") was being planned to be operational in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> This project was later canceled.
</p><p>ROAD (Rotterdam Capture and Storage Demonstration project) is a joint project by E.ON Benelux and Electrabel Nederland / GDF SUEZ Group. Every year, starting in 2015 ROAD will capture around 1.1 million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> at the new power plant on the Maasvlakte. This will be stored in depleted gas reservoirs under the North Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Pending financial approval, the plant hopes to be operational sometime after 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> But due to uncertainty of the continuance of coal-fired power plant in the Netherlands, the ROAD project is cancelled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>Developed in the Netherlands, an electrocatalysis by a copper complex helps <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">reduce carbon dioxide</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouwman_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwman-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Norway, the CO<sub>2</sub> Technology Centre (TCM) at <a href="/wiki/Mongstad" title="Mongstad">Mongstad</a> began construction in 2009, and completed in 2012. It includes two capture technology plants (one advanced amine and one chilled ammonia), both capturing fluegas from two sources. This includes a gas-fired power plant and refinery cracker fluegas (similar to coal-fired power plant fluegas).
</p><p>In addition to this, the Mongstad site was also planned to have a full-scale CCS demonstration plant. The project was delayed to 2014, 2018, and then indefinitely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> The project cost rose to USD 985 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
Then in October 2011, Aker Solutions' wrote off its investment in Aker Clean Carbon, declaring the carbon sequestration market to be "dead".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 1 October 2013 Norway asked <a href="/wiki/Gassnova" title="Gassnova">Gassnova</a> not to sign any contracts for Carbon capture and storage outside Mongstad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 Norway was reviewing feasibility studies and hoping to have a full-scale carbon capture demonstration project by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Belchatów, Poland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-https://www.ccsnetwork.eu_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-https://www.ccsnetwork.eu-114">[114]</a></sup> a lignite-fired energy plant of more than 858 MW is planned to be in operation in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2008, the DOE awarded a $66.9 million eight-year grant to a research partnership headed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montana_State_University_%E2%80%93_Bozeman" title="Montana State University – Bozeman">Montana State University</a> to demonstrate that underground geologic formations "can store huge volumes of carbon dioxide economically, safely and permanently".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Researchers under the Big Sky Regional Carbon Sequestration Project plan to inject up to one million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> into <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> beneath southwestern Wyoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, four different <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic fuel</a> projects are moving forward, which have publicly announced plans to incorporate carbon capture and storage:
</p>
<ol><li>American Clean Coal Fuels, in their Illinois Clean Fuels (ICF) project, is developing a 30,000-barrel (4,800 m<sup>3</sup>) per day biomass and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_to_liquids" title="Coal to liquids">coal to liquids</a> project in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_Illinois" title="Oakland, Illinois">Oakland, Illinois</a>, which will market the CO<sub>2</sub> created at the plant for enhanced oil recovery applications. By combining sequestration and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> feedstocks, the ICF project will achieve dramatic reductions in the life-cycle carbon footprint of the fuels they produce. If sufficient biomass is used, the plant should have the capability to go life-cycle carbon negative, meaning that effectively, for each gallon of their fuel that is used, carbon is pulled out of the air, and put into the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></li>
<li>Baard Energy, in their Ohio River Clean Fuels project, is developing a 53,000 bbl/d (8,400 m<sup>3</sup>/d) coal and biomass to liquids project, which has announced plans to market the plant's CO<sub>2</sub> for enhanced oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></li>
<li>Rentech is developing a 29,600-barrel (4,710 m<sup>3</sup>) per day coal and biomass to liquids plant in <a href="/wiki/Natchez,_Mississippi" title="Natchez, Mississippi">Natchez, Mississippi</a>, which will market the plant's CO<sub>2</sub> for enhanced oil recovery. The first phase of the project is expected in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></li>
<li>DKRW Energy is developing a 15,000–20,000-barrel (2,400–3,200 m<sup>3</sup>) per day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_to_liquids" title="Coal to liquids">coal to liquids</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Medicine_Bow,_Wyoming" title="Medicine Bow, Wyoming">Medicine Bow, Wyoming</a>, which will market its plant's CO<sub>2</sub> for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>. The project is expected to begin operation in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>In October 2009, the U.S. Department of Energy awarded grants to twelve Industrial Carbon Capture and Storage (ICCS) projects to conduct a Phase 1 feasibility study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The DOE plans to select 3 to 4 of those projects to proceed into Phase 2, design and construction, with operational startup to occur by 2015. <a href="/wiki/Battelle_Memorial_Institute" title="Battelle Memorial Institute">Battelle Memorial Institute</a>, Pacific Northwest Division, Boise, Inc., and <a href="/wiki/Fluor_Corporation" title="Fluor Corporation">Fluor Corporation</a> are studying a CCS system for capture and storage of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions associated with the pulp and paper production industry. The site of the study is the Boise White Paper L.L.C. paper mill located near the township of Wallula in Southeastern Washington State. The plant generates approximately 1.2 MMT of CO<sub>2</sub> annually from a set of three recovery boilers that are mainly fired with black liquor, a recycled byproduct formed during the pulping of wood for paper-making. Fluor Corporation will design a customized version of their Econamine Plus carbon capture technology. The Fluor system also will be designed to remove residual quantities of remnant air pollutants from stack gases as part of the CO<sub>2</sub> capture process. Battelle is leading preparation of an Environmental Information Volume (EIV) for the entire project, including geologic storage of the captured CO<sub>2</sub> in deep flood basalt formations that exist in the greater region. The EIV will describe the necessary site characterization work, sequestration system infrastructure, and monitoring program to support permanent sequestration of the CO<sub>2</sub> captured at the plant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (February 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In addition to individual carbon capture and sequestration projects, there are a number of U.S. programs designed to research, develop, and deploy CCS technologies on a broad scale. These include the National Energy Technology Laboratory's (NETL) Carbon Sequestration Program, regional carbon sequestration partnerships and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Sequestration_Leadership_Forum" title="Carbon Sequestration Leadership Forum">Carbon Sequestration Leadership Forum</a> (CSLF).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="SECARB">SECARB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: SECARB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In October 2007, the Bureau of Economic Geology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a> received a 10-year, $38 million subcontract to conduct the first intensively monitored long-term project in the United States studying the feasibility of injecting a large volume of CO<sub>2</sub> for underground storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> The project is a research program of the Southeast Regional Carbon Sequestration Partnership (SECARB), funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Technology_Laboratory" title="National Energy Technology Laboratory">National Energy Technology Laboratory</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> (DOE).
</p><p>The SECARB partnership will demonstrate CO<sub>2</sub> injection rate and storage capacity in the Tuscaloosa-Woodbine geologic system that stretches from Texas to Florida. The region has the potential to store more than 200 billion tons<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (September 2008)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> of CO<sub>2</sub> from major point sources in the region, equal to about 33 years of overall U.S. emissions at present rates. Beginning in fall 2007, the project will inject CO<sub>2</sub> at the rate of one million tons<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (September 2008)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> per year, for up to 1.5 years, into brine up to 10,000 feet (3,000 m) below the land surface near the Cranfield oil field, which lays about 15 miles (24 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Natchez,_Mississippi" title="Natchez, Mississippi">Natchez, Mississippi</a>. Experimental equipment will measure the ability of the subsurface to accept and retain CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>The $1.4 billion <a href="/wiki/FutureGen" title="FutureGen">FutureGen</a> power generation and carbon sequestration demonstration project, announced in 2003 by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, was cancelled in 2015, due to delays and inability to raise required private funding.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kemper_Project">Kemper Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Kemper Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, is a natural gas-fired power plant under construction in <a href="/wiki/Kemper_County,_Mississippi" title="Kemper County, Mississippi">Kemper County, Mississippi</a>, which was originally planned as a coal-fired plant. <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a>, began construction of the plant in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> The project was considered central to President Obama's Climate Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp2016_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-kemp2016-127">[127]</a></sup>  Had it become operational as a coal plant, the Kemper Project would have been a first-of-its-kind electricity plant to employ gasification and carbon capture technologies at this scale. The emission target was to reduce CO2 to the same level an equivalent natural gas plant would produce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-128">[128]</a></sup> However, in June 2017 the proponents - Southern Company and Mississippi Power - announced that they would only burn natural gas at the plant at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars_nat_gas_2017_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars_nat_gas_2017-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plant experienced project management problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp2016_127-1"><a href="#cite_note-kemp2016-127">[127]</a></sup> Construction was delayed and the scheduled opening was pushed back over two years, at a cost of $6.6 billion—three times original cost estimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amy2_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amy2-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-131">[131]</a></sup>  According to a <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> analysis, Kemper is the most expensive power plant ever built for the watts of electricity it will generate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drajem_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drajem-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas_Clean_Energy_Project">Texas Clean Energy Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: Texas Clean Energy Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Texas Clean Energy Project (TCEP) is a "NowGen" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle" title="Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle">Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle</a> (IGCC) facility that will incorporate carbon capture, utilization and storage (CCUS) technology in a first-of-its-kind commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_coal" title="Clean coal">clean coal</a> power plant. This project is expected to be operational in 2018. It will be the first US-based power plant to combine both IGCC and capture 90% of its emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Brown_Steam_Electric_Station">Big Brown Steam Electric Station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Big Brown Steam Electric Station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Examples of carbon sequestration at an existing US coal plant can be found at utility company <a href="/wiki/Luminant" title="Luminant">Luminant</a>'s pilot version at its <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Big_Brown_Steam_Electric_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Big Brown Steam Electric Station (page does not exist)">Big Brown Steam Electric Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fairfield,_Texas" title="Fairfield, Texas">Fairfield, Texas</a>. This system is converting carbon from smokestacks into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baking_soda" title="Baking soda">baking soda</a>. Skyonic plans to circumvent storage problems of liquid CO<sub>2</sub> by storing baking soda in mines, landfills, to be sold as industrial or food grade baking soda,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Green Fuel Technologies is piloting and implementing algae based carbon capture, circumventing storage issues by then converting algae into fuel or feed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> though this may lead to re-release of the carbon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The government of the United Kingdom launched a first tender process for a CCS demonstration project in 2007. The project were to use post-combustion technology on coal-fired power generation at 300–400 megawatts or equivalent. The project aimed to be operational by 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> The Government announced in June 2008 that four companies had pre-qualified for the competition: BP Alternative Energy International Limited, EON UK Plc, Peel Power Limited and Scottish Power Generation Limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> BP subsequently withdrew from the competition, claiming it could not find a power generator partner, and RWE <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Npower_(UK)" title="Npower (UK)">npower</a> sought a judicial review of the process after it did not qualify.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> This first CCS tender was cancelled in late 2011 when government could not reach agreement with the ScottishPower/Shell/National Grid consortium on terms and cost, for the project based on retrofitting the existing Longannet coal-fired power station in Scotland.
</p><p>A second tender process was launched by government in 2012 as part of DECC's CCS Commercialisation Programme and two bidders, namely the Shell Peterhead gas-fired power station CCS project and the White Rose CCS project based upon a new oxy-fuel coal-fired unit at Drax power station were selected in 2013 to proceed to a funded front-end engineering and design phase.  This second tender was cancelled in November 2015 following a government spending review at the time of the Chancellor's Autumn Statement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>
</p><p>Doosan Babcock has modified their Clean Combustion Test Facility (CCTF) in Renfrew, Scotland to create the largest Oxyfuel test facility currently in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Oxyfuel firing on pulverized coal with recycled flue gas demonstrates the operation of a full scale 40 MW burner for use in coal-fired boilers. Sponsors of the project include the UK Department for Business Enterprise and Regulatory Reform (BERR,) as well as a group of industrial sponsors and university partners comprising Scottish and Southern Energy (Prime Sponsor), E.ON UK PLC, Drax Power Limited, ScottishPower, EDF Energy, Dong Energy Generation, Air Products Plc (Sponsors), and Imperial College and University of Nottingham (University Partners).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009 UK firm 2Co Energy was awarded planning permission for a £5bn power station and carbon-capture-and-storage project at Hatfield, near Doncaster and £164m of EU funding. Technology company Samsung agreed to take a 15% stake in the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> It is planned to construct a 60 km (37 mi) pipeline from Stainforth, near Hatfield in South Yorkshire to Barmston in the East Riding of Yorkshire. CO<sub>2</sub> would then be stored in natural porous rock beneath the North Sea. National Grid believes the project has the potential to reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from power stations across Yorkshire and the Humber by up to 90% and both the proposed White Rose CCS project at Drax Power Station in North Yorkshire and the proposed Don Valley Power Project at Hatfield could benefit from the scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_of_England" title="Northeast of England">Northeast of England</a>, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_of_England_Process_Industry_Cluster" title="Northeast of England Process Industry Cluster">Northeast of England Process Industry Cluster</a> (NEPIC) of commodity chemical manufacturers are amongst the largest single point producers of carbon dioxide in the United Kingdom and they have created within NEPIC the Process Industry Carbon Capture and Storage Initiative<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup>  (PICCSI) to study the possibility of a carbon capture and storage (CCS) solution being provided for the chemical and steel manufacturing industry on <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a>, as well as for any carbon based energy production. This CCS technology option is being considered as a result of climate change regulations and the carbon taxation that could become a prohibitive cost for such energy intensive industries.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Crown_Estate" title="Crown Estate">Crown Estate</a> is responsible for storage rights on the UK continental shelf and it has facilitated work on offshore carbon dioxide storage technical and commercial issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Beijing, as of 2009, one major power plant is capturing and re-selling a small fraction of its CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> Technologies like CCS result in uncertainties that also need to be monitored. Interval fuzzy stochastic programming(IFSP) is used to deal with the multiple uncertainties that may arise in the research of CCS technology.Currently an IFSP for CCS is used in planning CCS in Bayingolin. The results reveal that CCs technology can help lower carbon dioxide emissions in a long term scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The German industrial area of Schwarze Pumpe, about 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) south of the city of <a href="/wiki/Spremberg" title="Spremberg">Spremberg</a>, is home to the world's first demonstration CCS coal plant, the <a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe power station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> The mini pilot plant is run by an <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>-built <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel" title="Oxy-fuel">oxy-fuel</a> boiler and is also equipped with a flue gas cleaning facility to remove <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a>. The Swedish company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vattenfall_AB" title="Vattenfall AB">Vattenfall AB</a> invested some €70 million in the two-year project, which began operation September 9, 2008. The power plant, which is rated at 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a>, is a pilot project to serve as a prototype for future full-scale power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> 240 tonnes a day of CO<sub>2</sub> are being trucked 350 kilometers (220 mi) where it will be injected into an empty gas field. Germany's <a href="/wiki/Bund_f%C3%BCr_Umwelt_und_Naturschutz_Deutschland" title="Bund für Umwelt und Naturschutz Deutschland">BUND group</a> called it a "<a href="/wiki/Fig_leaf" title="Fig leaf">fig leaf</a>". For each tonne of coal burned, 3.6 tonnes of carbon dioxide is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> The CCS program at Schwarze Pumpe ended in 2014 due to inviable costs and energy use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p><p>German utility <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a> operates a pilot-scale CO<sub>2</sub> scrubber at the lignite-fired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niederau%C3%9Fem_power_station" title="Niederaußem power station">Niederaußem power station</a> built in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> (supplier of detergent) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Linde_Group" title="The Linde Group">Linde</a> engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Jänschwalde, Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> a plan is in the works for an Oxyfuel boiler, rated at 650 thermal MW (around 250 electric MW), which is about 20 times more than Vattenfall's 30 MW pilot plant under construction, and compares to today's largest Oxyfuel test rigs of 0.5 MW. Post-combustion capture technology will also be demonstrated at Jänschwalde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia" title="Carbon capture and storage in Australia">Carbon capture and storage in Australia</a></div>
<p>The Federal Resources and Energy Minister Martin Ferguson opened the first geosequestration project in the southern hemisphere in April 2008. The demonstration plant is near Nirranda South in South Western Victoria. (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;params=35.31_S_149.14_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">35°19′S</span> <span class="longitude">149°08′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">35.31°S 149.14°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">-35.31; 149.14</span></span></span></a></span>) The plant is owned by  CO2CRC Limited. CO2CRC is a non profit research collaboration supported by government and industry. The project has stored and monitored over 80,000 tonnes of carbon dioxide-rich gas which was extracted from a natural gas reservoir via a well, compressed and piped 2.25 km to a new well. There the gas has been injected into a depleted natural gas reservoir approximately two kilometers below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> The project has moved to a second stage and is investigating carbon dioxide trapping in a saline aquifer 1500 meters below the surface. The Otway Project is a research and demonstration project, focused on comprehensive monitoring and verification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup>
</p><p>This plant does not propose to capture CO<sub>2</sub> from coal-fired power generation, though two CO2CRC demonstration projects at a Victorian power station and research gasifier are demonstrating solvent, membrane, and adsorbent capture technologies from coal combustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> Currently, only small-scale projects are storing CO<sub>2</sub> stripped from the products of combustion of coal burnt for electricity generation at coal-fired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations">power stations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> Work currently being carried out by the GreenMag Group and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Newcastle_(Australia)" title="University of Newcastle (Australia)">University of Newcastle</a> and funded by the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and Australian Governments and industry intends to have a working mineral carbonation pilot plant in operation by 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.dpi.nsw.gov.au_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-www.dpi.nsw.gov.au-49">[49]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Planet_Venus">The Planet Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=56" title="Edit section: The Planet Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Terraforming" title="Terraforming">Terraforming</a> the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> by removing CO2 from the atmosphere was first scholarly proposed by the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> in 1961,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Planet_Venus_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Planet_Venus-164">[164]</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Venus_in_fiction" title="Venus in fiction">fictional treatments</a>, such as <i>The Big Rain</i> of <a href="/wiki/The_Psychotechnic_League" title="The Psychotechnic League">The Psychotechnic League</a> by novelist <a href="/wiki/Poul_Anderson" title="Poul Anderson">Poul Anderson</a>, preceded it. Adjustments to the existing environment of Venus to support human life would require at least three major changes to the planet's atmosphere:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis2011_165-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis2011-165">[165]</a></sup> Reducing Venus's surface temperature of 462 °C (864 °F), eliminating most of the planet's dense 9.2 MPa (91 atm) <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">atmosphere</a> via removal or conversion to some other form, and the addition of breathable <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to the atmosphere. These three changes are closely interrelated, because Venus's extreme temperature is due to the high pressure of its dense atmosphere, and the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_CCS_for_power_stations">Limitations of CCS for power stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: Limitations of CCS for power stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critics say large-scale CCS deployment is unproven and decades away from being commercialized. They say that it is risky and expensive and that a better option is <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. Some environmental groups point out that CCS technology leaves behind dangerous waste material that has to be stored, just like nuclear power stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti12_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-ti12-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another limitation of CCS is its energy penalty - the reduction in overall plant efficiency due to the carbon capturing process. It has been estimated that about 60% of the energy penalty originates from the capture process itself, 30% comes from compression of CO<sub>2</sub>, while the remaining 10% comes from electricity requirements for necessary pumps and fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rubin2012_167-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin2012-167">[167]</a></sup> CCS technology is expected to use between 10 and 40 percent of the energy produced by a power station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falsehope_168-0"><a href="#cite_note-falsehope-168">[168]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thorbjornsson_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thorbjornsson-169">[169]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wide-scale adoption of CCS may erase efficiency gains in coal power plants of the last 50 years, and increase <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a> by one third. Even taking the fuel penalty into account, however, overall levels of CO<sub>2</sub> abatement would remain high at approximately 80–90%, compared to a plant without CCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> It is possible for CCS, when combined with biomass, to result in net negative emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup> Though, all of the currently (as of Feb 2011) operational BECCS (<a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a>) plants operate on point emissions other than power stations, such as <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> refineries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of CCS can reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from the stacks of coal power plants by 85–90% or more, but it has no effect on CO<sub>2</sub> emissions due to the mining and transport of coal. It will actually "increase such emissions and of air pollutants per unit of net delivered power and will increase all ecological, land-use, air-pollution, and water-pollution impacts from coal mining, transport, and processing, because the CCS system requires 25% more energy, thus 25% more coal combustion, than does a system without CCS".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWS2010_173-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWS2010-173">[173]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another concern regards the permanence of storage schemes. Opponents to CCS claim that safe and permanent storage of CO<sub>2</sub> cannot be guaranteed and that even very low leakage rates could undermine any climate mitigation effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falsehope_168-1"><a href="#cite_note-falsehope-168">[168]</a></sup> In 1986 a large leakage of naturally sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> rose from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> and asphyxiated 1,700 people. While the carbon had been sequestered naturally, some point to the event as evidence for the potentially catastrophic effects of sequestering carbon artificially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forbes.com_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti12_166-1"><a href="#cite_note-ti12-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>On one hand, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> claims that CCS could lead to a doubling of coal plant costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-falsehope_168-2"><a href="#cite_note-falsehope-168">[168]</a></sup> It is also claimed by opponents to CCS that money spent on CCS will divert investments away from other solutions to climate change. On the other hand, CCS is pointed out as economically attractive in comparison to other forms of low carbon electricity generation and seen by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> and others as a critical component for meeting <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios" title="Climate change mitigation scenarios">mitigation targets</a> such as 450 ppm and 350 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=58" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the processes involved in CCS have been demonstrated in other industrial applications, no commercial scale projects which integrate these processes exist; the costs therefore are somewhat uncertain. Some recent credible estimates indicate that the cost of capturing and storing carbon dioxide is US$60 per ton,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science323_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science323-177">[177]</a></sup> corresponding to an increase in electricity prices of about US 6c per kWh (based on typical coal-fired power plant emissions of 0.97 kg (2.13 lb) CO<sub>2</sub> per kWh). This would double the typical US industrial electricity price (now at around 6c per kWh) and increase the typical retail residential electricity price by about 50% (assuming 100% of power is from coal, which may not necessarily be the case, as this varies from state to state). Similar (approximate) price increases would likely be expected in coal dependent countries such as Australia, because the capture technology and chemistry, as well as the transport and injection costs from such power plants would not, in an overall sense, vary significantly from country to country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The reasons that CCS is expected to cause such power price increases are several. Firstly, the increased energy requirements of capturing and compressing CO<sub>2</sub> significantly raises the operating costs of CCS-equipped power plants. In addition, there are added investment and capital costs. The process would increase the fuel requirement of a plant with CCS by about 25% for a coal-fired plant, and about 15% for a gas-fired plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-5">[5]</a></sup> The cost of this extra fuel, as well as storage and other system costs, are estimated to increase the costs of energy from a power plant with CCS by 30–60%, depending on the specific circumstances. Pre-commercial CCS demonstration projects are likely to be more expensive than mature CCS technology; the total additional costs of an early large-scale CCS demonstration project are estimated to be €0.5-1.1 billion per project over the project lifetime. Other applications are possible. In the belief that use of sequestered carbon could be harnessed to offset the cost of capture and storage, Walker Architects published the first CO<sub>2</sub> gas <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air_energy_storage" title="Compressed air energy storage">CAES</a> application, proposing the use of sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> for Energy Storage on October 24, 2008. To date the feasibility of such potential offsets to the cost have not been examined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The cost of CCS depends on the cost of capture and storage, which varies according to the method used. Geological storage in saline formations or depleted oil or gas fields typically cost US$0.50–8.00 per tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> injected, plus an additional US$0.10–0.30 for monitoring costs. When storage is combined with <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> to extract extra oil from an oil field, however, the storage could yield net benefits of US$10–16 per tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> injected (based on 2003 oil prices). This would likely negate some of the effect of the carbon capture when the oil was burnt as fuel. Even taking this into account, as the table above shows, the benefits do not outweigh the extra costs of capture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Cost of electricity generated by different sources including those incorporating CCS technologies can be found in <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_electricity_by_source" title="Cost of electricity by source">cost of electricity by source</a>.
If CO<sub>2</sub> capture was part of a fuel cycle then the CO<sub>2</sub> would have value rather than be a cost. The proposed Solar Fuel or methane cycle proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> amongst others is an example. This "solar fuel"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup> cycle uses the excess electrical renewable energy to create hydrogen via electrolysis of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup> The hydrogen is then combined with CO<sub>2</sub> to create synthetic natural gas SNG and stored in the gas network. See the latest Cost Report on the Cost of CO<sub>2</sub> Capture produced by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.zeroemissionsplatform.eu/library/publication/166-zep-cost-report-capture.html" rel="nofollow">Zero Emissions Platform</a>
</p><p>Governments around the world have provided a range of different types of funding support to CCS demonstration projects, including tax credits, allocations and grants. The funding is associated with both a desire to accelerate innovation activities for CCS as a low-carbon technology and the need for economic stimulus activities. As of 2011, approximately US$23.5bn has been made available to support large-scale CCS demonstration projects around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_storage_and_the_Kyoto_Protocol">Carbon capture and storage and the Kyoto Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=59" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and storage and the Kyoto Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One way to finance future CCS projects could be through the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP16" title="COP16">COP16</a> in 2010, The Subsidiary Body for Scientific and Technological Advice, at its thirty-third session, issued a draft document recommending the inclusion of Carbon dioxide capture and storage in geological formations in Clean Development Mechanism project activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup> At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP17" title="COP17">COP17</a> in <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a>, a final agreement was reached enabling CCS projects to receive support through the Clean Development Mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=60" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(January 2009)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The theoretical merit of CCS systems is the reduction of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by up to 90%, depending on plant type. Generally, environmental effects from use of CCS arise during power production, CO<sub>2</sub> capture, transport, and storage. Issues relating to storage are discussed in those sections.
</p><p>Additional energy is required for CO<sub>2</sub> capture, and this means that substantially more fuel has to be used to produce the same amount of power, depending on the plant type. For new super-critical pulverized coal (PC) plants using current technology, the extra energy requirements range from 24 to 40%, while for natural gas combined cycle (NGCC) plants the range is 11–22% and for coal-based gasification combined cycle (IGCC) systems it is 14–25% [IPCC, 2005].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup> Obviously, fuel use and environmental problems arising from mining and extraction of coal or gas increase accordingly. Plants equipped with <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a> (FGD) systems for <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> control require proportionally greater amounts of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, and systems equipped with <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">selective catalytic reduction</a> systems for <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">nitrogen oxides produced during combustion</a> require proportionally greater amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>.
</p><p>IPCC has provided estimates of air emissions from various CCS plant designs (see table below). While CO<sub>2</sub> is drastically reduced though never completely captured, emissions of air pollutants increase significantly, generally due to the energy penalty of capture. Hence, the use of CCS entails a reduction in air quality. Type and amount of air pollutants still depends on technology. CO<sub>2</sub> is captured with alkaline solvents catching the acidic CO<sub>2</sub> at low temperatures in the absorber and releasing CO<sub>2</sub> at higher temperatures in a desorber. Chilled Ammonia CCS Plants have inevitable ammonia emissions to air. "Functionalized Ammonia" emit less ammonia, but amines may form secondary amines and these will emit volatile nitrosamines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> by a side reaction with nitrogendioxide, which is present in any flue gas even after DeNOx. Nevertheless, there are advanced amines in testing with little to no vapor pressure to avoid these amine- and consecutive nitrosamine emissions. Nevertheless, all the capture plants amines have in common, that practically 100% of remaining sulfur dioxide from the plant is washed out of the flue gas, the same applies to dust/ash.
</p>
