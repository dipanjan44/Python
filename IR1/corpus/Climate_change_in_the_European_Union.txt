<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_inventory">Greenhouse gas inventory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas inventory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2016 European Energy Agency (EEA) report documents greenhouse gas emissions between 1990 and 2014 for the EU-28 individual member states by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eea-2016-a_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eea-2016-a-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eea-2016-b_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-eea-2016-b-2">[2]</a></sup>
Total greenhouse gas emissions fell by 24% between 1990 and 2014, but road transport emissions rose by 17%.  Cars, vans, and trucks had the largest absolute increase in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions of any sector over the last 25 years, growing by 124<span class="nowrap"> </span>Mt.  <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">Aviation</a> also grew by 93<span class="nowrap"> </span>Mt over the same period, a massive 82% increase.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrofluorocarbons">Hydrofluorocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hydrofluorocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trifluoromethane" title="Trifluoromethane">Trifluoromethane</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HFC-23" title="HFC-23">HFC-23</a>) is generated and emitted as a byproduct during the production of <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">chlorodifluoromethane</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCFC-22" title="HCFC-22">HCFC-22</a>). HCFC-22 is used both in emissive applications (primarily <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a>) and as a feedstock for production of  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_polymer" title="Synthetic polymer">synthetic polymers</a>. Because HCFC-22 depletes stratospheric ozone, its production for non-feedstock uses is scheduled to be phased out under the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>. However, feedstock production is permitted to continue indefinitely.
</p><p>In the developed world, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HFC-23" title="HFC-23">HFC-23</a> emissions decreased between 1990 and 2000 due to process
optimization and thermal destruction, although there were increased emissions in the intervening years.
</p><p>The United States (U.S.) and the European Union drove these trends in the developed world.Although emissions increased in the EU between 1990 and 1995 due to increased production of HCFC-22, a combination of process optimization and thermal oxidation led to a sharp decline in EU emissions after 1995, resulting in a net decrease in emissions of 67 percent for this region between 1990 and 2000.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane">Methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The decline in emissions from 1990 to 1995 in the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> is largely due to non-climate regulatory programs and the collection and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flaring" title="wikt:flaring">flaring</a> or use of <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill methane</a>. In many OECD countries, landfill <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> are not expected to grow, despite continued or even increased waste generation, because of non-climate change related regulations that result in mitigation of air emissions, collection of gas, or closure of facilities. A major driver in the OECD is the <a href="/wiki/Landfill_Directive" title="Landfill Directive">European Union Landfill Directive</a>, which limits the amount of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> that can enter <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_waste_facility&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solid waste facility (page does not exist)">solid waste facilities</a>. Although organic matter is expected to decrease rapidly in the EU, emissions occur as a result of total waste in place. Emissions will have a gradual decline over time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_(1900-2017).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1205" data-file-width="1200" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png/330px-Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png/495px-Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png/660px-Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_%281900-2017%29.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Increase_of_average_yearly_temperature_in_Europe_(1900-2017).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Increase of average yearly temperature in selected cities in Europe (1900-2017)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Climate change affects both people and the environment in the world as well as in Europe. Human-induced climate change has the potential to alter the prevalence and severity of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> like storms, floods, droughts, heat waves and cold waves. These extreme weather changes may increase the severity of diseases in animals as well as humans. The heat waves will increase the forest fires. Experts have warned that the climate change may increase the number of global climate refugees from 150 million in 2008 to 800 million in future. <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">International agreement of refugees</a> does not recognize the climate change refugees.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">The summer of 2003</a> was probably the <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">hottest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> since at latest ad 1500, and unusually large numbers of heat-related deaths were reported in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. According to  <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature (journal)</a> it is very likely that the heat wave was human induced by greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> (2012) the average temperature over land in Europe in the last decade was 1.3 °C warmer than the pre-industrial level, which makes it the warmest decade on record. Exceptional melting in the Greenland ice sheet was recorded in the summer of 2012. Arctic sea ice extent and volume have been decreasing much faster than projected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study of future changes in flood, heat-waves, and drought impacts for 571 European cities, using climate model runs from the Coupled Model Inter-comparison Project Phase 5 (CMIP5) found that heat-wave days increase across all cities, but especially in southern Europe, whilst the greatest heatwave temperature increases are expected in central European cities. For the low impact scenario drought conditions intensify in southern European cities while river flooding worsens in northern European cities. However, the high impact scenario projects that most European cities will see increases in both drought and river flood risks. Over 100 cities are particularly vulnerable to two or more climate impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

</p>
