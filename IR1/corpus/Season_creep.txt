<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Season creep</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TakiZakura2012Apr29(SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg/220px-TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg/330px-TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg/440px-TakiZakura2012Apr29%28SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TakiZakura2012Apr29(SakuraLooksWaterfall-MiharuFukushimaJapan).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cherry blossoms in Japan have been emerging earlier in recent years due to climate change</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a>, <b>season creep</b> refers to observed changes in the timing of the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7506-8320-1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7506-8320-1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macmillan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan-2">[2]</a></sup> such as earlier indications of spring<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MED_Magazine_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MED_Magazine-3">[3]</a></sup> widely observed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> areas across the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleland2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleland2006-5">[5]</a></sup>
Phenological records analyzed by climate scientists have shown significant temporal trends in the observed time of seasonal events,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-7">[7]</a></sup>
from the end of the 20th century and continuing into the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleland2006_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cleland2006-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record-Eagle_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Record-Eagle-8">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, season creep has been associated with the arrival of spring moving up by approximately one week in a recent 30-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suddenly_spring_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suddenly_spring-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_NEWS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_NEWS-10">[10]</a></sup>
Other studies have put the rate of season creep measured by plant phenology in the range of 2–3 days per decade advancement in spring, and 0.3–1.6 days per decade delay in autumn, over the past 30–80 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherry2007_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherry2007-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Observable changes in nature related to season creep include birds laying their eggs earlier and buds appearing on some trees in late winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman.com_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman.com-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition to advanced budding, <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering</a> trees have been blooming earlier, for example the culturally-important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakura" title="Sakura">cherry blossoms</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller-rushing2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-rushing2007-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleland2007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleland2007-14">[14]</a></sup>
and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-226-45513-0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-226-45513-0-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chung2011_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chung2011-17">[17]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Northern_hardwood_forest" title="Northern hardwood forest">Northern hardwood forests</a>  have been trending toward leafing out sooner, and retaining their green <a href="/wiki/Canopy_(biology)" title="Canopy (biology)">canopies</a> longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson2006-18">[18]</a></sup>
The agricultural <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a> has also expanded by 10–20 days over the last few decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linderholm2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Linderholm2006-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effects of season creep have been noted by non-scientists as well, including gardeners who have advanced their spring planting times,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philadelphia_Inquirer_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philadelphia_Inquirer-20">[20]</a></sup>
and experimented with plantings of less <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">hardy</a> warmer climate varieties of non-native plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knoxville_News_Sentinel_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knoxville_News_Sentinel-21">[21]</a></sup> While summer growing seasons are expanding, winters are getting warmer and shorter, resulting in reduced winter ice cover on bodies of water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magnuson2000_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magnuson2000-22">[22]</a></sup>
earlier ice-out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodgkins2002_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgkins2002-23">[23]</a></sup>
earlier melt water flows,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost-24">[24]</a></sup>
and earlier spring lake level peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Scientist_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Scientist-25">[25]</a></sup>
Some spring events, or "phenophases", have become intermittent or unobservable; for example, bodies of water that once froze regularly most winters now freeze less frequently,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record-Eagle_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Record-Eagle-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYSDEC_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYSDEC-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freep_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freep-27">[27]</a></sup>
and formerly <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory birds</a> are now seen year-round in some areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Portsmouth_Herald_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Portsmouth_Herald-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_global_warming">Relationship to global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Season_creep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relationship to global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:American_Robin_(23225302456).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2572" data-file-width="4269" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg/220px-American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg/330px-American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg/440px-American_Robin_%2823225302456%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:American_Robin_(23225302456).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Robin" title="American Robin">American Robin</a> has ceased to <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrate</a> in some areas</div></div></div>
<p>The full <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">impact of global warming</a> is forecast to happen in the future, but climate scientists have cited season creep as an easily observable effect of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> that has already occurred and continues to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleland2006_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cleland2006-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman.com_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman.com-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linderholm2006_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Linderholm2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
A large systematic phenological examination of data on 542 plant species in 21 European countries from 1971–2000 showed that 78% of all leafing, flowering, and fruiting records advanced while only 3% were significantly delayed, and these observations were consistent with measurements of observed warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_NEWS_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_NEWS-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Menzel2006_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Menzel2006-31">[31]</a></sup>
Similar changes in the phenology of plants and animals are occurring across marine, freshwater, and terrestrial groups studied, and these changes are also consistent with the expected impact of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parmesan2006_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parmesan2006-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>While phenology fairly consistently points to an earlier spring across temperate regions of North America, a recent comprehensive study of the <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> showed greater variability in the timing of green-up, with some areas advancing, and some having no discernible trend over a recent 44-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delbart2007_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delbart2007-33">[33]</a></sup>
Another 40 year phenological study in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> found greater warming over that period in the more northerly sites studied, with sites experiencing cooling mostly in the south, indicating that the temperature variation with latitude is decreasing there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-34">[34]</a></sup>
This study also confirmed that season creep was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlated" title="Correlated">correlated</a> with warming, but the effect is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a>—phenophases advanced less with greater warming, and retarded more with greater cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-China-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Shorter winters and longer growing seasons may appear to be a benefit to society from <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, but the effects of advanced phenophases may also have serious consequences for human populations. Modeling of snowmelt predicted that warming of 3 to 5 °C in the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western United States</a> could cause snowmelt-driven runoff to occur as much as two months earlier, with profound effects on <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a>, land use, agriculture, and water management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
Since 1980, earlier snowmelt and associated warming has also been associated with an increase in length and severity of the <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a> season there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Season creep may also have adverse effects on plant species as well. Earlier flowering could occur before pollinators such as <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a> become active, which would have negative consequences for <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> and reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chung2011_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chung2011-17">[17]</a></sup> Shorter and warmer winters may affect other environmental <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a> including <a href="/wiki/Cold_hardening" title="Cold hardening">cold hardening</a> of trees, which could result in frost damage during more severe winters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chung2011_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chung2011-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Season_creep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Season creep</i> was included in the 9th edition of the <a href="/wiki/Collins_English_Dictionary" title="Collins English Dictionary">Collins English Dictionary</a> published in London June 4, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_News_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_News-38">[38]</a></sup>
The term was popularized in the media after the report titled "Season Creep: How Global Warming Is Already Affecting The World Around Us" was published by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">environmental organization</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clear_the_Air_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clear the Air (United States) (page does not exist)">Clear the Air</a> on March 21, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Word_Spy_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Word_Spy-39">[39]</a></sup>
In the "Season Creep" report, Jonathan Banks, Policy Director for Clear the Air, introduced the term as follows:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>While to some, an early arrival of spring may sound good, an imbalance in the ecosystem can wreak havoc. Natural processes like flowers blooming, birds nesting, insects emerging, and ice melting are triggered in large part by temperature. As temperatures increase globally, the delicately balanced system begins to fall into ecological disarray. We call this season creep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Word_Spy_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Word_Spy-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>

<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/season_creep" title="wiktionary:Special:Search/season creep">season creep</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The term "season creep" has been applied in other contexts as well:
</p>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Professional_sports" title="Professional sports">professional sports</a>, <i>season creep</i> refers to lengthening of the playing season, especially the extension of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball_schedule" title="Major League Baseball schedule">MLB season</a> to 162 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/College_athletics" title="College athletics">college athletics</a>, <i>season creep</i> refers to longer periods athletes spend training in their sport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_States" title="Politics of the United States">American politics</a>, <i>campaign season creep</i> refers to the need for candidates to start fund raising activities sooner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post-Bulletin_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post-Bulletin-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retailing" title="Retailing">retailing</a>,  <i>holiday season creep</i>, also known as <i><a href="/wiki/Christmas_creep" title="Christmas creep">Christmas creep</a></i> refers to the earlier appearance of Christmas-themed merchandising, extending the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_and_holiday_season" title="Christmas and holiday season">holiday shopping season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macmillan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titleChicago_Sun-Times::_Search_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-titleChicago_Sun-Times::_Search-43">[43]</a></sup></li></ul>
