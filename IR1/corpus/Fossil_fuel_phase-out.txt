<p><b>Fossil fuel phase out</b> refers to the discontinuation of the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>, through the decommissioning of operating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_station" title="Fossil-fuel power station">fossil fuel-fired power plants</a>, the prevention of the construction of new ones, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a> to replace the role of fossil fuels.
</p><p>The purpose of fossil fuel phase-out is to reduce the negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Externalities" title="Externalities">externalities</a> that use of fossil fuels cause. Negative externalities refer to the costs a certain activity has over people who did not choose to incur in them. A direct negative externality from fossil fuels' use is <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and an indirect negative externality are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mining_accidents" title="Mining accidents">mining accidents</a>, that happen as a consequence of the extraction of fossil fuels. Fossil fuel burning contributes to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, as it releases <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuels">Fossil fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fossil fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="2200" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png/220px-Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png/330px-Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png/440px-Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Top_5_Coal_Consumers.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coal consumption trends 1980–2012 in the top five coal-consuming countries (US EIA)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castle_Gate_Power_Plant,_Utah_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1439" data-file-width="2293" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/220px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/330px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/440px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castle_Gate_Power_Plant,_Utah_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coal-fired power plants provided 30% of consumed electricity in the United States in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This is the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Power_Plant" title="Carbon Power Plant">Castle Gate Plant</a> near <a href="/wiki/Helper,_Utah" title="Helper, Utah">Helper, Utah</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Coal is one of the largest sources of energy, supplying 28.6% percent of the world's primary energy in 2014 (equivalent to 3,917 <a href="/wiki/Tonne_of_oil_equivalent" title="Tonne of oil equivalent">Mtoe</a>) according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Coal combustion accounted for 14,863 Mt of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions in 2014, which is equivalent to a 45.9% of fossil fuel emissions from combustion (excluding non-energy emissions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>To decrease carbon emissions and thus possibly stop extreme climate change, some have called for coal to be phased out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Climatologist James E. Hansen said "We need a moratorium on coal now...with phase-out of existing plants over the next two decades."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to a study published in <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a> in 2017, coal has to be phased-out globally by about 2030, if the agreed 2 °C target is taken seriously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some nations have decreased their coal consumption thus far in the 21st century, the greatest reductions being in the United States (coal consumption reduced by 176 million metric tons per year over the period 2000-2012), Canada (reduced by 21 million tons per year) and Spain (20 million tons per year). Other nations have increased their coal consumption in the same period, led by China (increased 2,263 million metric tons per year in the period 2000-2012), India (increased 367 million tons per year), and South Korea (59 million tons per year). Worldwide, coal consumption increased 60% during the period 2000-2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As of 2012, 1200 new coal power plants were reportedly being planned worldwide, most of them in China and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 2011-2013 period, the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> group of Western European countries has increased the use of coal, attributed largely to the low cost of coal and the high price of imported natural gas in Western Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
However, coal consumption has peaked in China in 2013 or 2014, depending on the data used and fell in 2015 by 3.6%, even though there was a growth of GDP of 6.9%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Worldwide coal consumption peaked in 2014 and declined in 2015 and 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a>, the average coal plant emits more than 100 times as much radiation per year than does a comparatively sized nuclear power plant, in the form of fly ash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some like the "coal advisory board" of the IEA believe that coal should not be phased out, considering that longer-term global economic growth cannot be achieved without adequate and affordable energy supplies, which will require continuing significant contributions from fossil fuels including coal. In this viewpoint, <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">clean coal technology</a> could reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions compatible with a low-emissions future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Some environmentalists and climatologists support a phase-out and criticise clean coal as not a solution to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Entrepreneurs promote improved regulations and modernised technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sometimes coal is replaced by natural gas, which has lower carbon emissions and produces less pollutants. However natural gas is also a fossil fuel, so a switch from coal to natural gas does not contribute to a fossil fuel phase-out.
</p><p>As of October 2018, 28 national governments, 19 sub-national governments and 28 organisations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> had become members of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Powering_Past_Coal_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Powering Past Coal Alliance (page does not exist)">Powering Past Coal Alliance</a>, each making a declaration to advance the transition away from unabated coal power generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil">Oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Oil is refined into <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. The refined products are primarily for transportation by conventional cars, trucks, trains, planes and ships.  Popular alternatives are <a href="/wiki/Human-powered_transport" title="Human-powered transport">Human-powered transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_vehicles" title="Electric vehicles">electric vehicles</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofuels" title="Biofuels">biofuels</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a></div>
<p>Although natural gas has about half the carbon intensity of coal it is also the single largest source of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a> in the United States. It is seen by many as a temporary "bridge fuel" to replace coal, but in turn to be replaced by renewable sources. However this "bridge fuel" is likely to  significantly extend the use of fossil fuel as the average plant life is 35 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Gas consumption has tripled since 1971, and by 2015 it was generating half as much electricity as coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Since the consumption of gas is expected to grow an additional 10% by 2040, the phase out is likely to be many years in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basis">Basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basis of phasing-out fossil fuels consists mainly of the projected lower cost of renewable sources of energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> but the avoidance of risks in health and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation of global warming</a> are also important considerations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using computer modeling he developed over 20 years, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Z._Jacobson" title="Mark Z. Jacobson">Mark Z. Jacobson</a> has found that <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbonaceous</a> fuel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">soot emissions</a> (which lead to respiratory illness, heart disease, and asthma) have resulted in 1.5 million premature deaths each year, mostly in the developing world where the non-fossil fuels wood and animal dung are used for cooking. Jacobson has also said that soot from diesel engines, coal-fired power plants, and burning wood is a "bigger cause of global warming than previously thought, and is the major cause of the rapid melting of the Arctic's sea ice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dp_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-dp-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, new evidence has emerged that there are considerable risks associated with traditional energy sources, and that major changes to the mix of energy technologies are needed:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Several mining tragedies globally have underscored the human toll of the coal supply chain. New EPA initiatives targeting air toxics, coal ash, and effluent releases highlight the environmental impacts of coal and the cost of addressing them with control technologies. The use of fracking in natural gas exploration is coming under scrutiny, with evidence of groundwater contamination and greenhouse gas emissions. Concerns are increasing about the vast amounts of water used at coal-fired plants, particularly in regions of the country facing water shortages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming_mitigation">Global warming mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global warming mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div><p>In 2008, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James Hansen</a> and nine other scientists published a journal article titled "Target atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>: Where should humanity aim?" which calls for a complete phase-out of coal power by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen-etal-2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-etal-2008-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, Hansen has stated that continued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_to_nuclear_power" title="Opposition to nuclear power">opposition to nuclear power</a> threatens humanity's ability to avoid dangerous climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The letter, co-authored with other climate change experts declared "If we stay on the current path," he said, "those are the consequences we'll be leaving to our children. The best candidate to avoid that is nuclear power. It's ready now. We need to take advantage of it." and "Continued opposition to nuclear power threatens humanity's ability to avoid dangerous climate change."
</p><p>Also in 2008, Pushker Kharecha and James Hansen published a peer-reviewed scientific study analyzing the effect of a coal phase-out on atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Their baseline mitigation scenario was a phaseout of global coal emissions by 2050. The authors describe the scenario as follows:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The second scenario, labeled Coal Phase-out, is meant to approximate a situation in which developed countries freeze their CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from coal by 2012 and a decade later developing countries similarly halt increases in coal emissions.  Between 2025 and 2050 it is assumed that both developed and developing countries will linearly phase out emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> from coal usage.  Thus in Coal Phase-out we have global CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from coal increasing 2% per year until 2012, 1% per year growth of coal emissions between 2013 and 2022, flat coal emissions for 2023–2025, and finally a linear decrease to zero CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from coal in 2050.  These rates refer to emissions to the atmosphere and do not constrain consumption of coal, provided the CO<sub>2</sub> is captured and sequestered. Oil and gas emissions are assumed to be the same as in the BAU [business as usual] scenario.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Kharecha and Hansen also consider three other mitigation scenarios, all with the same coal phase-out schedule but each making different assumptions about the size of oil and gas reserves and the speed at which they are depleted. Under the Business as Usual scenario, atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> peaks at 563 parts per million (ppm) in the year 2100. Under the four coal phase-out scenarios, atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> peaks at 422-446 ppm between 2045 and 2060 and declines thereafter. The key implications of the study are as follows: a phase-out of coal emissions is the most important remedy for mitigating human-induced global warming; actions should be taken toward limiting or stretching out the use of conventional oil and gas; and strict emissions-based constraints are needed for future use of unconventional fossil fuels such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrates" title="Methane hydrates">methane hydrates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The impulse of renewable energy can create jobs through the construction of new power plants and the manufacturing of the equipment that they need, as could be seen in the case of Germany and the wind power industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_285–305_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs_285–305-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_about_fossil_fuel_phase-out">Studies about fossil fuel phase-out</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Studies about fossil fuel phase-out">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> and EREC's Energy (R)evolution scenario,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the world would eliminate all fossil fuel use by 2090.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2015 <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_Action_Network" title="Climate Action Network">Climate Action Network Europe</a> released a report highlighting the need for an active phase-out of coal-fired generation across Europe.  Their analysis derived from a database of 280 coal plants and included emissions data from official EU registries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones-and-gutmann-2015_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones-and-gutmann-2015-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>A September 2016 report by Oil Change International concludes that the carbon emissions embedded in the coal, oil, and gas in currently working mines and fields, assuming that these run to the end of their working lifetimes, will take the world to just beyond the 2 °C limit contained in the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> and even further from the 1.5 °C goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mathiesen-2016_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-mathiesen-2016-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turnbull-2016_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-turnbull-2016-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muttitt-2016_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-muttitt-2016-35">[35]</a></sup>  The report observes that "one of the most powerful climate policy levers is also the simplest: stop digging for more fossil fuels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muttitt-2016_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-muttitt-2016-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>
</p><p>In October 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> (ODI) and 11 other <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a> released a report on the impact of building new coal-fired power plants in countries where a significant proportion of the population lacks access to electricity.  The report concludes that, on the whole, building coal-fired power plants does little to help the poor and may make them poorer.  Moreover, wind and solar generation are beginning to challenge coal on cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuccitelli-2016_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuccitelli-2016-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-granoff-etal-2016-webpage_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-granoff-etal-2016-webpage-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-granoff-etal-2016-report_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-granoff-etal-2016-report-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2018 study in Nature Energy suggests that 10 countries in Europe could completely phase out coal-fired electricity generation with their current infrastructure, whilst the US and Russia could phase out at least 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilson-and-staffell-2018_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilson-and-staffell-2018-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_of_fossil_fuel_phase-out">Challenges of fossil fuel phase-out</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Challenges of fossil fuel phase-out">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phase-out of fossil fuels involves many challenges, and one of them is the reliance that currently the world has on them. In 2014, fossil fuels provided 81.1% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_energy_consumption" title="Primary energy consumption">primary energy consumption</a> of the world, with approximately 11,109 Mtoe. This number is composed by 4,287 Mtoe of oil consumption; 3,918 Mtoe of coal consumption, and 2,904 Mtoe of natural gas consumption .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fossil fuel phase-out can lead to an increment in electricity prices, because of the new investments needed to replace their share in the electricity mix with alternative energy sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Another cause to increasing electricity price comes from the need to import the electricity that can't be generated nationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another impact of a phase-out of fossil fuels is in the employment. In the case of employments in the fossil fuel industry, a phase-out is logically undesired, therefore, people in the industry will usually oppose any measures that put their industries under scrutiny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_285–305_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs_285–305-27">[27]</a></sup> Endre Tvinnereim and Elisabeth Ivarsflaten studied the relationship between employment in the fossil fuel industry with the support to climate change policies. They proposed that one opportunity for displaced drilling employments in the fossil fuel industry could be in the geothermal energy industry. This was suggested as a result of their conclusion: people and companies in the fossil fuel industry will likely oppose measures that endanger their employments, unless they have other stronger alternatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This can be extrapolated to political interests, that can push against the phase-out of fossil fuels initiative. One example is how the vote of U.S. Congress members is related to the preeminence of fossil fuel industries in their respective states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation_and_initiatives_to_phase_out_coal">Legislation and initiatives to phase out coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legislation and initiatives to phase out coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_economies">Major economies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Major economies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 8 June 2015, several newspapers ran an article wrote that the leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Seven" title="Group of Seven">Group of Seven</a> (or G7, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>) agreed to phase-out fossil fuel use by 2100,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> as part of the efforts to keep global temperature increase under 2 °C. This was done as a prelude for the United Nations Climate Change Conference (a.k.a. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP_21" title="COP 21">COP 21</a>) hosted in Paris, on December of the same year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg/220px-Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg/330px-Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg/440px-Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graph_RE_Australia_2010.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Electricity generation from renewable sources in Australia in 2010</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a> party have proposed to phase out coal power stations. The NSW Greens proposed an immediate moratorium on coal-fired power stations and want to end all coal mining and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_industry_subsidies" title="Coal industry subsidies">coal industry subsidies</a>.  The Australian Greens and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a> also oppose nuclear power.  The Federal Government and Victorian State Government want to modify existing coal-fired power stations into clean coal power stations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Federal Labor government extended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandatory_Renewable_Energy_Target#Australia" title="Mandatory Renewable Energy Target">mandatory renewable energy targets</a>, an initiative to ensure that new sources of electricity are more likely to be from wind power, solar power and other sources of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Australia" title="Renewable energy in Australia">renewable energy in Australia</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> is one of the largest consumers of coal per capita, and also the largest exporter. The proposals are strongly opposed by industry, unions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and the main Opposition Party in Parliament (now forming the party in government after the September 2013 election).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Canada annually burned 60 million tons of coal, mainly for electrical power, increasing by 15 percent annually.
</p><p>In November 2016, Canada announced plans to phase-out coal-fired electricity generation by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the-guardian-2016_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-the-guardian-2016-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in 2005 <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> planned coal phase-out legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Ontario annually consumed 15 million tons of coal in large power plants to supplement nuclear power. <a href="/wiki/Nanticoke_Generating_Station" title="Nanticoke Generating Station">Nanticoke Generating Station</a> was a major source of air pollution, and Ontario suffered "smog days" during the summer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
In 2007, Ontario's Liberal government committed to phasing out all coal generation in the province by 2014. Premier Dalton McGuinty said, "By 2030 there will be about 1,000 more new coal-fired generating stations built on this planet. There is only one place in the world that is phasing out coal-fired generation and we're doing that right here in Ontario."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Ontario Power Authority projects that in 2014, with no coal generation, the largest sources of electrical power in the province will be nuclear (57 percent), hydroelectricity (25 percent), and natural gas (11 percent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In April 2014 <a href="/wiki/Ontario_electricity_policy" title="Ontario electricity policy">Ontario Canada</a> was the first jurisdiction in North America to eliminate coal in electricity generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The final coal plant in Ontario, <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bay_Generating_Station" title="Thunder Bay Generating Station">Thunder Bay Generating Station</a>, stopped burning coal in April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are currently no plans to phase out coal burning power stations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> on the national level.
</p><p>China’s exceedingly high energy demand has pushed the demand for relatively cheap coal-fired power. Each week, another 2GW of coal-fired power is put online in China.  Coal supplies about 80% of China's energy needs today, and that ratio is expected to continue, even as overall power usage grows rapidly. Serious air quality deterioration has resulted from the massive use of coal and many Chinese cities suffer severe smog events.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>As a consequence the region of Beijing has decided to phase out all its coal-fired power generation by the end of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (November 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2009 China had 172GW of installed hydro capacity the largest in the world, producing 16% of China's electricity, the Eleventh Five-Year Plan has set a 300GW target for 2020. China built the world's largest power plant of any kind, the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a>.
</p><p>In addition to the huge investments in coal power, China has 32<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> reactors under construction, the highest number in the world.
</p><p>Analysis in 2016 shows that China's coal consumption appears to have peaked in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carrington-2016_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-carrington-2016-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-qi-etal-2016_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-qi-etal-2016-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2014, <a href="/wiki/Climate_Action_Network" title="Climate Action Network">CAN Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF European Policy Office</a>, HEAL, <a href="/wiki/European_Environmental_Bureau" title="European Environmental Bureau">EEB</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate-Alliance_Germany" title="Climate-Alliance Germany">Climate-Alliance Germany</a> published a report calling for the decommissioning of the thirty most polluting coal-fired power plants in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gutmann-etal-2014_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-gutmann-etal-2014-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As part of their Climate policy Plan, Denmark stated that it will phase out oil for heating purposes and coal by 2030. Additionally, their goal is to supply a 100% of their electricity and heating needs with renewable energy five years later (i.e. 2035).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emmanuel_Macron_(2017-05-29,_cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="939" data-file-width="1326" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg/220px-Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg/330px-Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg/440px-Emmanuel_Macron_%282017-05-29%2C_cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emmanuel_Macron_(2017-05-29,_cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>On 30 December 2017, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron" title="Emmanuel Macron">Emmanuel Macron</a> signed a law planning the end of fossil-fuel extraction in French territories.</div></div></div>
<p>In December 2017, to fight against <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, France adopted a law banning new <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> exploitation projects and closing current ones by 2040 in all of its territories. France thus became the first country to programme the end of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="800" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg/220px-Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg/330px-Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg/440px-Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ende_Gelaende_Einzug_Tagebau_Tag_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3,500-4,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_activist" title="Environmental activist">environmental activists</a> blocking a coal mine to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">limit climate change</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ende_Gel%C3%A4nde_2016" title="Ende Gelände 2016">Ende Gelände 2016</a>).</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_coal" title="Hard coal">Hard coal</a> mining has long been subsidized in Germany, reaching a peak of €6.7<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in 1996 and dropping to €2.7<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in 2005 due to falling output.  These subsidies represent a burden on public finances and imply a substantial <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity cost</a>, diverting funds away from other, more beneficial public investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frondel-etal-2007_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-frondel-etal-2007-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007 Germany announced plans to phase out hard coal-industry subsidies by 2018, a move which is expected to end hard coal mining in Germany. <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (January 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-67">[67]</a></sup> This exit is later than the EU-mandated end by 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Solar and wind are major sources of energy and renewable energy generation, around 15% as of December 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> and growing. Coal is still the largest source of power in Germany.
</p><p>In 2007 <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">German Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> and her party agreed to legislation to phase out Germany's hard coal mining sector. That does not mean that they support phasing out coal in general. There were plans to build about 25 new plants in the coming years. Most German coal power plants were built in the 1960s, and have a low energy efficiency. Public sentiment against coal power plants is growing and the construction or planning of some plants has been stopped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-67">[67]</a></sup> A number are under construction and still being built. No concrete plan is in place to reduce coal-fired electricity generation. As of October 2015, the remaining coal plants still under planning include: Niederaussem, Profen, and Stade. The coal plants currently under construction include: Mannheim, Hamm D, Datteln, and Willhelmshaven. Between 2012 and 2015, six new plants went online. All of these plants are 600–1800 MW<sub>e</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014 Germany's coal consumption dropped for the first time, having risen each year since the low during the 2009 recession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bp-statistical-review-2015_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-bp-statistical-review-2015-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2014 study finds that coal is not making a comeback in Germany, as is sometimes claimed.  Rather renewables have more than offset the nuclear facilities that have been shutdown as a result of Germany's <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_phase-out#Germany" title="Nuclear power phase-out">nuclear phase-out</a> (<i>Atomausstieg</i>).  Hard coal plants now face financial stringency as their operating hours are cut back by the market.  But in contrast, <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>-fired generation is in a safe position until the <span class="nowrap">mid-2020s</span> unless government policies change.  To phase-out coal, Germany should seek to strength the emissions trading system (<a href="/wiki/European_Union_Emission_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emission Trading Scheme">EU-ETS</a>), consider a carbon tax, promote <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>, and strengthen the use of natural gas as a bridge fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jungjohann-and-morris-2014_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-jungjohann-and-morris-2014-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 the German government and affected <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> power plant operators <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitteldeutsche_Braunkohlengesellschaft" title="de:Mitteldeutsche Braunkohlengesellschaft">Mibrag</a>, <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a> reached an understanding (<i>Verständigung</i>) on the transfer of lignite power plant units into security standby (<i>Überführung von Braunkohlekraftwerksblöcken in die Sicherheitsbereitschaft</i>).  As a result, eight lignite-fired power plants are to be mothballed and later closed, with the first plant scheduled to cease operation in October 2016 and the last in October 2019.  The affected operators will receive state compensation for foregone profits.  The <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> has declared government plans to use €1.6<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion of public financing for this purpose to be in line with EU <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_aid" title="State aid">state aid</a> rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-german-energy-blog-2016_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-german-energy-blog-2016-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2016 study finds that the phase-out of lignite in <a href="/wiki/Lusatia" title="Lusatia">Lusatia</a> (<i>Lausitz</i>) by 2030 can be financed by future owner <a href="/wiki/Energetick%C3%BD_a_pr%C5%AFmyslov%C3%BD_holding" title="Energetický a průmyslový holding">EPH</a> in a manner that avoids taxpayer involvement.  Instead, liabilities covering decommissioning and land rehabilitation could be paid by EPH directly into a foundation, perhaps run by the public company <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lausitzer_und_Mitteldeutsche_Bergbau-Verwaltungsgesellschaft" title="de:Lausitzer und Mitteldeutsche Bergbau-Verwaltungsgesellschaft">LMBV</a>.  The study calculates the necessary provisions at €2.6<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieefa-2016_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieefa-2016-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wynn-and-julve-2016_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-wynn-and-julve-2016-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2016 the German utility <a href="/wiki/Evonik_Industries#Energy_Business_Area" title="Evonik Industries">STEAG</a> announced it will be decommissioning five coal-fired generating units in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a> due to low wholesale electricity prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clew-2016a_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-clew-2016a-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handelsblatt-2016_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-handelsblatt-2016-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>A coal phase-out for Germany is implied in Germany's <a href="/wiki/German_Climate_Action_Plan_2050" title="German Climate Action Plan 2050">Climate Action Plan 2050</a>, environment minister <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hendricks_(politician)" title="Barbara Hendricks (politician)">Barbara Hendricks</a> said in an interview on 21<span class="nowrap"> </span>November 2016.  "If you read the Climate Action Plan carefully, you will find that the exit from coal-fired power generation is the immanent consequence of the energy sector target.<span class="nowrap"> </span>... By 2030<span class="nowrap"> </span>... half of the coal-fired power production must have ended, compared to 2014", she said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clew-2016b_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-clew-2016b-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goetze-and-schwarz-2016_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-goetze-and-schwarz-2016-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plans to cut down the ancient <a href="/wiki/Hambach_Forest" title="Hambach Forest">Hambach Forest</a> to extend the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hambach_open_pit_mine" title="Hambach open pit mine">Hambach open pit mine</a> in 2018 have resulted in massive protests.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 22 September 2016 the Dutch parliament voted for a 55% cut in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by 2030, a move which would require the closure of the country's five coal-fired power plants.  The vote is not binding on the government however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neslen=2016_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-neslen=2016-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In October 2018, the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A1nchez_government" title="Sánchez government">Sánchez government</a> and spanish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_union" title="Labour union">Labour unions</a> settled an agreement to close ten <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining#SPain" title="Coal mining">spanish coal mines</a> at the end of 2018. The government pre-engaged to spend 250 million Euro to pay for early retirements, ocupational retraining and structural change. In 2018, about 2,3 per cent of the electric energy produced in Spain was produced in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-burning_power_plant" title="Coal-burning power plant">coal-burning power plants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sweden is constructing hydrogen based pilot steel plant to replace coke and coal usage in steel production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Once this technology is commercialized with the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen generated</a> from renewable energy sources (<a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> or electricity), the carbon foot print of steel production would reduce drastically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cf_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-cf-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:India_Coal_Production.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2150" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/India_Coal_Production.png/220px-India_Coal_Production.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/India_Coal_Production.png/330px-India_Coal_Production.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/India_Coal_Production.png/440px-India_Coal_Production.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:India_Coal_Production.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coal Production in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, with a 1959-2020 axis (appears to end at 2012)</div></div></div>
<p>India is the third largest consumer of coal in the world. India's federal energy minister is planning to stop importing thermal coal by 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
The annual report of India's Power Ministry has a plan to grow power by about 80GW as part of their 11th 5-year plan, and 79% of that growth will be in fossil fuel–fired power plants, primarily coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>  India plans four new "ultra mega" coal-fired power plants as part of that growth, each 4000MW in capacity. As of 2015 there are six nuclear reactors under construction. In the first half of 2016, the amount of coal-fired generating capacity in pre-construction planning in India fell by 40,000 MW, according to results released by the Global Coal Plant Tracker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> In June 2016, India's Ministry of Power stated that no further power plants would be required in the next three years, and "any thermal power plant that has yet to begin construction should back off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>In cement production, carbon neutral biomass is being used to replace coal for reducing carbon foot print drastically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cf_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-cf-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Helen_Clark" title="Helen Clark">Clark</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour</a> government introduced a 10<span class="nowrap"> </span>year moratorium on new fossil fuel thermal power generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>  The ban was limited to state-owned utilities, although an extension to the private sector was considered.  The <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">new government</a> under MP <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">NZNP</a>) <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">elected in November 2008</a> repealed this legislation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2014, almost 80 per cent of the electricity produced in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> was from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> 
On 6 August 2015, <a href="/wiki/Genesis_Energy_Limited" title="Genesis Energy Limited">Genesis Energy Limited</a> announced that it would close its two last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_station" title="Fossil-fuel power station">coal-fired power stations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2007, South Africa's power sector is the 8th highest global emitter of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> In 2005/2006, 77% of South Africa's energy demand was directly met by coal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> and when current projects come online, this ratio will increase in the near term.
</p><p>There are no plans to phase out coal-fired power plants in South Africa, and indeed, the country is investing in building massive amounts of new coal-fired capacity to meet power demands, as well as modernizing the existing coal-fired plants to meet environmental requirements.
</p><p>On April 6, 2010, the World Bank approved a $3.75B loan to South Africa to support the construction of the world's 4th largest coal-fired plant, at Medupi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
The proposed World Bank loan includes a relatively small amount - $260 million  - for wind and solar power.
</p><p>Rated at 4800MW, <a href="/wiki/Medupi_Power_Station" title="Medupi Power Station">Medupi Power Station</a> would join other mammoth coal-fired power plants already in operation in the country, namely <a href="/wiki/Kendal_Power_Station" title="Kendal Power Station">Kendal Power Station</a> (4100MW), <a href="/wiki/Majuba_Power_Station" title="Majuba Power Station">Majuba Power Station</a> (4100), and <a href="/wiki/Matimba_Power_Station" title="Matimba Power Station">Matimba Power Station</a> (4000), as well as a similar-capacity <a href="/wiki/Kusile_Power_Station" title="Kusile Power Station">Kusile Power Station</a>, at 4800MW, currently under construction.  Kusile is expected to come online in stages, starting in 2012, while Medupi is expected to first come online in 2013, with full capacity available by 2017.  These schedules are provisional, and may change.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Since 2008, South Africa's government started funding <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> installations. As of January 2016, there have been 400 000 domestic installations in total, with free-of-charge installation of low-pressure solar water heaters for low-cost homes or low-income households which have access to the electricity grid, while other installations are subsidised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a> (<a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change">energy secretary</a> from 3 October 2008 – 11 May 2010) announced that no new coal-fired power stations will be built in Britain from 2009 onwards unless they capture and bury at least 25% of greenhouse gases immediately and 100% by 2025 although at the time this was a statement of intent rather than something he was able to enforce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chris_Huhne" title="Chris Huhne">Chris Huhne</a> (energy secretary from 12 May 2010 – 5 February 2012) has confirmed that the legislation required to allow his office to enforce <a href="/wiki/Emission_standard#UK" title="Emission standard">emissions standards</a> are proceeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK is also subject to the EU's <a href="/wiki/Large_Combustion_Plant_Directive" title="Large Combustion Plant Directive">Large Combustion Plant Directive</a> covering non-CO2 emissions which is expected to bring many older plants to a close over the next few years as they are too expensive to upgrade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Amber_Rudd" title="Amber Rudd">Amber Rudd</a> (energy secretary from 11 May 2015) announced on 18 November 2015 that all coal-fired power stations would close by 2025. This will not be a complete phase out of fossil fuels because gas-fired power stations will continue to provide some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firm_power" title="Firm power">firm power</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>The closure of the last coal power station in March 2016 ended coal-fired <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_Scotland" title="Energy policy of Scotland">power production in Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, fossil fuels provided 81 percent of the energy consumed in the United States, down from 86 percent in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 2007, 154 new coal-fired plants were on the drawing board in 42 states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> By 2012, that had dropped to 15, mostly due to new rules limiting mercury emissions, and limiting carbon emissions to 1,000 pounds of CO<sub>2</sub> per megawatt-hour of electricity produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2013, US Secretary of Energy <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Moniz" title="Ernest Moniz">Ernest Moniz</a> outlined Obama administration policy on fossil fuels:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In the last four years, we’ve more than doubled renewable energy generation from wind and solar power.  However, coal and other fossil fuels still provide 80 percent of our energy, 70 percent of our electricity, and will be a major part of our energy future for decades.  That’s why any serious effort to protect our kids from the worst effects of climate change must also include developing, demonstrating and deploying the technologies to use our abundant fossil fuel resources as cleanly as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Then-US Energy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Steven Chu</a> and researchers for the US <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> have noted that greater electrical generation by non-<a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">dispatchable</a> renewables, such as wind and solar, will also increase the need for flexible natural gas-powered generators, to supply electricity during those times when solar and wind power are unavailable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Gas-powered generators have the ability to ramp up and down quickly to meet changing loads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the US, many of the fossil fuel phase-out initiatives have taken place at the state or local levels.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="California">California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>'s SB 1368 created the first governmental moratorium on new coal plants in the United States. The law was signed in September 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> took effect for investor-owned utilities in January 2007, and took effect for publicly owned utilities in August 2007. SB 1368 applied to long-term investments (five years or more) by California utilities, whether in-state or out-of-state. It set the standard for greenhouse gas emissions at 1,100 pounds of carbon dioxide per megawatt-hour, equal to the emissions of a combined-cycle natural gas plant. This standard created a de facto moratorium on new coal, since it could not be met without carbon capture and sequestration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maine">Maine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Maine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On April 15, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_E._Baldacci" title="John E. Baldacci">John E. Baldacci</a> signed LD 2126, "An Act To Minimize Carbon Dioxide Emissions from New Coal-Powered Industrial and Electrical Generating Facilities in the State." The law, which was sponsored by Rep. W. Bruce MacDonald (D-<a href="/wiki/Boothbay,_Maine" title="Boothbay, Maine">Boothbay</a>), requires the Board of Environmental Protection to develop greenhouse gas emission standards for coal gasification facilities. It also puts a moratorium in place on building any new coal gasification facilities until the standards are developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oregon">Oregon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Oregon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early March 2016, Oregon lawmakers approved a plan to stop paying for out-of-state coal plants by 2030 and require a 50 percent renewable energy standard by 2040.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Environmental groups such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Wind_Energy_Association" title="American Wind Energy Association">American Wind Energy Association</a> and leading Democrats praised the bill.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas">Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Texas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2006 a coalition of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> groups organized a campaign in favor of a statewide moratorium on new coal-fired power plants. The campaign culminated in a "Stop the Coal Rush" mobilization, including rallying and lobbying, at the state capital in  <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a> on February 11 and 12th, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Over 40 citizen groups supported the mobilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January, 2007, A resolution calling for a 180-day moratorium on new pulverized coal plants was filed in the Texas Legislature by State Rep. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_%22Doc%22_Anderson" title='Charles "Doc" Anderson'>Charles "Doc" Anderson</a> (R-Waco) as House Concurrent Resolution 43.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> The resolution was left pending in committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> On December 4, 2007, Rep. Anderson announced his support for two proposed  <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC) coal plants proposed by <a href="/wiki/Luminant" title="Luminant">Luminant</a> (formerly TXU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington_state">Washington state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Washington state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> has followed the same approach as California, prohibiting coal plants whose emissions would exceed those of natural gas plants. Substitute Senate Bill 6001 (SSB 6001), signed on May 3, 2007, by Governor <a href="/wiki/Christine_Gregoire" title="Christine Gregoire">Christine Gregoire</a>, enacted the standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitmanpioneer.com_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitmanpioneer.com-117">[117]</a></sup> As a result of SSB 6001, the <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090716184525/http://www.energy-northwest.com/generation/igcc/index.php" rel="nofollow">Pacific Mountain Energy Center</a> in Kalama was rejected by the state. However, a new plant proposal, the <a class="external text" href="http://www.wallulaenergy.com/" rel="nofollow">Wallula Energy Resource Center</a>, shows the limits of the "natural gas equivalency" approach as a means of prohibiting new coal plants. The proposed plant would meet the standard set by SSB 6001 by capturing and sequestering a portion (65 percent, according to a plant spokesman) of its carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitmanpioneer.com_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitmanpioneer.com-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_action_in_the_US">Utility action in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Utility action in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Progress_Energy_Carolinas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Progress Energy Carolinas (page does not exist)">Progress Energy Carolinas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> announced on June 1, 2007, that it was beginning a two-year moratorium on proposals for new coal-fired power plants while it undertook more aggressive efficiency and conservation programs. The company added, "Additional reductions in future electricity demand growth through energy efficiency could push the need for new power plants farther into the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Service_of_Colorado&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Public Service of Colorado (page does not exist)">Public Service of Colorado</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> concluded in its November 2007 Resource Plan: "In sum, in light of the now likely regulation of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions in the future due to broader interest in climate change issues, the increased costs of constructing new coal facilities, and the increased risk of timely permitting to meet planned in-service dates, Public Service does not believe it would not be prudent to consider at this time any proposals for new coal plants that do not include CO<sub>2</sub> capture and sequestration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burned_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-burned-121">[121]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Xcel_Energy" title="Xcel Energy">Xcel Energy</a> noted in its 2007 Resource Plan that "given the likelihood of future carbon regulation, we have only modeled a future coal-based resource option that includes carbon capture and storage."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burned_121-1"><a href="#cite_note-burned-121">[121]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minnesota_Power_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minnesota Power Company (page does not exist)">Minnesota Power Company</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> announced in December 2007 that it would not consider a new coal resource without a carbon solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burned_121-2"><a href="#cite_note-burned-121">[121]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avista_Utilities" title="Avista Utilities">Avista Utilities</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> announced that it does not anticipate pursuing coal-fired power plants in the foreseeable future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burned_121-3"><a href="#cite_note-burned-121">[121]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NorthWestern_Energy" title="NorthWestern Energy">NorthWestern Energy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> announced on December 17, 2007, that it planned to double its wind power capacity over the next seven years and steer away from new baseload coal plants. The plans are detailed in the company's 2007 Montana Electric Supply Resource Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/California_Energy_Commission" title="California Energy Commission">California Energy Commission</a> (CEC) has initiated its review of two 53.4-megawatt solar thermal power plants that will each include a 40-megawatt biomass power plant to supplement the solar power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Japan, the world's third-largest economy, made a major move to use more fossil fuels in 2012, when the nation shut down nuclear reactors following the Fukishima accident. Nuclear, which had supplied 30 percent of Japanese electricity from 1987 to 2011, supplied only 2 percent in 2012 (hydropower supplied 8 percent). Nuclear electricity was replaced with electricity from petroleum, coal, and liquified natural gas. As a result, electricity generation from fossil fuels rose to 90 percent in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2017, the Japanese government announced plans to build 45 new coal-fired power plants in the next ten years, largely to replace expensive electricity from petroleum power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opinion_polls">Opinion polls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Opinion polls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opinion_research">Opinion research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Opinion research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In October, 2007, <a class="external text" href="http://www.civilsocietyinstitute.org/" rel="nofollow">Civil Society Institute</a> released the results of a poll of 1,003 U.S. citizens conducted by Opinion Research Corporation.
</p><p>The authors of the poll reported:
"75 percent of Americans—including 65 percent of Republicans, 83 percent of Democrats and 76 percent of Independents—would 'support a five-year moratorium on new coal-fired power plants in the United States if there was stepped-up investment in clean, safe renewable energy—such as wind and solar—and improved home energy-efficiency standards.' Women (80 percent) were more likely than men (70 percent) to support this idea. Support also was higher among college graduates (78 percent) than among those who did not graduate from high school (68 percent)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p>The exact question posed by the survey was as follows:
More than half of power plant-generated electricity comes from coal. Experts say that power plants are responsible for about 40 percent of U.S. carbon dioxide pollution linked to global warming. There are plans to build more than 150 new coal-fired power plants over the next several years. Would you support a five-year moratorium on new coal-fired power plants in the United States if there was stepped-up investment in clean, safe and renewable energy—such as wind and solar—and improved home energy-efficiency standards? Would you say definitely yes, probably yes, probably no, definitely no, or don't know.
</p><p>The results were as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>30% "definitely yes"</li>
<li>45% "probably yes"</li>
<li>13% "probably no"</li>
<li>8% "definitely no"</li>
<li>4% "don't know"</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallup">Gallup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Gallup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2013, the Gallup organization determined that 41% of Americans wanted less emphasis placed on coal energy, versus 31% who wanted more. Large majorities wanted more emphasis placed on solar (76%), wind (71%), and natural gas (65%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="ABC_News.2FWashington_Post"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="ABC_News/Washington_Post">ABC News/Washington Post</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: ABC News/Washington Post">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A 2009 ABC/Washington Post poll found 52% of Americans favored more coal mining (33% strongly favored), while 45% opposed (27% strongly opposed). The most support was for wind and solar, which were favored by 91% (79% strongly favored).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CLEAN_call_to_action">CLEAN call to action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: CLEAN call to action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Defense_Fund">Environmental Defense Fund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Environmental Defense Fund">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The US-based <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defense_Fund" title="Environmental Defense Fund">Environmental Defense Fund</a> (EDF) has taken a stand in favor of natural gas production and <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a>, while pressing for stricter environmental controls on gas drilling, as a feasible way to replace coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> The organization has funded studies jointly with the petroleum industry on the environmental effects of natural gas production. The organization sees natural gas as a way to quickly replace coal, and that natural gas in time will be replaced by renewable energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup> The policy has been criticized by some environmentalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> EDF counsel and blogger Mark Brownstein answered:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Demand for natural gas is not going away, and neither is hydraulic fracturing.  We must be clear-eyed about this, and fight to protect public health and the environment from unacceptable impacts.  We must also work hard to put policies in place that ensure that natural gas serves as an enabler of renewable power generation, not an impediment to it.  We fear that those who oppose all natural gas production everywhere are, in effect, making it harder for the U.S. economy to wean itself from dirty coal.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Mark Brownstein, EDF council<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_Sources_of_Energy">Alternative Sources of Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Alternative Sources of Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">Alternative energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_transition" title="Energy transition">Energy transition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">100% renewable energy</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">Alternative energy</a> refers to any source of energy that can substitute the role of fossil fuels. <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a>, or energy that is harnessed from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable sources</a>, is an alternative energy. However, alternative energy can refer to non renewable sources as well, like <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a>. Between the alternative sources of energy are: <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">marine energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">Hydrogen</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">Energy efficiency</a> is complementary to the use of alternative energy sources, when phasing-out fossil fuels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="482" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif/220px-Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif/330px-Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif/440px-Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bp_world_energy_consumption_2016.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The worldwide growth of renewable energy is shown by the green line<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Renewable energy is <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> that comes from resources which are naturally replenished such as <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_energy" title="Tidal energy">tides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">waves</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal heat</a>. As of 2014, 19% of global final energy consumption comes from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a>, with 9% of all energy from traditional <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, mainly used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">heating</a>, 1% from biofuels, 4% from <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> and 4% from biomass, geothermal or solar heat . Popular renewables (wind, solar, geothermal and biomass for power) accounted for another 1.4% and are growing rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> While renewable energy supplies are growing and have displaced coal in some regions, the amount of coal burned in 2021, is expected to be the same as it was in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="993" data-file-width="1500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg/220px-Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg/330px-Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg/440px-Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chief_Joseph_Dam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chief_Joseph_Dam" title="Chief Joseph Dam">Chief Joseph Dam</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bridgeport,_Washington" title="Bridgeport, Washington">Bridgeport, Washington</a>, USA, is a major run-of-the-river station without a sizeable reservoir.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2015 <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric energy</a> generated 16.6% of the worlds total electricity and 70% of all renewable electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> In Europe and North America environmental concerns around land flooded by large <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a> ended 30 years of dam construction in the 1990s. Since then large dams and reservoirs continue to be built in countries like China, Brazil and India. <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Small_hydro" title="Small hydro">small hydro</a> have become popular alternatives to conventional dams that may create reservoirs in environmentally sensitive areas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">Wind farm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji,_2011_ubt.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1729" data-file-width="3072" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG/220px-Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG/330px-Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG/440px-Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji%2C_2011_ubt.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turbiny_wiatrowe_w_Szwecji,_2011_ubt.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lillgrund_Wind_Farm" title="Lillgrund Wind Farm">Lillgrund Wind Farm</a> in Sweden</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="593" data-file-width="2785" height="47" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg/220px-Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg/330px-Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg/440px-Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Windpark_Estinnes_20juli2010_kort_voor_voltooiing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First wind farm consisting of 7,5 MW Enercon E-126 turbines, <a href="/wiki/Estinnes" title="Estinnes">Estinnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, 20 July 2010, two months before completion; note the 2-part blades.</div></div></div>
<p>A wind farm is a group of <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> in the same location used to produce electric power. A large wind farm may consist of several hundred individual wind turbines, and cover an extended area of hundreds of square miles, but the land between the turbines may be used for agricultural or other purposes. A wind farm may also be located offshore.
</p><p>Wind power has grown dramatically since 2005 and by 2015 supplied almost 1% of global energy consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the largest operational onshore wind farms are located in the United States and China. The <a href="/wiki/Gansu_Wind_Farm" title="Gansu Wind Farm">Gansu Wind Farm</a> in China has over 5,000 MW installed with a goal of 20,000 MW by 2020. China has several other "wind power bases" of similar size. The <a href="/wiki/Alta_Wind_Energy_Center" title="Alta Wind Energy Center">Alta Wind Energy Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States is the largest onshore wind farm outside of China, with a capacity of 1020 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a> of power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terragen_163-0"><a href="#cite_note-terragen-163">[163]</a></sup> As of February 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Walney_Wind_Farm" title="Walney Wind Farm">Walney Wind Farm</a> in the United Kingdom is the largest offshore wind farm in the world at 367 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a>, followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanet_Offshore_Wind_Project" title="Thanet Offshore Wind Project">Thanet Offshore Wind Project</a> (300 MW), also in the United Kingdom. As of February 2012, the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A2nt%C3%A2nele-Cogealac_Wind_Farm" title="Fântânele-Cogealac Wind Farm">Fântânele-Cogealac Wind Farm</a> in Romania is the largest onshore wind farm in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> at 600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cez_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-cez-164">[164]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many large wind farms under construction and these include <a href="/wiki/Sinus_Holding_Wind_Farm" title="Sinus Holding Wind Farm">Sinus Holding Wind Farm</a> (700 MW), <a href="/wiki/Anholt_Offshore_Wind_Farm" title="Anholt Offshore Wind Farm">Anholt Offshore Wind Farm</a> (400 MW), <a href="/wiki/BARD_Offshore_1" title="BARD Offshore 1">BARD Offshore 1</a> (400 MW), <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Wind_Farm" title="Clyde Wind Farm">Clyde Wind Farm</a> (350 MW), <a href="/wiki/Greater_Gabbard_wind_farm" title="Greater Gabbard wind farm">Greater Gabbard wind farm</a> (500 MW), <a href="/wiki/Lincs_Wind_Farm" title="Lincs Wind Farm">Lincs Wind Farm</a> (270 MW), <a href="/wiki/London_Array" title="London Array">London Array</a> (1000 MW), <a href="/wiki/Lower_Snake_River_Wind_Project" title="Lower Snake River Wind Project">Lower Snake River Wind Project</a> (343 MW), <a href="/wiki/Macarthur_Wind_Farm" title="Macarthur Wind Farm">Macarthur Wind Farm</a> (420 MW), <a href="/wiki/Shepherds_Flat_Wind_Farm" title="Shepherds Flat Wind Farm">Shepherds Flat Wind Farm</a> (845 MW), and <a href="/wiki/Sheringham_Shoal_Offshore_Wind_Farm" title="Sheringham Shoal Offshore Wind Farm">Sheringham Shoal</a> (317 MW).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Denmark" title="Wind power in Denmark">Wind power in Denmark</a> produced the equivalent of 42.1% of total electricity consumption in 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euractiv1_165-0"><a href="#cite_note-euractiv1-165">[165]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enet42_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-enet42-166">[166]</a></sup> however, use of wind for heating is minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wittboil_167-0"><a href="#cite_note-wittboil-167">[167]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2020 the solar contribution to global final energy consumption will exceed 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_photovoltaics">Solar photovoltaics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Solar photovoltaics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LieberoseSolarpark.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="315" data-file-width="500" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LieberoseSolarpark.jpg/220px-LieberoseSolarpark.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LieberoseSolarpark.jpg/330px-LieberoseSolarpark.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/LieberoseSolarpark.jpg/440px-LieberoseSolarpark.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LieberoseSolarpark.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 71.8 MW <a href="/wiki/Lieberose_Photovoltaic_Park" title="Lieberose Photovoltaic Park">Lieberose Photovoltaic Park</a> in Germany.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_photovoltaic_power_stations" title="List of photovoltaic power stations">List of photovoltaic power stations</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_photovoltaic_cell" title="Solar photovoltaic cell">Solar photovoltaic cells</a> convert sunlight into electricity and many solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_station" title="Photovoltaic power station">photovoltaic power stations</a> have been built. The size of these stations has increased progressively over the last decade with frequent new capacity <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_station#Noteworthy_solar_parks" title="Photovoltaic power station">records</a>.
</p><p>As of January 2013, the largest individual photovoltaic (PV) power plants in the world are <a href="/wiki/Agua_Caliente_Solar_Project" title="Agua Caliente Solar Project">Agua Caliente Solar Project</a>, (Arizona, over 247 MW connected - to increase to 397 MW), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golmud_Solar_Park" title="Golmud Solar Park">Golmud Solar Park</a> (China, 200 MW), <a href="/wiki/Mesquite_Solar_project" title="Mesquite Solar project">Mesquite Solar project</a> (Arizona, 150 MW), <a href="/wiki/Neuhardenberg_Solar_Park" title="Neuhardenberg Solar Park">Neuhardenberg Solar Park</a> (Germany, 145 MW), <a href="/wiki/Templin_Solar_Park" title="Templin Solar Park">Templin Solar Park</a> (Germany, 128 MW), <a href="/wiki/Toul-Rosi%C3%A8res_Solar_Park" title="Toul-Rosières Solar Park">Toul-Rosières Solar Park</a> (France, 115 MW), and <a href="/wiki/Perovo_Solar_Park" title="Perovo Solar Park">Perovo Solar Park</a> (Ukraine, 100 MW). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charanka_Solar_Park" title="Charanka Solar Park">Charanka Solar Park</a> is a collection of solar power stations of which 214 MW were reported complete in April 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charanka_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charanka-172">[172]</a></sup> on a 2000 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a> site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup> It is part of <a href="/wiki/Gujarat_Solar_Park" title="Gujarat Solar Park">Gujarat Solar Park</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup> a group of solar farms at various locations in the <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> state of India, with overall capacity of 702 MW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup> There are a total of 570 MW of solar parks in Golmud, with 500 MW more expected in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many large plants are under construction. The <a href="/wiki/Desert_Sunlight_Solar_Farm" title="Desert Sunlight Solar Farm">Desert Sunlight Solar Farm</a> is a 550 MW <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> plant under construction in <a href="/wiki/Riverside_County,_California" title="Riverside County, California">Riverside County, California</a>, that will use thin-film solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> modules made by <a href="/wiki/First_Solar" title="First Solar">First Solar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rew11_178-0"><a href="#cite_note-rew11-178">[178]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Topaz_Solar_Farm" title="Topaz Solar Farm">Topaz Solar Farm</a> is a 550 MW photovoltaic power plant, being built in <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Obispo_County,_California" title="San Luis Obispo County, California">San Luis Obispo County, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-re11_179-0"><a href="#cite_note-re11-179">[179]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blythe_Solar_Power_Project" title="Blythe Solar Power Project">Blythe Solar Power Project</a> is a 500 MW photovoltaic station under construction in <a href="/wiki/Riverside_County,_California" title="Riverside County, California">Riverside County, California</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Agua_Caliente_Solar_Project" title="Agua Caliente Solar Project">Agua Caliente Solar Project</a> is a 290 megawatt photovoltaic solar generating facility being built in <a href="/wiki/Yuma_County,_Arizona" title="Yuma County, Arizona">Yuma County, Arizona</a>. The <a href="/wiki/California_Valley_Solar_Ranch" title="California Valley Solar Ranch">California Valley Solar Ranch</a> (CVSR) is a 250 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> (MW) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_photovoltaic" title="Solar photovoltaic">solar photovoltaic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plant</a>, which is being built by <a href="/wiki/SunPower" title="SunPower">SunPower</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Carrizo_Plain" title="Carrizo Plain">Carrizo Plain</a>, northeast of <a href="/wiki/California_Valley,_California" title="California Valley, California">California Valley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> The 230 MW <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_plants_in_the_Mojave_Desert#Antelope_Valley_Solar_Ranch" title="Solar power plants in the Mojave Desert">Antelope Valley Solar Ranch</a> is a <a href="/wiki/First_Solar" title="First Solar">First Solar</a> photovoltaic project which is under construction in the Antelope Valley area of the Western Mojave Desert, and due to be completed in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of these plants are integrated with agriculture and some use innovative tracking systems that follow the sun's daily path across the sky to generate more electricity than conventional fixed-mounted systems. Solar power plants have no fuel costs or emissions during operation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Concentrated_solar_power">Concentrated solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: Concentrated solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:12-05-08_AS1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/12-05-08_AS1.JPG/220px-12-05-08_AS1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/12-05-08_AS1.JPG/330px-12-05-08_AS1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/12-05-08_AS1.JPG/440px-12-05-08_AS1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:12-05-08_AS1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 150 MW <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andasol_solar_power_station" title="Andasol solar power station">Andasol solar power station</a> is a commercial <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic trough</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" title="Solar thermal">solar thermal</a> power plant, located in <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Spain" title="Renewable energy in Spain">Spain</a>. The Andasol plant uses tanks of molten salt to store solar energy so that it can continue generating electricity even when the sun isn't shining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">Concentrated solar power</a></div>
<p>Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) systems use lenses or mirrors and tracking systems to focus a large area of sunlight into a small beam. The concentrated heat is then used as a heat source for a conventional power plant. A wide range of concentrating technologies exists; the most developed are the parabolic trough, the concentrating linear fresnel reflector, the Stirling dish and the solar power tower. Various techniques are used to track the Sun and focus light. In all of these systems a <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a> is heated by the concentrated sunlight, and is then used for power generation or energy storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_2005_183-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_2005-183">[183]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">Biomass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=56" title="Edit section: Biomass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a> is <a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biological material</a> from living, or recently living organisms, most often referring to plants or plant-derived materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biomass_Energy_Center_186-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biomass_Energy_Center-186">[186]</a></sup> As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_source" title="Renewable energy source">renewable energy source</a>, biomass can either be used directly, or indirectly—once or converted into another type of energy product such as <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>. Biomass can be converted to energy in three ways: <i>thermal conversion</i>, <i>chemical conversion</i>, and <i>biochemical conversion</i>.
</p><p>Using biomass as a fuel produces <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a> (nitrogen oxides), VOCs (<a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a>), particulates and other pollutants at levels above those from traditional fuel sources such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>  in some cases (such as with indoor heating and cooking).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup> Utilization of wood biomass as a fuel can also produce fewer particulate and other pollutants than open burning as seen in wildfires or direct heat applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-190"><a href="#cite_note-190">[190]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">Black carbon</a> – a pollutant created by combustion of fossil fuels, biofuels, and biomass – is possibly the second largest contributor to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-starke-2009_191-0"><a href="#cite_note-starke-2009-191">[191]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56–57</span></sup> In 2009 a Swedish study of the giant brown haze that periodically covers large areas in South Asia determined that it had been principally produced by biomass burning, and to a lesser extent by fossil fuel burning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Denmark" title="Energy in Denmark">Denmark</a> has increased the use of biomass and garbage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> and decreased the use of coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_energy">Nuclear energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: Nuclear energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2014 <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> report identifies nuclear energy as one of the technologies that can  provide electricity with less than 5% of the lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions of coal power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup> There are more than 60 nuclear reactors shown as under construction in the list of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_by_country" title="Nuclear power by country">Nuclear power by country</a> with China leading at 23. Globally, more nuclear power reactors have closed than opened in recent years but overall capacity has increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ13_196-0"><a href="#cite_note-econ13-196">[196]</a></sup> China has stated its plans to double nuclear generation by 2030. India also plans to greatly increase its nuclear power.
</p><p>Several countries have enacted laws to cease construction on new nuclear power stations. Several European countries have debated <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_phase-out" title="Nuclear power phase-out">nuclear phase-outs</a> and others have completely shut down some reactors. Three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_accidents" title="Nuclear accidents">nuclear accidents</a> have influenced the slowdown of nuclear power: the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">Three Mile Island accident</a> in the United States, the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a> in the USSR, and the 2011 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima nuclear disaster">Fukushima nuclear disaster</a> in Japan. Following the March 2011 Fukushima nuclear disaster, Germany has permanently shut down eight of its 17 reactors and pledged to close the rest by the end of 2022.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annika_Breidthardt_197-0"><a href="#cite_note-Annika_Breidthardt-197">[197]</a></sup> Italy voted overwhelmingly to keep their country non-nuclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup> Switzerland and Spain have banned the construction of new reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nweek11_199-0"><a href="#cite_note-nweek11-199">[199]</a></sup> Japan’s prime minister has called for a dramatic reduction in Japan’s reliance on nuclear power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup> Taiwan’s president did the same. <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a>, the new prime minister of Japan since December 2012, announced a plan to restart some of the 54 Japanese nuclear power plants and to continue some nuclear reactors under construction.
</p><p>As of 2016, countries such as <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Australia" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Austria" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_in_Denmark" title="Nuclear energy in Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_in_Greece" title="Nuclear energy in Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_in_Malaysia" title="Nuclear energy in Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand%27s_nuclear-free_zone" title="New Zealand's nuclear-free zone">New Zealand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_in_Norway" title="Nuclear energy in Norway">Norway</a> have no nuclear power stations and remain opposed to nuclear power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_201-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-201">[201]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gl2011_202-0"><a href="#cite_note-gl2011-202">[202]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Germany" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Spain" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Switzerland" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Switzerland">Switzerland</a> are phasing-out their nuclear power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ13_196-1"><a href="#cite_note-econ13-196">[196]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gl2011_202-1"><a href="#cite_note-gl2011-202">[202]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_Kanter_204-0"><a href="#cite_note-James_Kanter-204">[204]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">Energy efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_phase-out&amp;action=edit&amp;section=58" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">Efficient energy use</a></div>
<p>Moving away from fossil fuels will require changes not only in the way energy is supplied, but in the way it is used, and reducing the amount of energy required to deliver various goods or services is essential. Opportunities for improvement on the demand side of the energy equation are as rich and diverse as those on the supply side, and often offer significant economic benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-205"><a href="#cite_note-205">[205]</a></sup>
</p><p>A sustainable energy economy requires commitments to both renewables and efficiency.  Renewable energy and <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> are said to be the "twin pillars" of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a> policy. <a href="/wiki/American_Council_for_an_Energy-Efficient_Economy" title="American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy">The American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy</a> has explained that both resources must be developed in order to stabilize and reduce carbon dioxide emissions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-am_206-0"><a href="#cite_note-am-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Efficiency is essential to slowing the energy demand growth so that rising clean energy supplies can make deep cuts in fossil fuel use. If energy use grows too fast, renewable energy development will chase a receding target. Likewise, unless clean energy supplies come online rapidly, slowing demand growth will only begin to reduce total emissions; reducing the carbon content of energy sources is also needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-am_206-1"><a href="#cite_note-am-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The IEA has stated that renewable energy and energy efficiency policies are complementary tools for the development of a sustainable energy future, and should be developed together instead of being developed in isolation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[207]</a></sup>
</p>
