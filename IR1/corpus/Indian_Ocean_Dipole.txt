<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Indian Ocean Dipole</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="219" data-file-width="350" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg/220px-Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg/330px-Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f6/Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sstanom_199711_krig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water temperatures around the Mentawai Islands dropped about 4° Celsius during the height of a positive phase of the Indian Ocean Dipole in November 1997. During these events unusually strong winds from the east push warm surface water towards Africa, allowing cold water to upwell along the Sumatran coast. In this image blue areas are colder than normal, while red areas are warmer than normal.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Indian Ocean Dipole</b> (<b>IOD</b>), also known as the Indian Niño, is an irregular oscillation of sea-surface temperatures in which the western <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> becomes alternately warmer and then colder than the eastern part of the ocean.
</p><p>Monsoon in India is generally affected by the temperature between bay of Bengal in the east and The Arabian sea in the west.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomenon">Phenomenon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Ocean_Dipole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phenomenon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IOD involves an aperiodic oscillation of sea-surface temperatures (SST), between "positive", "neutral" and "negative" phases. A positive phase sees greater-than-average sea-surface temperatures and greater precipitation in the western Indian Ocean region, with a corresponding cooling of waters in the eastern Indian Ocean—which tends to cause droughts in adjacent land areas of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. The negative phase of the IOD brings about the opposite conditions, with warmer water and greater precipitation in the eastern Indian Ocean, and cooler and drier conditions in the west.
</p><p>The IOD also affects the strength of monsoons over the Indian subcontinent. A significant positive IOD occurred in 1997–98, with another in 2006. The IOD is one aspect of the general cycle of global climate, interacting with similar phenomena like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.
</p><p>The IOD phenomenon was first identified by climate researchers in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>An average of four each positive-negative IOD events occur during each 30-year period with each event lasting around six months. However, there have been 12 positive IODs since 1980 and no negative events from 1992 until a strong negative event in late 2010. The occurrence of consecutive positive IOD events is extremely rare with only two such events recorded, 1913–1914 and the three consecutive events from 2006 to 2008 which preceded the <a href="/wiki/Black_Saturday_bushfires" title="Black Saturday bushfires">Black Saturday bushfires</a>. Modelling suggests that consecutive positive events could be expected to occur twice over a 1,000-year period. The positive IOD in 2007 evolved together with <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a>, which is a very rare phenomenon that has happened only once in the available historical records (in 1967).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Argo-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perry_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perry-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosebro_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosebro-6">[6]</a></sup> A strong negative IOD developed in October 2010,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which, coupled with a strong and concurrent La Niña, caused the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%932011_Queensland_floods" title="2010–2011 Queensland floods">2010–2011 Queensland floods</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Victorian_floods" title="2011 Victorian floods">2011 Victorian floods</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Nerilie_Abram" title="Nerilie Abram">Nerilie Abram</a> used coral records from the eastern and western Indian Ocean to construct a coral Dipole Mode Index extending back to 1846 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This extended perspective on IOD behaviour suggested that positive IOD events increased in strength and frequency during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_Australian_droughts">Effect on Australian droughts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Ocean_Dipole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effect on Australian droughts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2009 study by Ummenhofer <i>et al.</i> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales" title="University of New South Wales">University of New South Wales</a> (UNSW) <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Research_Centre" title="Climate Change Research Centre">Climate Change Research Centre</a> has demonstrated a significant correlation between the IOD and drought in the southern half of Australia, in particular the south-east. Every major southern drought since 1889 has coincided with positive-neutral IOD fluctuations including the <a href="/wiki/Federation_Drought" title="Federation Drought">1895–1902</a>, 1937–1945 and the <a href="/wiki/2000s_Australian_drought" title="2000s Australian drought">1995–2009</a> droughts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The research shows that when the IOD is in its negative phase, with cool Indian Ocean water west of Australia and warm <a href="/wiki/Timor_Sea" title="Timor Sea">Timor Sea</a> water to the north, winds are generated that pick up moisture from the ocean and then sweep down towards southern Australia to deliver higher rainfall. In the IOD-positive phase, the pattern of ocean temperatures is reversed, weakening the winds and reducing the amount of moisture picked up and transported across Australia.  The consequence is that rainfall in the south-east is well below average during periods of a positive IOD.
</p><p>The study also shows that the IOD has a much more significant effect on the rainfall patterns in south-east Australia than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) in the Pacific Ocean as already shown in several recent studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_El_Nino">Effect on El Nino</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Ocean_Dipole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effect on El Nino">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2018 study by Hameed et al. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aizu" title="University of Aizu">University of Aizu</a> simulated the impact of a positive IOD event on Pacific surface wind and SST variations. They show that IOD induced surface wind anomalies can produce El Nino like SST anomalies, with the IOD's impact on SST being the strongest in the far-eastern Pacific. They further demonstrated that IOD-ENSO interaction is a key for the generation of Super El Ninos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>.
</p>
