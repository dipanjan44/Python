<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Tuvalu</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></b> is dangerous in <b><a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a></b> since the average height of the islands is less than 2 metres (6.6 ft) above sea level, with the highest point of <a href="/wiki/Niulakita" title="Niulakita">Niulakita</a> being about 4.6 metres (15 ft) above sea level. Tuvalu islands have increased in size between 1971 and 2014, during a period of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Over 4 decades, there had been a net increase in land area in Tuvalu of 73.5 ha (2.9%), although the changes are not uniform, with 74% increasing and 27% decreasing in size. The sea level at the Funafuti tide gauge has risen at 3.9 mm per year, which is approximately twice the global average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSK2-2">[2]</a></sup> The rising sea levels are identified as creating an increased transfer of wave energy across reef surfaces, which shifts sand, resulting in accretion to island shorelines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSK-3">[3]</a></sup> although this process does not result in additional habitable land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ML_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ML-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tuvalu could be one of the first nations to experience the effects of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Not only could parts of the island be flooded but the rising saltwater table could also destroy deep rooted food crops such as <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulaka" title="Pulaka">pulaka</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taro" title="Taro">taro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-root_crop_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-root_crop-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_systems_that_affect_Tuvalu">Climate systems that affect Tuvalu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate systems that affect Tuvalu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/2011_Tuvalu_drought" title="2011 Tuvalu drought">2011 Tuvalu drought</a></div>
<p>Tuvalu participates in the operations of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Regional_Environment_Programme" title="Pacific Regional Environment Programme">Secretariat of the Pacific Regional Environment Programme</a> (SPREP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPREP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPREP-8">[8]</a></sup> The climate of the Pacific region at the equator is influenced by a number of factors; the science of which is the subject of continuing research. The SPREP described the climate of Tuvalu as being:
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">[I]nfluenced by a number of factors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade wind</a> regimes, the paired <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walker_circulation" title="Walker circulation">Walker circulation</a>, seasonally varying convergence zones such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Convergence_Zone" title="South Pacific Convergence Zone">South Pacific Convergence Zone</a> (SPCZ), semi-permanent <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical high-pressure belts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quasi-biennial_oscillation" title="Quasi-biennial oscillation">zonal westerlies</a> to the south, with the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) as the dominant mode of year to year variability (…). The <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a> (MJO) also is a major mode of variability of the tropical atmosphere-ocean system of the Pacific on times scales of 30 to 70 days (…), while the leading mode with decadal time-scale is the <a href="/wiki/Interdecadal_Pacific_Oscillation" title="Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation">Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation</a> (IPO) (…). A number of studies suggest the influence of global warming could be a major factor in accentuating the current climate regimes and the changes from normal that come with ENSO events (…).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1440" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg/300px-Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg/450px-Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg/600px-Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_November_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea surface temperature anomalies in November 2007 showing <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> conditions. Blue=temperature below average; red=temperature above average</div></div></div>
<p>The sea level in Tuvalu varies as a consequence of a wide range of atmospheric and oceanographic influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CFCT_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CFCT-10">[10]</a></sup> The 2011 report of the <i>Pacific Climate Change Science Program</i> published by the Australian Government,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> describes a strong zonal (east‑to-west) sea-level slope along the equator, with sea level west of the International Date Line (180° longitude) being about a half metre higher than found in the eastern equatorial Pacific and South American coastal regions. The trade winds that push surface water westward create this zonal tilting of sea level on the equator. Below the equator a higher sea level can also be found about 20° to 40° south (Tuvalu is spread out from 6° to 10° south).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP1ch2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCP1ch2-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Pacific Climate Change Science Program</i> Report (2011) describes the year-by-year volatility in the sea-level as resulting from the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO):
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">ENSO has a major influence on sea levels across the Pacific and this can influence the occurrence of extreme sea levels. During <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> events, strengthened trade winds cause higher than normal sea levels in the western tropical Pacific, and lower than normal levels in the east. Conversely, during <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events, weakened trade winds are unable to maintain the normal gradient of sea level across the tropical Pacific, leading to a drop in sea level in the west and a rise in the east. Pacific islands within about 10° of the equator are most strongly affected by ENSO‑related sea-level variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP1ch2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-CCP1ch2-12">[12]</a></sup>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pacific_decadal_oscillation" title="Pacific decadal oscillation">Pacific (inter-)decadal oscillation</a> is a climate switch phenomenon that results in changes from periods of La Niña to periods of El Niño. This has an effect on sea levels  as El Niño events can actually result in sea levels falling by 11 inches (28.4 centimeters) as compared to the sea level during a La Niña events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MF_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MF-13">[13]</a></sup>. For example, in 2000 there was a switch from periods of downward pressure of El Niño on sea levels to an upward pressure of La Niña on sea levels, which upward pressure causes more frequent and higher high tide levels. The <a href="/wiki/Perigean_spring_tide" title="Perigean spring tide">Perigean spring tide</a> (often called a <a href="/wiki/King_tide" title="King tide">king tide</a>) can result in seawater flooding low-lying areas of the islands of Tuvalu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Packard_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Packard-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_climate_change_effects_in_Tuvalu">Measuring climate change effects in Tuvalu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measuring climate change effects in Tuvalu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg/300px-Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg/450px-Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg/600px-Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_side_Funafuti.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ocean side of <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a> atoll showing the storm dunes, the highest point on the atoll.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1978 a tide gauge was installed at <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a> by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PSK-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the uncertainty as to the accuracy of the data from this tide gauge resulted in a modern Aquatrak acoustic gauge being installed in 1993 by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Australian National Tidal Facility</a> (NTF) as part of the AusAID-sponsored South Pacific Sea Level and Climate Monitoring Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JRH_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-JRH-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The highest elevation is 4.6 metres (15 ft) above sea level on <a href="/wiki/Niulakita" title="Niulakita">Niulakita</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which gives Tuvalu the second-lowest maximum elevation of any country (after the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>). However, the highest elevations are typically in narrow storm dunes on the ocean side of the islands which are prone to over topping in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a>, such as occurred with <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Bebe" title="Cyclone Bebe">Cyclone Bebe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM1975_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM1975-17">[17]</a></sup> In March 2015 the storm surge created by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Pam" title="Cyclone Pam">Cyclone Pam</a> resulted in waves of 3 to 5 metres (9.8 to 16.4 ft) breaking over the reef of the outer islands caused damage to houses, crops and infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TuvaluReport_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-TuvaluReport-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNZI19_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI19-19">[19]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Nui_(atoll)" title="Nui (atoll)">Nui</a> the sources of fresh water were destroyed or contaminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBS15_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBS15-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNZI14_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI14-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tuvalu is also affected by <a href="/wiki/Perigean_spring_tide" title="Perigean spring tide">perigean spring tide</a> events (often called a <a href="/wiki/King_tide" title="King tide">king tide</a>), which raise the sea level higher than a normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_tide" title="High tide">high tide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The highest peak tide recorded by the <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu_Meteorological_Service" title="Tuvalu Meteorological Service">Tuvalu Meteorological Service</a> was 3.4 metres (11 ft) on 24 February 2006 and again on 19 February 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As a result of historical sea level rise, the <a href="/wiki/King_tide" title="King tide">king tide</a> events lead to flooding of low-lying areas, which is compounded when sea levels are further raised by <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> effects or local storms and waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Packard_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Packard-14">[14]</a></sup> In the future, sea level rise may threaten to submerge the nation entirely as it is estimated that a sea level rise of 20–40 centimetres (7.9–15.7 inches) in the next 100 years could make Tuvalu uninhabitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patel_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Patel-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staff.acecrc.org.au_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-staff.acecrc.org.au-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The atolls have shown resilience to gradual sea-level rise, with atolls and reef islands being able to grow under current climate conditions by generating sufficient sand and broken coral that accumulates and gets dumped on the islands during cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK2014_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSK2014-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AWPK_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AWPK-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG15_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NG15-29">[29]</a></sup> There remains the risk that the dynamic response of atolls and reef islands does not result in stable islands as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> can strip the low-lying islands of their vegetation and soil. <a href="/wiki/Pukasavilivili" title="Pukasavilivili">Tepuka Vili Vili</a> <a href="/wiki/Islet" title="Islet">islet</a> of <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a> atoll was devastated by <a href="/wiki/1975%E2%80%9380_Southern_Hemisphere_tropical_cyclone_seasons#Cyclone_Meli" title="1975–80 Southern Hemisphere tropical cyclone seasons">Cyclone Meli</a> in 1979, with all its vegetation and most of its sand swept away during the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KFCA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-KFCA-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vasafua" title="Vasafua">Vasafua</a> islet, part of the <a href="/wiki/Funafuti_Conservation_Area" title="Funafuti Conservation Area">Funafuti Conservation Area</a>, was severely damaged by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Pam" title="Cyclone Pam">Cyclone Pam</a> in 2015. The coconut palms were washed away, leaving the islet as a sand bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VIV_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-VIV-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TO13_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-TO13-32">[32]</a></sup> The effect of Cyclone Pam, which did not pass directly over the islands, shows that Tuvaluans are exposed to storm surges causing damage to their houses and crops, and also the risk of water born disease as a consequence of contamination of the water supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TuvaluReport_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-TuvaluReport-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW22_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-RW22-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW30_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-RW30-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gradual sea-level rise also allows for coral <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyp</a> activity to raise the atolls with the sea level. However, if the increase in sea level occurs at faster rate as compared to coral growth, or if <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyp</a> activity is damaged by <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, then the resilience of the atolls and reef islands is less certain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PK14_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-PK14-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is further contention as to whether saltwater encroachment that is destroying the gardens for <a href="/wiki/Pulaka" title="Pulaka">pulaka</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taro" title="Taro">taro</a> and coconut palms is the consequence of changes in the sea level;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> or the consequence of the fresh water being extracted from the <a href="/wiki/Lens_(hydrology)" title="Lens (hydrology)">freshwater lens</a> in the sub-surface of the atoll or the consequence of the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Borrow_pit" title="Borrow pit">borrow pits</a>, which are the result of the extraction of coral to build the <a href="/wiki/Funafuti_International_Airport" title="Funafuti International Airport">runway at Funafuti during World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MF_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-MF-13">[13]</a></sup> The investigation of groundwater dynamics of <a href="/wiki/Fongafale" title="Fongafale">Fongafale</a> Islet, <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a>, show that <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal forcing</a> results in salt water contamination of the <a href="/wiki/Surficial_aquifer" title="Surficial aquifer">surficial aquifer</a> during <a href="/wiki/Perigean_spring_tide" title="Perigean spring tide">spring tides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAS_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-EAS-37">[37]</a></sup> The degree of aquifer salinization depends on the specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographic" title="Topographic">topographic</a> characteristics and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrologic" title="Hydrologic">hydrologic</a> controls in the sub-surface of the atoll. About half of Fongafale islet is reclaimed swamp that contains porous, highly permeable coral blocks that allow the tidal forcing of salt water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GWD_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-GWD-38">[38]</a></sup> Increases in the sea level will exacerbate the aquifer salinization as the result of increases in <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal forcing</a>.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Tuvalu" title="Geography of Tuvalu">Geography of Tuvalu</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimates_as_to_changes_in_the_sea_level_relative_to_the_islands_of_Tuvalu">Estimates as to changes in the sea level relative to the islands of Tuvalu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Estimates as to changes in the sea level relative to the islands of Tuvalu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg/150px-Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg/225px-Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg/300px-Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tuvalu_Meteorology_Service.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tuvalu_Meteorological_Service" title="Tuvalu Meteorological Service">Tuvalu Meteorological Service</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fongafale" title="Fongafale">Fongafale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a> atoll</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Service_for_Mean_Sea_Level" title="Permanent Service for Mean Sea Level">Permanent Service for Mean Sea Level</a> (PSMSL) analysis of data from Funafuti is that the sea level has risen at 3.9 mm per year, which is approximately twice the global average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-PSK2-2">[2]</a></sup> Sea level observation has been made at two at locations within the Funafuti lagoon. The University of Hawai’i Sea Level Center (UHSLC) operated a tide gauge from November 1979 until December 2001. Since June 1993 the National Tidal Centre of the Australian Bureau of Meteorology has operated a Aquatrak acoustic gauge. The two records were synthesised into a single data source by averaging the difference between the two records over the period during which both gauges operated simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSK2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-PSK2-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are observable changes that have occurred over the last ten to fifteen years that show Tuvaluans that there have been changes to sea levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Those observable changes include sea water bubbling up through the porous coral rock to form pools on each high tide and flooding of low-lying areas including the airport on a regular basis during <a href="/wiki/Perigean_spring_tide" title="Perigean spring tide">spring tides</a> and <a href="/wiki/King_tide" title="King tide">king tides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHK1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHK1-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_report_of_the_Pacific_Climate_Change_Science_Program">2011 report of the Pacific Climate Change Science Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2011 report of the Pacific Climate Change Science Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2011 report of the "Pacific Climate Change Science Program"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup> of Australian concludes in relation to Tuvalu that over the course of the 21st century:
</p><p>• Surface air temperature and sea‑surface temperature are projected to continue to increase (very high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• Annual and seasonal mean rainfall is projected to increase (high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• The intensity and frequency of days of extreme heat are projected to increase (very high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• The intensity and frequency of days of extreme rainfall are projected to increase (high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-4"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• The incidence of drought is projected to decrease (moderate confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-5"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• Tropical cyclone numbers are projected to decline in the south-east Pacific Ocean basin (0–40ºS, 170ºE–130ºW) (moderate confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-6"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• Ocean acidification is projected to continue (very high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-7"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>• Mean sea-level rise is projected to continue (very high confidence).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCP2ch15_43-8"><a href="#cite_note-CCP2ch15-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_response_to_the_challenges_faced_by_Tuvalu">National response to the challenges faced by Tuvalu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National response to the challenges faced by Tuvalu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuvalu faces challenges which will be exacerbated by climate change, those challenges are:
</p><p>i) Coastal erosion, saltwater intrusion and increasing vector and water borne diseases due to sea level rise;
</p><p>ii) Inadequate potable water due to less rainfall and prolonged droughts;
</p><p>iii) <a href="/wiki/Pulaka" title="Pulaka">Pulaka</a> pit salinisation due to saltwater intrusion; and
</p><p>iv) Decreasing fisheries population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_National_Advisory_Council_on_Climate_Change">The National Advisory Council on Climate Change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The National Advisory Council on Climate Change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a speech on 16 September 2005 to the 60th Session of the UN General Assembly, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Maatia_Toafa" title="Maatia Toafa">Maatia Toafa</a> emphasized the impact of climate change as a "broader security issue which relates to environmental security. Living in a very fragile island environment, our long-term security and sustainable development is closely linked to issues of climate change, preserving biodiversity, managing our limited forests and water
resources."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIR_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-PIR-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>The threat of climate change to the islands is not a dominant motivation for migration as Tuvaluans appear to prefer to continue living in Tuvalu for reasons of lifestyle, culture and identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LC12_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-LC12-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMJB_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMJB-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2013 <a href="/wiki/Enele_Sopoaga" title="Enele Sopoaga">Enele Sopoaga</a>, the prime minister of Tuvalu, said that relocating Tuvaluans to avoid the impact of sea level rise "should never be an option because it is self defeating in itself. For Tuvalu I think we really need to mobilise public opinion in the Pacific as well as in the [rest of] world to really talk to their lawmakers to please have some sort of moral obligation and things like that to do the right thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNZI030913_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI030913-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>‘The Economics of Climate Change in the Pacific’ 2013 report of the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank" title="Asian Development Bank">Asian Development Bank</a> estimates the range of potential economic impacts of climate change for agriculture, fisheries, tourism, coral reefs, and human health in the Pacific region; with agriculture production, such as taro, particularly vulnerable to the effect of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABAdec13_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABAdec13-50">[50]</a></sup> The Pacific countries are projected incur economic losses in the range of 4.6% to 12.7% of the region’s annual GDP equivalent by 2100, with the degree of severity changing with different CO2 emission scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBdec13_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBdec13-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tuvalu has said it wants all its energy to come from renewable sources by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> On 16 January 2014 Prime Minister Enele Sopoaga established the National Advisory Council on Climate Change, which functions are "to identify actions or strategies: to achieve energy efficiencies; to increase the use of renewable energy; to encourage the private sector and NGOs to reduce greenhouse gas emissions; to ensure a whole of government response to adaptation and climate change related disaster risk reduction; and to encourage the private sector and NGOs to develop locally appropriate technologies for adaptation and climate change mitigation (reductions in [greenhouse gas])."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SL30114_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-SL30114-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the 20th Conference of Parties to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="UN Framework Convention on Climate Change">UN Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> in December 2014 at Lima, Peru, Sopoaga said "Climate change is the single greatest challenge facing my country. It is threatening the livelihood, security and wellbeing of all Tuvaluans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peru_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peru-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Te Kakeega III - National Strategy for Sustainable Development-2016-2020</i> (TK III) sets out the development agenda of the Government of Tuvalu. TK III includes new strategic areas, in addition to the eight identified in TK II. The additional strategic areas are climate change; environment; migration and urbanization; and oceans and seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TK_III_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-TK_III-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Tuvalu.27s_role_at_the_Copenhagen_Climate_Change_Conference_2009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuvalu's_role_at_the_Copenhagen_Climate_Change_Conference_2009">Tuvalu's role at the Copenhagen Climate Change Conference 2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tuvalu's role at the Copenhagen Climate Change Conference 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu_and_the_United_Nations" title="Tuvalu and the United Nations">Tuvalu and the United Nations</a></div>
<p>In December 2009 the islands stalled talks at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference_2009" title="United Nations Climate Change Conference 2009">United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, fearing some other developing countries were not committing fully to binding deals on a reduction in carbon emission, their chief negotiator stated "Tuvalu is one of the most vulnerable countries in the world to climate change, and our future rests on the outcome of this meeting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> When the conference failed to reach a binding, meaningful agreement, Tuvalu's representative Ian Fry said, "It looks like we are being offered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30_pieces_of_silver" title="30 pieces of silver">30 pieces of silver</a> to betray our people and our future... Our future is not for sale. I regret to inform you that Tuvalu cannot accept this document."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fry's speech to the conference was a highly impassioned plea for countries around the world to address the issues of man-made global warming resulting in climate change. The five-minute speech addressed the dangers of rising sea levels to Tuvalu and the world. In his speech Fry claimed man-made global warming to be currently "the greatest threat to humanity", and ended with an emotional "the fate of my country rests in your hands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_leadership_and_the_Majuro_Declaration_2013">Climate change leadership and the Majuro Declaration 2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate change leadership and the Majuro Declaration 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2011, Tuvalu was one of the eight founding members of <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_Leaders_Group" title="Polynesian Leaders Group">Polynesian Leaders Group</a>, a regional grouping intended to cooperate on a variety of issues including culture and language, education, responses to climate change, and trade and investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Tuvalu participates in the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Small_Island_States" title="Alliance of Small Island States">Alliance of Small Island States</a> (AOSIS), which is a coalition of small island and low-lying coastal countries that have concerns about their vulnerability to the adverse effects of global climate change. The <a href="/wiki/Sopoaga_Ministry" title="Sopoaga Ministry">Sopoaga Ministry</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Enele_Sopoaga" title="Enele Sopoaga">Enele Sopoaga</a> made a commitment under the <a href="/wiki/Majuro_Declaration" title="Majuro Declaration">Majuro Declaration</a>, which was signed on 5 September 2013, to implement power generation of 100% renewable energy (between 2013 and 2020). This commitment is proposed to be implemented using Solar PV (95% of demand) and biodiesel (5% of demand). The feasibility of wind power generation will be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MD050913_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-MD050913-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Marshall Islands President <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Loeak" title="Christopher Loeak">Christopher Loeak</a> presented the Majuro Declaration to the UN <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary-General" title="Secretary-General">Secretary-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> during General Assembly Leaders’ week from 23 September 2013. The Majuro Declaration is offered as a "Pacific gift" to the UN Secretary-General in order to catalyze more ambitious climate action by world leaders beyond that achieved at the December <a href="/wiki/2009_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2009 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2009 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP15" title="COP15">COP15</a>). On 29 September 2013 the Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Vete_Sakaio" title="Vete Sakaio">Vete Sakaio</a> concluded his speech to the General Debate of the 68th Session of the United Nations General Assembly with an appeal to the world, "please save Tuvalu against climate change. Save Tuvalu in order to save yourself, the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VPSUN_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-VPSUN-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference_.28COP21.29_and_the_Paris_Agreement"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference_(COP21)_and_the_Paris_Agreement">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP21) and the Paris Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP21) and the Paris Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span id="Tuvalu.27s_COP21_goal_of_global_temperature_below_1.5_degrees_Celsius_relative_to_pre-industrial_levels"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuvalu's_COP21_goal_of_global_temperature_below_1.5_degrees_Celsius_relative_to_pre-industrial_levels">Tuvalu's COP21 goal of global temperature below 1.5 degrees Celsius relative to pre-industrial levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tuvalu's COP21 goal of global temperature below 1.5 degrees Celsius relative to pre-industrial levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Enele_Sopoaga" title="Enele Sopoaga">Enele Sopoaga</a> said at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> (COP21) that the goal for COP21 should a global temperature goal of below 1.5 degrees Celsius relative to pre-industrial levels, which is the position of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Small_Island_States" title="Alliance of Small Island States">Alliance of Small Island States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASI_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASI-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SP_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-SP-64">[64]</a></sup> Ms. Pepetua Latasi, the director of the Department of Environment, was the Chief Negotiator for Tuvalu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLCOP21_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-PLCOP21-65">[65]</a></sup>
Prime Minister Sopoaga said in his speech to the meeting of heads of state and government:
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Tuvalu’s future at current warming, is already bleak, any further temperature increase will spell the total demise of Tuvalu…. For Small Island Developing States, Least Developed Countries and many others, setting a global temperature goal of below 1.5 degrees Celsius relative to pre-industrial levels is critical. I call on the people of Europe to think carefully about their obsession with 2 degrees. Surely, we must aim for the best future we can deliver and not a weak compromise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COP21ESS_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-COP21ESS-66">[66]</a></sup>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”

<p>The countries participating in the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a> agreed to reduce their carbon output "as soon as possible" and to do their best to keep global warming "to well below 2 degrees C".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCCC_CP_2015_L9_Rev1_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FCCC_CP_2015_L9_Rev1-67">[67]</a></sup> Enele Sopoaga described the important outcomes of the Paris Agreement as including the stand-alone provision for assistance to small island states and some of the least developed countries for loss and damage resulting from climate change and the ambition of limiting temperature rise to 1.5 degrees by the end of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNZI1515_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI1515-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Tuvalu_Survival_Fund_.28TSF.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuvalu_Survival_Fund_(TSF)">Tuvalu Survival Fund (TSF)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tuvalu Survival Fund (TSF)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Tuvaluan government established the Tuvalu Survival Fund (TSF) in 2016 to finance climate change programs and as a fund available to respond promptly to natural disasters, such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMF2018_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMF2018-69">[69]</a></sup> Contributions are made to the TSF from the national budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMF2018_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMF2018-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOOM1_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-TOOM1-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_National_Adaptation_Programme_of_Action_and_the_Tuvalu_Coastal_Adaptation_Project">The National Adaptation Programme of Action and the Tuvalu Coastal Adaptation Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The National Adaptation Programme of Action and the Tuvalu Coastal Adaptation Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuvalu’s <a href="/wiki/National_Adaptation_Programme_of_Action" title="National Adaptation Programme of Action">National Adaptation Programme of Action</a> (NAPA) describes a response to the climate change problem as using the combined efforts of several local bodies on each island that will work with the local community leaders (the <a href="/wiki/Falekaupule" title="Falekaupule">Falekaupule</a>). The main office, named the Department of Environment, is responsible for coordinating the non-governmental organizations, religious bodies, and stakeholders. Each of the named groups are responsible for implementing Tuvalu’s NAPA, the main plan to adapt to the adverse effects of human use and climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAP_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAP-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Program" title="United Nations Development Program">United Nations Development Program</a> (UNDP) assisted the government of Tuvalu to acquire <i>MV Talamoana</i>, a 30-metre vessel that will be used to implement Tuvalu's <a href="/wiki/National_Adaptation_Programme_of_Action" title="National Adaptation Programme of Action">National Adaptation Programme of Action</a>  to transport government officials and project personnel to the outer islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDP15_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNDP15-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2017 the Government of Tuvalu along and the UNDP launched the Tuvalu Coastal Adaptation Project (TCAP) that is financed with US$36 million from the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvaluan_dollar" title="Tuvaluan dollar">T</a>$2.9 million from the Government of Tuvalu. The TCAP focuses on construction works to defend infrastructure including roads, schools, hospitals and government buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDP17_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNDP17-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCFtcap_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCFtcap-74">[74]</a></sup> over a period of seven years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCF15_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCF15-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMF2018_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMF2018-69">[69]</a></sup> The goal of the Tuvalu Coastal Adaptation Project is to build coastal resilience in three of Tuvalu’s nine inhabited islands and to manage coastal inundation risks by reducing the impact of increasingly intensive wave action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMF2018_69-3"><a href="#cite_note-IMF2018-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
