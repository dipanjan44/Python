<p><b>Population Matters</b>, formerly known as the <b>Optimum Population Trust</b>, is a UK-based charity that addresses population size and its effects on <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">environmental sustainability</a>. It considers population growth as a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>, conflict and involuntary migration and societal problems such as housing scarcity and transport congestion.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_background">History and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population Matters was launched as the Optimum Population Trust following a meeting on 24 July 1991 by the late David Willey and others concerned about population numbers and sustainability. They were impelled to act by the failure of United Kingdom governments to respond to a series of recommendations regarding <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> and sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Optimum Population Trust prepared analyses and lobbied on issues affected by population growth. It also lobbied developmental and environmental campaigners on the need to incorporate population issues in their thinking.  It was granted charitable status on 9 May 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Population Matters was adopted as its campaign name in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_and_aims">Views and aims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Views and aims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population Matters aims to achieve a future with decent living standards for all, a healthy and biodiverse environment and a sustainable population size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The charity holds the following policy positions:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Population growth increases damage to the environment and depletes natural resources. Therefore, human numbers should be reduced voluntarily to a sustainable level that enables an acceptable quality of life for all. 
</p>
<ul><li>Given that human activity already exceeds Earth’s capacity to support it, Population Matters argues that population stabilisation should be strived for without delay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The United Nations projects that global population size could grow by 2.5 billion between 2015 and 2050, which illustrates the urgency of the matter further according to the organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_climate_change">Development and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Population growth increases the number of wealthy <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">carbon emitters</a> and poorer <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> victims and hampers mitigation and adaptation efforts. In 2016, humanity used the sustainable resource output of 1.6 Earths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Evidence has been presented that less equal affluent countries consume more resources and generate more waste than other affluent countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>Consequently, Population Matters supports greater income equality.</li>
<li>Developed countries are responsible for the majority of <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a> as well as the associated global environmental degradation. Therefore, the developed world has a responsibility to support developing nations according to the organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Population Matters supports the concept of <a href="/wiki/Contraction_and_Convergence" title="Contraction and Convergence">Contraction and Convergence</a> as conceived by the Global Commons Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Women.E2.80.99s_rights_and_reproductive_health"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women’s_rights_and_reproductive_health">Women’s rights and reproductive health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Women’s rights and reproductive health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Women%27s_empowerment" title="Women's empowerment">Women’s empowerment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> are essential for reproductive health, economic development and population stabilisation. Population Matters therefore supports programmes to improve the status of women.
</p>
<ul><li>Population Matters embraces the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> that see women’s empowerment as a necessary condition for sustainable development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Comparisons made between developing nations that experienced rapid fertility decline and those that did not found that high fertility increases absolute levels of poverty by slowing economic growth and worsening the distribution of additionally acquired resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Consequently, the organisation promotes policies improving access to contraceptives.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Migration often results from conflict, poverty, inequality or population and consumption pressures. Population Matters calls for fair trade terms and increased foreign aid and knowledge transfer to promote sustainable development, global justice and resilience.
</p>
<ul><li>Population Matters believes that the only just and long-term solution to migration pressure is to address its underlying causes in the countries of origin, such as poverty, lack or over exploitation of resources, climate change and conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The organisation believes that developed countries have a moral responsibility to help with this because they contribute to migratory pressures by being both major consumers of resources from developing countries and are the principal source of the causes of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ageing_and_parenthood">Ageing and parenthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ageing and parenthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Population Matters rejects the case that more young people are required <a class="external text" href="https://www.populationmatters.org/empower-to-plan/" rel="nofollow">to</a> care for an increasing number of elderly. It believes that governments should promote responsible parenthood and limit subsidies to the first two children unless a family is living in poverty.</li>
<li>Population Matters promotes the idea that society should deal with <a href="/wiki/Ageing_of_Europe" title="Ageing of Europe">ageing</a> by enabling employment for untrained, underemployed and older people and by optimising the use of technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population Matters campaigns to stabilise population at a sustainable level through encouraging a culture shift towards smaller family sizes worldwide and improving resources for women's empowerment and family planning in lower income countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Over the years, the organization has supported various campaigns, including <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a>’ Bill to make Personal, Social, Health and Economic education           (<i><a href="/wiki/Personal,_Social_and_Health_Education" title="Personal, Social and Health Education">PSHE</a></i>) a statutory requirement in state-funded schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It also produces material to help its supporters raise awareness of population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The charity also runs Empower to Plan, a crowdfunding project that offers members of the public the opportunity to donate directly towards family planning and women's empowerment projects around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other activities include the Population Matters Overshoot Index, which presents assessments of the extent to which countries and regions of the world are considered to be able to support themselves on the basis of their own renewable resources. It also produces short films, such as “Zombie Overpopulation”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisational_structure">Organisational structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organisational structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population Matters consists of patrons, an advisory council, a board, a team of staff/contractors and volunteers and members. It relies on members and donors for its funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patrons">Patrons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patrons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Population Matters' patrons include prominent and successful public figures such as the broadcaster <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">Sir David Attenborough</a>, the economist <a href="/wiki/Partha_Dasgupta" title="Partha Dasgupta">Sir Partha Dasgupta</a>, the biologist <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Professor Paul Ehrlich</a>, the primatologist <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall" title="Jane Goodall">Dr Jane Goodall</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Guillebaud" title="John Guillebaud">Professor John Guillebaud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Matters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, Population Matters was criticised for advocating that Syrian refugees should not be accommodated in the UK,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup>  calling for “zero-net migration” to the UK and for supporting a UK government policy of stopping child benefit and tax credits for third and subsequent children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2017, the organisation stopped advocating for these policies, replacing them with a call for a Sustainable Population Policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  Family planning strategies in Africa have also been <a class="external text" href="https://www.christianpost.com/news/the-west-is-colonizing-africa-abortion-population-control-obianuju-ekeocha-says-226029/" rel="nofollow">criticised</a>.       
</p>
