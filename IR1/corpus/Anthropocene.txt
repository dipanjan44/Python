<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anthropocene</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the documentary film, see <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene:_The_Human_Epoch" title="Anthropocene: The Human Epoch">Anthropocene: The Human Epoch</a>.</div>
<p>
The <b>Anthropocene</b> is a proposed <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a> dating from the commencement of significant human impact on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, including, but not limited to, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20141014-SB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20141014-SB-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  August 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, neither the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">International Commission on Stratigraphy</a> nor the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geological_Sciences" title="International Union of Geological Sciences">International Union of Geological Sciences</a> has yet officially approved the term as a recognized subdivision of <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geological time</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gixmodo001_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gixmodo001-7">[7]</a></sup> although the Anthropocene Working Group (AWG) of the Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy (SQS) of the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS)</a>, voted to proceed towards a formal <a href="/wiki/Global_Boundary_Stratotype_Section_and_Point" title="Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point">golden spike (GSSP)</a> proposal to define the Anthropocene epoch in the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">Geologic Time Scale</a> and presented the recommendation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Geological_Congress" title="International Geological Congress">International Geological Congress</a> on 29 August 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Various different start dates for the Anthropocene have been proposed, ranging from the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Agricultural Revolution</a> 12,000–15,000 years ago, to as recent as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinity_test" title="Trinity test">Trinity test</a> in 1945. As of  February 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the ratification process continues and thus a date remains to be decided definitively, but the latter date has been more favored than others.
</p><p>The most recent period of the Anthropocene has been referred to by several authors as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Acceleration" title="Great Acceleration">Great Acceleration</a> during which the socioeconomic and earth system trends are increasing dramatically, especially after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. For instance, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_Society" title="Geological Society">Geological Society</a> cites the year 1945 termed <i>The Great Acceleration</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An early concept for the Anthropocene was the <a href="/wiki/Noosphere" title="Noosphere">Noosphere</a> by <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Vernadsky" title="Vladimir Vernadsky">Vladimir Vernadsky</a>, who in 1938 wrote of "scientific thought as a geological force."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Scientists in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> appear to have used the term "anthropocene" as early as the 1960s to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>, the most recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">geological period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (June 2018)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ecologist <a href="/wiki/Eugene_F._Stoermer" title="Eugene F. Stoermer">Eugene F. Stoermer</a> subsequently used "anthropocene" with a different sense in the 1980s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revkin2011_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-revkin2011-12">[12]</a></sup> and the term was widely popularized in 2000 by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_J._Crutzen" title="Paul J. Crutzen">Paul J. Crutzen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> who regards the influence of human behavior on Earth's atmosphere in recent centuries as so significant as to constitute a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_epoch" title="Geological epoch">geological epoch</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, the Stratigraphy Commission of the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_London" title="Geological Society of London">Geological Society of London</a> considered a proposal to  make the Anthropocene a formal unit of geological epoch divisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz08_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz08-14">[14]</a></sup> A majority of the commission decided the proposal had merit and should be examined further. Independent working groups of scientists from various geological societies have begun to determine whether the Anthropocene will be formally accepted into the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">Geological Time Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz10_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz10-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "anthropocene" is informally used in scientific contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_America" title="Geological Society of America">Geological Society of America</a> entitled its 2011 annual meeting: <i><a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> to Anthropocene: The past is the key to the future</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The new epoch has no agreed start-date, but one proposal, based on atmospheric evidence, is to fix the start with the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> <i>ca.</i> 1780, with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz08_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz08-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrutzenStoermer2000Anthropocene_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CrutzenStoermer2000Anthropocene-18">[18]</a></sup> Other scientists link the new term to earlier events, such as the rise of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> (around 12,000 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>). Evidence of relative human impact – such as the <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation#Effects_of_human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">growing human influence</a> on land use, ecosystems, <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">species extinction</a> – is substantial; scientists think that human impact has significantly changed (or halted) the growth of biodiversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-19">[19]</a></sup> Those arguing for earlier dates posit that the proposed Anthropocene may have begun as early as 14,000 to 15,000 years <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_present" title="Before present">before present</a>, based on geologic evidence; this has led other scientists to suggest that "the onset of the Anthropocene should be extended back many thousand years";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doughty10_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doughty10-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup> this would be closely synchronous with the current term, <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1483" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg/220px-Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg/330px-Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg/440px-Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trinity_Test_Fireball_16ms.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Trinity_(nuclear_test)" title="Trinity (nuclear test)">Trinity test</a> in 1945 has been proposed as the start of the Anthropocene.</div></div></div>
<p>In January 2015, 26 of the 38 members of the International Anthropocene Working Group published a paper suggesting the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_(nuclear_test)" title="Trinity (nuclear test)">Trinity test</a> on 16 July 1945 as the starting point of the proposed new epoch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trinity_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-trinity-21">[21]</a></sup> However, a significant minority supports one of several alternative dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trinity_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-trinity-21">[21]</a></sup> A March 2015 report suggested either 1610 or 1964 as the beginning of Anthropocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_&amp;_Maslin_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis_&amp;_Maslin-22">[22]</a></sup> Other scholars point to the <a href="/wiki/Diachronous" title="Diachronous">diachronous</a> character of the physical strata of the Anthropocene, arguing that onset and impact are spread out over time, not reducible to a single instant or date of start.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>A January 2016 report on the climatic, biological, and geochemical signatures of human activity in sediments and ice cores suggested the era since the mid-20th century should be recognised as a distinct geological epoch from the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Anthropocene Working Group met in Oslo in April 2016 to consolidate evidence supporting the argument for the Anthropocene as a true geologic epoch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Evidence was evaluated and the group voted to recommend "Anthropocene" as the new geological age in August 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-8">[8]</a></sup> Should the International Commission on Stratigraphy approve the recommendation, the proposal to adopt the term will have to be ratified by the International Union of Geological Sciences before its formal adoption as part of the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geologic time scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gixmodo001_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gixmodo001-7">[7]</a></sup>

<p>The name <i>Anthropocene</i> is a combination of <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthropo-#English" title="wikt:anthropo-">anthropo-</a></i> from <i>anthropos</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">ἄνθρωπος</span>) meaning "human" and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-cene#English" title="wikt:-cene">-cene</a></i> from <i>kainos</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">καινός</span>) meaning "new" or "recent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>As early as 1873, the Italian geologist <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Stoppani" title="Antonio Stoppani">Antonio Stoppani</a> acknowledged the increasing power and effect of humanity on the Earth's systems and referred to an 'anthropozoic era'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crutzen02_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crutzen02-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the biologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Stoermer" title="Eugene Stoermer">Eugene Stoermer</a> is often credited with coining the term "anthropocene", it was in informal use in the mid-1970s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Crutzen" title="Paul Crutzen">Paul Crutzen</a> is credited with independently re-inventing and popularizing it. Stoermer wrote, "I began using the term 'anthropocene' in the 1980's, but never formalized it until Paul contacted me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Crutzen has explained, "I was at a conference where someone said something about the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>. I suddenly thought this was wrong. The world has changed too much. So I said: 'No, we are in the Anthropocene.' I just made up the word on the spur of the moment. Everyone was shocked. But it seems to have stuck."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup> In 2008, Zalasiewicz suggested in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GSA_Today" title="GSA Today">GSA Today</a></i> that an anthropocene <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a> is now appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz08_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz08-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_human_effects">Nature of human effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nature of human effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Human impact on the environment</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homogenocene">Homogenocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Homogenocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Homogenocene</b> (from old Greek: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Homo-#English" title="wikt:Homo-">homo-</a>, <i>same</i> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/geno-#English" title="wikt:geno-">geno-</a>, <i>kind</i>, <b>kainos-</b>, <i>new</i> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-cene#English" title="wikt:-cene">-cene</a>, <i>period</i>) is a more specific term used to define our current <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geological</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a>, in which <a href="#Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> is diminishing and <a href="#Biogeography">biogeography</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> around the globe seem more and more similar to one another mainly due to <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> that have been introduced around the globe either on purpose (crops, livestock) or inadvertently.
</p><p>The term Homogenocene was first used by Michael Samways in his editorial article in the <i>Journal of Insect Conservation</i> from 1999 titled "Translocating fauna to foreign lands: here comes the Homogenocene."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term was used again by John L. Curnutt in the year 2000 in <i>Ecology</i>, in a short list titled "A Guide to the Homogenocene",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> which reviewed <i>Alien species in North America and Hawaii: impacts on natural ecosystems</i> by George Cox. <a href="/wiki/Charles_C._Mann" title="Charles C. Mann">Charles C. Mann</a>, in his acclaimed book <i><a href="/wiki/1493:_Uncovering_the_New_World_Columbus_Created" title="1493: Uncovering the New World Columbus Created">1493: Uncovering the New World Columbus Created</a></i>, gives a bird's eye view of the mechanisms and ongoing implications of the homogenocene.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">Biodiversity loss</a></div>
<p>The human impact on biodiversity forms one of the primary attributes of the Anthropocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Humankind has entered what is sometimes called the Earth's sixth major extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Most experts agree that human activities have accelerated the rate of species extinction. The exact rate remains controversial – perhaps 100 to 1000 times the normal background rate of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A 2010 study found that "marine phytoplankton – the vast range of tiny algae species accounting for roughly half of Earth's total photosynthetic biomass – had declined substantially in the world's oceans over the past century. From 1950 alone, algal biomass decreased by around 40%, probably in response to ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Warming" title="Global Warming">warming</a> – and that the decline had gathered pace in recent years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (November 2015)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Some authors have postulated that without human impacts the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> of the planet would continue to grow at an exponential rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increases in global rates of extinction have been elevated above background rates since at least 1500, and appear to have accelerated in the 19th century and further since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup> A <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> op-ed on 13 July 2012 by ecologist Roger Bradbury predicted the end of biodiversity for the oceans, labelling coral reefs doomed: "Coral reefs will be the first, but certainly not the last, major ecosystem to succumb to the Anthropocene."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This op-ed quickly generated much discussion among conservationists; <a href="/wiki/The_Nature_Conservancy" title="The Nature Conservancy">The Nature Conservancy</a> rebutted Bradbury on its website, defending its position of protecting coral reefs despite continued human impacts causing reef declines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a pair of studies published in 2015, extrapolation from observed extinction of Hawaiian snails led to the conclusion that "the biodiversity crisis is real", and that 7% of all species on Earth may have disappeared already.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Human predation was noted as being unique in the history of life on Earth as being a globally distributed 'superpredator', with predation of the adults of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apex_predators" title="Apex predators">apex predators</a> and with widespread impacts on food webs worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A study published in May 2017 in <i><a href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a></i> posited that a "biological annihilation" akin to a sixth mass extinction event is underway as a result of anthropogenic causes, such as <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human overpopulation</a>, continued population growth and <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, particularly by the wealthy. The study suggested that as much as 50% of the number of animal individuals that once lived on Earth were already extinct, threatening the basis for human existence too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> A different study published in <i>PNAS</i> in May 2018 says that since the dawn of human civilization, 83% of wild mammals have disappeared. Today livestock make up 60% of the <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> of all mammals on earth, followed by humans (36%) and wild mammals (4%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeography">Biogeography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biogeography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">Biogeography</a></div>
<p>Permanent changes in the distribution of organisms from human influence will become identifiable in the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_record" title="Geologic record">geologic record</a>. Researchers have documented the movement of many species into regions formerly too cold for them, often at rates faster than initially expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> This has occurred in part as a result of changing climate, but also in response to farming and fishing, and to the accidental introduction of non-native species to new areas through global travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup> The ecosystem of the entire <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> may have changed during the last 2000 years as a result of nutrient and silica input from eroding deforested lands along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">Danube River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">Anthropogenic climate change</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropocene_extinction" title="Anthropocene extinction">Anthropocene extinction</a></div>
<p>One geological symptom resulting from human activity is increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) content. During the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">glacial–interglacial</a> cycles of the past million years, natural processes have varied CO<sub>2</sub> by approximately 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> (from 180 ppm to 280 ppm). As of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, anthropogenic net emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> increased atmospheric concentration by a comparable amount from 280 ppm (Holocene or pre-industrial "equilibrium") to approximately 400 ppm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> with 2015–16 monthly monitoring data of CO<sub>2</sub> displaying a rising trend above 400 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This signal in the Earth's climate system is especially significant because it is occurring much faster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and to a greater extent, than previous, similar changes. Most of this increase is due to the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">gas</a>, although smaller fractions are the result of <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> production and land-use changes (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changes in drainage patterns traceable to human activity will persist over geologic time in large parts of the continents where the geologic regime is erosional.  This includes the paths of roads and highways defined by their grading and drainage control. Direct changes to the form of the Earth's surface by human activities (e.g., quarrying, landscaping) also record human impacts.
</p><p>It has been suggested the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Calthemite" title="Calthemite">calthemite</a> formations are one example of a natural process which has not previously occurred prior to the human modification of the Earth's surface, and therefore represents a unique process of the Anthropocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Calthemite is a secondary deposit, derived from <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a> or other calcareous material <i>outside the <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> environment</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithGK2016_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithGK2016-52">[52]</a></sup> Calthemites grow on or under, man-made structures (including mines and tunnels) and mimic the shapes and forms of cave <a href="/wiki/Speleothem" title="Speleothem">speleothems</a>, such as  <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalactites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a>, flowstone <i>etc.</i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratigraphy">Stratigraphy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stratigraphy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentological_record">Sedimentological record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sedimentological record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Human activities like deforestation and road construction are believed to have elevated average total sediment fluxes across the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup> However, construction of dams on many rivers around the world means the rates of sediment deposition in any given place do not always appear to increase in the Anthropocene. For instance, many <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> around the world are actually currently starved of sediment by such dams, and are subsiding and failing to keep up with sea level rise, rather than growing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increases in erosion due to farming and other operations will be reflected by changes in sediment composition and increases in deposition rates elsewhere. In land areas with a depositional regime, engineered structures will tend to be buried and preserved, along with litter and debris. Litter and debris thrown from boats or carried by rivers and creeks will accumulate in the marine environment, particularly in coastal areas. Such manmade artifacts preserved in stratigraphy are known as "technofossils."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Changes in biodiversity will also be reflected in the fossil record, as will species introductions.  An example cited is the domestic chicken, originally the <a href="/wiki/Red_junglefowl" title="Red junglefowl">red junglefowl</a> <i>Gallus gallus</i>, native to south-east Asia but has since become the world's most common bird through human breeding and consumption, with over 60 billion consumed a year and whose bones would become fossilized in landfill sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trace_elements">Trace elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Trace elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In terms of trace elements, there are distinct signatures left by modern societies. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Fremont_Glacier" title="Upper Fremont Glacier">Upper Fremont Glacier</a> in Wyoming, there is a layer of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> present in ice cores from 1960's atomic weapon testing programs, as well as a layer of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> plants in the 1980s. From 1945 to 1951, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear fallout</a> is found locally around atomic device test sites, whereas from 1952 to 1980, tests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermonuclear" title="Thermonuclear">thermonuclear</a> devices have left a clear, global signal of excess <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">14</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">239</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Pu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, and other artificial <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a>. The highest global concentration of radionuclides was in 1965, one of the dates which has been proposed as a possible benchmark for the start of the formally defined Anthropocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Human burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> has also left distinctly elevated concentrations of black carbon, inorganic ash, and spherical carbonaceous particles in recent sediments across the world. Concentrations of these components increases markedly and almost simultaneously around the world beginning around 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science2016_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Science2016-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporal_limit">Temporal limit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Temporal limit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Anthropocene Working Group voted on the "Base/beginning of the Anthropocene", and most members favored some time around 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  This time period has also been referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Acceleration" title="Great Acceleration">Great Acceleration</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Early_anthropocene.22_model"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Early_anthropocene"_model'>"Early anthropocene" model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title='Edit section: "Early anthropocene" model'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Early_anthropocene" title="Early anthropocene">Early anthropocene</a></div>
<p>While much of the environmental change occurring on Earth is suspected to be a direct consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Ruddiman" title="William Ruddiman">William Ruddiman</a> has argued that the proposed Anthropocene began approximately 8,000 years ago with the development of farming and sedentary cultures. At this point, humans were dispersed across all of the continents (except <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> was ongoing. During this period, humans developed <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a> to supplement or replace <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> subsistence. Such innovations were followed by a wave of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinctions</a>, beginning with large <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and land <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>. This wave was driven by both the direct activity of humans (e.g. hunting) and the indirect consequences of land-use change for agriculture.
</p><p>From the past to present, some authors consider the Anthropocene and the Holocene to be the same or coeval geologic time span,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> others that the onset of the Anthropocene was just a bit more recent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Ruddiman claims that the Anthropocene, as defined by significant human impact on greenhouse gas emissions, began not in the industrial era, but 8,000 years ago, as ancient farmers cleared forests to grow crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruddiman01_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruddiman01-63">[63]</a></sup> Ruddiman's work has, in turn, been challenged on the grounds that comparison with an earlier interglaciation ("Stage 11", approximately 400,000 years ago) suggests that 16,000 more years must elapse before the current Holocene interglaciation comes to an end, and that thus the early anthropogenic hypothesis is invalid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ruddiman argues in rebuttal that this results from an invalid alignment of recent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> maxima with insolation minima from the past, among other irregularities, which invalidate the criticism. Furthermore, the argument that "something" is needed to explain the differences in the Holocene is challenged by more recent research showing that all interglacials differ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although 8,000 years ago the planet sustained a few million people and was still fundamentally pristine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> this is the basis for an assertion that an early date for the proposed Anthropocene term does account for a substantial human footprint on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A plausible starting point of the Anthropocene could be at <i>ca.</i> 2,000 years ago,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which roughly coincides with the start of the final phase of <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Subatlantic" title="Subatlantic">Subatlantic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Certini01_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Certini01-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>At this time, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> encompassed large portions of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the Middle East, and North Africa. In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">classical dynasties</a> were flowering. The <a href="/wiki/Middle_kingdoms_of_India" title="Middle kingdoms of India">Middle kingdoms of India</a> had already the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Largest_economy" title="Largest economy">largest economy</a> of the ancient and medieval world. The Napata/Meroitic kingdom extended over the current <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olmec" title="Olmec">Olmecs</a> controlled central <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, and the pre-Incan Chavín people managed areas of northern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Although often apart from each other and intermixed with buffering ecosystems, the areas directly impacted by these civilizations and others were large. Additionally, some activities, such as mining, implied much more widespread perturbation of natural conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Over the last 11,500 years or so humans have spread around Earth, increased in number, and profoundly altered the material world. They have taken advantage of global environmental conditions not of their own making.The end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> – when as much as 30% of Earth's surface was ice-bound – led to a warmer world with more water (<span class="Unicode"> </span><a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>). Although humans existed in the previous Pleistocene epoch, it is only in the recent Holocene period that they have flourished. Today there are more humans alive than at any previous point in Earth's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crutzen proposed the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> as the start of Anthropocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crutzen02_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crutzen02-28">[28]</a></sup> Lovelock proposes that the Anthropocene began with the first application of the <a href="/wiki/Newcomen_atmospheric_engine" title="Newcomen atmospheric engine">Newcomen atmospheric engine</a> in 1712. The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> takes the pre-industrial era (chosen as the year 1750) as the baseline related to changes in long-lived, well mixed greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Although it is apparent that the Industrial Revolution ushered in an unprecedented global human impact on the planet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> much of Earth's landscape already had been profoundly modified by human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The human impact on Earth has grown progressively, with few substantial slowdowns.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropocene_marker">Anthropocene marker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anthropocene marker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A marker that accounts for a substantial global impact of humans on the total environment, comparable in scale to those associated with significant perturbations of the geological past, is needed in place of minor changes in atmosphere composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>A useful candidate for this purpose is the <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>, which can retain information of its climatic and geochemical history with features lasting for centuries or millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Human activity is now firmly established as the sixth factor of soil formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> It affects pedogenesis either directly, by, for example, land levelling, trenching and embankment building for various purposes, organic matter enrichment from additions of manure or other waste, organic matter impoverishment due to continued cultivation, compaction from overgrazing or, indirectly, by drift of eroded materials or pollutants. Anthropogenic soils are those markedly affected by human activities, such as repeated ploughing, the addition of fertilizers, contamination, sealing, or enrichment with artefacts (in the <a href="/wiki/World_Reference_Base_for_Soil_Resources" title="World Reference Base for Soil Resources">World Reference Base for Soil Resources</a> they are classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthrosols" title="Anthrosols">Anthrosols</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technosols" title="Technosols">Technosols</a>). They are recalcitrant repositories of artefacts and properties that testify to the dominance of the human impact, and hence appear to be reliable markers for the Anthropocene. Some anthropogenic soils may be viewed as the 'golden spikes' of geologists (<a href="/wiki/Global_Boundary_Stratotype_Section_and_Point" title="Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point">Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point</a>), which are locations where there are strata successions with clear evidences of a worldwide event, including the appearance of distinctive fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Certini01_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Certini01-67">[67]</a></sup> Drilling for fossil fuels has also created holes and tubes which are expected to be detectable for millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of the Anthropocene has also been approached via humanities such as philosophy, literature and art. In the scholarly world, it has been the subject of increasing attention through special journal issues,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> conferences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> and disciplinary reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> The Anthropocene, its attendant timescale, and ecological implications prompts questions about death and the ends of civilization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> memory and archives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> the scope and methods of humanistic inquiry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> and emotional responses to the "end of nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> It has been also criticized as an ideological construct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Some environmentalists on the political left suggest that "Capitalocene" is a more historically appropriate term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> At the same time, others suggest that the Anthropocene is overly focused on the human species, while ignoring systematic inequalities, such as <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, that have also shaped the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several philosophical approaches on how to handle the future of  Anthropocene: Business-as-usual, mitigation, geo-engineering options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
