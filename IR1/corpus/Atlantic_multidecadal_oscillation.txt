<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atlantic multidecadal oscillation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</b> (<b>AMO</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_cycle" title="Climate cycle">climate cycle</a> that affects the <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> (SST) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> based on different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mode_of_variability" title="Mode of variability">modes</a> on multidecadal timescales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> While there is some support for this mode in models and in historical observations, controversy exists with regard to its <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a>, and in particular, the attribution of sea surface temperature change to natural or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthropogenic" title="wiktionary:anthropogenic">anthropogenic</a> causes, especially in tropical Atlantic areas important for hurricane development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ting_2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ting_2009-2">[2]</a></sup> The Atlantic multidecadal oscillation is also connected with shifts in hurricane activity, rainfall patterns and intensity, and changes in fish populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frajka-Williams_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frajka-Williams-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) was identified by Schlesinger and Ramankutty in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The AMO signal is usually defined from the patterns of SST variability in the North Atlantic once any linear trend has been removed. This detrending is intended to remove the influence of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>-induced <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> from the analysis. However, if the global warming signal is significantly non-linear in time (i.e. not just a smooth linear increase), variations in the forced signal will leak into the AMO definition. Consequently, correlations with the AMO index may mask effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mann_2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mann_2014-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="AMO_Index">AMO Index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: AMO Index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several methods have been proposed to remove the global trend and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO)</a> influence over the North Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">SST</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kevin_E._Trenberth" title="Kevin E. Trenberth">Trenberth</a> and Shea, assuming that the effect of global forcing over the North Atlantic is similar to the global ocean, subtracted the global (60°N-60°S) mean SST from the North Atlantic SST to derive a revised AMO index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trenberth_2005_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-trenberth_2005-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ting <i>et al.</i> however argue that the forced SST pattern is not globally uniform; they separated the forced and internally generated variability using signal to noise maximizing <a href="/wiki/Empirical_orthogonal_functions" title="Empirical orthogonal functions">EOF</a> analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ting_2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ting_2009-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Van Oldenborgh <i>et al.</i> derived an AMO index as the SST averaged over the extra-tropical North Atlantic (to remove the influence of ENSO that is greater at tropical latitude) minus the regression on global mean temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Oldenborgh_2009_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_Oldenborgh_2009-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Guan and Nigam removed the non stationary global trend and Pacific natural variability before applying an <a href="/wiki/Empirical_orthogonal_functions" title="Empirical orthogonal functions">EOF</a> analysis to the residual North Atlantic SST.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guan_2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-guan_2009-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The linearly detrended index suggests that the North Atlantic SST anomaly at the end of the twentieth century is equally divided between the externally forced component and internally generated variability, and that the current peak is similar to middle twentieth century; by contrast the others methodology suggest that a large portion of the North Atlantic anomaly at the end of the twentieth century is externally forced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ting_2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ting_2009-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Frajka-Williams et al. 2017 pointed out that recent changes in cooling of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpolar_gyre" title="Subpolar gyre">subpolar gyre</a>, warm temperatures in the subtropics and cool anomalies over the tropics, increased the spatial distribution of meridional gradient in sea surface temperatures, which is not captured by the AMO Index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frajka-Williams_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frajka-Williams-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Atlantic meridional overturning circulation">Atlantic meridional overturning circulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">Ocean heat content</a></div>
<p>Based on the about 150-year instrumental record a quasi-periodicity of about 70 years, with a few distinct warmer phases between ca. 1930–1965 and after 1995, and cool between 1900–1930 and 1965–1995 has been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
In models, AMO-like variability is associated with small changes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermohaline" title="Thermohaline">Thermohaline Circulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, historical oceanic observations are not sufficient to associate the derived AMO index to present-day circulation anomalies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Models and observations indicate that changes in atmospheric circulation, which induce changes in clouds, atmospheric dust and surface heat flux, are largely responsible for the tropical portion of the AMO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) is important for how external forcings are linked with North Atlantic SSTs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_impacts_worldwide">Climate impacts worldwide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate impacts worldwide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AMO is correlated to air temperatures and rainfall over much of the Northern Hemisphere, in particular in the summer climate in North America and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Through changes in atmospheric circulation, the AMO can also modulate spring snowfall over the Alps <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and glaciers' mass variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Rainfall patterns are affected in North Eastern Brazilian and African Sahel. It is also associated with changes in the frequency of North American <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> and is reflected in the frequency of severe Atlantic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trenberth_2005_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-trenberth_2005-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent research suggests that the AMO is related to the past occurrence of major droughts in the US Midwest and the Southwest. When the AMO is in its warm phase, these droughts tend to be more frequent or prolonged. Two of the most severe droughts of the 20th century occurred during the positive AMO between 1925 and 1965: The <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> of the 1930s and the 1950s drought. Florida and the Pacific Northwest tend to be the opposite—warm AMO, more rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">Climate models</a> suggest that a warm phase of the AMO strengthens the summer rainfall over <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">North Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatologic</a> studies have confirmed this pattern—increased rainfall in AMO warmphase, decreased in cold phase—for the Sahel over the past 3,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A 2008 study correlated the Atlantic Multidecadal Mode (AMM), with <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a> data (1851–2007), and noted a positive linear trend for minor hurricanes (category 1 and 2), but removed when the authors adjusted their model for undercounted storms, and stated "If there is an increase in hurricane activity connected to a greenhouse gas induced global warming, it is currently obscured by the 60 year quasi-periodic cycle."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chylek/Lesins_2007_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chylek/Lesins_2007-21">[21]</a></sup> With full consideration of meteorological science, the number of tropical storms that can mature into severe hurricanes is much greater during warm phases of the AMO than during cool phases, at least twice as many; the AMO is reflected in the frequency of severe Atlantic hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. Archived, cites no sources (October 2017)">unreliable source</span></a></i>]</sup> Based on the typical duration of negative and positive phases of the AMO, the current warm regime is expected to persist at least until 2015 and possibly as late as 2035. Enfield <i>et al.</i> assume a peak around 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enfield2009_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enfield2009-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1995, there have been nine Atlantic hurricane seasons considered "extremely active" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a> - <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995</a>, <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996</a>, <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1998 Atlantic hurricane season">1998</a>, <a href="/wiki/1999_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1999 Atlantic hurricane season">1999</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>, <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010</a> and <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Missing RS, appears as OR (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Periodicity_and_prediction_of_AMO_shifts">Periodicity and prediction of AMO shifts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Periodicity and prediction of AMO shifts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are only about 130–150 years of data based on instrument data, which are too few samples for conventional statistical approaches. With the aid of multi-century proxy reconstruction, a longer period of 424 years was used by Enfield and Cid–Serrano as an illustration of an approach as described in their paper called "The Probabilistic Projection of Climate Risk".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Their histogram of zero crossing intervals from a set of five re-sampled and smoothed version of Gray et al. (2004) index together with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_Likelihood_Estimate" title="Maximum Likelihood Estimate">Maximum Likelihood Estimate</a> gamma distribution fit to the histogram, showed that the largest frequency of regime interval was around 10–20 year. The cumulative probability for all intervals 20 years or less was about 70%.
</p><p>There is no demonstrated predictability for when the AMO will switch, in any deterministic sense. Computer models, such as those that predict <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>, are far from being able to do this. Enfield and colleagues have calculated the probability that a change in the AMO will occur within a given future time frame, assuming that historical variability persists. <a class="external text" href="http://www.aoml.noaa.gov/phod/d2m_shift/index.php" rel="nofollow">Probabilistic projections</a> of this kind may prove to be useful for long-term planning in climate sensitive applications, such as water management.
</p><p>Assuming that the AMO continues with a quasi-cycle of roughly 70 years, the peak of the current warm phase would be expected in c. 2020,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curry2008_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curry2008-24">[24]</a></sup> or based on a 50–90 year quasi-cycle, between 2000 and 2040 (after peaks in c. 1880 and c. 1950).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enfield2009_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enfield2009-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Stay_on_topic" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The material near this tag may contain information that is not relevant to the article's main topic. (April 2010)">relevant?</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation#The_AMO_and_its_periodicity" title="Talk:Atlantic multidecadal oscillation">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A 2017 study predicts a continued cooling shift beginning 2014, and the authors note, "..unlike the last cold period in the Atlantic, the spatial pattern of sea surface temperature anomalies in the Atlantic is not uniformly cool, but instead has anomalously cold temperatures in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subpolar_gyre" title="Subpolar gyre">subpolar gyre</a>, warm temperatures in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a> and cool anomalies over the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. The tripole pattern of anomalies has increased the subpolar to subtropical meridional gradient in SSTs, which are not represented by the AMO index value, but which may lead to increased atmospheric baroclinicity and storminess."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frajka-Williams_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frajka-Williams-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
