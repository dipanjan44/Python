<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Population model</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>population model</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical model</a> that is applied to the study of <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rationale">Rationale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rationale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">Models</a> allow a better understanding of how complex interactions and processes work. Modeling of dynamic interactions in nature can provide a manageable way of understanding how numbers change over time or in relation to each other. Many patterns can be noticed by using population modeling as a tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worster_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worster-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">Ecological</a> population modeling is concerned with the changes in parameters such as <a href="/wiki/Population_size" title="Population size">population size</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_distribution" title="Age distribution">age distribution</a> within a population. This might be due to interactions with the environment, individuals of their own species, or other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uyenoyama_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uyenoyama-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Population models are used to determine maximum harvest for agriculturists, to understand the dynamics of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_invasion" title="Biological invasion">biological invasions</a>, and for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_conservation" title="Environmental conservation">environmental conservation</a>. Population models are also used to understand the spread of parasites, viruses, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_spread" title="Disease spread">disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uyenoyama_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Uyenoyama-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another way populations models are useful are when species become endangered. Population models can track the fragile species and work and curb the decline. <a class="external autonumber" href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/1447430?seq=1#page_scan_tab_contents" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Late 18th-century biologists began to develop techniques in population modeling in order to understand dynamics of growing and shrinking ball populations of living organisms. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> was one of the first to note that populations grew with a geometric pattern while contemplating the fate of humankind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McIntosh_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-McIntosh-3">[3]</a></sup> One of the most basic and milestone models of population growth was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logistic_model_of_population_growth" title="Logistic model of population growth">logistic model of population growth</a> formulated by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Fran%C3%A7ois_Verhulst" title="Pierre François Verhulst">Pierre François Verhulst</a> in 1838. The logistic model takes the shape of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sigmoid_curve" title="Sigmoid curve">sigmoid curve</a> and describes the growth of a population as exponential, followed by a decrease in growth, and bound by a carrying capacity due to environmental pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Population modeling became of particular interest to biologists in the 20th century as pressure on limited means of sustenance due to increasing human populations in parts of Europe were noticed by biologist like <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Pearl" title="Raymond Pearl">Raymond Pearl</a>. In 1921 Pearl invited physicist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_J._Lotka" title="Alfred J. Lotka">Alfred J. Lotka</a> to assist him in his lab. Lotka developed paired <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> that showed the effect of a parasite on its prey. Mathematician <a href="/wiki/Vito_Volterra" title="Vito Volterra">Vito Volterra</a> equated the relationship between two species independent from Lotka. Together, Lotka and Volterra formed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotka%E2%80%93Volterra_equation" title="Lotka–Volterra equation">Lotka–Volterra model</a> for competition that applies the logistic equation to two species illustrating competition, predation, and parasitism interactions between species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McIntosh_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-McIntosh-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1939 contributions to population modeling were given by Patrick Leslie as he began work in biomathematics. Leslie emphasized the importance of constructing a life table in order to understand the effect that key life history strategies played in the dynamics of whole populations. Matrix algebra was used by Leslie in conjunction with life tables to extend the work of Lotka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Matrix_population_models" title="Matrix population models">Matrix models</a> of populations calculate the growth of a population with life history variables. Later, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_MacArthur" title="Robert MacArthur">Robert MacArthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/E._O._Wilson" title="E. O. Wilson">E. O. Wilson</a> characterized island biogeography. The <a href="/wiki/Ecology#Biogeography" title="Ecology">equilibrium model of island biogeography</a> describes the number of species on an island as an equilibrium of immigration and extinction. The logistic population model, the Lotka–Volterra model of community ecology, life table matrix modeling, the equilibrium model of island biogeography and variations thereof are the basis for ecological population modeling today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
