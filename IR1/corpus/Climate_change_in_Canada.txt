<p>In <b>Canada,</b> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a></b> is being addressed more seriously by the provinces than by the federal government. The 2015 election signals greater federal leadership as noted in Canada's National Statement at <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">COP21</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> making climate change a top priority, and pledging actions based on the best scientific evidence and advice.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_impacts">Observed impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observed impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Environment_and_Climate_Change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Environment and Climate Change in Canada (page does not exist)">Environment and Climate Change in Canada</a> (ECCC), formerly Environment Canada, is a federal department with the stated role of protecting the environment, conserving national natural heritage, and also providing weather and meteorological information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> According to ECCC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> "warming over the 20th century is indisputable and largely due to human activities" adding "Canada's rate of warming is about twice the global rate: a 2°C increase globally means a 3 to 4ºC increase for Canada".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Earth" title="Berkeley Earth">Berkeley Earth</a> has reported that  2015 was "unambiguously" the warmest  year on record across the world, with the Earth’s temperature more than 1.0 C (1.8 F) above the 1850-1900 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>ECCC lists impacts of climate change consistent with global changes. Temperature-related changes include longer growing season, more heat waves and fewer cold spells, thawing permafrost, earlier river ice break-up, earlier spring runoff, and earlier budding of trees. Meteorological changes include an increase in precipitation and more snowfall in northwest Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Highlighting that "Warming is not uniform ...(the) Arctic is warming even faster", ECCC notes 2012 had the lowest extent of Arctic sea ice on record up to 2014.
</p><p>ECCC's Climate Research Division summarized annual precipitation changes to support biodiversity assessments by the Canadian Councils of Resource Ministers. Evaluating records up to 2007 they observed: "Precipitation has generally increased over Canada since 1950 with the majority of stations with significant trends showing increases. The increasing trend is most coherent over northern Canada where many stations show significant increases. There is not much evidence of clear regional patterns in stations showing significant changes in seasonal precipitation except for significant decreases which tend to be concentrated in the winter season over southwestern and southeastern Canada. While the previous sentence might be technically correct in part, all seasons show increased precipitation in Canada, especially in the Winter, Spring, and Fall months. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>   Also, increasing precipitation over the Arctic appears to be occurring in all seasons except summer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>ECCC climate specialists have assessed trends in short-duration rainfall patterns using Engineering Climate Datasets: "Short-duration (5 minutes to 24 hours) rainfall extremes are important for a number of purposes, including engineering infrastructure design, because they represent the different meteorological scales of extreme rainfall events." A "general lack of a detectable trend signal", meaning no overall change in extreme,short-duration rainfall patterns was observed in the single station analysis. In relation to design criteria used for traditional water management and urban drainage design practice (e.g., Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) statistics), the evaluation "shows that fewer than 5.6% and 3.4% of the stations have significant increasing and decreasing trends, respectively, in extreme annual maximum single location observation amounts." On a regional basis, southwest and the east (Newfoundland) coastal regions generally showed significant increasing regional trends for 1- and 2-hour extreme rainfall durations. Decreasing regional trends for 5 to 15 minute rainfall amounts were observed in the St. Lawrence region of southern Quebec and in the Atlantic provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change melt ice and  increases the mobility of the ice. In May and June 2017 dense ice – up to 8 metres (25ft) thick –  was in the waters off the northern coast of Newfoundland, trapping fishing boats and ferries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions">Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2000 Canada ranked ninth out of 186 countries in terms of per capita <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions without taking into account land use changes. In 2005 it ranked eighth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2009, Canada was ranked seventh in total greenhouse gas emissions behind Germany and Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canada is a large country with a low population density, so transportation – often in cold weather when fuel efficiency drops – is a big part of the economy. About 25 per cent of Canada's <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> (GHG)s come from trucks, trains, airplanes and cars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Commerce, residential fuel consumption and industry (excluding oil and gas) account for 24 per cent of the total, but much of those emissions come from equipment (mining trucks, front-end loaders) that do not get recorded in the transportation ledger. Another 14 per cent come from non-energy sources. The rest come from the production and manufacture of energy and power. The following table summarizes forecast changes to annual emissions by sector in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megatonne" title="Megatonne">megatonnes</a>.
</p>

<p>As Canada creates targets for GHG reductions, policymakers will likely zero in on the three areas – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation">transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> production – in which the greatest reductions are possible. Together, these activities account for nearly two-thirds of Canada's greenhouse gases. Efficiencies can be found there.
</p><p>According to <i>Canada's Energy Outlook</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a> (NRCan) report,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> NRCan estimates that Canada's GHG emissions will increase by 139 million tonnes between 2004 and 2020, with more than a third of the total coming from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> production and refining. Upstream emissions will decline slightly, primarily from gas field depletion and from increasing production of <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a>, which requires less processing than conventional <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. Meanwhile, emissions from unconventional resources and refining will soar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the estimates for carbon emissions differ amongst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> by nearly 50%. The reasons for the differences have not been determined.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_policy">Public policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kyoto_Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kyoto Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canada_and_the_Kyoto_Protocol" title="Canada and the Kyoto Protocol">Canada and the Kyoto Protocol</a></div>
<p>Canada is a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>. However, the Liberal government that later signed the accord took little action towards meeting Canada's greenhouse gas emission targets. Although Canada committed itself to a 6% reduction below the 1990 levels for the 2008-2012  as a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, the country did not implement a plan to reduce greenhouse gases emissions. Soon after the 2006 federal election, the new minority government of <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a> announced that Canada could and would not meet Canada's commitments. The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> passed several opposition-sponsored bills calling for government plans for the implementation of emission reduction measures.
</p><p>Canadian and North American environmental groups feel that Canada lacks credibility on <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental policy</a> and regularly criticizes Canada in international venues. In the last few months of 2009, Canada's attitude was criticized at the Asia-Pacific Economic Co-operation (APEC) conference,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> at the Commonwealth summit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP15" title="COP15">Copenhagen conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Canada, Japan and Russia stated that they would not take on further Kyoto targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canadaguardian_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-canadaguardian-17">[17]</a></sup> The Canadian government invoked Canada's legal right to formally withdraw from the Kyoto Protocol on 12 December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Canada was committed to cutting its greenhouse emissions to 6% below 1990 levels by 2012, but in 2009 emissions were 17% higher than in 1990. Environment minister <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kent" title="Peter Kent">Peter Kent</a> cited Canada's liability to "enormous financial penalties" under the treaty unless it withdrew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canadaguardian_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-canadaguardian-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  He also suggested that the recently signed <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">Durban agreement</a> may provide an alternative way forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fire_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fire-20">[20]</a></sup> Canada's decision was strongly criticized by representatives of other ratifying countries, including France and China.
</p>

<p>Under the tenure of Stephen Harper, who was Prime Minister from 2006 to 2015, the Kyoto Accord was abandoned and the Clean Air Act was unveiled on October 19, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcmillan_2007_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcmillan_2007-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010 Graham Saul, who represented the Climate Action Network Canada (CAN) — a coalition of 60 non-governmental organisations —  commented on the 40-page CAN report "Troubling Evidence"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAN_Cuddy_2020_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAN_Cuddy_2020-22">[22]</a></sup> which claimed that,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2010_Leahy_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian_2010_Leahy-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying">Lobbying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lobbying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Wildlife_Federation" title="Canadian Wildlife Federation">Canadian Wildlife Federation</a> (CWF), one of the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_organisation" title="Conservation organisation">conservation organisations</a> in the country, takes an active stance in lobbying on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation of global warming</a>. According to CWF the organisation recognised the need for action in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It had published <i><a href="/wiki/Checkerspot_(magazine)" title="Checkerspot (magazine)">Checkerspot</a></i>, a now discontinued biannual climate change magazine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuel_divestment">Fossil fuel divestment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fossil fuel divestment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_divestment" title="Fossil fuel divestment">Fossil fuel divestment</a> is a social movement which urges everyone from individual investors to large institutions to remove their investments (to divest) from publicly listed oil, gas and coal companies, with the intention of combating climate change by reducing the amount of Green-house gases released into the atmosphere, and holding the oil, gas and coal companies responsible for their role in climate change.
</p><p>Founder of the movement <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a>, a researcher and academic from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">university of Victoria</a>, and creator of the webpage <a href="/wiki/350.org" title="350.org">350.org</a> stated: "If it is wrong to wreck the climate, then it is wrong to profit from the wreckage. We believe […] organizations that serve the public good should divest from fossil fuels"
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement_intentions">Movement intentions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Movement intentions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>1. Protect the investor from exposure to the financial risks of ‘unburnable carbon’ whereby fossil fuel reserves become uneconomic or are no longer viable to process due to future climate policy or market conditions</li>
<li>2. Divesting from these companies can keep a substantial portion of fossil fuels in the ground</li>
<li>3. Large institutions can substitute high-carbon investments with low-carbon transition investments</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits_of_divestment">Limits of divestment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Limits of divestment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although the impact of divestment is likely to have limited quantitative success in reducing carbon emissions, the movement can gain momentum as a symbolic gesture that has the potential to shift social expectations of investment practices within businesses. Divestment has the potential to be effective if the divested funds are re-invested into the infrastructure of a low-carbon economy
The impact of divestment is believed to be minimal as the continual purchase of oil and gas (and oil and gas derived products such as plastics) will still sustain the oil and gas companies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_by_province">Climate change by province</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate change by province">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the federal government was slow to develop a monitoring and credible reduction regime, several provincial governments have established substantial programs to reduce emissions on their respective territories. <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> have joined the <a href="/wiki/Western_Climate_Initiative" title="Western Climate Initiative">Western Climate Initiative</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> a group of 7 states of the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">Western United States</a> whose aim is to establish a common framework to establish a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">carbon credit</a> market. These provinces have also made commitments regarding the reduction and announced concrete steps to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> has an established "Climate Change Action Plan",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Government_of_Alberta_2008_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Government_of_Alberta_2008-27">[27]</a></sup> released in 2008. The Specified Gas Emitters Regulation in Alberta made it the first jurisdiction in North America to have a price on carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Government_of_Alberta_2008_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Government_of_Alberta_2008-27">[27]</a></sup> Reduction programs in other provinces are much less developed.
</p><p>However, a <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">cost-effectiveness analysis</a> of these programs by the <a href="/wiki/Fraser_Institute" title="Fraser Institute">Fraser Institute</a> has questioned their value. In other jurisdictions, carbon markets, renewable energy sources, electric vehicles, and energy efficiency programs have yielded disappointing benefits in comparison to the funds and regulations that have created them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canada's two largest provinces, Ontario and Quebec, are wary of federal policies shifting the burden of greenhouse reductions on them in order to give Alberta and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> more room to further develop their <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a> reserves, therefore chilling relations between the 13 provinces and territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alberta">Alberta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alberta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Specified Gas Emitters Regulation has placed a price on carbon dioxide emissions in Alberta since 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Queens_Printer_2007_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Queens_Printer_2007-30">[30]</a></sup> and was renewed to 2017 with increased stringency. It requires "large final emitters", defined as facilities emitting more than 100,000tCO2e per year, to comply with an emission intensity reduction which increases over time and caps at 12% in 2015, 15% in 2016 and 20% in 2017. Facilities have several options for compliance. They may actually make reductions, pay into the Climate Change and Emission Management Fund (CCEMF), purchase credits from other large final emitters or purchase credits from non large final emitters in the form of offset credits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Criticisms against the intensity based approach to pricing carbon include the fact that there is no hard cap on emissions and actual emissions may always continue to rise despite the fact that carbon has a price. Benefits of an intensity based system include the fact that during economic recessions, the carbon intensity reduction will remain equally as stringent and challenging, while hard caps tend to become easily met, irrelevant and do not work to reduce emissions. Alberta has also been criticized that its goals are too weak, and that the measures enacted are not likely to achieve the goals. In 2015, the newly elected government committed to revising the climate change strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_leadership_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate_leadership-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_leadership_doc_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate_leadership_doc-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> there has been a trend of high summer temperatures and low summer precipitation. This has led much of Alberta to face <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forecasting_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-forecasting-34">[34]</a></sup>  Drought conditions are harming the agriculture sector of this province, mainly the cattle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranching" title="Ranching">ranching</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agric_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-agric-35">[35]</a></sup>  When there is a drought there is a shortage of feed for cattle (hay, grain).  With the shortage on crops ranchers are forced to purchase the feed at the increased prices while they can.  For those who cannot afford to pay top money for feed are forced to sell their herds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_2009_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_2009-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC_2002_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC_2002-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the drought of 2002, Ontario had a good season and produced enough crops to send a vast amount of hay to those hit the hardest in Alberta.  However this is not something that can or will be expected every time there is a drought in the prairie provinces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  This causes a great deficit in income for many as they are buying heads of cattle for high prices and selling them for very low prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  By looking at historical forecasts, there is a strong indication that there is no true way to estimate or to know the amount of rain to expect for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a>.  This does not allow for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_sector" title="Agricultural sector">agricultural sector</a> to plan accordingly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
As of 2008, Alberta's electricity sector was the most <a href="/wiki/Emission_intensity" title="Emission intensity">carbon-intensive</a> of all Canadian provinces and territories, with total emissions of 55.9 million tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> equivalent</a> in 2008, accounting for 47% of all Canadian emissions in the electricity and heat generation sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GHG2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-GHG2008-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2015, Premier <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Notley" title="Rachel Notley">Rachel Notley</a> unveiled plans to increase the province's carbon tax to $20 per tonne in 2017, increasing further to $30 per tonne by 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This policy shift came about partly because of the rejection of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keystone_XL" title="Keystone XL">Keystone XL</a> pipeline, which the premier likened to a "kick in the teeth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The province's new climate policies also include phasing out coal-fired power plants by 2030, and cutting emissions of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> by 45% by 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alberta witnessed the effects of climate change in a dramatic manner when a "perfect storm" of El Niño and global warming contributed to the <a href="/wiki/2016_Fort_McMurray_wildfire" title="2016 Fort McMurray wildfire">2016 Fort McMurray wildfire</a>, which led to the evacuation of the oil-producing town at the heart of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a> industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The area has witnessed an increased frequency of wildfires, as Canada's wildfire season now starts a month earlier that it used to and the annual area burned is twice what it was in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateCentral_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateCentral-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Columbia">British Columbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: British Columbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The extreme weather events of greatest concern in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> include heavy rain and snow falls, heat waves, and drought.  They are linked to flooding and landslides, water shortages, forest fires, reduced air quality, as well as costs related to damage to property and infrastructure, business disruptions, and increased illness and mortality.  In recent years, significant extreme events and climate impacts in BC have included:
</p>
<ul><li>the pine beetle epidemic, which resulted in 18 million hectares of dead trees and economic impacts for forest dependent communities;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>330,000 hectares of forest lost to forest fire in the 2010 fire season alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  and the loss of 334 homes in the 2003 forest fire season;</li>
<li>flooding in 2010 leading to the destruction of the Bella Coola highway and evacuation of residents from Kingcome Inlet;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>  and</li>
<li>heat waves, including the one in the summer of 2009, which are associated with increases in heat stroke and respiratory illness.</li></ul>
<p>BC has implemented many ambitious policies to address climate change mitigation, particularly through its Climate Action Plan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  released in 2008. It has set legislated greenhouse gas reduction targets of 33% below 2007 levels by 2020 and 80% by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> 
BC’s revenue neutral carbon tax is the first of its kind in North America. It was introduced at $10/tonne of CO2e in 2008 and has risen by $5/tonne annual increases until it reached $30/tonne in 2012, where the rate has remained.  It is required in legislation that all revenues from the carbon tax are returned to British Columbians through tax cuts in other areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>BC’s public sector became the first in North America to be carbon neutral in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>  The Clean Energy Vehicles Program provides incentives for the purchase of approved clean energy vehicles and for charging infrastructure installation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>  There has been action across sectors including financing options and incentives for building retrofits, a Forest Carbon Offset Protocol, a Renewable and Low Carbon Fuel Standard, and <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill gas</a> management regulation.
</p><p>BC’s GHG emissions have been going down, and in 2012 (based on 2010 data) BC declared it was within reach of meeting its interim target of a 6% reduction below 2007 levels by 2012.  GHG emissions went down by 4.5% between 2007 and 2010, and consumption of all the main fossil fuels are down in BC as well while GDP and population have both been growing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, BC launched its Adaptation Strategy which focuses on building knowledge and tools to guide decision making affected by future climate, integrating adaptation into government business, and building adaptation approaches for key sectors such as agriculture and industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontario">Ontario</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ontario">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ontario is Canada’s most populated province <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and, in 2010, had the second highest <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions inventory in the country. In 1990, Ontario’s greenhouse gas emissions were 176 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megatonnes" title="Megatonnes">megatonnes</a> (Mt) of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent. According to Canada’s 2012 National Inventory Report <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Ontario’s emissions were 171 Mt in 2010, an amount that represented 25% of Canada’s total emissions for that year. Over the 20-year period between 1990 and 2010, Ontario’s emissions continued to increase until the mid-2000s. Emissions declined significantly in 2008-2009 due in large part to the economic <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a>. In 2010, Ontario emitted 12.95 tonnes per person,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> compared with the Canadian average of 20.3 tonnes per person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2007, the Ontario government released Go Green: Ontario’s Action Plan on Climate Change. The plan established three targets: a 6% reduction in emissions by 2014, 15% by 2020 and 80% by 2050. The government has committed to report annually on the actions it is taking to reduce emissions and adapt to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> With the initiatives currently in place, the government projects it will achieve 90% of the reductions needed to meet its 2014 target, and only 60% of those needed to meet the 2020 target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest emissions reductions to date have come from the phase out of coal-fired power generation by <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Power_Generation" title="Ontario Power Generation">Ontario Power Generation</a>. In August 2007, the government issued a regulation that required the end of coal burning at Ontario’s four remaining coal-fired power plants by the end of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Since 2003, emissions from these plants have dropped from 36.5 Mt to 4.2 Mt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In January 2013, the government announced that coal will be completely phased out one year early, by the end of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through the <i>Green Energy and Green Economy Act, 2009</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Ontario implemented a <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariff</a> to promote the development of renewable energy generation. Ontario is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Climate_Initiative" title="Western Climate Initiative">Western Climate Initiative</a>. In January 2013, a discussion paper was posted on the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Registry" title="Environmental Registry">Environmental Registry</a> seeking input on the development of a greenhouse gas emissions reduction program for industry.
</p><p>Over the years, transportation emissions have continued to increase. Growing from 44.8 Mt in 1990 to 59.5 Mt in 2010, transportation is responsible for the largest amount of greenhouse gas emissions in the province. Efforts to reduce these emissions include investing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">public transit</a> and providing incentives for the purchase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_vehicles" title="Electric vehicles">electric vehicles</a>.
</p><p>The government also recognizes the need for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">climate change adaptation</a> and, in April 2011, released Climate Ready: Ontario’s Adaptation Strategy and Action Plan 2011-2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>As required by the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Bill_of_Rights,_1993" title="Environmental Bill of Rights, 1993">Environmental Bill of Rights, 1993</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Commissioner_of_Ontario" title="Environmental Commissioner of Ontario">Environmental Commissioner of Ontario</a> does an independent review and reports annually to the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Ontario" title="Legislative Assembly of Ontario">Legislative Assembly of Ontario</a> on the progress of activities in the province to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quebec">Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenhouse gas emissions increased by 3.8% in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> between 1990 and 2007, to 85.7 megatonnes of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent</a>. At 11.1 tonnes per capita, Quebec's emissions are well below the Canadian average (22.1 tonnes) and accounted for 11.6% of Canada's total in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GES2007_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-GES2007-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>The latest data confirm a strong trend towards declining emissions in the industrial and residential sectors, which decreased by 23.6% and 27.9% respectively and a sharp rise in transportation (+29.5%) and in the tertiary sector (+53.2%). Emissions in the electricity sector have also spiked in 2007, due to the operation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TransCanada_Corp." title="TransCanada Corp.">TransCanada Energy</a> <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle gas turbine</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Becancour,_Quebec" title="Becancour, Quebec">Becancour</a>. The generating station, Quebec's largest source of greenhouse gas emissions that year, released 1,687,314 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent in 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> or 72.1% of all emissions from the sector and 2% of total emissions. The plant was closed in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becancour2007_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Becancour2007-70">[70]</a></sup> in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becancour2008_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Becancour2008-71">[71]</a></sup> and in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Becancour2009_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Becancour2009-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1990 — the reference year of the Kyoto Protocol — and 2006, Quebec's population grew by 9.2% and Quebec's GDP of 41.3%. The emission intensity relative to GDP declined from 28.1% during this period, dropping from 4,500 to 3,300 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent per million <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">dollars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GES2006_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-GES2006-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2009, Quebec became the first juridsiction in the Americas to impose an emissions cap after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quebec_National_Assembly" title="Quebec National Assembly">Quebec National Assembly</a> passed a bill capping emissions from certain sectors. The move was coordinated with a similar policy in the neighboring province of Ontario and reflects the commitment of both provinces as members of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Climate_Initiative" title="Western Climate Initiative">Western Climate Initiative</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 23, 2009, the Quebec government pledged to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions by 20% below the 1990 base year level by 2020, a goal similar to that adopted by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The government intends to achieve its target by promoting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">public transit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_freight_transport" title="Intermodal freight transport">intermodal freight transport</a>. The plan also calls for the increased use of wood as a building material, <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">energy recovery</a> from <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning">land use planning</a> reform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> According to simulations conducted with Quebec's Ministry of Finance <a href="/wiki/Econometric_model" title="Econometric model">econometric model</a>, the reduction goal should impact the province's real GDP by 0.16% in 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_on_forestry">Impacts on forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Impacts on forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Environment Canada’s 2011 annual report, there is evidence that some regional areas within the western Canadian boreal forest have increased by 2 °C since 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>  The rate of the changing climate is leading to drier conditions in the boreal forest, which leads to a whole host of subsequent issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestry_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-forestry-78">[78]</a></sup>  This leads to a challenge for the forestry industry to sustainably manage and conserve trees within boreal forest. Climate change will have a direct impact on the productivity of the boreal forest, as well as health and regeneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestry_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-forestry-78">[78]</a></sup>  As a result of the rapidly changing climate, trees are migrating to higher latitudes and altitudes (northward), but some species may not be migrating fast enough to follow their climatic habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-81">[81]</a></sup>  Moreover, trees within the southern limit of their range may begin to show declines in growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>  Drier conditions are also leading to a shift from conifers to aspen in more fire and drought-prone areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forestry_78-2"><a href="#cite_note-forestry-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assisted migration of tree species within the boreal forest is one tool that has been proposed and is currently under study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-sector-83">[83]</a></sup> It involves deliberately moving tree species to locations that may better climatically suit them in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-sector-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>  For species that may not be able to disperse easily, have long generation times or have small populations, this form of adaptative management and human intervention may help them survive in this rapidly changing climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-sector-83">[83]</a></sup> Assisted migration may offer a potential option to lessen the risks that climate change poses to towards maintaining a sustainable industry, in terms of productivity and health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forest_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-forest-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>There may be benefits and/or consequences to applying assisted migration on wide scale in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-test-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_83-3"><a href="#cite_note-sector-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forest_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-forest-85">[85]</a></sup> Assisted migration may prevent the extinction of certain tree species, enable and conserve market-based goods such as wood products, and conserve processes and services of an ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sector_83-4"><a href="#cite_note-sector-83">[83]</a></sup> Unfortunately, assisted migration could result in competition between the already established trees with the introduced trees, breeding of the introduced trees with established trees or the disruption of key ecological processes. Any decision made on assisted migration to be implemented in the forestry industry will need continued and rely on informed research and long-term studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-test-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forest_85-2"><a href="#cite_note-forest-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
