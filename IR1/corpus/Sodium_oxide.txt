<p><b>Sodium oxide</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the formula <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>.  It is used in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a>, though not in a raw form. It is the <a href="/wiki/Base_anhydride" title="Base anhydride">base anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, so when water is added to sodium oxide NaOH is produced.
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>O  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  →  2 NaOH</dd></dl>
<p>The alkali metal oxides M<sub>2</sub>O (M = Li, Na, K, Rb) crystallise in the antifluorite structure. In this motif the positions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> are reversed relative to their positions in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">CaF<sub>2</sub></a>, with sodium ions tetrahedrally coordinated to 4 oxide ions and oxide cubically coordinated to 8 sodium ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sodium oxide is produced by the reaction of sodium with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_peroxide" title="Sodium peroxide">sodium peroxide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_nitrite" title="Sodium nitrite">sodium nitrite</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaOH  +  2 Na   →  2 Na<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + 2 Na → 2 Na<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>2 NaNO<sub>2</sub>  +  6 Na  →  4 Na<sub>2</sub>O  +  N<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Most of these reactions rely on the reduction of something by sodium, whether it is hydroxide, peroxide, or nitrite.
</p><p>Burning sodium in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> will produce Na<sub>2</sub>O and about 20% <a href="/wiki/Sodium_peroxide" title="Sodium peroxide">sodium peroxide</a> Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<dl><dd>6 Na + 2 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Na<sub>2</sub>O + Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> can be heated to 851 °C, producing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and sodium oxide.
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_making">Glass making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Glass making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium oxide is a significant component of glasses and windows although it is added in the form of "soda" (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>). Sodium oxide does not explicitly exist in glasses, since glasses are complex cross-linked polymers. Typically, manufactured <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> contains around 15% sodium oxide, 70% silica (<a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>) and 9% lime (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>). The sodium carbonate "soda" serves as a flux to lower the temperature at which the silica melts. Soda glass has a much lower melting temperature than pure silica, and has slightly higher elasticity. These changes arise because the silicon dioxide and soda react to form sodium silicates of the general formula Na<sub>2</sub>[SiO<sub>2</sub>]<sub>x</sub>[SiO<sub>3</sub>].
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>Na<sub>2</sub>O + SiO<sub>2</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
