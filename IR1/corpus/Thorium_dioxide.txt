<p><b>Thorium dioxide</b> (ThO<sub>2</sub>), also called <b>thorium(IV) oxide</b>, is a crystalline solid, often white or yellow in color. Also known as <b>thoria</b>,  it is produced mainly as a by-product of <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamashita_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yamashita-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thorianite" title="Thorianite">Thorianite</a> is the name of the mineralogical form of thorium dioxide. It is moderately rare and crystallizes in an isometric system. The melting point of thorium oxide is 3300 °C – the highest of all known oxides. Only a few elements (including <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>) and a few compounds (including <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_carbide" title="Tantalum carbide">tantalum carbide</a>) have higher melting points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  All thorium compounds are radioactive because there are no stable <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_thorium" title="Isotopes of thorium">isotopes of thorium</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_reactions">Structure and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thoria exists as two polymorphs. One has a <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> crystal structure. This is uncommon among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary</a> dioxides (others with fluorite structure include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerium_dioxide" title="Cerium dioxide">cerium dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium_dioxide" title="Plutonium dioxide">plutonium dioxide</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of thoria is about 6 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a>. A tetragonal form of thoria is also known.
</p><p>Thorium dioxide is more stable than <a href="/wiki/Thorium_monoxide" title="Thorium monoxide">thorium monoxide</a> (ThO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Only with careful control of reaction conditions can oxidation of thorium metal give the monoxide rather than the dioxide. At extremely high temperatures, the dioxide can convert to the monoxide either in by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disproportionation_reaction" title="Disproportionation reaction">disproportionation reaction</a> (equilibrium with liquid thorium metal) above 1,850 K (1,580 °C; 2,870 °F) or by simple dissociation (evolution of oxygen) above 2,500 K (2,230 °C; 4,040 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fuels">Nuclear fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nuclear fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thorium dioxide (thoria) can be used in nuclear reactors as ceramic fuel pellets, typically contained in nuclear fuel rods clad with zirconium alloys.  Thorium is not fissile (but is "fertile", breeding fissile <a href="/wiki/Uranium-233" title="Uranium-233">uranium-233</a> under neutron bombardment); hence, it must be used as a nuclear reactor fuel in conjunction with fissile isotopes of either uranium or plutonium.  This can be achieved by blending thorium with uranium or plutonium, or using it in its pure form in conjunction with separate fuel rods containing uranium or plutonium.  Thorium dioxide offers advantages over conventional uranium dioxide fuel pellets, because of its higher thermal conductivity (lower operating temperature), considerably higher melting point, and chemical stability (does not oxidize in the presence of water/oxygen, unlike uranium dioxide).
</p><p>Thorium dioxide can be turned into a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear</a> fuel by breeding it into uranium-233 (see below and refer to the article on <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> for more information on this). The high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_stability" title="Thermal stability">thermal stability</a> of thorium dioxide allows applications in flame spraying and high-temperature ceramics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thorium dioxide is used as a stabilizer in <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> electrodes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten_inert_gas_welding" title="Tungsten inert gas welding">TIG welding</a>, electron tubes, and aircraft engines.  As an alloy, thoriated tungsten metal is not easily deformed because the high-fusion material thoria augments the high-temperature mechanical properties, and thorium helps stimulate the emission of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermion" title="Thermion">thermions</a>).  It is the most popular oxide additive because of its low cost, but is being phased out in favor of non-radioactive elements such as <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>.
</p><p>Thoria dispersed nickel finds its applications in various high temperature operations like combustion engines because it is a good creep resistant material. It can also be used for hydrogen trapping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysis">Catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thorium dioxide has almost no value as a commercial catalyst, but such applications have been well investigated.  It is a catalyst in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruzicka_large_ring_synthesis" title="Ruzicka large ring synthesis">Ruzicka large ring synthesis</a>.   Other applications that have been explored include <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">petroleum cracking</a>, conversion of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and preparation of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiocontrast_agents">Radiocontrast agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radiocontrast agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thorium dioxide was the primary ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Thorotrast" title="Thorotrast">Thorotrast</a>, a once-common <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast_agent" title="Radiocontrast agent">radiocontrast agent</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_angiography" title="Cerebral angiography">cerebral angiography</a>, however, it causes a rare form of cancer (hepatic <a href="/wiki/Angiosarcoma" title="Angiosarcoma">angiosarcoma</a>) many years after administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This use was replaced with injectable <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> or ingestable <a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate_suspension" title="Barium sulfate suspension">barium sulfate suspension</a> as standard <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> contrast agents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lamp_mantles">Lamp mantles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lamp mantles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another major use in the past was in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">gas mantle</a> of lanterns developed by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> in 1890, which are composed of 99 percent ThO<sub>2</sub> and 1% <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a>.  Even as late as the 1980s it was estimated that about half of all ThO<sub>2</sub> produced (several hundred tonnes per year) was used for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some mantles still use thorium, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a> (or sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconium_oxide" title="Zirconium oxide">zirconium oxide</a>) is used increasingly as a replacement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_manufacture">Glass manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thorium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Glass manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thorium dioxide was formerly added to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> during manufacture to increase their <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Thoriated_glass" title="Thoriated glass">thoriated glass</a> with up to 40% ThO<sub>2</sub> content. These glasses were used in the construction of high-quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_lens" title="Photographic lens">photographic lenses</a>. However, the radioactivity of the thorium caused both a safety and pollution hazard and self-degradation of the glass (turning it yellow or brown over time). <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_oxide" title="Lanthanum oxide">Lanthanum oxide</a> has replaced thorium dioxide in almost all modern high-index glasses.
</p>
