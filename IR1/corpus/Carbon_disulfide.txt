<p><b>Carbon disulfide</b> is a colorless <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> CS<sub>2</sub>. The compound is used frequently as a building block in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> as well as an industrial and chemical non-polar <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>. It has an "<a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">ether</a>-like" odor, but commercial samples are typically contaminated with foul-smelling impurities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Occurrence.2C_manufacture.2C_properties"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence,_manufacture,_properties">Occurrence, manufacture, properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence, manufacture, properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Small amounts of carbon disulfide are released by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> eruptions and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>. CS<sub>2</sub> once was manufactured by combining <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> at high temperatures.
</p>
<dl><dd>C + 2S → CS<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>A lower-temperature reaction, requiring only 600 °C, utilizes <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> as the carbon source in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 CH<sub>4</sub> + S<sub>8</sub> → 2 CS<sub>2</sub> + 4 H<sub>2</sub>S</dd></dl>
<p>The reaction is analogous to the combustion of methane.
</p><p>Global production/consumption of carbon disulfide is approximately one million tonnes, with China consuming 49%, followed by India at 13%, mostly for the production of rayon fiber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> United States production in 2007 was 56,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent">Solvent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solvent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon disulfide is a solvent for <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, sulfur, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  It has been used in the purification of single-walled carbon nanotubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CS<sub>2</sub> is highly flammable:
</p>
<dl><dd>CS<sub>2</sub> + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → CO<sub>2</sub> + 2 <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a></dd></dl>
<p>It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> but it is more reactive toward <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophiles</a> and more easily reduced. These differences in reactivity can be attributed to the weaker π donor-ability of the sulfido centers, which renders the carbon more electrophilic.  Amines afford <a href="/wiki/Sodium_diethyldithiocarbamate" title="Sodium diethyldithiocarbamate">dithiocarbamates</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 R<sub>2</sub>NH + CS<sub>2</sub> → [R<sub>2</sub>NH<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>][R<sub>2</sub>NCS<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>]</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Xanthate" title="Xanthate">Xanthates</a> form similarly from <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>RONa + CS<sub>2</sub> → [Na<sup>+</sup>][ROCS<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>]</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is the basis of the manufacture of regenerated <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, the main ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Viscose" title="Viscose">viscose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a>. Both xanthates and the related <a href="/wiki/Thioxanthate" title="Thioxanthate">thioxanthates</a> (derived from treatment of CS<sub>2</sub> with sodium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thiolate" title="Thiolate">thiolates</a>) are used as flotation agents in mineral processing.
</p><p>Sodium sulfide affords <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trithiocarbonate" title="Trithiocarbonate">trithiocarbonate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>S + CS<sub>2</sub> → [Na<sup>+</sup>]<sub>2</sub>[CS<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>]</dd></dl>
<p>Carbon disulfide does not hydrolyze readily, although the process is catalyzed by an enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide_hydrolase" title="Carbon disulfide hydrolase">carbon disulfide hydrolase</a>.
</p><p>Reduction of carbon disulfide with sodium affords <a href="/wiki/Sodium_1,3-dithiole-2-thione-4,5-dithiolate" title="Sodium 1,3-dithiole-2-thione-4,5-dithiolate">sodium 1,3-dithiole-2-thione-4,5-dithiolate</a> together with sodium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trithiocarbonate" title="Trithiocarbonate">trithiocarbonate</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Na  +  4 CS<sub>2</sub>   →   Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>3</sub>S<sub>5</sub>  +   Na<sub>2</sub>CS<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chlorination">Chlorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chlorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorination_reaction" title="Chlorination reaction">Chlorination</a> of CS<sub>2</sub> is the principal route to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>CS<sub>2</sub> + 3 <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl<sub>2</sub></a> → CCl<sub>4</sub> + <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_dichloride" title="Disulfur dichloride">S<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub></a></dd></dl>
<p>This conversion proceeds via the intermediacy of <a href="/wiki/Thiophosgene" title="Thiophosgene">thiophosgene</a>, CSCl<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_chemistry">Coordination chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Coordination chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CS<sub>2</sub> is a ligand for many metal complexes, forming pi complexes. One example is <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">Cp</a>Co(<a href="/wiki/Hapticity" title="Hapticity">η<sup>2</sup></a>-CS<sub>2</sub>)(P<a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">Me</a><sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymerization">Polymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CS<sub>2</sub> polymerizes upon photolysis or under high pressure to give an insoluble material called "Bridgman's black", named after the discoverer of the polymer, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Williams_Bridgman" title="Percy Williams Bridgman">Percy Williams Bridgman</a>. Trithiocarbonate (-S-C(S)-S-) linkages comprise, in part, the backbone of the polymer, which is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal industrial uses of carbon disulfide, consuming 75% of the annual production, are the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscose_rayon" title="Viscose rayon">viscose rayon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellophane" title="Cellophane">cellophane</a> film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also a valued intermediate in chemical synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>.  It is widely used in the synthesis of organosulfur compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Metam_sodium" title="Metam sodium">metam sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xanthate" title="Xanthate">xanthates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dithiocarbamates" title="Dithiocarbamates">dithiocarbamates</a>, which are used in extractive metallurgy and rubber chemistry.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It can be used in <a href="/wiki/Fumigation" title="Fumigation">fumigation</a> of airtight storage warehouses, airtight flat storages, bins, grain elevators, railroad box cars, shipholds, barges and cereal mills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Carbon disulfide is also used as an insecticide for the fumigation of grains, nursery stock, in fresh fruit conservation and as a soil disinfectant against insects and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nematodes" title="Nematodes">nematodes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_disulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon disulfide is highly toxic. It has been linked to both acute and chronic forms of poisoning.<a class="external autonumber" href="https://www.atsdr.cdc.gov/phs/phs.asp?id=472&amp;tid=84#bookmark05" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>  To identify the effects of carbon disulfide, it is necessary to confirm exposure, compatible signs and symptoms, and exclude other health conditions, as its effects are non-specific. Typical recommended <a href="/wiki/Threshold_limit_value" title="Threshold limit value">TLV</a> is 30 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, 10 ppm. Symptoms include tingling or numbness, "cramps, muscle weakness, pain, distal sensory loss, and neurophysiological impairment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chemical_hazard" title="Chemical hazard">Occupational exposure</a> to carbon disulfide is associated with <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, in particular: <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SBU2017_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-SBU2017-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
