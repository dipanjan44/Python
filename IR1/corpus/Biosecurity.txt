<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Biosecurity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Biosafety" title="Biosafety">Biosafety</a>.</div>
<p><b>Biosecurity</b> has multiple meanings and is defined differently according to various disciplines. The original definition of biosecurity started out as a set of preventive measures designed to reduce the risk of transmission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> in crops and livestock, quarantined pests, <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive alien species</a>, and living <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">modified organisms</a> (Koblentz, 2010). The emerging nature of biosecurity threats means that small scale risks blow up rapidly, thus an effective policy becomes a challenge for there are limitations on time and resources available for analysing threats and estimating the likelihood of their occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term was first used by the agricultural and environmental communities. Starting from the late 1990s in response to the threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_terrorism" title="Biological terrorism">biological terrorism</a>, biosecurity encompasses the prevention of the intentional removal (theft) of biological materials from research laboratories. These preventative measures are a combination of systems and practices put into its place at bioscience laboratories to prevent the use of dangerous pathogens and toxins for malicious use, as well as by customs agents and agricultural and natural resource managers to prevent the spread of these <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological agents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencemag.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencemag.org-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Advances in technology have meant that many civilian research projects in medicine have the potential to be used in military applications (<a href="/wiki/Dual-use_technology" title="Dual-use technology">dual-use research</a>) and biosecurity protocols are used to prevent dangerous biological materials from falling into the hands of malevolent parties. The <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> define biosecurity as "security against the inadvertent, inappropriate, or intentional malicious or malevolent use of potentially dangerous biological agents or <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>, including the development, production, stockpiling, or use of biological weapons as well as outbreaks of newly emergent and epidemic disease". Biosecurity requires the cooperation of scientists, technicians, policy makers, security engineers, and law enforcement officials.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_international_security_issue">As international security issue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: As international security issue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Controversial experiments in <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic biology</a>, including the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">poliovirus</a> from its genetic sequence, and the modification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H5N1" title="H5N1">H5N1</a> for airborne transmission in mammals, have led to calls for tighter controls on the materials and information used to perform similar feats.  Ideas include better enforcement by national governments and private entities concerning shipments and downloads of such materials, and registration or background check requirements for anyone handling such materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Initially, health security or biosecurity issues have not been considered as an international security issue especially in the traditional view of international relations. However, some changes in trend have contributed to inclusion of biosecurity (health security) in discussions of security (Koblentz, 2010).
</p><p>As time progressed, there was a movement towards securitization. Non-traditional security issues such as climate change, organized crime, terrorism, and landmines came to be included in the definition of international security (Koblentz, 2010). There was a general realization that the actors in the international system not only involved nation-states but also included international organizations, institutions, and individuals (Koblentz, 2010). Therefore, ensuring the security of various actors within each nation became an important agenda. Biosecurity is one of the issues to be securitized under this trend. In fact, on January 10, 2000, the UN Security Council convened to discuss HIV/AIDS as a security issue in Africa and designated it a threat in the following month. The UNDP <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> also recognize health issues as international security issue (Koblentz, 2010). 
Several instances of epidemics that followed such as SARS increased awareness of health security (biosecurity). Recently several factors have rendered biosecurity issues more severe. There is a continuing advancement of biotechnology which increases the possibility for malevolent use, evolution of infectious diseases, and globalizing force which is making the world more interdependent and more susceptible to spread of epidemics (Koblentz, 2010).
</p><p>Some uncertainties about the policy implementation for biosecurity remain for future. In order to carefully plan out preventative policies, policy makers need to be able to somewhat predict the probability and assess the risks; however, as the uncertain nature of the biosecurity issue goes it is largely difficult to predict and also involves a complex process as it requires a multidisciplinary approach(Koblentz, 2010). The policy choices they make to address an immediate threat could pose another threat in the future, facing an unintended trade-off. Policy makers are also constantly looking for a more effective way to coordinate international actors- governmental organizations and NGOs- and actors from different nations so that they could tackle the problem of resource overlap (Koblentz, 2010).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_program">Laboratory program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Laboratory program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Components of a laboratory biosecurity program include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Physical_security" title="Physical security">Physical security</a></li>
<li>Personnel security</li>
<li>Material control &amp; <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">accountability</a></li>
<li>Transport security</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">Information security</a></li>
<li>Program management</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal">Animal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Animal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Animal biosecurity is the product of all actions undertaken by an entity to prevent introduction of disease agents into a specific area. Animal biosecurity differs from biosecurity which are measures taken to reduce the risk of infectious agent theft and dispersal by means of bioterrorism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup>
Animal biosecurity is a comprehensive approach, encompassing different means of prevention and containment. A critical element in animal biosecurity, biocontainment, is the control of disease agents already present in a particular area, and works to prevent novel transmissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Animal biosecurity may protect organisms from infectious agents or noninfectious agents such as toxins or pollutants, and can be executed in areas as large as a nation or as small as a local farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Animal biosecurity takes into account the epidemiological triad for disease occurrence: the individual host, the disease, and the environment in contributing to disease susceptibility. It aims to improve nonspecific immunity of the host to resist the introduction of an agent, or limit the risk that an agent will be sustained in an environment at adequate levels. Biocontainment, an element of animal biosecurity, works to improve specific immunity towards already present pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biosecurity means the prevention of the illicit use of pathogenic bioorganisms by laboratory staff or others.
Biosafety means the protection of laboratory staff from being infected by pathogenic bioorganisms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_countermeasures">Medical countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medical countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_countermeasure" title="Medical countermeasure">Medical countermeasures</a> ("MCMs") are products such as biologics and pharmaceutical drugs that can protect from or treat the effects of a chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear ("CBRN") attack. MCMs can also be used for prevention and diagnosis of symptoms associated with CBRN attacks or threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fdamcm_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fdamcm-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The FDA runs a program called the FDA Medical Countermeasures Initiative ("MCMi"). The program helps support "partner"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup> agencies and organizations prepare for public health emergencies that could require MCMs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fdamcm_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-fdamcm-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The federal government provides funding for MCM-related programs. In June 2016, a Senate Appropriations subcommittee approved a bill that would continue funding four specific medical countermeasure programs:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>$512 million for the <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_Advanced_Research_and_Development_Authority" title="Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority">Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority</a> (BARDA)</li>
<li>$510 million for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BioShield_Project" title="BioShield Project">BioShield</a> Special Reserve Fund (SRF)</li>
<li>$575 million for the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_National_Stockpile" title="Strategic National Stockpile">Strategic National Stockpile</a> (SNS)</li>
<li>$72 million for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandemic_influenza" title="Pandemic influenza">pandemic influenza</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The destruction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973-2001)" title="World Trade Center (1973-2001)">World Trade Center</a> in Manhattan on <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11, 2001</a> by terrorists and subsequent wave of <a href="/wiki/2001_anthrax_attacks" title="2001 anthrax attacks">anthrax attacks</a> on U.S. media and government outlets (both real and <a href="/wiki/Anthrax_hoaxes" title="Anthrax hoaxes">hoax</a>) led to increased attention on the risk of bioterrorism attacks in the United States. Proposals for serious structural reforms, national and/or regional border controls, and a single co-ordinated system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biohazard" title="Biohazard">biohazard</a> response abounded.
</p><p>One of the major challenges of biosecurity is that harmful technology is becoming more available and accessible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Biomedical advances and the globalization of scientific and technical expertise have made it possible to greatly improve public health. However, there is also the risk that these advances can make it easier for terrorists to produce biological weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The proliferation of high biosafety level laboratories around the world has many experts worried about availability of targets for those that might be interested in stealing dangerous pathogens. Emerging and re-emerging disease is also a serious biosecurity concern. The recent growth in containment laboratories is often in response to emerging diseases, many new containment labs' main focus is to find ways to control these diseases. By strengthening national disease surveillance, prevention, control and response systems, these labs are raising international public health to new heights.
</p><p>Research into biosecurity &amp; biosafety conducted by the United Nations University Institute for the Advanced Study of Sustainability (UNU-IAS)  emphasizes "long-term consequences of the development and use of <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>" and need for "an honest broker to create avenues and forums to unlock the impasses."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In the October 2011 Bio-Response Report Card, the WMD Center stated that the major challenges to biosecurity are:
</p>
<ul><li>attribution</li>
<li>communication</li>
<li>detection and diagnosis</li>
<li>environmental cleanup</li>
<li>medical countermeasure availability</li>
<li>medical countermeasure development and approval process</li>
<li>medical countermeasure dispensing</li>
<li>medical management</li></ul>
<p>Communication between the citizen and law enforcement officials is imperative. Indicators of agro-terrorism at a food processing plant may include persons taking notes or photos of a business,theft of employee uniforms,employees changing working hours,or persons attempting to gain information about security measures and personnel. Unusual activity should be  reported to law enforcement personnel promptly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Communication between policymakers and life sciences scientists is also important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The MENA region, with its socio-political unrest, diverse cultures and societies, and recent biological weapons programs, faces particular challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_education">Role of education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Role of education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The advance of the life sciences and biotechnology has the potential to bring great benefits to humankind through responding to societal challenges. However, it is also possible that such advances could be exploited for hostile purposes, something evidenced in a small number of incidents of bioterrorism, but more particularly by the series of large-scale offensive <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> programmes carried out by major states in the last century. Dealing with this challenge, which has been labelled the 'dual-use' dilemma requires a number of different activities such as those identified above as being require for biosecurity. However, one of the essential ingredients in ensuring that the life sciences continue to generate great benefits and do not become subject to misuse for hostile purposes is a process of engagement between scientists and the security community and the development of strong ethical and normative frameworks to compliment legal and regulatory measures that are being developed by states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosecurity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="279" data-file-width="400" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg/200px-Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg/300px-Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vervoersverbod_sign.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Biosecurity sign for use on a farm or agricultural area experiencing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swine_fever" title="Swine fever">swine fever</a> (Dutch example).</div></div></div>
<ul><li>US Select Agent Regulations
<ul><li>Facility registration if it possesses one of 81 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_Agent" title="Select Agent">Select Agents</a></li>
<li>Facility must designate a Responsible Official</li>
<li>Background checks for individuals with access to Select Agents</li>
<li>Access controls for areas and containers that contain Select Agents</li>
<li>Detailed inventory requirements for Select Agents</li>
<li>Security, safety, and emergency response plans</li>
<li>Safety and security training</li>
<li>Regulation of transfers of Select Agents</li>
<li>Extensive documentation and recordkeeping</li>
<li>Safety and security inspections</li></ul></li>
<li>Biological Weapons Convention addresses three relevant issues:
<ul><li>National Implementing Legislation</li>
<li>National Pathogen Security (biosecurity)</li>
<li>International Cooperation</li>
<li>States Parties agree to pursue national implementation of laboratory and transportation biosecurity (2003)</li></ul></li>
<li>UN 1540
<ul><li>urges States to take preventative measures to mitigate the threat of WMD proliferation by non-state actors</li>
<li>"Take and enforce effective measures to establish domestic controls to prevent the proliferation of ... biological weapons ...; including by establishing appropriate controls over related materials"</li></ul></li>
<li>European Commission Green Paper on Bio-Preparedness (November 2007)
<ul><li>recommends developing European standards on laboratory biosecurity including Physical protection, access controls, accountability of pathogens, and registration of researchers</li></ul></li>
<li>Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development
<ul><li>published "Best Practice Guidelines for Biological Resource Centers" including a section on biosecurity in February 2007</li></ul></li>
<li>Kampala Compact (October 2005) and the Nairobi Announcement (July 2007)
<ul><li>stress importance of implementing laboratory biosafety and biosecurity in Africa</li></ul></li></ul>
