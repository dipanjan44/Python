<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Molecular geometry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_molecule_dimensions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="363" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Water_molecule_dimensions.svg/200px-Water_molecule_dimensions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Water_molecule_dimensions.svg/300px-Water_molecule_dimensions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Water_molecule_dimensions.svg/400px-Water_molecule_dimensions.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_molecule_dimensions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geometry of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water molecule</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Molecular geometry</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">three-dimensional</a> arrangement of the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> that constitute a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>. It includes the general shape of the molecule as well as <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond lengths</a>, bond angles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torsional_angle" title="Torsional angle">torsional angles</a> and any other geometrical parameters that determine the position of each atom.
</p><p>Molecular geometry influences several properties of a substance including its <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polarity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase of matter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_activity" title="Biological activity">biological activity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-article0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-article0-3">[3]</a></sup> The angles between bonds that an atom forms depend only weakly on the rest of molecule, i.e. they can be understood as approximately local and hence <a href="/wiki/Transferability_(chemistry)" title="Transferability (chemistry)">transferable properties</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination">Determination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Determination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The molecular geometry can be determined by various <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic methods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> methods. <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">IR</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">microwave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">Raman spectroscopy</a> can give information about the molecule geometry from the details of the vibrational and rotational absorbance detected by these techniques. <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_diffraction" title="Neutron diffraction">neutron diffraction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron_diffraction" title="Electron diffraction">electron diffraction</a> can give molecular structure for crystalline solids based on the distance between nuclei and concentration of electron density. <a href="/wiki/Gas_electron_diffraction" title="Gas electron diffraction">Gas electron diffraction</a> can be used for small molecules in the gas phase. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a> and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B6rster_resonance_energy_transfer" title="Förster resonance energy transfer">FRET</a> methods can be used to determine complementary information including relative distances,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
dihedral angles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
angles, and connectivity.  Molecular geometries are best determined at low temperature because at higher temperatures the molecular structure is averaged over more accessible geometries (see next section). Larger molecules often exist in multiple stable geometries (<a href="/wiki/Conformational_isomerism" title="Conformational isomerism">conformational isomerism</a>) that are close in energy on the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy_surface" title="Potential energy surface">potential energy surface</a>.  Geometries can also be computed by <a href="/wiki/Ab_initio_quantum_chemistry_methods" title="Ab initio quantum chemistry methods">ab initio quantum chemistry methods</a> to high accuracy.  The molecular geometry can be different as a solid, in solution, and as a gas.
</p><p>The position of each atom is determined by the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a> by which it is connected to its neighboring atoms. The molecular geometry can be described by the positions of these atoms in space, evoking <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond lengths</a> of two joined atoms, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_angle" title="Bond angle">bond angles</a> of three connected atoms, and <a href="/wiki/Torsion_of_a_curve" title="Torsion of a curve">torsion angles</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dihedral_angle" title="Dihedral angle">dihedral angles</a>) of three <a href="/wiki/Path_graph" title="Path graph">consecutive</a> bonds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_influence_of_thermal_excitation">The influence of thermal excitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The influence of thermal excitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the motions of the atoms in a molecule are determined by quantum mechanics, one
must define "motion" in a quantum mechanical way. The overall (external) quantum mechanical motions translation and rotation hardly change the geometry of the molecule. (To some extent rotation influences
the geometry via <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis forces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">centrifugal distortion</a>, but this is negligible for the present discussion.)
In addition to translation and rotation, a third type of motion is <a href="/wiki/Molecular_vibration" title="Molecular vibration">molecular vibration</a>, which corresponds to internal motions of the atoms such as bond stretching and bond angle variation. The molecular vibrations are <a href="/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator" title="Quantum harmonic oscillator">harmonic</a> (at least to good approximation), and the atoms oscillate about their equilibrium positions, even at the absolute zero of temperature. At absolute zero all atoms are in their vibrational ground state and show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_point_energy" title="Zero point energy">zero point quantum mechanical motion</a>, so that the wavefunction of a single vibrational mode is not a sharp peak, but an exponential of finite width (the wavefunction for <i>n</i> = 0 depicted in the article on the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator" title="Quantum harmonic oscillator">quantum harmonic oscillator</a>).  At higher temperatures the vibrational modes may be thermally excited (in a classical interpretation one expresses this by stating that "the molecules will vibrate faster"), but they oscillate still around the recognizable geometry of the molecule.

Thus, at room temperature less than 0.07 percent of all the molecules of a given amount of water will vibrate faster than at absolute zero.
</p><p>As stated above, rotation hardly influences the molecular geometry. But, as a quantum mechanical motion, it is thermally excited at relatively (as compared to vibration) low temperatures. From a classical point of view it can be stated that at higher temperatures more molecules will rotate faster,
which implies that they have higher <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. In quantum mechanical language: more eigenstates of higher angular momentum become <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution" title="Boltzmann distribution">thermally populated</a> with rising temperatures. Typical rotational excitation energies are on the order of a few cm<sup>−1</sup>. The results of many spectroscopic experiments are broadened because they involve an averaging over rotational states. It is often difficult to extract geometries from spectra at high temperatures, because the number of rotational states probed in the experimental averaging increases with increasing temperature.  Thus, many spectroscopic observations can only be expected to yield reliable molecular geometries at temperatures close to absolute zero, because at higher temperatures too many higher rotational states are thermally populated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding">Bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molecules, by definition, are most often held together with <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> involving single, double, and/or triple bonds, where a "bond" is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shared_pair" title="Shared pair">shared pair</a> of electrons (the other method of bonding between atoms is called <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">ionic bonding</a> and involves a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> and a negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>).
</p><p>Molecular geometries can be specified in terms of <b>bond lengths</b>, <b>bond angles</b> and <b>torsional angles</b>. The bond length is defined to be the average distance between the nuclei of two atoms bonded together in any given molecule. A bond angle is the angle formed between three atoms across at least two bonds. For four atoms bonded together in a chain, the <a href="/wiki/Dihedral_angle" title="Dihedral angle">torsional angle</a> is the angle between the plane formed by the first three atoms and the plane formed by the last three atoms.
</p><p>There exists a mathematical relationship among the bond angles for one central atom and four peripheral atoms (labeled 1 through 4) expressed by the following determinant.  This constraint removes one degree of freedom from the choices of (originally) six free bond angles to leave only five choices of bond angles.  (Note that the angles <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \theta _{11}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">

<p>Molecular geometry is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical</a> behavior of the electrons. Using the <a href="/wiki/Valence_bond_theory" title="Valence bond theory">valence bond approximation</a> this can be understood by the type of bonds between the atoms that make up the molecule. When atoms interact to form a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a>, the atomic orbitals of each atom are said to combine in a process called <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridisation" title="Orbital hybridisation">orbital hybridisation</a>.  The two most common types of bonds are <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">sigma bonds</a> (usually formed by hybrid orbitals) and <a href="/wiki/Pi_bond" title="Pi bond">pi bonds</a> (formed by unhybridized p orbitals for atoms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_group_element" title="Main group element">main group elements</a>).  The geometry can also be understood by <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital_theory" title="Molecular orbital theory">molecular orbital theory</a> where the electrons are delocalised.
</p><p>An understanding of the wavelike behavior of electrons in atoms and molecules is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chemistry" title="Quantum chemistry">quantum chemistry</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isomers">Isomers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isomers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">Isomers</a> are types of molecules that share a chemical formula but have different geometries, resulting in different properties:
</p>
<ul><li>A <b>pure</b> substance is composed of only one type of isomer of a molecule (all have the same geometrical structure).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_isomerism" title="Structural isomerism">Structural isomers</a> have the same chemical formula but different physical arrangements, often forming alternate molecular geometries with very different properties. The atoms are not bonded (connected) together in the same orders.
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functional_isomer" title="Functional isomer">Functional isomers</a> are special kinds of structural isomers, where certain groups of atoms exhibit a special kind of behavior, such as an ether or an alcohol.</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" title="Stereoisomer">Stereoisomers</a> may have many similar physicochemical properties (melting point, boiling point) and at the same time very different <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> activities. This is because they exhibit a <a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">handedness</a> that is commonly found in living systems. One manifestation of this <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chirality</a> or handedness is that they have the ability to rotate polarized light in different directions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Protein_folding" title="Protein folding">Protein folding</a> concerns the complex geometries and different isomers that <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> can take.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_molecular_structure">Types of molecular structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types of molecular structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A bond angle is the geometric angle between two adjacent bonds. Some common shapes of simple molecules include:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Linear_molecular_geometry" title="Linear molecular geometry">Linear</a>:</b> In a linear model, atoms are connected in a straight line. The bond angles are set at 180°. For example, carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> have a linear molecular shape.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Trigonal_planar_molecular_geometry" title="Trigonal planar molecular geometry">Trigonal planar</a>:</b> Molecules with the trigonal planar shape are somewhat triangular and in one <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane (flat)</a>.  Consequently, the bond angles are set at 120°. For example, <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bent_molecular_geometry" title="Bent molecular geometry">Angular</a>:</b> Angular molecules (also called <i>bent</i> or <i>V-shaped</i>) have a non-linear shape. For example, water (H<sub>2</sub>O), which has an angle of about 105°. A water molecule has two pairs of bonded electrons and two unshared lone pairs.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">Tetrahedral</a>:</b> <i>Tetra-</i> signifies four, and <i>-hedral</i> relates to a face of a solid, so "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral" title="Tetrahedral">tetrahedral</a>" literally means "having four faces".  This shape is found when there are <a href="/wiki/Star_(graph_theory)" title="Star (graph theory)">four bonds all on one central atom</a>, with no extra unshared <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> pairs.  In accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSEPR" title="VSEPR">VSEPR</a> (valence-shell electron pair repulsion theory), the bond angles between the electron bonds are <a href="/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions" title="Inverse trigonometric functions">arccos</a>(−1/3) = 109.47°. For example, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) is a tetrahedral molecule.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Octahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Octahedral molecular geometry">Octahedral</a>:</b> <i>Octa-</i> signifies eight, and <i>-hedral</i> relates to a face of a solid, so "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral" title="Octahedral">octahedral</a>" means "having eight faces". The bond angle is 90 degrees. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">sulfur hexafluoride</a> (SF<sub>6</sub>) is an octahedral molecule.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal_molecular_geometry" title="Trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry">Trigonal pyramidal</a>:</b> A trigonal pyramidal molecule has a <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry)" title="Pyramid (geometry)">pyramid-like shape</a> with a triangular base.  Unlike the linear and trigonal planar shapes but similar to the tetrahedral orientation, pyramidal shapes require three dimensions in order to fully separate the electrons.  Here, there are only three pairs of bonded electrons, leaving one unshared lone pair. Lone pair – bond pair repulsions change the bond angle from the tetrahedral angle to a slightly lower value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (NH<sub>3</sub>).</li></ul>

<p>The greater the amount of lone pairs contained in a molecule the smaller the angles between the atoms of that molecule. The <a href="/wiki/VSEPR_theory" title="VSEPR theory">VSEPR theory</a> predicts that lone pairs repel each other, thus pushing the different atoms away from them.
