<p>A <b>coccolithophore</b> (or <b>coccolithophorid</b>, from the adjective<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">alga</a>). They belong either to the kingdom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protista" title="Protista">Protista</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Whittaker" title="Robert Whittaker">Robert Whittaker</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">Five kingdom classification</a>, or clade <a href="/wiki/Hacrobia" title="Hacrobia">Hacrobia</a>, according to the newer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_Classification" title="Biological Classification">biological classification system</a>. Within the Hacrobia, the coccolithophorids are in the <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Division_(botany)" title="Division (botany)">division</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haptophyta" title="Haptophyta">Haptophyta</a>, class <a href="/wiki/Prymnesiophyceae" title="Prymnesiophyceae">Prymnesiophyceae</a> (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccolithophyceae" title="Coccolithophyceae">Coccolithophyceae</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hay1967_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hay1967-3">[3]</a></sup> Coccolithophorids are distinguished by special <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> plates (or scales) of uncertain function called <i><a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a></i>, which are also important <a href="/wiki/Micropaleontology" title="Micropaleontology">microfossils</a>. However, there are Prymnesiophyceae species lacking coccoliths (e.g. in genus <i><a href="/wiki/Prymnesium" title="Prymnesium">Prymnesium</a></i>), so not every member of Prymnesiophyceae is coccolithophorid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Coccolithophores are almost exclusively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> and are found in large numbers throughout the <a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">sunlight zone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.
</p><p>The most abundant species of coccolithophore, <i><a href="/wiki/Emiliania_huxleyi" title="Emiliania huxleyi">Emiliania huxleyi</a></i>, belongs to the order <a href="/wiki/Isochrysidales" title="Isochrysidales">Isochrysidales</a> and family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No%C3%ABlaerhabdaceae" title="Noëlaerhabdaceae">Noëlaerhabdaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hay1967_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hay1967-3">[3]</a></sup> It is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foissner2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foissner2006-5">[5]</a></sup> This makes <i>E. huxleyi</i> an important part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planktonic" title="Planktonic">planktonic</a> base of a large proportion of marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_webs" title="Food webs">food webs</a>. It is also the fastest growing coccolithophore in laboratory cultures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buitenhuis2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buitenhuis2008-6">[6]</a></sup> It is studied for the extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phytoplankton_bloom" title="Phytoplankton bloom">blooms</a> it forms in nutrient depleted waters after the reformation of the summer <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egge1992_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egge1992-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hup.harvard.edu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hup.harvard.edu-8">[8]</a></sup> and for its production of molecules known as <a href="/wiki/Alkenone" title="Alkenone">alkenones</a> that are commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth scientists</a> as a means to estimate past <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betaleb1999_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Betaleb1999-9">[9]</a></sup> Coccolithophores are of particular interest to those studying global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> because as <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidity</a> increases, their coccoliths may become even more important as a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2012_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2012-10">[10]</a></sup> Furthermore, management strategies are being employed to prevent eutrophication-related coccolithophore blooms, as these blooms lead to a decrease in nutrient flow to lower levels of the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yunev2007_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yunev2007-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="450" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg/220px-Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg/330px-Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg/440px-Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coccolithus_pelagicus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Coccolithus" title="Coccolithus">Coccolithus</a> pelagicus</i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Coccolithophores are spherical cells about 5–100 micrometres across, enclosed by calcareous plates called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccoliths" title="Coccoliths">coccoliths</a>, which are about 2–25 micrometres across. Each cell contains two brown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroplasts" title="Chloroplasts">chloroplasts</a> which surround the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moheimani2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moheimani2012-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Exoskeleton_.28coccosphere.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Exoskeleton_(coccosphere)">Exoskeleton (coccosphere)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exoskeleton (coccosphere)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each unicellular plankton is enclosed in its own collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccoliths" title="Coccoliths">coccoliths</a>, the calcified scales, which make up its <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> or coccosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Falkowski2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Falkowski2007-13">[13]</a></sup> The coccoliths are created inside the cell and while some species maintain a single layer throughout life only producing new coccoliths as the cell grows, others continually produce and shed coccoliths.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The primary constituent of coccoliths is calcium carbonate, or <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>. Calcium carbonate is transparent, so the organisms’ photosynthetic activity is not compromised by encapsulation in a coccosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogan2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan2009-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Coccoliths are produced by a <a href="/wiki/Biomineralization" title="Biomineralization">biomineralization</a> process known as coccolithogenesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moheimani2012_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moheimani2012-12">[12]</a></sup>  Generally, calcification of coccoliths occurs in the presence of light, and these scales are produced much more during the exponential phase of growth than the stationary phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linschooten1991_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Linschooten1991-15">[15]</a></sup> Although not yet entirely understood, the biomineralization process is tightly regulated by <a href="/wiki/Calcium_signaling" title="Calcium signaling">calcium signaling</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">Calcite</a> formation begins in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golgi_complex" title="Golgi complex">golgi complex</a> where protein templates nucleate the formation of CaCO<sub>3</sub> crystals and complex acidic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a> control the shape and growth of these crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deVargas2007_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-deVargas2007-16">[16]</a></sup>  As each scale is produced, it is exported in a Golgi-derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicle_(biology)" title="Vesicle (biology)">vesicle</a> and added to the inner surface of the coccosphere. This means that the most recently produced coccoliths may lie beneath older coccoliths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2003_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young2003-17">[17]</a></sup>
Depending upon the phytoplankton’s stage in the life cycle, two different types of coccoliths may be formed.  Holococcoliths are produced only in the haploid phase, lack radial symmetry, and are composed of anywhere from hundreds to thousands of similar minute (ca 0.1 µm) rhombic <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> crystals.  These crystals are thought to form at least partially outside the cell.  Heterococcoliths occur only in the diploid phase, have radial symmetry, and are composed of relatively few complex crystal units (less than 100).  Although they are rare, combination coccospheres, which contain both holococcoliths and heterococcoliths, have been observed in the plankton recording coccolithophore life cycle transitions.  Finally, the coccospheres of some species are highly modified with various appendages made of specialized coccoliths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While the exact function of the coccosphere is unclear, many potential functions have been proposed.  Most obviously coccoliths may protect the phytoplankton from  predators. In addition, these exoskeletons may confer an advantage in energy production, as coccolithogenesis seems highly coupled with photosynthesis. Organic precipitation of calcium carbonate from bicarbonate solution produces free carbon dioxide directly within the cellular body of the alga, this additional source of gas is then available to the Coccolithophore for photosynthesis.  It has been suggested that they may provide a cell-wall like barrier to isolate intracellular chemistry from the marine environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Westbroek1983_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Westbroek1983-19">[19]</a></sup> More specific, defensive properties of coccoliths may include protection from osmotic changes, chemical or mechanical shock, and short-wavelength light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan2012_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan2012-20">[20]</a></sup> It has also been proposed that the added weight of multiple layers of coccoliths allows the organism to sink to lower, more nutrient rich layers of the water and conversely, that coccoliths add buoyancy, stopping the cell from sinking to dangerous depths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irie2010_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irie2010-21">[21]</a></sup> Coccolith appendages have also been proposed to serve several functions, such as inhibiting grazing by zooplankton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Coccoliths are the main component  of the Chalk, a Late Cretaceous rock formation which outcrops widely in southern England and forms the <a href="/wiki/White_Cliffs_of_Dover" title="White Cliffs of Dover">White Cliffs of Dover</a>, and of other similar rocks in many other parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hup.harvard.edu_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hup.harvard.edu-8">[8]</a></sup> At the present day sedimented coccoliths are a major component of the calcareous oozes that cover up to 35% of the ocean floor and is kilometres thick in places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deVargas2007_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-deVargas2007-16">[16]</a></sup>  Because of their abundance and wide geographic ranges, the coccoliths which make up the layers of this ooze and the chalky sediment formed as it is compacted serve as valuable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfossils" title="Microfossils">microfossils</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellular_anatomy">Cellular anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cellular anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Enclosed in each coccosphere is a single cell with <a href="/wiki/Membrane" title="Membrane">membrane</a> bound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a>.  Two large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroplasts" title="Chloroplasts">chloroplasts</a> with brown <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> are located on either side of the cell and surround the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">golgi apparatus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>, and other organelles.  Each cell also has two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flagella" title="Flagella">flagellar</a> structures, which are involved not only in motility, but also in <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> and formation of the <a href="/wiki/Cytoskeleton" title="Cytoskeleton">cytoskeleton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billard2004_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billard2004-22">[22]</a></sup>  In some species, a functional or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vestigial" title="Vestigial">vestigial</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haptonema" title="Haptonema">haptonema</a> is also present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan2012_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan2012-20">[20]</a></sup> This structure, which is unique to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haptophytes" title="Haptophytes">haptophytes</a>, coils and uncoils in response to environmental stimuli.  Although poorly understood, it has been proposed to be involved in prey capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billard2004_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Billard2004-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_history_strategy">Life history strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Life history strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The life cycle of coccolithophores is characterized by an alternation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> phases. They alternate from the haploid to diploid phase through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syngamy" title="Syngamy">syngamy</a> and from diploid to haploid through <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a>. In contrast with most organisms with alternating life cycles, asexual reproduction by mitosis is possible in both phases of the life cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2003_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young2003-17">[17]</a></sup> Both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abiotic" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biotic_factors" title="Biotic factors">biotic factors</a> may affect the frequency with which each phase occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vardi2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vardi2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coccolithophores <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">reproduce asexually</a> through <a href="/wiki/Fission_(biology)" title="Fission (biology)">binary fission.</a> In this process the coccoliths from the parent cell are divided between the two daughter cells. There have been suggestions stating the possible presence of a sexual reproduction process due to the diploid stages of the coccolithophores, but this process has never been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdan2006_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houdan2006-24">[24]</a></sup>
K or r- selected strategies of coccolithophores depend on their life cycle stage. When coccolithophores are diploid, they are r-selected. In this phase they tolerate a wider range of nutrient compositions.  When they are haploid they are K- selected and are often more competitive in stable low nutrient environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdan2006_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Houdan2006-24">[24]</a></sup> Most coccolithophores are K strategist and are usually found on nutrient-poor surface waters. They are poor competitors when compared to other phytoplankton and thrive in habitats where other phytoplankton would not survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogan2009_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan2009-14">[14]</a></sup>
These two stages in the life cycle of coccolithophores occur seasonally, where more nutrition is available in warmer seasons and less is available in cooler seasons. This type of life cycle is known as a complex heteromorphic life cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdan2006_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Houdan2006-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_distribution">Global distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Global distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="408" data-file-width="569" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png/300px-Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png/450px-Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b8/Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coccolithophore%2BAbundance.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Generalization of Distribution of Coccolithophores in ocean</div></div></div>
<p>Coccolithophores occur throughout the world ocean. Their distribution varies vertically by stratified layers in the ocean and geographically by different temporal zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geisen2004_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geisen2004-25">[25]</a></sup> While most modern coccolithophores can be located in their associated stratified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oligotrophic" title="Oligotrophic">oligotrophic</a> conditions, the most abundant areas of coccolithophores where there is the highest species diversity are located in subtropical zones with a temperate climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan1997_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan1997-26">[26]</a></sup> While water temperature and the amount of light intensity entering the water’s surface are the more influential factors in determining where species are located, the ocean currents also can determine the location where certain species of coccolithophores are found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boeckel2006_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boeckel2006-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although motility and colony formation vary according to the life cycle of different coccolithophore species, there is often alternation between a motile, haploid phase, and a non-motile diploid phase.  In both phases, the organism’s dispersal is largely due to ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(ocean)" title="Current (ocean)">currents</a> and circulation patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deVargas2007_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-deVargas2007-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the Pacific Ocean, approximately 90 species have been identified with six separate zones relating to different Pacific currents that contain unique groupings of different species of coccolithophores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okada1973_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Okada1973-28">[28]</a></sup> The highest diversity of coccolithophores in the Pacific Ocean was in an area of the ocean considered the Central North Zone which is an area between 30 <sup>o</sup>N and 5 <sup>o</sup>N, composed of the North Equatorial Current and the Equatorial Countercurrent. These two currents move in opposite directions, east and west, allowing for a strong mixing of waters and allowing a large variety of species to populate the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okada1973_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Okada1973-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Atlantic Ocean, the most abundant species are <i><a href="/wiki/Emiliania_huxleyi" title="Emiliania huxleyi">E. huxleyi</a></i> and <i>Florisphaera profunda</i> with smaller concentrations of the species <i>Umbellosphaera</i> <i>irregularis</i>, <i>Umbellosphaera tenuis</i> and different species of <i>Gephyrocapsa</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Okada1973_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Okada1973-28">[28]</a></sup> Deep-dwelling coccolithophore species abundance is greatly affected by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nutricline&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nutricline (page does not exist)">nutricline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a> depths. These coccolithophores increase in abundance when the nutricline and thermocline are deep and decrease when they are shallow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinkel2000_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kinkel2000-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The complete distribution of coccolithophores is currently not known and some regions, such as the Indian Ocean, are not as well known as other locations in the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans. It is also very hard to explain distributions due to multiple constantly changing factors involving the ocean properties, such as coastal and equatorial <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>, frontal systems, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> environments, unique oceanic topography, and pockets of isolated high or low water temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2009_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Young2009-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Table 1 and Table 2 show simplified specific species preferences based on geographic location and water depth respectively. These do not contain all coccolithophore species and are just a simplification of major, more well-known species.
</p><p><br/>
</p><p>The upper photic zone is low in nutrient concentration, high in light intensity and penetration, and usually higher in temperature. The lower photic zone is high in nutrient concentration, low in light intensity and penetration and relatively cool. The middle photic zone is an area that contains the same values in between that of the lower and upper photic zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordan1997_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan1997-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_global_climate_change_on_distribution_and_abundance">Effect of global climate change on distribution and abundance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effect of global climate change on distribution and abundance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Recent studies show that climate change has direct and indirect impacts on Coccolithophore distribution and productivity. They will inevitably be affected by the increasing temperatures and thermal stratification of the top layer of the ocean, since these are prime controls on their ecology, although it is not clear whether global warming would result in net increase or decrease of coccolithophores. It has been suggested that since they calcify <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> due to increasing carbon dioxide could severely affect coccolithophores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinkel2000_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kinkel2000-29">[29]</a></sup> Recent CO2 increases have seen a sharp increase in the population of coccolithophores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_food_web">Role in the food web</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Role in the food web">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg/220px-Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg/330px-Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg/440px-Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bloom_in_the_Barents_Sea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The milky blue colour of this <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> bloom in <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a> strongly suggests that it contains coccolithophores.</div></div></div>
<p>Coccolithophores are one of the more abundant primary producers in the ocean.  As such, they are a large contributor to the primary productivity of the tropical and subtropical oceans, however, exactly how much has yet to have been recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rost2004_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rost2004-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dependence_on_nutrients">Dependence on nutrients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dependence on nutrients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ratio between the concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> in particular areas of the ocean dictates <a href="/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy" title="Dominance hierarchy">competitive dominance</a> within phytoplankton communities.  Each ratio essentially tips the odds in favor of either <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a> or other groups of phytoplankton, such as coccolithophores.  A low silicate to nitrogen and phosphorus ratio allows coccolithophores to outcompete other phytoplankton species; however, when silicate to phosphorus to nitrogen ratios are high coccolithophores are outcompeted by diatoms. The increase in agricultural processes lead to <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of waters and thus, coccolithophore blooms in these high nitrogen and phosphorus, low silicate environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yunev2007_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yunev2007-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_water_column_productivity">Impact on water column productivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Impact on water column productivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> in calcium carbonate allows coccoliths to scatter more light  than they absorb.  This has two important consequences: 1) Surface waters become brighter, meaning they have a higher <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, and 2) there is induced <a href="/wiki/Photoinhibition" title="Photoinhibition">photoinhibition</a>, meaning deeper waters become darker.  Hence, a high concentration of coccoliths leads to a simultaneous increase in surface water temperature and decrease in the temperature of deeper waters. This results in more <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratification</a> in the water column and a decrease in the vertical mixing of nutrients.  However, a recent study estimated that the overall effect of coccolithophores on the increased in <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> of the ocean is less than that from anthropogenic factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrissey2012_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morrissey2012-32">[32]</a></sup> Therefore, the overall result of large blooms of coccolithophores is a decrease in water column productivity, rather than a contribution to global warming.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Predator-prey_interactions">Predator-prey interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Predator-prey interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Their predators include the common predators of all phytoplankton including small fish, zooplankton, and shellfish larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogan2009_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hogan2009-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdan2004_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houdan2004-33">[33]</a></sup> Viruses specific to this species have been isolated from several locations worldwide and appear to play a major role in spring bloom dynamics.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>No environmental evidence of coccolithophore toxicity has been reported, but they belong to the class Prymnesiophyceae which contain orders with toxic species. Toxic species have been found in the genera <i>Prymnesium</i> Massart and <i>Chrysochromulina</i> Lackey. Members of the genus <i>Prymnesium</i> have been found to produce haemolytic compounds, the agent responsible for toxicity.  Some of these toxic species are responsible for large fish kills and can be accumulated in organisms such as shellfish; transferring it through the food chain. In laboratory tests for toxicity members of the oceanic coccolithophore genera <i>Emiliania, Gephyrocapsa, Calcidiscus</i> and <i>Coccolithus</i> were shown to be non-toxic as were species of the coastal genus <i>Hymenomonas</i>, however several species of <i>Pleurochrysis</i> and <i>Jomonlithus</i>, both coastal genera were toxic to <i>Artemia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdan2004_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Houdan2004-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_interactions">Community interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Community interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coccolithophorids are predominantly found as single, free-floating haploid or diploid cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geisen2004_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Geisen2004-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> need sunlight and nutrients from the ocean to survive, so they thrive in areas with large inputs of nutrient rich water upwelling from the lower levels of the ocean.  Most coccolithophores, only require sunlight for energy production and have a higher ratio of nitrate uptake over ammonium uptake (nitrogen is required for growth and can be used directly from nitrate but not ammonium). Because of this they thrive in still, nutrient-poor environments where other phytoplankton are starving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Litchman2007_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Litchman2007-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trade-off" title="Trade-off">Trade-offs</a> associated with these faster growth rates, however, include a smaller cell radius and lower cell volume than other types of phytoplankton.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viral_infection_and_coevolution">Viral infection and coevolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Viral infection and coevolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Giant <a href="/wiki/DNA_virus" title="DNA virus">DNA-containing viruses</a> are known to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lytic_Cycle" title="Lytic Cycle">lytically</a> infect coccolithophores, particularly <i>E. huxleyi</i>.  These viruses, known as E. huxleyi viruses (EhVs), appear to infect the coccosphere coated diploid phase of the life cycle almost exclusively.  It has been proposed that as the haploid organism is not infected and therefore not affected by the virus, the co-evolutionary “<a href="/wiki/Arms_race" title="Arms race">arms race</a>” between coccolithophores and these viruses does not follow the classic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Queen%27s_Hypothesis" title="Red Queen's Hypothesis">Red Queen</a> evolutionary framework, but instead a “Cheshire Cat” ecological dynamic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frada2008_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frada2008-35">[35]</a></sup>  More recent work has suggested that viral synthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphingolipids" title="Sphingolipids">sphingolipids</a> and induction of <a href="/wiki/Programmed_cell_death" title="Programmed cell death">programmed cell death</a> provides a more direct link to study a Red Queen-like <a href="/wiki/Coevolution" title="Coevolution">coevolutionary</a> arms race at least between the coccolithoviruses and diploid organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vardi2012_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vardi2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_in_global_climate_change">Importance in global climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Importance in global climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_the_carbon_cycle">Impact on the carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Impact on the carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coccolithophores have both long and short term effects on the carbon cycle. The production of coccoliths requires the uptake of dissolved inorganic carbon and calcium.  <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">Calcium carbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> are produced from calcium and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> by the following chemical reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca<sup>2+</sup> + 2HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>  ←→ CaCO<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mejia2011_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mejia2011-36">[36]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Because coccolithophores are photosynthetic organisms, they are able to use some of the CO<sub>2</sub> released in the calcification reaction for <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mackinder2010_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mackinder2010-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the production of calcium carbonate drives surface alkalinity down, and in conditions of low alkalinity the CO<sub>2</sub> is instead released back into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates1996_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bates1996-38">[38]</a></sup>
As a result of this, researchers have postulated that large blooms of coccolithophores may contribute to global warming in the short term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh2003_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh2003-39">[39]</a></sup> A more widely accepted idea, however, is that over the long term coccolithophores contribute to an overall decrease in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations.  During calcification two carbon atoms are taken up and one of them becomes trapped as calcium carbonate.  This calcium carbonate sinks to the bottom of the ocean in the form of coccoliths and becomes part of sediment; thus, coccolithophores provide a sink for emitted carbon, mediating the effects of greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh2003_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh2003-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_responses_to_ocean_acidification">Evolutionary responses to ocean acidification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Evolutionary responses to ocean acidification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research also suggests that <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> due to increasing concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere may affect the calcification machinery of coccolithophores.  This may not only affect immediate events such as increases in population or coccolith production, but also may induce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolutionary_adaptation" title="Evolutionary adaptation">evolutionary adaptation</a> of coccolithophore species over longer periods of time.  For example, coccolithophores use H<sup>+</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_channels" title="Ion channels">ion channels</a> in to constantly pump H<sup>+</sup> ions out of the cell during coccolith production.  This allows them to avoid <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>, as coccolith production would otherwise produce a toxic excess of H<sup>+</sup> ions.  When the function of these ion channels is disrupted, the coccolithophores stop the calcification process to avoid acidosis, thus forming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaufort2011_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beaufort2011-40">[40]</a></sup> Low ocean <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a>, impairs ion channel function and therefore places evolutionary selective pressure on coccolithophores and makes them (and other ocean calcifiers) vulnerable to ocean acidification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyrell1999_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyrell1999-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2008, field evidence indicating an increase in calcification of newly formed ocean sediments containing coccolithophores bolstered the first ever experimental data showing that an increase in ocean CO<sub>2</sub> concentration results in an increase in calcification of these organisms.
Decreasing coccolith mass is related to both the increasing concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> and decreasing concentrations of CO<sub>3</sub> in the world’s oceans.  This lower calcification is assumed to put coccolithophores at ecological disadvantage.  Some species like <i>Calcidiscus</i> <i>leptoporus</i>, however, are not affected in this way, while the most abundant coccolithophore species, <i>E. huxleyi</i> might be (study results are mixed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaufort2011_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beaufort2011-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodriguez2008_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodriguez2008-42">[42]</a></sup> Also, highly calcified coccolithophorids have been found in conditions of low CO<sup>−3</sup> contrary to predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2012_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2012-10">[10]</a></sup> Understanding the effects of increasing ocean acidification on coccolithophore species is absolutely essential to predicting the future chemical composition of the ocean, particularly its carbonate chemistry.  Viable conservation and management measures will come from future research in this area.  Groups like the European-based <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CALMARO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CALMARO (page does not exist)">CALMARO</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calmaro_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Calmaro-43">[43]</a></sup> are monitoring the responses of coccolithophore populations to varying pH’s and working to determine environmentally sound measures of control.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_microfossil_record">Impact on microfossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Impact on microfossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coccolith fossils are prominent and valuable <a href="/wiki/Calcareous" title="Calcareous">calcareous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfossil" title="Microfossil">microfossils</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Micropaleontology" title="Micropaleontology">Micropaleontology</a>).   Of particular interest are fossils dating back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Palaeocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Palaeocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> 55 million years ago.  This period is thought to correspond most directly to the current levels of CO<sub>2</sub> in the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Self-Trail2012_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Self-Trail2012-44">[44]</a></sup>  Finally, field evidence of coccolithophore fossils in rock were used to show that the deep-sea fossil record bears a <a href="/wiki/Megabias" title="Megabias">rock record bias</a> similar to the one that is widely accepted to affect the land-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lloyd2011_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd2011-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_the_oceans">Impact on the oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Impact on the oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coccolithophore&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(December 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The coccolithophorids help in regulating the temperature of the oceans.  They thrive in warm seas and release DMS (dimethyl sulphide) into the air whose nuclei help to produce thicker clouds to block the sun. When the oceans cool, the number of coccolithophorids decrease and the amount of clouds also decrease. When there are fewer clouds blocking the sun, the temperature also rises. This, therefore, maintains the balance and equilibrium of nature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
