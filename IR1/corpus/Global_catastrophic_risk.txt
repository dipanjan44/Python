<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Global catastrophic risk</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Hypothetical future event that has the potential to damage human well-being on a global scale</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Global catastrophic risks" redirects here. For the book, see <a href="/wiki/Global_Catastrophic_Risks_(book)" title="Global Catastrophic Risks (book)">Global Catastrophic Risks (book)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Impact_event.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="517" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Impact_event.jpg/300px-Impact_event.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Impact_event.jpg/450px-Impact_event.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/Impact_event.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Impact_event.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's impression of a major <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">asteroid impact</a>. An asteroid with an impact strength of a billion atomic bombs may have caused the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">extinction of the dinosaurs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulte10_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulte10-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>global catastrophic risk</b> is a hypothetical future event which could damage human well-being on a global scale,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> even crippling or destroying <a href="/wiki/Modernity" title="Modernity">modern civilization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-world-3">[3]</a></sup> An event that could cause <a href="/wiki/Human_extinction" title="Human extinction">human extinction</a> or permanently and drastically curtail humanity's potential is known as an <b>existential risk</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-types_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-types-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Potential global catastrophic risks include anthropogenic risks, caused by humans (technology, governance, climate change), and natural or external risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-world-3">[3]</a></sup> Examples of technology risks are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_risk_from_advanced_artificial_intelligence" title="Existential risk from advanced artificial intelligence">hostile artificial intelligence</a> and destructive <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecular_nanotechnology#Risks" title="Molecular nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>. Insufficient or malign <a href="/wiki/Global_governance" title="Global governance">global governance</a> creates risks in the social and political domain, such as a <a href="/wiki/World_War_III" title="World War III">global war</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_holocaust" title="Nuclear holocaust">nuclear holocaust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">bioterrorism</a> using <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">genetically modified organisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyberterrorism" title="Cyberterrorism">cyberterrorism</a> destroying <a href="/wiki/Critical_infrastructure" title="Critical infrastructure">critical infrastructure</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a>; or the failure to manage a natural <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a>. Problems and risks in the domain of <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_governance" title="Earth system governance">earth system governance</a> include <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of species, <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Social_development_theory" title="Social development theory">non-equitable</a> resource distribution, <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human overpopulation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_failures" title="Crop failures">crop failures</a> and non-<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>. Examples of non-anthropogenic risks are an asteroid <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact event</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Supervolcano" title="Supervolcano">supervolcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Types_of_volcanic_eruptions" title="Types of volcanic eruptions">eruption</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst#Hypothetical_effects_on_Earth_in_the_past" title="Gamma-ray burst">lethal gamma-ray burst</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storm</a> destroying electronic equipment, natural long-term <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, or hostile <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classifications">Classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="574" data-file-width="587" height="489" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg/500px-X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg/750px-X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg/1000px-X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X-risk-chart-en-01a.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scope/intensity grid from Bostrom's paper "Existential Risk Prevention as Global Priority"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-priority_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-priority-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_catastrophic_vs._existential">Global catastrophic vs. existential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Global catastrophic vs. existential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bostrom" title="Nick Bostrom">Nick Bostrom</a> classifies risks according to their scope and intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-priority_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-priority-5">[5]</a></sup> A "global catastrophic risk" is any risk that is at least "global" in scope, and is not subjectively "imperceptible" in intensity. Those that are at least "trans-generational" (affecting all future generations) in scope and "terminal"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title='does "terminal" mean human extinction or not? (April 2017)'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in intensity are classified as existential risks. While a global catastrophic risk may kill the vast majority of life on earth, humanity could still potentially recover. An existential risk, on the other hand, is one that either destroys humanity (and, presumably, all but the most rudimentary species of <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">non-human lifeforms</a> and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plant life</a>) entirely or at least prevents any chance of civilization recovering. Bostrom considers existential risks to be far more significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waste_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-waste-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, in <i><a href="/wiki/Catastrophe:_Risk_and_Response" title="Catastrophe: Risk and Response">Catastrophe: Risk and Response</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Posner" title="Richard Posner">Richard Posner</a> singles out and groups together events that bring about "utter overthrow or ruin" on a global, rather than a "local or regional" scale. Posner singles out such events as worthy of special attention on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-benefit" title="Cost-benefit">cost-benefit</a> grounds because they could directly or indirectly jeopardize the survival of the human race as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Posner's events include <a href="/wiki/Impact_event#Frequency_and_Risk" title="Impact event">meteor impacts</a>, runaway <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">grey goo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">bioterrorism</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_of_high_energy_particle_collision_experiments" title="Safety of high energy particle collision experiments">particle accelerator accidents</a>.
</p><p>Researchers experience difficulty in studying near human extinction directly, since humanity has never been destroyed before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observation_selection_effects_cirkovic_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-observation_selection_effects_cirkovic-8">[8]</a></sup> While this does not mean that it will not be in the future, it does make modelling existential risks difficult, due in part to <a href="/wiki/Survivorship_bias" title="Survivorship bias">survivorship bias</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_classifications">Other classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bostrom identifies four types of existential risk. "Bangs" are sudden catastrophes, which may be accidental or deliberate. He thinks the most likely sources of bangs are malicious use of <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear war</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Simulation_hypothesis" title="Simulation hypothesis">the possibility that the universe is a simulation</a> that will end. "Crunches" are scenarios in which humanity survives but civilization is slowly destroyed. The most likely causes of this, he believes, are <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">exhaustion of natural resources</a>, a stable <a href="/wiki/World_government" title="World government">global government</a> that prevents technological progress, or <a href="/wiki/Dysgenics" title="Dysgenics">dysgenic pressures</a> that lower average intelligence. "Shrieks" are undesirable futures. For example, if a single mind enhances its powers by merging with a computer, it could dominate human civilization. Bostrom believes that this scenario is most likely, followed by flawed superintelligence and a repressive totalitarian regime. "Whimpers" are the gradual decline of human civilization or current values. He thinks the most likely cause would be evolution changing moral preference, followed by extraterrestrial invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-types_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-types-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Likelihood">Likelihood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Likelihood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some risks, such as that from asteroid impact, with a one-in-a-million chance of causing humanity's extinction in the next century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matheny_xrisk_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matheny_xrisk-9">[9]</a></sup> have had their probabilities predicted using straightforward, well-understood, and (in principle) precise methods (although even in cases like these, the exact rate of large impacts is contested).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Similarly, the frequency of volcanic eruptions of sufficient magnitude to cause catastrophic climate change, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Toba_catastrophe_theory" title="Toba catastrophe theory">Toba Eruption</a>, which may have almost caused the extinction of the human race,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> has been estimated at about 1 in every 50,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The relative danger posed by other threats is much more difficult to calculate. Given the limitations of ordinary calculation and modeling, expert elicitation is frequently used instead to obtain probability estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-probabilities_and_methodologies_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-probabilities_and_methodologies-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2008, an informal survey of experts on different global catastrophic risks at the Global Catastrophic Risk Conference at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> suggested a 19% chance of human extinction by the year 2100. The conference report cautions that the results should be taken "with a grain of salt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The 2016 annual report by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Challenges_Foundation" title="Global Challenges Foundation">Global Challenges Foundation</a> estimates that an average American is more than five times more likely to die during a human-extinction event than in a car crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcf_atlantic_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-gcf_atlantic-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcf_home_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-gcf_home-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are significant methodological challenges in estimating these risks with precision. Most attention has been given to risks to human civilization over the next 100 years, but forecasting for this length of time is difficult. The types of threats posed by nature may prove relatively constant, though new risks could be discovered. Anthropogenic threats, however, are likely to change dramatically with the development of new technology; while volcanoes have been a threat throughout history, nuclear weapons have only been an issue since the 20th century. Historically, the ability of experts to predict the future over these timescales has proved very limited. Man-made threats such as nuclear war or <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> are harder to predict than natural threats, due to the inherent methodological difficulties in the social sciences. In general, it is hard to estimate the magnitude of the risk from this or other dangers, especially as both international relations and technology can change rapidly.
</p><p>Existential risks pose unique challenges to prediction, even more than other long-term events, because of <a href="/wiki/Anthropic_principle" title="Anthropic principle">observation selection effects</a>. Unlike with most events, the failure of a complete <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a> to occur in the past is not evidence against their likelihood in the future, because every world that has experienced such an extinction event has no observers, so regardless of their frequency, no civilization observes existential risks in its history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observation_selection_effects_cirkovic_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-observation_selection_effects_cirkovic-8">[8]</a></sup> These <a href="/wiki/Anthropic_principle" title="Anthropic principle">anthropic</a> issues can be avoided by looking at evidence that does not have such selection effects, such as asteroid impact craters on the Moon, or directly evaluating the likely impact of new technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-priority_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-priority-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to known and tangible risks, unforeseeable <a href="/wiki/Black_swan_theory" title="Black swan theory">black swan</a> extinction events may occur, presenting an additional methodological problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moral_importance_of_existential_risk">Moral importance of existential risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moral importance of existential risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some scholars have strongly favored reducing existential risk on the grounds that it greatly benefits future generations. <a href="/wiki/Derek_Parfit" title="Derek Parfit">Derek Parfit</a> argues that extinction would be a great loss because our descendants could potentially survive for four billion years before the <a href="/wiki/Sun#After_core_hydrogen_exhaustion" title="Sun">expansion of the Sun</a> makes the Earth uninhabitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bostrom" title="Nick Bostrom">Nick Bostrom</a> argues that there is even greater potential in colonizing space. If future humans colonize space, they may be able to support a very large number of people on other planets, potentially lasting for trillions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waste_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-waste-6">[6]</a></sup> Therefore, reducing existential risk by even a small amount would have a very significant impact on the expected number of people who will exist in the future.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Exponential_discounting" title="Exponential discounting">Exponential discounting</a> might make these future benefits much less significant. However, Jason Matheny has argued that such discounting is inappropriate when assessing the value of existential risk reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matheny_xrisk_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matheny_xrisk-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some economists have discussed the importance of global catastrophic risks, though not existential risks. Martin Weitzman argues that most of the expected economic damage from climate change may come from the small chance that warming greatly exceeds the mid-range expectations, resulting in catastrophic damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weitzman1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-weitzman1-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Posner" title="Richard Posner">Richard Posner</a> has argued that we are doing far too little, in general, about small, hard-to-estimate risks of large-scale catastrophes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_biases" title="Cognitive biases">cognitive biases</a> can influence people's judgment of the importance of existential risks, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scope_insensitivity" title="Scope insensitivity">scope insensitivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_discounting" title="Hyperbolic discounting">hyperbolic discounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Availability_heuristic" title="Availability heuristic">availability heuristic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_fallacy" title="Conjunction fallacy">conjunction fallacy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Affect_heuristic" title="Affect heuristic">affect heuristic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Overconfidence_effect" title="Overconfidence effect">overconfidence effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biases_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biases-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scope insensitivity influences how bad people consider the extinction of the human race to be. For example, when people are motivated to donate money to altruistic causes, the quantity they are willing to give does not increase linearly with the magnitude of the issue: people are roughly as concerned about 200,000 birds getting stuck in oil as they are about 2,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Similarly, people are often more concerned about threats to individuals than to larger groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biases_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biases-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are economic reasons that can explain why so little effort is going into existential risk reduction. It is a global good, so even if a large nation decreases it, that nation will only enjoy a small fraction of the benefit of doing so. Furthermore, the vast majority of the benefits may be enjoyed by far future generations, and though these quadrillions of future people would in theory perhaps be willing to pay massive sums for existential risk reduction, no mechanism for such a transaction exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-priority_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-priority-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_sources_of_risk">Potential sources of risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Potential sources of risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some sources of catastrophic risk are natural, such as meteor impacts or supervolcanoes. Some of these have caused mass extinctions in the past. On the other hand, some risks are man-made, such as global warming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR5_attribution_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR5_attribution-24">[24]</a></sup> environmental degradation, engineered pandemics and nuclear war. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic">Anthropogenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Cambridge Project at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> states that the "greatest threats" to the human species are man-made; they are artificial intelligence, global warming, nuclear war, and rogue biotechnology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Humanity_Institute" title="Future of Humanity Institute">Future of Humanity Institute</a> also states that human extinction is more likely to result from anthropogenic causes than natural causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-priority_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-priority-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frequently_Asked_Questions_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frequently_Asked_Questions-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_intelligence">Artificial intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Artificial intelligence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existential_risk_from_advanced_artificial_intelligence" title="Existential risk from advanced artificial intelligence">Existential risk from advanced artificial intelligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/AI_takeover" title="AI takeover">AI takeover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friendly_artificial_intelligence" title="Friendly artificial intelligence">Friendly artificial intelligence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">Technological singularity</a></div>
<p>It has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_artificial_intelligence#Unintended_consequences" title="Ethics of artificial intelligence">learning computers</a> that rapidly become <a href="/wiki/Superintelligence" title="Superintelligence">superintelligent</a> may take unforeseen actions, or that <a href="/wiki/Robot#Social_impact" title="Robot">robots</a> would out-compete humanity (one <a href="/wiki/Technological_singularity" title="Technological singularity">technological singularity</a> scenario).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-billjoy_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-billjoy-27">[27]</a></sup> Because of its exceptional scheduling and organizational capability and the range of novel technologies it could develop, it is possible that the first Earth superintelligence to emerge could rapidly become matchless and unrivaled: conceivably it would be able to bring about almost any possible outcome, and be able to foil virtually any attempt that threatened to prevent it achieving its objectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bostrom_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bostrom-28">[28]</a></sup> It could eliminate, wiping out if it chose, any other challenging rival intellects; alternatively it might manipulate or persuade them to change their behavior towards its own interests, or it may merely obstruct their attempts at interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bostrom_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bostrom-28">[28]</a></sup> In Bostrom's book, <i><a href="/wiki/Superintelligence:_Paths,_Dangers,_Strategies" title="Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies">Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies</a></i>, he defines this as the control problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bostrom2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bostrom2-29">[29]</a></sup> Physicist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> and <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> have echoed these concerns, with Hawking theorizing that this could "spell the end of the human race".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Artificial_Intelligence" title="Association for the Advancement of Artificial Intelligence">Association for the Advancement of Artificial Intelligence</a> (AAAI) hosted a conference to discuss whether computers and robots might be able to acquire any sort of <a href="/wiki/Autonomy" title="Autonomy">autonomy</a>, and how much these abilities might pose a threat or hazard. They noted that some robots have acquired various forms of semi-autonomy, including being able to find power sources on their own and being able to independently choose targets to attack with weapons. They also noted that some computer viruses can evade elimination and have achieved "cockroach intelligence." They noted that self-awareness as depicted in science-fiction is probably unlikely, but that there were other potential hazards and pitfalls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_july09_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_july09-31">[31]</a></sup> Various media sources and scientific groups have noted separate trends in differing areas which might together result in greater robotic functionalities and autonomy, and which pose some inherent concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A survey of AI experts estimated that the chance of human-level machine learning having an "extremely bad (e.g., human extinction)" long-term effect on humanity is 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AI_survey_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-AI_survey-34">[34]</a></sup> A survey by the Future of Humanity Institute estimated a 5% probability of extinction by superintelligence by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eliezer_Yudkowsky" title="Eliezer Yudkowsky">Eliezer Yudkowsky</a> believes that risks from artificial intelligence are harder to predict than any other known risks due to bias from <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphism</a>. Since people base their judgments of artificial intelligence on their own experience, he claims that they underestimate the potential power of AI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yudkowsky_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yudkowsky-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology_risk" title="Biotechnology risk">Biotechnology risk</a></div>
<p>Biotechnology can pose a global catastrophic risk in the form of bioengineered organisms (<a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>). In many cases the organism will be a <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> of humans, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, crops or other organisms we depend upon (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut bacteria</a>). However, any organism able to catastrophically disrupt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystem_function" title="Ecosystem function">ecosystem functions</a>, e.g. highly competitive <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a>, outcompeting essential crops, poses a biotechnology risk.
</p><p>A biotechnology catastrophe may be caused by accidentally releasing a genetically engineered organism escaping from controlled environments, by the planned release of such an organism which then turns out to have unforeseen and catastrophic interactions with essential natural or agro-ecosystems, or by intentional usage of <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological agents</a> in <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">bioterrorism</a> attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> Pathogens may be intentionally or unintentionally genetically modified to change virulence and other characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, a group of Australian researchers unintentionally changed characteristics of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mousepox" title="Mousepox">mousepox</a> virus while trying to develop a virus to sterilize rodents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> The modified virus became highly lethal even in vaccinated and naturally resistant mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandberg_xrisk_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sandberg_xrisk-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mousepoxstudy_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-mousepoxstudy-38">[38]</a></sup> The technological means to genetically modify virus characteristics are likely to become more widely available in the future if not properly regulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">Terrorist</a> applications of biotechnology have historically been infrequent. To what extent this is due to a lack of capabilities or motivation is not resolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> However, given current development, more risk from novel, engineered pathogens is to be expected in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> Exponential growth has been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> sector, and Noun and Chyba predict that this will lead to major increases in biotechnological capabilities in the coming decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> They argue that risks from <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">bioterrorism</a> are distinct from nuclear and chemical threats because biological pathogens are easier to mass-produce and their production is hard to control (especially as the technological capabilities are becoming available even to individual users).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-7"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup> A survey by the Future of Humanity Institute estimated a 2% probability of extinction from engineered pandemics by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Noun and Chyba propose three categories of measures to reduce risks from biotechnology and natural pandemics: Regulation or prevention of potentially dangerous research, improved recognition of outbreaks and developing facilities to mitigate disease outbreaks (e.g. better and/or more widely distributed vaccines).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_bio-chapter_36-8"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_bio-chapter-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyberattack">Cyberattack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cyberattack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyberattack" title="Cyberattack">Cyberattack</a></div>
<p>Cyberattacks have the potential to destroy everything from personal data to electric grids. <a href="/wiki/Christine_Peterson" title="Christine Peterson">Christine Peterson</a>, co-founder and past president of the <a href="/wiki/Foresight_Institute" title="Foresight Institute">Foresight Institute</a>, believes a cyberattack on electric grids has the potential to be a catastrophic risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">Runaway climate change</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> refers to the warming caused by human technology since the 19th century or earlier. Projections of future climate change suggest further global warming, <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, and an increase in the frequency and severity of some extreme weather events and weather-related disasters. Effects of global warming include <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>, stresses to existing food-producing systems, increased spread of known infectious diseases such as malaria, and rapid mutation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a>. In November 2017, a statement by 15,364 scientists from 184 countries indicated that increasing levels of greenhouse gases from use of fossil fuels, human population growth, deforestation, and overuse of land for agricultural production, particularly by farming <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> for meat consumption, are trending in ways that forecast an increase in human misery over coming decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-world-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_disaster">Environmental disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_disaster" title="Environmental disaster">Environmental disaster</a></div>
<p>An environmental or ecological disaster, such as world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_failure" title="Crop failure">crop failure</a> and collapse of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, could be induced by the present trends of <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiarelli1998_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiarelli1998-40">[40]</a></sup> and non-<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>. Most environmental scenarios involve one or more of the following: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction_event" title="Holocene extinction event">Holocene extinction event</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">scarcity of water</a> that could lead to approximately one half of the Earth's population being without safe drinking water, <a href="/wiki/Pollinator_decline" title="Pollinator decline">pollinator decline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, massive <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, or massive <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> episodes. Detected in the early 21st century, a threat in this direction is <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> a phenomenon that might foreshadow the imminent extinction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovgren_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lovgren-43">[43]</a></sup> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_honeybee" title="Western honeybee">Western honeybee</a>. As the bee plays a vital role in pollination, its extinction would severely disrupt the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>.
</p><p>An October 2017 report published in <a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">The Lancet</a> stated that toxic air, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_pollution" title="Soil pollution">soils</a>, and workplaces were collectively responsible for 9 million deaths worldwide in 2015, particularly from <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> which was linked to deaths by increasing susceptibility to non-infectious diseases, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carrington_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-carrington-44">[44]</a></sup> The report warned that the pollution crisis was exceeding "the envelope on the amount of pollution the Earth can carry" and “threatens the continuing survival of human societies”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carrington_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-carrington-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_resource_exhaustion">Mineral resource exhaustion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mineral resource exhaustion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_American" title="Romanian American">Romanian American</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_considered_father_or_mother_of_a_scientific_field" title="List of people considered father or mother of a scientific field">progenitor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift#Kuhnian_paradigm_shifts" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm founder</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>, has argued that the <a href="/wiki/Sustainability#Carrying_capacity" title="Sustainability">carrying capacity</a> of Earth — that is, Earth's capacity to sustain human populations and consumption levels — is bound to decrease sometime in the future as Earth's finite stock of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral resources</a> is presently being extracted and put to use; and consequently, that the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a> as a whole is heading towards an <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Population_pressure,_mineral_resource_exhaustion_and_the_end_of_mankind" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">inevitable future collapse, leading to the demise of human civilization itself</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngr01_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngr01-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>303f</span></sup> Ecological economist and <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy#Herman_Daly's_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy" title="Steady-state economy">steady-state theorist</a> <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, a student of Georgescu-Roegen, has <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy#Daly's_credo" title="Steady-state economy">propounded the same argument</a> by asserting that "... all we can do is to avoid wasting the limited capacity of <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">creation</a> to support present and future life [on Earth]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hd01_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-hd01-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>370</span></sup>
</p><p>Ever since Georgescu-Roegen and Daly published these views, various scholars in the field have been discussing the existential impossibility of allocating earth's finite stock of mineral resources evenly among an unknown number of present and future generations. This number of generations is likely to remain unknown to us, as there is no way — or only little way — of knowing in advance if or when <a href="/wiki/Human_extinction" title="Human extinction">mankind will ultimately face extinction</a>. In effect, <i>any</i> conceivable intertemporal allocation of the stock will inevitably end up with universal economic decline at some future point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jr01_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-jr01-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>253–256</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kb01_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-kb01-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>165</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jm01_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-jm01-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>168–171</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jg01_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-jg01-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>150–153</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-js01_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-js01-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>106–109</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ck01_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ck01-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>546–549</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap01_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap01-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>142–145</span></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_technology_accident">Experimental technology accident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Experimental technology accident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">Grey goo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">Bioterrorism</a></div>
<p>Nick Bostrom suggested that in the pursuit of knowledge, humanity might inadvertently create a device that could destroy Earth and the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physicsaccident_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-physicsaccident-54">[54]</a></sup> Investigations in nuclear and high-energy physics could create unusual conditions with catastrophic consequences. For example, scientists worried that the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_(nuclear_test)#Test_predictions" title="Trinity (nuclear test)">first nuclear test</a> might ignite the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamming_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamming-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LA602_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-LA602-56">[56]</a></sup> More recently, others <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Heavy_Ion_Collider#Critics_of_high-energy_experiments" title="Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider">worried that the RHIC</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_of_particle_collisions_at_the_Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Safety of particle collisions at the Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> might start a chain-reaction global disaster involving black holes, strangelets, or false vacuum states. These particular concerns have been refuted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> but the general concern remains.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</a> could lead to the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a> could be taken to an extreme, <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a> could lead to <a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">grey goo</a> in which out-of-control <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_replication" title="Self replication">self-replicating</a> robots consume all living matter on earth while building more of themselves—in both cases, either deliberately or by accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drexler_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-drexler-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nanotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">Grey goo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_risks_of_nanotechnology" title="Potential risks of nanotechnology">Potential risks of nanotechnology</a></div>
<p>Many nanoscale technologies are in development or currently in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup> The only one that appears to pose a significant global catastrophic risk is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_manufacturing" title="Molecular manufacturing">molecular manufacturing</a>, a technique that would make it possible to build complex structures at atomic precision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foresight_nano_FAQ_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foresight_nano_FAQ-64">[64]</a></sup> Molecular manufacturing requires significant advances in nanotechnology, but once achieved could produce highly advanced products at low costs and in large quantities in nanofactories of desktop proportions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foresight_nano_FAQ_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foresight_nano_FAQ-64">[64]</a></sup> When nanofactories gain the ability to produce other nanofactories, production may only be limited by relatively abundant factors such as input materials, energy and software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molecular manufacturing could be used to cheaply produce, among many other products, highly advanced, durable weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-3"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup> Being equipped with compact computers and motors these could be increasingly autonomous and have a large range of capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-4"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chris_Phoenix_(nanotechnologist)" title="Chris Phoenix (nanotechnologist)">Chris Phoenix</a> and Treder classify catastrophic risks posed by nanotechnology into three categories:
</p>
<ol><li>From augmenting the development of other technologies such as AI and biotechnology.</li>
<li>By enabling mass-production of potentially dangerous products that cause risk dynamics (such as arms races) depending on how they are used.</li>
<li>From uncontrolled self-perpetuating processes with destructive effects.</li></ol>
<p>Several researchers state that the bulk of risk from nanotechnology comes from the potential to lead to war, arms races and destructive global government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sandberg_xrisk_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sandberg_xrisk-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-5"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Several reasons have been suggested why the availability of nanotech weaponry may with significant likelihood lead to unstable arms races (compared to e.g. nuclear arms races):
</p>
<ol><li>A large number of players may be tempted to enter the race since the threshold for doing so is low;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-6"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>The ability to make weapons with molecular manufacturing will be cheap and easy to hide;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-7"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>Therefore, lack of insight into the other parties' capabilities can tempt players to arm out of caution or to launch preemptive strikes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-8"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li>Molecular manufacturing may reduce dependency on international trade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-9"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup> a potential peace-promoting factor;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wars_of_aggression" title="Wars of aggression">Wars of aggression</a> may pose a smaller economic threat to the aggressor since manufacturing is cheap and humans may not be needed on the battlefield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-10"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Since self-regulation by all state and non-state actors seems hard to achieve,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> measures to mitigate war-related risks have mainly been proposed in the area of <a href="/wiki/Multilateralism" title="Multilateralism">international cooperation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-11"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crnano_int_control_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-crnano_int_control-68">[68]</a></sup> International infrastructure may be expanded giving more sovereignty to the international level. This could help coordinate efforts for arms control. International institutions dedicated specifically to nanotechnology (perhaps analogously to the International Atomic Energy Agency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAEA" title="IAEA">IAEA</a>) or general arms control may also be designed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crnano_int_control_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-crnano_int_control-68">[68]</a></sup> One may also jointly make <a href="/wiki/Differential_technological_development" title="Differential technological development">differential technological progress</a> on defensive technologies, a policy that players should usually favour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCRs_nano-chapter_63-12"><a href="#cite_note-GCRs_nano-chapter-63">[63]</a></sup> The Center for Responsible Nanotechnology also suggests some technical restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Improved transparency regarding technological capabilities may be another important facilitator for arms-control.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">Grey goo</a> is another catastrophic scenario, which was proposed by <a href="/wiki/K._Eric_Drexler" title="K. Eric Drexler">Eric Drexler</a> in his 1986 book <i><a href="/wiki/Engines_of_Creation" title="Engines of Creation">Engines of Creation</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and has been a theme in mainstream media and fiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> This scenario involves tiny <a href="/wiki/Self-replicating_machine" title="Self-replicating machine">self-replicating robots</a> that consume the entire biosphere using it as a source of energy and building blocks. Nowadays, however, nanotech experts—including Drexler—discredit the scenario. According to Phoenix, a "so-called grey goo could only be the product of a deliberate and difficult engineering process, not an accident".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Warfare_and_mass_destruction">Warfare and mass destruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Warfare and mass destruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_holocaust" title="Nuclear holocaust">Nuclear holocaust</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_War_III" title="World War III">World War III</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cold_War_II" title="Cold War II">Cold War II</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="9728" data-file-width="7040" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg/190px-Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg/285px-Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg/380px-Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liberty-shall-not-perish-Pennell.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pennell" title="Joseph Pennell">Joseph Pennell</a>'s 1918 <a href="/wiki/Liberty_bond" title="Liberty bond">Liberty bond</a> poster calls up the pictorial image of an invaded, burning <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The scenarios that have been explored most frequently are nuclear warfare and <a href="/wiki/Doomsday_device" title="Doomsday device">doomsday devices</a>. Although the probability of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_war" title="Nuclear war">nuclear war</a> per year is slim, Professor <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hellman" title="Martin Hellman">Martin Hellman</a> has described it as inevitable in the long run; unless the probability approaches zero, inevitably there will come a day when civilization's luck runs out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> During the Cuban missile crisis, U.S. president <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> estimated the odds of nuclear war at "somewhere between one out of three and even".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The United States and Russia have a combined <a href="/wiki/Arsenal" title="Arsenal">arsenal</a> of 14,700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FASNukes_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FASNukes-76">[76]</a></sup> and there is an estimated total of 15,700 nuclear weapons in existence worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FASNukes_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-FASNukes-76">[76]</a></sup> Beyond nuclear, other military threats to humanity include <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> (BW). By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a>, while able to create multiple local catastrophes, is unlikely to create a global one.
</p><p>Nuclear war could yield unprecedented human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_tolls" title="Death tolls">death tolls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. Detonating large numbers of nuclear weapons would have an immediate, short term and long-term effects on the climate, causing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_winter" title="Nuclear winter">cold weather</a> and reduced sunlight and <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> that may generate significant upheaval in advanced civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuclearwinter_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuclearwinter-78">[78]</a></sup> However, while popular perception sometimes takes nuclear war as "the end of the world", experts assign low probability to human extinction from nuclear war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin1982_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin1982-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shulman2012_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shulman2012-80">[80]</a></sup> In 1982, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Martin_(social_scientist)" title="Brian Martin (social scientist)">Brian Martin</a> estimated that a US–Soviet nuclear exchange might kill 400–450 million directly, mostly in the United States, Europe and Russia, and maybe several hundred million more through follow-up consequences in those same areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin1982_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Martin1982-79">[79]</a></sup> A survey by the Future of Humanity Institute estimated a 4% probability of extinction from warfare by 2100, with a 1% chance of extinction from nuclear warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_population_and_agricultural_crisis">World population and agricultural crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: World population and agricultural crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_catastrophe" title="Malthusian catastrophe">Malthusian catastrophe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Human overpopulation</a></div>
<p>The 20th century saw a rapid increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a> due to <a href="/wiki/History_of_medicine#Modern_medicine" title="History of medicine">medical developments</a> and massive increases in agricultural productivity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> such as the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Between 1950 and 1984, as the Green Revolution transformed agriculture around the globe, world grain production increased by 250%. The Green Revolution in agriculture helped food production to keep pace with worldwide <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> or actually enabled population growth. The energy for the Green Revolution was provided by fossil fuels in the form of fertilizers (natural gas), pesticides (oil), and <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a>-fueled <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> David Pimentel, professor of ecology and agriculture at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, and Mario Giampietro, senior researcher at the National Research Institute on Food and Nutrition (INRAN), place in their 1994 study <i>Food, Land, Population and the U.S. Economy</i> the maximum U.S. population for a <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable economy</a> at 200 million. To achieve a sustainable economy and avert disaster, the United States must reduce its population by at least one-third, and world population will have to be reduced by two-thirds, says the study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>The authors of this study believe that the mentioned agricultural crisis will begin to have an effect on the world after 2020, and will become critical after 2050. Geologist <a href="/wiki/Dale_Allen_Pfeiffer" title="Dale Allen Pfeiffer">Dale Allen Pfeiffer</a> claims that coming decades could see spiraling food prices without relief and massive starvation on a global level such as never experienced before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wheat is humanity's third-most-produced cereal. Extant fungal infections such as <a href="/wiki/Ug99" title="Ug99">Ug99</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Stem_rust" title="Stem rust">stem rust</a>) can cause 100% crop losses in most modern varieties. Little or no treatment is possible and infection spreads on the wind. Should the world's large grain-producing areas become infected, the ensuing crisis in wheat availability would lead to price spikes and shortages in other food products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-anthropogenic">Non-anthropogenic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Non-anthropogenic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Asteroid_impact">Asteroid impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Asteroid impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid-impact_avoidance" title="Asteroid-impact avoidance">Asteroid-impact avoidance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">Impact event</a></div>
<p>Several asteroids have collided with earth in recent geological history. The <a href="/wiki/Chicxulub_crater" title="Chicxulub crater">Chicxulub asteroid</a>, for example, is theorized to have caused the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs 66 million years ago at the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">end of the Cretaceous</a>. No sufficiently large asteroid currently exists in an Earth-crossing orbit; however, a comet of sufficient size to cause human extinction could impact the Earth, though the annual probability may be less than 10<sup>−8</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comets_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-comets-89">[89]</a></sup> Geoscientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brian_Toon" title="Brian Toon">Brian Toon</a> estimates that a 60-mile meteorite would be large enough to "incinerate everybody".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Asteroids with around a 1 km diameter have impacted the Earth on average once every 500,000 years; these are probably too small to pose an extinction risk, but might kill billions of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comets_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-comets-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meteor_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-meteor-91">[91]</a></sup> Larger asteroids are less common. Small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid" title="Near-Earth asteroid">near-Earth asteroids</a> are regularly observed and can impact anywhere on the Earth injuring local populations <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>. As of 2013, <a href="/wiki/Spaceguard" title="Spaceguard">Spaceguard</a> estimates it has identified 95% of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_earth_object" title="Near earth object">NEOs</a> over 1 km in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2018, the <a href="/wiki/B612_Foundation" title="B612 Foundation">B612 Foundation</a> reported "It's a 100 per cent certain we'll be hit [by a devastating asteroid], but we're not 100 per cent sure when."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DS-20180428_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-DS-20180428-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INQ-20180428_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-INQ-20180428-95">[95]</a></sup> In June 2018, the US <a href="/wiki/National_Science_and_Technology_Council" title="National Science and Technology Council">National Science and Technology Council</a> warned that America is unprepared for an asteroid impact event, and has developed and released the <i>"<a class="external text" href="https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/National-Near-Earth-Object-Preparedness-Strategy-and-Action-Plan-23-pages-1MB.pdf" rel="nofollow">National Near-Earth Object Preparedness Strategy Action Plan</a>"</i> to better prepare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WH-20180621_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-WH-20180621-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GIZ-20180621_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-GIZ-20180621-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICARUS-220180522_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICARUS-220180522-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20180614_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20180614-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20180614c_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20180614c-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_invasion">Extraterrestrial invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alien_invasion" title="Alien invasion">Alien invasion</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">Extraterrestrial life</a> could invade Earth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aliens_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-aliens-101">[101]</a></sup> either to exterminate and supplant human life, enslave it under a colonial system, steal the planet's resources, or destroy the planet altogether.
</p><p>Although evidence of alien life has never been documented, scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> have postulated that the existence of extraterrestrial life is very likely. In 1969, the "<a href="/wiki/Extra-Terrestrial_Exposure_Law" title="Extra-Terrestrial Exposure Law">Extra-Terrestrial Exposure Law</a>" was added to the United States <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a> (Title 14, Section 1211) in response to the possibility of biological contamination resulting from the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">U.S. Apollo Space Program</a>. It was removed in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Scientists consider such a scenario technically possible, but unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aliensunlikely_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-aliensunlikely-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>An article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> discussed the possible threats for humanity of intentionally sending messages aimed at extraterrestrial life into the cosmos in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence" title="Search for extraterrestrial intelligence">SETI</a> efforts. Several renowned public figures such as Stephen Hawking and Elon Musk have argued against sending such messages on the grounds that extraterrestrial civilizations with technology are probably far more advanced than humanity and could pose an existential threat to humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_climate_change">Natural climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Natural climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(April 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> refers to a lasting change in the Earth's climate. The climate has ranged from ice ages to warmer periods when palm trees grew in Antarctica. It has been hypothesized that there was also a period called "<a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">snowball Earth</a>" when all the oceans were covered in a layer of ice. These global climatic changes occurred slowly, prior to the rise of human civilization about 10 thousand years ago near the end of the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age#Major_Ice_Ages" title="Ice Age">Major Ice Age</a> when the climate became more stable. However, <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a> on the decade time scale has occurred regionally. Since civilization originated during a period of stable climate, a natural variation into a new climate regime (colder or hotter) could pose a threat to civilization.
</p><p>In the history of the Earth, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">ice ages</a> are known to have occurred. More ice ages will be possible at an interval of 40,000–100,000 years. An ice age would have a serious impact on civilization because vast areas of land (mainly in North America, Europe, and Asia) could become uninhabitable. It would still be possible to live in the tropical regions, but with possible loss of humidity and water. Currently, the world is in an <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial period</a> within a much older glacial event. The last glacial expansion ended about 10,000 years ago, and all civilizations evolved later than this. Scientists do not predict that a natural ice age will occur anytime soon. This may be due to manmade emissions potentially delaying the possible onset or another ice age for at least another 50,000 years.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_threats">Cosmic threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Cosmic threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A number of astronomical threats have been identified. Massive objects, e.g. a star, large planet or <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>, could be catastrophic if a close encounter occurred in the Solar System. In April 2008, it was announced that two simulations of long-term planetary movement, one at the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">Paris Observatory</a> and the other at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>, indicate a 1% chance that <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>'s orbit could be made unstable by Jupiter's gravitational pull sometime during the lifespan of the Sun. Were this to happen, the simulations suggest a collision with Earth could be one of four possible outcomes (the others being Mercury colliding with the Sun, colliding with Venus, or being ejected from the Solar System altogether). If Mercury were to collide with Earth, all life on Earth could be obliterated entirely: an asteroid 15 km wide is believed to have caused the extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs, whereas Mercury is 4,879 km in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another cosmic threat is a <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst#Rate_of_occurrence_and_potential_effects_on_life_on_Earth" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray burst</a>, typically produced by a <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> when a star collapses inward on itself and then "bounces" outward in a massive explosion. Under certain circumstances, these events are thought to produce massive bursts of gamma radiation emanating outward from the axis of rotation of the star. If such an event were to occur oriented towards the Earth, the massive amounts of gamma radiation could significantly affect the Earth's atmosphere and pose an existential threat to all life. Such a gamma-ray burst may have been the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_events" title="Ordovician–Silurian extinction events">Ordovician–Silurian extinction events</a>. Neither this scenario nor the destabilization of Mercury's orbit are likely in the foreseeable future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unlikely_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-unlikely-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>If the Solar System were to pass through a <a href="/wiki/Dark_nebula" title="Dark nebula">dark nebula</a>, a cloud of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a>, severe global climate change would occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galacticdust_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galacticdust-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>A powerful <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flare</a> or solar superstorm, which is a drastic and unusual decrease or increase in the Sun's power output, could have severe consequences for life on Earth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>If our universe lies within a <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum" title="False vacuum">false vacuum</a>, a bubble of lower-energy vacuum could come to exist by chance or otherwise in our universe, and catalyze the conversion of our universe to a lower energy state in a volume expanding at nearly the speed of light, destroying all that we know without forewarning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colemandeluccia_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-colemandeluccia-108">[108]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (July 2015)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Such an occurrence is called <a href="/wiki/False_vacuum#Vacuum_decay" title="False vacuum">vacuum decay</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetic_reversal">Geomagnetic reversal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Geomagnetic reversal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">Geomagnetic reversal</a></div>
<p>The magnetic poles of the Earth shifted many times in geologic history. The duration of such a shift is still debated. Theories exist that during such times, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> would be substantially weakened, threatening civilization by allowing radiation from the Sun, especially <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" title="Cosmic radiation">cosmic radiation</a>, to reach the surface. These theories have been somewhat discredited, as statistical analysis shows no evidence for a correlation between past reversals and past extinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glassmeier2010_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glassmeier2010-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_pandemic">Global pandemic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Global pandemic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">Pandemic</a></div>
<p>Numerous historical examples of pandemics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> had a devastating effect on a large number of people. The present, unprecedented scale and speed of human movement make it more difficult than ever to contain an epidemic through local <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantines</a>. A global pandemic has become a realistic threat to human civilization.
</p><p>Naturally <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolving</a> pathogens will ultimately develop an upper limit to their virulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_SA_1996_37–78_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank_SA_1996_37–78-112">[112]</a></sup> Pathogens with the highest virulence, quickly killing their hosts reduce their chances of spread the infection to new hosts or carriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> This simple model predicts that - if <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a> and transmission are not genetically linked - pathogens will evolve towards low virulence and rapid transmission. However, this is not necessarily a safeguard against a global catastrophe, for the following reasons:
</p><p>1. The fitness advantage of limited virulence is primarily a function of a limited number of hosts. Any pathogen with a high virulence, high transmission rate and long incubation time may have already caused a catastrophic pandemic before ultimately virulence is limited through natural selection.
2. In models where virulence level and rate of transmission are related, high levels of virulence can evolve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Virulence is instead limited by the existence of complex populations of hosts with different susceptibilities to infection, or by some hosts being geographically isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_SA_1996_37–78_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frank_SA_1996_37–78-112">[112]</a></sup> The size of the host population and competition between different strains of pathogens can also alter virulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> 
3. A pathogen that infects humans as a secondary host and primarily infects another species (a <a href="/wiki/Zoonosis" title="Zoonosis">zoonosis</a>) has no constraints on its virulence in people, since the accidental secondary infections do not affect its evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanism">Volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supervolcano" title="Supervolcano">Supervolcano</a></div>
<p>A geological event such as massive <a href="/wiki/Flood_basalt" title="Flood basalt">flood basalt</a>, volcanism, or the eruption of a supervolcano<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supervolcano_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-supervolcano-117">[117]</a></sup> could lead to a so-called volcanic winter, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_winter" title="Nuclear winter">nuclear winter</a>. One such event, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toba_eruption" title="Toba eruption">Toba eruption</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tobaeruption_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tobaeruption-118">[118]</a></sup> occurred in Indonesia about 71,500 years ago. According to the <a href="/wiki/Toba_catastrophe_theory" title="Toba catastrophe theory">Toba catastrophe theory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tobacatastrophetheory_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tobacatastrophetheory-119">[119]</a></sup> the event may have reduced human populations to only a few tens of thousands of individuals. <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_Caldera" title="Yellowstone Caldera">Yellowstone Caldera</a> is another such supervolcano, having undergone 142 or more <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a>-forming eruptions in the past 17 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
A massive volcano eruption would eject extraordinary volumes of volcanic dust, toxic and greenhouse gases into the atmosphere with serious effects on global climate (towards extreme global cooling:  <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">volcanic winter</a> if short-term, and ice age if long-term) or <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> (if greenhouse gases were to prevail).
</p><p>When the supervolcano at <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone</a> last erupted 640,000 years ago, the thinnest layers of the ash ejected from the <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_Caldera" title="Yellowstone Caldera">caldera</a> spread over most of the United States west of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> and part of northeastern Mexico. The <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> covered much of what is now Yellowstone National Park and extended beyond, covering much of the ground from Yellowstone River in the east to the Idaho falls in the west, with some of the flows extending north beyond Mammoth Springs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a recent study, if the Yellowstone caldera erupted again as a supervolcano, an ash layer one to three millimeters thick could be deposited as far away as New York, enough to "reduce traction on roads and runways, short out electrical transformers and cause respiratory problems". There would be centimeters of thickness over much of the U.S. Midwest, enough to disrupt crops and livestock, especially if it happened at a critical time in the growing season. The worst-affected city would likely be <a href="/wiki/Billings,_Montana" title="Billings, Montana">Billings, Montana</a>, population 109,000, which the model predicted would be covered with ash estimated as 1.03 to 1.8 meters thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main long-term effect is through global climate change, which reduces the temperature globally by about 5–15 degrees C for a decade, together with the direct effects of the deposits of ash on their crops. A large supervolcano like Toba would deposit one or two meters thickness of ash over an area of several million square kilometers.(1000 cubic kilometers is equivalent to a one-meter thickness of ash spread over a million square kilometers). If that happened in some densely populated agricultural area, such as India, it could destroy one or two seasons of crops for two billion people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, Yellowstone shows no signs of a supereruption at present, and it is not certain that a future supereruption will occur there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research published in 2011 finds evidence that massive volcanic eruptions caused massive coal combustion, supporting models for significant generation of greenhouse gases. Researchers have suggested that massive volcanic eruptions through coal beds in Siberia would generate significant greenhouse gases and cause a <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Massive eruptions can also throw enough pyroclastic debris and other material into the atmosphere to partially block out the sun and cause a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_winter" title="Volcanic winter">volcanic winter</a>, as happened on a smaller scale in 1816 following the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">Mount Tambora</a>, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">Year Without a Summer</a>. Such an eruption might cause the immediate deaths of millions of people several hundred miles from the eruption, and perhaps billions of deaths<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Supervolcano_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-Supervolcano-127">[127]</a></sup> worldwide, due to the failure of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, resulting in major crop failures causing starvation on a profound scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Supervolcano_127-1"><a href="#cite_note-Supervolcano-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p><p>A much more speculative concept is the <a href="/wiki/Verneshot" title="Verneshot">verneshot</a>: a hypothetical volcanic eruption caused by the buildup of gas deep underneath a <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">craton</a>. Such an event may be forceful enough to launch an extreme amount of material from the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-orbital" title="Sub-orbital">sub-orbital</a> trajectory.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_mitigation">Proposed mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Proposed mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Planetary_management" title="Planetary management">Planetary management</a> and respecting <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">planetary boundaries</a> have been proposed as approaches to preventing ecological catastrophes. Within the scope of these approaches, the field of <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a> encompasses the deliberate large-scale engineering and manipulation of the planetary environment to combat or counteract anthropogenic changes in atmospheric chemistry. <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">Space colonization</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Space_and_survival" title="Space and survival">proposed alternative</a> to improve the odds of surviving an extinction scenario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg20100809_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorg20100809-128">[128]</a></sup> Solutions of this scope may require <a href="/wiki/Megascale_engineering" title="Megascale engineering">megascale engineering</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Food_storage" title="Food storage">Food storage</a> has been proposed globally, but the monetary cost would be high. Furthermore, it would likely contribute to the current millions of deaths per year due to <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Survivalist" title="Survivalist">survivalists</a> stock <a href="/wiki/Retreat_(survivalism)" title="Retreat (survivalism)">survival retreats</a> with multiple-year food supplies.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Svalbard_Global_Seed_Vault" title="Svalbard Global Seed Vault">Svalbard Global Seed Vault</a> is buried 400 feet (120 m) inside a mountain on an island in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>. It is designed to hold 2.5 billion seeds from more than 100 countries as a precaution to preserve the world's crops. The surrounding rock is −6 °C (21 °F) (as of 2015) but the vault is kept at −18 °C (0 °F) by refrigerators powered by locally sourced coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p>More speculatively, if society continues to function and if the biosphere remains habitable, calorie needs for the present human population might in theory be met during an extended absence of sunlight, given sufficient advance planning. Conjectured solutions include growing mushrooms on the dead plant biomass left in the wake of the catastrophe, converting cellulose to sugar, or feeding natural gas to methane-digesting bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_catastrophic_risks_and_global_governance">Global catastrophic risks and global governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Global catastrophic risks and global governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insufficient <a href="/wiki/Global_governance" title="Global governance">global governance</a> creates risks in the social and political domain, but the governance mechanisms develop more slowly than technological and social change. There are concerns from governments, the private sector, as well as the general public about the lack of governance mechanisms to efficiently deal with risks, negotiate and adjudicate between diverse and conflicting interests. This is further underlined by an understanding of the interconnectedness of global systemic risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_catastrophic_risk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bulletin_of_the_Atomic_Scientists" title="Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists">Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists</a> (est. 1945) is one of the oldest global risk organizations, founded after the public became alarmed by the potential of atomic warfare in the aftermath of WWII. It studies risks associated with nuclear war and energy and famously maintains the <a href="/wiki/Doomsday_Clock" title="Doomsday Clock">Doomsday Clock</a> established in 1947. The <a href="/wiki/Foresight_Institute" title="Foresight Institute">Foresight Institute</a> (est. 1986) examines the risks of nanotechnology and its benefits. It was one of the earliest organizations to study the unintended consequences of otherwise harmless technology gone haywire at a global scale. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/K._Eric_Drexler" title="K. Eric Drexler">K. Eric Drexler</a> who postulated "<a href="/wiki/Grey_goo" title="Grey goo">grey goo</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning after 2000, a growing number of scientists, philosophers and tech billionaires created organizations devoted to studying global risks both inside and outside of academia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p><p>Independent <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a> (NGOs) include the <a href="/wiki/Machine_Intelligence_Research_Institute" title="Machine Intelligence Research Institute">Machine Intelligence Research Institute</a> (est. 2000), which aims to reduce the risk of a catastrophe caused by artificial intelligence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> with donors including <a href="/wiki/Peter_Thiel" title="Peter Thiel">Peter Thiel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jed_McCaleb" title="Jed McCaleb">Jed McCaleb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Threat_Initiative" title="Nuclear Threat Initiative">Nuclear Threat Initiative</a> (est. 2001) seeks to reduce global threats from nuclear, biological and chemical threats, and containment of damage after an event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> It maintains a nuclear material security index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> The Lifeboat Foundation (est. 2009) funds research into preventing a technological catastrophe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> Most of the research money funds projects at universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> The Global Catastrophic Risk Institute (est. 2011) is a think tank for catastrophic risk. It is funded by the NGO Social and Environmental Entrepreneurs. The <a href="/wiki/Global_Challenges_Foundation" title="Global Challenges Foundation">Global Challenges Foundation</a> (est. 2012), based in Stockholm and founded by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Szombatfalvy" title="László Szombatfalvy">Laszlo Szombatfalvy</a>, releases a yearly report on the state of global risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcf_atlantic_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-gcf_atlantic-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gcf_home_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-gcf_home-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Life_Institute" title="Future of Life Institute">Future of Life Institute</a> (est. 2014) aims to support research and initiatives for safeguarding life considering new technologies and challenges facing humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> is one of its biggest donors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p><p>University-based organizations include the <a href="/wiki/Future_of_Humanity_Institute" title="Future of Humanity Institute">Future of Humanity Institute</a> (est. 2005) which researches the questions of humanity's long-term future, particularly existential risk. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Nick_Bostrom" title="Nick Bostrom">Nick Bostrom</a> and is based at Oxford University. The <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_the_Study_of_Existential_Risk" title="Centre for the Study of Existential Risk">Centre for the Study of Existential Risk</a> (est. 2012) is a Cambridge-based organization which studies four major technological risks: artificial intelligence, biotechnology, global warming and warfare. All are man-made risks, as <a href="/wiki/Huw_Price" title="Huw Price">Huw Price</a> explained to the AFP news agency, "It seems a reasonable prediction that some time in this or the next century intelligence will escape from the constraints of biology". He added that when this happens "we're no longer the smartest things around," and will risk being at the mercy of "machines that are not malicious, but machines whose interests don't include us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> was an acting adviser. The <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Alliance_for_Humanity_and_the_Biosphere" title="Millennium Alliance for Humanity and the Biosphere">Millennium Alliance for Humanity and the Biosphere</a> is a Stanford University-based organization focusing on many issues related to global catastrophe by bringing together members of academic in the humanities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich" title="Paul Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a> among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> Stanford University also has the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_International_Security_and_Cooperation" title="Center for International Security and Cooperation">Center for International Security and Cooperation</a> focusing on political cooperation to reduce global catastrophic risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other risk assessment groups are based in or are part of governmental organizations. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) includes a division called the Global Alert and Response (GAR) which monitors and responds to global epidemic crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> GAR helps member states with training and coordination of response to epidemics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">United States Agency for International Development</a> (USAID) has its Emerging Pandemic Threats Program which aims to prevent and contain naturally generated pandemics at their source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory</a> has a division called the Global Security Principal Directorate which researches on behalf of the government issues such as bio-security and counter-terrorism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup>
</p>
