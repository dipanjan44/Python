<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of the oil shale industry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2006" data-file-width="3072" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG/250px-Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG/375px-Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG/500px-Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kivioli_chemical_plant.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kiviõli Oil Shale Processing &amp; Chemicals Plant in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ida-Virumaa" title="Ida-Virumaa">Ida-Virumaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Environmental impact of the oil shale industry</b> includes the consideration of issues such as <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale_industry" title="Oil shale industry">extraction and processing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>. <a href="/wiki/Surface_mining" title="Surface mining">Surface mining</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale_reserves" title="Oil shale reserves">oil shale deposits</a> causes the usual environmental impacts of <a href="/wiki/Open-pit_mining" title="Open-pit mining">open-pit mining</a>. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shale_oil_extraction" title="Shale oil extraction">thermal processing</a> generate waste material, which must be disposed of, and harmful atmospheric emissions, including <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, a major <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. Experimental in-situ conversion processes and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> technologies may reduce some of these concerns in future, but may raise others, such as the pollution of groundwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BartisBoston_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BartisBoston-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mittal_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mittal-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_mining_and_retorting">Surface mining and retorting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Surface mining and retorting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use_and_waste_management">Land use and waste management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Land use and waste management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surface mining and <i>in-situ</i> processing requires extensive land use. Mining, processing and waste disposal require land to be withdrawn from traditional uses, and therefore should avoid high density population areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup> Oil shale mining reduces the original ecosystem diversity with habitats supporting a variety of plants and animals. After mining the land has to be reclaimed. However, this process takes time and cannot necessarily re-establish the original biodiversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kattel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kattel-4">[4]</a></sup>  The impact of sub-surface mining on the surroundings will be less than for open pit mines. However, sub-surface mining may also cause subsidence of the surface due to the collapse of mined-out area and abandoned stone drifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Disposal of mining wastes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spent_oil_shale" title="Spent oil shale">spent oil shale</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-coke" title="Semi-coke">semi-coke</a>) and combustion ashes needs additional land use.  According to the study of the European Academies Science Advisory Council, after processing, the waste material occupies a greater volume than the material extracted, and therefore cannot be wholly disposed underground. According to this, production of a barrel of <a href="/wiki/Shale_oil" title="Shale oil">shale oil</a> can generate up to 1.5 tonnes of semi-coke, which may occupy up to 25% greater volume than the original shale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>  This is not confirmed by the results of <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale_in_Estonia" title="Oil shale in Estonia">Estonia's oil shale industry</a>. The mining and processing of about one billion tonnes of oil shale in Estonia has created about 360-370 million tonnes of solid waste, of which 90 million tonnes is a mining waste, 70–80 million tonnes is a semi-coke, and 200 million tonnes are combustion ashes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kahru_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kahru-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The waste material may consist of several pollutants including <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycylic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)</a>, some of which are <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oilshale2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oilshale2-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tuvikene_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-tuvikene-7">[7]</a></sup>  To avoid contamination of the groundwater, the solid waste from the thermal treatment process is disposed in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_dump" title="Open dump">open dump</a> (<a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> or "heaps"), not underground. As semi-coke consists of, in addition to minerals, up to 10% <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organics</a> that may pose hazard to the environment owing  to leaching of toxic compounds as well as to the possibility of self-ignition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kahru_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-kahru-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_management">Water management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mining influences the water runoff pattern of the area affected. In some cases it requires the lowering of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> levels below the level of the oil shale strata, which may have harmful effects on the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a> and forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>  In Estonia, for each cubic meter of oil shale mined, 25 cubic meters of water must be pumped from the mine area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-symposium_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-symposium-8">[8]</a></sup>  At the same time, the thermal processing of oil shale needs water for quenching hot products and the control of dust. Water concerns are particularly sensitive issue in arid regions, such as the western part of the United States and Israel's <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev Desert</a>, where there are plans to expand the oil shale industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deseret2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-deseret2-9">[9]</a></sup>  Depending on technology, above-ground retorting uses between one and five barrels of water per barrel of produced shale oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BartisBoston_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BartisBoston-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wnonline_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wnonline-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-water_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-water-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rand_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-rand-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLM2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLM2008-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-myths_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-myths-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>In situ</i> processing, according to one estimate, uses about one-tenth as much water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hopes-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water represents the major vector of transfer of oil shale industry pollutants. One environmental issue is to prevent noxious materials leaching from spent shale into the water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>  The oil shale processing is accompanied by the formation of process waters and waste waters containing <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a> and several other products, heavily separable and toxic to the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kahru_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-kahru-5">[5]</a></sup>  A 2008 programmatic <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_statement" title="Environmental impact statement">environmental impact statement</a> issued by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">United States Bureau of Land Management</a> stated that surface mining and retort operations produce 2 to 10 U.S. gallons (7.6 to 37.9 l; 1.7 to 8.3 imp gal) of waste water per 1 short ton (0.91 t) of processed oil shale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLM2008_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLM2008-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_pollution_management">Air pollution management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Air pollution management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Main air pollution is caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">oil shale-fired power plants</a>, which provide the atmospheric emissions of gaseous products like <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a>, and the airborne particulate matter (<a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a>). It includes particles of different types (carbonaceous, inorganic ones) and different sizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arvoots_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-arvoots-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirso_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirso-17">[17]</a></sup>  The concentration of air pollutants in <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> depends primarily on the combustion technology and burning regime, while the emissions of solid particles are determined by the efficiency of fly ash-capturing devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arvoots_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-arvoots-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Open deposition of semi-coke causes distribution of pollutants in addition to aqueous vectors also via air (dust).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kahru_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-kahru-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are possible links from being in an oil shale area to a higher risk of asthma and lung cancer than other areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions">Greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide emissions from the production of shale oil and shale gas are higher than conventional oil production and a report for the <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_European_Union" title="Energy policy of the European Union">European Union</a> warns that increasing public concern about the adverse consequences of global warming may lead to opposition to oil shale development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BartisBoston_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BartisBoston-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Emissions arise from several sources. These include <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> released by the decomposition of the <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">kerogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonate minerals</a> in the extraction process, the generation of the energy needed to heat the shale and in the other oil and gas processing operations, and fuel used in the mining of the rock and the disposal of waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arvoots_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-arvoots-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koel_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-koel-19">[19]</a></sup>  As the varying mineral composition and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value">calorific value</a> of oil shale deposits varies widely, the actual values vary considerably.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup>  At best, the direct combustion of oil shales produces carbon emissions similar to those from the lowest form of coal, <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, at 2.15 <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a> CO<sub>2</sub>/<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">MJ</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-eu-3">[3]</a></sup> an energy source which is also politically contentious due to its high emission levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
For both power generation and oil extraction, the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions can be reduced by better utilization of <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> from the product streams.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In-situ_processing"><i>In-situ</i> processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_the_oil_shale_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In-situ processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ" title="In-situ">in-situ</a></i> process is the most attractive proposition due to the reduction in standard surface environmental problems. However, <i>in-situ</i> processes do involve possible significant environmental costs to <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, especially since <i>in-situ</i> methods may require ice-capping or some other form of barrier to restrict the flow of the newly gained oil into the groundwater aquifers. However, after the removal of the freeze wall these methods can still cause groundwater contamination as the hydraulic conductivity of the remaining shale increases allowing groundwater to flow through and leach salts from the newly toxic aquifer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rand_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-rand-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grunewald_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-grunewald-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
