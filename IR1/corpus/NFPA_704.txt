<p>"<b>NFPA 704: Standard System for the Identification of the Hazards of Materials for Emergency Response</b>" is a standard maintained by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a>-based <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Protection_Association" title="National Fire Protection Association">National Fire Protection Association</a>. First "tentatively adopted as a guide" in 1960,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and revised several times since then, it defines the colloquial "<b>fire diamond</b>" used by emergency personnel to quickly and easily identify the risks posed by hazardous materials. This helps determine what, if any, special equipment should be used, procedures followed, or precautions taken during the initial stages of an emergency response.
</p>

<p>The four divisions are typically color-coded with red on top indicating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammability</a>, blue on the left indicating level of <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> hazard, yellow on the right for <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">chemical reactivity</a>, and white containing codes for special hazards. Each of health, flammability and reactivity is rated on a scale from 0 (minimal hazard) to 4 (severe hazard). The latest version of NFPA 704 sections 5, 6, 7 and 8 for the specifications of each classification are listed below. The numeric values in the first column are designated in the standard by "Degree of Hazard" using Arabic numerals (0, 1, 2, 3, 4), not to be confused with other classification systems, such as that in the NFPA 30 Flammable and Combustible Liquids Code, where flammable and combustible liquid categories are designated by "Class", using Roman numerals (I, II, III).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFPA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NFPA-2">[2]</a></sup>
<span id="Red"></span>
</p>
<table class="wikitable" style="text-align:center;width:100%; vertical-align:center;">
<tbody><tr>
<th colspan="2" style="color:black; background:#ff6666; width:75%;">Flammability (red)
</th></tr>
<tr>
<th style="width:3em"><b>0</b>
</th>
<td>Materials that will not burn under typical fire conditions (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">Carbon tetrachloride</a>), including intrinsically noncombustible materials such as concrete, stone, and sand.  Materials that will not burn in air when exposed to a temperature of 820 °C (1,500 °F) for a period of 5 minutes.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>1
</th>
<td>Materials that require considerable preheating, under all ambient temperature conditions, before ignition and combustion can occur (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>). Includes some finely divided suspended solids that do not require heating before ignition can occur. <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">Flash point</a> at or above 93.3 °C (200 °F).
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>2
</th>
<td>Must be moderately heated or exposed to relatively high ambient temperature before ignition can occur (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, paper, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>) and multiple finely divided suspended solids that do not require heating before ignition can occur. Flash point between 37.8 and 93.3 °C (100 and 200 °F).
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>3
</th>
<td>Liquids and solids (including finely divided suspended solids) that can be ignited under almost all ambient temperature conditions (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>). Liquids having a flash point below 22.8 °C (73 °F) and having a boiling point at or above 37.8 °C (100 °F) or having a flash point between 22.8 and 37.8 °C (73 and 100 °F).
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>4
</th>
<td>Will rapidly or completely vaporize at normal atmospheric pressure and temperature, or is readily dispersed in air and will burn readily (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen gas</a>). Includes <a href="/wiki/Pyrophoricity" title="Pyrophoricity">pyrophoric</a> substances. <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">Flash point</a> below room temperature at 22.8 °C (73 °F).
</td></tr></tbody></table>

<th colspan="2" style="color:black; background:#6691ff; width:75%;">Health (blue)
</th></tr>
<tr>
<th style="max-width:10em">0
</th>
<td>Poses no health hazard, no precautions necessary and would offer no hazard beyond that of ordinary combustible materials (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>1
</th>
<td>Exposure would cause irritation with only minor residual injury (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bromate" title="Sodium bromate">sodium bromate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>2
</th>
<td>Intense or continued but not chronic exposure could cause temporary incapacitation or possible residual injury (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_phosphate" title="Ammonium phosphate">ammonium phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>3
</th>
<td>Short exposure could cause serious temporary or moderate residual injury (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hypochlorite" title="Calcium hypochlorite">calcium hypochlorite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexafluorosilicic_acid" title="Hexafluorosilicic acid">hexafluorosilicic acid</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>4
</th>
<td>Very short exposure could cause death or major residual injury (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isocyanate" title="Methyl isocyanate">methyl isocyanate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>)
</td></tr></tbody></table>

</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>4
</th>

<tr>
<td><b><s>W</s></b></td>
<td>Reacts with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in an unusual or dangerous manner (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>).
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>SA</b></td>
<td>Simple <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiant gas</a> (specifically <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>). The SA symbol shall also be used for liquefied <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> vapor withdrawal systems and where large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> are used in confined areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NFPA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NFPA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<table class="wikitable" style="text-align:center;width=100%; vertical-align:center;">
<tbody><tr>
<th colspan="2" style="color:black; background:#ffffff; width:50%;">Non-standard symbols (white)
</th></tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="2">These hazard codes are <i><b>not part of the NFPA 704 standard</b></i>, but are occasionally used in an unofficial manner. The use of non-standard codes may be permitted, required or disallowed by the authority having jurisdiction (e.g. fire department).
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>COR</b><br/><b>ACID</b>, <b>ALK</b></td>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">Corrosive</a>; strong <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>)<br/>Acid or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a>, to be more specific
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>BIO</b> or <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Biohazard_symbol.svg"><img alt="Biohazard symbol.svg" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="520" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Biohazard_symbol.svg/20px-Biohazard_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Biohazard_symbol.svg/30px-Biohazard_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Biohazard_symbol.svg/40px-Biohazard_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>
<td><a href="/wiki/Biological_hazard" title="Biological hazard">Biological hazard</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flu_virus" title="Flu virus">flu virus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabies_virus" title="Rabies virus">rabies virus</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>POI</b></td>
<td><a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">Poisonous</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Strychnine" title="Strychnine">strychnine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpha-Amanitin" title="Alpha-Amanitin">alpha-Amanitin</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>RA</b>, <b>RAD</b> or <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg"><img alt="Radiation warning symbol2.svg" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg/20px-Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg/30px-Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg/40px-Radiation_warning_symbol2.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>
<td><a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">Radioactive</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">cobalt-60</a>)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>CRY</b> or <b>CRYO</b></td>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">Cryogenic</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a>)
</td></tr></tbody></table>
