<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change mitigation scenarios</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">Climate change mitigation</a> scenarios</b> are possible futures in which <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is reduced by deliberate actions, such as a comprehensive switch to <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">energy sources other than fossil fuels</a>.  A typical mitigation scenario is constructed by selecting a long-range target, such as a desired atmospheric concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), and then fitting the actions to the target, for example by placing a cap on net global and national emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.
</p><p>An increase of global temperature by more than 2<span class="nowrap"> </span><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> has come to be the majority definition of what would constitute intolerably dangerous <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> with efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5<span class="nowrap"> </span>°C above pre-industrial levels per the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>.   Some climate scientists are increasingly of the opinion that the goal should be a complete restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>'s preindustrial condition, on the grounds that too protracted a <a href="/wiki/Deviation_(statistics)" title="Deviation (statistics)">deviation</a> from those conditions will produce irreversible changes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Target_levels_of_CO2">Target levels of CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Target levels of CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Contributions to climate change, whether they cool or warm the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, are often described in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> or imbalance they introduce to the planet's <a href="/wiki/Energy_budget" title="Energy budget">energy budget</a>. Now and in the future, <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> carbon dioxide is believed to be the major component of this forcing, and the contribution of other components is often quantified in terms of "parts-per-million CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2e" title="CO2e">CO<sub>2</sub>e</a>), or the increment/decrement in carbon dioxide concentrations which would create a radiative forcing of the same magnitude.
</p><p>At present, non-CO<sub>2</sub> contributions to climate change, positive and negative, are believed to roughly cancel out, so that the net radiative forcing being experienced at present, expressed in ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-e, is more or less the same as the actual current level of carbon dioxide (406.75 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>, as of December 2017). To some extent this legitimates the statement of targets just in terms of ppm CO<sub>2</sub>, as is usually the case. However, the positive and negative non-CO<sub>2</sub> will not necessarily balance in future, and so a target stated in terms of CO<sub>2</sub>e is less ambiguous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stabilization_wedges">Stabilization wedges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stabilization wedges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Climate_stabilization_wedge" title="Climate stabilization wedge">stabilization wedge</a></b> (or simply "wedge") is an action which incrementally reduces projected emissions. The name is derived from the triangular shape of the gap between reduced and unreduced emissions trajectories when graphed over time. For example, a reduction in electricity demand due to increased efficiency means that less electricity needs to be generated and thus fewer emissions need to be produced. The term originates in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stabilization_Wedge_Game" title="Stabilization Wedge Game">Stabilization Wedge Game</a>. As a reference unit, a stabilization wedge is equal to the following examples of mitigation initiatives: deployment of two million 1 MW <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a>; completely halting the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and planting of 300 million hectares of trees; the increase in the average energy efficiency of all the world's buildings by 25 percent; or the installation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> facilities in 800 large coal-fired power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Pacala and Socolow proposed in their work, <i>Stabilization Wedges</i>, that seven wedges are required to be delivered by 2050 - at current technologies - to make a significant impact on the mitigation of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are, however, sources that estimate the need for 14 wedges because Pacala and Socolow's proposal would only stabilize carbon dioxide emissions at current levels but not the atmospheric concentration, which is increasing by more than 2 ppm/year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2011, Socolow revised their earlier estimate to nine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="350_ppm">350 ppm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 350 ppm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the target level advocated in a 2008 paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> by climate scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a> and others such as: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajendra_Pachauri" title="Rajendra Pachauri">Rajendra Pachauri</a>, the chairperson of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">U.N.</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the Director of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Institute_on_the_Environment" title="Institute on the Environment">Institute on the Environment</a>, Jonathan Foley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> President of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Institute" title="Pacific Institute">Pacific Institute</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gleick" title="Peter Gleick">Peter H. Gleick</a>, and the Policy Director of the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">Brookings Institution</a>'s Climate and Energy Economics Project Adele C. Morris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  This maximum level is advocated by the <a href="/wiki/350.org" title="350.org">350.org</a> campaign, along with other organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A4llberg_Forum" title="Tällberg Forum">Tällberg Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A strategy proposed is (1) no further <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_and_gas_exploration" title="Oil and gas exploration">oil and gas exploration</a> (so that only already-known reserves will be consumed), (2) the elimination of all <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">uncaptured</a> burning of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> by 2030, and (3) an intensive program of reforestation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative emissions</a> with technologies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a> agriculture and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_capture" title="Air capture">direct air capture</a>.
</p><p>A mitigation scenario modeled by Malte Meinshausen <i>et al.</i> suggested in 2006 that to stabilize CO<sub>2</sub> at 350 ppm, we would have needed to reduce emissions by slightly more than 5%  per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multigas_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-multigas-12">[12]</a></sup> The amount of CO<sub>2</sub> that has been released  to date is believed to be too much to be able to be absorbed by conventional <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">sinks</a> such as trees and soil in order to reach low emission targets, which implies that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative emissions</a> with technologies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a> are needed to reach the 350 ppm target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hare_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hare-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>From an "energy technology and policy perspective", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joe_Romm" title="Joe Romm">Joe Romm</a> says a 350-ppm target will require eight wedges, each saving 1 gigaton of carbon per year, by 2030, and another ten by 2060.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_Stern" title="Nicholas Stern">Nicholas Stern</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stern_Review_on_the_Economics_of_Climate_Change" title="Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change">Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change</a>, called the  "world’s top climate economist", endorses 350 ppm as “a very sensible long-term target.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2009 report, <a href="/wiki/Johan_Rockstr%C3%B6m" title="Johan Rockström">Johan Rockström</a> states 350 ppm is one of the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">planetary boundaries</a> for CO<sub>2</sub> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rockström_a_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rockström_a-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Supporters of the 350 upper limit include <a href="/wiki/Eban_Goodstein" title="Eban Goodstein">Eban Goodstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Ackerman" title="Frank Ackerman">Frank Ackerman</a>, Kristen Sheeran of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economics_for_Equity_and_the_Environment_Network_(E3)" title="Economics for Equity and the Environment Network (E3)">Economics for Equity and the Environment Network (E3)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lester_R._Brown" title="Lester R. Brown">Lester R. Brown</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Worldchanging" title="Worldchanging">Worldchanging</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Alex_Steffen" title="Alex Steffen">Alex Steffen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
the <a href="/wiki/Will_Steger" title="Will Steger">Will Steger</a> Foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Barbara_Kingsolver" title="Barbara Kingsolver">Barbara Kingsolver</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ed_Begley,_Jr." title="Ed Begley, Jr.">Ed Begley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Raitt" title="Bonnie Raitt">Bonnie Raitt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In her speech "The World's <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">Tipping Point</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bianca_Jagger" title="Bianca Jagger">Bianca Jagger</a>
states “the safe upper limit for atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> is no more than 350 ppm." and quotes the report "The Economics of 350: The Benefits and Costs of Climate Stabilization" by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_DeCanio" title="Stephen DeCanio">Stephen J. DeCanio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eban_Goodstein" title="Eban Goodstein">Eban Goodstein</a>, Richard B. Howarth, <a href="/wiki/Richard_B._Norgaard" title="Richard B. Norgaard">Richard B. Norgaard</a> and  Catherine S. Norman, stressing " the need for immediate, direct intervention".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Blue_Ocean_Institute" title="Blue Ocean Institute">Blue Ocean Institute</a> authors Marah J. Hardt and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Safina" title="Carl Safina">Carl Safina</a> support 350 ppm as the maximum upper limit of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere for <a href="/wiki/Marine_life" title="Marine life">marine life</a> <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="450_ppm">450 ppm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 450 ppm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BLUE scenarios in the IEA's <a href="/wiki/Energy_Technology_Perspectives" title="Energy Technology Perspectives">Energy Technology Perspectives</a> publication of 2008 describe pathways to a long-range concentration of 450 ppm. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Romm" title="Joseph Romm">Joseph Romm</a> has sketched how to achieve this target through the application of 14 wedges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>World Energy Outlook 2008</i>, mentioned above, also describes a "450 Policy Scenario", in which extra energy investments to 2030 amount to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>9.3 trillion over the Reference Scenario. The scenario also features, after 2020, the participation of major economies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PRC" title="PRC">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in a global cap-and-trade scheme initially operating in <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> countries. Also the less conservative 450 ppm scenario calls for extensive deployment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative emissions</a>, i.e. the removal of CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere. According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> (IEA) and OECD, "Achieving lower concentration targets (450 ppm) depends significantly on the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OECD_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="550_ppm">550 ppm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 550 ppm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the target advocated (as an upper bound) in the <a href="/wiki/Stern_Review" title="Stern Review">Stern Review</a>. As approximately a doubling of CO<sub>2</sub> levels relative to <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">preindustrial</a> times, it implies a temperature increase of about three degrees, according to conventional estimates of <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming#Pacala_and_Socolow" title="Mitigation of global warming">Pacala and Socolow</a> list 15 "wedges", any 7 of which in combination should suffice to keep CO<sub>2</sub> levels below 550 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/World_Energy_Outlook" title="World Energy Outlook">World Energy Outlook</a> report for 2008 describes a "Reference Scenario" for the world's energy future "which assumes no new government policies beyond those already adopted by mid-2008", and then a "550 Policy Scenario" in which further policies are adopted, a mixture of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap-and-trade" title="Cap-and-trade">cap-and-trade</a> systems,  <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">sectoral</a> agreements and national measures". In the Reference Scenario, between 2006 and 2030 the world invests $26.3 trillion in energy-supply infrastructure; in the 550 Policy Scenario, a further $4.1 trillion is spent in this period, mostly on <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">efficiency</a> increases which deliver fuel cost savings of over $7 trillion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weo08_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-weo08-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_greenhouse_gases">Other greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greenhouse gas concentrations are aggregated in terms of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent#Equivalent_carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a>. Some multi-gas mitigation scenarios have been modeled by Meinshausen <i>et al.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-multigas_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-multigas-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_short-term_focus">As a short-term focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: As a short-term focus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 2000 paper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Hansen argued that the 0.75<span class="nowrap"> </span>°C<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (July 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> rise in average global temperatures over the last 100 years has been driven mainly by greenhouse gases other than carbon dioxide, since warming due to CO<sub>2</sub> had been offset by cooling due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, implying the viability of a strategy initially based around reducing emissions of non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases and of <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>, focusing on CO<sub>2</sub> only in the longer run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>This was also argued by <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">Veerabhadran Ramanathan</a> and Jessica Seddon Wallack the September/October 2009 <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Affairs" title="Foreign Affairs">Foreign Affairs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
