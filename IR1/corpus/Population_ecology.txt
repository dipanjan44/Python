<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Population ecology</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Study of the dynamics of species populations and how these populations interact with the environment</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg/300px-UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg/450px-UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg/600px-UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UN_DESA_continent_population_1950_to_2100.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_growth" title="Human population growth">The human population is growing</a> at a logistic rate and has been affecting the populations of other species in return.  <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">Chemical pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, and population is a very big problem.<a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> are examples of means by which humans may influence the population ecology of other species. As the human population increases, its effect on the populations of other species may also increase.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="784" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/300px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/450px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/600px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Populations cannot grow indefinitely. Population ecology involves studying factors that affect population growth and survival. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" title="Mass extinctions">Mass extinctions</a> are examples of factors that have radically reduced populations' sizes and populations' <a href="/wiki/Survivability" title="Survivability">survivability</a>. The survivability of populations is critical to maintaining high levels of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> on Earth.</div></div></div> <p><b>Population ecology</b>  is a sub-field of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> that deals with the dynamics of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">populations</a> and how these populations interact with the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum1959_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odum1959-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the study of how the <a href="/wiki/Population_size" title="Population size">population sizes</a> of species change over time and space. The term population ecology is often used interchangeably with <a href="/wiki/Population_biology" title="Population biology">population biology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>. 
</p><p>The development of population ecology owes much to <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actuary" title="Actuary">actuarial</a> <a href="/wiki/Life_table" title="Life table">life tables</a>. Population ecology is important in <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation biology</a>, especially in the development of <a href="/wiki/Population_viability_analysis" title="Population viability analysis">population viability analysis</a> (PVA) which makes it possible to predict the long-term probability of a species persisting in a given habitat patch. Although population ecology is a subfield of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, it provides interesting problems for <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematicians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statisticians</a> who work in <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>.
</p>

<p>The most fundamental law of population ecology is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a>' exponential law of population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turchin01_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turchin01-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p><i>A population will grow (or decline) exponentially as long as the environment experienced by all individuals in the population remains constant.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turchin01_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turchin01-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>18</span></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This principle in population ecology provides the basis for formulating predictive theories and tests that follow:
</p><p>Simplified population models usually start with four key variables (four <b>demographic processes</b>) including death, birth, immigration, and emigration. Mathematical models used to calculate changes in population demographics and evolution hold the assumption (or <a href="/wiki/Null_hypothesis" title="Null hypothesis">null hypothesis</a>) of no external influence. <p>where <i>N</i> is the biomass density, <i>a</i> is the maximum per-capita rate of change, and <i>K</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of the population. The formula can be read as follows: the rate of change in the population (<i>dN/dT</i>) is equal to growth (<i>aN</i>) that is limited by carrying capacity <i>(1-N/K)</i>. From these basic mathematical principles the discipline of population ecology expands into a field of investigation that queries the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">demographics</a> of real populations and tests these results against the statistical models. The field of population ecology often uses data on life history and matrix algebra to develop projection matrices on fecundity and survivorship. This information is used for managing wildlife stocks and setting harvest quotas <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandermeer03_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vandermeer03-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berryman92_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berryman92-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg/220px-Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg/330px-Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg/440px-Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Operophtera.brumata.6961.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Operophtera brumata</i> (Winter moth) populations are geometric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The population model below can be manipulated to mathematically infer certain properties of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric" title="Geometric">geometric</a> populations. A population with a size that increases geometrically is a population where generations of reproduction do <b>not</b> overlap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afrc.uamont.edu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-afrc.uamont.edu-8">[8]</a></sup> In each generation there is an <a href="/wiki/Effective_population_size" title="Effective population size">effective population size</a> denoted as <big>N<sub>e</sub></big> which constitutes the number of individuals in the population that are able to reproduce <i>and</i> will reproduce in any reproductive generation in concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the population model below it is assumed that <big>N</big> is the effective population size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afrc.uamont.edu_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-afrc.uamont.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doubling_time_of_geometric_populations">Doubling time of geometric populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Doubling time of geometric populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_(td)_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="963" data-file-width="2075" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png/400px-G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png/600px-G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png/800px-G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_%28td%29_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:G._stearothermophilus_has_a_shorter_doubling_time_(td)_than_E._coli_and_N._meningitidis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b><i>G. stearothermophilus</i> has a shorter doubling time (td) than <i>E. coli</i> and <i>N. meningitidis</i>.</b> Growth rates of 2 <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> species will differ by unexpected orders of magnitude if the doubling times of the 2 species differ by even as little as 10 minutes. In <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a> such as animals, fungi, plants, and protists, doubling times are much longer than in bacteria. This reduces the growth rates of eukaryotes in comparison to Bacteria. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacillus_stearothermophilus" title="Bacillus stearothermophilus">G. stearothermophilus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Neisseria_meningitidis" title="Neisseria meningitidis">N. meningitidis</a></i> have 20 minute,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> 30 minute,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and 40 minute<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> doubling times under optimal conditions respectively. If bacterial populations could grow indefinitely (which they do not) then the number of bacteria in each species would approach infinity (<b>∞</b>). However, the percentage of <i>G. stearothermophilus</i> bacteria out of all the bacteria would approach <b>100%</b> whilst the percentage of <i>E. coli</i> and <i>N. meningitidis</i> combined out of all the bacteria would approach <b>0%</b>. <p>The <a href="/wiki/Doubling_time" title="Doubling time">doubling time</a> of a population is the time required for the population to grow to twice its size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> We can calculate the <a href="/wiki/Doubling_time" title="Doubling time">doubling time</a> of a geometric population using the equation: <big><b>N<sub>t+1</sub> = λ<sup>t</sup>N<sub>t</sub></b></big> by exploiting our knowledge of the fact that the population (<big>N</big>) is twice its size (<big>2N</big>) after the doubling time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afrc.uamont.edu_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-afrc.uamont.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><big><b>2N<sub>t<sub>d</sub></sub> = λ<sup>t<sub>d</sub></sup> × N<sub>t</sub></b>
<b></b></big>
</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<th>Term</th>
<th>Definition
</th></tr>
<tr>

<td>Doubling time.
</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<p><big><b>λ<sup>t<sub>d</sub></sup> = 2N<sub>t<sub>d</sub></sub> /  N<sub>t</sub></b></big>
</p><p><b><big>λ<sup>t<sub>d</sub></sup> = 2</big></b>
</p><p>The doubling time can be found by taking logarithms. For instance:
</p><p><big><b>t<sub>d</sub> × log<sub>2</sub>(λ) = log<sub>2</sub>(2)</b></big>
</p><p><big><b>log<sub>2</sub>(2) = 1</b></big>
</p><p><big><b>t<sub>d</sub> × log<sub>2</sub>(λ) = 1</b></big>
</p><p><b><big>t<sub>d</sub> = 1 / log<sub>2</sub>(λ)</big></b>
</p><p>Or:
</p><p><big><b>t<sub>d</sub> × ln(λ) = ln(2)</b></big>
</p><p><b><big>t<sub>d</sub> = ln(2) / ln(λ)</big></b>
</p><p><b><big>t<sub>d</sub> = 0.693... / ln(λ)</big></b>
</p><p>Therefore:
</p><p><b><big>t<sub>d</sub> = 1 / log<sub>2</sub>(λ) = 0.693... / ln(λ) </big></b>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Half-life_of_geometric_populations">Half-life of geometric populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Half-life of geometric populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of a population is the time taken for the population to decline to half its size. We can calculate the half-life of a geometric population using the equation: <big><b>N<sub>t+1</sub> = λ<sup>t</sup>N<sub>t</sub></b></big> by exploiting our knowledge of the fact that the population (<big>N</big>) is half its size (<big>0.5N</big>) after a half-life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afrc.uamont.edu_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-afrc.uamont.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><big><b>0.5N<sub>t<sub>1/2</sub></sub> = λ<sup>t<sub>1/2</sub></sup> × N<sub>t</sub></b>
<b></b></big>
</p>

<th>Definition

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_relationship_between_geometric_and_exponential_populations">Mathematical relationship between geometric and exponential populations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mathematical relationship between geometric and exponential populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In geometric populations, <big>R</big> and <big>λ</big> represent growth constants (see <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology#Geometric_populations" title="Population ecology">2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology#Geometric_.28R.29_and_finite_.28.CE.BB.29_growth_constants" title="Population ecology">2.3</a>). In <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential</a> populations however, the <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics#Intrinsic_rate_of_increase" title="Population dynamics">intrinsic growth rate</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics#Intrinsic_rate_of_increase" title="Population dynamics">intrinsic rate of increase</a> (<big>r</big>) is the relevant growth constant. Since generations of reproduction in a geometric population do not overlap (e.g. reproduce once a year) but do in an exponential population, geometric and exponential populations are usually considered to be mutually exclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, geometric constants and exponential constants share the mathematical relationship below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afrc.uamont.edu_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-afrc.uamont.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The growth equation for exponential populations is
</p><p><big><b>N<sub>t</sub> = N<sub>0</sub>e<sup>rt</sup></b></big>
</p>

<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler%27s_number" title="Euler's number">Euler's number</a> - A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_constant" title="Universal constant">universal constant</a> often applicable in exponential equations.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><b>r</b></td>
<td><a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics#Intrinsic_rate_of_increase" title="Population dynamics">intrinsic growth rate</a> - also known as <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics#Intrinsic_rate_of_increase" title="Population dynamics">intrinsic rate of increase</a>.
</td></tr>
</tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="767" data-file-width="614" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg/220px-Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg/330px-Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg/440px-Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leonhard_Euler_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> was the mathematician who established the universal constant 2.71828... also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler%27s_number" title="Euler's number">Euler's number</a> or <b>e</b>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Assumption:</b> <big>N<sub>t</sub></big> <i>(of a geometric population)</i> <big>=</big> <big>N<sub>t</sub></big> <i>(of an exponential population)</i>.
</p><p>Therefore:
</p><p><b><big>N<sub>0</sub>e<sup>rt</sup> = N<sub>0</sub>λ<sup>t</sup></big></b>
</p><p><big><b>N<sub>0</sub></b></big> cancels on both sides.
</p><p><b><big>N<sub>0</sub>e<sup>rt</sup> / N<sub>0</sub> = λ<sup>t</sup></big></b>
</p><p><b><big>e<sup>rt</sup> = λ<sup>t</sup></big></b>
</p><p>Take the <a href="/wiki/Natural_logarithm" title="Natural logarithm">natural logarithms</a> of the equation. Using natural logarithms instead of base 10 or base 2 logarithms simplifies the final equation as ln(e) = 1.
</p><p><big><b>rt × ln(e) = t × ln(λ)</b></big>

<h2><span id="r.2FK_selection"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="r/K_selection">r/K selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: r/K selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R/K_selection" title="R/K selection">r/K selection</a></div>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">At its most elementary level, interspecific competition involves two species utilizing a similar <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resource</a>. It rapidly gets more complicated, but stripping the phenomenon of all its complications, this is the basic principle: two consumers consuming the same resource.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandermeer03_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vandermeer03-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>222</span></sup></div>
</div>
<p>An important concept in population ecology is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R/K_selection" title="R/K selection">r/K selection</a> theory. The first variable is <i>r</i> (the intrinsic rate of natural increase in population size, density independent) and the second variable is <i>K</i> (the carrying capacity of a population, density dependent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begon06_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Begon06-15">[15]</a></sup>
An <i>r</i>-selected species (e.g., many kinds of insects, such as aphids<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitman78_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitman78-16">[16]</a></sup>) is one that has high rates of fecundity, low levels of parental investment in the young, and high rates of mortality before individuals reach maturity. Evolution favors productivity in r-selected species. In contrast, a <i>K</i>-selected species (such as humans) has low rates of fecundity, high levels of parental investment in the young, and low rates of mortality as individuals mature. Evolution in <i>K</i>-selected species favors efficiency in the conversion of more <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resources</a> into fewer offspring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacArthur67_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacArthur67-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pianka72_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pianka72-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metapopulation">Metapopulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Metapopulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metapopulation" title="Metapopulation">Metapopulation</a></div><p>
Populations are also studied and conceptualized through the "<a href="/wiki/Metapopulation" title="Metapopulation">metapopulation</a>" concept. The metapopulation concept was introduced in 1969:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levins69_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levins69-19">[19]</a></sup></p><blockquote><p> "as a population of populations which go extinct locally and recolonize."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levins70_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levins70-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>105</span></sup></p></blockquote><p> Metapopulation ecology is a simplified model of the landscape into patches of varying levels of quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanski98_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanski98-21">[21]</a></sup> Patches are either occupied or they are not. Migrants moving among the patches are structured into metapopulations either as sources or sinks. Source patches are productive sites that generate a seasonal supply of migrants to other patch locations. Sink patches are unproductive sites that only receive migrants. In metapopulation terminology there are emigrants (individuals that leave a patch) and immigrants (individuals that move into a patch). Metapopulation models examine patch dynamics over time to answer questions about spatial and demographic ecology. An important concept in metapopulation ecology is the rescue effect, where small patches of lower quality (i.e., sinks) are maintained by a seasonal influx of new immigrants. Metapopulation structure evolves from year to year, where some patches are sinks, such as dry years, and become sources when conditions are more favorable. Ecologists utilize a mixture of computer models and field studies to explain metapopulation structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanski04_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanski04-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_ecology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The older term, autecology (from Greek: αὐτο, <i>auto</i>, "self"; οίκος, oikos, "household"; and λόγος, logos, "knowledge"), refers to roughly the same field of study as population ecology. It derives from the division of ecology into autecology—the study of individual species in relation to the environment—and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_ecology" title="Community ecology">synecology</a>—the study of groups of organisms in relation to the environment—or community ecology. Odum (1959, p. 8) considered that synecology should be divided into population ecology, community ecology, and ecosystem ecology, defining autecology as essentially "species ecology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum1959_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Odum1959-1">[1]</a></sup> However, for some time biologists have recognized that the more significant level of organization of a species is a population, because at this level the species gene pool is most coherent. In fact, Odum regarded "autecology" as no longer a "present tendency" in ecology (i.e., an archaic term), although included "species ecology"—studies emphasizing <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life history</a> and behavior as adaptations to the environment of individual organisms or species—as one of four subdivisions of ecology.
</p>
