<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Melting point</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the physical processes that take place at the melting point, see <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">Melting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">Freezing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">Crystallization</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Freezing point" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Freezing_point_(disambiguation)" title="Freezing point (disambiguation)">Freezing point (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>melting point</b> (or, rarely, <b>liquefaction point</b>) of a substance is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> at which it changes <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">state</a> from <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> to <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> at atmospheric pressure. At the melting point the solid and liquid phase exist in equilibrium. The melting point of a substance depends on pressure and is usually specified at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard pressure</a>. When considered as the temperature of the reverse change from liquid to solid, it is referred to as the <b>freezing point</b> or <b>crystallization point</b>. Because of the ability of some substances to <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercool</a>, the freezing point is not considered as a characteristic property of a substance. When the "characteristic freezing point" of a substance is determined, in fact the actual methodology is almost always "the principle of observing the disappearance rather than the formation of ice", that is, the <a href="#Melting_point_measurements">melting point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elements_by_melting_point" title="List of elements by melting point">List of elements by melting point</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="574" data-file-width="921" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg/220px-Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg/330px-Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg/440px-Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carboxylic.Acids.Melting.%26.Boiling.Points.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Melting points  and boiling points (in pink) of the first eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acids" title="Carboxylic acids">carboxylic acids</a> (°C)</div></div></div>
<p>For most substances, <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> points are approximately equal. For example, the melting point <i>and</i> freezing point of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> is 234.32 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a> (−38.83 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> or −37.89 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, certain substances possess differing solid-liquid transition temperatures. For example, <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a> melts at 85 °C (185 °F) and solidifies from 31 °C (88 °F; 304 K); such direction dependence is known as <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>. The melting point of ice at 1 atmosphere of pressure is very close <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to 0 °C (32 °F; 273 K); this is also known as the ice point. In the presence of <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleating substances</a>, the freezing point of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is not always the same as the melting point. In the absence of nucleators water can exist as a <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercool</a>ed liquid down to −48.3 °C (−55°F, 224.8 K) before freezing. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with the highest melting point is <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, at 3,414 °C (6,177 °F; 3,687 K);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> this property makes tungsten excellent for use as filaments in light bulbs. The often-cited <b><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a></b> does not melt at ambient pressure but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(physics)" title="Sublimation (physics)">sublimes</a> at about 3,726.85 °C (6,740.33 °F; 4,000.00 K); a liquid phase only exists above pressures of 10 MPa (99 atm) and estimated 4,030–4,430 °C (7,290–8,010 °F; 4,300–4,700 K) (see <a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_basic_phase_diagram.png" title="File:Carbon basic phase diagram.png">carbon phase diagram</a>). <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_hafnium_carbide" title="Tantalum hafnium carbide">Tantalum hafnium carbide</a> (Ta<sub>4</sub>HfC<sub>5</sub>) is a <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> compound with a very high melting point of 4215 K (3942 °C, 7128 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> At the other end of the scale, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> does not freeze at all at normal pressure even at temperatures close to <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>; pressure greater than twenty times of atmosphere of normal one is necessary.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Melting_point_measurements">Melting point measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Melting point measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melting_point_apparatus" title="Melting point apparatus">Melting point apparatus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Koflerbank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1196" data-file-width="1988" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koflerbank.jpg/220px-Koflerbank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koflerbank.jpg/330px-Koflerbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Koflerbank.jpg/440px-Koflerbank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Koflerbank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kofler bench with samples for calibration</div></div></div>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laboratory_techniques" title="Laboratory techniques">laboratory techniques</a> exist for the determination of melting points.
A <a href="/wiki/Kofler_bench" title="Kofler bench">Kofler bench</a> is a metal strip with a temperature gradient (range from room temperature to 300 °C). Any substance can be placed on a section of the strip, revealing its thermal behaviour at the temperature at that point. <a href="/wiki/Differential_scanning_calorimetry" title="Differential scanning calorimetry">Differential scanning calorimetry</a> gives information on melting point together with its <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_fusion" title="Enthalpy of fusion">enthalpy of fusion</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1212" data-file-width="1194" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg/220px-Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg/330px-Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg/440px-Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kr%C3%BCss_M5000.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Automatic digital melting point meter</div></div></div>
<p>A basic melting point apparatus for the analysis of crystalline solids consists of an <a href="/wiki/Oil_bath" title="Oil bath">oil bath</a> with a transparent window (most basic design: a <a href="/wiki/Thiele_tube" title="Thiele tube">Thiele tube</a>) and a simple magnifier. The several grains of a solid are placed in a thin glass tube and partially immersed in the oil bath. The oil bath is heated (and stirred) and with the aid of the magnifier (and external light source) melting of the individual crystals at a certain temperature can be observed. In large/small devices, the sample is placed in a heating block, and optical detection is automated.
</p><p>The measurement can also be made continuously with an operating process. For instance, oil refineries measure the freeze point of diesel fuel online, meaning that the sample is taken from the process and measured automatically. This allows for more frequent measurements as the sample does not have to be manually collected and taken to a remote laboratory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques_for_refractory_materials">Techniques for refractory materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Techniques for refractory materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For refractory materials (e.g. platinum, tungsten, tantalum, some carbides and nitrides, etc.) the extremely high melting point (typically considered to be above, say, 1800°C) may be determined by heating the material in a black body furnace and measuring the black-body temperature with an optical <a href="/wiki/Pyrometer" title="Pyrometer">pyrometer</a>. For the highest melting materials, this may require extrapolation by several hundred degrees. The spectral radiance from an incandescent body is known to be a function of its temperature. An optical pyrometer matches the radiance of a body under study to the radiance of a source that has been previously calibrated as a function of temperature. In this way, the measurement of the absolute magnitude of the intensity of radiation is unnecessary. However, known temperatures must be used to determine the calibration of the pyrometer. For temperatures above the calibration range of the source, an extrapolation technique must be employed. This extrapolation is accomplished by using <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck's law">Planck's law</a> of radiation. The constants in this equation are not known with sufficient accuracy, causing errors in the extrapolation to become larger at higher temperatures. However, standard techniques have been developed to perform this extrapolation.
</p><p>Consider the case of using gold as the source (mp = 1063°C). In this technique, the current through the filament of the pyrometer is adjusted until the light intensity of the filament matches that of a black-body at the melting point of gold. This establishes the primary calibration temperature and can be expressed in terms of current through the pyrometer lamp. With the same current setting, the pyrometer is sighted on another black-body at a higher temperature. An absorbing medium of known transmission is inserted between the pyrometer and this black-body. The temperature of the black-body is then adjusted until a match exists between its intensity and that of the pyrometer filament. The true higher temperature of the black-body is then determined from Planck's Law. The absorbing medium is then removed and the current through the filament is adjusted to match the filament intensity to that of the black-body. This establishes a second calibration point for the pyrometer. This step is repeated to carry the calibration to higher temperatures. Now, temperatures and their corresponding pyrometer filament currents are known and a curve of temperature versus current can be drawn. This curve can then be extrapolated to very high temperatures.
</p><p>In determining melting points of a refractory substance by this method, it is necessary to either have black body conditions or to know the <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> of the material being measured. The containment of the high melting material in the liquid state may introduce experimental difficulties. Melting temperatures of some refractory metals have thus been measured by observing the radiation from a black body cavity in solid metal specimens that were much longer than they were wide. To form such a cavity, a hole is drilled perpendicular to the long axis at the center of a rod of the material. These rods are then heated by passing a very large current through them, and the radiation emitted from the hole is observed with an optical pyrometer. The point of melting is indicated by the darkening of the hole when the liquid phase appears, destroying the black body conditions. Today, containerless laser heating techniques, combined with fast pyrometers and spectro-pyrometers, are employed to allow for precise control of the time for which the sample is kept at extreme temperatures. Such experiments of sub-second duration address several of the challenges associated with more traditional melting point measurements made at very high temperatures, such as sample vaporization and reaction with the container.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Melting_curve_of_water.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="567" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Melting_curve_of_water.svg/220px-Melting_curve_of_water.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Melting_curve_of_water.svg/330px-Melting_curve_of_water.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Melting_curve_of_water.svg/440px-Melting_curve_of_water.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Melting_curve_of_water.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pressure dependence of water melting point.</div></div></div>
<p>For a solid to melt, heat is required to raise its temperature to the melting point. However, further heat needs to be supplied for the melting to take place: this is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_fusion" title="Heat of fusion">heat of fusion</a>, and is an example of <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>.
</p><p>From a thermodynamics point of view, at the melting point the change in <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> (ΔG) of the material is zero, but the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> (<i>H</i>) and the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> (<i>S</i>) of the material are increasing (ΔH, ΔS &gt; 0). Melting phenomenon happens when the Gibbs free energy of the liquid becomes lower than the solid for that material. At various pressures this happens at a specific temperature. It can also be shown that:

<p>Here <i>T</i>, <i>ΔS</i> and <i>ΔH</i> are respectively the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> at the melting point, change of entropy of melting and the change of enthalpy of melting.
</p><p>The melting point is sensitive to extremely large changes in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, but generally this sensitivity is orders of magnitude less than that for the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>, because the solid-liquid transition represents only a small change in volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> If, as observed in most cases, a substance is more dense in the solid than in the liquid state, the melting point will increase with increases in pressure. Otherwise the reverse behavior occurs. Notably, this is the case of water, as illustrated graphically to the right, but also of Si, Ge, Ga, Bi. With extremely large changes in pressure, substantial changes to the melting point are observed. For example, the melting point of silicon at ambient pressure (0.1 MPa) is 1415 °C, but at pressures in excess of 10 GPa it decreases to 1000 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Melting points are often used to characterize organic and inorganic compounds and to ascertain their <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Purity" title="wiktionary:Purity">purity</a>. The melting point of a pure substance is always higher and has a smaller range than the melting point of an impure substance or, more generally, of mixtures. The higher the quantity of other components, the lower the melting point and the broader will be the melting point range, often referred to as the "pasty range". The temperature at which melting begins for a mixture is known as the "solidus" while the temperature where melting is complete is called the "liquidus". Eutectics are special types of mixtures that behave like single phases. They melt sharply at a constant temperature to form a liquid of the same composition. Alternatively, on cooling a liquid with the eutectic composition will solidify as uniformly dispersed, small (fine-grained) mixed crystals with the same composition.
</p><p>In contrast to crystalline solids, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> do not possess a melting point;
on heating they undergo a smooth <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glass transition</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Viscous_liquid" title="Viscous liquid">viscous liquid</a>.
Upon further heating, they gradually soften, which can be characterized by certain <a href="/wiki/Softening_point" title="Softening point">softening points</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freezing-point_depression">Freezing-point depression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Freezing-point depression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Freezing-point_depression" title="Freezing-point depression">Freezing-point depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">Supercooling</a></div>
<p>The freezing point of a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> is depressed when another compound is added, meaning that a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> has a lower freezing point than a pure solvent. This phenomenon is used in technical applications to avoid freezing, for instance by adding salt or ethylene glycol to water.
</p>
<h2><span id="Carnelley.27s_rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carnelley's_rule">Carnelley's rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carnelley's rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, <b>Carnelley's rule</b>, established in 1882 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Carnelley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Carnelley (page does not exist)">Thomas Carnelley</a>, states that <i>high <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a> is associated with high melting point</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> Carnelley based his rule on examination of 15,000 chemical compounds. For example, for three <a href="/wiki/Structural_isomer" title="Structural isomer">structural isomers</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>12</sub> the melting point increases in the series <a href="/wiki/Isopentane" title="Isopentane">isopentane</a> −160 °C (113 K) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-pentane" title="N-pentane">n-pentane</a> −129.8 °C (143 K) and <a href="/wiki/Neopentane" title="Neopentane">neopentane</a> −16.4 °C (256.8 K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> Likewise in <a href="/wiki/Xylene" title="Xylene">xylenes</a> and also <a href="/wiki/Dichlorobenzene" title="Dichlorobenzene">dichlorobenzenes</a> the melting point increases in the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arene_substitution_patterns" title="Arene substitution patterns">meta, ortho and then para</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">Pyridine</a> has a lower symmetry than <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> hence its lower melting point but the melting point again increases with <a href="/wiki/Diazine" title="Diazine">diazine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triazine" title="Triazine">triazines</a>. Many cage-like compounds like <a href="/wiki/Adamantane" title="Adamantane">adamantane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cubane" title="Cubane">cubane</a> with high symmetry have relatively high melting points.
</p><p>A high melting point results from a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_fusion" title="Heat of fusion">heat of fusion</a>, a low <a href="/wiki/Entropy_of_fusion" title="Entropy of fusion">entropy of fusion</a>, or a combination of both. In highly symmetrical molecules the crystal phase is densely packed with many efficient intermolecular interactions resulting in a higher enthalpy change on melting.
</p>
<h2><span id="Predicting_the_melting_point_of_substances_.28Lindemann.27s_criterion.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Predicting_the_melting_point_of_substances_(Lindemann's_criterion)">Predicting the melting point of substances (Lindemann's criterion)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Predicting the melting point of substances (Lindemann's criterion)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Melting_point_prediction">Melting point prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melting_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Melting point prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2011, <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Aesar" title="Alfa Aesar">Alfa Aesar</a> released over 10,000 melting points of compounds from their catalog as <a href="/wiki/Open_data" title="Open data">open data</a>. This dataset has been used to create a <a href="/wiki/Random_forest" title="Random forest">random forest</a> model for melting point prediction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lang_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-18">[16]</a></sup> which is now freely available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LangGuha_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-LangGuha-19">[17]</a></sup> Open melting point data are also available from <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Precedings" title="Nature Precedings">Nature Precedings</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-20">[18]</a></sup> High quality data mined from patents and also models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCHEM_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCHEM-21">[19]</a></sup> developed with these data were published by Tetko <i>et al</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tetko_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tetko-22">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
