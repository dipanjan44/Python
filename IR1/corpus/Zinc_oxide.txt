<p><b>Zinc oxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">Zn</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>. ZnO is a white powder that is insoluble in water, and it is widely used as an additive in numerous materials and products including rubbers, plastics, ceramics, glass, cement, lubricants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wear_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wear-5">[5]</a></sup> paints, ointments, adhesives, sealants, pigments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_metabolism" title="Zinc metabolism">foods</a>, batteries, ferrites, fire retardants, and first-aid tapes.  Although it occurs naturally as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Zincite" title="Zincite">zincite</a>, most zinc oxide is produced synthetically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>ZnO is a wide-bandgap semiconductor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/II-VI_semiconductor" title="II-VI semiconductor">II-VI semiconductor group</a>. The native <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">doping</a> of the semiconductor due to oxygen vacancies or zinc interstitials is n-type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozgur_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ozgur-7">[7]</a></sup> This semiconductor has several favorable properties, including good transparency, high <a href="/wiki/Electron_mobility" title="Electron mobility">electron mobility</a>, wide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandgap" title="Bandgap">bandgap</a>, and strong room-temperature <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">luminescence</a>. Those properties are valuable in emerging applications for: transparent <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">liquid crystal displays</a>, energy-saving or heat-protecting windows, and electronics as thin-film <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span></h2>
<p>Pure ZnO is a white powder, but in nature it occurs as the rare mineral <a href="/wiki/Zincite" title="Zincite">zincite</a>, which usually contains manganese and other impurities that confer a yellow to red color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-k1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crystalline zinc oxide is <a href="/wiki/Thermochromism" title="Thermochromism">thermochromic</a>, changing from white to yellow when heated in air and reverting to white on cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup> This color change is caused by a small loss of oxygen to the environment at high temperatures to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric" title="Non-stoichiometric">non-stoichiometric</a> Zn<sub>1+x</sub>O, where at 800 °C, x = 0.00007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc oxide is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric_oxide" title="Amphoteric oxide">amphoteric oxide</a>. It is nearly <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">insoluble</a> in water, but it will dissolve in most <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrochloric" title="Hydrochloric">hydrochloric</a> acid:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ZnO  +  2 HCl   →   ZnCl<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Solid zinc oxide will also dissolve in alkalis to give soluble zincates:
</p>
<dl><dd>ZnO  +  2 NaOH  +  H<sub>2</sub>O   →   Na<sub>2</sub>[Zn(OH)<sub>4</sub>]</dd></dl>
<p>ZnO reacts slowly with fatty acids in oils to produce the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Carboxylate" title="Carboxylate">carboxylates</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oleate" title="Oleate">oleate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stearate" title="Stearate">stearate</a>. ZnO forms cement-like products when mixed with a strong aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a> and these are best described as zinc hydroxy chlorides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This cement was used in dentistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferracane_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferracane-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hopeite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="700" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hopeite.jpg/150px-Hopeite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hopeite.jpg/225px-Hopeite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Hopeite.jpg/300px-Hopeite.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hopeite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hopeite</div></div></div>
<p>ZnO also forms cement-like material when treated with <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>; related materials are used in dentistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferracane_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ferracane-12">[12]</a></sup> A major component of zinc phosphate cement produced by this reaction is <a href="/wiki/Hopeite" title="Hopeite">hopeite</a>, Zn<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>ZnO decomposes into zinc vapor and oxygen at around 1975 °C with a standard oxygen pressure. In a <a href="/wiki/Carbothermic_reaction" title="Carbothermic reaction">carbothermic reaction</a>, heating with carbon converts the oxide into zinc vapor at a much lower temperature (around 950 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ZnO  +  C   →   Zn<sub>(Vapor)</sub>  +  CO</dd></dl>
<p>Zinc oxide can react violently with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> powders, with chlorinated rubber and <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a> on heating causing fire and explosion hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a>. This reaction is used commercially.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wurtzite_polyhedra.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1417" data-file-width="1417" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Wurtzite_polyhedra.png/150px-Wurtzite_polyhedra.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Wurtzite_polyhedra.png/225px-Wurtzite_polyhedra.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Wurtzite_polyhedra.png/300px-Wurtzite_polyhedra.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wurtzite_polyhedra.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wurtzite structure</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1001" data-file-width="1100" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png/150px-Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png/225px-Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png/300px-Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sphalerite-unit-cell-depth-fade-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A zincblende unit cell</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h3>
<p>Zinc oxide crystallizes in two main <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">forms</a>, hexagonal <a href="/wiki/Wurtzite_crystal_structure" title="Wurtzite crystal structure">wurtzite</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fierro_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-fierro-17">[17]</a></sup> and cubic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zincblende_structure" title="Zincblende structure">zincblende</a>.  The wurtzite structure is most stable at ambient conditions and thus most common. The zincblende form can be stabilized by growing ZnO on substrates with cubic lattice structure. In both cases, the zinc and oxide centers are <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a>, the most characteristic geometry for Zn(II).  ZnO converts to the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">rocksalt</a> motif at relatively high pressures about 10 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozgur_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ozgur-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hexagonal and zincblende polymorphs have no <a href="/wiki/Reflection_symmetry" title="Reflection symmetry">inversion symmetry</a> (reflection of a crystal relative to any given point does not transform it into itself). This and other lattice symmetry properties result in <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a> of the hexagonal and zincblende ZnO, and <a href="/wiki/Pyroelectricity" title="Pyroelectricity">pyroelectricity</a> of hexagonal ZnO.
</p><p>The hexagonal structure has a point group 6 mm (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann-Mauguin_notation" title="Hermann-Mauguin notation">Hermann-Mauguin notation</a>) or C<sub>6v</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Schoenflies_notation" title="Schoenflies notation">Schoenflies notation</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> is P6<sub>3</sub>mc or C<sub>6v</sub><sup>4</sup>. The lattice constants are <i>a</i> = 3.25 Å and <i>c</i> = 5.2 Å; their ratio <i>c/a</i> ~ 1.60 is close to the ideal value for hexagonal cell <i>c/a</i> = 1.633.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As in most <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group II-VI</a> materials, the bonding in ZnO is largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic</a> (Zn<sup>2+</sup>–O<sup>2−</sup>) with the corresponding radii of 0.074 nm for Zn<sup>2+</sup> and 0.140 nm for O<sup>2−</sup>. This property accounts for the preferential formation of wurtzite rather than zinc blende structure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> as well as the strong <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a> of ZnO. Because of the polar Zn-O bonds, zinc and oxygen planes are electrically charged. To maintain electrical neutrality, those planes reconstruct at atomic level in most relative materials, but not in ZnO – its surfaces are atomically flat, stable and exhibit no reconstruction. This anomaly of ZnO is not fully explained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutta_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-dutta-20">[20]</a></sup> However, studies using wurtzoid structures explained the origin of surface flatness and the absence of reconstruction at ZnO wurtzite surfaces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in addition to the origin of charges on ZnO planes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_properties">Mechanical properties</span></h3>
<p>ZnO is a relatively soft material with approximate hardness of 4.5 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_scale" title="Mohs scale">Mohs scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wear_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wear-5">[5]</a></sup> Its elastic constants are smaller than those of relevant III-V semiconductors, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GaN" title="GaN">GaN</a>. The high heat capacity and heat conductivity, low thermal expansion and high melting temperature of ZnO are beneficial for ceramics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porter_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-porter-22">[22]</a></sup> ZnO exhibits a very long lived optical phonon E2(low) with a lifetime as high as 133 ps at 10 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Among the tetrahedrally bonded semiconductors, it has been stated that ZnO has the highest piezoelectric tensor, or at least one comparable to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GaN" title="GaN">GaN</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AlN" title="AlN">AlN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This property makes it a technologically important material for many <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectrical</a> applications, which require a large electromechanical coupling.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_properties">Electrical properties</span></h3>
<p>ZnO has a relatively large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_bandgap" title="Direct bandgap">direct</a> <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of ~3.3 eV at room temperature. Advantages associated with a large band gap include higher breakdown voltages, ability to sustain large electric fields, lower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_noise" title="Electronic noise">electronic noise</a>, and high-temperature and high-power operation. The bandgap of ZnO can further be tuned to ~3–4 eV by its alloying with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_oxide" title="Cadmium oxide">cadmium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozgur_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ozgur-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most ZnO has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" title="N-type semiconductor"><i>n</i>-type</a> character, even in the absence of intentional <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">doping</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">Nonstoichiometry</a> is typically the origin of n-type character, but the subject remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Look1999_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Look1999-25">[25]</a></sup> An alternative explanation has been proposed, based on theoretical calculations, that unintentional substitutional hydrogen impurities are responsible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janotti2007_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janotti2007-26">[26]</a></sup> Controllable n-type doping is easily achieved by substituting Zn with group-III elements such as Al, Ga, In or by substituting oxygen with group-VII elements <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reliable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-type_semiconductor" title="P-type semiconductor">p-type</a> doping of ZnO remains difficult. This problem originates from low solubility of p-type dopants and their compensation by abundant n-type impurities. This problem is observed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GaN" title="GaN">GaN</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZnSe" title="ZnSe">ZnSe</a>. Measurement of p-type in "intrinsically" n-type material is complicated by the inhomogeneity of samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Current limitations to p-doping limit electronic and optoelectronic applications of ZnO, which usually require junctions of n-type and p-type material. Known p-type dopants include group-I elements Li, Na, K; group-V elements N, P and As; as well as copper and silver. However, many of these form deep acceptors and do not produce significant p-type conduction at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ozgur_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-ozgur-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Electron_mobility" title="Electron mobility">Electron mobility</a> of ZnO strongly varies with temperature and has a maximum of ~2000 cm<sup>2</sup>/(V·s) at 80 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Data on hole mobility are scarce with values in the range 5–30 cm<sup>2</sup>/(V·s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>ZnO discs, acting as a  <a href="/wiki/Varistor" title="Varistor">varistor</a>, are the active material in most <a href="/wiki/Surge_arrester" title="Surge arrester">surge arresters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Zinc_smelting" title="Zinc smelting">Zinc smelting</a></div>
<p>For industrial use, ZnO is produced at levels of 10<sup>5</sup> tons per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-k1-8">[8]</a></sup> by three main processes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porter_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-porter-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_process">Indirect process</span></h3>
<p>In the indirect or French process, metallic zinc is melted in a graphite crucible and vaporized at temperatures above 907 °C (typically around 1000 °C). Zinc vapor reacts with the oxygen in the air to give ZnO, accompanied by a drop in its temperature and bright luminescence. Zinc oxide particles are transported into a cooling duct and collected in a bag house. This indirect method was popularized by LeClaire (France) in 1844 and therefore is commonly known as the French process. Its product normally consists of agglomerated zinc oxide particles with an average size of 0.1 to a few micrometers. By weight, most of the world's zinc oxide is manufactured via French process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_process">Direct process</span></h3>
<p>The direct or American process starts with diverse contaminated zinc composites, such as <a href="/wiki/Calamine_(mineral)" title="Calamine (mineral)">zinc ores</a> or smelter by-products. The zinc precursors are reduced (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbothermal_reduction" title="Carbothermal reduction">carbothermal reduction</a>) by heating with a source of carbon such as <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">anthracite</a> to produce zinc vapor, which is then oxidized as in the indirect process. Because of the lower purity of the source material, the final product is also of lower quality in the direct process as compared to the indirect one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wet_chemical_process">Wet chemical process</span></h3>
<p>A small amount of industrial production involves wet chemical processes, which start with aqueous solutions of zinc salts, from which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_carbonate" title="Zinc carbonate">zinc carbonate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc_hydroxide" title="Zinc hydroxide">zinc hydroxide</a> is precipitated. The solid precipitate is then calcined at temperatures around 800 °C.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_synthesis">Laboratory synthesis</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="275" data-file-width="353" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1a/Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg/220px-Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1a/Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg/330px-Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/1a/Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Synthetic_Zincite_Crystals.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The red and green colors of these synthetic ZnO crystals result from different concentrations of oxygen vacancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-growth_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-growth-33">[33]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Numerous specialised methods exist for producing ZnO for scientific studies and niche applications. These methods can be classified by the resulting ZnO form (bulk, thin film, <a href="/wiki/Nanowire" title="Nanowire">nanowire</a>), temperature ("low", that is close to room temperature or "high", that is T ~ 1000 °C), process type (vapor deposition or growth from solution) and other parameters.
</p><p>Large single crystals (many cubic centimeters) can be grown by the gas transport (vapor-phase deposition), <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_synthesis" title="Hydrothermal synthesis">hydrothermal synthesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutta_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-dutta-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-growth_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-growth-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> or melt growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melt_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-melt-2">[2]</a></sup> However, because of high <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> of ZnO, growth from the melt is problematic. Growth by gas transport is difficult to control, leaving the hydrothermal method as a preference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-melt_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-melt-2">[2]</a></sup> Thin films can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapour_phase_epitaxy" title="Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy">metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrophoretic_deposition" title="Electrophoretic deposition">electrodeposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulsed_laser_deposition" title="Pulsed laser deposition">pulsed laser deposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">sputtering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a> synthesis, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>, spray pyrolysis, etc.
</p><p>Ordinary white powdered zinc oxide can be produced in the laboratory by electrolyzing a solution of sodium bicarbonate with a zinc anode. Zinc hydroxide and hydrogen gas are produced. The zinc hydroxide upon heating decomposes to zinc oxide.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ZnO_nanostructures">ZnO nanostructures</span></h3>
<p>Nanostructures of ZnO can be synthesized into a variety of morphologies including nanowires, <a href="/wiki/Nanorod" title="Nanorod">nanorods</a>, tetrapods, nanobelts, nanoflowers, nanoparticles etc. Nanostructures can be obtained with most above-mentioned techniques, at certain conditions, and also with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapor-liquid-solid_method" title="Vapor-liquid-solid method">vapor-liquid-solid method</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutta_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-dutta-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The synthesis is typically carried out at temperatures of about 90 °C, in an equimolar aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_nitrate" title="Zinc nitrate">zinc nitrate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexamine" title="Hexamine">hexamine</a>, the latter providing the basic environment. Certain additives, such as polyethylene glycol or polyethylenimine, can improve the aspect ratio of the ZnO nanowires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Doping of the ZnO nanowires has been achieved by adding other metal nitrates to the growth solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The morphology of the resulting nanostructures can be tuned by changing the parameters relating to the precursor composition (such as the zinc concentration and pH) or to the thermal treatment (such as the temperature and heating rate).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aligned ZnO nanowires on pre-seeded <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> substrates have been grown using aqueous zinc salts such as zinc nitrate and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_acetate" title="Zinc acetate">zinc acetate</a> in basic environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Pre-seeding substrates with ZnO creates sites for homogeneous nucleation of ZnO crystal during the synthesis. Common pre-seeding methods include in-situ thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_acetate" title="Zinc acetate">zinc acetate</a> crystallites, spincoating of ZnO nanoparticles and the use of <a href="/wiki/Physical_vapor_deposition" title="Physical vapor deposition">physical vapor deposition</a> methods to deposit ZnO thin films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Pre-seeding can be performed in conjunction with top down patterning methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam_lithography" title="Electron beam lithography">electron beam lithography</a> and nanosphere lithography to designate nucleation sites prior to growth. Aligned ZnO nanowires can be used in <a href="/wiki/Dye-sensitized_solar_cell" title="Dye-sensitized solar cell">dye-sensitized solar cells</a> and field emission devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Zinc compounds were probably used by early humans, in processed and unprocessed forms, as a paint or medicinal ointment, but their composition is uncertain. The use of <i>pushpanjan</i>, probably zinc oxide, as a salve for eyes and open wounds, is mentioned in the Indian medical text the <a href="/wiki/Charaka_Samhita" title="Charaka Samhita">Charaka Samhita</a>, thought to date from 500 BC or before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Zinc oxide ointment is also mentioned by the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> physician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscorides" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a> (1st century AD.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c111_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-c111-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> suggested treating ulcerating cancers with zinc oxide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> as did <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i>. Zinc oxide is no longer used for treating skin cancer, though it is still used as an ingredient in products such as <a href="/wiki/Baby_powder" title="Baby powder">baby powder</a> and creams against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaper_rash" title="Diaper rash">diaper rashes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calamine" title="Calamine">calamine</a> cream, anti-<a href="/wiki/Dandruff" title="Dandruff">dandruff</a> <a href="/wiki/Shampoo" title="Shampoo">shampoos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> ointments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harding_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harding-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Romans produced considerable quantities of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> (an alloy of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>) as early as 200 BC by a cementation process where copper was reacted with zinc oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The zinc oxide is thought to have been produced by heating zinc ore in a shaft furnace. This liberated metallic zinc as a vapor, which then ascended the flue and condensed as the oxide. This process was described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscorides" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a> in the 1st century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Zinc oxide has also been recovered from zinc mines at Zawar in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, dating from the second half of the first millennium BC. This was presumably also made in the same way and used to produce brass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c111_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-c111-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>From the 12th to the 16th century zinc and zinc oxide were recognized and produced in India using a primitive form of the direct synthesis process. From India, zinc manufacture moved to China in the 17th century. In 1743, the first European zinc smelter was established in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main usage of zinc oxide (zinc white) was in paints and as an additive to ointments. Zinc white was accepted as a pigment in oil paintings by 1834 but it did not mix well with oil. This problem was solved by optimizing the synthesis of ZnO. In 1845, LeClaire in Paris was producing the oil paint on a large scale, and by 1850, zinc white was being manufactured throughout Europe. The success of zinc white paint was due to its advantages over the traditional white lead: zinc white is essentially permanent in sunlight, it is not blackened by sulfur-bearing air, it is non-toxic and more economical. Because zinc white is so "clean" it is valuable for making tints with other colors, but it makes a rather brittle dry film when unmixed with other colors. For example, during the late 1890s and early 1900s, some artists used zinc white as a ground for their oil paintings. All those paintings developed cracks over the years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent times, most zinc oxide was used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> industry to resist <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>. In the 1970s, the second largest application of ZnO was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photocopying" title="Photocopying">photocopying</a>. High-quality ZnO produced by the "French process" was added to photocopying paper as a filler. This application was soon displaced by <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porter_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-porter-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<p>The applications of zinc oxide powder are numerous, and the principal ones are summarized below. Most applications exploit the reactivity of the oxide as a precursor to other zinc compounds. For material science applications, zinc oxide has high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, high thermal conductivity, binding, antibacterial and UV-protection properties. Consequently, it is added into materials and products including plastics, ceramics, glass, cement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> rubber, lubricants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wear_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-wear-5">[5]</a></sup> paints, ointments, adhesive, sealants, <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> manufacturing, pigments, foods, batteries, ferrites, fire retardants, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap2_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap2-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rubber_manufacture">Rubber manufacture</span></h3>
<p>Between 50% and 60% of ZnO use is in the rubber industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moezzi_2012_1–22_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moezzi_2012_1–22-55">[55]</a></sup> Zinc oxide along with <a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">stearic acid</a> is used in the <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanization</a> of rubber<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porter_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-porter-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap1-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> ZnO additive also protect rubber from fungi (see medical applications) and UV light.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceramic_industry">Ceramic industry</span></h3>
<p>Ceramic industry consumes a significant amount of zinc oxide, in particular in ceramic glaze and frit compositions. The relatively high heat capacity, thermal conductivity and high temperature stability of ZnO coupled with a comparatively low coefficient of expansion are desirable properties in the production of ceramics. ZnO affects the melting point and optical properties of the glazes, enamels, and ceramic formulations. Zinc oxide as a low expansion, secondary flux improves the elasticity of glazes by reducing the change in viscosity as a function of temperature and helps prevent crazing and shivering. By substituting ZnO for BaO and PbO, the heat capacity is decreased and the thermal conductivity is increased. Zinc in small amounts improves the development of glossy and brilliant surfaces. However, in moderate to high amounts, it produces matte and crystalline surfaces. With regard to color, zinc has a complicated influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moezzi_2012_1–22_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moezzi_2012_1–22-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span></h3>
<p>Zinc oxide as a mixture with about 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is called <a href="/wiki/Calamine" title="Calamine">calamine</a> and is used in calamine lotion. Two minerals, <a href="/wiki/Zincite" title="Zincite">zincite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemimorphite" title="Hemimorphite">hemimorphite</a>, have been historically called <a href="/wiki/Calamine_(mineral)" title="Calamine (mineral)">calamine</a>. When mixed with <a href="/wiki/Eugenol" title="Eugenol">eugenol</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide_eugenol" title="Zinc oxide eugenol">zinc oxide eugenol</a> is formed, which has applications as a <a href="/wiki/Dental_restoration" title="Dental restoration">restorative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prosthodontics" title="Prosthodontics">prosthodontic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferracane_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ferracane-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reflecting the basic properties of ZnO, fine particles of the oxide have deodorizing and antibacterial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> properties and for that reason are added into materials including cotton fabric, rubber, oral care products,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> and food packaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Enhanced antibacterial action of fine particles compared to bulk material is not exclusive to ZnO and is observed for other materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> This property results from the increased surface area of the fine particles.
</p><p>Zinc oxide is widely used to treat a variety of skin conditions, including dermatitis, itching due to eczema, diaper rash and acne.
</p><p>It is used in products such as <a href="/wiki/Baby_powder" title="Baby powder">baby powder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barrier_cream" title="Barrier cream">barrier creams</a> to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaper_rash" title="Diaper rash">diaper rashes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calamine" title="Calamine">calamine</a> cream, anti-<a href="/wiki/Dandruff" title="Dandruff">dandruff</a> <a href="/wiki/Shampoo" title="Shampoo">shampoos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> ointments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harding_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harding-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> It is also a component in tape (called "zinc oxide tape") used by athletes as a bandage to prevent soft tissue damage during workouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc oxide can be used in ointments, creams, and <a href="/wiki/Lotion" title="Lotion">lotions</a> to protect against <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a> and other damage to the skin caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">sunscreen</a>). It is the broadest spectrum UVA and UVB absorber<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> that is approved for use as a sunscreen by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> and is completely photostable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> When used as an ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">sunscreen</a>, zinc oxide blocks both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV-A" title="UV-A">UVA</a> (320–400 nm) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UVB" title="UVB">UVB</a> (280–320 nm) rays of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>. Zinc oxide and the other most common physical <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">sunscreen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a>, are considered to be nonirritating, nonallergenic, and non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comedogenic" title="Comedogenic">comedogenic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Zinc from zinc oxide is, however, slightly absorbed into the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many sunscreens use nanoparticles of zinc oxide (along with nanoparticles of titanium dioxide) because such small particles do not scatter light and therefore do not appear white. There has been concern that they might be absorbed into the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> A study published in 2010 found a 0.23% to 1.31% (mean 0.42%) of blood zinc levels in venous blood samples could be traced to zinc from ZnO nanoparticles applied to human skin for 5 days, and traces were also found in urine samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In contrast, a comprehensive review of the medical literature from 2011 says that no evidence of systemic absorption can be found in the literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc oxide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoparticles" title="Nanoparticles">nanoparticles</a> can enhance the antibacterial activity of <a href="/wiki/Ciprofloxacin" title="Ciprofloxacin">ciprofloxacin</a>. It has been shown that nano ZnO which has the average size between 20 nm and 45 nm can enhance the antibacterial activity of <a href="/wiki/Ciprofloxacin" title="Ciprofloxacin">ciprofloxacin</a> against <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">Staphylococcus aureus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> <a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a>. The enhancing effect of this nanomaterial is concentration dependent against all test strains. This effect may be due to two reasons. First, zinc oxide nanoparticles can interfere with NorA protein, which is developed for conferring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotic_resistance" title="Antibiotic resistance">resistance</a> in bacteria and has pumping activity that mediate the <a href="/wiki/Efflux_(microbiology)" title="Efflux (microbiology)">effluxing</a> of hydrophilic fluoroquinolones from a cell. Second, zinc oxide nanoparticles can interfere with Omf protein, which is responsible for the permeation of <a href="/wiki/Quinolone_antibiotic" title="Quinolone antibiotic">quinolone antibiotics</a> into the cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cigarette_filters">Cigarette filters</span></h3>
<p>Zinc oxide is a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Cigarette_filter" title="Cigarette filter">cigarette filters</a>. A filter consisting of charcoal impregnated with zinc oxide and iron oxide removes significant amounts of hydrogen cyanide (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">HCN</a>) and hydrogen sulfide (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">H<sub>2</sub>S</a>) from tobacco smoke without affecting its flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap2_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap2-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_additive">Food additive</span></h3>
<p>Zinc oxide is added to many food products, including <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">breakfast cereals</a>, as a source of zinc,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> a necessary <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfate" title="Zinc sulfate">Zinc sulfate</a> is also used for the same purpose.) Some prepackaged foods also include trace amounts of ZnO even if it is not intended as a nutrient.
</p><p>Zinc oxide was linked to dioxin contamination in pork exports in the <a href="/wiki/2008_Chilean_pork_crisis" title="2008 Chilean pork crisis">2008 Chilean pork crisis</a>. The contamination was found to be due to dioxin contaminated zinc oxide used in pig feed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigment">Pigment</span></h3>
<p>Zinc white is used as a pigment in <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a> and is more opaque than <a href="/wiki/Lithopone" title="Lithopone">lithopone</a>, but less opaque than <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">[6]</a></sup> It is also used in coatings for paper. Chinese white is a special grade of zinc white used in artists' <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a>. The use of zinc white (zinc oxide) as a pigment in oil painting started in the middle of 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> It has partly replaced the poisonous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_white" title="Lead white">lead white</a> and was used by painters such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%B6cklin" title="Böcklin">Böcklin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Gogh" title="Van Gogh">Van Gogh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manet" title="Manet">Manet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Munch" title="Edvard Munch">Munch</a> and others. It is also a main ingredient of mineral makeup (CI 77947).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UV_absorber">UV absorber</span></h3>
<p>Micronized and nano-scale zinc oxide and titanium dioxide provide strong protection against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV-A" title="UV-A">UVA</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UVB" title="UVB">UVB</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a>, and are used in <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">suntan lotion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> and also in UV-blocking <a href="/wiki/Sunglasses#Space" title="Sunglasses">sunglasses</a> for use in space and for protection when <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>, following research by scientists at Jet Propulsion Laboratory (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JPL" title="JPL">JPL</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coatings">Coatings</span></h3>
<p>Paints containing zinc oxide powder have long been utilized as anticorrosive coatings for metals. They are especially effective for galvanized iron. Iron is difficult to protect because its reactivity with organic coatings leads to brittleness and lack of adhesion. Zinc oxide paints retain their flexibility and adherence on such surfaces for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap2_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-ap2-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>ZnO highly n-type doped with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a> is transparent and conductive (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparency_(optics)" title="Transparency (optics)">transparency</a> ~90%, lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> ~10<sup>−4</sup> Ω·cm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mende_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-mende-85">[85]</a></sup>). ZnO:Al coatings are used for energy-saving or heat-protecting windows. The coating lets the visible part of the spectrum in but either reflects the infrared (IR) radiation back into the room (energy saving) or does not let the IR radiation into the room (heat protection), depending on which side of the window has the coating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-k1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plastics, such as <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_naphthalate" title="Polyethylene naphthalate">polyethylene naphthalate</a> (PEN), can be protected by applying zinc oxide coating. The coating reduces the diffusion of oxygen with PEN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Zinc oxide layers can also be used on <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> (PC) in outdoor applications. The coating protects PC from solar radiation and decreases the oxidation rate and photo-yellowing of PC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrosion_prevention_in_nuclear_reactors">Corrosion prevention in nuclear reactors</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Depleted_zinc_oxide" title="Depleted zinc oxide">Depleted zinc oxide</a></div>
<p>Zinc oxide depleted in <sup>64</sup>Zn (the <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_zinc" title="Isotopes of zinc">zinc isotope</a> with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> 64) is used in corrosion prevention in nuclear <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactors</a>. The depletion is necessary, because <sup>64</sup>Zn is <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">transformed</a> into radioactive <sup>65</sup>Zn under irradiation by the reactor neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_reforming">Methane reforming</span></h3>
<p>Zinc oxide (ZnO) is used as a pretreatment step to remove <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S) from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> following <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of any <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compounds prior to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_reformer" title="Methane reformer">methane reformer</a>, which can poison the catalyst.  At temperatures between about 230–430 °C (446–806 °F), H<sub>2</sub>S is converted to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> by the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>S + ZnO → H<sub>2</sub>O + ZnS</dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a> (ZnS) is replaced with fresh zinc oxide when the zinc oxide has been consumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_applications">Potential applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZnO_laser_diode.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1214" data-file-width="832" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/ZnO_laser_diode.png/220px-ZnO_laser_diode.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/ZnO_laser_diode.png/330px-ZnO_laser_diode.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/ZnO_laser_diode.png/440px-ZnO_laser_diode.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZnO_laser_diode.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph of an operating ZnO UV <a href="/wiki/Laser_diode" title="Laser diode">laser diode</a> and the corresponding device structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="941" data-file-width="353" height="586" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg/220px-ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg/330px-ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/20/ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZnO_nanorod_gas_sensor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flexible gas sensor based on ZnO nanorods and its internal structure. ITO stands for <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> and PET for <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>ZnO has wide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_band_gap" title="Direct band gap">direct band gap</a> (3.37 eV or 375 nm at room temperature). Therefore, its most common potential applications are in laser diodes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">light emitting diodes</a> (LEDs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Some optoelectronic applications of ZnO overlap with that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GaN" title="GaN">GaN</a>, which has a similar bandgap (~3.4 eV at room temperature). Compared to GaN, ZnO has a larger exciton binding energy (~60 meV, 2.4 times of the room-temperature thermal energy), which results in bright room-temperature emission from ZnO. ZnO can be combined with GaN for LED-applications. For instance as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparent_conducting_oxide" title="Transparent conducting oxide">transparent conducting oxide</a> layer and ZnO nanostructures provide better light outcoupling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Other properties of ZnO favorable for electronic applications include its stability to high-energy radiation and its possibility to be patterned by wet chemical etching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Radiation resistance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> makes ZnO a suitable candidate for space applications. ZnO is the most promising candidate in the field of <a href="/wiki/Random_laser" title="Random laser">random lasers</a> to produce an electronically pumped UV laser source.
</p><p>The pointed tips of ZnO nanorods result in a strong enhancement of an electric field. Therefore, they can be used as <a href="/wiki/Field_electron_emission" title="Field electron emission">field emitters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aluminium-doped ZnO layers are used as transparent <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>. The constituents Zn and Al are much cheaper and less toxic compared to the generally used <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> (ITO). One application which has begun to be commercially available is the use of ZnO as the front contact for solar cells or of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">liquid crystal displays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>Transparent thin-film <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> (TTFT) can be produced with ZnO. As field-effect transistors, they even may not need a p–n junction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> thus avoiding the p-type doping problem of ZnO. Some of the field-effect transistors even use ZnO nanorods as conducting channels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zinc_oxide_nanorod_sensor">Zinc oxide nanorod sensor</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide_nanorod_sensor" title="Zinc oxide nanorod sensor">Zinc oxide nanorod sensors</a> are devices detecting changes in <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> passing through zinc oxide <a href="/wiki/Nanowire" title="Nanowire">nanowires</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> of gas molecules. Selectivity to hydrogen gas was achieved by sputtering Pd clusters on the nanorod surface. The addition of Pd appears to be effective in the catalytic dissociation of hydrogen molecules into atomic hydrogen, increasing the sensitivity of the sensor device. The sensor detects hydrogen concentrations down to 10 parts per million at room temperature, whereas there is no response to oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spintronics">Spintronics</span></h3>
<p>ZnO has also been considered for <a href="/wiki/Spintronics" title="Spintronics">spintronics</a> applications: if doped with 1–10% of magnetic ions (Mn, Fe, Co, V, etc.), ZnO could become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a>, even at room temperature. Such room temperature <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a> in ZnO:Mn has been observed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> but it is not clear yet whether it originates from the matrix itself or from secondary oxide phases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Piezoelectricity">Piezoelectricity</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> fibers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coated" title="Coated">coated</a> in ZnO have been shown capable of fabricating "self-powered nanosystems" with everyday mechanical stress from wind or body movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008 the <i>Center for Nanostructure Characterization</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Institute of Technology</a> reported producing an electricity generating device (called flexible charge pump generator) delivering alternating current by stretching and releasing zinc oxide nanowires. This mini-generator creates an oscillating voltage up to 45 millivolts, converting close to seven percent of the applied mechanical energy into electricity. Researchers used wires with lengths of 0.2–0.3 mm and diameters of three to five micrometers, but the device could be scaled down to smaller size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Li-ion_battery">Li-ion battery</span></h3>
<p>ZnO is a promising anode material for <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion battery</a> because it is cheap, biocompatible, and environmentally friendly. ZnO has a higher theoretical capacity (978 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) than many other transition metal oxides such as CoO (715 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), NiO (718 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and CuO (674 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>As a food additive, zinc oxide is on the U.S. FDA's list of <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>, or GRAS, substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc oxide itself is non-toxic; however it is hazardous to inhale zinc oxide fumes, as generated when zinc or zinc alloys are melted and oxidized at high temperature. This problem occurs while melting <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> because the melting point of brass is close to the boiling point of zinc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> Exposure to zinc oxide in the air, which also occurs while welding galvanized (zinc plated) <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, can result in a nervous malady called <a href="/wiki/Metal_fume_fever" title="Metal fume fever">metal fume fever</a>. For this reason, typically galvanized steel is not welded, or the zinc is removed first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
