<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gmelin database</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Gmelin database</b> is a large <a href="/wiki/Chemical_database" title="Chemical database">database</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> compounds updated quarterly. It is based on the German publication <i><b>Gmelins Handbuch der anorganischen Chemie</b></i> ("Gmelin's handbook of inorganic chemistry") which was originally published by <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Gmelin" title="Leopold Gmelin">Leopold Gmelin</a> in 1817;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_Chemie_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC_Chemie-1">[1]</a></sup> the last print edition, the 8th, appeared in the 1990s.
</p><p>The database currently contains every compound/reaction discovered between 1772 and 1995, amounting to 1.5 million compounds and 1.3 million different reactions, with over 85,000 titles, keywords and abstracts. It has over 800 different data fields on subjects such as the compounds electric, magnetic, thermal, crystal and physiological information.
</p><p>The Gmelin database is maintained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elsevier_MDL" title="Elsevier MDL">Elsevier MDL</a>. It is the sister database to the <a href="/wiki/Beilstein_database" title="Beilstein database">Beilstein database</a>, which deals with organic chemicals and reactions; both are now part of the <a href="/wiki/Reaxys" title="Reaxys">Reaxys</a> system.
</p>
