<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Brewing</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="921" data-file-width="723" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/220px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/330px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/440px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 16th-century brewery</div></div></div>
<p><b>Brewing</b> is the production of <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> by <a href="/wiki/Steeping" title="Steeping">steeping</a> a <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> source (commonly <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> grains, the most popular of which is <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in water and <a href="#Fermenting">fermenting</a> the resulting sweet liquid with <a href="/wiki/Yeast#Beer" title="Yeast">yeast</a>.  It may be done in a <a href="/wiki/Brewery" title="Brewery">brewery</a> by a commercial brewer, at home by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homebrewer" title="Homebrewer">homebrewer</a>, or by a variety of traditional methods such as communally by the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">indigenous peoples in Brazil</a> when making <a href="/wiki/Cauim" title="Cauim">cauim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Brewing has taken place since around the 6th millennium BC, and archaeological evidence suggests that emerging civilizations including <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> brewed beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartman-4">[4]</a></sup> Since the nineteenth century the <a href="#brewing_industry">brewing industry</a> has been part of most western economies.
</p><p>The basic ingredients of beer are water and a <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentable</a> starch source such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malted_barley" title="Malted barley">malted barley</a>. Most beer is fermented with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a> and flavoured with <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alabev.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-alabev.com-5">[5]</a></sup> Less widely used starch sources include <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeerHunter.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeerHunter.com-6">[6]</a></sup> Secondary sources (<a href="/wiki/Adjuncts" title="Adjuncts">adjuncts</a>), such as maize (corn), rice, or sugar, may also be used, sometimes to reduce cost, or to add a feature, such as adding wheat to aid in retaining the foamy head of the beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beer-brewing.com_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-beer-brewing.com-7">[7]</a></sup> The proportion of each starch source in a beer recipe is collectively called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_bill" title="Grain bill">grain bill</a>.
</p><p>Steps in the brewing process include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting">malting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">milling</a>, <a href="#Mashing">mashing</a>, <a href="#Lautering">lautering</a>, <a href="#Boiling">boiling</a>, <a href="#Fermenting">fermenting</a>, <a href="#Conditioning">conditioning</a>, <a href="#Filtering">filtering</a>, and <a href="#Packaging">packaging</a>. There are three main fermentation methods, <a href="#Warm_fermenting">warm</a>, <a href="#Cool_fermenting">cool</a> and <a href="#Spontaneous_fermentation">spontaneous</a>. Fermentation may take place in an open or closed fermenting vessel; a secondary fermentation may also occur in the <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">cask</a> or <a href="#Bottle_conditioning">bottle</a>. There are several additional <a href="/wiki/Brewing_methods" title="Brewing methods">brewing methods</a>, such as barrel aging, double dropping, and Yorkshire Square.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_beer" title="History of beer">History of beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Women_in_brewing" title="Women in brewing">Women in brewing</a></div>
<p>Brewing has taken place since around the 6th millennium BC, and archaeological evidence suggests emerging civilizations including <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> brewed beer. Descriptions of various beer recipes can be found in <a href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">cuneiform</a> (the oldest known writing) from ancient <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arnold_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In Mesopotamia the brewer's craft was the only profession which derived social sanction and divine protection from female deities/goddesses, specifically: <a href="/wiki/Ninkasi" title="Ninkasi">Ninkasi</a>, who covered the production of beer, <a href="/wiki/Siris_(goddess)" title="Siris (goddess)">Siris</a>, who was used in a metonymic way to refer to beer, and <a href="/wiki/Siduri" title="Siduri">Siduri</a>, who covered the enjoyment of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartman_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hartman-4">[4]</a></sup> In pre-industrial times, and in developing countries, women are frequently the main brewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>As almost any cereal containing certain sugars can undergo spontaneous <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Fermenting" title="Brewing">fermentation</a> due to wild yeasts in the air, it is possible that beer-like beverages were independently developed throughout the world soon after a tribe or culture had domesticated cereal. Chemical tests of ancient pottery jars reveal that beer was produced as far back as about 7,000 years ago in what is today Iran. This discovery reveals one of the earliest known uses of fermentation and is the earliest evidence of brewing to date. In Mesopotamia, the oldest evidence of beer is believed to be a 6,000-year-old Sumerian tablet depicting people drinking a beverage through reed straws from a communal bowl. A 3900-year-old Sumerian poem honouring <a href="/wiki/Ninkasi" title="Ninkasi">Ninkasi</a>, the patron goddess of brewing, contains the oldest surviving beer recipe, describing the production of <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> from barley via bread. The invention of bread and beer has been argued to be responsible for humanity's ability to develop technology and build civilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The earliest chemically confirmed barley beer to date was discovered at <a href="/wiki/Godin_Tepe" title="Godin Tepe">Godin Tepe</a> in the central <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a> of Iran, where fragments of a jug, at least 5,000 years old was found to be coated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beerstone" title="Beerstone">beerstone</a>, a by-product of the brewing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Beer may have been known in <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Europe" title="Neolithic Europe">Neolithic Europe</a> as far back as 5,000 years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and was mainly brewed on a domestic scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ale produced before the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> continued to be made and sold on a domestic scale, although by the 7th century AD beer was also being produced and sold by European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monasteries" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a>. During the Industrial Revolution, the production of beer moved from <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisanal</a> manufacture to <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">industrial manufacture</a>, and domestic manufacture ceased to be significant by the end of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sotp_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-sotp-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The development of <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">hydrometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a> changed brewing by allowing the brewer more control of the process, and greater knowledge of the results. Today, the brewing industry is a global business, consisting of several dominant <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational companies</a> and many thousands of smaller producers ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewpub" title="Brewpub">brewpubs</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_brewery" title="Regional brewery">regional breweries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-market_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-market-19">[19]</a></sup> More than 133 billion litres (35 billion gallons) are sold per year—producing total global revenues of $294.5 billion (£147.7 billion) in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients">Ingredients</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/220px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/330px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/440px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malted" title="Malted">Malted</a> barley before kilning or roasting</div></div></div>
<p>The basic ingredients of beer are water; a starch source, such as <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, able to be fermented (converted into alcohol); a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a> to produce the fermentation; and a flavouring, such as <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alabev.com_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-alabev.com-5">[5]</a></sup> to offset the sweetness of the malt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A mixture of starch sources may be used, with a secondary saccharide, such as maize (corn), rice, or sugar, often being termed an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjunct_(beer)" title="Adjunct (beer)">adjunct</a>, especially when used as a lower-cost substitute for malted barley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beer-brewing.com_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-beer-brewing.com-7">[7]</a></sup> Less widely used starch sources include <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> root in Africa, potato in Brazil, and <a href="/wiki/Agave" title="Agave">agave</a> in Mexico, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeerHunter.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BeerHunter.com-6">[6]</a></sup>  The amount of each starch source in a beer recipe is collectively called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_bill" title="Grain bill">grain bill</a>.
</p>
<dl><dt>Water</dt></dl>
<p>Beer is composed mostly of water. Regions have water with different mineral components; as a result, different regions were originally better suited to making certain types of beer, thus giving them a regional character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geot_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-geot-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> has <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a> well suited to making <a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">stout</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a>; while <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Pilsen</a> has soft water well suited to making <a href="/wiki/Pale_lager" title="Pale lager">pale lager</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pilsner_Urquell" title="Pilsner Urquell">Pilsner Urquell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geot_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-geot-22">[22]</a></sup> The waters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burton_upon_Trent_brewing" title="Burton upon Trent brewing">Burton</a> in England contain <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, which benefits making <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a> to such a degree that brewers of pale ales will add gypsum to the local water in a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burtonisation" title="Burtonisation">Burtonisation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Starch source</dt></dl>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">Malt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mash_ingredients" title="Mash ingredients">Mash ingredients</a></div>
<p>The starch source in a beer provides the fermentable material and is a key determinant of the strength and flavour of the beer. The most common starch source used in beer is malted grain. Grain is malted by soaking it in water, allowing it to begin <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>, and then drying the partially germinated grain in a kiln. Malting grain produces enzymes that will allow conversion from starches in the grain into fermentable sugars during the mash process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Different roasting times and temperatures are used to produce different colours of malt from the same grain. Darker malts will produce darker beers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nearly all beer includes barley malt as the majority of the starch. This is because of its fibrous husk, which is important not only in the sparging stage of brewing (in which water is washed over the <a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">mashed</a> barley grains to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">wort</a>) but also as a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestive</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> that facilitates conversion of starch into sugars. Other malted and unmalted grains (including wheat, rice, oats, and <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, and, less frequently, maize (corn) and sorghum) may be used. In recent years, a few brewers have produced <a href="/wiki/Gluten-free_beer" title="Gluten-free beer">gluten-free beer</a> made with sorghum with no barley malt for people who cannot digest <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a>-containing grains like wheat, barley, and rye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Hops</dt></dl>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">Hops</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/220px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/330px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/440px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">Hop cone</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Hallertau" title="Hallertau">Hallertau</a>, Germany, hop yard</div></div></div>
<p>Hops are the female flower clusters or seed cones of the hop vine <i><a href="/wiki/Humulus_lupulus" title="Humulus lupulus">Humulus lupulus</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which are used as a flavouring and preservative agent in nearly all beer made today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  Hops had been used for medicinal and food flavouring purposes since Roman times; by the 7th century in <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty" title="Carolingian dynasty">Carolingian</a> monasteries in what is now Germany, beer was being made with hops,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> though it isn't until the thirteenth century that widespread cultivation of hops for use in beer is recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sotp62_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-sotp62-31">[31]</a></sup> Before the thirteenth century, beer was flavoured with plants such as <a href="/wiki/Achillea_millefolium" title="Achillea millefolium">yarrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhododendron_tomentosum" title="Rhododendron tomentosum">wild rosemary</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Myrica_gale" title="Myrica gale">bog myrtle</a>, and other ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Juniper_berry" title="Juniper berry">juniper berries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aniseed" title="Aniseed">aniseed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>, which would be combined into a mixture known as <a href="/wiki/Gruit" title="Gruit">gruit</a> and used as hops are now used; between the thirteenth and the sixteenth century, during which hops took over as the dominant flavouring, beer flavoured with gruit was known as ale, while beer flavoured with hops was known as beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellix_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellix-33">[33]</a></sup> Some beers today, such as <i>Fraoch</i> by the Scottish <a href="/wiki/Williams_Bros_Brewing_Co" title="Williams Bros Brewing Co">Heather Ales company</a> and <i>Cervoise Lancelot</i> by the French Brasserie-Lancelot company, use plants other than hops for flavouring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hops contain several characteristics that brewers desire in beer: they contribute a bitterness that balances the sweetness of the malt; they provide floral, citrus, and herbal aromas and flavours; they have an <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> effect that favours the activity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a> over less desirable microorganisms; and they aid in "head retention", the length of time that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_(beer)" title="Head (beer)">foamy head</a> will last.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The preservative in hops comes from the lupulin glands which contain soft resins with alpha and beta acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Though much studied, the preservative nature of the soft resins is not yet fully understood, though it has been observed that unless stored at a cool temperature, the preservative nature will decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Brewing is the sole major commercial use of hops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Yeast</dt></dl>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">Brewer's yeast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></div>
<p>Yeast is the <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> that is responsible for fermentation in beer. Yeast <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolises</a> the sugars extracted from grains, which produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and thereby turns <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> into beer. In addition to fermenting the beer, yeast influences the character and flavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
The dominant types of yeast used to make beer are <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, known as ale yeast, and <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></i>, known as lager yeast;  <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces" title="Brettanomyces">Brettanomyces</a></i> ferments <a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">lambics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Torulaspora_delbrueckii" title="Torulaspora delbrueckii">Torulaspora delbrueckii</a></i> ferments Bavarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weissbier" title="Weissbier">weissbier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Before the role of yeast in fermentation was understood, fermentation involved wild or airborne yeasts, and a few styles such as <a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">lambics</a> still use this method today. <a href="/wiki/Emil_Christian_Hansen" title="Emil Christian Hansen">Emil Christian Hansen</a>, a Danish biochemist employed by the <a href="/wiki/Carlsberg_Laboratory" title="Carlsberg Laboratory">Carlsberg Laboratory</a>, developed pure yeast <a href="/wiki/Microbiological_culture" title="Microbiological culture">cultures</a> which were introduced into the Carlsberg brewery in 1883,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and pure yeast strains are now the main fermenting source used worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Clarifying agent</dt></dl>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Finings" title="Finings">Finings</a></div>
<p>Some brewers add one or more <a href="/wiki/Clarifying_agent" title="Clarifying agent">clarifying agents</a> to beer, which typically <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a> (collect as a solid) out of the beer along with protein solids and are found only in trace amounts in the finished product. This process makes the beer appear <a href="/wiki/Bright_beer" title="Bright beer">bright</a> and clean, rather than the cloudy appearance of ethnic and older styles of beer such as <a href="/wiki/Wheat_beer" title="Wheat beer">wheat beers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of clarifying agents include <a href="/wiki/Isinglass" title="Isinglass">isinglass</a>, obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimbladder" title="Swimbladder">swimbladders</a> of fish; <a href="/wiki/Chondrus_crispus" title="Chondrus crispus">Irish moss</a>, a seaweed; kappa <a href="/wiki/Carrageenan" title="Carrageenan">carrageenan</a>, from the seaweed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kappaphycus_cottonii" title="Kappaphycus cottonii">Kappaphycus cottonii</a></i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyclar" title="Polyclar">Polyclar</a> (artificial); and <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> If a beer is marked "suitable for Vegans", it was generally clarified either with seaweed or with artificial agents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> although the "Fast Cask" method invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marston%27s_plc" title="Marston's plc">Marston's</a> in 2009 may provide another method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing_process">Brewing process</span></h2>

<p>There are several steps in the brewing process, which may include malting, mashing, lautering, <a href="#Boiling">boiling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermenting</a>, <a href="#Conditioning">conditioning</a>, <a href="#Filtering">filtering</a>, and <a href="#Packaging">packaging</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting">Malting</a> is the process where barley grain is made ready for brewing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Malting is broken down into three steps in order to help to release the starches in the barley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> First, during steeping, the grain is added to a vat with water and allowed to soak for approximately 40 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malting_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-malting-54">[54]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>, the grain is spread out on the floor of the germination room for around 5 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malting_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-malting-54">[54]</a></sup> The final part of malting is kilning when the malt goes through a very high temperature drying in a kiln; with gradual temperature increase over several hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>When kilning is complete, the grains are now termed <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>, and they will be milled or crushed to break apart the kernels and expose the <a href="/wiki/Cotyledon" title="Cotyledon">cotyledon</a>, which contains the majority of the carbohydrates and sugars; this makes it easier to extract the sugars during mashing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-4_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref-4-56">[56]</a></sup> Milling also separates the seed from the <a href="/wiki/Husk" title="Husk">husk</a>. Care must be taken when milling to ensure that the starch reserves are sufficiently milled without damaging the husk and providing coarse enough grits that a good filter bed can be formed during lautering.   Grains are typically dry-milled with <a href="/wiki/Roller_mill" title="Roller mill">roller mills</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hammer_mill" title="Hammer mill">hammer mills</a>.   Hammer mills, which produce a very fine mash, are often used when mash filters are going to be employed in the lautering process because the grain does not have to form its own filter bed. In modern plants, the grain is often conditioned with water before it is milled to make the husk more pliable, thus reducing breakage and improving lauter speed.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">Mashing</a> converts the starches released during the malting stage into sugars that can be fermented. The milled grain is mixed with hot water in a large vessel known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mash_tun" title="Mash tun">mash tun</a>. In this vessel, the grain and water are mixed together to create a cereal mash. During the mash, naturally occurring enzymes present in the malt convert the starches (long chain carbohydrates) in the grain into smaller molecules or simple sugars (mono-, di-, and tri-saccharides). This "conversion" is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saccharification" title="Saccharification">saccharification</a> which occurs between the temperatures 140 - 158 degrees F.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The result of the mashing process is a sugar-rich liquid or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">"wort"</a>, which is then strained through the bottom of the mash tun in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Lautering" title="Lautering">lautering</a>. Prior to lautering, the mash temperature may be raised to about 75–78 °C (167–172 °F) (known as a mashout) to free up more starch and reduce mash viscosity. Additional water may be sprinkled on the grains to extract additional sugars (a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sparging_(beer)" title="Sparging (beer)">sparging</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-21_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref-21-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>The wort is moved into a large tank known as a "copper" or <a href="/wiki/Kettle" title="Kettle">kettle</a> where it is boiled with <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> and sometimes other ingredients such as herbs or sugars. This stage is where many chemical reactions take place, and where important decisions about the flavour, colour, and aroma of the beer are made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fosters_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fosters-59">[59]</a></sup> The boiling process serves to terminate enzymatic processes, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a> proteins, <a href="/wiki/Isomerization" title="Isomerization">isomerize</a> hop <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a>, and concentrate and <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilize</a> the wort. Hops add flavour, <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">aroma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_(taste)" title="Bitter (taste)">bitterness</a> to the beer. At the end of the boil, the hopped wort settles to clarify in a vessel called a "whirlpool", where the more solid particles in the wort are separated out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-2_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref-2-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the whirlpool, the wort is drawn away from the compacted hop trub, and rapidly cooled via a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> to a temperature where yeast can be added. A variety of heat exchanger designs are used in breweries, with the most common a plate-style. Water or glycol run in channels in the opposite direction of the wort, causing a rapid drop in temperature. It is very important to quickly cool the wort to a level where yeast can be added safely as yeast is unable to grow in very high temperatures, and will start to die in temperatures above 60 °C (140 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-4_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref-4-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> After the wort goes through the heat exchanger, the cooled wort goes into a fermentation tank. A type of yeast is selected and added, or "pitched", to the fermentation tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fosters_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fosters-59">[59]</a></sup> When the yeast is added to the wort, the fermenting process begins, where the sugars turn into alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and other components. When the fermentation is complete the brewer may rack the beer into a new tank, called a conditioning tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-21_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref-21-58">[58]</a></sup>  Conditioning of the beer is the process in which the beer ages, the flavour becomes smoother, and flavours that are unwanted dissipate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-2_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref-2-60">[60]</a></sup> After conditioning for a week to several months, the beer may be <a href="/wiki/Filtered_beer" title="Filtered beer">filtered</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">force carbonated</a> for bottling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Finings" title="Finings">fined</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">cask</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mashing">Mashing</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">Mashing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="657" data-file-width="800" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg/220px-Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg/330px-Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg/440px-Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mash_Tun_exhibit_in_the_Brewery_Museum_at_Burton-upon-Trent_-_geograph.org.uk_-_2664334.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A mash tun at the <a href="/wiki/National_Brewery_Centre" title="National Brewery Centre">Bass Museum</a> in Burton-upon-Trent</div></div></div>
<p>Mashing is the process of combining a mix of milled grain (typically <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> with <a href="/wiki/Adjuncts" title="Adjuncts">supplementary grains</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> or wheat), known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_bill" title="Grain bill">grain bill</a>", and water, known as "liquor", and heating this mixture in a vessel called a "mash tun". Mashing is a form of steeping,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and defines the act of brewing, such as with making tea, <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">soy sauce</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Technically, wine, <a href="/wiki/Cider" title="Cider">cider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a> are not brewed but rather <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vinified" title="Vinified">vinified</a>, as there is no steeping process involving solids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Mashing allows the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> in the malt to break down the <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> in the grain into sugars, typically <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a> to create a malty liquid called <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> There are two main methods – <a href="/wiki/Infusion" title="Infusion">infusion</a> mashing, in which the grains are heated in one vessel; and <a href="/wiki/Decoction" title="Decoction">decoction</a> mashing, in which a proportion of the grains are boiled and then returned to the mash, raising the temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Mashing involves pauses at certain temperatures (notably 45–62–73 °C or 113–144–163 °F), and takes place in a "mash tun" – an insulated brewing vessel with a <a href="/wiki/False_bottom" title="False bottom">false bottom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The end product of mashing is called a "mash".
</p><p>Mashing usually takes 1 to 2 hours, and during this time the various temperature rests activate different enzymes depending upon the type of malt being used, its modification level, and the intention of the brewer. The activity of these enzymes convert the starches of the grains to <a href="/wiki/Dextrin" title="Dextrin">dextrins</a> and then to fermentable sugars such as <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a>. A mash rest from 49–55 °C (120–131 °F) activates various <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">proteases</a>, which break down proteins that might otherwise cause the beer to be hazy. This rest is generally used only with undermodified (i.e. undermalted) malts which are decreasingly popular in Germany and the Czech Republic, or non-malted grains such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> and rice, which are widely used in North American beers. A mash rest at 60 °C (140 °F) activates β-<a href="/wiki/Glucanase" title="Glucanase">glucanase</a>, which breaks down gummy β-glucans in the mash, making the sugars flow out more freely later in the process. In the modern mashing process, commercial fungal based β-glucanase may be added as a supplement. Finally, a mash rest temperature of 65–71 °C (149–160 °F) is used to convert the starches in the malt to sugar, which is then usable by the yeast later in the brewing process. Doing the latter rest at the lower end of the range favours <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-amylase" title="Β-amylase">β-amylase</a> enzymes, producing more low-order sugars like <a href="/wiki/Maltotriose" title="Maltotriose">maltotriose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> which are more fermentable by the <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>. This in turn creates a beer lower in body and higher in alcohol. A rest closer to the higher end of the range favours <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-amylase" title="Α-amylase">α-amylase</a> enzymes, creating more higher-order sugars and <a href="/wiki/Dextrin" title="Dextrin">dextrins</a> which are less fermentable by the yeast, so a fuller-bodied beer with less alcohol is the result. Duration and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> variances also affect the sugar composition of the resulting wort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lautering">Lautering</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lautering" title="Lautering">Lautering</a></div>
<p>Lautering is the separation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">wort</a> (the liquid containing the sugar extracted during mashing) from the grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> This is done either in a mash tun outfitted with a false bottom, in a lauter tun, or in a mash filter. Most separation processes have two stages: first wort run-off, during which the extract is separated in an undiluted state from the spent grains, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sparging_(beer)" title="Sparging (beer)">sparging</a>, in which extract which remains with the grains is rinsed off with hot water. The lauter tun is a tank with holes in the bottom small enough to hold back the large bits of grist and hulls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The bed of grist that settles on it is the actual filter. Some lauter tuns have provision for rotating <a href="/wiki/Mash_rake" title="Mash rake">rakes</a> or knives to cut into the bed of grist to maintain good flow. The knives can be turned so they push the grain, a feature used to drive the spent grain out of the vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The mash filter is a plate-and-frame filter. The empty frames contain the mash, including the spent grains, and have a capacity of around one hectoliter. The plates contain a support structure for the filter cloth. The plates, frames, and filter cloths are arranged in a carrier frame like so: frame, cloth, plate, cloth, with plates at each end of the structure. Newer mash filters have bladders that can press the liquid out of the grains between spargings. The grain does not act like a filtration medium in a mash filter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiling">Boiling</span></h2>
<p>After mashing, the beer <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> is boiled with <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> (and other flavourings if used) in a large tank known as a "copper" or brew kettle – though historically the mash vessel was used and is still in some small breweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>  The boiling process is where chemical reactions take place,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fosters_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fosters-59">[59]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilization</a> of the wort to remove unwanted bacteria, releasing of hop flavours, bitterness and aroma compounds through <a href="/wiki/Isomerization" title="Isomerization">isomerization</a>, stopping of enzymatic processes, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of proteins, and concentration of the wort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamforth_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bamforth-79">[79]</a></sup> Finally, the vapours produced during the boil volatilise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off-flavours" title="Off-flavours">off-flavours</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> precursors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamforth_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bamforth-79">[79]</a></sup> The boil is conducted so that it is even and intense – a continuous "rolling boil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamforth_79-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bamforth-79">[79]</a></sup> The boil on average lasts between 45 and 90 minutes, depending on its intensity, the hop addition schedule, and volume of water the brewer expects to evaporate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> At the end of the boil, solid particles in the hopped wort are separated out, usually in a vessel called a "whirlpool".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-2_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-ref-2-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brew_kettle_or_copper">Brew kettle or copper</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="383" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg/220px-Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg/330px-Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a4/Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brasserie_La_Choulette_12-11-2006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brew kettles at Brasserie La Choulette in France</div></div></div>
<p>Copper is the traditional material for the boiling vessel, because copper transfers heat quickly and evenly, and because the bubbles produced during boiling, and which would act as an insulator against the heat, do not cling to the surface of copper, so the wort is heated in a consistent manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brew272_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brew272-81">[81]</a></sup> The simplest boil kettles are direct-fired, with a burner underneath. These can produce a vigorous and favourable boil, but are also apt to scorch the wort where the flame touches the kettle, causing caramelisation and making cleanup difficult. Most breweries use a steam-fired kettle, which uses steam jackets in the kettle to boil the wort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamforth_79-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bamforth-79">[79]</a></sup> Breweries usually have a boiling unit either inside or outside of the kettle, usually a tall, thin cylinder with vertical tubes, called a calandria, through which wort is pumped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cal_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cal-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Whirlpool">Whirlpool</span></h3>
<p>At the end of the boil, solid particles in the hopped wort are separated out, usually in a vessel called a "whirlpool" or "settling tank".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref-2_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-ref-2-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-whirl-83">[83]</a></sup>  The whirlpool was devised by Henry Ranulph Hudston while working for the <a href="/wiki/Molson_Brewery" title="Molson Brewery">Molson Brewery</a> in 1960 to utilise the so-called <a href="/wiki/Tea_leaf_paradox" title="Tea leaf paradox">tea leaf paradox</a> to force the denser solids known as "trub" (coagulated proteins, vegetable matter from hops) into a cone in the centre of the whirlpool tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tap_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-tap-86">[86]</a></sup>  Whirlpool systems vary: smaller breweries tend to use the brew kettle, larger breweries use a separate tank,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-whirl-83">[83]</a></sup> and design will differ, with tank floors either flat, sloped, conical or with a cup in the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The principle in all is that by swirling the wort the <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">centripetal force</a> will push the trub into a cone at the centre of the bottom of the tank, where it can be easily removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whirl_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-whirl-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hopback">Hopback</span></h3>
<p>A hopback is a traditional additional chamber that acts as a sieve or filter by using whole <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> to clear debris (or "<a href="/wiki/Trub_(brewing)" title="Trub (brewing)">trub</a>") from the unfermented (or "green") <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> as the whirlpool does, and also to increase hop aroma in the finished beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> It is a chamber between the brewing kettle and wort chiller. Hops are added to the chamber, the hot wort from the kettle is run through it, and then immediately cooled in the wort chiller before entering the fermentation chamber. Hopbacks utilizing a sealed chamber facilitate maximum retention of volatile hop aroma compounds that would normally be driven off when the hops contact the hot wort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> While a hopback has a similar filtering effect as a whirlpool, it operates differently: a whirlpool uses centrifugal forces, a hopback uses a layer of whole hops to act as a filter bed. Furthermore, while a whirlpool is useful only for the removal of pelleted hops (as flowers do not tend to separate as easily), in general hopbacks are used only for the removal of whole flower hops (as the particles left by pellets tend to make it through the hopback).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The hopback has mainly been substituted in modern breweries by the whirlpool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wort_cooling">Wort cooling</span></h3>
<p>After the whirlpool, the wort must be brought down to fermentation temperatures 20–26 °C (68–79 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-69">[69]</a></sup> before yeast is added. In modern breweries this is achieved through a plate <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a>. A plate heat exchanger has many ridged plates, which form two separate paths. The wort is pumped into the heat exchanger, and goes through every other gap between the plates. The cooling medium, usually water, goes through the other gaps. The ridges in the plates ensure turbulent flow. A good heat exchanger can drop 95 °C (203 °F) wort to 20 °C (68 °F) while warming the cooling medium from about 10 °C (50 °F) to 80 °C (176 °F). The last few plates often use a cooling medium which can be cooled to below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a>, which allows a finer control over the wort-out temperature, and also enables cooling to around 10 °C (50 °F). After cooling, oxygen is often dissolved into the wort to revitalize the yeast and aid its reproduction.
</p><p>While boiling, it is useful to recover some of the energy used to boil the wort. On its way out of the brewery, the steam created during the boil is passed over a coil through which unheated water flows. By adjusting the rate of flow, the output temperature of the water can be controlled. This is also often done using a plate heat exchanger. The water is then stored for later use in the next mash, in equipment cleaning, or wherever necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kunze_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kunze-94">[94]</a></sup> Another common method of energy recovery takes place during the wort cooling. When cold water is used to cool the wort in a heat exchanger, the water is significantly warmed. In an efficient brewery, cold water is passed through the heat exchanger at a rate set to maximize the water's temperature upon exiting. This now-hot water is then stored in a hot water tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kunze_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kunze-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermenting">Fermenting <span id="Brewing#Fermenting"></span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1290" data-file-width="1183" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg/220px-Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg/330px-Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg/440px-Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cuves_de_fermentations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern closed fermentation vessels</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">Fermentation</a> takes place in fermentation vessels which come in various forms, from enormous cylindroconical vessels, through open stone vessels, to wooden vats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm1_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferm1-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm2_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferm2-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm3_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferm3-97">[97]</a></sup> After the wort is cooled and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerated" title="Aerated">aerated</a> – usually with <a href="/wiki/Asepsis" title="Asepsis">sterile</a> air – yeast is added to it, and it begins to ferment. It is during this stage that sugars won from the <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a> are converted into alcohol and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and the product can be called beer for the first time.
</p><p>Most breweries today use cylindroconical vessels, or CCVs, which have a conical bottom and a cylindrical top. The cone's <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> is typically around 60°, an angle that will allow the yeast to flow towards the cone's apex, but is not so steep as to take up too much vertical space. CCVs can handle both fermenting and conditioning in the same tank. At the end of fermentation, the yeast and other solids which have fallen to the cone's apex can be simply flushed out of a port at the apex. Open fermentation vessels are also used, often for show in brewpubs, and in Europe in wheat beer fermentation. These vessels have no tops, which makes harvesting top-fermenting yeasts very easy. The open tops of the vessels make the risk of infection greater, but with proper cleaning procedures and careful protocol about who enters fermentation chambers, the risk can be well controlled. Fermentation tanks are typically made of stainless steel. If they are simple cylindrical tanks with beveled ends, they are arranged vertically, as opposed to conditioning tanks which are usually laid out horizontally. Only a very few breweries still use wooden vats for fermentation as wood is difficult to keep clean and infection-free and must be repitched more or less yearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm1_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferm1-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm2_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferm2-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferm3_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferm3-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation_methods">Fermentation methods</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Beer_style" title="Beer style">Beer style</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG/220px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG/330px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG/440px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anchor_Brewing_Company_fermentation_tank.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Open vessel showing fermentation taking place</div></div></div>
<p>There are three main fermentation methods, <a href="#Warm_fermentation">warm</a>, <a href="#Cool_fermentation">cool</a> and <a href="#Spontaneous_fermentation">wild or spontaneous</a>. Fermentation may take place in open or closed vessels. There may be a secondary fermentation which can take place in the brewery, in the <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">cask</a> or in the <a href="#Bottle_conditioning">bottle</a>.
</p><p>Brewing yeasts are traditionally classed as "top-cropping" (or "top-fermenting") and "bottom-cropping" (or "bottom-fermenting").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Yeast were termed top or bottom cropping, because in traditional brewing yeast was collected from the top or bottom of the fermenting wort to be reused for the next brew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tom_Colicchio_2011_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tom_Colicchio_2011-99">[99]</a></sup> This terminology is somewhat inappropriate in the modern era; after the widespread application of brewing mycology it was discovered that the two separate collecting methods involved two different yeast species that favoured different temperature regimes, namely <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> in top-cropping at warmer temperatures and <i>Saccharomyces pastorianus</i> in bottom-cropping at cooler temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> As brewing methods changed in the 20th century, cylindro-conical fermenting vessels became the norm and the collection of yeast for both <i>Saccharomyces</i> species is done from the bottom of the fermenter. Thus the method of collection no longer implies a species association. There are a few remaining breweries who collect yeast in the top-cropping method, such as Samuel Smiths brewery in Yorkshire, Marstons in Staffordshire and several German hefeweizen producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tom_Colicchio_2011_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tom_Colicchio_2011-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>For both types, yeast is fully distributed through the beer while it is fermenting, and both equally <a href="/wiki/Yeast_flocculation" title="Yeast flocculation">flocculate</a> (clump together and precipitate to the bottom of the vessel) when fermentation is finished. By no means do all top-cropping yeasts demonstrate this behaviour, but it features strongly in many English yeasts that may also exhibit chain forming (the failure of budded cells to break from the mother cell), which is in the technical sense different from true flocculation. The most common top-cropping brewer's yeast, <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i>, is the same species as the common baking yeast. However, baking and brewing yeasts typically belong to different strains, cultivated to favour different characteristics: baking yeast strains are more aggressive, in order to carbonate <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">dough</a> in the shortest amount of time; brewing yeast strains act slower, but tend to tolerate higher alcohol concentrations (normally 12–15% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">abv</a> is the maximum, though under special treatment some ethanol-tolerant strains can be coaxed up to around 20%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Modern quantitative genomics has revealed the complexity of <i>Saccharomyces</i> species to the extent that yeasts involved in beer and wine production commonly involve hybrids of so-called pure species. As such, the yeasts involved in what has been typically called top-cropping or top-fermenting ale may be both <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> and complex hybrids of <i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i> and <i>Saccharomyces kudriavzevii</i>. Three notable ales, <a href="/wiki/Chimay_Brewery" title="Chimay Brewery">Chimay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orval_Brewery#Beers" title="Orval Brewery">Orval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westmalle_Brewery" title="Westmalle Brewery">Westmalle</a>, are fermented with these hybrid strains, which are identical to wine yeasts from Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Warm_fermentation">Warm fermentation</span></h4>
<p><span id="Warm_fermentation"></span><span id="Warm_fermenting"></span>
</p><p>In general, yeasts such as <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> are fermented at warm temperatures between 15 and 20 °C (59 and 68 °F), occasionally as high as 24 °C (75 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBP_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FBP-103">[103]</a></sup> while the yeast used by <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Brewery" title="Dupont Brewery">Brasserie Dupont</a> for <a href="/wiki/Saison" title="Saison">saison</a> ferments even higher at 29 to 35 °C (84 to 95 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> They generally form a foam on the surface of the fermenting beer, which is called <a href="/wiki/Barm" title="Barm">barm</a>, as during the fermentation process its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> surface causes the flocs to adhere to CO<sub>2</sub> and rise; because of this, they are often referred to as "top-cropping" or "top-fermenting"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> – though this distinction is less clear in modern brewing with the use of cylindro-conical tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamforth_2005_66_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bamforth_2005_66-106">[106]</a></sup> Generally, warm-fermented beers, which are usually termed <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a>, are ready to drink within three weeks after the beginning of fermentation, although some brewers will condition them for several months.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cool_fermentation">Cool fermentation</span></h4>
<p><span id="Cool_fermentation"></span><span id="Cool_fermenting"></span>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">Lager</a></div>
<p>When a beer has been brewed using a cool fermentation of around 10 °C (50 °F), compared to typical warm fermentation temperatures of 18 °C (64 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> then stored (or lagered) for typically several weeks (or months) at temperatures close to <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> point, it is termed a "<a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">lager</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> During the lagering or storage phase several flavour components developed during fermentation dissipate, resulting in a "cleaner" flavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Though it is the slow, cool fermentation and cold conditioning (or lagering) that defines the character of lager,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> the main technical difference is with the yeast generally used, which is <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Technical differences include the ability of lager yeast to metabolize <a href="/wiki/Melibiose" title="Melibiose">melibiose</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-difference_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-difference-114">[114]</a></sup> and the tendency to settle at the bottom of the fermenter (though ales yeasts can also become bottom settling by selection);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-difference_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-difference-114">[114]</a></sup> though these technical differences are not considered by scientists to be influential in the character or flavour of the finished beer, brewers feel otherwise - sometimes cultivating their own yeast strains which may suit their brewing equipment or for a particular purpose, such as brewing beers with a high abv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brewers in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> had for centuries been selecting cold-fermenting yeasts by storing ("lagern") their beers in cold alpine caves. The process of natural selection meant that the wild yeasts that were most cold tolerant would be the ones that would remain actively fermenting in the beer that was stored in the caves. A sample of these Bavarian yeasts was sent from the Spaten brewery in Munich to the Carlsberg brewery in Copenhagen in 1845 who began brewing with it. In 1883 Emile Hansen completed a study on pure yeast culture isolation and the pure strain obtained from Spaten went into industrial production in 1884 as Carlsberg yeast No 1. Another specialized pure yeast production plant was installed at the Heineken Brewery in Rotterdam the following year and together they began the supply of pure cultured yeast to brewers across Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> This yeast strain was originally classified as <i>Saccharomyces carlsbergensis</i>, a now defunct species name which has been superseded by the currently accepted taxonomic classification <i>Saccharomyces pastorianus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spontaneous_fermentation">Spontaneous fermentation</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Wild yeast" redirects here. For the role of wild yeast in winemaking, see <a href="/wiki/Yeast_in_winemaking" title="Yeast in winemaking">Yeast in winemaking</a>.</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">Lambic</a> beers are historically brewed in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Pajottenland" title="Pajottenland">Pajottenland</a> region of Belgium without any yeast inoculation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> They are fermented in oak barrels with the resident microbiota present in the wood and can take up to 2 years to come into condition for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> However, with the advent of yeast banks and England's <a href="/wiki/National_Collection_of_Yeast_Cultures" title="National Collection of Yeast Cultures">National Collection of Yeast Cultures</a>, brewing these beers – albeit not through spontaneous fermentation – is possible anywhere. Specific bacteria cultures are also available to reproduce certain styles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces" title="Brettanomyces">Brettanomyces</a></i> is a genus of yeast important in brewing <a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">lambic</a>, a beer produced not by the deliberate addition of brewer's yeasts, but by spontaneous fermentation with wild yeasts and bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verachtert1995_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verachtert1995-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditioning">Conditioning</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG/220px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG/330px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG/440px-Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anchor_Brewing_Company_conditioning_tanks.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Conditioning tanks at <a href="/wiki/Anchor_Brewing_Company" title="Anchor Brewing Company">Anchor Brewing Company</a></div></div></div>
<p>After an initial or primary fermentation, beer is <i>conditioned</i>, matured or aged,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> in one of several ways,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> which can take from 2 to 4 weeks, several months, or several years, depending on the brewer's intention for the beer. The beer is usually transferred into a second container, so that it is no longer exposed to the dead yeast and other debris (also known as "trub") that have settled to the bottom of the primary fermenter. This prevents the formation of unwanted flavours and harmful compounds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetylaldehyde" title="Acetylaldehyde">acetylaldehyde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Kräusening</dt></dl>
<p>Kräusening is a conditioning method in which fermenting <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> is added to the finished beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krause_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-krause-129">[129]</a></sup> The active yeast will restart fermentation in the finished beer, and so introduce fresh carbon dioxide; the conditioning tank will be then sealed so that the carbon dioxide is dissolved into the beer producing a lively "condition" or level of carbonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krause_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-krause-129">[129]</a></sup> The kräusening method may also be used to condition bottled beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krause_129-2"><a href="#cite_note-krause-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Lagering</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">Lagers</a> are stored at cellar temperature or below for 1–6 months while still on the yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> The process of storing, or conditioning, or maturing, or aging a beer at a low temperature for a long period is called "lagering", and while it is associated with lagers, the process may also be done with ales, with the same result – that of cleaning up various chemicals, acids and compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Secondary fermentation<span id="Brewing#Secondary_fermentation"></span></dt></dl>
<p>During secondary fermentation, most of the remaining yeast will settle to the bottom of the second fermenter, yielding a less hazy product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt><span id="Bottle_conditioning"></span>Bottle fermentation</dt></dl>
<p>Some beers undergo an additional fermentation in the bottle giving natural carbonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> This may be a second or third fermentation. They are bottled with a viable yeast population in suspension. If there is no residual fermentable sugar left, sugar or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">wort</a> or both may be added in a process known as priming. The resulting fermentation generates <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> that is trapped in the bottle, remaining in solution and providing natural carbonation. Bottle-conditioned beers may be either filled unfiltered direct from the fermentation or conditioning tank, or filtered and then reseeded with yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Cask conditioning</dt></dl>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">Cask ale</a></div>
<p>Cask ale or cask-conditioned beer is <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">unfiltered</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">unpasteurised</a> beer that is conditioned (including secondary fermentation) and served from a cask, either pumped up from a cellar via a <a href="/wiki/Beer_engine" title="Beer engine">beer engine</a> (hand pump), or from a tap by gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> Sometimes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cask_breather" title="Cask breather">cask breather</a> is used to keep the beer fresh by allowing carbon dioxide to replace oxygen as the beer is drawn off the cask.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup> The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_ale" title="Real ale">real ale</a>" as used by the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Real_Ale" title="Campaign for Real Ale">Campaign for Real Ale</a> (CAMRA) refers to beer "served without the use of <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">extraneous carbon dioxide</a>", which would disallow the use of a cask breather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAMRA_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAMRA-137">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filtering">Filtering</span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Brewing&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(April 2012)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filtered_beer" title="Filtered beer">Filtered beer</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="658" data-file-width="907" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg/220px-DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg/330px-DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg/440px-DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DE_and_yeast_crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A mixture of <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> after filtering</div></div></div>
<p>Filtering the beer stabilizes the flavour, and gives beer its polished shine and brilliance. Not all beer is filtered. When tax determination is required by local laws, it is typically done at this stage in a calibrated tank. There are several forms of filters, they may be in the form of sheets or "candles", or they may be a fine powder such as <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a>, also called kieselguhr. The powder is added to the beer and recirculated past screens to form a filtration bed.
</p><p>Filters range from rough filters that remove much of the yeast and any solids (e.g., hops, grain particles) left in the beer, to filters tight enough to strain colour and body from the beer. Filtration ratings are divided into rough, fine, and sterile. Rough filtration leaves some cloudiness in the beer, but it is noticeably clearer than unfiltered beer. Fine filtration removes almost all cloudiness. Sterile filtration removes almost all microorganisms.
</p>
<dl><dt>Sheet (pad) filters</dt></dl>
<p>These filters use sheets that allow only particles smaller than a given size to pass through. The sheets are placed into a filtering frame, sanitized (with boiling water, for example) and then used to filter the beer. The sheets can be flushed if the filter becomes blocked. The sheets are usually disposable and are replaced between filtration sessions. Often the sheets contain powdered filtration media to aid in filtration.
</p><p>Pre-made filters have two sides. One with loose holes, and the other with tight holes. Flow goes from the side with loose holes to the side with the tight holes, with the intent that large particles get stuck in the large holes while leaving enough room around the particles and filter medium for smaller particles to go through and get stuck in tighter holes.
</p><p>Sheets are sold in nominal ratings, and typically 90% of particles larger than the nominal rating are caught by the sheet.
</p>
<dl><dt>Kieselguhr filters</dt></dl>
<p>Filters that use a powder medium are considerably more complicated to operate, but can filter much more beer before regeneration. Common media include <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perlite" title="Perlite">perlite</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Beer_bottle" title="Beer bottle">Beer bottle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage_can" title="Beverage can">Beverage can</a>, <a href="/wiki/Widget_(beer)" title="Widget (beer)">Widget (beer)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Draught_beer" title="Draught beer">Draught beer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">Cask ale</a></div>
<p>Packaging is putting the beer into the containers in which it will leave the brewery. Typically, this means putting the beer into bottles, aluminium cans, <a href="/wiki/Keg" title="Keg">kegs</a>, or casks, but it may include putting the beer into bulk tanks for high-volume customers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By-products">By-products</span></h2>
<p><span id="Brewer's_spent_grain"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spent_grain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Spent_grain.jpg/220px-Spent_grain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Spent_grain.jpg/330px-Spent_grain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Spent_grain.jpg/440px-Spent_grain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spent_grain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Spent grain, a brewing by-product</div></div></div>
<p>Brewing by-products are "spent grain" and the sediment (or "<a href="/wiki/Sediment#Dregs" title="Sediment">dregs</a>") from the filtration process which may be dried and resold as "brewers dried yeast" for poultry feed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> or made into <a href="/wiki/Yeast_extract" title="Yeast extract">yeast extract</a> which is used in brands such as <a href="/wiki/Vegemite" title="Vegemite">Vegemite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marmite" title="Marmite">Marmite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> The process of turning the yeast sediment into edible yeast extract was discovered by German scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justus_Liebig" title="Justus Liebig">Justus Liebig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brewer's spent grain (also called spent grain, brewer's grain or draff) is the main by-product of the brewing process;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> it consists of the residue of malt and grain which remains in the mash-kettle after the mashing and lautering process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beldman_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-beldman-143">[143]</a></sup> It consists primarily of grain husks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pericarp" title="Pericarp">pericarp</a>, and fragments of <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forssell_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-forssell-144">[144]</a></sup>  As it mainly consists of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forssell_144-1"><a href="#cite_note-forssell-144">[144]</a></sup> and is readily consumed by animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-145">[145]</a></sup> spent grain is used in <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">animal feed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_145-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-145">[145]</a></sup>  Spent grains can also be used as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>, whole grains in bread,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinhart_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reinhart-146">[146]</a></sup> as well as in the production of <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> Spent grain is also an ideal medium for growing <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">mushrooms</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Shiitake" title="Shiitake">shiitake</a>, and already some breweries are either growing their own mushrooms or supplying spent grain to mushroom farms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> Spent grains can be used in the production of red bricks, to improve the open porosity and reduce thermal conductivity of the ceramic mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing_industry">Brewing industry</span></h2>
<p>The brewing industry is a global business, consisting of several dominant <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational companies</a> and many thousands of smaller producers known as  <a href="/wiki/Microbrewery" title="Microbrewery">microbreweries</a> or regional breweries depending on size and region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-market_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-market-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> More than 133 billion liters (3.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> U.S. gallons; 2.9<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> imperial gallons) are sold per year—producing total global revenues of $294.5 billion (£147.7 billion) as of 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-researchandmarkets1_152-0"><a href="#cite_note-researchandmarkets1-152">[152]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/SABMiller" title="SABMiller">SABMiller</a> became the largest brewing company in the world when it acquired Royal Grolsch, brewer of Dutch premium beer brand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grolsch" title="Grolsch">Grolsch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/InBev" title="InBev">InBev</a> was the second-largest beer-producing company in the world and <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch" title="Anheuser-Busch">Anheuser-Busch</a> held the third spot, but after the acquisition of Anheuser-Busch by InBev, the new <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch_InBev" title="Anheuser-Busch InBev">Anheuser-Busch InBev</a> company is currently the largest brewer in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Homebrewing" title="Homebrewing">Brewing at home</a> is subject to regulation and prohibition in many countries. Restrictions on homebrewing were lifted in the UK in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ukrepeal_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-ukrepeal-155">[155]</a></sup> Australia followed suit in 1972,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausrepeal_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-ausrepeal-156">[156]</a></sup> and the USA in 1978, though individual states were allowed to pass their own laws limiting production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-157">[157]</a></sup>
</p>
