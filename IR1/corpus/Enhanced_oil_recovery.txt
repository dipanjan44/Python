<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Enhanced oil recovery</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about processes applicable to conventional oil fields. For processes applied to oil sands, see <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:151px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oil_%26_gas_injection_well.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="629" data-file-width="149" height="629" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Oil_%26_gas_injection_well.jpg" width="149"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oil_%26_gas_injection_well.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Injection well used for enhanced oil recovery</div></div></div>
<p><b>Enhanced oil recovery</b> (abbreviated <b>EOR</b>), also called <b>tertiary recovery</b>, is the extraction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil field</a> that cannot be extracted otherwise. EOR can extract 30% to 60%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or more of a reservoir's oil,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_Fossil_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOE_Fossil-2">[2]</a></sup> compared to 20% to 40% using <a href="/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum#Primary_recovery" title="Extraction of petroleum">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extraction_of_petroleum#Secondary_recovery" title="Extraction of petroleum">secondary recovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> According to the US Department of Energy, there are three primary techniques for EOR: thermal, gas injection, and chemical injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_Fossil_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DOE_Fossil-2">[2]</a></sup> More advanced, speculative EOR techniques are sometimes called <b>quaternary recovery</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hobson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hobson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three primary techniques of EOR: gas injection, thermal injection, and chemical injection. Gas injection, which uses gases such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), accounts for nearly 60 percent of EOR production in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_Fossil_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-DOE_Fossil-2">[2]</a></sup> Thermal injection, which involves the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, accounts for 40 percent of EOR production in the United States, with most of it occurring in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_Fossil_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-DOE_Fossil-2">[2]</a></sup> Chemical injection, which can involve the use of long-chained molecules called <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> to increase the effectiveness of waterfloods, accounts for about one percent of EOR production in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_Fossil_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-DOE_Fossil-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2013, a technique called Plasma-Pulse technology was introduced into the United States from Russia. This technique can result in another 50 percent of improvement in existing well production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Press_Release_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Press_Release-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_injection">Gas injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gas injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gas injection or miscible flooding is presently the most-commonly used approach in enhanced oil recovery. Miscible flooding is a general term for injection processes that introduce miscible gases into the reservoir. A miscible displacement process maintains reservoir pressure and improves oil displacement because the interfacial tension between oil and water is reduced. This refers to removing the interface between the two interacting fluids. This allows for total displacement efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchlumbergerGlossMisc_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchlumbergerGlossMisc-10">[10]</a></sup>
Gases used include <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. The fluid most commonly used for miscible displacement is carbon dioxide because it reduces the oil <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> and is less expensive than <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied petroleum gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchlumbergerGlossMisc_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-SchlumbergerGlossMisc-10">[10]</a></sup> Oil displacement by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_flooding" title="Carbon dioxide flooding">carbon dioxide injection</a> relies on the phase behavior of the mixtures of that gas and the crude, which are strongly dependent on reservoir temperature, pressure and crude oil composition.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_injection">Thermal injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermal injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Steam_injection_(oil_industry)" title="Steam injection (oil industry)">Steam injection (oil industry)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steam_eor1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="343" data-file-width="240" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Steam_eor1.jpg/220px-Steam_eor1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/Steam_eor1.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steam_eor1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The steam flooding technique</div></div></div>
<p>In this approach, various methods are used to heat the crude oil in the formation to reduce its viscosity and/or vaporize part of the oil and thus decrease the mobility ratio. The increased heat reduces the surface tension and increases the permeability of the oil. The heated oil may also vaporize and then condense forming improved oil. Methods include <a href="/wiki/Steam_injection_(oil_industry)" title="Steam injection (oil industry)">cyclic steam injection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steam_injection_(oil_industry)" title="Steam injection (oil industry)">steam flooding</a> and combustion. These methods improve the sweep efficiency and the displacement efficiency. Steam injection has been used commercially since the 1960s in California fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2011 <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Solar thermal enhanced oil recovery">solar thermal enhanced oil recovery</a> projects were started in California and <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, this method is similar to thermal EOR but uses a solar array to produce the steam.
</p><p>In July 2015, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_Development_Oman" title="Petroleum Development Oman">Petroleum Development Oman</a> and <a href="/wiki/GlassPoint_Solar" title="GlassPoint Solar">GlassPoint Solar</a> announced that they signed a $600 million agreement to build a 1 GWth solar field on the Amal oilfield. The project, named <a href="/wiki/Miraah" title="Miraah">Miraah</a>, will be the world's largest solar field measured by peak thermal capacity. In August 2017, GlassPoint and its contractors crossed the threshold of 1.5 million man-hours worked without lost time injury (LTI) at Miraah.
</p><p>In November 2017, GlassPoint and Petroleum Development Oman (PDO) completed construction on the first block of the Miraah solar plant safely on schedule and on budget, and successfully delivered steam to the Amal West oilfield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in November 2017, GlassPoint and <a href="/wiki/Aera_Energy" title="Aera Energy">Aera Energy</a> announced a joint project to create California’s largest solar EOR field at the <a href="/wiki/South_Belridge_Oil_Field" title="South Belridge Oil Field">South Belridge Oil Field</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield, California</a>. The facility is projected to produce approximately 12 million barrels of steam per year through a 850MW thermal solar steam generator. It will also cut carbon emissions from the facility by 376,000 metric tons per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_flooding">Steam flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Steam flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Steam flooding (see sketch) is one means of introducing heat to the reservoir by pumping steam into the well with a pattern similar to that of water injection. Eventually the steam condenses to hot water; in the steam zone the oil evaporates, and in the hot water zone the oil expands. As a result, the oil expands, the viscosity drops, and the permeability increases. To ensure success the process has to be cyclical. This is the principal enhanced oil recovery program in use today.
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Solar thermal enhanced oil recovery">Solar EOR</a></i> is a form of steam flooding that uses <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar arrays</a> to concentrate the sun’s energy to heat water and generate steam. Solar EOR is proving to be a viable alternative to gas-fired steam production for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_industry" title="Oil industry">oil industry</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg/220px-GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg/330px-GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg/440px-GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GlassPoint_Solar_EOR_Project_in_Oilfield.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar enhanced oil recovery site</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_flooding">Fire flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fire flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fire flooding works best when the oil saturation and porosity are high. Combustion generates the heat within the reservoir itself. Continuous injection of air or other gas mixture with high oxygen content will maintain the flame front. As the fire burns, it moves through the reservoir toward production wells. Heat from the fire reduces oil viscosity and helps vaporize reservoir water to steam. The steam, hot water, combustion gas and a bank of distilled solvent all act to drive oil in front of the fire toward production wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three methods of combustion: Dry forward, reverse and wet combustion. Dry forward uses an igniter to set fire to the oil. As the fire progresses the oil is pushed away from the fire toward the producing well. In reverse the air injection and the ignition occur from opposite directions. In wet combustion water is injected just behind the front and turned into steam by the hot rock. This quenches the fire and spreads the heat more evenly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_injection">Chemical injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The injection of various chemicals, usually as dilute solutions, have been used to aid mobility and the reduction in <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>. Injection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corrosive_substance" title="Corrosive substance">caustic</a> solutions into reservoirs with oil that have <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid" title="Organic acid">organic acids</a> naturally occurring in the oil will result in the production of <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a> that may lower the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interfacial_tension" title="Interfacial tension">interfacial tension</a> enough to increase production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Injection of a dilute solution of a water-soluble <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> to increase the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the injected water can increase the amount of oil recovered in some formations. Dilute solutions of <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a> such as petroleum <a href="/wiki/Sulfonate" title="Sulfonate">sulfonates</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosurfactant" title="Biosurfactant">biosurfactants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Rhamnolipid" title="Rhamnolipid">rhamnolipids</a> may be injected to lower the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interfacial_tension" title="Interfacial tension">interfacial tension</a> or <a href="/wiki/Capillary_pressure" title="Capillary pressure">capillary pressure</a> that impedes oil droplets from moving through a reservoir. Special formulations of oil, water and surfactant, <a href="/wiki/Microemulsion" title="Microemulsion">microemulsions</a>, can be particularly effective in this. Application of these methods is usually limited by the cost of the chemicals and their adsorption and loss onto the rock of the oil containing formation. In all of these methods the chemicals are injected into several wells and the production occurs in other nearby wells.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_flooding">Polymer flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Polymer flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Polymer flooding consists in mixing long chain polymer molecules with the injected water in order to increase the water viscosity. This method improves the vertical and areal sweep efficiency as a consequence of improving the water/oil Mobility ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surfactants may be used in conjunction with polymers; They decrease the surface tension between the oil and water. This reduces the residual oil saturation and improves the macroscopic efficiency of the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Primary surfactants usually have co-surfactants, activity boosters, and co-solvents added to them to improve stability of the formulation.
</p><p>Caustic flooding is the addition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> to injection water. It does this by lowering the surface tension, reversing the rock wettability, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsification" title="Emulsification">emulsification</a> of the oil, mobilization of the oil and helps in drawing the oil out of the rock.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_injection">Microbial injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Microbial injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Primary_sources" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="Question book-new.svg" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>relies too much on <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">references</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research">primary sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please improve this section by adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research">secondary or tertiary sources</a>.</span> <small><i>(October 2012)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Microbial injection is part of <a href="/wiki/Microbial_enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Microbial enhanced oil recovery">microbial enhanced oil recovery</a> and is rarely used because of its higher cost and because the <a href="/wiki/Microbial_biodegradation#Oil_biodegradation" title="Microbial biodegradation">developments</a> is not widely accepted. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbes</a> function either by partially digesting long <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> molecules, by generating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosurfactant" title="Biosurfactant">biosurfactants</a>, or by emitting carbon dioxide (which then functions as described in Gas injection above).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Three approaches have been used to achieve microbial injection. In the first approach, bacterial cultures mixed with a food source (a carbohydrate such as <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a> is commonly used) are injected into the oil field. In the second approach, used since 1985,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> nutrients are injected into the ground to nurture existing microbial bodies; these nutrients cause the bacteria to increase production of the natural surfactants they normally use to metabolize crude oil underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> After the injected nutrients are consumed, the microbes go into near-shutdown mode, their exteriors become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a>, and they migrate to the oil-water interface area, where they cause oil droplets to form from the larger oil mass, making the droplets more likely to migrate to the wellhead. This approach has been used in oilfields near the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners" title="Four Corners">Four Corners</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Beverly Hills Oil Field">Beverly Hills Oil Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills,_California" title="Beverly Hills, California">Beverly Hills, California</a>.
</p><p>The third approach is used to address the problem of <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin wax</a> components of the crude oil, which tend to precipitate as the crude flows to the surface, since the Earth's surface is considerably cooler than the petroleum deposits (a temperature drop of 9-10-14 °C per thousand feet of depth is usual).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_carbon_dioxide_superfluids">Liquid carbon dioxide superfluids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Liquid carbon dioxide superfluids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_flooding" title="Carbon dioxide flooding">Carbon dioxide flooding</a></div>
<p>Carbon dioxide is particularly effective in reservoirs deeper than 2,000 ft., where CO<sub>2</sub> will be in a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> state. In high pressure applications with lighter oils, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> is miscible with the oil, with resultant swelling of the oil, and reduction in viscosity, and possibly also with a reduction in the surface tension with the reservoir rock. In the case of low pressure reservoirs or heavy oils, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> will form an immiscible fluid, or will only partially mix with the oil. Some oil swelling may occur, and oil viscosity can still be significantly reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In these applications, between one-half and two-thirds of the injected
CO<sub>2</sub> returns with the produced oil and is usually re-injected into the reservoir to minimize operating costs. The remainder is trapped in the oil reservoir by various means. Carbon Dioxide as a solvent has the benefit of being more economical than other similarly miscible fluids such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Water-Alternating-Gas_.28WAG.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Water-Alternating-Gas_(WAG)">Water-Alternating-Gas (WAG)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water-Alternating-Gas (WAG)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Water-alternating-gas (WAG) injection is another technique employed in EOR.  Water is used in addition to carbon dioxide.  A saline solution is used here so that carbonate formations in oil wells are not disturbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Water and carbon dioxide are injected into the oil well for larger recovery, as they typically have low miscibility with oil. Use of both water and carbon dioxide also lowers the mobility of carbon dioxide, making the gas more effective at displacing the oil in the well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup> According to a study done by Kovscek, using small slugs of both carbon dioxide and water allows for quick recovery of the oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup> Additionally, in a study done by Dang in 2014, using water with a lower salinity allows for greater oil removal, and greater geochemical interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasma-Pulse">Plasma-Pulse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Plasma-Pulse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plasma-Pulse technology is the newest technique used in the USA as of 2013.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The technology originated in the Russian Federation at the St. Petersburg State Mining University with funding and assistance from the <a href="/wiki/Skolkovo_Innovation_Center" title="Skolkovo Innovation Center">Skolkovo Innovation Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skolkovo_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skolkovo-27">[27]</a></sup> The development team in Russia and deployment teams across Russia, Europe and now the USA have tested this technology in vertical wells with nearly 90% of wells showing positive effects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The Plasma-Pulse Oil Well EOR uses low energy emissions to create the same effect that many other technologies can produce except without negative ecological impact<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In nearly every case the volume of water pulled with the oil is actually reduced from pre-EOR treatment instead of increased<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Current clients and users of the new technology include <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ONGC" title="ONGC">ONGC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gazprom" title="Gazprom">Gazprom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosneft" title="Rosneft">Rosneft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lukoil" title="Lukoil">Lukoil</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>It is based in the same technology as the Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulsed_Plasma_Thruster" title="Pulsed Plasma Thruster">Pulsed Plasma Thruster</a> which was used on two space ships and is currently being advanced for use in horizontal wells<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_costs_and_benefits">Economic costs and benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economic costs and benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adding oil recovery methods adds to the cost of oil —in the case of CO<sub>2</sub> typically between 0.5-8.0 US$ per tonne of CO<sub>2</sub>. The increased extraction of oil on the other hand, is an economic benefit with the revenue depending on prevailing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_prices" title="Oil prices">oil prices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Onshore EOR has paid in the range of a net 10-16 US$ per tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> injected for oil prices of 15-20 US$/<a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrel</a>. Prevailing prices depend on many factors but can determine the economic suitability of any procedure, with more procedures and more expensive procedures being economically viable at higher prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Example: With oil prices at around 90 US$/barrel, the economic benefit is about 70 US$ per tonne CO<sub>2</sub>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> estimates that 20 billion tons of captured CO<sub>2</sub> could produce 67 billion barrels of economically recoverable oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OilandGas_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-OilandGas-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is believed that the use of captured, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">anthropogenic carbon dioxide</a>, derived from the exploitation of <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal reserves, to drive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_power_generation" title="Electric power generation">electric power generation</a> and support EOR from existing and future oil and gas wells offers a multifaceted solution to U.S. energy, environmental, and economic challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OilandGas_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-OilandGas-30">[30]</a></sup> There is no doubt that <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and oil resources are finite. The U.S. is in a strong position to leverage such traditional energy sources to supply future power needs while other sources are being explored and developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OilandGas_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-OilandGas-30">[30]</a></sup> For the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_industry" title="Coal industry">coal industry</a>, CO<sub>2</sub> EOR creates a market for coal <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproducts" title="Byproducts">byproducts</a> and reduces the costs associated with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon sequestration and storage</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_EOR_projects">CO<sub>2</sub> EOR projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: CO2 EOR projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Boundary_Dam.2C_Canada"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_Dam,_Canada">Boundary Dam, Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Boundary Dam, Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SaskPower's Boundary Dam project retrofitted its coal-fired power station in 2014 with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon Capture and Sequestration</a> (CCS) technology. The plant will capture 1 million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> annually, which it sold to <a href="/wiki/Cenovus_Energy" title="Cenovus Energy">Cenovus Energy</a> for enhanced oil recovery at its Weyburn Oil Field<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>, prior to the sale of Cenovus's Saskatchewan assets in 2017 to Whitecap Resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitecap_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitecap-32">[32]</a></sup> The project is expected to inject a net 18 million ton CO<sub>2</sub> and recover an additional 130 million barrels (21,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of oil, extending the life of the oil field by 25 years.(<a href="#CITEREFBrown2001">Brown 2001</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownmay2001_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brownmay2001-33">[33]</a></sup> There is a projected 26+ million tonnes (net of production) of CO<sub>2</sub> to be stored in Weyburn, plus another 8.5 million tonnes (net of production) stored at the <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project</a>, resulting in a net reduction in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">CO<sub>2</sub> storage</a> in the oilfield . That's the equivalent of taking nearly 7 million cars off the road for a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Since CO<sub>2</sub> injection began in late 2000, the EOR project has performed largely as predicted. Currently, some 1600 m<sup>3</sup> (10,063 barrels) per day of incremental oil is being produced from the field.
</p>
<h3><span id="Petra_Nova.2C_United_States"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Petra_Nova,_United_States">Petra Nova, United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Petra Nova, United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Petra_Nova" title="Petra Nova">Petra Nova</a> project uses post-combustion amine absorption to capture some of the carbon dioxide emissions from one of the boilers at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W.A_Parish" title="W.A Parish">W.A Parish</a> power plant in Texas, and transports it by pipeline to the West Ranch oil field for use in enhanced oil recovery.
</p>
<h3><span id="Kemper_Project.2C_United_States"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kemper_Project,_United_States">Kemper Project, United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Kemper Project, United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a>'s Kemper County energy facility, or <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, was to have been a first-of-its-kind plant in the U.S. that was expected to be online in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>. Its coal gasification component has since been canceled, and the plant has been converted to a conventional natural gas combined cycle power plant without carbon capture.  The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a> subsidiary worked with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> and other partners to develop cleaner, less expensive, more reliable methods for producing electricity with coal that also support EOR production. For the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, rather than burning <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> directly to make electricity, <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> technology was intended to break down the <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_components" title="Chemical components">chemical components</a>, removes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impurities" title="Impurities">impurities</a> before it is fired, avoid certain emissions, and take gases that result from this chemical breakdown to fuel the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> power plant. This plant, along with others like it, would have been more efficient and therefore cleaner than traditional coal-fired power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OilandGas_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-OilandGas-30">[30]</a></sup> Additionally, the unique location of the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, and its proximity to <a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">oil reserves</a>, made it an ideal candidate for enhanced oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Weyburn-Midale.2C_Canada"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Weyburn-Midale,_Canada">Weyburn-Midale, Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Weyburn-Midale, Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="434" data-file-width="636" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg/220px-Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg/330px-Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg/440px-Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cenovus_Unit_Oil_Production.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Weyburn-Midale Oil production over time, both before and after EOR was introduced yo the field.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale</a> oil field began to employ EOR as a method of oil extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2008, the oilfield became the worlds largest storage site of Carbon Dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It is estimated that the EOR project will store around 20 million tons of Carbon Dioxide, generate about 130 million barrels of oil, and extend the life of the field by over two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The site is also notable as it hosted a study on the effects of EOR on nearby seismic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_EOR_in_the_United_States">CO<sub>2</sub> EOR in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: CO2 EOR in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States has been using CO<sub>2</sub> EOR for several decades. For over 30 years, oil fields in the Permian Basin have implemented CO<sub>2</sub> EOR using naturally sourced CO<sub>2</sub> from New Mexico and Colorado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRI_CO2_EOR_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-WRI_CO2_EOR-40">[40]</a></sup> The Department of Energy (DOE) has estimated that full use of 'next generation' CO<sub>2</sub>-EOR in United States could generate an additional 240 billion barrels (38 km<sup>3</sup>) of recoverable oil resources. Developing this potential would depend on the availability of commercial CO<sub>2</sub> in large volumes, which could be made possible by widespread use of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>. For comparison, the total undeveloped US domestic oil resources still in the ground total more than 1 trillion barrels (160 km<sup>3</sup>), most of it remaining unrecoverable. The DOE estimates that if the EOR potential were to be fully realized, state and local treasuries would gain $280 billion in revenues from future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royalties" title="Royalties">royalties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Severance_tax" title="Severance tax">severance taxes</a>, and state income taxes on oil production, aside from other economic benefits.
</p><p>The main barrier to taking further advantage of CO<sub>2</sub> EOR in the United States has been an insufficient supply of affordable CO<sub>2</sub>. Currently, there is a cost gap between what an oilfield operate could afford to pay for CO<sub>2</sub> under normal market conditions and the cost to capture and transport CO<sub>2</sub> from power plants and industrial sources, so most CO<sub>2</sub> comes from natural sources. However, using CO<sub>2</sub> from power plants or industrial sources could reduce the carbon footprint (if the CO<sub>2</sub> is stored underground). For some industrial sources, such as natural gas processing or fertilizer and ethanol production, the cost gap is small (potentially $10–20/tonne CO<sub>2</sub>). For other man-made sources of CO<sub>2</sub>, including power generation and a variety of industrial processes, capture costs are greater, and the cost gap becomes much larger (potentially $30–50/tonne CO<sub>2</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The Enhanced Oil Recovery Initiative has brought together leaders from industry, the environmental community, labor, and state governments to advance CO<sub>2</sub> EOR in the United States and close the price gap.
</p><p>In the US, regulations can both assist and slow down the development of EOR for use in carbon capture &amp; utilization, as well as general oil production. One of the primary regulations governing EOR is the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a> of 1974 (SDWA), which gives most of the regulatory power over EOR and similar oil recovery operations to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-42">[42]</a></sup> The agency in turn delegated some of this power to its own Underground Injection Control Program,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:32-43">[43]</a></sup> and much of the rest of this regulatory authority to state and tribal governments, making much of EOR regulation a localized affair under the minimum requirements of the SDWA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The EPA then collects information from these local governments and individual wells to ensure they follow overall federal regulation, such as the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, which dictates reporting guidelines for any Carbon Dioxide sequestration operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:33_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-:33-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Beyond the atmospheric concerns, most of these federal guidelines are to ensure that the Carbon Dioxide injection causes no major damage to America;s waterways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Overall, the locality of EOR regulation can make EOR projects more difficult, as different standards in different regions can slow down construction and force separate approaches to utilize the same technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2018, Congress passed and the President signed an expansion of the carbon capture tax credits defined in section 45Q of the IRS' Internal Revenue code.  Previously, these credits were limited to $10/ton and capped at a total of 75 million tons.  Under the expansion, carbon capture and utilization projects like EOR will be eligible for a tax credit of $35/ton, and sequestration projects will receive a $50/ton credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The expanded tax credit would be available for 12 years to any plant constructed by 2024, with no volume cap. If successful, these credits "could help sequester between 200 million and 2.2 billion metric tons of carbon dioxide" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and bring down carbon capture and sequestration costs from a currently estimated $60/ton at Petra Nova to as low as $10/ton.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enhanced_oil_recovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Enhanced oil recovery wells typically pump large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Produced_water" title="Produced water">produced water</a> to the surface. This water contains <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> and may also contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> heavy metals and <a href="/wiki/Naturally_occurring_radioactive_material" title="Naturally occurring radioactive material">radioactive substances</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> This can be very damaging to <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> sources and the environment generally if not properly controlled. Disposal wells are used to prevent surface contamination of soil and water by injecting the produced water deep underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_UIC_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_UIC-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Injection_well" title="Injection well">injection well</a> activity is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) and state governments under the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Basicinfo_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Basicinfo-54">[54]</a></sup> EPA has issued Underground Injection Control (UIC) regulations in order to protect drinking water sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Regs_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_Regs-55">[55]</a></sup> Enhanced oil recovery wells are regulated as "Class II" wells by the EPA. The regulations require well operators to reinject the brine used for recovery deep underground in Class II disposal wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_UIC_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_UIC-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
