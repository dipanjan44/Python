<p><b>Climate change denial</b>, or <b>global warming denial</b>, is part of the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">global warming controversy</a>. It involves <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a>, dismissal, or unwarranted doubt that contradicts the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific opinion on climate change</a>, including the extent to which it is <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">caused by humans</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">impacts on nature and human society</a>, or the  potential of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to global warming</a> by human actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncse-pillars_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncse-pillars-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE-why-denial_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE-why-denial-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powell2012-4">[4]</a></sup> Some deniers endorse the term, while others prefer the term <b>climate change skepticism</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE-why-denial_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE-why-denial-3">[3]</a></sup> Several scientists have noted that "skepticism" is <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">an inaccurate description</a> for those who deny anthropogenic global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_p2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_p2-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010-7">[7]</a></sup> In effect, the two terms form a continuous, overlapping range of views, and generally have the same characteristics: both reject, to a greater or lesser extent, the scientific consensus on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-continuum_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-continuum-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timmer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timmer-9">[9]</a></sup> Climate change denial can also be implicit, when individuals or social groups accept the science but fail to come to terms with it or to translate their acceptance into action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_implicit_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_implicit-10">[10]</a></sup> Several <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> studies have analyzed these positions as forms of <a href="/wiki/Denialism" title="Denialism">denialism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-continuum_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-continuum-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timmer_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Timmer-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The campaign to undermine public trust in climate science has been described as a "denial machine" organized by industrial, political and ideological interests, and supported by conservative media and skeptical bloggers to <a href="/wiki/Manufactured_controversy" title="Manufactured controversy">manufacture uncertainty</a> about global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVaidyanathan2014_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVaidyanathan2014-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_machine_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-denial_machine-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_denial_machine_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek_denial_machine-14">[14]</a></sup> In the public debate, phrases such as <i>climate skepticism</i> have frequently been used with the same meaning as <i>climate denialism</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nerlich_2010_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nerlich_2010-15">[15]</a></sup> The labels are contested: those actively challenging climate science commonly describe themselves as "skeptics", but many do not comply with common standards of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_skepticism" title="Scientific skepticism">scientific skepticism</a> and, regardless of evidence, persistently deny the validity of human caused global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-continuum_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-continuum-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific opinion on climate change</a> is that human activity is extremely likely to be the primary driver of climate change,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_MIT_Press_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_MIT_Press-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">politics of global warming</a> have been affected by climate change denial, hindering efforts to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">prevent climate change</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adapt to the warming climate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freeman351_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-freeman351-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-public-success_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-public-success-20">[20]</a></sup> Those promoting denial commonly use rhetorical tactics to give the appearance of a scientific controversy where there is none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deithelm_McKie_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deithelm_McKie-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the world's countries, the climate change denial industry is most powerful in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_industry_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-denial_industry-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book_industry_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-book_industry-24">[24]</a></sup> From 2015 to 2017 (after having already served from 2003 to 2007), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Environment_and_Public_Works" title="United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works">United States Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works</a> was chaired by <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels_lobby" title="Fossil fuels lobby">oil lobbyist</a> and climate change denier <a href="/wiki/Jim_Inhofe" title="Jim Inhofe">Jim Inhofe</a>, who had previously called climate change "the greatest hoax ever perpetrated against the American people" and claimed to have debunked the alleged hoax in February 2015 when he brought a snowball with him in the Senate chamber and tossed it across the floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He was succeeded in 2017 by <a href="/wiki/John_Barrasso" title="John Barrasso">John Barrasso</a>, who similarly said: "The climate is constantly changing. The role human activity plays is not known."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Organised campaigning to undermine public trust in climate science is associated with <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a> policies and backed by industrial interests opposed to the regulation of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klein2011_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-klein2011-27">[27]</a></sup> Climate change denial has been associated with the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels_lobby" title="Fossil fuels lobby">fossil fuels lobby</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Political_activities_of_the_Koch_brothers" title="Political activities of the Koch brothers">Koch brothers</a>, industry advocates and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservative" title="Conservative">conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tanks</a>, often in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freeman351_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-freeman351-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michaels_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michaels-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoggan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoggan-30">[30]</a></sup> More than 90% of papers sceptical on climate change originate from right-wing think tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
The total annual income of these climate change counter-movement-organizations is roughly $900 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Between 2002 and 2010, nearly $120 million (£77 million) was anonymously donated via the <a href="/wiki/Donors_Trust" title="Donors Trust">Donors Trust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donors_Capital_Fund" title="Donors Capital Fund">Donors Capital Fund</a> to more than 100 organisations seeking to undermine the public perception of the science on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial-thinktanks-network_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-denial-thinktanks-network-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2013 the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Media_and_Democracy" title="Center for Media and Democracy">Center for Media and Democracy</a> reported that the <a href="/wiki/State_Policy_Network" title="State Policy Network">State Policy Network</a> (SPN), an umbrella group of 64 U.S. think tanks, had been lobbying on behalf of major corporations and conservative donors to oppose climate change regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the late 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil companies</a> have <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil_climate_change_controversy" title="ExxonMobil climate change controversy">published research</a> broadly in line with the standard views on global warming.  Despite this, oil companies organized  a climate change denial campaign to disseminate public disinformation for several decades, a strategy that has been compared to the organized denial of the hazards of tobacco smoking by the <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_industry" title="Tobacco industry">tobacco industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151105_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151105-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20150708_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20150708-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2322" data-file-width="4128" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg/350px-Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg/525px-Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg/700px-Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amardeo_Sarma_lecture_1_EuroSkepCon2015.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Amardeo Sarma lecturing about climate change denialism and the future world energy and environmental problems during the <a href="/wiki/European_Skeptics_Congress" title="European Skeptics Congress">European Skeptics Congress</a> 2015</div></div></div>
<p>"Climate change skepticism" and "climate change denial" refer to denial, dismissal or unwarranted doubt of the scientific consensus on the rate and extent of global warming, its significance, or its connection to human behavior, in whole or in part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_2012_why_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_2012_why-39">[39]</a></sup> Though there is a distinction between <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a> which indicates doubting the truth of an assertion and outright <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a> of the truth of an assertion, in the public debate phrases such as "climate scepticism" have frequently been used with the same meaning as climate denialism or <a href="/wiki/Contrarian" title="Contrarian">contrarianism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nerlich_2010_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nerlich_2010-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>The terminology emerged in the 1990s. Even though all scientists adhere to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_skepticism" title="Scientific skepticism">scientific skepticism</a> as an inherent part of the process, by mid November 1995 the word "skeptic" was being used specifically for the minority who publicised views contrary to the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific consensus</a>. This small group of scientists presented their views in public statements and the media, rather than to the scientific community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This usage continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In his December 1995 article <i>The Heat is On: The warming of the world's climate sparks a blaze of denial </i>, <a href="/wiki/Ross_Gelbspan" title="Ross Gelbspan">Ross Gelbspan</a> said industry had engaged "a small band of skeptics" to confuse public opinion in a "persistent and well-funded campaign of denial".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelbspan_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gelbspan-44">[44]</a></sup> His 1997 book <i>The Heat is On</i> may have been the first to concentrate specifically on the topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In it, Gelbspan discussed a "pervasive denial of global warming" in a "persistent campaign of denial and suppression" involving "undisclosed funding of these 'greenhouse skeptics' " with "the climate skeptics" confusing the public and influencing decision makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelbspan_1998_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gelbspan_1998-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>A November 2006 <a href="/wiki/CBC_Television" title="CBC Television">CBC Television</a> documentary on the campaign was titled "The Denial Machine".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlóci_Denial_Machine_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orlóci_Denial_Machine-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2007 journalist <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Begley" title="Sharon Begley">Sharon Begley</a> reported on the "denial machine",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begley_machine_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Begley_machine-49">[49]</a></sup> a phrase subsequently used by academics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_machine_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-denial_machine-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orlóci_Denial_Machine_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Orlóci_Denial_Machine-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to <i>explicit denial</i>, social groups have shown <i>implicit denial</i> by accepting the scientific consensus, but failing to come to terms with its implications or take action to reduce the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_implicit_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_implicit-10">[10]</a></sup> This was exemplified in <a href="/wiki/Kari_Norgaard" title="Kari Norgaard">Kari Norgaard</a>'s study of a village in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> affected by climate change, where residents diverted their attention to other issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norgaard_2011_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norgaard_2011-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The terminology is debated: most of those actively rejecting the scientific consensus use the terms <i>skeptic</i> and <i>climate change skepticism</i>, and only a few have expressed preference for being described as deniers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_2012_why_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_2012_why-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Washington_p2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_p2-6">[6]</a></sup> but the word "skepticism" is incorrectly used, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_skepticism" title="Scientific skepticism">scientific skepticism</a> is an intrinsic part of scientific methodology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The term <i>contrarian</i> is more specific, but used less frequently. In academic literature and journalism, the terms <i>climate change denial</i> and <i>climate change deniers</i> have well established usage as descriptive terms without any pejorative intent. Both the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Science_Education" title="National Center for Science Education">National Center for Science Education</a> and historian <a href="/wiki/Spencer_R._Weart" title="Spencer R. Weart">Spencer R. Weart</a> recognise that either option is problematic, but have decided to use "climate change denial" rather than "skepticism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSEpreference_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSEpreference-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Terms related to <i>denialism</i> have been criticised for introducing a moralistic tone, and potentially implying a link with <a href="/wiki/Holocaust_denial" title="Holocaust denial">Holocaust denial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-O'Neill_Boykoff_2010-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderegg_Prall_E152_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderegg_Prall_E152-55">[55]</a></sup> There have been claims that this link is intentional, which academics have strongly disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VerbalWarming_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-VerbalWarming-56">[56]</a></sup> The usage of "denial" long predates the Holocaust, and is commonly applied in other areas such as <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS_denialism" title="HIV/AIDS denialism">HIV/AIDS denialism</a>: the claim is described by John Timmer of <i><a href="/wiki/Ars_Technica" title="Ars Technica">Ars Technica</a></i> as itself being a form of denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timmer_HIV_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Timmer_HIV-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2014, an open letter from the <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_Skeptical_Inquiry" title="Committee for Skeptical Inquiry">Committee for Skeptical Inquiry</a> called on the media to stop using the term "skepticism" when referring to climate change denial. They contrasted scientific skepticism—which is "foundational to the scientific method"—with denial—"the a priori rejection of ideas without objective consideration"—and the behavior of those involved in political attempts to undermine climate science. They said "Not all individuals who call themselves climate change skeptics are deniers. But virtually all deniers have falsely branded themselves as skeptics. By perpetrating this misnomer, journalists have granted undeserved credibility to those who reject science and scientific inquiry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VerbalWarming_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-VerbalWarming-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSI_open_letter_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSI_open_letter-58">[58]</a></sup> In June 2015 <a href="/wiki/Media_Matters_for_America" title="Media Matters for America">Media Matters for America</a> were told by <i>The New York Times</i> Public Editor that the newspaper was increasingly tending to use "denier" when "someone is challenging established science", but assessing this on an individual basis with no fixed policy, and would not use the term when someone was "kind of wishy-washy on the subject or in the middle." The executive director of the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Environmental_Journalists" title="Society of Environmental Journalists">Society of Environmental Journalists</a> said that while there was reasonable skepticism about specific issues, she felt that denier was "the most accurate term when someone claims there is no such thing as global warming, or agrees that it exists but denies that it has any cause we could understand or any impact that could be measured."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMfA_20150622_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMfA_20150622-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a></div>
<p>Research on the effect of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> on the climate began in 1824, when <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> inferred the existence the atmospheric "<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>". In 1860, <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> quantified the effects of greenhouse gases on absorption of infrared radiation. <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> in 1896 showed that coal burning could cause global warming, and in 1938 <a href="/wiki/Guy_Stewart_Callendar" title="Guy Stewart Callendar">Guy Stewart Callendar</a> found it already happening to some extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conwayoreskes170_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-conwayoreskes170-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-powell36_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-powell36-61">[61]</a></sup> Research advanced rapidly after 1940; from 1957, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Revelle" title="Roger Revelle">Roger Revelle</a> alerted the public to risks that fossil fuel burning was "a grandiose scientific experiment" on climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> took on research, the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Jule_Gregory_Charney" title="Jule Gregory Charney">Charney Report</a> concluded that substantial warming was already on the way, and "A wait-and-see policy may mean waiting until it is too late."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959 a scientist working for <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Shell</a> suggested in a <a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a> article, that carbon cycles are too vast to upset Nature's balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to increasing public awareness of the greenhouse effect in the 1970s, conservative reaction built up, denying environmental concerns which could lead to government regulation. With the 1981 <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Ronald_Reagan" title="Presidency of Ronald Reagan">Presidency of Ronald Reagan</a>, global warming became a political issue, with immediate plans to cut spending on environmental research, particularly climate related, and stop funding for CO<sub>2</sub> monitoring. Reagan appointed as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Secretary of Energy">Secretary of Energy</a> <a href="/wiki/James_B._Edwards" title="James B. Edwards">James B. Edwards</a>, who said that there was no real global warming problem. Congressman <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> had studied under Revelle and was aware of the developing science: he joined others in arranging congressional hearings from 1981 onwards, with testimony by scientists including Revelle, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Schneider" title="Stephen Schneider">Stephen Schneider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Smith_Broecker" title="Wallace Smith Broecker">Wallace Smith Broecker</a>. The hearings gained enough public attention to reduce the cuts in atmospheric research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A polarized party-political debate developed. In 1982 <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_B._Idso" title="Sherwood B. Idso">Sherwood B. Idso</a> published his book <i>Carbon Dioxide: Friend or Foe?</i> which said increases in CO<sub>2</sub> would not warm the planet, but would fertilize crops and were "something to be encouraged and not suppressed", while complaining that his theories had been rejected by the "scientific establishment". An <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) report in 1983 said global warming was "not a theoretical problem but a threat whose effects will be felt within a few years", with potentially "catastrophic" consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The Reagan administration reacted by calling the report "alarmist", and the dispute got wide news coverage. Public attention turned to other issues, then the 1985 finding of a polar <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone hole</a> brought a swift international response. To the public, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_and_climate_change" title="Ozone depletion and climate change">this was related to climate change</a> and the possibility of effective action, but news interest faded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Public attention was renewed amidst summer droughts and heat waves when <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> testified to a Congressional hearing on 23 June 1988,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_testimony_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_testimony-70">[70]</a></sup> stating with high confidence that long term warming was under way with severe warming likely within the next 50 years, and warning of likely storms and floods. There was increasing media attention: the scientific community had reached a broad consensus that the climate was warming, human activity was very likely the primary cause, and there would be significant consequences if the warming trend was not curbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earlyconsensus_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-earlyconsensus-71">[71]</a></sup> These facts encouraged discussion about new laws concerning environmental regulation, which was opposed by the fossil fuel industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1989 onwards industry funded organisations including the <a href="/wiki/Global_Climate_Coalition" title="Global Climate Coalition">Global Climate Coalition</a> and the <a href="/wiki/George_C._Marshall_Institute" title="George C. Marshall Institute">George C. Marshall Institute</a> sought to spread doubt among the public, in a strategy already developed by the tobacco industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-approach_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-approach-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-docs_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-docs-75">[75]</a></sup> A small group of scientists opposed to the consensus on global warming became politically involved, and with support from conservative political interests, began publishing in books and the press rather than in scientific journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> This small group of scientists included some of the same people that were part of the strategy already tried by the tobacco industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merchants_of_doubt_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-merchants_of_doubt-77">[77]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spencer_Weart" title="Spencer Weart">Spencer Weart</a> identifies this period as the point where legitimate skepticism about basic aspects of climate science was no longer justified, and those spreading mistrust about these issues became deniers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weart_p46_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weart_p46-78">[78]</a></sup> As their arguments were increasingly refuted by the scientific community and new data, deniers turned to political arguments, making personal attacks on the reputation of scientists, and promoting ideas of a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_conspiracy_theory" title="Global warming conspiracy theory">global warming conspiracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">1989 fall of communism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a>'s international reach at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">1992 Rio Earth Summit</a>, the attention of <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States#Think_tanks_and_foundations" title="Conservatism in the United States">U.S. conservative think tanks</a>, which had been organised in the 1970s as an intellectual counter-movement to socialism, turned from the "red scare" to the "green scare" which they saw as a threat to their aims of private property, free trade market economies and global capitalism. As a counter-movement, they used <a href="/wiki/Environmental_skepticism" title="Environmental skepticism">environmental skepticism</a> to promote denial of the reality of problems such as loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1992, an EPA report linked <a href="/wiki/Passive_smoking" title="Passive smoking">second-hand smoke</a> with lung cancer. The tobacco industry engaged the <a href="/wiki/APCO_Worldwide" title="APCO Worldwide">APCO Worldwide</a> public relations company, which set out a strategy of <a href="/wiki/Astroturfing" title="Astroturfing">astroturfing</a> campaigns to cast doubt on the science by linking smoking anxieties with other issues, including global warming, in order to turn public opinion against calls for government intervention. The campaign depicted public concerns as "unfounded fears" supposedly based only on "junk science" in contrast to their "sound science", and operated through <a href="/wiki/Front_organization" title="Front organization">front groups</a>, primarily the <a href="/wiki/Advancement_of_Sound_Science_Center" title="Advancement of Sound Science Center">Advancement of Sound Science Center</a> (TASSC) and its Junk Science website, run by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Milloy" title="Steven Milloy">Steven Milloy</a>. A tobacco company memo commented "Doubt is our product since it is the best means of competing with the 'body of fact' that exists in the mind of the general public. It is also the means of establishing a controversy." During the 1990s, the tobacco campaign died away, and TASSC began taking funding from oil companies including Exxon. Its website became central in distributing "almost every kind of climate-change denial that has found its way into the popular press."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-requiem_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-requiem-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1990s, the Marshall Institute began campaigning against increased regulations on environmental issues such as <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, second-hand smoke, and the dangers of <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marshall_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-marshall-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-requiem_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-requiem-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merchants_of_doubt_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-merchants_of_doubt-77">[77]</a></sup> In each case their argument was that the science was too uncertain to justify any government intervention, a strategy it borrowed from earlier efforts to downplay the health effects of tobacco in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-approach_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-approach-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-docs_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-docs-75">[75]</a></sup> This campaign would continue for the next two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>These efforts succeeded in influencing public perception of climate science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Between 1988 and the 1990s, public discourse shifted from the science and data of climate change to discussion of politics and surrounding controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>The campaign to spread doubt continued into the 1990s, including an advertising campaign funded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_industry" title="Coal industry">coal industry</a> advocates intended to "reposition global warming as theory rather than fact,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> and a 1998 proposal written by the <a href="/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute" title="American Petroleum Institute">American Petroleum Institute</a> intending to recruit scientists to convince politicians, the media and the public that climate science was too uncertain to warrant environmental regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The proposal included a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">US$</a> 5,000,000 multi-point strategy to "maximize the impact of scientific views consistent with ours on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">Congress</a>, the media and other key audiences", with a goal of "raising questions about and undercutting the 'prevailing scientific wisdom'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, Gelbspan noted that his fellow journalists accepted that global warming was occurring, but said they were in "'stage-two' denial of the climate crisis", unable to accept the feasibility of answers to the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGelbspan19983,_35,_46,_197_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGelbspan19983,_35,_46,_197-89">[89]</a></sup> A subsequent book by Milburn and Conrad on <i>The Politics of Denial</i> described "economic and psychological forces" producing denial of the consensus on global warming issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilburnConrad1998_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-MilburnConrad1998-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>These efforts by climate change denial groups were recognized as an organized campaign beginning in the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The sociologists <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Riley_Dunlap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Riley Dunlap (page does not exist)">Riley Dunlap</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aaron_McCright&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aaron McCright (page does not exist)">Aaron McCright</a> played a significant role in this shift when they published an article in 2000 exploring the connection between conservative think tanks and climate change denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Later work would continue the argument specific groups were marshalling skepticism against climate change - A study in 2008 from the University of Central Florida analyzed the sources of "environmentally skeptical" literature published in the United States. The analysis demonstrated that 92% of the literature was partly or wholly affiliated with a self-proclaimed conservative think tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> A later piece of research from 2015 identified 4,556 individuals with overlapping network ties to 164 organizations which are responsible for the most efforts to downplay the threat of climate change in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gelbspan's <i>Boiling Point</i>, published in 2004, detailed the fossil-fuel industry's campaign to deny climate change and undermine public confidence in climate science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gelbspan_2004_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gelbspan_2004-96">[96]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a>'</i>s August 2007 cover story "The Truth About Denial", <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Begley" title="Sharon Begley">Sharon Begley</a> reported that "the denial machine is running at full throttle", and said that this "well-coordinated, well-funded campaign" by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_scientists_opposing_the_mainstream_scientific_assessment_of_global_warming" title="List of scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming">contrarian scientists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-market" title="Free-market">free-market</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tanks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermajor" title="Supermajor">industry</a> had "created a paralyzing fog of doubt around climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Begley_machine_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Begley_machine-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Referencing work of sociologists Robert Antonio and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brulle" title="Robert Brulle">Robert Brulle</a>, Wayne A. White has written that climate change denial has become the top priority in a broader agenda against environmental regulation being pursued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoliberals" title="Neoliberals">neoliberals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White2012_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-White2012-97">[97]</a></sup> Today, climate change skepticism is most prominently seen in the United States, where the media disproportionately features views of the climate change denial community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In addition to the media, the contrarian movement has also been sustained by the growth of the internet, having gained some of its support from internet bloggers, talk radio hosts and newspaper columnists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and others reported in 2015 that <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil companies</a> knew that <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">burning oil and gas</a> could cause climate change and global warming since the 1970s but nonetheless funded deniers for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151105_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151105-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TG-20150708_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-TG-20150708-36">[36]</a></sup> Dana Nuccitelli wrote in <i>The Guardian</i> that a small fringe group of climate deniers were no longer taken seriously at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>, in an agreement that "we need to stop delaying and start getting serious about preventing a climate crisis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> However, <i>The New York Times</i> says any implementation is voluntary and will depend on any future world leaders—and every Republican candidate in 2016 has questioned or denied the science of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151212_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151212-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial_networks">Denial networks</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Climate change policy of the United States">Climate change policy of the United States</a></div>
<p>A poll conducted by the New York Times Magazine in 2006 found that only 56% of Americans believe that average global temperatures have risen even though scientists think that they have. A majority of Americans also believe that scientists are still divided on the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study was conducted to determine whether or not conservative <a href="/wiki/White_Americans" title="White Americans">white Americans</a> were more likely to deny climate change.  Researchers from <a href="/wiki/Lyman_Briggs_College" title="Lyman Briggs College">Lyman Briggs College</a> took samples of conservative white males of different understandings of global warming and categorized them separately.  Categorized into three groups:  conservative white males who claim to understand global warming, conservative white males who claim not to understand global warming, and other individuals.  It was concluded that many of these conservative white males who self-report understanding global warming believe that the mass media has over exaggerated the effects of global warming and climate change and that the effects of it have never happened.  48.4% of conservative white males who report understanding global warming very well in the study said that the effects of global warming will never happen compared to 19% who do not understand it very well and 7.4% of all other adults. Many people appear to be confused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_science" title="Climate science">climate science</a> however, the study showed that the people who self reported understanding global warming were most prone to denying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another study by <a href="/wiki/Utah_State_University" title="Utah State University">Utah State University</a> discusses geographic variation on climate change opinions at state and local scales in the United States. It was noted that prior research was found that public climate change policy support and behavior are significantly influenced by public beliefs, attitudes and risk perceptions. In addition, to complement previous stated factors, they're also influenced by knowledge, emotion, ideology, demographics, and personal experience. Where people live can account for some significance, since some people live in weather extreme areas, they could be desensitized to the overall change in climates as well.  Surveys were conducted and showed that 63% of Americans believe that global warming is happening, 47% believe that it is human caused and 42% believe that most scientists think it is happening. In the study, a model was constructed to determine the public opinion of climate change across the nation. Representative telephone based surveys were used to investigate opinions in four states: California, Texas, Ohio and Colorado in addition to the Columbus and San Francisco metropolitan areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>While it had been the case as of 2010 that more than two-thirds of television weathercasters had denied that warming was real and largely caused by human activity, as of March of 2018 the rate of acceptance among TV forecasters that the climate is changing has increased to ninety-five percent. The number of local television stories about global warming has also increased, by fifteen-fold. <a href="/wiki/Climate_Central" title="Climate Central">Climate Central</a> has received some of the credit for this because they provide classes for meteorologists and graphics for television stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span></h3>
<p>The Clexit Coalition claims to be: "A new international organisation (which) aims to prevent ratification of the costly and dangerous Paris global warming treaty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> It has members in 26 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> According to The Guardian newspaper: "Clexit leaders are heavily involved in tobacco and fossil fuel-funded organizations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_and_positions_on_global_warming">Arguments and positions on global warming</span></h2>
<p>Some climate change denial groups say that because CO<sub>2</sub> is only a trace gas in the atmosphere (roughly 400ppm, or 0.04%) it can only have a minor effect on the climate. Scientists have known for over a century that even this small proportion has a significant warming effect, and doubling the proportion leads to a large temperature increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trace_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-trace-109">[109]</a></sup> The scientific consensus, as summarized by the IPCC's fourth assessment report, the U.S. Geological Survey, and other reports, is that human activity is the leading cause of climate change. The burning of fossil fuels accounts for around 30 billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub> each year, which is 130 times the amount produced by volcanoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Some groups allege that water vapor is a more significant greenhouse gas, and is left out of many climate models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trace_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-trace-109">[109]</a></sup> While water vapor is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, the very short atmospheric lifetime of water vapor (about 10 days) compared that of CO<sub>2</sub> (hundreds of years) means that  CO<sub>2</sub> is the primary driver of increasing temperatures; water vapour acts as a feedback, not a forcing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>. Water vapor has been incorporated into climate models since their inception in the late 1800s. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate denial groups may also argue that global warming stopped recently, a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_hiatus" title="Global warming hiatus">global warming hiatus</a>, or that global temperatures are actually decreasing, leading to <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">global cooling</a>. These arguments are based on short term fluctuations, and ignore the long term pattern of warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>These groups often point to natural variability, such as sunspots and cosmic rays, to explain the warming trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> According to these groups, there is natural variability that will abate over time, and human influences have little to do with it. These factors are already taken into account when developing climate models, and the scientific consensus is that they cannot explain the observed warming trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>At a May 2018 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Science,_Space,_and_Technology" title="United States House Committee on Science, Space, and Technology">United States House Committee on Science, Space, and Technology</a>, Alabama's Representative <a href="/wiki/Mo_Brooks" title="Mo Brooks">Mo Brooks</a> claimed that sea level rise is caused not by melting glaciers but rather by coastal erosion and silt that flows from rivers into the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change denial literature often features the suggestion that we should wait for better technologies before addressing climate change, when they will be more affordable and effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracy_theories">Conspiracy theories</span></h3>
<p>Global warming conspiracy theories have been posited which allege that the scientific consensus is illusory, or that climatologists are acting on their own financial interests by causing undue alarm about a changing climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plot_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-plot-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> Despite leaked emails during the <a href="/wiki/Climatic_Research_Unit_email_controversy" title="Climatic Research Unit email controversy">Climatic Research Unit email controversy</a>, as well as multinational, independent research on the topic, no evidence of such a conspiracy has been presented, and strong consensus exists among scientists from a multitude of political, social, organizational and national backgrounds about the extent and cause of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6committees_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-6committees-122">[122]</a></sup> Several researchers have concluded that around 97% of climate scientists agree with this consensus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> As well, much of the data used in climate science is publicly available to be viewed and interpreted by competing researchers as well as the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, research by <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Lewandowsky" title="Stephan Lewandowsky">Stephan Lewandowsky</a> (then of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Australia" title="University of Western Australia">University of Western Australia</a>) concluded that belief in other conspiracy theories, such as that the FBI was responsible for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr._assassination#Allegations_of_conspiracy" title="Martin Luther King, Jr. assassination">assassination of Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>, was associated with being more likely to endorse climate change denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_of_climate_change_denial">Taxonomy of climate change denial</span></h3>
<p>In 2004 <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Rahmstorf" title="Stefan Rahmstorf">Stefan Rahmstorf</a> described how the media give the misleading impression that climate change was still disputed within the scientific community, attributing this impression to PR efforts of climate change skeptics. He identified different positions argued by climate skeptics, which he used as a <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(general)" title="Taxonomy (general)">taxonomy</a> of climate change skepticism:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rahmstorf_2004_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rahmstorf_2004-126">[126]</a></sup> Later the model was also applied on denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote">
<ol><li>Trend sceptics/deniers (who deny there is global warming), [and] argue that no significant climate warming is taking place at all, claiming that the warming trend measured by weather stations is an artefact due to urbanisation around those stations ("<a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect").</li>
<li>Attribution sceptics/deniers (who accept the global warming trend but see natural causes for this), [and] doubt that human activities are responsible for the observed trends. A few of them even deny that the rise in the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> content is anthropogenic [while others argue that] additional CO<sub>2</sub> does not lead to discernible warming [and] that there must be other—natural—causes for warming.</li>
<li>Impact sceptics/deniers (who think global warming is harmless or even beneficial). <div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rahmstorf_2004_126-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rahmstorf_2004-126">[126]</a></sup></cite></div></li></ol>
</blockquote>
<p>This taxonomy has been used in <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> for analysis of publications, and to categorize climate change skepticism and climate change denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlap_Jacques_2013_p._702_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlap_Jacques_2013_p._702-128">[128]</a></sup> Sometimes, a fourth category called "consensus denial" is added, which describes people who question the scientific consensus on anthropogenic global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Center for Science Education describes climate change denial as disputing differing points in the scientific consensus, a sequential range of arguments from denying the occurrence of climate change, accepting that but denying any significant human contribution, accepting these but denying scientific findings on how this would affect nature and human society, to accepting all these but denying that humans can mitigate or reduce the problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncse-pillars_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ncse-pillars-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_L._Powell" title="James L. Powell">James L. Powell</a> provides a more extended list,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell2012_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Powell2012-4">[4]</a></sup> as does climatologist <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Mann" title="Michael E. Mann">Michael E. Mann</a> in "six stages of denial", a ladder in which deniers have over time conceded acceptance of points, while retreating to a position which still rejects the mainstream consensus:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann2013p23_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mann2013p23-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote">
<ol><li>CO<sub>2</sub> is not actually increasing.</li>
<li>Even if it is, the increase has no impact on the climate since there is no convincing evidence of warming.</li>
<li>Even if there is warming, it is due to natural causes.</li>
<li>Even if the warming cannot be explained by natural causes, the human impact is small, and the impact of continued greenhouse gas emissions will be minor.</li>
<li>Even if the current and future projected human effects on Earth's climate are not negligible, the changes are generally going to be good for us.</li>
<li>Whether or not the changes are going to be good for us, humans are very adept at adapting to changes; besides, it's too late to do anything about it, and/or a technological fix is bound to come along when we really need it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann2013p23_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mann2013p23-129">[129]</a></sup></li></ol>
</blockquote>
<p>Journalists and newspaper columnists including <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_ind_guardian_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-denial_ind_guardian-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodman_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-goodman-131">[131]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-semantics_guardian_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-semantics_guardian-132">[132]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Goodman" title="Ellen Goodman">Ellen Goodman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodman_131-1"><a href="#cite_note-goodman-131">[131]</a></sup> among others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christoff_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christoff-133">[133]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConnellyHarm_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConnellyHarm-134">[134]</a></sup> have described climate change denial as a form of <a href="/wiki/Denialism" title="Denialism">denialism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewsweekTimeline_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewsweekTimeline-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Denialism" title="Denialism">Denialism</a> in this context has been defined by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hoofnagle" title="Chris Hoofnagle">Chris and</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Hoofnagle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mark Hoofnagle (page does not exist)">Mark Hoofnagle</a> as the use of <a href="/wiki/Rhetorical_device" title="Rhetorical device">rhetorical devices</a> "to give the appearance of legitimate debate where there is none, an approach that has the ultimate goal of rejecting a proposition on which a scientific consensus exists." This process characteristically uses one or more of the following tactics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deithelm_McKie_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deithelm_McKie-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark_Hoofnagle_Graun09_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark_Hoofnagle_Graun09-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Allegations that scientific consensus involves conspiring to fake data or suppress the truth: a global warming conspiracy theory.</li>
<li>Fake experts, or individuals with views at odds with established knowledge, at the same time marginalising or denigrating published topic experts. Like the <a href="/wiki/Doubt_Is_Their_Product" title="Doubt Is Their Product">manufactured doubt over smoking and health</a>, a few contrarian scientists oppose the climate consensus, some of them <a href="/wiki/Merchants_of_Doubt" title="Merchants of Doubt">the same individuals</a>.</li>
<li>Selectivity, such as <a href="/wiki/Cherry_picking" title="Cherry picking">cherry picking</a> atypical or even obsolete papers, in the same way that the <a href="/wiki/MMR_vaccine_controversy" title="MMR vaccine controversy">MMR vaccine controversy</a> was based on one paper: examples include discredited ideas of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_warm_period" title="Medieval warm period">medieval warm period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark_Hoofnagle_Graun09_137-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mark_Hoofnagle_Graun09-137">[137]</a></sup></li>
<li>Unworkable demands of research, claiming that any uncertainty invalidates the field or exaggerating  uncertainty while rejecting probabilities and mathematical models.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Formal_fallacy" title="Formal fallacy">Logical fallacies</a>.</li></ol>
<p>In 2015, environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a> accused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_Obama" title="President Obama">President Obama</a> (widely regarded as strongly in favour of action on climate change<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>) of "Catastrophic Climate-Change Denial", for his approval of oil-drilling permits in offshore Alaska. According to McKibben, the President has also "opened huge swaths of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powder_River_basin" title="Powder River basin">Powder River basin</a> to new coal mining." McKibben calls this "climate denial of the status quo sort", where the President denies "the meaning of the science, which is that we must keep carbon in the ground." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudoscience">Pseudoscience</span></h2>
<p>Various groups, including the National Center for Science Education, have described climate change denial as a form of <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE-140">[140]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lahsen_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lahsen-141">[141]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownM_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownM-142">[142]</a></sup> Climate change skepticism, while in some cases professing to do research on climate change, has focused instead on influencing the opinion of the public, legislators and the media, in contrast to legitimate science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a review of the book <i>The Pseudoscience Wars: Immanuel Velikovsky and the Birth of the Modern Fringe</i> by Michael D. Gordin, David Morrison wrote:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In his final chapter, Gordin turns to the new phase of pseudoscience, practiced by a few rogue scientists themselves. Climate change denialism is the prime example, where a handful of scientists, allied with an effective PR machine, are publicly challenging the scientific consensus that global warming is real and is due primarily to human consumption of fossil fuels. Scientists have watched in disbelief that as the evidence for global warming has become ever more solid, the deniers have been increasingly successful in the public and political arena. … Today pseudoscience is still with us, and is as dangerous a challenge to science as it ever was in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrison_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morrison-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_opinion">Public opinion</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Public opinion on climate change">Public opinion on climate change</a></div>
<p>Public opinion on climate change is significantly impacted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_coverage_of_climate_change" title="Media coverage of climate change">media coverage of climate change</a>, and the effects of climate change denial campaigns. Campaigns to undermine public confidence in climate science have decreased public belief in climate change, which in turn have impacted legislative efforts to curb CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disinformation_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-disinformation-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>The popular media in the U.S. gives greater attention to climate change skeptics than the scientific community as a whole, and the level of agreement within the scientific community has not been accurately communicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> In some cases, news outlets have allowed climate change skeptics to explain the science of climate change instead of experts in climatology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> US and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> media coverage differ from that presented in other countries, where reporting is more consistent with the scientific literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> Some journalists attribute the difference to climate change denial being propagated, mainly in the US, by business-centered organizations employing tactics worked out previously by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_lobby" title="Tobacco lobby">US tobacco lobby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-approach_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-approach-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the US and the UK, the opinions of climate change skeptics appear much more frequently in conservative news outlets than other news, and in many cases those opinions are left uncontested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p><p>The efforts of <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> and other environmental campaigns have focused on the effects of global warming and have managed to increase awareness and concern, but despite these efforts, the number of Americans believing humans are the cause of global warming was holding steady at 61% in 2007, and those believing the popular media was understating the issue remained about 35%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad2007HollywoodHeathUpConcern_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad2007HollywoodHeathUpConcern-155">[155]</a></sup> A recent poll from 2015 suggests that while Americans are growing more aware of the dangers and implications of climate change for future generations, the majority are not worried about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> From a survey conducted in 2004, it was found that more than 30% of news presented in the previous decade showed equal attention to both human and non human contributions to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study assessed the public perception and actions to climate change, on grounds of belief systems, and identified seven psychological barriers affecting the behavior that otherwise would facilitate mitigation, adaptation, and environmental stewardship. The author found the following barriers: cognition, ideological world views, comparisons to key people, costs and momentum, discredence toward experts and authorities, perceived risks of change, and inadequate behavioral changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalism">Nationalism</span></h2>
<p>It has been suggested that climate change can conflict with a <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalistic</a> view because it is "unsolvable" at the national level and requires collective action between nations or between local communities, and that therefore populist nationalism tends to reject the science of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup>
</p><p>
In a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TED_talk" title="TED talk">TED talk</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuval_Noah_Harari" title="Yuval Noah Harari">Yuval Noah Harari</a> notes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> </p><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...nationalism has no solution to climate change. If you want to be a nationalist in the 21st century, you have to deny the problem. If you accept the reality of the problem, then you must accept that, yes, there is still room in the world for patriotism, there is still room in the world for having special loyalties and obligations towards your own people, towards your own country. I don't think anybody is really thinking of abolishing that. But in order to confront climate change, we need additional loyalties and commitments to a level beyond the nation.
</p></blockquote>
<p>On the other hand, it has been argued that effective climate action is polycentric rather than international, and national interest in multilateral groups can be furthered by overcoming climate change denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup>  Climate change contrarians may believe in a "caricature" of internationalist state intervention that is perceived as threatening national sovereignty, and may re-attribute risks such as flooding to international institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a> policy on climate change has been influenced by noted contrarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christopher_Monckton" title="Christopher Monckton">Christopher Monckton</a> and then by its energy spokesman <a href="/wiki/Roger_Helmer" title="Roger Helmer">Roger Helmer</a> MEP who stated in a speech "It is not clear that the rise in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> is anthropogenic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying">Lobbying<span id="Tobacco_lobby"></span></span></h2>
<p>Efforts to lobby against environmental regulation have included campaigns to manufacture doubt about the science behind climate change, and to obscure the scientific consensus and data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup> These efforts have undermined public confidence in climate science, and impacted climate change lobbying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freeman351_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-freeman351-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disinformation_145-1"><a href="#cite_note-disinformation-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>The political advocacy organizations <a href="/wiki/FreedomWorks" title="FreedomWorks">FreedomWorks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Americans_for_Prosperity" title="Americans for Prosperity">Americans for Prosperity</a>, funded by <a href="/wiki/Political_activities_of_the_Koch_brothers" title="Political activities of the Koch brothers">brothers David and Charles Koch</a> of <a href="/wiki/Koch_Industries" title="Koch Industries">Koch Industries</a>, were important in supporting the <a href="/wiki/Tea_Party_movement" title="Tea Party movement">Tea Party movement</a> and in encouraging the movement to focus on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup> Other conservative organizations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heritage_Foundation" title="Heritage Foundation">Heritage Foundation</a>, Marshall Institute, <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a> were significant participants in these lobbying attempts, seeking to halt or eliminate environmental regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>This approach to downplay the significance of climate change was copied from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_lobbyists" title="Tobacco lobbyists">tobacco lobbyists</a>; in the face of scientific evidence linking tobacco to <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, to prevent or delay the introduction of regulation. Lobbyists attempted to <a href="/wiki/Discrediting_tactic" title="Discrediting tactic">discredit</a> the scientific research by creating doubt and manipulating debate. They worked to discredit the scientists involved, to dispute their findings, and to create and maintain an apparent controversy by promoting claims that contradicted scientific research. ""Doubt is our product," boasted a now infamous 1969 industry memo. Doubt would shield the tobacco industry from litigation and regulation for decades to come."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup> In 2006, <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> about similarities between the methods of groups funded by <a href="/wiki/Exxon" title="Exxon">Exxon</a>, and those of the tobacco giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altria_Group" title="Altria Group">Philip Morris</a>, including direct attacks on <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed</a> science, and attempts to <a href="/wiki/Manufactured_controversy" title="Manufactured controversy">create public controversy</a> and doubt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_ind_guardian_130-1"><a href="#cite_note-denial_ind_guardian-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p>Former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> president <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Seitz" title="Frederick Seitz">Frederick Seitz</a>, who, according to an article by Mark Hertsgaard in <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, earned about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US$" title="US$">US$</a>585,000 in the 1970s and 1980s as a consultant to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.J._Reynolds_Tobacco_Company" title="R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Company">R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Company</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HertsgaardSlept_168-0"><a href="#cite_note-HertsgaardSlept-168">[168]</a></sup> went on to chair groups such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_and_Environmental_Policy_Project" title="Science and Environmental Policy Project">Science and Environmental Policy Project</a> and the George C. Marshall Institute alleged to have made efforts to "downplay" global warming. Seitz stated in the 1980s that "Global warming is far more a matter of politics than of climate." Seitz authored the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Petition" title="Oregon Petition">Oregon Petition</a>, a document published jointly by the Marshall Institute and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oregon_Institute_of_Science_and_Medicine" title="Oregon Institute of Science and Medicine">Oregon Institute of Science and Medicine</a> in opposition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a>. The petition and accompanying "Research Review of Global Warming Evidence" claimed:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The proposed limits on greenhouse gases would harm the environment, hinder the advance of science and technology, and damage the health and welfare of mankind. There is no convincing scientific evidence that human release of carbon dioxide, methane, or other greenhouse gases is causing or will, in the foreseeable future, cause catastrophic heating of the Earth's atmosphere and disruption of the Earth's climate. … We are living in an increasingly lush environment of plants and animals as a result of the carbon dioxide increase. Our children will enjoy an Earth with far more plant and animal life than that with which we now are blessed. This is a wonderful and unexpected gift from the Industrial Revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_ind_guardian_130-2"><a href="#cite_note-denial_ind_guardian-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>George Monbiot wrote in <i>The Guardian</i> that this petition, which he criticizes as misleading and tied to industry funding, "has been cited by almost every journalist who claims that climate change is a myth." Efforts by climate change denial groups played a significant role in the eventual rejection of the Kyoto protocol in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup>
</p><p>Monbiot has written about another group founded by the tobacco lobby, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Advancement_of_Sound_Science_Coalition" title="The Advancement of Sound Science Coalition">The Advancement of Sound Science Coalition</a> (TASSC), that now campaigns against measures to combat global warming. In again trying to <a href="/wiki/Astroturfing" title="Astroturfing">manufacture the appearance of a grass-roots movement</a> against "unfounded fear" and "over-regulation," Monbiot states that TASSC "has done more damage to the campaign to halt [climate change] than any other body."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denial_ind_guardian_130-3"><a href="#cite_note-denial_ind_guardian-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Drexel_University" title="Drexel University">Drexel University</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sociology" title="Environmental sociology">environmental sociologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Brulle" title="Robert Brulle">Robert Brulle</a> analysed the funding of 91 organizations opposed to restrictions on carbon emissions, which he termed the "climate change counter-movement." Between 2003 and 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Donor-advised_fund" title="Donor-advised fund">donor-advised funds</a> <a href="/wiki/Donors_Trust" title="Donors Trust">Donors Trust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donors_Capital_Fund" title="Donors Capital Fund">Donors Capital Fund</a>, combined, were the largest funders, accounting for about one quarter of the total funds, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a> was the largest recipient, 16% of the total funds. The study also found that the amount of money donated to these organizations by means of  foundations whose funding sources cannot be traced had risen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brulle2013_170-0"><a href="#cite_note-brulle2013-170">[170]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian20131220_171-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian20131220-171">[171]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciam_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciam-172">[172]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian20130214_173-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian20130214-173">[173]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frontline20121-23_174-0"><a href="#cite_note-frontline20121-23-174">[174]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_sector">Private sector</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Business_action_on_climate_change" title="Business action on climate change">Business action on climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil_climate_change_controversy" title="ExxonMobil climate change controversy">ExxonMobil climate change controversy</a></div>
<p>Several large corporations within the fossil fuel industry provide significant funding for attempts to mislead the public about the trustworthiness of climate science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Koch_family_foundations" title="Koch family foundations">Koch family foundations</a> have been identified as especially influential funders of climate change contrarianism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the IPCC released its <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">February 2007 report</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a> offered British, American and other scientists $10,000 plus travel expenses to publish articles critical of the assessment. The institute had received more than US$1.6 million from Exxon, and its vice-chairman of trustees was former head of Exxon <a href="/wiki/Lee_Raymond" title="Lee Raymond">Lee Raymond</a>. Raymond sent letters that alleged the IPCC report was not "supported by the analytical work." More than 20 AEI employees worked as consultants to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration" title="George W. Bush administration">George W. Bush administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup> Despite her initial conviction that climate change denial would abate with time, Senator <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Boxer" title="Barbara Boxer">Barbara Boxer</a> said that when she learned of the AEI's offer, she "realized there was a movement behind this that just wasn't giving up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_178-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> conducted a survey that found ExxonMobil had given US$2.9 million to American groups that "misinformed the public about climate change," 39 of which "misrepresented the science of climate change by outright denial of the evidence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G2_179-0"><a href="#cite_note-G2-179">[179]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> In 2006, the Royal Society issued a demand that ExxonMobil withdraw funding for climate change denial. The letter drew criticism, notably from <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Ball" title="Timothy Ball">Timothy Ball</a> who argued the society attempted to "politicize the private funding of science and to censor scientific debate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup>
</p><p>ExxonMobil denied that it has been trying to mislead the public about global warming. A spokesman, Gantt Walton, said that ExxonMobil's funding of research does not mean that it acts to influence the research, and that ExxonMobil supports taking action to curb the output of greenhouse gasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup> Research conducted at an Exxon archival collection at the University of Texas and interviews with former employees by journalists indicate the scientific opinion within the company and their public posture towards climate change was contradictory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1989 and 2002 the <a href="/wiki/Global_Climate_Coalition" title="Global Climate Coalition">Global Climate Coalition</a>, a group of mainly United States businesses, used aggressive lobbying and public relations tactics to oppose action to reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions and fight the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>. The coalition was financed by large corporations and trade groups from the oil, coal and auto industries. <i>The New York Times</i> reported that "even as the coalition worked to sway opinion [towards skepticism], its own scientific and technical experts were advising that the science backing the role of greenhouse gases in global warming could not be refuted."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup> In 2000, Ford Motor Company was the first company to leave the coalition as a result of pressure from environmentalists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup> followed by Daimler-Chrysler, Texaco, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a> and General Motors subsequently left to GCC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> The organization closed in 2002.
</p><p>From January 2009 through June 2010, the oil, coal and utility industries spent $500 million in lobby expenditures in opposition to legislation to address climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20101020_187-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20101020-187">[187]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cap20100927_188-0"><a href="#cite_note-cap20100927-188">[188]</a></sup>
</p><p>In early 2015, several media reports emerged saying that <a href="/wiki/Willie_Soon" title="Willie Soon">Willie Soon</a>, a popular scientist among climate change deniers, had failed to disclose conflicts of interest in at least 11 scientific papers published since 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup> They reported that he received a total of $1.25m from ExxonMobil, Southern Company, the American Petroleum Institute and a foundation run by the Koch brothers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-190"><a href="#cite_note-190">[190]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Alcock" title="Charles R. Alcock">Charles R. Alcock</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics" title="Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics">Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics</a>, where Soon was based, said that allowing funders of Soon's work to prohibit disclosure of funding sources was a mistake, which will not be permitted in future grant agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_sector">Public sector</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party in the United States</a> is unique in denying <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">anthropogenic climate change</a> among conservative political parties across the Western world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> In 1994, according to a leaked memo, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican</a> strategist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Luntz" title="Frank Luntz">Frank Luntz</a> advised members of the Republican Party, with regard to climate change, that "you need to continue to make the lack of scientific certainty a primary issue" and "challenge the science" by "recruiting experts who are sympathetic to your view."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_178-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-178">[178]</a></sup> (In 2006, Luntz said he still believes "back [in] '97, '98, the science was uncertain", but he now agrees with the scientific consensus.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup> From 2008 to 2017, the Republican Party went from "debating how to combat human-caused climate change to arguing that it does not exist," according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup> In 2011 "more than half of the Republicans in the House and three-quarters of Republican senators" said "that the threat of global warming, as a man-made and highly threatening phenomenon, is at best an exaggeration and at worst an utter "hoax"" according to <a href="/wiki/Judith_Warner" title="Judith Warner">Judith Warner</a> writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20110227_196-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20110227-196">[196]</a></sup>
In 2014, more than 55% of congressional Republicans were climate change deniers, according to <a href="/wiki/NBC_News" title="NBC News">NBC News</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc20140512_197-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc20140512-197">[197]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup>
According to <a href="/wiki/PolitiFact" title="PolitiFact">PolitiFact</a> in May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a>'s statement that 'virtually no Republican' in Washington accepts climate change science, was "mostly true"; PolitiFact counted "eight out of 278, or about 3 percent" of Republican members of Congress who "accept the prevailing scientific conclusion that global warming is both real and man-made."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported that <a href="/wiki/Philip_Cooney" title="Philip Cooney">Philip Cooney</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels_lobby" title="Fossil fuels lobby">fossil fuel lobbyist</a> and "climate team leader" at the American Petroleum Institute and President George W. Bush's chief of staff of the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="Council on Environmental Quality">Council on Environmental Quality</a>, had "repeatedly edited government climate reports in ways that play down links between such emissions and global warming, according to internal documents."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_201-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-201">[201]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Begley" title="Sharon Begley">Sharon Begley</a> reported in <i>Newsweek</i> that Cooney "edited a 2002 report on climate science by sprinkling it with phrases such as 'lack of understanding' and 'considerable uncertainty.'" Cooney reportedly removed an entire section on climate in one report, whereupon another lobbyist sent him a fax saying "You are doing a great job."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_178-2"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-178">[178]</a></sup> Cooney announced his resignation two days after the story of his tampering with scientific reports broke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-202"><a href="#cite_note-202">[202]</a></sup> but a few days later it was announced that Cooney would take up a position with ExxonMobil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Secretary of Energy">United States Secretary of Energy</a> <a href="/wiki/Rick_Perry" title="Rick Perry">Rick Perry</a>, in a 19 June 2017 interview with CNBC, acknowledged the existence of climate change and impact from humans, but said that he did not agree with the idea that carbon dioxide was the primary driver of global warming pointing instead to "the ocean waters and this environment that we live in".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-204"><a href="#cite_note-204">[204]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> responded in a letter to Perry saying that it is "critically important that you understand that emissions of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases are the primary cause", pointing to conclusions of scientists worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-205"><a href="#cite_note-205">[205]</a></sup>
</p><p>Republican <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bridenstine" title="Jim Bridenstine">Jim Bridenstine</a>, the first elected politician to serve as NASA administrator, had previously stated that global temperatures were not rising. A month after the Senate confirmed his NASA position in April 2018, he acknowledged that human emissions of greenhouse gases are raising global temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span></h3>
<p>According to documents leaked in February 2012, <a href="/wiki/The_Heartland_Institute" title="The Heartland Institute">The Heartland Institute</a> is developing a <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curriculum</a> for use in schools which frames climate change as a scientific controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTHeartland_207-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTHeartland-207">[207]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-208"><a href="#cite_note-208">[208]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-209"><a href="#cite_note-209">[209]</a></sup> In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Branch" title="Glenn Branch">Glenn Branch</a>, Deputy Director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Science_Education" title="National Center for Science Education">National Center for Science Education </a>(NCSE), wrote that "the Heartland Institute is continuing to inflict its climate change denial literature on science teachers across the country". He also described how some science teachers were reacting to Heartland's mailings: "Fortunately, the Heartland mailing continues to be greeted with skepticism and dismissed with scorn."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_Branch_6/5/2017_210-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_Branch_6/5/2017-210">[210]</a></sup> The NCSE has prepared Classroom Resources in response to Heartland and other anti-science threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_Classroom_2017_211-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_Classroom_2017-211">[211]</a></sup>
</p><p>Branch also referred to an article by ClimateFeedback.org<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCSE_Branch_6/5/2017_210-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCSE_Branch_6/5/2017-210">[210]</a></sup> which reviewed an unsolicited Heartland booklet, entitled "Why Scientists Disagree about Global Warming", which was sent to science teachers in the United States. Their intention was to send it to "more than 200,000 K-12 teachers". Each significant claim was rated for accuracy by scientists who were experts on that topic. Overall, they scored the accuracy of the booklet with an "F": "it could hardly score lower", and "the "Key Findings" section are incorrect, misleading, based on flawed logic, or simply factually inaccurate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_Feedback_2017_212-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_Feedback_2017-212">[212]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect">Effect</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Manufactured_controversy" title="Manufactured controversy">Manufactured uncertainty</a> over climate change, the fundamental strategy of climate change denial, has been very effective, particularly in the US. It has contributed to low levels of public concern and to government inaction worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-public-success_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-public-success-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-213"><a href="#cite_note-213">[213]</a></sup> An Angus Reid poll released in 2010 indicates that global warming skepticism in the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom has been rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorcoranCoolDown_214-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorcoranCoolDown-214">[214]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-215"><a href="#cite_note-215">[215]</a></sup> There may be multiple causes of this trend, including a focus on economic rather than environmental issues, and a negative perception of the United Nations and its role in discussing climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RasmussenPoll_216-0"><a href="#cite_note-RasmussenPoll-216">[216]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another cause may be weariness from overexposure to the topic: secondary polls suggest that the public may have been discouraged by extremism when discussing the topic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorcoranCoolDown_214-1"><a href="#cite_note-CorcoranCoolDown-214">[214]</a></sup> while other polls show 54% of U.S. voters believe that "the news media make global warming appear worse than it really is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RasmussenMediaHype_217-0"><a href="#cite_note-RasmussenMediaHype-217">[217]</a></sup> A poll in 2009 regarding the issue of whether "some scientists have falsified research data to support their own theories and beliefs about global warming" showed that 59% of Americans believed it "at least somewhat likely", with 35% believing it was "very likely".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RasmussenPoll_216-1"><a href="#cite_note-RasmussenPoll-216">[216]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Tim_Wirth" title="Tim Wirth">Tim Wirth</a>, "They patterned what they did after the tobacco industry. […] Both figured, sow enough doubt, call the science uncertain and in dispute. That's had a huge impact on both the public and Congress."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobacco-approach_73-3"><a href="#cite_note-tobacco-approach-73">[73]</a></sup> This approach has been propagated by the US media, presenting a false balance between climate science and climate skeptics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-218"><a href="#cite_note-218">[218]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> reports that the majority of Europe and Japan accept the consensus on scientific climate change, but only one third of Americans considered human activity to play a major role in climate change in 2006; 64% believed that scientists disagreed about it "a lot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-219"><a href="#cite_note-219">[219]</a></sup> A 2007 <i>Newsweek</i> poll found these numbers were declining, although majorities of Americans still believed that scientists were uncertain about climate change and its causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-220"><a href="#cite_note-220">[220]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rush_D._Holt,_Jr." title="Rush D. Holt, Jr.">Rush Holt</a> wrote a piece for <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>, which appeared in <i>Newsweek</i>:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>… for more than two decades scientists have been issuing warnings that the release of greenhouse gases, principally carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), is probably altering Earth's climate in ways that will be expensive and even deadly. The American public yawned and bought bigger cars. Statements by the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, and others underscored the warnings and called for new government policies to deal with climate change. Politicians, presented with noisy statistics, shrugged, said there is too much doubt among scientists, and did nothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-221"><a href="#cite_note-221">[221]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Deliberate attempts by the <a href="/wiki/Western_Fuels_Association" title="Western Fuels Association">Western Fuels Association</a> "to confuse the public" have succeeded in their objectives. This has been "exacerbated by media treatment of the climate issue". According to a Pew poll in 2012, 57% of the US public are unaware of, or outright reject, the scientific consensus on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_222-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-222">[222]</a></sup> Some organizations promoting climate change denial have asserted that scientists are increasingly rejecting climate change, but this notion is contradicted by <a href="/wiki/Surveys_of_scientists%27_views_on_climate_change#John_Cook_et_al.,_2013" title="Surveys of scientists' views on climate change">research showing that 97%</a> of published papers endorse the scientific consensus, and that percentage is increasing with time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_222-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-222">[222]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social psychologist Craig Foster compares climate change denialists to flat-earth believers and the reaction to the latter by the scientific community. Foster states, "the potential and kinetic energy devoted to counter the flat-earth movement is wasteful and misguided... I don't understand why anybody would worry about the flat-earth gnat while facing the climate change mammoth... Climate change denial does not require belief. It only requires neglect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foster_223-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foster-223">[223]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, Aaron McCright argued that <a href="/wiki/Anti-environmentalism" title="Anti-environmentalism">anti-environmentalism</a>—and climate change denial specifically—has expanded to a point in the US where it has now become "a central tenet of the current <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> identity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-224"><a href="#cite_note-224">[224]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the other hand, global oil companies have begun to acknowledge the existence of climate change and its risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-225"><a href="#cite_note-225">[225]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manufactured climate change denial is also influencing how scientific knowledge is communicated to the public.  According to climate scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Mann" title="Michael E. Mann">Michael E. Mann</a>, "...universities and scientific societies and organizations, publishers, etc.—are too often risk averse when it comes to defending and communicating science that is perceived as threatening by powerful interests..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-226"><a href="#cite_note-226">[226]</a></sup>
</p>
