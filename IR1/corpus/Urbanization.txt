<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Urbanization</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Urbanization</b> refers to the population shift from rural to urban residency, the gradual increase in the proportion of people living in urban areas, and the ways in which each society adapts to this change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is predominantly the process by which towns and cities are formed and become larger as more people begin living and working in central areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although the two concepts are sometimes used interchangeably, urbanization should be distinguished from urban growth: urbanization is "the proportion of the total national population living in areas classed as urban," while urban growth refers to "the absolute number of people living in areas classed as urban".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> projected that half of the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world's population</a> would live in urban areas at the end of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is predicted that by 2050 about 64% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">developing world</a> and 86% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">developed world</a> will be urbanized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> That is equivalent to approximately 3 billion urbanites by 2050, much of which will occur in Africa and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Notably, the United Nations has also recently projected that nearly all global population growth from 2017 to 2030 will be absorbed by cities, about 1.1 billion new urbanites over the next 13 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urbanization is relevant to a range of disciplines, including <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>. The phenomenon has been closely linked to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernization" title="Modernization">modernization</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, and the sociological process of <a href="/wiki/Rationalization_(sociology)" title="Rationalization (sociology)">rationalization</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>. Urbanization can be seen as a specific condition at a set time (e.g. the proportion of total population or area in cities or towns) or as an increase in that condition over time. So urbanization can be quantified either in terms of, say, the level of urban development relative to the overall population, or as the <a href="/wiki/Rate_(mathematics)" title="Rate (mathematics)">rate</a> at which the urban proportion of the population is increasing. Urbanization creates enormous social, economic and environmental changes, which provide an opportunity for sustainability with the “potential to use resources more efficiently, to create more sustainable land use and to protect the biodiversity of natural ecosystems.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urbanization is not merely a modern phenomenon, but a rapid and historic transformation of human <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social roots</a> on a global scale, whereby predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_culture" title="Rural culture">rural culture</a> is being rapidly replaced by predominantly <a href="/wiki/Urban_culture" title="Urban culture">urban culture</a>. The first major change in settlement patterns was the accumulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a> into villages many thousand years ago. Village culture is characterized by common bloodlines, intimate relationships, and communal behavior, whereas urban culture is characterized by distant bloodlines, unfamiliar relations, and competitive behavior. This unprecedented movement of people is forecast to continue and intensify during the next few decades, mushrooming cities to sizes unthinkable only a century ago. As a result, the world urban population growth curve has up till recently followed a quadratic-hyperbolic pattern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, in Asia the urban agglomerations of <a href="/wiki/Keihanshin" title="Keihanshin">Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> are each already home to over 20 million people, while <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> are forecast to approach or exceed 40 million people each within the coming decade. Outside Asia, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> are, or soon will be, home to over 10 million people each.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the development of the earliest cities in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> until the 18th century, an equilibrium existed between the vast majority of the population who engaged in <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence agriculture</a> in a rural context, and small centres of populations in the towns where economic activity consisted primarily of trade at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_(place)" title="Market (place)">markets</a> and manufactures on a small scale. Due to the primitive and relatively stagnant state of agriculture throughout this period, the ratio of rural to urban population remained at a fixed equilibrium. However, a significant increase in the percentage of the global urban population can be traced in the 1st millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Another significant increase can be traced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mughal_India" title="Mughal India">Mughal India</a>, where 15% of its population lived in urban centers during the 16th–17th centuries, higher than in Europe at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eraly_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-eraly-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-habib170_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-habib170-13">[13]</a></sup> In comparison, the percentage of the European population living in cities was 8–13% in 1800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the onset of the <a href="/wiki/British_Agricultural_Revolution" title="British Agricultural Revolution">British agricultural</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> in the late 18th century, this relationship was finally broken and an unprecedented growth in urban population took place over the course of the 19th century, both through continued migration from the countryside and due to the tremendous <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic expansion</a> that occurred at that time. In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, the proportion of the population living in cities with more than 20,000 people jumped from 17% in 1801 to 54% in 1891. Moreover, and adopting a broader definition of urbanization, we can say that while the urbaized population in England and Wales represented 72% of the total in 1891, for other countries the figure was 37% in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, 41% in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> and 28% in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urbanization_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-urbanization-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1037" data-file-width="1426" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png/400px-Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png/600px-Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png/800px-Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Historical_global_urban_-_rural_population_trends.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical global urban/rural population trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>As labourers were freed up from working the land due to higher agricultural productivity they converged on the new industrial cities like <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> which were experiencing a boom in commerce, trade and industry. Growing trade around the world also allowed cereals to be imported from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunedin_(ship)" title="Dunedin (ship)">refrigerated meat</a> from <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Spatially, <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">cities also expanded</a> due to the development of <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport systems</a>, which facilitated commutes of longer distances to the city centre for the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>.
</p><p>Urbanization rapidly spread across the Western world and, since the 1950s, it has begun to take hold in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">developing world</a> as well. At the turn of the 20th century, just 15% of the world population lived in cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a>, the year 2007 witnessed the turning point when more than 50% of the world population were living in cities, for the first time in human history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urbanization_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-urbanization-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in June 2016 published urbanization data from the time period 3700 BC to 2000 AD, the data was used to make a <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> showing the development of cities on the <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">world</a> during the time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rural_flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1534" data-file-width="1742" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Rural_flight.jpg/250px-Rural_flight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Rural_flight.jpg/375px-Rural_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Rural_flight.jpg/500px-Rural_flight.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rural_flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Population age comparises between rural <a href="/wiki/Pocahontas_County,_Iowa" title="Pocahontas County, Iowa">Pocahontas County, Iowa</a> and urban <a href="/wiki/Johnson_County,_Iowa" title="Johnson County, Iowa">Johnson County, Iowa</a>, illustrating the flight of young adults (red) to urban centres in <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg/220px-Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg/330px-Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg/440px-Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Downtown_Aerial_View.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The City of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a> is an example of the early American grid system of development. The grid is enforced even on uneven topography.</div></div></div>
<p>Urbanization occurs either organically or planned as a result of individual, collective and state action. Living in a city can be culturally and economically beneficial since it can provide greater opportunities for access to the labor market, reduce the time and expense of commuting and transportation better education, housing and safety conditions. Condition like density, proximity, diversity, and marketplace competition are elements of an urban environment that deemed positive. However, there are also negative social phenomena that arise, alienation, stress, increased cost of living, and mass marginalization that are connected to an urban way of living. <a href="/wiki/Suburbanization" title="Suburbanization">Suburbanization</a>, which is happening in the cities of the largest developing countries, may be regarded as an attempt to balance these negative aspects of urban life while still allowing access to the large extent of shared resources.
</p><p>In cities, money, services, wealth and opportunities are centralized. Many rural inhabitants come to the city to seek their fortune and alter their social position. Businesses, which provide jobs and exchange capital, are more concentrated in urban areas. Whether the source is trade or tourism, it is also through the ports or banking systems, commonly located in cities, that foreign money flows into a country.
</p><p>Many people move into cities for the economic opportunities, but this does not fully explain the very high recent urbanization rates in places like China and India. <a href="/wiki/Rural_flight" title="Rural flight">Rural flight</a> is a contributing factor to urbanization. In rural areas, often on small family farms or collective farms in villages, it has historically been difficult to access manufactured goods, though the relative overall <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> is very subjective, and may certainly surpass that of the city. Farm living has always been susceptible to unpredictable environmental conditions, and in times of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> or pestilence, survival may become extremely problematic.
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25em; ; background-color: #B0C4DE;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><div style="text-align:left;">Thai farmers are seen as poor, stupid, and unhealthy. As young people flee the farms, the values and knowledge of rice farming and the countryside are fading, including the tradition of long kek, helping neighbors plant, harvest, or build a house. We are losing what we call Thai-ness, the values of being kind, helping each other, having mercy and gratefulness. — Iam Thongdee, Professor of Humanities, Mahidol University in Bangkok<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Thai_Farm_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Thai_Farm-22">[22]</a></sup></div></div>
</div>
<p>In a New York Times article concerning the acute migration away from farming in Thailand, life as a farmer was described as "hot and exhausting". "Everyone says the farmer works the hardest but gets the least amount of money". In an effort to counter this impression, the Agriculture Department of Thailand is seeking to promote the impression that farming is "honorable and secure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Thai_Farm_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Thai_Farm-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, in Thailand, urbanization has also resulted in massive increases in problems such as obesity. City life, especially in modern urban slums of the developing world, is certainly hardly immune to pestilence or climatic disturbances such as floods, yet continues to strongly attract migrants. Examples of this were the <a href="/wiki/2011_Thailand_floods" title="2011 Thailand floods">2011 Thailand floods</a> and <a href="/wiki/2007_Jakarta_flood" title="2007 Jakarta flood">2007 Jakarta flood</a>. Urban areas are also far more prone to <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs">drugs</a>, and other urban social problems. In the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_farming" title="Factory farming">industrialization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> has negatively affected the economy of small and middle-sized farms and strongly reduced the size of the rural labour market.
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; ; background-color: #B0C4DE;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote right-aligned" style=""><div style="text-align:left;">These are the costs of participating in the urban economy. Your increased income is canceled out by increased expenditure. In the end, you have even less left for food. — Madhura Swaminathan, economist at Kolkata’s Indian Statistical Institute<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_900_Million_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_900_Million-23">[23]</a></sup></div></div>
</div>
<p>Particularly in the developing world, conflict over land rights due to the effects of <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> has led to less politically powerful groups, such as farmers, losing or forfeiting their land, resulting in obligatory migration into cities. In China, where land acquisition measures are forceful, there has been far more extensive and rapid urbanization (54%) than in India (36%), where peasants form militant groups (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naxalites" title="Naxalites">Naxalites</a>) to oppose such efforts. Obligatory and unplanned migration often results in rapid growth of slums.   
This is also similar to areas of violent conflict, where people are driven off their land due to violence. <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bogotá</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> is one example of this.
</p><p>Cities offer a larger variety of services, including specialist services not found in rural areas. These services requires workers, resulting in more numerous and varied job opportunities. Elderly people may be forced to move to cities where there are doctors and hospitals that can cater for their health needs. Varied and high quality educational opportunities are another factor in urban migration, as well as the opportunity to join, develop, and seek out social communities.
</p><p>Urbanization also creates opportunities for women that are not available in rural areas. This creates a gender-related transformation where women are engaged in paid employment and have access to education. This may cause fertility to decline. However, women are sometimes still at a disadvantage due to their unequal position in the labour market, their inability to secure assets independently from male relatives and exposure to violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFPA_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>People in cities are more productive than in rural areas. An important question is whether this is due to <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_agglomeration" title="Economies of agglomeration">agglomeration effects</a> or whether cities simply attract those who are more productive. Economists have recently shown that there exists a large productivity gain due to locating in dense agglomerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It is thus possible that agents<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> locate in cities in order to benefit from these agglomeration effects.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominant_conurbation">Dominant conurbation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dominant conurbation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Primate_city" title="Primate city">Primate city</a></div>
<p>The dominant <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbation</a>(s) of a country can benefit to a greater extent from the same things cities offer, making them magnets for not just the non-urban population, but also urban and suburban population from other cities. Dominant conurbations are quite often <a href="/wiki/Primate_city" title="Primate city">primate cities</a>, but do not have to be. For instance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Manila" title="Greater Manila">Greater Manila</a> is rather a conurbation than a city: its 20 million overall population (over 20% national population) make it very much a primate city, but Quezon City (2.7 million), the largest municipality in Greater Manila, and Manila (1.6 million), the capital, are not. A conurbation's dominance can be measured by output, wealth, and especially population, each expressed as a percentage of an entire country. Greater Seoul is one conurbation with massive dominance over South Korea, it is home to 50% of the entire national population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though Greater Busan-Ulsan (15%, 8 million) and Greater Osaka (14%, 18 million) exhibit strong dominance in their respective countries, they are losing population to their even more dominant rivals, Seoul and Tokyo respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_effect">Economic effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As cities develop, effects can include a dramatic increase and change in costs, often pricing the local <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> out of the market, including such functionaries as employees of the local municipalities. For example, Eric Hobsbawm's book <i>The age of revolution: 1789–1848</i> (published 1962 and 2005) chapter 11, stated "Urban development in our period [1789–1848] was a gigantic process of class segregation, which pushed the new labouring poor into great morasses of misery outside the centres of government and business and the newly specialized residential areas of the bourgeoisie. The almost universal European division into a 'good' west end and a 'poor' east end of large cities developed in this period." This is likely due the prevailing south-west wind which carries coal smoke and other airborne pollutants downwind, making the western edges of towns preferable to the eastern ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Similar problems now affect the developing world, rising inequality resulting from rapid urbanization trends. The drive for rapid urban growth and often efficiency can lead to less equitable urban development. Think tanks such as the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> have proposed policies that encourage labor-intensive growth as a means of absorbing the influx of low-skilled and unskilled labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> One problem these migrant workers are involved with is the growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slums" title="Slums">slums</a>. In many cases, the rural-urban low skilled or unskilled migrant workers, attracted by economic opportunities in urban areas, cannot find a job and afford housing in cities and have to dwell in slums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Urban problems, along with infrastructure developments, are also fueling suburbanization trends in developing nations, though the trend for core cities in said nations tends to continue to become ever denser. Urbanization is often viewed as a negative trend, but there are positives in the reduction of expenses in commuting and transportation while improving opportunities for jobs, education, housing, and transportation. Living in cities permits individuals and families to take advantage of the opportunities of proximity and diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brand_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-brand-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> While cities have a greater variety of markets and goods than rural areas, infrastructure congestion, monopolization, high overhead costs, and the inconvenience of cross-town trips frequently combine to make marketplace competition harsher in cities than in rural areas.
</p><p>In many developing countries where economies are growing, the growth is often erratic and based on a small number of industries. For young people in these countries barriers exist such as, lack of access to financial services and business advisory services, difficulty in obtaining credit to start a business, and lack of entrepreneurial skills, in order for them to access opportunities in these industries. Investment in human capital so that young people have access to quality education and infrastructure to enable access to educational facilities is imperative to overcoming economic barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The existence of <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat islands</a> has become a growing concern over the years. An urban heat island is formed when industrial and urban areas produce and retain heat. Much of the solar energy that reaches rural areas is consumed by evaporation of water from vegetation and soil. In cities, where there is less vegetation and exposed soil, most of the sun's energy is instead absorbed by buildings and asphalt; leading to higher surface temperatures. Vehicles, factories and industrial and domestic heating and cooling units release even more heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> As a result, cities are often 1 to 3 °C (1.8 to 5.4 °F) warmer than surrounding landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Impacts also include reducing soil moisture and a reduction in reabsorption of carbon dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The occurrence of eutrophication in bodies of water is another effect large urban populations have on the environment. When rain occurs in these large cities, the rain filters down the pollutants such as CO<sub>2</sub> and other green house gases in the air onto the ground below. Then, those chemicals are washed directly into rivers, streams and oceans, causing a decline in water quality and damaging marine ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Whole_Earth_Discipline" title="Whole Earth Discipline">Whole Earth Discipline</a></i>, Stewart Brand argues that the effects of urbanization are primarily positive for the environment. First, the birth rate of new urban dwellers falls immediately to replacement rate, and keeps falling, reducing environmental stresses caused by population growth. Secondly, emigration from rural areas reduces destructive subsistence farming techniques, such as improperly implemented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> agriculture.
</p><p>In July 2013 a report issued by the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> warned that with 2.4 billion more people by 2050, the amount of food produced will have to increase by 70%, straining food resources, especially in countries already facing food insecurity due to changing environmental conditions. The mix of changing environmental conditions and the growing population of urban regions, according to UN experts, will strain basic sanitation systems and health care, and potentially cause a humanitarian and environmental disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_Waste">Food Waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Food Waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rapid growth of communities create new challenges in the developed world and one such challenge is an increase in <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> also known as urban food waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Food waste is the disposal of food products that can no longer be used due to unused products, expiration, or spoilage.  The increase of food waste can raise environmental concerns such as increase production of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gases and attraction of <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">disease vectors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Landfills are the third leading cause of the release of methane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> causing a concern on its impact to our ozone and on the health of individuals.  Accumulation of food waste causes increased fermentation, which increases the risk of rodent and bug migration. An increase in migration of disease vectors creates greater potential of disease spreading to humans. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_social_effects">Health and social effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health and social effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25em; ; background-color: #B0C4DE;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><div style="text-align:left;">When cities don’t plan for increases in population it drives up house and land prices, creating rich (ghettos) and poor ghettos. "You get a very unequal society and that inequality is manifested where people live, in our neighbourhoods, and it means there can be less capacity for empathy and less development for all society." — Jack Finegan, Urban Programme Specialist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN-Habitat" title="UN-Habitat">UN-Habitat</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</div></div>
</div>
<p>In the developing world, urbanization does not translate into a significant increase in <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-49">[49]</a></sup> Rapid urbanization has led to increased mortality from <a href="/wiki/Non-communicable_disease" title="Non-communicable disease">non-communicable diseases</a> associated with lifestyle, including <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-50">[50]</a></sup> Differences in mortality from <a href="/wiki/Contagious_disease" title="Contagious disease">contagious diseases</a> vary depending on the particular disease and location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urban health levels are on average better in comparison to rural areas. However, residents in poor urban areas such as slums and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informal_settlement" title="Informal settlement">informal settlements</a> suffer "disproportionately from disease, injury, premature death, and the combination of ill-health and poverty entrenches disadvantage over time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFPA_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-24">[24]</a></sup> Many of the urban poor have difficulty accessing health services due to their inability to pay for them; so they resort to less qualified and unregulated providers.
</p><p>While urbanization is associated with improvements in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_hygiene" title="Public hygiene">public hygiene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> and access to <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, it also entails changes in occupational, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dietary" title="Dietary">dietary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercise</a> patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allender-2008_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allender-2008-50">[50]</a></sup> It can have mixed effects on health patterns, alleviating some problems and accentuating others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eckert-2014_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eckert-2014-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One such effect is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Food_desert" title="Food desert">food deserts</a>. Nearly 23.5 million people in the United States lack access to supermarkets within one mile of their home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Several studies suggest that long distances to a grocery store are associated with higher rates of obesity and other health disparities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Food deserts in developed countries often correspond to areas with a high-density of fast food chains and convenience stores that offer little to no fresh food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Urbanization has been shown to be associated with the consumption of less fresh fruits, vegetables, and whole grains and a higher consumption of processed foods and sugar-sweetened beverages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-52">[52]</a></sup> Poor access to healthy food and high intakes of fat, sugar and salt are associated with a greater risk for obesity, diabetes and related chronic disease. Overall, <a href="/wiki/Body_mass_index" title="Body mass index">body mass index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> levels increase sharply with national income and the degree of urbanization.<a href="/wiki/Urbanization#cite_note-Allender-2008-40" title="Urbanization">[40]</a>
</p><p>Food deserts in the United States are most commonly found in low-income and predominately African American neighborhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-52">[52]</a></sup> One study on food deserts in <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, Colorado found that, in addition to minorities, the affected neighborhoods also had a high proportion of children and new births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In children, urbanization is associated with a lower risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undernutrition" title="Undernutrition">under-nutrition</a> but a higher risk of <a href="/wiki/Overweight" title="Overweight">overweight</a>.<a href="/wiki/Urbanization#cite_note-Eckert-2014-39" title="Urbanization"><sup>[39]</sup></a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asthma">Asthma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Asthma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urbanization has also been associated with an increased risk for asthma as well. Throughout the world, as communities transition from rural to more urban societies, the number of people effected by asthma increases. The odds of reduced rates of hospitalization and death from asthmas has decreased for children and young adults in urbanized municipalities in Brazil. This finding indicates that urbanization may have a negative impact on population health particularly affecting people’s susceptibility to asthma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In low and middle income countries many factors contribute to the high numbers of people with asthma. Similar to areas in the United States with increasing urbanization, people living in growing cities in low income countries experience high exposure to air pollution, which increases the prevalence and severity of asthma among these populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Links have been found between exposure to traffic-related air pollution and allergic diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-57">[57]</a></sup> Children living in poor, urban areas in the United States now have an increased risk of morbidity due to asthma in comparison to other low-income children in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In addition, children with croup living in urban areas have higher hazard ratios for asthma than similar children living in rural areas. Researchers suggest that this difference in hazard ratios is due to the higher levels of air pollution and exposure to environmental allergens found in urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Exposure to elevated levels of ambient air pollutants such as nitrogen dioxide (NO<sub>2</sub>), carbon monoxide (CO), and particulate matter with a diameter of less than 2.5 micrometers (PM<sub>2.5</sub>), can cause DNA methylation of CpG sites in immune cells, which increases children’s risk of developing asthma. Studies have shown a positive correlation between <i>Foxp3</i> methylation and children’s exposure to NO<sub>2</sub>, CO, and PM<sub>2.5</sub>. Furthermore, any amount of exposure to high levels of air pollution have shown long term effects on the <i>Foxp3</i> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the increase in access to health services that usually accompanies urbanization, the rise in population density negatively affects air quality ultimately mitigating the positive value of health resources as more children and young adults develop asthma due to high pollution rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-55">[55]</a></sup> However, urban planning as well as emission control can lessen the effects of traffic-related air pollution on allergic diseases such as asthma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically crime and urbanization have gone hand in hand. The simplest explanation is that areas with a higher population density are surrounded by a greater availability of goods. Committing crimes in urbanized areas is also more feasible. Modernization has led to more crime as well. There is a greater awareness of the income gap between the rich and poor due to modern media. This leads to feelings of deprivation which can lead to crime. In some regions where urbanization happens in wealthier areas, a rise in property crime and a decrease in violent crime is seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data shows that there is an increase of crime in urbanized areas. Some factors include per capita income, income inequality, and overall population size. There is also a smaller association between unemployment rate, police expenditures and crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The presence of crime also has the ability to produce more crime. These areas have less social cohesion, and therefore less social control. This is evident in the geographical regions that crime occurs in. As most crime tends to cluster in city centers, the further the distance from the center of the city, the lower the occurrence of crimes are.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Migration is also a factor that can increase crime in urbanized areas. People from one area are displaced and forced to move into an urbanized society. Here they are in a new environment with new norms and social values. This can lead to less social cohesion and more crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_activity">Physical activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Physical activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although urbanization tends to produce more negative effects, one positive effect that urbanization has impacted is an increase in physical activity in comparison to rural areas.  Residents of rural areas and communities in the United States have higher rates of obesity and engage in less physical activity than urban residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-65">[65]</a></sup>  Rural residents consume a higher percent of fat calories and are less likely to meet the guidelines for physical activity and more likely to be physically inactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-67">[67]</a></sup>  In comparison to regions within the United States, the west has the lowest prevalence of physical <i>inactivity</i> and the south has the highest prevalence of physical <i>inactivity</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-67">[67]</a></sup>  Metropolitan and large urban areas across all regions have the highest prevalence of physical activity among residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-:6-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>Barriers such as geographic isolation, busy and unsafe roads, and social stigmas lead to decreased physical activity in rural environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-68">[68]</a></sup>  Faster speed limits on rural roads prohibits the ability to have bike lanes, sidewalks, footpaths, and shoulders along the side of the roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-65">[65]</a></sup>  Less developed open spaces in rural areas, like parks and trails, suggest that there is lower walkability in these areas in comparison to urban areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-65">[65]</a></sup>  Many residents in rural settings have to travel long distances to utilize exercise facilities, taking up too much time in the day and deterring residents from using recreational facilities to obtain physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-68">[68]</a></sup>  Additionally, residents of rural communities are traveling further for work, decreasing the amount of time that can be spent on leisure physical activity and significantly decreases the opportunity to partake in active transportation to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_65-3"><a href="#cite_note-:4-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neighborhoods and communities with nearby fitness venues, a common feature of urbanization, have residents that partake in increased amounts of physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-68">[68]</a></sup>  Communities with sidewalks, street lights, and traffic signals have residents participating in more physical activity than communities without those features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_65-4"><a href="#cite_note-:4-65">[65]</a></sup>  Having a variety of destinations close to where people live, increases the use of active transportation, such as walking and biking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-69">[69]</a></sup>  Active transportation is also enhanced in urban communities where there is easy access to public transportation due to residents walking or biking to transportation stops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a study comparing different regions in the United States, opinions across all areas were shared that environmental characteristics like access to sidewalks, safe roads, recreational facilities, and enjoyable scenery are positively associated with participation in leisure physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_67-3"><a href="#cite_note-:6-67">[67]</a></sup>  Perceiving that resources are nearby for physical activity increases the likelihood that residents of all communities will meet the guidelines and recommendations for appropriate physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-:8-69">[69]</a></sup>  Specific to rural residents, safety of outdoor developed spaces and convenient availability to recreational facilities matters most when making decisions on increasing physical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-66">[66]</a></sup>  In order to combat the levels of inactivity in rural residents, more convenient recreational features, such as the ones discussed in this paragraph, need to be implemented into rural communities and societies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_health">Mental health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mental health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urbanization factors that contribute to <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> can be thought of as factors that affect the individual and factors that affect the larger social group. At the macro, social group level, changes related to urbanization are thought to contribute to social disintegration and disorganization. These macro factors contribute to social disparities which affect individuals by creating perceived insecurity. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Perceived insecurity can be due problems with the physical environment, such as issues with personal safety, or problems with the social environment, such as a loss of positive self-concepts from negative events. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Increased stress is a common individual psychological stressor that accompanies urbanization and is thought to be due to perceived insecurity. Changes in social organization, a consequence of urbanization, are thought to lead to reduced social support, increased violence, and overcrowding. It is these factors that are thought to contribute to increased stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srivastava_IPJ_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Srivastava_IPJ-72">[72]</a></sup>It is important to note that urbanization or population density alone does not cause mental health problems. It is the combination of urbanization with physical and social risk factors that contribute to mental health problems. As cities continue to expand it is important to consider and account for mental health along with other public health measures that accompany urbanization.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_forms">Changing forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urbanization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Changing forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different forms of urbanization can be classified depending on the style of architecture and planning methods as well as historic growth of areas.
</p><p>In cities of the <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed world</a> urbanization traditionally exhibited a concentration of human activities and settlements around the downtown area, the so-called <i>in-migration</i>. In-migration refers to migration from former colonies and similar places. The fact that many immigrants settle in impoverished city centres led to the notion of the "peripheralization of the core", which simply describes that people who used to be at the periphery of the former empires now live right in the centre.
</p><p>Recent developments, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner-city" title="Inner-city">inner-city</a> redevelopment schemes, mean that new arrivals in cities no longer necessarily settle in the centre. In some developed regions, the reverse effect, originally called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_urbanization" title="Counter urbanization">counter urbanization</a> has occurred, with cities losing population to rural areas, and is particularly common for richer families. This has been possible because of improved communications, and has been caused by factors such as the fear of crime and poor urban environments. It has contributed to the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Shrinking_cities" title="Shrinking cities">shrinking cities</a> experienced by some parts of the industrialized world.
</p><p>When the residential area shifts outward, this is called suburbanization. A number of researchers and writers suggest that suburbanization has gone so far to form new points of concentration outside the downtown both in developed and developing countries such as India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> This networked, poly-centric form of concentration is considered by some emerging pattern of urbanization. It is called variously exurbia, edge city (Garreau, 1991), network city (Batten, 1995), or postmodern city (Dear, 2000). Los Angeles is the best-known example of this type of urbanization. In the United States, this process has reversed as of 2011, with "re-urbanization" occurring as <i>suburban flight</i> due to chronically high transport costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rural migrants are attracted by the possibilities that cities can offer, but often settle in <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">shanty towns</a> and experience extreme poverty. The inability of countries to provide adequate housing for these rural migrants is related to <a href="/wiki/Overurbanization" title="Overurbanization">overurbanization</a>, a phenomenon in which the rate of urbanization grows more rapidly than the rate of economic development, leading to high unemployment and high demand for resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-75">[75]</a></sup> In the 1980s, this was attempted to be tackled with the <b>urban bias theory</b> which was promoted by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lipton" title="Michael Lipton">Michael Lipton</a>.
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25em; ; background-color: #B0C4DE;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><div style="text-align:left;">...the most important class conflict in the poor countries of the world today is not between labour and capital. Nor is it between foreign and national interests. It is between rural classes and urban classes. The rural sector contains most of the poverty and most of the low-cost sources of potential advance; but the urban sector contains most of the articulateness, organization and power. So the urban classes have been able to win most of the rounds of the struggle with the countryside...". — Michael Lipton, author of urban bias theory<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</div></div>
</div>
<p>Most of the urban poor in developing countries unable to find work, can spend their lives in insecure, poorly paid jobs. According to research by the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> pro-poor urbanization will require labour-intensive growth, supported by labour protection, flexible land use regulation and investments in basic services.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urbanization can be planned urbanization or organic. Planned urbanization, i.e.: <a href="/wiki/Planned_community" title="Planned community">planned community</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Garden_city_movement" title="Garden city movement">garden city movement</a>, is based on an advance plan, which can be prepared for military, aesthetic, economic or <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a> reasons. Examples can be seen in many ancient cities; although with exploration came the collision of nations, which meant that many invaded cities took on the desired planned characteristics of their occupiers. Many ancient organic cities experienced redevelopment for military and economic purposes, new roads carved through the cities, and new parcels of land were cordoned off serving various planned purposes giving cities distinctive geometric designs. UN agencies prefer to see <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">urban infrastructure</a> installed before urbanization occurs. <a href="/wiki/Landscape_planning" title="Landscape planning">Landscape planners</a> are responsible for landscape infrastructure (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_park" title="Public park">public parks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_urban_drainage_systems" title="Sustainable urban drainage systems">sustainable urban drainage systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">greenways</a> etc.) which can be planned before urbanization takes place, or afterward to revitalize an area and create greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livability" title="Livability">livability</a> within a region. Concepts of control of the urban expansion are considered in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Planners" title="American Institute of Planners">American Institute of Planners</a>.
</p><p>As population continues to grow and urbanize at unprecedented rates, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_urbanism" title="New urbanism">new urbanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smart_growth" title="Smart growth">smart growth</a> techniques are implemented to create a transition into developing environmentally, economically, and socially sustainable cities. Smart Growth and New Urbanism’s principles include <a href="/wiki/Walkability" title="Walkability">walkability</a>, mixed-use development, comfortable high-density design, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Land_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Land conservation (page does not exist)">land conservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_equity" title="Social equity">social equity</a>, and economic diversity. Mixed-use communities work to fight <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> with <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordable housing</a> to promote social equity, decrease <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependency</a> to lower use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, and promote a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Localized_economy" title="Localized economy">localized economy</a>. Walkable communities have a 38% higher average GDP per capita than less walkable urban metros (Leinberger, Lynch). By combining economic, environmental, and social sustainability, cities will become equitable, resilient, and more appealing than <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> that <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overuses</a> <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land</a>, promotes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Automobile_use&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Automobile use (page does not exist)">automobile use</a>, and segregates the population economically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-control_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-control-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
