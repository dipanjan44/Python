<p>The <b>retreat of glaciers since 1850</b> affects the availability of fresh water for irrigation and domestic use, mountain recreation, animals and plants that depend on glacier-melt, and, in the longer term, the level of the oceans.  Studied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciologist" title="Glaciologist">glaciologists</a>, the temporal coincidence of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> retreat with the measured increase of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> is often cited as an evidentiary underpinning of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.  Mid-latitude mountain ranges such as the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a>, and the southern <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, as well as isolated tropical summits such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a> in Africa, are showing some of the largest proportionate glacial losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">Glacier mass balance</a> is the key determinant of the health of a glacier.  If the amount of frozen precipitation in the <a href="/wiki/Accumulation_zone" title="Accumulation zone">accumulation zone</a> exceeds the quantity of glacial ice lost due to melting or in the <a href="/wiki/Ablation_zone" title="Ablation zone">ablation zone</a> a glacier will advance; if the accumulation is less than the ablation, the glacier will retreat.  Glaciers in retreat will have negative mass balances, and if they do not find an equilibrium between accumulation and ablation, will eventually disappear.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> was a period from about 1550 to 1850 when the world experienced relatively cooler temperatures compared to the present.  Subsequently, until about 1940, glaciers around the world retreated as the climate warmed substantially.  Glacial retreat slowed and even reversed temporarily, in many cases, between 1950 and 1980 as global temperatures <a href="/wiki/File:Global_monthly_temperature_record.png" title="File:Global monthly temperature record.png">cooled slightly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelto1-3">[3]</a></sup>  Since 1980, a significant <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> has led to glacier retreat becoming increasingly rapid and ubiquitous, so much so that some glaciers have disappeared altogether, and the existences of many of the remaining glaciers are threatened.  In locations such as the Andes of South America and Himalayas in Asia, the demise of glaciers in these regions has the potential to affect water supplies in those areas.
</p><p>The retreat of mountain glaciers, notably in western North America, Asia, the Alps and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> regions of South America, Africa and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, provide evidence for the rise in global temperatures since the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The acceleration of the rate of retreat since 1995 of key <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outlet_glacier" title="Outlet glacier">outlet glaciers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="West Antarctic Ice Sheet">West Antarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> may foreshadow a <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">rise in sea level</a>, which would affect coastal regions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacier_mass_balance">Glacier mass balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Glacier mass balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="650" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/280px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/420px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/560px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This map of mountain glacier mass balance changes since 1970 shows thinning in yellow and red, and thickening in blue.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="658" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Glacier_Mass_Balance.png/280px-Glacier_Mass_Balance.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Glacier_Mass_Balance.png/420px-Glacier_Mass_Balance.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Glacier_Mass_Balance.png/560px-Glacier_Mass_Balance.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global glacial mass balance in the last fifty years, reported to the <a href="/wiki/World_Glacier_Monitoring_Service" title="World Glacier Monitoring Service">WGMS</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Snow_and_Ice_Data_Center" title="National Snow and Ice Data Center">NSIDC</a>.  The increasing downward trend in the late 1980s is symptomatic of the increased rate and number of retreating glaciers.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">Glacier mass balance</a></div>
<p>The mass balance, or difference between <a href="/wiki/Glacier_ice_accumulation" title="Glacier ice accumulation">accumulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ablation_zone" title="Ablation zone">ablation</a> (melting and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a>), of a glacier is crucial to its survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mote-kaser_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mote-kaser-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> may cause variations in both temperature and snowfall, resulting in changes in mass balance. A glacier with a sustained negative balance loses equilibrium and retreats. A sustained positive balance is also out of equilibrium and will advance to reestablish equilibrium. Currently, nearly all glaciers have a negative mass balance and are retreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Glacier retreat results in the loss of the low-elevation region of the glacier. Since higher elevations are cooler, the disappearance of the lowest portion decreases overall ablation, thereby increasing mass balance and potentially reestablishing equilibrium. If the mass balance of a significant portion of the accumulation zone of the glacier is negative, it is in disequilibrium with the climate and will melt away without a colder climate and or an increase in frozen precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubbard_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hubbard-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelto2-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methods for measuring retreat include staking <a href="/wiki/Glacier_terminus" title="Glacier terminus">terminus location</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_positioning" title="Global positioning">global positioning</a> mapping, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_mapping" title="Aerial mapping">aerial mapping</a> and laser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altimetry" title="Altimetry">altimetry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubbard_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hubbard-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-clark-10">[10]</a></sup> The key symptom of disequilibrium is thinning along the entire length of the glacier. This indicates a diminishment of the accumulation zone. The result is marginal recession of the accumulation zone margin, not just of the terminus. In effect, the glacier no longer has a consistent accumulation zone and without an accumulation zone cannot survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pelto2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Easton_Glacier" title="Easton Glacier">Easton Glacier</a> in Washington state, U.S. will likely shrink to half its size but at a slowing rate of reduction and stabilize at that size despite the warmer temperature over a few decades. However, the <a href="/wiki/Grinnell_Glacier" title="Grinnell Glacier">Grinnell Glacier</a> in Montana, U.S. will shrink at an increasing rate until it disappears. The difference is that the upper section of Easton Glacier remains healthy and snow-covered, while even the upper section of the Grinnell Glacier is bare, is melting and has thinned. Small glaciers with minimal altitude range are most likely to fall into disequilibrium with the climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto2_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-pelto2-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_latitude_glaciers">Middle latitude glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle latitude glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">Middle latitude</a> glaciers are located either between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>, or between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Both areas support glacier ice from mountain glaciers, valley glaciers and even smaller icecaps, which are usually located in higher mountainous regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-clark-10">[10]</a></sup> All are located in mountain ranges, notably the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>; <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Coast_Range" title="Pacific Coast Range">Pacific Coast Ranges</a> of North America; the <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonian</a> Andes in South America; and mountain ranges in New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hensen_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hensen-13">[13]</a></sup> Glaciers in these latitudes are more widespread and tend to be greater in mass the closer they are to the polar regions. They are the most widely studied over the past 150 years. As with examples located in the tropical zone, virtually all the glaciers in the mid-latitudes are in a state of negative mass balance and are retreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-clark-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Northern_hemisphere_.E2.80.93_Eurasia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_hemisphere_–_Eurasia">Northern hemisphere – Eurasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Northern hemisphere – Eurasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alps-glaciers.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="404" data-file-width="479" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Alps-glaciers.png/320px-Alps-glaciers.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Alps-glaciers.png 1.5x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alps-glaciers.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This map from the annual Glacier Commission surveys in Italy and Switzerland shows the percentage of advancing glaciers in the Alps. Mid-20th century saw strong retreating trends, but not as extreme as the present; current retreats represent additional reductions of already smaller glaciers.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In France all six of the major glaciers in that country are in retreat. On <a href="/wiki/Mont_Blanc" title="Mont Blanc">Mont Blanc</a>, the highest peak in the Alps, the <a href="/wiki/Argenti%C3%A8re_Glacier" title="Argentière Glacier">Argentière Glacier</a> has receded 1,150 m (3,770 ft) since 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other Mont Blanc glaciers have also been in retreat, including the <a href="/wiki/Mer_de_Glace" title="Mer de Glace">Mer de Glace</a>, which is the largest glacier in France at 12 km (7.5 mi) in length but retreated 500 m (1,600 ft) between 1994 and 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelto4-16">[16]</a></sup> The glacier has retreated 2,300 m (7,500 ft) since the end of the Little Ice Age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto4_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-pelto4-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bossons_Glacier" title="Bossons Glacier">Bossons Glacier</a> once extended from the summit of Mont Blanc at 4,807 m (15,771 ft) to an elevation of 1,050 m (3,440 ft) in 1900. By 2008 Bossons Glacier had retreated to a point that was 1,400 m (4,600 ft) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Other researchers have found that glaciers across the Alps appear to be retreating at a faster rate than a few decades ago. In a paper published in 2009 by the University of Zurich, the Swiss glacier survey of 89 glaciers found 76 retreating, 5 stationary and 8 advancing from where they had been in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zurich_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-zurich-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Trift_Glacier" title="Trift Glacier">Trift Glacier</a> had the greatest recorded retreat, losing 350 m (1,150 ft) of its length between the years 2003 and 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zurich_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-zurich-18">[18]</a></sup>  The Grosser <a href="/wiki/Aletsch_Glacier" title="Aletsch Glacier">Aletsch Glacier</a> is the largest glacier in Switzerland and has been studied since the late 19th century. Aletsch Glacier retreated 2.8 km (1.7 mi) from 1880 to 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jouvet_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-jouvet-19">[19]</a></sup> This rate of retreat has also increased since 1980, with 30%, or 800 m (2,600 ft), of the total retreat occurring in the last 20% of the time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jouvet_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-jouvet-19">[19]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg/220px-Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg/330px-Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg/440px-Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Morteratsch-_und_Persgletscher.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Morteratsch (right) and Pers (left) glaciers in 2005</div></div></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Morteratsch_Glacier" title="Morteratsch Glacier">Morteratsch Glacier</a> in Switzerland has had one of the longest periods of scientific study with yearly measurements of the glacier's length commencing in 1878. The overall retreat from 1878 to 1998 has been 2 km (1.2 mi) with a mean annual retreat rate of approximately 17 m (56 ft) per year. This long-term average was markedly surpassed in recent years with the glacier receding 30 m (98 ft) per year during the period between 1999–2005. Similarly, of the glaciers in the Italian Alps, only about a third were in retreat in 1980, while by 1999, 89% of these glaciers were retreating. In 2005, the Italian Glacier Commission found that 123 glaciers in Lombardy were retreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A random study of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sforzellina_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sforzellina Glacier (page does not exist)">Sforzellina Glacier</a> in the Italian Alps indicated that the rate of retreat from 2002 to 2006 was much higher than in the preceding 35 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> To study glaciers located in the alpine regions of Lombardy, researchers compared a series of aerial and ground images taken from the 1950s through the early 21st century and deduced that between the years 1954-2003 the mostly smaller glaciers found there lost more than half of their area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Repeat photography of glaciers in the Alps indicates that there has been significant retreat since studies commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swiss1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-swiss1-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><div style="clear:both;"></div><p>
Though the glaciers of the Alps have received more attention from glaciologists than in other areas of Europe, research indicates that glaciers in northern Europe are also retreating. Since the end of World War II, <a href="/wiki/Storglaci%C3%A4ren" title="Storglaciären">Storglaciären</a> in Sweden has undergone the longest continuous mass balance study in the world conducted from the <a href="/wiki/Tarfala_research_station" title="Tarfala research station">Tarfala research station</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Kebnekaise" title="Kebnekaise">Kebnekaise</a> Mountains of northern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, a study of 16 glaciers between 1990 and 2001 found that 14 glaciers were retreating, one was advancing and one was stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wikland_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-wikland-24">[24]</a></sup> In Norway, glacier studies have been performed since the early 19th century, with systematic surveys undertaken regularly since the 1990s. Inland glaciers have had a generally negative mass balance, whereby during the 1990s, maritime glaciers showed a positive mass balance and advanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nesje_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nesje-25">[25]</a></sup> The maritime advances have been attributed to heavy snowfall in the period 1989-1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nesje_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-nesje-25">[25]</a></sup> However, reduced snowfall since has caused most Norwegian glaciers to retreat significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nesje_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-nesje-25">[25]</a></sup> A survey of 31 Norwegian glaciers in 2010 indicated that 27 were in retreat, one had no change and three advanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norway_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-norway-26">[26]</a></sup> Similarly, in 2013, of 33 Norwegian glaciers surveyed, 26 were retreating, four showed no change and three advanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norway_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-norway-26">[26]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Svartisen_glacier.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="601" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Svartisen_glacier.JPG/220px-Svartisen_glacier.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Svartisen_glacier.JPG/330px-Svartisen_glacier.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Svartisen_glacier.JPG/440px-Svartisen_glacier.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Svartisen_glacier.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engabreen Glacier in Norway extended to within 7 m (23 ft) above sea level in 2014, the lowest altitude of any glacier in Europe.</div></div></div><p> Engabreen Glacier in Norway, an outlet glacier of the <a href="/wiki/Svartisen" title="Svartisen">Svartisen ice cap</a>, had several advances in the 20th century, though it retreated 200 m (660 ft) between 1999 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Brenndalsbreen glacier retreated 56 m (184 ft) between the years 2000 and 2014, while the Rembesdalsskåka glacier, which has retreated 2 km (1.2 mi) since the end of the Little Ice Age, retreated 200 m (660 ft) between 1997–2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The Briksdalsbreen glacier retreated 230 m (750 ft) between 1996 and 2004 with 130 m (430 ft) of that in the last year of that study; the greatest annual retreat recorded on that glacier since studies began there in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This figure was exceeded in 2006 with five glaciers retreating over 100 m (330 ft) from the fall of 2005 to the fall of 2006. Four outlets from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jostedalsbreen" title="Jostedalsbreen">Jostedalsbreen</a> ice cap, the largest body of ice in continental Europe, <a href="/wiki/Kjenndalsbreen" title="Kjenndalsbreen">Kjenndalsbreen</a>, Brenndalsbreen, <a href="/wiki/Briksdalsbreen" title="Briksdalsbreen">Briksdalsbreen</a> and Bergsetbreen had a frontal retreat of more than 100 m (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nussbaumer_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nussbaumer-30">[30]</a></sup> Overall, from 1999 to 2005, Briksdalsbreen retreated 336 metres (1,102 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nussbaumer_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-nussbaumer-30">[30]</a></sup> Gråfjellsbrea, an outlet glacier of the <a href="/wiki/Folgefonna" title="Folgefonna">Folgefonna ice cap</a>, had a retreat of almost 100 m (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nussbaumer_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-nussbaumer-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, recent studies have shown important losses in extent and volume of the glaciers of the <a href="/wiki/Maladeta" title="Maladeta">Maladeta</a> massif during the period 1981–2005. These include a reduction in area of 35.7%, from 2.41 km<sup>2</sup> (600 acres) to 1.55 km<sup>2</sup> (380 acres), a loss in total ice volume of 0.0137 km<sup>3</sup> (0.0033 cu mi) and an increase in the mean altitude of the glacial termini of 43.5 m (143 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> For the Pyrenees as a whole 50–60% of the glaciated area has been lost since 1991. The Balaitus, Perdigurero and La Munia glaciers have disappeared in this period. Monte Perdido Glacier has shrunk from 90 hectares to 40 hectares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>As initial cause for glacier retreat in the alps since 1850, a decrease of the glaciers <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, caused by industrial <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> can be identified. According to a report, this may have accelerated the retreat of glaciers in Europe which otherwise may have continued to expand until approximately the year 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Siberia_and_the_Russian_Far_East">Siberia and the Russian Far East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Siberia and the Russian Far East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> is typically classified as a polar region, owing to the dryness of the winter climate and has glaciers only in the high <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verkhoyansk_Range" title="Verkhoyansk Range">Verkhoyansk Range</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherskiy_Range" title="Cherskiy Range">Cherskiy Range</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suntar-Khayata_Range" title="Suntar-Khayata Range">Suntar-Khayata Range</a>, plus possibly a few very small glaciers in the ranges near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, which have never been monitored and may have completely disappeared since 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surazakov_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-surazakov-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dyurgerov_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-dyurgerov-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ananicheva_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ananicheva-36">[36]</a></sup> Between the years 1952 and 2006, the glaciers found in the Aktru Basin region shrank by 7.2 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surazakov_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-surazakov-34">[34]</a></sup> This shrinkage has been primarily in the ablation zone of the glaciers, with recession of several hundred meters being observed for some glaciers. The Altai region has also experienced an overall temperature increase of 1.2 degrees Celsius in the last 120 years according to a report from 2006, with most of that increase occurring since the late 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surazakov_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-surazakov-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the more maritime and generally wetter <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamchatka" title="Kamchatka">Kamchatka</a>, exposed during winter to moisture from the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Low" title="Aleutian Low">Aleutian Low</a>, has much more extensive glaciation totaling around 906 km<sup>2</sup> (350 sq mi) with 448 known glaciers as of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ananicheva_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-ananicheva-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones-37">[37]</a></sup> Despite generally heavy winter snowfall and cool summer temperatures, the high summer rainfall of the more southerly <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a> in historic times melt rates have been too high for a positive mass balance even on the highest peaks. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chukotskiy_Peninsula" title="Chukotskiy Peninsula">Chukotskiy Peninsula</a> small alpine glaciers are numerous, but the extent of glaciation, though larger than further west, is much smaller than in Kamchatka, totalling around 300 square kilometres (120 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dyurgerov_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-dyurgerov-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Details on the retreat of Siberian and Russian Far East glaciers less adequate than in most other glaciated areas of the world. There are several reasons for this, the principal one being that since the collapse of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communism</a> there has been a large reduction in the number of monitoring stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-unep-38">[38]</a></sup> Another factor is that in the Verkhoyansk and Cherskiy Ranges it was thought glaciers were absent before they were discovered during the 1940s, whilst in ultra-remote Kamchatka and Chukotka, although the existence of glaciers was known earlier, monitoring of their size dates back no earlier than the end of World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ananicheva_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-ananicheva-36">[36]</a></sup> Nonetheless, available records do indicate a general retreat of all glaciers in the Altai Mountains with the exception of volcanic glaciers in Kamchatka. <a href="/wiki/Sakha_Republic" title="Sakha Republic">Sakha’s</a> glaciers, totaling seventy square kilometers, have shrunk by around 28 percent since 1945 reaching several percent annually in some places, whilst in the Altai and Chukotkan mountains and non-volcanic areas of Kamchatka, the shrinkage is considerably larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-unep-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Himalayas_and_Central_Asia">Himalayas and Central Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Himalayas and Central Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacial_lakes,_Bhutan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1665" data-file-width="3000" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg/390px-Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg/585px-Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg/780px-Glacial_lakes%2C_Bhutan.jpg 2x" width="390"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacial_lakes,_Bhutan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> image shows the formation of numerous glacial lakes at the termini of receding glaciers in <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalaya</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Himalayas and other mountain chains of central Asia support large glaciated regions. An estimated 15,000 glaciers can be found in the greater Himalayas, with double that number in the Hindu Kush and Karakoram and Tien Shan ranges, and comprise the largest glaciated region outside the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> These glaciers provide critical water supplies to arid countries such as <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, western China, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. As with glaciers worldwide, those of the greater Himalayan region are experiencing a decline in mass, and researchers claim that between the early 1970s and early 2000s, there had been a 9 percent reduction in ice mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Change in temperature has led to melting and the formation and expansion of glacial lakes which could cause an increase in the number of glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs). If the present trends persist the ice mass will gradually be reduced, and will affect the availability of water resources, though water loss is not expected to cause problems for many decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Wakhan Corridor of Afghanistan 28 of 30 glaciers examined retreated significantly between 1976–2003, with an average retreat of 11 m (36 ft) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haritashya_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-haritashya-42">[42]</a></sup>  One of these glaciers, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zemestan_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zemestan Glacier (page does not exist)">Zemestan Glacier</a>, retreated 460 m (1,510 ft) during this period, not quite 10% of its 5.2 km (3.2 mi) length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In examining 612 glaciers in China between 1950 and 1970, 53% of the glaciers studied were retreating. After 1990, 95% of these glaciers were measured to be retreating, indicating that retreat of these glaciers was becoming more widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Glaciers in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> region of the Himalayas are all in a state of retreat.  The <a href="/wiki/Rongbuk_Glacier" title="Rongbuk Glacier">Rongbuk Glacier</a>, draining the north side of Mount Everest into <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, has been retreating 20 m (66 ft) per year. In the Khumbu region of Nepal along the front of the main Himalaya of 15 glaciers examined from 1976–2007 all retreated significantly and the average retreat was 28 m (92 ft) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bajracharya_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-bajracharya-45">[45]</a></sup> The most famous of these, the Khumbu Glacier, retreated at a rate of 18 m (59 ft) per year from 1976–2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bajracharya_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-bajracharya-45">[45]</a></sup> In India, the <a href="/wiki/Gangotri_Glacier" title="Gangotri Glacier">Gangotri Glacier</a> retreated 1,147 m (3,763 ft) between the years 1936 and 1996 with 850 m (2,790 ft) of that retreat occurring in the last 25 years of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gangotri_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-gangotri-47">[47]</a></sup> However, the glacier is still over 30 km (19 mi) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gangotri_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-gangotri-47">[47]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>, 26 glaciers examined between the years 1976 and 2005 were retreating at an average rate of 13.02 m (42.7 ft) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raina_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-raina-48">[48]</a></sup>  
Overall, glaciers in the Greater Himalayan region that have been studied are retreating an average of between 18 and 20 m (59 and 66 ft) annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The only region in the Greater Himalaya that has seen glacial advances is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karakoram_Range" title="Karakoram Range">Karakoram Range</a> and only in the highest elevation glaciers, but this has been attributed possibly increased precipitation as well as to the correlating glacial surges, where the glacier tongue advances due to pressure build up from snow and ice accumulation further up the glacier. Between the years 1997 and 2001, 68 km (42 mi) long <a href="/wiki/Biafo_Glacier" title="Biafo Glacier">Biafo Glacier</a> thickened 10 to 25 m (33 to 82 ft) mid-glacier, however it did not advance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the retreat of glaciers in the Himalayas, a number of glacial lakes have been created. A growing concern is the potential for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GLOF" title="GLOF">GLOFs</a> researchers estimate 21 glacial lakes in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and 24 in <a href="/wiki/Glaciers_of_Bhutan" title="Glaciers of Bhutan">Bhutan</a> pose hazards to human populations should their terminal moraines fail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> One glacial lake identified as potentially hazardous is Bhutan's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Raphstreng_Tsho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Raphstreng Tsho (page does not exist)">Raphstreng Tsho</a>, which measured 1.6 km (0.99 mi) long, 0.96 km (0.60 mi) wide and 80 m (260 ft) deep in 1986. By 1995 the lake had swollen to a length of  1.94 km (1.21 mi), 1.13 km (0.70 mi) in width and a depth of 107 m (351 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In 1994 a GLOF from Luggye Tsho, a glacial lake adjacent to Raphstreng Tsho, killed 23 people downstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Glaciers in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ak-shirak_Range&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ak-shirak Range (page does not exist)">Ak-shirak Range</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a> experienced a slight loss between 1943 and 1977 and an accelerated loss of 20% of their remaining mass between 1977 and 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tien_Shan" title="Tien Shan">Tien Shan</a> mountains, which Kyrgyzstan shares with China and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, studies in the northern areas of that mountain range show that the glaciers that help supply water to this arid region,  lost nearly 2 km<sup>3</sup> (0.48 cu mi) of ice per year between 1955 and 2000. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> study also reported that an average of 1.28% of the volume of these glaciers had been lost per year between 1974 and 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamirs" title="Pamirs">Pamirs</a> mountain range located primarily in <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, has approximately eight thousand glaciers, many of which are in a general state of retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kayumov_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-kayumov-56">[56]</a></sup> During the 20th century, the glaciers of Tajikistan lost 20 km<sup>3</sup> (4.8 cu mi) of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kayumov_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-kayumov-56">[56]</a></sup> The 70 km (43 mi) long <a href="/wiki/Fedchenko_Glacier" title="Fedchenko Glacier">Fedchenko Glacier</a>, which is the largest in Tajikistan and the largest non-polar glacier on Earth, retreated 1 km (0.62 mi) between the years 1933 and 2006, and lost 44 km<sup>2</sup> (17 sq mi) of its surface area due to shrinkage between the years 1966 and 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kayumov_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-kayumov-56">[56]</a></sup> Tajikistan and neighboring countries of the Pamir Range are highly dependent upon glacial runoff to ensure river flow during droughts and the dry seasons experienced every year. The continued demise of glacier ice will result in a short-term increase, followed by a long-term decrease in glacial melt water flowing into rivers and streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Northern_hemisphere_.E2.80.94_North_America"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_hemisphere_—_North_America">Northern hemisphere — North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Northern hemisphere — North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lewist.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="255" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Lewist.jpg/220px-Lewist.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Lewist.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lewist.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Lewis Glacier, <a href="/wiki/North_Cascades_National_Park" title="North Cascades National Park">North Cascades National Park</a> after melting away in 1990</div></div></div>
<p>North American glaciers are primarily located along the spine of the Rocky Mountains in the United States and Canada, and the Pacific Coast Ranges extending from northern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. While <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> is geologically associated with North America, it is also a part of the Arctic region. Apart from the few tidewater glaciers such as <a href="/wiki/Taku_Glacier" title="Taku Glacier">Taku Glacier</a>, in the advance stage of their <a href="/wiki/Tidewater_glacier_cycle" title="Tidewater glacier cycle">tidewater glacier cycle</a> prevalent along the coast of Alaska, virtually all of those in North America are in a state of retreat. This  rate has increased rapidly since around 1980, and overall each decade since has seen greater rates of retreat than the preceding one. There are also small remnant glaciers scattered throughout the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> mountains of California and <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cascade_Range">Cascade Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cascade Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> of western North America extends from southern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> in Canada to northern California. Excepting Alaska, about half of the glacial area in the U.S. is contained within the over 700 glaciers of the <a href="/wiki/North_Cascades_National_Park" title="North Cascades National Park">North Cascades</a>, a portion of those located between the Canada–US border and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I-90" title="I-90">I-90</a> in central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>. These contain much water as is found in all the lakes and reservoirs in the rest of the state, and provide much of the stream and river flow in the dry summer months, approximating some 870,000 m<sup>3</sup> (1,140,000 cu yd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bouldert.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bouldert.jpg/250px-Bouldert.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bouldert.jpg/375px-Bouldert.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Bouldert.jpg/500px-Bouldert.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bouldert.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Boulder_Glacier_(Washington)" title="Boulder Glacier (Washington)">Boulder Glacier</a> retreated 450 m (1,480 ft) from 1987 to 2003.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eastonterm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1344" data-file-width="2048" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Eastonterm.jpg/250px-Eastonterm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Eastonterm.jpg/375px-Eastonterm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Eastonterm.jpg/500px-Eastonterm.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eastonterm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Easton Glacier retreated 255 m (837 ft) from 1990 to 2005.</div></div></div>
<p>As recently as 1975 many North Cascade glaciers were advancing due to cooler weather and increased precipitation that occurred from 1944 to 1976. By 1987 the North Cascade glaciers were retreating and the pace had increased each decade since the mid-1970s. Between 1984 and 2005 the North Cascade glaciers lost an average of more than 12.5 metres (41 ft) in thickness and 20–40 percent of their volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto2_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-pelto2-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Glaciologists researching the North Cascades found that all 47 monitored glaciers are receding while four glaciers—<a href="/wiki/Spider_Glacier_(Phelps_Ridge,_Washington)" title="Spider Glacier (Phelps Ridge, Washington)">Spider Glacier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Glacier_(Oregon)" title="Lewis Glacier (Oregon)">Lewis Glacier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milk_Lake_Glacier" title="Milk Lake Glacier">Milk Lake Glacier</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Glacier" title="David Glacier">David Glacier</a>—have disappeared completely since 1985. The <a href="/wiki/White_Chuck_Glacier" title="White Chuck Glacier">White Chuck Glacier</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Glacier_Peak" title="Glacier Peak">Glacier Peak</a>) is a particularly dramatic example. The glacier area shrank from 3.1 km<sup>2</sup> (1.2 sq mi) in 1958 to 0.9 km<sup>2</sup> (0.35 sq mi) by 2002. Between 1850 and 1950, the <a href="/wiki/Boulder_Glacier_(Washington)" title="Boulder Glacier (Washington)">Boulder Glacier</a> on the southeast flank of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Baker" title="Mount Baker">Mount Baker</a> retreated 8,700 feet (2,700 m). William Long of the United States Forest Service observed the glacier beginning to advance due to cooler/wetter weather in 1953. This was followed by a 743 metres (2,438 ft) advance by 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The glacier again retreated 450 m (1,480 ft) from 1987 to 2005, leaving barren terrain behind. This retreat has occurred during a period of reduced winter snowfall and higher summer temperatures. In this region of the Cascades, winter <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpack</a> has declined 25% since 1946, and summer temperatures have risen 0.7 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (1.2 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) during the same period. The reduced snowpack has occurred despite a small increase in winter precipitation—thus, it reflects warmer winter temperatures leading to rainfall and melting on glaciers even during the winter. As of 2005, 67% of the North Cascade glaciers observed are in disequilibrium and will not survive the continuation of the present climate. These glaciers will eventually disappear unless temperatures fall and frozen precipitation increases. The remaining glaciers are expected to stabilize, unless the climate continues to warm, but will be much reduced in size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Rocky_Mountains">U.S. Rocky Mountains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: U.S. Rocky Mountains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On the sheltered slopes of the highest peaks of <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eponymous" title="Eponymous">eponymous</a> glaciers are diminishing rapidly. The area of each glacier has been mapped for decades by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> and the U.S. Geological Survey. Comparing photographs from the mid-19th century with contemporary images provides ample evidence that they have retreated notably since 1850. Repeat photography since clearly show that glaciers such as <a href="/wiki/Grinnell_Glacier" title="Grinnell Glacier">Grinnell Glacier</a> are all retreating. The larger glaciers are now approximately a third of their former size when first studied in 1850, and numerous smaller glaciers have disappeared completely. Only 27% of the 99 km<sup>2</sup> (38 sq mi) area of Glacier National Park covered by glaciers in 1850 remained covered by 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Researchers believe that by the year 2030, the vast majority of glacial ice in Glacier National Park will be gone unless current climate patterns reverse their course.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Grinnell Glacier is just one of many glaciers in Glacier National Park that have been well documented by photographs for many decades. The photographs below clearly demonstrate the retreat of this glacier since 1938.
</p>

<p>The semiarid climate of <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> still manages to support about a dozen small glaciers within <a href="/wiki/Grand_Teton_National_Park" title="Grand Teton National Park">Grand Teton National Park</a>, which all show evidence of retreat over the past 50 years. <a href="/wiki/Schoolroom_Glacier" title="Schoolroom Glacier">Schoolroom Glacier</a> is located slightly southwest of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Teton" title="Grand Teton">Grand Teton</a> is one of the more easily reached glaciers in the park and it is expected to disappear by 2025.  Research between 1950 and 1999 demonstrated that the glaciers in <a href="/wiki/Bridger-Teton_National_Forest" title="Bridger-Teton National Forest">Bridger-Teton National Forest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoshone_National_Forest" title="Shoshone National Forest">Shoshone National Forest</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wind_River_Range" title="Wind River Range">Wind River Range</a> shrank by over a third of their size during that period. Photographs indicate that the glaciers today are only half the size as when first photographed in the late 1890s. Research also indicates that the glacial retreat was proportionately greater in the 1990s than in any other decade over the last 100 years. <a href="/wiki/Gannett_Glacier" title="Gannett Glacier">Gannett Glacier</a> on the northeast slope of <a href="/wiki/Gannett_Peak" title="Gannett Peak">Gannett Peak</a> is the largest single glacier in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> south of Canada. It has reportedly lost over 50% of its volume since 1920, with almost half of that loss occurring since 1980. Glaciologists believe the remaining glaciers in Wyoming will disappear by the middle of the 21st century if the current climate patterns continue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_Rockies_and_Coast_and_Columbia_Mountains">Canadian Rockies and Coast and Columbia Mountains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canadian Rockies and Coast and Columbia Mountains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Valdez_Glacier.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Valdez_Glacier.jpg/250px-Valdez_Glacier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Valdez_Glacier.jpg/375px-Valdez_Glacier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Valdez_Glacier.jpg/500px-Valdez_Glacier.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Valdez_Glacier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Valdez Glacier has thinned 90 m (300 ft) over the last century, exposing barren ground near the glacial margins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto5_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelto5-66">[66]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Rockies" title="Canadian Rockies">Canadian Rockies</a>, glaciers are generally larger and more widespread than to the south in the Rocky Mountains. One of the more accessible in the Canadian Rockies is the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athabasca" title="Mount Athabasca">Athabasca Glacier</a>, which is an outlet glacier of the 325 km<sup>2</sup> (125 sq mi) <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Icefield" title="Columbia Icefield">Columbia Icefield</a>. The Athabasca Glacier has retreated 1,500 m (4,900 ft) since the late 19th century. Its rate of retreat has increased since 1980, following a period of slow retreat from 1950 to 1980. The <a href="/wiki/Peyto_Glacier" title="Peyto Glacier">Peyto Glacier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> covers an area of about 12 km<sup>2</sup> (4.6 sq mi), and retreated rapidly during the first half of the 20th century, stabilized by 1966, and resumed shrinking in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Illecillewaet_Glacier" title="Illecillewaet Glacier">Illecillewaet Glacier</a> in British Columbia's <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(Canada)" title="Glacier National Park (Canada)">Glacier National Park (Canada)</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Selkirk_Mountains" title="Selkirk Mountains">Selkirk Mountains</a> (west of the Rockies) has retreated 2 km (1.2 mi) since first photographed in 1887.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Garibaldi_Provincial_Park" title="Garibaldi Provincial Park">Garibaldi Provincial Park</a> in Southwestern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> over 505 km<sup>2</sup> (195 sq mi), or 26%, of the park, was covered by glacier ice at the beginning of the 18th century. Ice cover decreased to 297 km<sup>2</sup> (115 sq mi) by 1987–1988 and to 245 km<sup>2</sup> (95 sq mi) by 2005, 50% of the 1850 area. The 50 km<sup>2</sup> (19 sq mi) loss in the last 20 years coincides with negative mass balance in the region. During this period all nine glaciers examined have retreated significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alaska">Alaska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alaska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacierbaymap.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="420" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Glacierbaymap.gif/220px-Glacierbaymap.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Glacierbaymap.gif/330px-Glacierbaymap.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/Glacierbaymap.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacierbaymap.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Glacier Bay. Red lines show glacial terminus positions and dates during retreat of the Little Ice Age glacier.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muir_Glacier_retreat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="602" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Muir_Glacier_retreat.png/220px-Muir_Glacier_retreat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Muir_Glacier_retreat.png/330px-Muir_Glacier_retreat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Muir_Glacier_retreat.png/440px-Muir_Glacier_retreat.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muir_Glacier_retreat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maps showing retreat of <a href="/wiki/Muir_Glacier" title="Muir Glacier">Muir Glacier</a> from 1941 to 1982</div></div></div>
<p>There are thousands of glaciers in Alaska but only few have been named. The <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Glacier_(Alaska)" title="Columbia Glacier (Alaska)">Columbia Glacier</a> near <a href="/wiki/Valdez,_Alaska" title="Valdez, Alaska">Valdez</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prince_William_Sound" title="Prince William Sound">Prince William Sound</a> has retreated 15 km (9.3 mi) in the last 25 years. Its calved icebergs partially caused the <a href="/wiki/Exxon_Valdez" title="Exxon Valdez">Exxon Valdez</a> oil spill, when the tanker changed course to avoid the ice tips. The Valdez Glacier is in the same area, and though it does not calve, has also retreated significantly. "A 2005 aerial survey of Alaskan coastal glaciers identified more than a dozen glaciers, many former tidewater and <a href="/wiki/Ice_calving" title="Ice calving">calving</a> glaciers, including Grand Plateau, Alsek, Bear, and Excelsior Glaciers that are rapidly retreating. Of 2,000 glaciers observed, 99% are retreating."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto5_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-pelto5-66">[66]</a></sup> Icy Bay in Alaska is fed by three large glaciers—Guyot, Yahtse, and Tyndall Glaciers—all of which have experienced a loss in length and thickness and, consequently, a loss in area. Tyndall Glacier became separated from the retreating Guyot Glacier in the 1960s and has retreated 24 km (15 mi) since, averaging more than 500 m (1,600 ft) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Juneau Icefield Research Program has monitored outlet glaciers of the <a href="/wiki/Juneau_Icefield" title="Juneau Icefield">Juneau Icefield</a> since 1946. On the west side of the ice field, the terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Mendenhall_Glacier" title="Mendenhall Glacier">Mendenhall Glacier</a>, which flows into suburban <a href="/wiki/Juneau,_Alaska" title="Juneau, Alaska">Juneau, Alaska</a>, has retreated 580 m (1,900 ft). Of the nineteen glaciers of the Juneau Icefield, eighteen are retreating, and one, the Taku Glacier, is advancing. Eleven of the glaciers have retreated more than 1 km (0.62 mi) since 1948 — Antler Glacier, 5.4 km (3.4 mi); Gilkey Glacier, 3.5 km (2.2 mi); Norris Glacier, 1.1 km (0.68 mi) and Lemon Creek Glacier, 1.5 km (0.93 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Taku Glacier has been advancing since at least 1890, when naturalist <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> observed a large iceberg calving front. By 1948 the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a> had filled in, and the glacier no longer calved and was able to continue its advance. By 2005 the glacier was only 1.5 km (0.93 mi) from reaching Taku Point and blocking <a href="/wiki/Taku_Inlet" title="Taku Inlet">Taku Inlet</a>. The advance of Taku Glacier averaged 17 m (56 ft) per year between 1988 and 2005. The mass balance was very positive for the 1946–88 period fueling the advance; however, since 1988 the mass balance has been slightly negative, which should in the future slow the advance of this mighty glacier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Long-term mass balance records from Lemon Creek Glacier in Alaska show slightly declining mass balance with time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The mean annual balance for this glacier was −0.23 m (0.75 ft) each year during the period of 1957 to 1976. Mean annual balance has been increasingly negatively averaging −1.04 m (3.4 ft) per year from 1990 to 2005. Repeat glacier altimetry, or altitude measuring, for 67 Alaska glaciers find rates of thinning have increased by more than a factor of two when comparing the periods from 1950 to 1995 (0.7 m (2.3 ft) per year) and 1995 to 2001 (1.8 m (5.9 ft) per year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> This is a systemic trend with loss in mass equating to loss in thickness, which leads to increasing retreat—the glaciers are not only retreating, but they are also becoming much thinner. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denali_National_Park" title="Denali National Park">Denali National Park</a>, all glaciers monitored are retreating, with an average retreat of 20 m (66 ft) per year. The terminus of the Toklat Glacier has been retreating 26 m (85 ft) per year and the Muldrow Glacier has thinned 20 m (66 ft) since 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Well documented in Alaska are <a href="/wiki/Surge_(glacier)" title="Surge (glacier)">surging</a> glaciers that have been known to rapidly advance, even as much as 100 m (330 ft) per day.  <a href="/wiki/Variegated_Glacier" title="Variegated Glacier">Variegated</a>, Black Rapids, Muldrow, Susitna and Yanert are examples of surging glaciers in Alaska that have made rapid advances in the past. These glaciers are all retreating overall, punctuated by short periods of advance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_hemisphere">Southern hemisphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Southern hemisphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Andes_and_Tierra_del_Fuego">Andes and Tierra del Fuego</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Andes and Tierra del Fuego">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="992" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/220px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/330px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif/440px-San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_Rafael_Glacier_animation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Retreat of <a href="/wiki/San_Rafael_Glacier" title="San Rafael Glacier">San Rafael Glacier</a> from 1990 to 2000. <a href="/wiki/San_Quint%C3%ADn_Glacier" title="San Quintín Glacier">San Quintín Glacier</a> is shown in the background</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dry_Andes" title="Dry Andes">Dry Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wet_Andes" title="Wet Andes">Wet Andes</a></div>
<p>A large region of population surrounding the central and southern Andes of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> reside in arid areas that are dependent on water supplies from melting glaciers. The water from the glaciers also supplies rivers that have in some cases been dammed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> power. Some researchers believe that by 2030, many of the large ice caps on the highest Andes will be gone if current climate trends continue. In Patagonia on the southern tip of the continent, the large ice caps have retreated a 1 km (0.62 mi) since the early 1990s and 10 km (6.2 mi) since the late 19th century. It has also been observed that Patagonian glaciers are receding at a faster rate than in any other world region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Patagonian_Ice_Field" title="Northern Patagonian Ice Field">Northern Patagonian Ice Field</a> lost 93 km<sup>2</sup> (36 sq mi) of glacier area during the years between 1945 and 1975, and 174 km<sup>2</sup> (67 sq mi) from 1975 to 1996, which indicates that the rate of retreat is increasing. This represents a loss of 8% of the ice field, with all glaciers experiencing significant retreat. The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Patagonian_Ice_Field" title="Southern Patagonian Ice Field">Southern Patagonian Ice Field</a> has exhibited a general trend of retreat on 42 glaciers, while four glaciers were in equilibrium and two advanced during the years between 1944 and 1986.  The largest retreat was on <a href="/wiki/O%27Higgins_Glacier" title="O'Higgins Glacier">O'Higgins Glacier</a>, which during the period 1896–1995 retreated 14.6 km (9.1 mi). The <a href="/wiki/Perito_Moreno_Glacier" title="Perito Moreno Glacier">Perito Moreno Glacier</a> is 30 km (19 mi) long and is a major outflow glacier of the Patagonian ice sheet, as well as the most visited glacier in Patagonia. Perito Moreno Glacier is in equilibrium, but has undergone frequent oscillations in the period 1947–96, with a net gain of 4.1 km (2.5 mi). This glacier has advanced since 1947, and has been essentially stable since 1992. Perito Moreno Glacier is one of three glaciers in Patagonia known to have advanced, compared to several hundred others in retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The two major glaciers of the Southern Patagonia Icefield to the north of Moreno, Upsala and Viedma Glacier have retreated 4.6 km (2.9 mi) in 21 years and 1 km (0.62 mi) in 13 years respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>  In the <a href="/wiki/Aconcagua_River" title="Aconcagua River">Aconcagua River</a> Basin, glacier retreat has resulted in a 20% loss in glacier area, declining from 151 km<sup>2</sup> (58 sq mi) to 121 km<sup>2</sup> (47 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Marinelli_Glacier" title="Marinelli Glacier">Marinelli Glacier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> has been in retreat since at least 1960 through 2008.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tasman_Glacier.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="992" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tasman_Glacier.gif/250px-Tasman_Glacier.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tasman_Glacier.gif/375px-Tasman_Glacier.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Tasman_Glacier.gif/500px-Tasman_Glacier.gif 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tasman_Glacier.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>These glaciers in New Zealand have continued to retreat rapidly in recent years.  Notice the larger terminal lakes, the retreat of the white ice (ice free of moraine cover), and the higher moraine walls due to ice thinning. <a href="/wiki/File:DSCN5238-muller-moraine_b.jpg" title="File:DSCN5238-muller-moraine b.jpg">Photo.</a></div></div></div>
<p>In New Zealand, mountain glaciers have been in general retreat since 1890, with an acceleration since 1920. Most have measurably thinned and reduced in size, and the snow accumulation zones have risen in elevation as the 20th century progressed. Between 1971–75 Ivory Glacier receded 30 m (98 ft) from the glacial terminus, and about 26% of its surface area was lost. Since 1980 numerous small glacial lakes formed behind the new terminal moraines of several of these glaciers. Glaciers such as Classen, Godley and Douglas now all have new glacial lakes below their terminal locations due to the glacial retreat over the past 20 years. Satellite imagery indicates that these lakes are continuing to expand. There has been significant and ongoing ice volume losses on the largest New Zealand glaciers, including the <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Glacier" title="Tasman Glacier">Tasman</a>, Ivory, Classen, <a href="/wiki/Mueller_Glacier" title="Mueller Glacier">Mueller</a>, Maud, <a href="/wiki/Hooker_Glacier_(New_Zealand)" title="Hooker Glacier (New Zealand)">Hooker</a>, Grey, Godley, Ramsay, <a href="/wiki/Murchison_Glacier" title="Murchison Glacier">Murchison</a>, Therma, <a href="/wiki/Volta_Glacier" title="Volta Glacier">Volta</a> and Douglas Glaciers. The retreat of these glaciers has been marked by expanding proglacial lakes and terminus region thinning.  The loss in Southern Alps total ice volume from 1976–2014 is 34 percent of the total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Several glaciers, notably the much-visited <a href="/wiki/Fox_Glacier" title="Fox Glacier">Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Glacier" title="Franz Josef Glacier">Franz Josef Glaciers</a> on New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/West_Coast,_New_Zealand" title="West Coast, New Zealand">West Coast</a>, have periodically advanced, especially during the 1990s, but the scale of these advances is small when compared to 20th-century retreat. Both are more than 2.5 km (1.6 mi) shorter than a century ago. These large, rapidly flowing glaciers situated on steep slopes have been very reactive to small mass-balance changes. A few years of conditions favorable to glacier advance, such as more westerly winds and a resulting increase in snowfall, are rapidly echoed in a corresponding advance, followed by equally rapid retreat when those favorable conditions end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The glaciers that have been advancing in a few locations in New Zealand have been doing so due to transient local weather conditions, which have brought more precipitation and cloudier, cooler summers since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p><div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_glaciers">Tropical glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tropical glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">Tropical</a> glaciers are located between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>, in the region that lies <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">23° 26′ 22″</a> north or south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. Strictly, a tropical glacier is located within the astronomical <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>; the area where the annual temperature variation is less than the daily variation, and is within the oscillation area of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tropical glaciers are the most uncommon of all glaciers for a variety of reasons. Firstly, the  regions are the warmest part of the planet. Secondly, the seasonal change is minimal with temperatures warm year round, resulting in a lack of a colder winter season in which snow and ice can accumulate. Thirdly, few taller mountains exist in these regions upon which enough cold air exists for the establishment of glaciers. Overall, tropical glaciers are smaller than those found elsewhere and are the most likely glaciers to show rapid response to changing climate patterns. A small temperature increase of only a few degrees can have almost immediate and adverse effect on tropical glaciers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Near the Equator, ice is still found in East Africa, the Andes of South America and New Guinea.  The retreat of equatorial glaciers has been documented via maps and photographs covering the period from the late 1800s to nearly the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> 99.64% of tropical glaciers are in Andean mountains of South America, 0.25% on the African glaciers of Rwenzori, Mount Kenya and Kilimanjaro, and 0.11% in the Irian Jaya region in New Guinea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="802" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG/250px-Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG/375px-Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG/500px-Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_at_summit_of_Mt_Kilimanjaro_001.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Furtw%C3%A4ngler_Glacier" title="Furtwängler Glacier">Furtwängler Glacier</a> atop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilimanjaro" title="Kilimanjaro">Kilimanjaro</a> in the foreground and snowfields and the Northern Icefields beyond.</div></div></div>
<p>Almost all Africa is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> climate zones. Its glaciers are found only in two isolated ranges and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruwenzori_Range" title="Ruwenzori Range">Ruwenzori Range</a>. Kilimanjaro, at 5,895 m (19,341 ft), is the highest peak on the continent. Since 1912 the glacier cover on the summit of Kilimanjaro has apparently retreated 75%, and the volume of glacial ice is now 80% less than it was a century ago due to both retreat and thinning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilimanjaro4_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-kilimanjaro4-86">[86]</a></sup>  In the 14-year period from 1984 to 1998, one section of the glacier atop the mountain receded 300 m (980 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> A 2002 study determined that if current conditions continue, the glaciers atop Kilimanjaro will disappear sometime between 2015 and 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> A March 2005 report indicated that there is almost no remaining glacial ice on the mountain, and it is the first time in 11,000 years that barren ground has been exposed on portions of the summit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Researchers reported Kilimanjaro's glacier retreat was due to a combination of increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a> and decreased snow fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mote-kaser_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mote-kaser-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Furtw%C3%A4ngler_Glacier" title="Furtwängler Glacier">Furtwängler Glacier</a> is located near the summit of Kilimanjaro. Between 1976 and 2000, the area of Furtwängler Glacier was cut almost in half, from 113,000 m<sup>2</sup> (1,220,000 sq ft) to 60,000 m<sup>2</sup> (650,000 sq ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> During fieldwork conducted early in 2006, scientists discovered a large hole near the center of the glacier. This hole, extending through the 6 m (20 ft) remaining thickness of the glacier to the underlying rock, was expected to grow and split the glacier in two by 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilimanjaro4_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-kilimanjaro4-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>To the north of Kilimanjaro lies <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kenya" title="Mount Kenya">Mount Kenya</a>, which at 5,199 m (17,057 ft) is the second tallest mountain on the  continent. Mount Kenya has a number of small glaciers that have lost at least 45% of their mass since the middle of the 20th century. According to research compiled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Geological_Survey" title="U.S. Geological Survey">U.S. Geological Survey</a> (USGS), there were eighteen glaciers atop Mount Kenya in 1900, and by 1986 only eleven remained. The total area covered by glaciers was 1.6 km<sup>2</sup> (0.62 sq mi) in 1900, however by the year 2000 only about 25%, or 0.4 km<sup>2</sup> (0.15 sq mi) remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> To the west of Mounts Kilimanjaro and Kenya, the Ruwenzori Range rises to 5,109 m (16,762 ft). Photographic evidence indicates a marked reduction in glacially covered areas over the past century. In the 35-year period between 1955 and 1990, glaciers on the <a href="/wiki/Rwenzori_Mountains" title="Rwenzori Mountains">Rwenzori Mountains</a> receded about 40%. It is expected that due to their proximity to the heavy moisture of the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo</a> region, the glaciers in the Ruwenzori Range may recede at a slower rate than those on Kilimanjaro or in Kenya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study by glaciologists of two small glaciers in South America reveals another retreat. More than 80% of all glacial ice in the northern Andes is concentrated on the highest peaks in small plains of approximately 1 km<sup>2</sup> (0.39 sq mi) in size. A 1992 to 1998 observation of the <a href="/wiki/Chacaltaya" title="Chacaltaya">Chacaltaya</a> Glacier in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> and Antizana Glacier in <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> indicate that between 0.6 m (2.0 ft) and 1.9 m (6.2 ft) of ice was lost per year on each glacier. Figures for Chacaltaya show a loss of 67% of its volume and 40% of its thickness over the same period. Chacaltaya Glacier has lost 90% of its mass since 1940 and was expected to disappear altogether sometime between 2010 and 2015. Antizana is also reported to have lost 40% of its surface area between 1979 and 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>  Research also indicates that since the mid-1980s, the rate of retreat for both of these glaciers has been increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, the glaciers atop <a href="/wiki/Nevado_del_Ruiz" title="Nevado del Ruiz">Nevado del Ruiz</a> have lost more than half their area in the last 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Further south in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, the Andes are at a higher altitude overall, and there are approximately 722 glaciers covering an area of 723 km<sup>2</sup> (279 sq mi).
</p><p>Research in the region has been less extensive but indicates an overall recline of 7% between 1977 and 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quelccaya_Ice_Cap" title="Quelccaya Ice Cap">Quelccaya Ice Cap</a> is the second largest tropical icecap in the world after the <a href="/wiki/Coropuna" title="Coropuna">Coropuna</a> ice cap,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> and all of the outlet glaciers from the icecap are retreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> In the case of <a href="/wiki/Qori_Kalis_Glacier" title="Qori Kalis Glacier">Qori Kalis Glacier</a>, which is one of Quelccayas' outlet glaciers, the rate of retreat had reached 155 m (509 ft) per year during the three-year period of 1995 to 1998. The melting ice has formed a large lake at the front of the glacier since 1983, and bare ground has been exposed for the first time in thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania_2">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_Carstensz" title="Jan Carstensz">Jan Carstensz</a>'s 1623 report of glaciers covering the <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_of_New_Guinea" title="List of highest mountains of New Guinea">equatorial mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> was originally met with ridicule, but in the early 20th century at least five subranges of the <a href="/wiki/Maoke_Mountains" title="Maoke Mountains">Maoke Mountains</a> (meaning "Snowy Mountains") were indeed still found to be covered with large ice caps.  Due to the location of the island within the tropical zone, there is little to no seasonal variation in temperature. The tropical location has a predictably steady level of rain and snowfall, as well as cloud cover year round, and there has been no noticeable change in the amount of moisture which has fallen during the 20th century.
</p><p>In 1913, 4,550 m (14,930 ft) high Prins Hendrik peaks (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puncak_Yamin" title="Puncak Yamin">Puncak Yamin</a>) was named and reported to have "eternal" snow, but this observation was never repeated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> The ice cap of 4,720 m (15,490 ft) <a href="/wiki/Puncak_Trikora" title="Puncak Trikora">Wilhelmina Peaks</a>, which reached below 4,400 m (14,400 ft) in 1909, vanished between 1939 and 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Puncak_Mandala" title="Puncak Mandala">Mandala / Juliana</a> ice cap disappeared in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and the Idenburg glacier on <a href="/wiki/Ngga_Pilimsit" title="Ngga Pilimsit">Ngga Pilimsit</a> dried up in 2003. This leaves only the remnants of the once continuous icecap on New Guinea's highest mountain, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Carstensz" title="Mount Carstensz">Mount Carstensz</a> with the 4,884 m (16,024 ft) high <a href="/wiki/Puncak_Jaya" title="Puncak Jaya">Puncak Jaya</a> summit, which is estimated to have had an area of 20 km<sup>2</sup> (7.7 sq mi) in 1850.
</p><p>For this mountain there is photographic evidence of massive glacial retreat since the region was first extensively explored by airplane in 1936 in preparation for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carstensz_Expedition" title="Carstensz Expedition">peak's first ascent</a>. Between then and 2010, the mountain lost 80 percent of its ice — two-thirds of which since another scientific expedition in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> That research between 1973 and 1976 showed glacier retreat for the Meren Glacier of 200 m (660 ft) while the <a href="/wiki/Carstensz_Glacier" title="Carstensz Glacier">Carstensz Glacier</a> lost 50 m (160 ft). The Northwall Firn, the largest remnant of the icecap that once was atop <a href="/wiki/Puncak_Jaya" title="Puncak Jaya">Puncak Jaya</a>, has itself split into two separate glaciers after 1942. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IKONOS" title="IKONOS">IKONOS</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> of the New Guinean glaciers indicated that by 2002 only 2.1 km<sup>2</sup> (0.81 sq mi) glacial area remained, that in the two years from 2000 to 2002, the <a href="/wiki/East_Northwall_Firn" title="East Northwall Firn">East Northwall Firn</a> had lost 4.5%, the <a href="/wiki/West_Northwall_Firn" title="West Northwall Firn">West Northwall Firn</a> 19.4% and the Carstensz 6.8% of their glacial mass, and that sometime between 1994 and 2000, the Meren Glacier had disappeared altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> An expedition to the remaining glaciers on Puncak Jaya in 2010 discovered that the ice on the glaciers there is about 32 metres (105 ft) thick and thinning at a rate of 7 metres (23 ft) annually. At that rate, the remaining glaciers were expected to last only to the year 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_regions">Polar regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Polar regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite their proximity and importance to human populations, the mountain and valley glaciers of tropical and mid-latitude glaciers amount to only a small fraction of glacial ice on the Earth. About 99 percent of all freshwater ice is in the great ice sheets of polar and subpolar <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. These continuous continental-scale ice sheets, 3 km (1.9 mi) or more in thickness, cap much of the polar and subpolar land masses. Like rivers flowing from an enormous lake, numerous outlet glaciers transport ice from the margins of the ice sheet to the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Iceland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The northern Atlantic island nation of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> is home to <a href="/wiki/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull" title="Vatnajökull">Vatnajökull</a>, which is the largest ice cap in Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Brei%C3%B0amerkurj%C3%B6kull" title="Breiðamerkurjökull">Breiðamerkurjökull</a> glacier is one of Vatnajökull's outlet glaciers, and receded by as much as 2 km (1.2 mi) between 1973 and 2004. In the early 20th century, Breiðamerkurjökull extended to within 250 m (820 ft) of the ocean, but by 2004 its terminus had retreated 3 km (1.9 mi) further inland. This glacier retreat exposed a rapidly expanding lagoon, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6kuls%C3%A1rl%C3%B3n" title="Jökulsárlón">Jökulsárlón</a>, which is filled with icebergs calved from its front. Jökulsárlón is 110 m (360 ft) deep and nearly doubled its size between 1994 and 2004. Mass-balance measurements of Iceland's glaciers show alternating positive and negative mass balance of glaciers during the period 1987–95, but the mass balance has been predominantly negative since. On Hofsjökull ice cap, mass balance has been negative each year from 1995–2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the Icelandic glaciers retreated rapidly during the warm decades from 1930 to 1960, slowing down as the climate cooled during the following decade, and started to advance after 1970. The rate of advance peaked in the 1980s, after which it slowed down until about 1990. As a consequence of rapid warming of the climate that has taken place since the mid-1980s, most glaciers in Iceland began to retreat after 1990, and by 2000 all monitored non-surge type glaciers in Iceland were retreating. An average of 45 non-surging termini were monitored each year by the Icelandic Glaciological Society from 2000–2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ward_Hunt_Island" title="Ward Hunt Island">Ward Hunt Island</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bylot_Islandedit.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="600" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bylot_Islandedit.png/280px-Bylot_Islandedit.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bylot_Islandedit.png/420px-Bylot_Islandedit.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Bylot_Islandedit.png/560px-Bylot_Islandedit.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bylot_Islandedit.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bylot Ice Cap on Bylot Island, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_islands" title="Canadian Arctic islands">Canadian Arctic islands</a>, August 14, 1975 (USGS)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_islands" title="Canadian Arctic islands">Canadian Arctic islands</a> contain the largest area and volume of land ice on Earth outside of the Greenland and Antarctic Ice Sheets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> and is home to a number of substantial ice caps, including <a href="/wiki/Penny_Ice_Cap" title="Penny Ice Cap">Penny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnes_Ice_Cap" title="Barnes Ice Cap">Barnes</a> ice caps on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, Bylot Ice Cap on <a href="/wiki/Bylot_Island" title="Bylot Island">Bylot Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Devon_Ice_Cap" title="Devon Ice Cap">Devon Ice Cap</a> on <a href="/wiki/Devon_Island" title="Devon Island">Devon Island</a>. Glaciers in the Canadian Arctic were near equilibrium between 1960 and 2000, losing 23 Gt of ice per year between 1995 and 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Since this time, Canadian Arctic glaciers have experienced a sharp increase in mass loss in response to warmer summer temperature, losing 92 Gt per year between 2007 and 2009 .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other studies show that between 1960 and 1999, the Devon Ice Cap lost 67 km<sup>3</sup> (16 cu mi) of ice, mainly through thinning. All major outlet glaciers along the eastern Devon Ice Cap margin have retreated from 1 km (0.62 mi) to 3 km (1.9 mi) since 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> On the Hazen Plateau of <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, the Simmon Ice Cap has lost 47% of its area since 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> If the current climatic conditions continue, the remaining glacial ice on the Hazen Plateau will be gone around 2050.  On August 13, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Ayles_Ice_Shelf" title="Ayles Ice Shelf">Ayles Ice Shelf</a> broke free from the north coast of Ellesmere Island. The 66 km<sup>2</sup> (25 sq mi) ice shelf drifted into the Arctic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>  This followed the splitting of the Ward Hunt Ice Shelf in 2002.  The Ward Hunt has lost 90% of its area in the last century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Europe">Northern Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Northern Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arctic islands north of Norway, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and Russia have all shown evidence of glacier retreat. In the <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>, the island of <a href="/wiki/Spitsbergen" title="Spitsbergen">Spitsbergen</a> has numerous glaciers. Research indicates that Hansbreen (Hans Glacier) on Spitsbergen retreated 1.4 km (0.87 mi) from 1936 to 1982 and another 400 m (1,300 ft) during the 16-year period from 1982 to 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> Blomstrandbreen, a glacier in the King's Bay area of Spitsbergen, has retreated approximately 2 km (1.2 mi) in the past 80 years. Since 1960 the average retreat of Blomstrandbreen has been about 35 m (115 ft) a year, and this average was enhanced due to an accelerated rate of retreat since 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Similarly, Midre Lovenbreen retreated 200 m (660 ft) between 1977 and 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> archipelago north of Russia, research indicates that in 1952 there was 208 km (129 mi) of glacier ice along the coast. By 1993 this had been reduced by 8% to 198 km (123 mi) of glacier coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland">Greenland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Greenland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier,_Greenland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="868" data-file-width="540" height="482" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg/300px-Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg/450px-Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier,_Greenland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Retreat of the Helheim Glacier, Greenland</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, glacier retreat has been observed in outlet glaciers, resulting in an increase of the ice flow rate and destabilization of the mass balance of the ice sheet that is their source. The net loss in volume and hence sea level contribution of the Greenland Ice Sheet (GIS) has doubled in recent years from 90 km<sup>3</sup> (22 cu mi) per year in 1996 to 220 km<sup>3</sup> (53 cu mi) per year in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> Researchers also noted that the acceleration was widespread affecting almost all glaciers south of 70 N by 2005. The period since 2000 has brought retreat to several very large glaciers that had long been stable. Three glaciers that have been researched—<a href="/wiki/Helheim_Glacier" title="Helheim Glacier">Helheim Glacier</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kangerdlugssuaq_Glacier" title="Kangerdlugssuaq Glacier">Kangerdlugssuaq Glacier</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakobshavn_Isbr%C3%A6" title="Jakobshavn Isbræ">Jakobshavn Isbræ</a>—jointly drain more than 16% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenland_Ice_Sheet" title="Greenland Ice Sheet">Greenland Ice Sheet</a>. In the case of Helheim Glacier, researchers used satellite images to determine the movement and retreat of the glacier. Satellite images and aerial photographs from the 1950s and 1970s show that the front of the glacier had remained in the same place for decades. In 2001 the glacier began retreating rapidly, and by 2005 the glacier had retreated a total of 7.2 km (4.5 mi), accelerating from 20 m (66 ft) per day to 35 m (115 ft) per day during that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jakobshavn Isbræ in west Greenland, a major outlet glacier of the Greenland Ice Sheet, has been the fastest moving glacier in the world over the past half century. It had been moving continuously at speeds of over 24 m (79 ft) per day with a stable terminus since at least 1950. In 2002 the 12 km (7.5 mi) long floating terminus of the glacier entered a phase of rapid retreat, with the ice front breaking up and the floating terminus disintegrating and  accelerating to a retreat rate of over 30 m (98 ft) per day. On a shorter timescale, portions of the main trunk of Kangerdlugssuaq Glacier that were flowing at 15 m (49 ft) per day from 1988 to 2001 were measured to be flowing at 40 m (130 ft) per day in the summer of 2005. Not only has Kangerdlugssuaq retreated, it has also thinned by more than 100 m (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rapid thinning, acceleration and retreat of Helheim, Jakobshavns and Kangerdlugssuaq glaciers in Greenland, all in close association with one another, suggests a common triggering mechanism, such as enhanced surface melting due to regional climate warming or a change in forces at the glacier front. The enhanced melting leading to lubrication of the glacier base has been observed to cause a small seasonal velocity increase and the release of meltwater lakes has also led to only small short term accelerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> The significant accelerations noted on the three largest glaciers began at the calving front and propagated inland and are not seasonal in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenland-pelto_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenland-pelto-125">[125]</a></sup> Thus, the primary source of outlet glacier acceleration widely observed on small and large calving glaciers in Greenland is driven by changes in dynamic forces at the glacier front, not enhanced meltwater lubrication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenland-pelto_125-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenland-pelto-125">[125]</a></sup>  This was termed the <i>Jakobshavns Effect</i> by Terence Hughes at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maine" title="University of Maine">University of Maine</a> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Indeed, a study published in 2015 on glacial underwater topography at 3 sites found cavities, due to warm subglacial water intrusion, which has been identified as a possible dominant force for ablation (surface erosion). Thus, suggests ocean temperature controls ice sheet surface runoff at specific sites. These findings also show that models underestimate the sensitivity of Greenland glaciers to ocean warming and resulting ice sheet runoff. Hence, without better modelling, new observations suggest that past projections of sea level rise attribution from the Greenland Ice Sheet require upward revision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antarctica">Antarctica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Antarctica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_glaciers_in_the_Antarctic" title="List of glaciers in the Antarctic">List of glaciers in the Antarctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Antarctic_ice_shelves" title="List of Antarctic ice shelves">List of Antarctic ice shelves</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="498" data-file-width="540" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg/250px-Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg/375px-Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg/500px-Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Larsen_B_Collapse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The collapsing Larsen B Ice Shelf in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is similar in area to the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Antarctica" title="Climate of Antarctica">Antarctica</a> is intensely cold and arid. Most of the world's freshwater ice is contained within its sheets. Its most dramatic example of glacier retreat is the loss of large sections of the <a href="/wiki/Larsen_Ice_Shelf" title="Larsen Ice Shelf">Larsen Ice Shelf</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Peninsula" title="Antarctic Peninsula">Antarctic Peninsula</a>. The recent collapse of Wordie Ice Shelf, Prince Gustav Ice Shelf, Mueller Ice Shelf, Jones Ice Shelf, Larsen-A and Larsen-B Ice Shelf on the Antarctic Peninsula has raised awareness of how dynamic ice shelf systems are.
</p><p>The Antarctic sheet is the largest known single mass of ice. It covers almost 14 million km<sup>2</sup> and some 30 million km<sup>3</sup> of ice. Around 90% of the fresh water on the planet's surface is held in this area and if melted would raise sea levels by 58 metres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-128">[128]</a></sup> The continent-wide average surface temperature trend of Antarctica is positive and significant at &gt;0.05 °C/decade since 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SteigSchneider2009_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-SteigSchneider2009-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Antarctic sheet is divided by the <a href="/wiki/Transantarctic_Mountains" title="Transantarctic Mountains">Transantarctic Mountains</a> into two unequal sections known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="East Antarctic ice sheet">East Antarctic ice sheet</a> (EAIS) and the smaller <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="West Antarctic Ice Sheet">West Antarctic Ice Sheet</a> (WAIS). The EAIS rests on a major land mass but the bed of the WAIS is, in places, more than 2,500 metres below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. It would be <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a> if the ice sheet were not there. The WAIS is classified as a marine-based ice sheet, meaning that its bed lies below sea level and its edges flow into floating ice shelves. The WAIS is bounded by the <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronne_Ice_Shelf" title="Ronne Ice Shelf">Ronne Ice Shelf</a>, and outlet glaciers that drain into the <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a>.
</p><p>Ice shelves are not stable when surface melting occurs, and the collapse of Larsen Ice Shelf has been caused by warmer melt season temperatures that have led to surface melting and the formation of shallow ponds of water on the ice shelf. The Larsen Ice Shelf lost 2,500 km<sup>2</sup> (970 sq mi) of its area from 1995 to 2001. In a 35-day period beginning on January 31, 2002, about 3,250 km<sup>2</sup> (1,250 sq mi) of shelf area disintegrated. The ice shelf is now 40% the size of its previous minimum stable extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> In 2015 a study concluded that the remaining <i>Larsen B</i> ice-shelf will disintegrate by the end of the decade, based on observations of faster flow and rapid thinning of glaciers in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> Jones Ice Shelf had an area of 35 km<sup>2</sup> (14 sq mi) in the 1970s but by 2008 it had disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apis_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-apis-132">[132]</a></sup> Wordie Ice Shelf has gone from an area of 1,500 km<sup>2</sup> (580 sq mi) in 1950 to 1,400 km<sup>2</sup> (540 sq mi) in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apis_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-apis-132">[132]</a></sup> Prince Gustav Ice Shelf has gone from an area of 1,600 km<sup>2</sup> (620 sq mi) to 1,100 km<sup>2</sup> (420 sq mi) in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apis_132-2"><a href="#cite_note-apis-132">[132]</a></sup> After their loss the reduced buttressing of feeder glaciers has allowed the expected speed-up of inland ice masses after shelf ice break-up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RignotCasassa2004_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-RignotCasassa2004-133">[133]</a></sup> The <b>Ross Ice Shelf</b> is the largest ice shelf of Antarctica (an area of roughly 487,000 square kilometres (188,000 sq mi) and about 800 kilometres (500 mi) across: about the size of France).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-134">[134]</a></sup> Wilkins Ice Shelf is another ice shelf that has suffered substantial retreat. The ice shelf had an area of 16,000 km<sup>2</sup> (6,200 sq mi) in 1998 when 1,000 km<sup>2</sup> (390 sq mi) was lost that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>  In 2007 and 2008 significant rifting developed and led to the loss of another 1,400 km<sup>2</sup> (540 sq mi) of area and some of the calving occurred in the Austral winter. The calving seemed to have resulted from preconditioning such as thinning, possibly due to basal melt, as surface melt was not as evident, leading to a reduction in the strength of the pinning point connections. The thinner ice then experienced spreading rifts and breakup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto7_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelto7-136">[136]</a></sup> This period culminated in the collapse of an ice bridge connecting the main ice shelf to Charcot Island leading to the loss of an additional 700 km<sup>2</sup> (270 sq mi) between February and June 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dakshin Gangotri Glacier, a small outlet glacier of the Antarctic ice sheet, receded at an average rate of 0.7 m (2.3 ft) per year from 1983 to 2002. On the Antarctic Peninsula, which is the only section of Antarctica that extends well north of the Antarctic Circle, there are hundreds of retreating glaciers. In one study of 244 glaciers on the peninsula, 212 have retreated an average of 600 m (2,000 ft) from where they were when first measured in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> Pine Island Glacier, an Antarctic outflow glacier that flows into the <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a>. A study from 1998 concluded that the glacier thinned 3.5 m (11 ft)± 0.9 m (3.0 ft) per year and retreated a total of 5 km (3.1 mi) in 3.8 years. The terminus of the Pine Island Glacier is a floating ice shelf, and the point at which it starts to float retreated 1.2 km (0.75 mi) per year from 1992 to 1996. This glacier drains a substantial portion of the <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="West Antarctic Ice Sheet">West Antarctic Ice Sheet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rignot1998_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rignot1998-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in 2014 found, rapid grounding line retreat in the years 1992-2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rignot2014_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rignot2014-140">[140]</a></sup> Based on a study from 2005, the greatest retreat was seen in Sjogren Glacier, which is now 13 km (8.1 mi) further inland than where it was in 1953. There are 32 glaciers that were measured to have advanced; however, these glaciers showed only a modest advance averaging 300 m (980 ft) per glacier, which is significantly smaller than the massive retreat observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> Thwaites Glacier, which has also shown evidence of thinning, has been referred to as the weak underbelly of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rignot1998_139-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rignot1998-139">[139]</a></sup> A study published in 2014 found rapid grounding line retreat in the years 1992–2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rignot2014_140-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rignot2014-140">[140]</a></sup> Totten Glacier, is a large glacier draining a major portion of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet. A study in 2008 concluded that Totten Glacier is currently losing mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup>  A study published in 2015 concluded that Totten Glacier,  has the largest contribution of ice thinning rate on the East Antarctic continent, and that the thinning is driven by enhanced basal melting, because of ocean processes, and affected by <a href="/wiki/Polynya" title="Polynya">polynya</a> activity. Additionally, warm Circumpolar Deep Water, has been observed during summer and winter months at the nearby continental shelf below 400 to 500 meters of cool Antarctic Surface Water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_glacier_retreat">Effects of glacier retreat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Effects of glacier retreat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">Deglaciation</a></div>
<p>The continued retreat of glaciers will have a number of different quantitative effects. In areas that are heavily dependent on water runoff from glaciers that melt during the warmer summer months, a continuation of the current retreat will eventually deplete the glacial ice and substantially reduce or eliminate runoff. A reduction in runoff will affect the ability to <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigate</a> crops and will reduce summer stream flows necessary to keep dams and reservoirs replenished. This situation is particularly acute for irrigation in South America, where numerous artificial lakes are filled almost exclusively by glacial melt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Asian" title="Central Asian">Central Asian</a> countries have also been historically dependent on the seasonal glacier melt water for irrigation and drinking supplies. In Norway, the Alps, and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> of North America, glacier runoff is important for hydropower.
</p><p>Some of this retreat has resulted in efforts to slow down the loss of glaciers in the Alps. To retard melting of the glaciers used by certain Austrian ski resorts, portions of the <a href="/wiki/Stubai_Alps" title="Stubai Alps">Stubai</a> and Pitztal Glaciers were partially covered with plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> In Switzerland plastic sheeting is also used to reduce the melt of glacial ice used as ski slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> While covering glaciers with plastic sheeting may prove advantageous to ski resorts on a small scale, this practice is not expected to be economically practical on a much larger scale.
</p><p>Many species of freshwater and saltwater plants and animals are dependent on glacier-fed waters to ensure the cold water habitat to which they have adapted. Some species of freshwater fish need cold water to survive and to reproduce, and this is especially true with <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cutthroat_trout" title="Cutthroat trout">cutthroat trout</a>. Reduced glacial runoff can lead to insufficient stream flow to allow these species to thrive.  Alterations to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" title="Ocean currents">ocean currents</a>, due to increased freshwater inputs from glacier melt, and the potential alterations to <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a>, may affect existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> upon which humans depend as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup>
</p><p>One major concern is the increased risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Outburst_Flood" title="Glacial Lake Outburst Flood">Glacial Lake Outburst Floods</a> (GLOF), which have in the past had great effect on lives and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep2_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-unep2-148">[148]</a></sup> Glacier meltwater left behind by the retreating glacier is often held back by <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> that can be unstable and have been known to collapse if breached or displaced by earthquakes, landslides or avalanches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> If the terminal moraine is not strong enough to hold the rising water behind it, it can burst, leading to a massive localized flood. The likelihood of such events is rising due to the creation and expansion of glacial lakes resulting from glacier retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unep2_148-1"><a href="#cite_note-unep2-148">[148]</a></sup> Past floods have been deadly and have resulted in enormous property damage. Towns and villages in steep, narrow valleys that are downstream from glacial lakes are at the greatest risk. In 1892 a GLOF released some 200,000 m<sup>3</sup> (260,000 cu yd) of water from the lake of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glacier_de_T%C3%AAte_Rousse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glacier de Tête Rousse (page does not exist)">Glacier de Tête Rousse</a>, resulting in the deaths of 200 people in the French town of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Gervais-les-Bains" title="Saint-Gervais-les-Bains">Saint-Gervais-les-Bains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelto5_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-pelto5-66">[66]</a></sup> GLOFs have been known to occur in every region of the world where glaciers are located. Continued glacier retreat is expected to create and expand glacial lakes, increasing the danger of future GLOFs.
</p><p>The potential for major <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> depends mostly on a significant melting of the polar ice caps of Greenland and Antarctica, as this is where the vast majority of glacial ice is located. If all the ice on the polar ice caps were to melt away, the oceans of the world would rise an estimated 70 m (230 ft). Although previously it was thought that the polar ice caps were not contributing heavily to sea level rise (IPCC 2007), recent studies have confirmed that both Antarctica and Greenland are contributing 0.5 millimetres (0.020 in) a year each to global sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17272686_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17272686-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> The fact that the IPCC estimates did not include rapid ice sheet decay into their sea level predictions makes it difficult to ascertain a plausible estimate for sea level rise but recent studies find that the minimum sea level rise will be around 0.8 metres (2.6 ft) by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18772435_153-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18772435-153">[153]</a></sup>
</p>
