<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beer</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the alcoholic drink. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Beer_(disambiguation)" title="Beer (disambiguation)">Beer (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:284px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GravityTap.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="282" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/78/GravityTap.jpg" width="282"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GravityTap.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Schlenkerla" title="Schlenkerla">Schlenkerla</a> Rauchbier, a traditional <a href="/wiki/Smoked_beer" title="Smoked beer">smoked beer</a>,  being poured from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cask" title="Cask">cask</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Beer</b> is one of the oldest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and most widely consumed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_drink" title="Alcoholic drink">alcoholic drinks</a> in the world, and the third most popular drink overall after <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Beer is <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewed</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal grains</a>—most commonly from <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> (corn), and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> are also used. During the brewing process, <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Fermenting" title="Brewing">fermentation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a> in the resulting beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barth_2014_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barth_2014-6">[6]</a></sup> Most modern beer is brewed with <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>, which add bitterness and other flavours and act as a natural <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservative</a> and stabilizing agent. Other flavouring agents such as <a href="/wiki/Gruit" title="Gruit">gruit</a>, herbs, or fruits may be included or used instead of hops. In commercial brewing, the natural <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a> effect is often removed during processing and replaced with forced carbonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carbonated_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-carbonated-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of humanity's earliest known writings refer to the production and distribution of beer: the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi" title="Code of Hammurabi">Code of Hammurabi</a> included laws regulating beer and beer parlours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and "The Hymn to <a href="/wiki/Ninkasi" title="Ninkasi">Ninkasi</a>", a prayer to the Mesopotamian goddess of beer, served as both a prayer and as a method of remembering the recipe for beer in a culture with few literate people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nin-kasi_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nin-kasi-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sumer_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sumer-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beer is distributed in bottles and cans and is also commonly available on <a href="/wiki/Draught_beer" title="Draught beer">draught</a>, particularly in pubs and bars. The brewing industry is a global business, consisting of several dominant <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational companies</a> and many thousands of smaller producers ranging from <a href="/wiki/Microbrewery#Brewpub" title="Microbrewery">brewpubs</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_brewery" title="Regional brewery">regional breweries</a>. The strength of modern beer is usually around 4% to 6% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a> (ABV), although it may vary between 0.5% and 20%, with some breweries creating examples of 40% ABV and above.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_News_2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_2010-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beer forms part of the culture of many nations and is associated with social traditions such as <a href="/wiki/Beer_festival" title="Beer festival">beer festivals</a>, as well as a rich <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> culture involving activities like <a href="/wiki/Pub_crawl" title="Pub crawl">pub crawling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pub_games" title="Pub games">pub games</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_beer" title="History of beer">History of beer</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg/220px-EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg/330px-EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg/440px-EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EMS-89615-Rosecrucian-Egyptian-BeerMaking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Egyptian wooden model of beer making in ancient Egypt, <a href="/wiki/Rosicrucian_Egyptian_Museum" title="Rosicrucian Egyptian Museum">Rosicrucian Egyptian Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a></div></div></div>
<p>Beer is one of the world's oldest prepared drinks. The earliest archaeological evidence of fermentation consists of 13,000 year old residues of a beer with the consistency of gruel, used by the semi-nomadic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natufian" title="Natufian">Natufians</a> for ritual feasting, at the Raqefet Cave in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmel_Mountains" title="Carmel Mountains">Carmel Mountains</a> near <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> There is evidence that beer was produced at <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6bekli_Tepe" title="Göbekli Tepe">Göbekli Tepe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Pottery_Neolithic" title="Pre-Pottery Neolithic">Pre-Pottery Neolithic</a> (around 8500 BC to 5500 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The earliest clear chemical evidence of beer produced from <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> dates to about 3500–3100 BC, from the site of <a href="/wiki/Godin_Tepe" title="Godin Tepe">Godin Tepe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a> of western <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGovern_2009_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGovern_2009-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is possible, but not proven, that it dates back even further — to about 10,000 BC, when cereal was first <a href="/wiki/History_of_agriculture" title="History of agriculture">farmed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Beer is recorded in the written history of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">ancient Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and archaeologists speculate that beer was instrumental in the formation of civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Approximately 5000 years ago, workers in the city of <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">Uruk</a> (modern day Iraq) were paid by their employers in beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> During the building of the <a href="/wiki/Giza_pyramid_complex" title="Giza pyramid complex">Great Pyramids in Giza, Egypt</a>, each worker got a daily ration of four to five litres of beer, which served as both nutrition and refreshment that was crucial to the pyramids' construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the earliest Sumerian writings contain references to beer; examples include a prayer to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Ninkasi" title="Ninkasi">Ninkasi</a>, known as "The Hymn to Ninkasi",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> which served as both a prayer as well as a method of remembering the recipe for beer in a culture with few literate people, and the ancient advice (<i>Fill your belly. Day and night make merry</i>) to <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh" title="Gilgamesh">Gilgamesh</a>, recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>, by the ale-wife <a href="/wiki/Siduri" title="Siduri">Siduri</a> may, at least in part, have referred to the consumption of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ebla_tablets" title="Ebla tablets">Ebla tablets</a>, discovered in 1974 in <a href="/wiki/Ebla" title="Ebla">Ebla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, show that beer was produced in the city in 2500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A fermented drink using rice and fruit was made in China around 7000 BC. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a>, mold was not used to saccharify the rice (amylolytic fermentation); the rice was probably prepared for fermentation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">chewing</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malting" title="Malting">malting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Almost any substance containing sugar can naturally undergo alcoholic fermentation. It is likely that many cultures, on observing that a sweet liquid could be obtained from a source of starch, independently invented beer. Bread and beer increased prosperity to a level that allowed time for development of other technologies and contributed to the building of civilizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_History_of_the_World_in_Six_Glasses_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_History_of_the_World_in_Six_Glasses-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> noted that during his travels, beer was being produced in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1446" data-file-width="2000" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg/220px-Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg/330px-Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg/440px-Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fran%C3%A7ois_Louis_Jaques_Paysans_fribourgeois_au_bistrot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>François Jaques: <i>Peasants Enjoying Beer at Pub in Fribourg</i> (Switzerland, 1923)</div></div></div>
<p>Beer was spread through Europe by <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> tribes as far back as 3000 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and it was mainly brewed on a domestic scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The product that the early Europeans drank might not be recognised as beer by most people today. Alongside the basic starch source, the early European beers might contain fruits, honey, numerous types of plants, spices and other substances such as <a href="/wiki/Narcotic" title="Narcotic">narcotic</a> herbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> What they did not contain was <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>, as that was a later addition, first mentioned in Europe around 822 by a Carolingian Abbot<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and again in 1067 by <a href="/wiki/Abbess" title="Abbess">abbess</a> <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_of_Bingen" title="Hildegard of Bingen">Hildegard of Bingen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1516, <a href="/wiki/William_IV,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="William IV, Duke of Bavaria">William IV</a>, Duke of Bavaria, adopted the <i><a href="/wiki/Reinheitsgebot" title="Reinheitsgebot">Reinheitsgebot</a></i> (purity law), perhaps the oldest food-quality regulation still in use in the 21st century, according to which the only allowed ingredients of beer are water, <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> and barley-<a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Beer produced before the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> continued to be made and sold on a domestic scale, although by the 7th century AD, beer was also being produced and sold by European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monasteries" title="Monasteries">monasteries</a>. During the Industrial Revolution, the production of beer moved from <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisanal</a> manufacture to <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">industrial manufacture</a>, and domestic manufacture ceased to be significant by the end of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sotp_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-sotp-38">[38]</a></sup> The development of <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">hydrometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a> changed brewing by allowing the brewer more control of the process and greater knowledge of the results.
</p><p>As of 2007, the brewing industry is a global business, consisting of several dominant multinational companies and many thousands of smaller producers ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewpub" title="Brewpub">brewpubs</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_brewery" title="Regional brewery">regional breweries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-market_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-market-39">[39]</a></sup> As of 2006, more than 133 billion litres (35 billion gallons), the equivalent of a cube 510 metres on a side, of beer are sold per year, producing total global revenues of $294.5 billion (£147.7 billion). In 2010, China's beer consumption hit 450 million hectolitres (45 billion litres), or nearly twice that of the United States, but only 5 per cent sold were premium draught beers, compared with 50 per cent in France and Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent and widely publicized study suggests that sudden decreases in barley production due to extreme drought and heat could in the future cause substantial volatility in the availability and price of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing">Brewing</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">Brewing</a></div>
<div class="noresize" role="img" style="width: 400px; float: right; clear: right; line-height: 1; text-align: center; background-color: #ffffff; position: relative; max-width: 100%; overflow-x: auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brewing-textless.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="850" data-file-width="600" height="567" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Brewing-textless.png/400px-Brewing-textless.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/Brewing-textless.png 1.5x" width="400"/></a>

</div>
<p><br/>
The process of making beer is known as brewing. A dedicated building for the making of beer is called a brewery, though beer can be made in the home and has been for much of its history. A company that makes beer is called either a brewery or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewing_company" title="Brewing company">brewing company</a>. Beer made on a domestic scale for non-commercial reasons is classified as <a href="/wiki/Homebrewing" title="Homebrewing">homebrewing</a> regardless of where it is made, though most homebrewed beer is made in the home. Brewing beer is subject to legislation and taxation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a>, which from the late 19th century largely restricted brewing to a commercial operation only. However, the UK government relaxed legislation in 1963, followed by Australia in 1972 and the US in 1978, allowing homebrewing to become a popular hobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The purpose of brewing is to convert the starch source into a sugary liquid called <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> and to convert the wort into the alcoholic drink known as beer in a fermentation process effected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">yeast</a>.
</p><p>The first step, where the wort is prepared by mixing the starch source (normally malted barley) with hot water, is known as "<a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">mashing</a>". Hot water (known as "liquor" in brewing terms) is mixed with crushed malt or malts (known as "<a href="/wiki/Grist" title="Grist">grist</a>") in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mash_tun" title="Mash tun">mash tun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The mashing process takes around 1 to 2 hours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> during which the starches are converted to sugars, and then the sweet wort is drained off the grains. The grains are now washed in a process known as "sparging". This washing allows the brewer to gather as much of the fermentable liquid from the grains as possible. The process of filtering the spent grain from the wort and sparge water is called <i>wort separation</i>. The traditional process for wort separation is <a href="/wiki/Lautering" title="Lautering">lautering</a>, in which the grain bed itself serves as the filter medium. Some modern breweries prefer the use of filter frames which allow a more finely ground grist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="921" data-file-width="723" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/170px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/255px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png/340px-The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Brewer_designed_and_engraved_in_the_Sixteenth._Century_by_J_Amman.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 16th-century brewery</div></div></div>
<p>Most modern breweries use a continuous sparge, collecting the original wort and the sparge water together. However, it is possible to collect a second or even third wash with the not quite spent grains as separate batches. Each run would produce a weaker wort and thus a weaker beer. This process is known as second (and third) runnings. Brewing with several runnings is called parti gyle brewing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sweet wort collected from sparging is put into a kettle, or "copper" (so called because these vessels were traditionally made from copper),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and boiled, usually for about one hour. During boiling, water in the wort evaporates, but the sugars and other components of the wort remain; this allows more efficient use of the starch sources in the beer. Boiling also destroys any remaining enzymes left over from the mashing stage. <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">Hops</a> are added during boiling as a source of bitterness, flavour and aroma. Hops may be added at more than one point during the boil. The longer the hops are boiled, the more bitterness they contribute, but the less hop flavour and aroma remains in the beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>After boiling, the hopped wort is now cooled, ready for the yeast. In some breweries, the hopped wort may pass through a hopback, which is a small vat filled with hops, to add aromatic hop flavouring and to act as a filter; but usually the hopped wort is simply cooled for the fermenter, where the yeast is added. During fermentation, the wort becomes beer in a process which requires a week to months depending on the type of yeast and strength of the beer. In addition to producing <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, fine <a href="/wiki/Suspended_solids" title="Suspended solids">particulate matter</a> suspended in the wort settles during fermentation. Once fermentation is complete, the yeast also settles, leaving the beer clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>During fermentation most of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is allowed to escape through a <a href="/wiki/Trap_(plumbing)" title="Trap (plumbing)">trap</a> and the beer is left with carbonation of only about one atmosphere of pressure. The carbonation is often increased either by transferring the beer to a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessel</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Keg" title="Keg">keg</a> and introducing pressurized carbon dioxide, or by transferring it before the fermentation is finished so that carbon dioxide pressure builds up inside the container as the fermentation finishes. Sometimes the beer is put unfiltered (so it still contains yeast) into bottles with some added sugar, which then produces the desired amount of carbon dioxide inside the bottle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carbonated_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-carbonated-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fermentation is sometimes carried out in two stages, primary and secondary. Once most of the alcohol has been produced during primary fermentation, the beer is transferred to a new vessel and allowed a period of <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Secondary_fermentation" title="Brewing">secondary fermentation</a>. Secondary fermentation is used when the beer requires long storage before packaging or greater clarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> When the beer has fermented, it is <a href="#Packaging">packaged</a> either into casks for <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">cask ale</a> or kegs, <a href="/wiki/Drink_can" title="Drink can">aluminium cans</a>, or bottles for other sorts of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients">Ingredients</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/220px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/330px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg/440px-Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sjb_whiskey_malt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malted_barley" title="Malted barley">Malted barley</a> before roasting</div></div></div>
<p>The basic ingredients of beer are water; a starch source, such as <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malted</a> <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, able to be saccharified (converted to sugars) then fermented (converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide); a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a> to produce the fermentation; and a flavouring such as <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> A mixture of starch sources may be used, with a secondary starch source, such as maize (corn), rice or sugar, often being termed an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjunct_(beer)" title="Adjunct (beer)">adjunct</a>, especially when used as a lower-cost substitute for malted barley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Less widely used starch sources include <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> root in Africa, and potato in Brazil, and <a href="/wiki/Agave" title="Agave">agave</a> in Mexico, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The amount of each starch source in a beer recipe is collectively called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_bill" title="Grain bill">grain bill</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a> is the main ingredient of beer, accounting for 93% of its weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Though water itself is, ideally, flavorless, its level of dissolved minerals, specifically, bicarbonate ion, does influence beer's finished taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> properties of each <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a>'s water, specific areas were originally the sole producers of certain types of beer, each identifiable by regional characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geot_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-geot-57">[57]</a></sup> Regional <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> accords that <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a> is well-suited to making <a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">stout</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88_Region" title="Plzeň Region">Plzeň Region</a>'s soft water is ideal for brewing <a href="/wiki/Pilsner" title="Pilsner">Pilsner</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pale_lager" title="Pale lager">pale lager</a>), such as <a href="/wiki/Pilsner_Urquell" title="Pilsner Urquell">Pilsner Urquell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geot_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-geot-57">[57]</a></sup> The waters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burton_Bridge_Brewery" title="Burton Bridge Brewery">Burton</a> in England contain <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, which benefits making <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a> to such a degree that brewers of pale ales will add gypsum to the local water in a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burtonisation" title="Burtonisation">Burtonisation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>The starch source, termed as the "<a href="/wiki/Mash_ingredients" title="Mash ingredients">mash ingredients</a>", in a beer provides the fermentable material and is a key determinant of the strength and flavour of the beer. The most common starch source used in beer is malted grain. Grain is malted by soaking it in water, allowing it to begin <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>, and then drying the partially germinated grain in a kiln. Malting grain produces enzymes that convert starches in the grain into fermentable sugars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Different roasting times and temperatures are used to produce different colours of malt from the same grain. Darker malts will produce darker beers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Nearly all beer includes barley malt as the majority of the starch. This is because its fibrous hull remains attached to the grain during threshing. After malting, barley is milled, which finally removes the hull, breaking it into large pieces. These pieces remain with the grain during the <a href="/wiki/Mashing" title="Mashing">mash</a>, and act as a filter bed during <a href="/wiki/Lautering" title="Lautering">lautering</a>, when sweet <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> is separated from insoluble grain material. Other malted and unmalted grains (including wheat, rice, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oats" title="Oats">oats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>, and less frequently, corn and sorghum) may be used. Some brewers have produced <a href="/wiki/Gluten-free_beer" title="Gluten-free beer">gluten-free beer</a>, made with sorghum with no barley malt, for those who cannot consume <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a>-containing grains like wheat, barley, and rye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/220px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/330px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg/440px-Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hopfendolde-mit-hopfengarten.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">Hop cone</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Hallertau" title="Hallertau">Hallertau</a>, Germany, hop yard</div></div></div>
<p>Flavouring beer is the sole major commercial use of <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The flower of the <a href="/wiki/Humulus_lupulus" title="Humulus lupulus">hop vine</a> is used as a flavouring and preservative agent in nearly all beer made today. The flowers themselves are often called "hops". The first historical mention of the use of hops in beer was from 822 AD in monastery rules written by Adalhard the Elder, also known as <a href="/wiki/Adalard_of_Corbie" title="Adalard of Corbie">Adalard of Corbie</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sotp_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-sotp-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmar_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-bmar-63">[63]</a></sup> though the date normally given for widespread cultivation of hops for use in beer is the thirteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sotp_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-sotp-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmar_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-bmar-63">[63]</a></sup> Before the thirteenth century, and until the sixteenth century, during which hops took over as the dominant flavouring, beer was flavoured with other plants; for instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grains_of_paradise" title="Grains of paradise">grains of paradise</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Glechoma_hederacea#Cultivation_and_uses" title="Glechoma hederacea">alehoof</a></i>. Combinations of various aromatic herbs, berries, and even ingredients like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absinth_Wormwood" title="Absinth Wormwood">wormwood</a> would be combined into a mixture known as <a href="/wiki/Gruit" title="Gruit">gruit</a> and used as hops are now used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Some beers today, such as Fraoch' by the Scottish Heather Ales company<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and Cervoise Lancelot by the French Brasserie-Lancelot company,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> use plants other than hops for flavouring.
</p><p>Hops contain several characteristics that brewers desire in beer. Hops contribute a bitterness that balances the sweetness of the malt; the bitterness of beers is measured on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Bitterness_Units_scale" title="International Bitterness Units scale">International Bitterness Units scale</a>. Hops contribute floral, citrus, and herbal aromas and flavours to beer. Hops have an <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> effect that favours the activity of brewer's yeast over less desirable microorganisms and aids in "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_(beer)" title="Head (beer)">head</a> retention",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> the length of time that a foamy head created by carbonation will last. The acidity of hops is a preservative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Yeast is the <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> that is responsible for fermentation in beer. Yeast <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolises</a> the sugars extracted from grains, which produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and thereby turns <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> into beer. In addition to fermenting the beer, yeast influences the character and flavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The dominant types of yeast used to make beer are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top-fermenting_yeast" title="Top-fermenting yeast">top-fermenting</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottom-fermenting" title="Bottom-fermenting">bottom-fermenting</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces" title="Brettanomyces">Brettanomyces</a></i> ferments <a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">lambics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Torulaspora_delbrueckii" title="Torulaspora delbrueckii">Torulaspora delbrueckii</a></i> ferments Bavarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weissbier" title="Weissbier">weissbier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Before the role of yeast in fermentation was understood, fermentation involved wild or airborne yeasts. A few styles such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambics" title="Lambics">lambics</a> rely on this method today, but most modern fermentation adds pure yeast <a href="/wiki/Microbiological_culture" title="Microbiological culture">cultures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some brewers add one or more clarifying agents or <a href="/wiki/Finings" title="Finings">finings</a> to beer, which typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> (collect as a solid) out of the beer along with protein solids and are found only in trace amounts in the finished product. This process makes the beer appear <a href="/wiki/Bright_beer" title="Bright beer">bright</a> and clean, rather than the cloudy appearance of ethnic and older styles of beer such as <a href="/wiki/Wheat_beer" title="Wheat beer">wheat beers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Examples of clarifying agents include <a href="/wiki/Isinglass" title="Isinglass">isinglass</a>, obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimbladder" title="Swimbladder">swimbladders</a> of fish; <a href="/wiki/Chondrus_crispus" title="Chondrus crispus">Irish moss</a>, a seaweed; kappa <a href="/wiki/Carrageenan" title="Carrageenan">carrageenan</a>, from the seaweed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kappaphycus_cottonii" title="Kappaphycus cottonii">Kappaphycus cottonii</a></i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyclar" title="Polyclar">Polyclar</a> (artificial); and <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> If a beer is marked "suitable for vegans", it was clarified either with seaweed or with artificial agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing_industry">Brewing industry</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beer_Consumption_by_Country_(with_legend).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1314" data-file-width="2560" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg/220px-Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg/330px-Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg/440px-Beer_Consumption_by_Country_%28with_legend%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beer_Consumption_by_Country_(with_legend).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Annual beer consumption per capita by country</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="750" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png/220px-2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png/330px-2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png/440px-2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2014_Beer_Countries_Export_Treemap.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beer Exports by Country (2014) from  Harvard Atlas of Economic Complexity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The history of breweries in the 21st century has been one of larger breweries absorbing smaller breweries in order to ensure economy of scale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Explain what economy of scale is and provide a more adequate explaination of how it relates to breweries. (June 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2002 <a href="/wiki/South_African_Breweries" title="South African Breweries">South African Breweries</a> bought the North American <a href="/wiki/Miller_Brewing_Company" title="Miller Brewing Company">Miller Brewing Company</a> to found <a href="/wiki/SABMiller" title="SABMiller">SABMiller</a>, becoming the second largest brewery, after North American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anheuser-Bush" title="Anheuser-Bush">Anheuser-Bush</a>. In 2004 the Belgian <a href="/wiki/Interbrew" title="Interbrew">Interbrew</a> was the third largest brewery by volume and the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/AmBev" title="AmBev">AmBev</a> was the fifth largest. They merged into <a href="/wiki/InBev" title="InBev">InBev</a>, becoming the largest brewery. In 2007, <a href="/wiki/SABMiller" title="SABMiller">SABMiller</a> surpassed InBev and Anheuser-Bush when it acquired Royal Grolsch, brewer of Dutch premium beer brand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grolsch" title="Grolsch">Grolsch</a> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In 2008, when <a href="/wiki/InBev" title="InBev">InBev</a> (the second-largest) bought <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch" title="Anheuser-Busch">Anheuser-Busch</a> (the third largest), the new <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch_InBev" title="Anheuser-Busch InBev">Anheuser-Busch InBev</a> company became again the largest brewer in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> As of  2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Beer&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> AB InBev remains the largest brewery, with SABMiller second, and <a href="/wiki/Heineken_International" title="Heineken International">Heineken International</a> third.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Microbrewery" title="Microbrewery">microbrewery</a>, or craft brewery, produces a limited amount of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barajas2017Untapped_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barajas2017Untapped-82">[82]</a></sup> The maximum amount of beer a brewery can produce and still be classed as a microbrewery varies by region and by authority, though is usually around 15,000 barrels (1.8 megalitres, 396 thousand imperial gallons or 475 thousand US gallons) a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> A brewpub is a type of microbrewery that incorporates a <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pub</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">drinking establishment</a>. The highest density of breweries in the world, most of them microbreweries, exists in the German Region of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, especially in the district of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a>, which has about 200 breweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Weihenstephan" title="Weihenstephan">Weihenstephan</a> brewery in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, Germany, can trace its roots to the year 768, as a document from that year refers to a hop garden in the area paying a tithe to the monastery. The brewery was licensed by the City of <a href="/wiki/Freising" title="Freising">Freising</a> in 1040, and therefore is the oldest working brewery in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giebel,_Wieland_1992_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giebel,_Wieland_1992-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Homebrewing" title="Homebrewing">Brewing at home</a> is subject to regulation and prohibition in many countries. Restrictions on homebrewing were lifted in the UK in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ukrepeal_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-ukrepeal-87">[87]</a></sup> Australia followed suit in 1972,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausrepeal_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-ausrepeal-88">[88]</a></sup> and the US in 1978, though individual states were allowed to pass their own laws limiting production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:183px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_beore.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="181" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Beowulf_-_beore.jpg" width="181"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beowulf_-_beore.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Old English word for beer</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>ale</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>ealu</i> (plural <i>ealoþ</i>), in turn from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> <i>*alu</i> (plural <i>*aluþ</i>), ultimately from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> base <i>*h₂elut-</i>, which holds connotations of "sorcery, magic, possession, intoxication".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The word <i>beer</i> comes from Old English <i>bēor</i>, from Proto-Germanic <i>*beuzą</i>, probably from Proto-Indo-European <i>*bʰeusóm</i>, originally "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a>, beer dregs", although other theories have been provided connecting the word with Old English <i>bēow</i>, "barley", or Latin <i>bibere</i>, "to drink".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> On the currency of two words for the same thing in the Germanic languages, the 12th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Icelandic" title="Old Icelandic">Old Icelandic</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Alv%C3%ADssm%C3%A1l" title="Alvíssmál">Alvíssmál</a></i> says, "Ale it is called among men, but among the gods, beer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_style" title="Beer style">Beer style</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HandPumps.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="586" data-file-width="548" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/HandPumps.jpg/220px-HandPumps.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/HandPumps.jpg/330px-HandPumps.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/HandPumps.jpg/440px-HandPumps.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HandPumps.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cask ale <a href="/wiki/Beer_engine" title="Beer engine">hand pumps</a> with pump clips detailing the beers and their breweries</div></div></div>
<p>While there are many types of beer brewed, the basics of brewing beer are shared across national and cultural boundaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> The traditional European brewing regions—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_beer" title="German beer">Germany</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_beer" title="Belgian beer">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_England" title="Beer in England">England</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_beer" title="Czech beer">Czech Republic</a>—have local varieties of beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>English writer <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson_(writer)" title="Michael Jackson (writer)">Michael Jackson</a>, in his 1977 book <i>The World Guide To Beer</i>, categorised beers from around the world in local style groups suggested by local customs and names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-98">[98]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fred_Eckhardt" title="Fred Eckhardt">Fred Eckhardt</a> furthered Jackson's work in <i>The Essentials of Beer Style</i> in 1989.
</p><p>Top-fermented beers are most commonly produced with <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top-fermenting_yeast" title="Top-fermenting yeast">top-fermenting yeast</a> which <a href="/wiki/Yeast_flocculation" title="Yeast flocculation">clumps</a> and rises to the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> typically between 15 and 25 °C (59 and 77 °F). At these temperatures, yeast produces significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> and other secondary flavour and aroma products, and the result is often a beer with slightly "fruity" compounds resembling apple, pear, pineapple, <a href="/wiki/Banana_beer" title="Banana beer">banana</a>, plum, or prune, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the introduction of hops into England from Flanders in the 15th century, "ale" referred to an unhopped fermented drink, "beer" being used to describe a brew with an infusion of hops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_ale" title="Real ale">Real ale</a></i> is the term coined by the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Real_Ale" title="Campaign for Real Ale">Campaign for Real Ale</a> (CAMRA) in 1973<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> for "beer brewed from traditional ingredients, matured by <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Secondary_fermentation" title="Brewing">secondary fermentation</a> in the container from which it is dispensed, and served without the use of <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">extraneous carbon dioxide</a>". It is applied to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottle_conditioned" title="Bottle conditioned">bottle conditioned</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cask_conditioned" title="Cask conditioned">cask conditioned</a> beers.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">Pale ale</a> is a beer which uses a top-fermenting yeast<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> and predominantly pale malt. It is one of the world's major beer styles.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">Stout</a> and porter are dark beers made using roasted malts or roast barley, and typically brewed with slow fermenting yeast. There are a number of variations including Baltic porter, dry stout, and Imperial stout. The name "porter" was first used in 1721 to describe a dark brown beer popular with the street and river <a href="/wiki/Porter_(carrier)" title="Porter (carrier)">porters</a> of London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> This same beer later also became known as stout, though the word stout had been used as early as 1677.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> The history and development of stout and porter are intertwined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mild_ale" title="Mild ale">Mild ale</a> has a predominantly malty palate. It is usually dark coloured with an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">abv</a> of 3% to 3.6%, although there are lighter hued milds as well as stronger examples reaching 6% abv and higher.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wheat_beer" title="Wheat beer">Wheat beer</a> is brewed with a large proportion of wheat although it often also contains a significant proportion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malted_barley" title="Malted barley">malted barley</a>. Wheat beers are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top-fermented" title="Top-fermented">top-fermented</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warner_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warner-107">[107]</a></sup> The flavour of wheat beers varies considerably, depending upon the specific style.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kriek_Beer_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1517" data-file-width="1104" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Kriek_Beer_1.jpg/170px-Kriek_Beer_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Kriek_Beer_1.jpg/255px-Kriek_Beer_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Kriek_Beer_1.jpg/340px-Kriek_Beer_1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kriek_Beer_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kriek_lambic" title="Kriek lambic">Kriek</a>, a variety of beer brewed with cherries</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lambic" title="Lambic">Lambic</a>, a beer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_beers" title="Belgian beers">Belgium</a>, is naturally fermented using wild yeasts, rather than cultivated. Many of these are not strains of brewer's yeast (<i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i>) and may have significant differences in aroma and sourness. Yeast varieties such as <i><a href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_bruxellensis" title="Brettanomyces bruxellensis">Brettanomyces bruxellensis</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brettanomyces_lambicus" title="Brettanomyces lambicus">Brettanomyces lambicus</a></i> are common in lambics. In addition, other organisms such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus" title="Lactobacillus">Lactobacillus</a></i> bacteria produce acids which contribute to the sourness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3310" data-file-width="3310" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg/220px-Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg/330px-Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg/440px-Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paulaner_Oktoberfest_Marzen_11.2oz_bottle_and_beer_mug.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marzen" title="Marzen">marzen</a> style lager</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lager" title="Lager">Lager</a> is cool fermented beer. <a href="/wiki/Pale_lager" title="Pale lager">Pale lagers</a> are the most commonly consumed beers in the world. The name "lager" comes from the German "lagern" for "to store", as brewers around Bavaria stored beer in cool cellars and caves during the warm summer months. These brewers noticed that the beers continued to ferment, and to also clear of sediment, when stored in cool conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lager yeast is a cool bottom-fermenting yeast (<i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_pastorianus" title="Saccharomyces pastorianus">Saccharomyces pastorianus</a></i>) and typically undergoes primary fermentation at 7–12 °C (45–54 °F) (the fermentation phase), and then is given a long secondary fermentation at 0–4 °C (32–39 °F) (the lagering phase). During the secondary stage, the lager clears and mellows. The cooler conditions also inhibit the natural production of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> and other byproducts, resulting in a "cleaner"-tasting beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern methods of producing lager were pioneered by Gabriel Sedlmayr the Younger, who perfected dark brown lagers at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaten_Brewery" title="Spaten Brewery">Spaten Brewery</a> in Bavaria, and <a href="/wiki/Anton_Dreher" title="Anton Dreher">Anton Dreher</a>, who began brewing a lager (now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_lager" title="Vienna lager">Vienna lager</a>), probably of amber-red colour, in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1840–1841. With improved modern yeast strains, most lager breweries use only short periods of cold storage, typically 1–3 weeks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_measurement" title="Beer measurement">Beer measurement</a></div>
<p>Beer is measured and assessed by bitterness, by strength and by colour. The perceived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_(taste)" title="Bitter (taste)">bitterness</a> is measured by the International Bitterness Units scale (IBU), defined in co-operation between the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Brewing_Chemists" title="American Society of Brewing Chemists">American Society of Brewing Chemists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Brewery_Convention" title="European Brewery Convention">European Brewery Convention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> The international scale was a development of the European Bitterness Units scale, often abbreviated as EBU, and the bitterness values should be identical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colour">Colour</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paulaner.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="318" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Paulaner.jpg/170px-Paulaner.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Paulaner.jpg/255px-Paulaner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Paulaner.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paulaner.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paulaner <a href="/wiki/Dunkel" title="Dunkel">dunkel</a> – a dark lager</div></div></div>
<p>Beer colour is determined by the malt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> The most common colour is a pale amber produced from using pale malts. <i>Pale lager</i> and <i>pale ale</i> are terms used for beers made from malt dried with the fuel <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>. Coke was first used for roasting malt in 1642, but it was not until around 1703 that the term <i>pale ale</i> was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of sales volume, most of today's beer is based on the pale lager brewed in 1842 in the town of <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Pilsen</a> in the present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_beer" title="Czech beer">Czech Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> The modern pale lager is light in colour with a noticeable carbonation (fizzy bubbles) and a typical <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a> content of around 5%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pilsner_Urquell" title="Pilsner Urquell">Pilsner Urquell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitburger" title="Bitburger">Bitburger</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heineken_Pilsener" title="Heineken Pilsener">Heineken</a> brands of beer are typical examples of pale lager, as are the American brands <a href="/wiki/Budweiser" title="Budweiser">Budweiser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coors_Brewing_Company" title="Coors Brewing Company">Coors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Miller_Brewing_Company" title="Miller Brewing Company">Miller</a>.
</p><p>Dark beers are usually brewed from a pale malt or lager malt base with a small proportion of darker malt added to achieve the desired shade. Other colourants—such as caramel—are also widely used to darken beers. Very dark beers, such as <a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">stout</a>, use dark or patent malts that have been roasted longer. Some have roasted unmalted barley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strength">Strength</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Beer_measurement#Strength" title="Beer measurement">Beer measurement § Strength</a></div>
<p>Beer ranges from less than 3% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a> (abv) to around 14% abv, though this strength can be increased to around 20% by re-pitching with champagne yeast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mill_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mill-119">[119]</a></sup> and to 55% abv by the freeze-distilling process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-End-120">[120]</a></sup> The alcohol content of beer varies by local practice or beer style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pale_lager" title="Pale lager">pale lagers</a> that most consumers are familiar with fall in the range of 4–6%, with a typical abv of 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The customary strength of British ales is quite low, with many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Session_beer" title="Session beer">session beers</a> being around 4% abv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> Some beers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_beer#Table_beer" title="Belgian beer">table beer</a> are of such low alcohol content (1%–4%) that they are served instead of <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a> in some schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alcohol in beer comes primarily from the metabolism of sugars that are produced during fermentation. The quantity of fermentable sugars in the wort and the variety of yeast used to ferment the wort are the primary factors that determine the amount of alcohol in the final beer. Additional fermentable sugars are sometimes added to increase alcohol content, and enzymes are often added to the wort for certain styles of beer (primarily "light" beers) to convert more complex carbohydrates (starches) to fermentable sugars. Alcohol is a by-product of yeast metabolism and is toxic to the yeast in higher concentrations; typical brewing yeast cannot survive at alcohol concentrations above 12% by volume. Low temperatures and too little fermentation time decreases the effectiveness of yeasts and consequently decreases the alcohol content.
</p><p>The weakest beers are <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dealcoholized_beer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dealcoholized beer (page does not exist)">dealcoholized beers</a>, which typically have less than 0.05% alcohol (also called "near beer") and <a href="/wiki/Light_beer" title="Light beer">light beers</a>, which usually have 4% alcohol.
</p><p><span id="Strongest_beer"></span>
The strength of beers has climbed during the later years of the 20th century. Vetter 33, a 10.5% abv (33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plato_scale" title="Plato scale">degrees Plato</a>, hence Vetter "33") <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppelbock" title="Doppelbock">doppelbock</a>, was listed in the 1994 <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a></i> as the strongest beer at that time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samichlaus" title="Samichlaus">Samichlaus</a>, by the Swiss brewer Hürlimann, had also been listed by the <i>Guinness Book of World Records</i> as the strongest at 14% abv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> Since then, some brewers have used champagne yeasts to increase the alcohol content of their beers. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Adams_(beer)" title="Samuel Adams (beer)">Samuel Adams</a> reached 20% abv with <i>Millennium</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mill_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mill-119">[119]</a></sup> and then surpassed that amount to 25.6% abv with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utopias_(beer)" title="Utopias (beer)">Utopias</a>. The strongest beer brewed in Britain was Baz's Super Brew by Parish Brewery, a 23% abv beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> In September 2011, the Scottish brewery <a href="/wiki/BrewDog" title="BrewDog">BrewDog</a> produced Ghost Deer, which, at 28%, they claim to be the world's strongest beer produced by fermentation alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ghostdeer_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-ghostdeer-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p>The product claimed to be the strongest beer made is Schorschbräu's 2011 <i>Schorschbock 57</i> with 57,5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> It was preceded by <i>The End of History</i>, a 55% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_ale" title="Belgian ale">Belgian ale</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-End_120-1"><a href="#cite_note-End-120">[120]</a></sup> made by BrewDog in 2010. The same company had previously made <i>Sink The Bismarck!</i>, a 41% abv <a href="/wiki/India_pale_ale" title="India pale ale">IPA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sink_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sink-135">[135]</a></sup> and <i>Tactical Nuclear Penguin</i>, a 32% abv <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_stout" title="Imperial stout">Imperial stout</a>. Each of these beers are made using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eisbock" title="Eisbock">eisbock</a> method of <a href="/wiki/Fractional_freezing" title="Fractional freezing">fractional freezing</a>, in which a strong ale is partially frozen and the ice is repeatedly removed, until the desired strength is reached,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> a process that may class the product as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled_drink" title="Distilled drink">spirits</a> rather than beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> The German brewery Schorschbräu's <i>Schorschbock</i>, a 31% abv eisbock,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hair_of_the_Dog_Brewing_Company" title="Hair of the Dog Brewing Company">Hair of the Dog</a>'s <i>Dave</i>, a 29% abv <a href="/wiki/Barley_wine" title="Barley wine">barley wine</a> made in 1994, used the same fractional freezing method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> A 60% abv <a href="/wiki/Beer_cocktail" title="Beer cocktail">blend of beer with whiskey</a> was jokingly claimed as the strongest beer by a <a href="/wiki/Brouwerij_%27t_Koelschip" title="Brouwerij 't Koelschip">Dutch brewery</a> in July 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serving">Serving</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Draught">Draught</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Draught_beer" title="Draught beer">Draught beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cask_ale" title="Cask ale">Cask ale</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cask_Ales.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="448" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Cask_Ales.jpg/220px-Cask_Ales.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Cask_Ales.jpg/330px-Cask_Ales.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Cask_Ales.jpg/440px-Cask_Ales.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cask_Ales.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A selection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cask_beer" title="Cask beer">cask beers</a></div></div></div>
<p>Draught (also spelled "draft") beer from a pressurised <a href="/wiki/Keg" title="Keg">keg</a> using a lever-style dispenser and a spout is the most common method of dispensing in bars around the world. A metal keg is pressurised with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) gas which drives the beer to the dispensing <a href="/wiki/Beer_tap" title="Beer tap">tap</a> or faucet. Some beers may be served with a nitrogen/carbon dioxide mixture. <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">Nitrogen</a> produces fine bubbles, resulting in a dense <a href="/wiki/Beer_head" title="Beer head">head</a> and a creamy <a href="/wiki/Mouthfeel" title="Mouthfeel">mouthfeel</a>. Some types of beer can also be found in smaller, disposable kegs called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer_ball" title="Beer ball">beer balls</a>. In traditional pubs, the pull levers for major beer brands may include the beer's logo and trademark.
</p><p>In the 1980s, Guinness introduced the <a href="/wiki/Widget_(beer)" title="Widget (beer)">beer widget</a>, a nitrogen-pressurised ball inside a can which creates a dense, tight head, similar to beer served from a nitrogen system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> The words <i>draft</i> and <i>draught</i> can be used as marketing terms to describe canned or <a href="/wiki/Beer_bottle" title="Beer bottle">bottled</a> beers containing a beer widget, or which are cold-filtered rather than pasteurised.
</p><p>Cask-conditioned ales (or cask ales) are unfiltered and unpasteurised beers. These beers are termed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_ale" title="Real ale">real ale</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Real_Ale" title="Campaign for Real Ale">CAMRA</a> organisation. Typically, when a cask arrives in a pub, it is placed horizontally on a frame called a "<a href="/wiki/Stillage" title="Stillage">stillage</a>" which is designed to hold it steady and at the right angle, and then allowed to cool to cellar temperature (typically between 11–13 °C or 52–55 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> before being tapped and vented—a tap is driven through a (usually rubber) bung at the bottom of one end, and a hard <a href="/wiki/Spile" title="Spile">spile</a> or other implement is used to open a hole in the side of the cask, which is now uppermost. The act of stillaging and then venting a beer in this manner typically disturbs all the sediment, so it must be left for a suitable period to "drop" (clear) again, as well as to fully <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Conditioning" title="Brewing">condition</a> — this period can take anywhere from several hours to several days. At this point the beer is ready to sell, either being pulled through a beer line with a hand pump, or simply being "gravity-fed" directly into the glass.
</p><p>Draught beer's environmental impact can be 68% lower than bottled beer due to packaging differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> A life cycle study of one beer brand, including grain production, brewing, bottling, distribution and waste management, shows that the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from a 6-pack of micro-brew beer is about 3 kilograms (6.6 pounds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newbelgium_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-newbelgium-149">[149]</a></sup> The loss of natural habitat potential from the 6-pack of micro-brew beer is estimated to be 2.5 square metres (26 square feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> Downstream emissions from distribution, retail, storage and disposal of waste can be over 45% of a bottled micro-brew beer's CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newbelgium_149-1"><a href="#cite_note-newbelgium-149">[149]</a></sup> Where legal, the use of a refillable jug, reusable bottle or other reusable containers to transport draught beer from a store or a bar, rather than buying pre-bottled beer, can reduce the environmental impact of beer consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Packaging">Packaging</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Beer_bottle" title="Beer bottle">Beer bottle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drink_can" title="Drink can">Drink can</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dutch_beers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="734" data-file-width="700" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Dutch_beers.jpg/220px-Dutch_beers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Dutch_beers.jpg/330px-Dutch_beers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Dutch_beers.jpg/440px-Dutch_beers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dutch_beers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Assortment of beer bottles</div></div></div>
<p>Most beers are cleared of yeast by <a href="/wiki/Filtered_beer" title="Filtered beer">filtering</a> when packaged in bottles and cans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottle_conditioning" title="Bottle conditioning">bottle conditioned</a> beers retain some yeast—either by being unfiltered, or by being filtered and then reseeded with fresh yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> It is usually recommended that the beer be poured slowly, leaving any yeast sediment at the bottom of the bottle. However, some drinkers prefer to pour in the yeast; this practice is customary with <a href="/wiki/Wheat_beer" title="Wheat beer">wheat beers</a>. Typically, when serving a <a href="/wiki/Wheat_beer#Weissbier" title="Wheat beer">hefeweizen wheat beer</a>, 90% of the contents are poured, and the remainder is swirled to suspend the sediment before pouring it into the glass. Alternatively, the bottle may be inverted prior to opening. Glass bottles are always used for bottle conditioned beers.
</p><p>Many beers are sold in cans, though there is considerable variation in the proportion between different countries. In Sweden in 2001, 63.9% of beer was sold in cans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> People either drink from the can or pour the beer into a glass. A technology developed by <a href="/wiki/Crown_Holdings" title="Crown Holdings">Crown Holdings</a> for the 2010 <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> is the 'full aperture' can, so named because the entire lid is removed during the opening process, turning the can into a drinking cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> Cans protect the beer from light (thereby preventing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skunked_beer" title="Skunked beer">"skunked"</a> beer) and have a seal less prone to leaking over time than bottles. Cans were initially viewed as a technological breakthrough for maintaining the quality of a beer, then became commonly associated with less expensive, mass-produced beers, even though the quality of storage in cans is much like bottles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> Plastic (<a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">PET</a>) bottles are used by some breweries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span></h3>
<p>The temperature of a beer has an influence on a drinker's experience; warmer temperatures reveal the range of flavours in a beer but cooler temperatures are more refreshing. Most drinkers prefer <a href="/wiki/Pale_lager" title="Pale lager">pale lager</a> to be served chilled, a low- or medium-strength <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a> to be served cool, while a strong <a href="/wiki/Barley_wine" title="Barley wine">barley wine</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_stout" title="Imperial stout">imperial stout</a> to be served at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beer writer <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson_(writer)" title="Michael Jackson (writer)">Michael Jackson</a> proposed a five-level scale for serving temperatures: well chilled (7 °C or 45 °F) for "light" beers (pale lagers); chilled (8 °C or 46 °F) for <a href="/wiki/Berliner_Weisse" title="Berliner Weisse">Berliner Weisse</a> and other wheat beers; lightly chilled (9 °C or 48 °F) for all dark lagers, <a href="/wiki/Altbier" title="Altbier">altbier</a> and German wheat beers; cellar temperature (13 °C or 55 °F) for regular British <a href="/wiki/Ale" title="Ale">ale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stout" title="Stout">stout</a> and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belgian_beer" title="Belgian beer">Belgian specialities</a>; and room temperature (15.5 °C or 60 °F for strong dark ales (especially <a href="/wiki/Trappist_beer" title="Trappist beer">trappist beer</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Barley_wine" title="Barley wine">barley wine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup>
</p><p>Drinking chilled beer began with the development of artificial <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> and by the 1870s, was spread in those countries that concentrated on brewing pale lager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> Chilling beer makes it more refreshing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> though below 15.5 °C (60 °F) the chilling starts to reduce taste awareness<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> and reduces it significantly below 10 °C (50 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup> Beer served unchilled—either cool or at room temperature—reveal more of their flavours. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cask_Marque" title="Cask Marque">Cask Marque</a>, a non-profit UK beer organisation, has set a temperature standard range of 12°–14 °C (53°–57 °F) for cask ales to be served.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vessels">Vessels</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_glassware" title="Beer glassware">Beer glassware</a></div>
<p>Beer is consumed out of a variety of vessels, such as a glass, a <a href="/wiki/Beer_stein" title="Beer stein">beer stein</a>, a mug, a <a href="/wiki/Pewter" title="Pewter">pewter</a> <a href="/wiki/Tankard" title="Tankard">tankard</a>, a beer bottle or a can; or at <a href="/wiki/Music_festival" title="Music festival">music festivals</a> and some bars and nightclubs, from a plastic cup. The shape of the glass from which beer is consumed can influence the perception of the beer and can define and accent the character of the style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup> Breweries offer branded glassware intended only for their own beers as a marketing promotion, as this increases sales of their product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pouring process has an influence on a beer's presentation. The rate of flow from the <a href="/wiki/Beer_tap" title="Beer tap">tap</a> or other serving vessel, tilt of the glass, and position of the pour (in the centre or down the side) into the glass all influence the end result, such as the size and longevity of the head, lacing (the pattern left by the head as it moves down the glass as the beer is drunk), and the release of <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup>
A <a href="/wiki/Beer_tower" title="Beer tower">beer tower</a> is a beer dispensing device, usually found in bars and pubs, that consists of a cylinder attached to a beer cooling device at the bottom. Beer is dispensed from the beer tower into a drinking vessel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Beer&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(January 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Short-term_effects_of_alcohol_consumption" title="Short-term effects of alcohol consumption">Short-term effects of alcohol consumption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_alcohol_consumption" title="Long-term effects of alcohol consumption">Long-term effects of alcohol consumption</a></div>
<p>Beer contains <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a>, which has short and long-term effects on the user when consumed. Different concentrations of alcohol in the human body have different effects on a person. The effects of alcohol depend on the amount an individual has drunk, the percentage of alcohol in the beer and the timespan over which the consumption has taken place, the amount of food eaten and whether an individual has taken other prescription, over-the-counter or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_drugs" title="Street drugs">street drugs</a>, among other factors. Drinking enough to cause a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_alcohol_concentration" title="Blood alcohol concentration">blood alcohol concentration</a> (BAC) of 0.03%–0.12% typically causes an overall improvement in mood and possible <a href="/wiki/Euphoria" title="Euphoria">euphoria</a>, increased self-confidence and sociability, decreased anxiety, a <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_flush_reaction" title="Alcohol flush reaction">flushed, red appearance in the face</a> and impaired judgement and fine muscle coordination. A BAC of 0.09% to 0.25% causes <a href="/wiki/Lethargy" title="Lethargy">lethargy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedation" title="Sedation">sedation</a>, balance problems and blurred vision. A BAC from 0.18% to 0.30% causes profound confusion, impaired speech (e.g., slurred speech), staggering, dizziness and vomiting. A BAC from 0.25% to 0.40% causes <a href="/wiki/Stupor" title="Stupor">stupor</a>, unconsciousness, <a href="/wiki/Anterograde_amnesia" title="Anterograde amnesia">anterograde amnesia</a>, vomiting (death may occur due to inhalation of vomit (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration" title="Pulmonary aspiration">pulmonary aspiration</a>) while unconscious) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory_depression" title="Respiratory depression">respiratory depression</a> (potentially life-threatening). A BAC from 0.35% to 0.80% causes a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a> (unconsciousness), life-threatening respiratory depression and possibly fatal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_poisoning" title="Alcohol poisoning">alcohol poisoning</a>. As with all alcoholic drinks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drinking_and_driving" title="Drinking and driving">drinking while driving</a>, operating an aircraft or heavy machinery increases the risk of an accident; many countries have severe criminal penalties against drunk driving.
</p><p>A 2016 systematic review and meta-analysis found that moderate ethanol consumption brought no mortality benefit compared with lifetime abstention from ethanol consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup>  Some studies have concluded that drinking small quantities of alcohol (less than one drink in women and two in men) is associated with a <i>decreased</i> risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes mellitus</a>, and early death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kee2014_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kee2014-169">[169]</a></sup> Some of these studies combined former ethanol drinkers and life-long abstainers into a single group of nondrinkers, which hides the health benefits of life-long abstention from ethanol. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_alcohol" title="Long-term effects of alcohol">long term health effects</a> of continuous, moderate or heavy alcohol consumption include the risk of developing <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_liver_disease" title="Alcoholic liver disease">alcoholic liver disease</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">Alcoholism</a>, also known as "alcohol use disorder", is a broad term for any drinking of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a> that results in problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> It was previously divided into two types: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol abuse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_dependence" title="Alcohol dependence">alcohol dependence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2003_171-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2003-171">[171]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013-172">[172]</a></sup> In a medical context, alcoholism is said to exist when two or more of the following conditions is present: a person drinks large amounts over a long time period, has difficulty cutting down, acquiring and drinking alcohol takes up a great deal of time, alcohol is strongly desired, usage results in not fulfilling responsibilities, usage results in social problems, usage results in health problems, usage results in risky situations, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_withdrawal_syndrome" title="Alcohol withdrawal syndrome">withdrawal</a> occurs when stopping, and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_tolerance" title="Alcohol tolerance">alcohol tolerance</a> has occurred with use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013_172-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013-172">[172]</a></sup> Alcoholism reduces a person's life expectancy by around ten years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schu2014_173-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schu2014-173">[173]</a></sup> and alcohol use is the third leading cause of early death in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kee2014_169-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kee2014-169">[169]</a></sup> No professional medical association recommends that people who are nondrinkers should start drinking wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kee2014_169-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kee2014-169">[169]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup> A total of 3.3 million deaths (5.9% of all deaths) are believed to be due to alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2015Stats_175-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015Stats-175">[175]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is considered that overeating and lack of muscle tone is the main cause of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer_belly" title="Beer belly">beer belly</a>, rather than beer consumption. A 2004 study, however, found a link between <a href="/wiki/Binge_drinking" title="Binge drinking">binge drinking</a> and a beer belly. But with most overconsumption, it is more a problem of improper exercise and overconsumption of carbohydrates than the product itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup> Several diet books quote beer as having an undesirably high <a href="/wiki/Glycemic_index" title="Glycemic index">glycemic index</a> of 110, the same as <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a>; however, the maltose in beer undergoes <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> by yeast during fermentation so that beer consists mostly of water, hop oils and only trace amounts of sugars, including maltose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_information">Nutritional information</span></h2>
<p>Beers vary in their nutritional content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sugar_178-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sugar-178">[178]</a></sup> The ingredients used to make beer, including the <a href="/wiki/Yeast#Beer" title="Yeast">yeast</a>, provide a rich source of nutrients; therefore beer may contain traceable amounts of nutrients, including <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotin" title="Biotin">biotin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>. Beer is sometimes referred to as "liquid <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup> though beer is not a meal in itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beers vary in their nutritional content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sugar_178-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sugar-178">[178]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">Brewer's yeast</a> is known to be a rich source of nutrients; therefore, as expected, beer can contain significant amounts of nutrients, including <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotin" title="Biotin">biotin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>. Beer is sometimes referred to as "liquid <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Beer_culture" title="Category:Beer culture">Category:Beer culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oktoberfest2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Oktoberfest2.jpg/220px-Oktoberfest2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Oktoberfest2.jpg/330px-Oktoberfest2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Oktoberfest2.jpg/440px-Oktoberfest2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oktoberfest2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A tent at Munich's <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a>—the world's largest beer festival</div></div></div>
<p>In many societies, beer is the most popular alcoholic drink. Various social traditions and activities are associated with beer drinking, such as playing cards, darts, or other pub games; attending <a href="/wiki/Beer_festival" title="Beer festival">beer festivals</a>; engaging in zythology (the study of beer);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup> visiting a <a href="/wiki/Pub_crawl" title="Pub crawl">series of pubs</a> in one evening; visiting breweries; beer-oriented tourism; or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rating_beer" title="Rating beer">rating beer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drinking_game" title="Drinking game">Drinking games</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Beer_pong" title="Beer pong">beer pong</a>, are also popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup> A relatively new profession is that of the <a href="/wiki/Beer_sommelier" title="Beer sommelier">beer sommelier</a>, who informs restaurant patrons about beers and food pairings.
</p><p>Beer is considered to be a social lubricant in many societies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup> and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer_consumption_by_country" title="Beer consumption by country">consumed in countries</a> all over the world. There are breweries in Middle Eastern countries such as <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_Syria" title="Beer in Syria">Syria</a>, and in some <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_Africa" title="Beer in Africa">African countries</a>. Sales of beer are four times those of wine, which is the second most popular alcoholic drink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in the <i>Neuropsychopharmacology</i> journal in 2013 revealed the finding that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavour" title="Flavour">flavour</a> of beer alone could provoke <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> activity in the brain of the male participants, who wanted to drink more as a result. The 49 men in the study were subject to <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> scans, while a computer-controlled device sprayed minute amounts of beer, water and a <a href="/wiki/Sports_drink" title="Sports drink">sports drink</a> onto their tongues. Compared with the taste of the sports drink, the taste of beer significantly increased the participants desire to drink. Test results indicated that the flavour of the beer triggered a <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> release, even though alcohol content in the spray was insufficient for the purpose of becoming intoxicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some breweries have developed beers to <a href="/wiki/Foodpairing" title="Foodpairing">pair with food</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_190-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-190">[190]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beervana_191-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beervana-191">[191]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strong_193-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strong-193">[193]</a></sup> Wine writer <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Gluck" title="Malcolm Gluck">Malcolm Gluck</a> disputed the need to pair beer with food, while beer writers Roger Protz and Melissa Cole contested that claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_drinks">Related drinks</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Types_of_beer" title="Category:Types of beer">Category:Types of beer</a></div>
<p>Around the world, there are many traditional and ancient starch-based drinks classed as beer. In Africa, there are various ethnic beers made from <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Oshikundu" title="Oshikundu">Oshikundu</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup> in Namibia and <a href="/wiki/Tella" title="Tella">Tella</a> in Ethiopia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a> also has a beer made from millet; it is a low alcohol, somewhat porridge-like drink called "Bozo".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, Nepal, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> also use millet in <a href="/wiki/Chhaang" title="Chhaang">Chhaang</a>, a popular semi-fermented rice/millet drink in the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup> Further east in China are found <a href="/wiki/Huangjiu" title="Huangjiu">Huangjiu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Choujiu" title="Choujiu">Choujiu</a>—traditional rice-based drinks related to beer.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> in South America has <a href="/wiki/Chicha" title="Chicha">Chicha</a>, made from germinated maize (corn); while the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">indigenous peoples in Brazil</a> have <a href="/wiki/Cauim" title="Cauim">Cauim</a>, a traditional drink made since pre-Columbian times by chewing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manioc" title="Manioc">manioc</a> so that an enzyme (<a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>) present in human saliva can break down the starch into fermentable sugars;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201"><a href="#cite_note-201">[201]</a></sup> this is similar to Masato in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-202"><a href="#cite_note-202">[202]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some beers which are <a href="/wiki/Beer_from_bread" title="Beer from bread">made from bread</a>, which is linked to the earliest forms of beer, are <a href="/wiki/Sahti" title="Sahti">Sahti</a> in Finland, <a href="/wiki/Kvass" title="Kvass">Kvass</a> in Russia and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bouza_(beer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bouza (beer) (page does not exist)">Bouza</a> in Sudan.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beer_chemistry" title="Beer chemistry">Beer chemistry</a></div>
<p>Beer contains the phenolic acids <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxyphenylacetic_acid" title="4-hydroxyphenylacetic acid">4-hydroxyphenylacetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanillic_acid" title="Vanillic acid">vanillic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caffeic_acid" title="Caffeic acid">caffeic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syringic_acid" title="Syringic acid">syringic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/P-Coumaric_acid" title="P-Coumaric acid"><i>p</i>-coumaric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferulic_acid" title="Ferulic acid">ferulic acid</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinapic_acid" title="Sinapic acid">sinapic acid</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_hydrolysis" title="Alkaline hydrolysis">Alkaline hydrolysis</a> experiments show that most of the phenolic acids are present as bound forms and only a small portion can be detected as free compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">Hops</a>, and beer made with it, contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/8-prenylnaringenin" title="8-prenylnaringenin">8-prenylnaringenin</a> which is a potent <a href="/wiki/Phytoestrogen" title="Phytoestrogen">phytoestrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-204"><a href="#cite_note-204">[204]</a></sup> Hop also contains <a href="/wiki/Myrcene" title="Myrcene">myrcene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humulene" title="Humulene">humulene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xanthohumol" title="Xanthohumol">xanthohumol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoxanthohumol" title="Isoxanthohumol">isoxanthohumol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myrcenol" title="Myrcenol">myrcenol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linalool" title="Linalool">linalool</a>, tannins, and <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>. The alcohol <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-Methyl-2-butanol" title="2-Methyl-2-butanol">2M2B</a> is a component of hops brewing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herb2K_205-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herb2K-205">[205]</a></sup>
</p><p>Barley, in the form of malt, brings the condensed <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannins</a> <a href="/wiki/Prodelphinidin_B3" title="Prodelphinidin B3">prodelphinidins B3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prodelphinidin_B9" title="Prodelphinidin B9">B9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prodelphinidin_C2" title="Prodelphinidin C2">C2</a> into beer. <a href="/wiki/Tryptophol" title="Tryptophol">Tryptophol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyrosol" title="Tyrosol">tyrosol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phenethyl_alcohol" title="Phenethyl alcohol">phenylethanol</a> are aromatic higher alcohols found in beer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup> as secondary products of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_fermentation" title="Alcoholic fermentation">alcoholic fermentation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[207]</a></sup> (products also known as <a href="/wiki/Congener_(alcohol)" title="Congener (alcohol)">congeners</a>) by <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>.
</p>
