<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Effects of global warming on humans</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a></div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="1200" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg/220px-Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg/330px-Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg/440px-Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flooding_in_the_US_Midwest_June_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flooding in the U.S. Midwest, June 2008</div></div></div>
<p>Climate change has brought about possibly permanent alterations to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth's</a> geological, biological and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecological systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericasClimateChoices-2011-FullReport_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmericasClimateChoices-2011-FullReport-1">[1]</a></sup> These changes have led to the emergence of a not so large-scale <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">environmental hazards to human health</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, increased danger of wildland fires,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fire_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fire-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-4">[4]</a></sup> stresses to food-producing systems and the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health#Impact_on_infectious_diseases" title="Effects of global warming on human health">global spread of infectious diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-story_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-story-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, climatic changes are estimated to cause over 150,000 deaths annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>To date, a neglected aspect of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_debate" title="Climate change debate">climate change debate</a>, much less research has been conducted on the impacts of climate change on health, food supply, <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">migration</a>, security, societal change, and <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, than on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysical" title="Geophysical">geophysical</a> changes related to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Human impacts can be both negative and positive. Climatic changes in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, for instance, are expected to improve food production and local economic activity, at least in the short to medium term. Whereas, Bangladesh has experienced an increase in climate-sensitive diseases such as malaria, dengue, childhood diarrhoea, and pneumonia, among vulnerable communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Numerous studies suggest, however, that the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">current and future impacts of climate change</a> on human society are and will continue to be overwhelmingly negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The majority of the adverse effects of climate change are experienced by poor and low-income communities around the world, who have much higher levels of vulnerability to environmental determinants of health, <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> and other factors, and much lower levels of capacity available for coping with environmental change. A report on the global human impact of climate change published by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Humanitarian_Forum" title="Global Humanitarian Forum">Global Humanitarian Forum</a> in 2009, estimated more than 300,000 deaths and about $125 billion in economic losses each year, and indicating that most climate change induced mortality is due to worsening floods and droughts in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_vulnerabilities">Key vulnerabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Key vulnerabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the key vulnerabilities to climate change are related to climate phenomena that exceed thresholds for adaptation; such as extreme weather events or abrupt climate change, as well as limited access to resources (financial, technical, human, institutional) to cope. In 2007, the IPCC published a report of key vulnerabilities of industry, settlements, and society to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilbanks_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilbanks-11">[11]</a></sup> This assessment included a level of confidence for each key vulnerability:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Very high confidence</b>: Interactions between climate change and urbanization: this is most notable in developing countries, where urbanization is often focused in vulnerable coastal areas.</li>
<li><b>High confidence</b>:
<ul><li>Interactions between climate change and global economic growth: Stresses due to climate change are not only linked to the impacts of climate change, but also to the impacts of climate change policies. For example, these policies might affect development paths by requiring high cost fuel choices.</li>
<li>Fixed physical infrastructures that are important in meeting human needs: These include infrastructures that are susceptible to damage from extreme weather events or sea level rise, and infrastructures that are already close to being inadequate.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Medium confidence</b>: Interactions with governmental and social cultural structures that already face other pressures, e.g., limited economic resources.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_human_health" title="Effects of climate change on human health">Effects of climate change on human health</a></div>
<p>Climate change poses a wide range of risks to population health – risks that will increase in future decades, often to critical levels, if global climate change continues on its current trajectory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancet_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet-12">[12]</a></sup> The three main categories of health risks include: (i) direct-acting effects (e.g. due to <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>, amplified <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and physical weather disasters), (ii) impacts mediated via climate-related changes in ecological systems and relationships (e.g. crop yields, <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a> ecology, marine productivity), and (iii) the more diffuse (indirect) consequences relating to impoverishment, displacement, resource conflicts (e.g. water), and post-disaster mental health problems.
</p><p>Climate change thus threatens to slow, halt or reverse international progress towards reducing child under-nutrition, deaths from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrheal_disease" title="Diarrheal disease">diarrheal diseases</a> and the spread of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a>. Climate change acts predominantly by exacerbating the existing, often enormous, health problems, especially in the poorer parts of the world. Current variations in weather conditions already have many adverse impacts on the health of poor people in developing nations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and these too are likely to be 'multiplied' by the added stresses of climate change.
</p><p>A changing climate thus affects the prerequisites of population health: clean air and water, sufficient food, natural constraints on infectious disease agents, and the adequacy and security of shelter. A warmer and more variable climate leads to higher levels of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" title="Air pollutant">air pollutants</a>. It increases the rates and ranges of transmission of infectious diseases through unclean water and contaminated food, and by affecting <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vector</a> organisms (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquitoes" title="Mosquitoes">mosquitoes</a>) and intermediate or reservoir host species that harbour the infectious agent (such as cattle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LundeLindtjorn_2013CattleClimate_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-LundeLindtjorn_2013CattleClimate-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a>). Changes in temperature, rainfall and seasonality compromise agricultural production in many regions, including some of the least developed countries, thus jeopardising child health and growth and the overall health and functional capacity of adults. As warming proceeds, the severity (and perhaps frequency) of weather-related disasters will increase – and appears to have done so in a number of regions of the world over the past several decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Therefore, in summary, global warming, together with resultant changes in food and water supplies, can indirectly cause increases in a range of adverse health outcomes, including <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injuries" title="Injuries">injuries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">respiratory diseases</a>, and water-borne and insect-transmitted diseases.
</p><p>Health equity and climate change have a major impact on human health and quality of life, and are interlinked in a number of ways. The report of the WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health points out that disadvantaged communities are likely to shoulder a disproportionate share of the burden of climate change because of their increased exposure and vulnerability to health threats. Over 90 percent of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and diarrhea deaths are borne by children aged 5 years or younger, mostly in developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-16">[16]</a></sup> Other severely affected population groups include women, the elderly and people living in <a href="/wiki/Small_Island_Developing_States" title="Small Island Developing States">small island developing states</a> and other coastal regions, mega-cities or mountainous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hdr.undp.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-hdr.undp.org-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A 2011 article in the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Psychologist" title="American Psychologist">American Psychologist</a></i> identified three classes of psychological impacts from global climate change:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doherty_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doherty-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Direct - "Acute or traumatic effects of extreme weather events and a changed environment"</li>
<li>Indirect - "Threats to emotional well-being based on observation of impacts and concern or uncertainty about future risks"</li>
<li>Psychosocial – "Chronic social and community effects of heat, drought, migrations, and climate-related conﬂicts, and postdisaster adjustment"   A psychological impact is shown through peoples behaviours and how they act towards the actual situation. The topic of climate change is very complex and difficult for people to understand, which effects how they act upon it. It is shown by Ranney and Clark(2016)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> that by informing people to make them understand the topic of climate science clearly, it promotes the change in behaviour towards mitigation of climate change.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather_events">Extreme weather events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extreme weather events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a></div>
<p>This trend towards more variability and fluctuation is perhaps more important, in terms of its impact on human health, than that of a gradual and long-term trend towards higher average temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-20">[20]</a></sup> Infectious disease often accompanies <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, such as floods, earthquakes and drought.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These local epidemics occur due to loss of infrastructure, such as hospitals and sanitation services, but also because of changes in local ecology and environment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_infectious_diseases" title="Effects of global warming on infectious diseases">Effects of global warming on infectious diseases</a></div>
<p>Climate change may lead to dramatic increases in prevalence of a variety of infectious diseases. Beginning in the mid-'70s, there has been an “emergence, resurgence and redistribution of infectious diseases”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-20">[20]</a></sup> Reasons for this are likely multi-causal, dependent on a variety of social, environmental and climatic factors, however, many argue that the “volatility of infectious disease may be one of the earliest biological expressions of climate instability”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Epstein_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Epstein-20">[20]</a></sup> Though many infectious diseases are affected by changes in climate, vector-borne diseases, such as malaria, <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a> and leishmaniasis, present the strongest <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">causal relationship</a>. One major reason that change in climate increases the prevalence of vector borne disease is that temperature and rainfall play a key role in the distribution, magnitude, and viral capacity of mosquitoes, who are primary vectors for many vector borne diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-21">[21]</a></sup> Observation and research detect a shift of pests and pathogens in the distribution away from the equator and towards Earth's poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A tool that has been used to predict this distribution trend is the Dynamic Mosquito Simulation Process (DyMSiM). DyMSiM uses epidemiological and entomological data and practices to model future mosquito distributions based upon climate conditions and mosquitos living in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This modeling technique helps identify the distribution of specific species of mosquito, some of which are more susceptible to viral infection than others.
</p><p>Beyond distribution, rising temperatures can decrease viral incubation time <i>in vivo</i> in vectors increasing the viral transmissibility leading to increases in infection rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaria">Malaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Malaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increased <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> like <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> could increase the number of <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitos</a> indirectly by expanding larval habitat and food supply. Malaria kills approximately 300,000 children (under age 5) annually, poses an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imminent_threat" title="Imminent threat">imminent threat</a> through temperature increase .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malaria_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-25">[25]</a></sup> Models suggest, conservatively, that risk of malaria will increase 5-15% by 2100 due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bhattacharya_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bhattacharya-26">[26]</a></sup> In Africa alone, according to the MARA Project (Mapping Malaria Risk in Africa),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mara_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-mara-27">[27]</a></sup> there is a projected increase of 16–28% in person-month exposures to malaria by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2453" data-file-width="3553" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aedes_aegypti_CDC9253.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aedes aegypti, the mosquito that is the vector for dengue transmission.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Dengue</b>
</p><p>There are 4 distinct viruses responsible for Dengue: DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, and DENV-4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-21">[21]</a></sup>. <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">Dengue fever</a> is spread by the bite of the female mosquito known as <a href="/wiki/Aedes_aegypti" title="Aedes aegypti"><i>Aedes aegypti.</i></a> This species of mosquito can travel up to 400 meters in search of water to lay their eggs, but often remain closer to human habitation. A <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquito</a> becomes infected with dengue when it bites and takes the blood of an infected human. After approximately one week, the mosquito can then transmit the dengue infection to other humans through her bite. While dengue cannot be spread from person to person, an infected person can infect more mosquitos, thus, furthering the spread of the disease. Overall, the female mosquito is a highly effective vector of this disease<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>. 
</p><p>When bitten by an infected mosquito, dengue has an <a href="/wiki/Incubation_period" title="Incubation period">incubation period</a> of 4-10 days. Once infected with the dengue virus, humans experience severe flu-like symptoms. Also known as "break-bone fever", dengue can affect infants, children, and adults and can be fatal. Those infected exhibit a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_fever" title="High fever">high fever</a> (40°C/ 104°F) along with at least two of the following symptoms: severe headache, pain behind the eye, nausea, vomiting, swollen glands, muscle and joint pains, and rash. These symptoms usually last 2-7 days. Dengue can become fatal due to plasma leaking, fluid accumulation, respiratory distress, severe bleeding, or organ impairment. Warning signs of this include a decrease in temperature decrease (below 38°C/ 100°F) in conjunction with: severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, rapid breathing, bleeding gums, blood in vomit, and/or fatigue and restlessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Where the mosquito, <i>Aedes aegypti</i>, lives and the amount of mosquitos present is strongly influenced by the amount of water-bearing containers or pockets of standstill water in an area, daily temperature and variation in temperature, moisture, and solar radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> While dengue fever is primarily considered a tropical and subtropical disease, the geographic ranges of the aedes aegypti are expanding. Globalization, trade, travel, demographic trends, and warming temperatures are all attributed to the recent spread to this primary vector of dengue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dengue is now ranked as the most important vector-borne viral disease in the world. Today, an estimated 50–100 million dengue fever infections occur annually.  In just the past 50 years, transmission has increased drastically with new cases of the disease (incidence) increasing 30-fold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[32]</a></sup> Once localized to a few areas in the tropics, dengue fever is now endemic in over 100 countries in Southeast Asia, the Americas, Africa, the Eastern Mediterranean, and the Western Pacific with Southeast Asia and the Western Pacific regions being the most seriously affected. Recently the number of reported cases has continually increased along with dengue spreading to new areas. Explosive outbreaks are also occurring. Moreover, there is the possible threat of outbreak in Europe with local transmission of dengue being reported for the first time in France and Croatia in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>One country that has seen significant impacts from dengue is <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. Dengue has been endemic in Bangladesh since its first major outbreak in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup> With its high population, shifting weather patterns, and low and flat geography that is only one meter above sea level, Bangladesh is also one of the world’s most vulnerable countries when it comes to climate change<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  Climate change is predicted to increase temperatures and precipitation, both of which affect dengue transmission, as dengue is weather dependent, and most often occurs in wetter and warmer climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">[36]</a></sup> Standing water allows habitats and breeding grounds for the mosquito vector, while warmer temperatures assist in larval development, replication of the virus, and period of infectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup> Studies have found lag effects of, on average, two months between high temperatures and dengue transmission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>, indicating the time that has lapsed between observed weather changes and new observed dengue cases.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="665" data-file-width="2047" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg/220px-Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg/330px-Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg/440px-Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Skyscraper_City_View_Dhaka_at_Panthapath_More.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>City of Dhaka</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dhaka" title="Dhaka">Dhaka</a> is Bangladesh's biggest city, and also the highest risk area in Bangladesh for transmission of dengue, with its topical climate and population of approximately 11.8 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup> The annual average temperature in Dhaka is 25°C and almost all of the average rainfall occurs during May through September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup> If higher temperature and increased precipitation continue, we could see temperatures increase and the rainy season extended, leading to an increased transmission period for dengue. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>There were 25,059 cases of dengue in Dhaka from 2000 to 2010, with an average of 168 cases a month<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>. While dengue testing is frequently performed in the private health care setting; it is frequently underperformed in the public health care setting, due to lack of testing accessibility<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">[36]</a></sup>. This indicates that there are potentially more cases of dengue than are getting diagnosed or reported.
</p><p>Dengue incidence has only increased in the last few decades, and is projected to continue to do so with changing climate conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-5"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup> There have been prediction models of temperature created to project the effects of global warming on the planet. Based on these, the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> estimates that the mean annual temperature of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> will have increased by 3.3 degrees Celsius by 2100, assuming no other changes.. Taking this estimate, researchers predict an increase of 16, 030 cases in Dhaka, Bangladesh by the year 2100. This represents a 40-times increase in dengue incidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-6"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increased <a href="/wiki/Public_health_surveillance" title="Public health surveillance">public health surveillance</a> and preparation is needed in areas like Bangladesh that are seeing an upward trend in climatic changes and <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vector-borne disease</a> like dengue virus. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-7"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-climatic_determinants">Non-climatic determinants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-climatic determinants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sociodemographic factors include, but are not limited to: patterns of <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">human migration</a> and travel, effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> and medical infrastructure in controlling and treating disease, the extent of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">anti-malarial</a> <a href="/wiki/Drug_resistance" title="Drug resistance">drug resistance</a>  and the underlying health status  of the population at hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infectious_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-infectious-38">[38]</a></sup> Environmental factors include: changes in <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land-use</a> (e.g. deforestation), expansion of agricultural and water development projects (which tend to increase mosquito breeding habitat), and the overall trend towards urbanization (i.e. increased concentration of human hosts). Patz and Olson argue that these changes in landscape can alter local weather more than long term climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malaria_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-25">[25]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and cultivation of natural swamps in the African highlands has created conditions favourable for the survival of mosquito larvae, and has, in part, led to the increasing incidence of malaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-malaria_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-malaria-25">[25]</a></sup> The effects of these non-climatic factors complicate things and make a direct causal relationship between climate change and malaria difficult to confirm. It is highly unlikely that climate exerts an isolated effect.
</p><p><b>Preparing for the Future</b>
</p><p>Effective policies which take into consideration predictive climate change models and measures are key to preparing for and managing changes in incidence and reestablishment of diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-39">[39]</a></sup> As climate change continues to alter where diseases are prevalent, harmonizing surveillance systems on a multi-national scale will be vital to improve evidence-based disease control and decision making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-39">[39]</a></sup> Implementation of vaccination and other prevention measures as well as increasing community education and awareness and education of the impacts of the disease and other adverse health events among public decision makers will help prepare and combat changes in disease rates and location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-:6-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate change may dramatically impact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss">habitat loss</a>, for example, <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> conditions may cause the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, as has occurred in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A sustained <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet-bulb temperature</a> exceeding 35° is a threshold at which the resilience of human systems is no longer able to adequately cool the skin. A study by NOAA from 2013 concluded that heat stress will reduce labor capacity considerably under current emissions scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> There is evidence to show that high temperatures can increase mortality rates among fetuses and children<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Although the main focus is often on the health impacts and risks of higher temperatures, it should be remembered that they also reduce learning and worker productivity, which can impact a country's economy and development.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">Water crisis</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> resources that humans rely on are highly sensitive to variations in weather and climate.  In 2007, the IPCC reported with high confidence that climate change has a net negative impact on water resources and freshwater ecosystems in all regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc2007_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc2007-43">[43]</a></sup>  The IPCC also found with very high confidence that arid and semi-arid areas are particularly exposed to freshwater impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc2007_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc2007-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>As the climate warms, it changes the nature of global rainfall, evaporation, snow, stream flow and other factors that affect water supply and quality. Specific impacts include:
</p>
<ul><li>Warmer water temperatures affect water quality and accelerate <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSE_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISSE-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sea level rise is projected to increase salt-water intrusion into <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> in some regions.  This reduces the amount of freshwater available for drinking and farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSE_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-ISSE-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li>In some areas, shrinking glaciers and snow deposits threaten the water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Areas that depend on melted water runoff will likely see that runoff depleted, with less flow in the late summer and spring peaks occurring earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISSE_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-ISSE-44">[44]</a></sup> This can affect the ability to <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigate</a> crops. (This situation is particularly acute for irrigation in South America,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peru2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-peru2-47">[47]</a></sup> for irrigation and drinking supplies in Central Asia, and for hydropower in Norway, the Alps, and the Pacific Northwest of North America.)</li>
<li>Increased extreme weather means more water falls on hardened ground unable to absorb it, leading to flash floods instead of a replenishment of soil moisture or groundwater levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>Increased evaporation will reduce the effectiveness of reservoirs.</li>
<li>At the same time, human demand for water will grow for the purposes of cooling and hydration.</li>
<li>Increased precipitation can lead to changes in water-borne and vector-borne diseases. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Displacement_and_migration">Displacement and migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Displacement and migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">Environmental migrant</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Refugee_camp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Refugee_camp.jpg/220px-Refugee_camp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Refugee_camp.jpg/330px-Refugee_camp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Refugee_camp.jpg/440px-Refugee_camp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Refugee_camp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A refugee camp</div></div></div>
<p>Climate change causes displacement of people in several ways, the most obvious—and dramatic—being through the increased number and severity of weather-related disasters which destroy homes and habitats causing people to seek shelter or livelihoods elsewhere. Effects of climate change such as <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rising_sea_levels" title="Rising sea levels">rising sea levels</a> gradually erode livelihood and force communities to abandon traditional homelands for more accommodating environments. This is currently happening in areas of Africa’s <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>, the semi-arid belt that spans the continent just below its northern deserts. Deteriorating environments triggered by climate change can also lead to increased conflict over resources which in turn can displace people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> has estimated that 150 million environmental migrants will exist by the year 2050, due mainly to the effects of coastal flooding, shoreline erosion and agricultural disruption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  However, the IPCC also cautions that it is extremely difficult to measure the extent of environmental migration due to the complexity of the issue and a lack of data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilbanks_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilbanks-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, more than 42 million people were displaced in Asia and the Pacific during 2010 and 2011, more than twice the population of Sri Lanka. This figure includes those displaced by storms, floods, and heat and cold waves. Still others were displaced drought and sea-level rise. Most of those compelled to leave their homes eventually returned when conditions improved, but an undetermined number became migrants, usually within their country, but also across national borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Asia and the Pacific is the global area most prone to natural disasters, both in terms of the absolute number of disasters and of populations affected. It is highly exposed to climate impacts, and is home to highly vulnerable population groups, who are disproportionately poor and marginalized. A recent Asian Development Bank report highlights “environmental hot spots” that are particular risk of flooding, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a>, and water stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beta.adb.org_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-beta.adb.org-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Pacific Ocean island nations, such as <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> are considering the eventual possibility of evacuation, as flood defense may become economically unrealistic. Tuvalu already has an ad hoc agreement with New Zealand to allow phased relocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> However, for some islanders relocation is not an option. They are not willing to leave their homes, land and families. Some simply don’t know the threat that climate change has on their island and this is mainly down to the lack of awareness that climate change even exists. In Vutia on Viti Levu, Fiji’s main island, half the respondents to a survey had not heard of climate change (Lata and Nuun 2012). Even where there is awareness many believe that it is a problem caused by developed countries and should therefore be solved by developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Governments have considered various approaches to reduce migration compelled by environmental conditions in at-risk communities, including programs of social protection, livelihoods development, basic urban infrastructure development, and disaster risk management.  Some experts even support migration as an appropriate way for people to cope with environmental changes.  However, this is controversial because migrants – particularly low-skilled ones – are among the most vulnerable people in society and are often denied basic protections and access to services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beta.adb.org_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-beta.adb.org-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change is only one factor that may contribute to a household's decision to migrate; other factors may include <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">employment</a> options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> For this reason, it is difficult to classify <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">environmental migrants</a> as actual "refugees" as legally defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNHCR" title="UNHCR">UNHCR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNHCR_on_environmental_migration_terminology_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNHCR_on_environmental_migration_terminology-57">[57]</a></sup> Neither the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="UN Framework Convention on Climate Change">UN Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> nor its <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, an international agreement on climate change, includes any provisions concerning specific assistance or protection for those who will be directly affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>In small islands and <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">megadeltas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">inundation</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> is expected to threaten vital infrastructure and human settlements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> This could lead to issues of <a href="/wiki/Statelessness" title="Statelessness">statelessness</a> for populations in countries such as the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_refugee" title="Climate refugee">homelessness</a> in countries with low-lying areas such as <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.
</p><p>The World Bank predicts that a “severe hit” will spur conflict and migration across the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_security" title="Climate security">Climate security</a></div>
<p>Climate change has the potential to exacerbate existing tensions or create new ones — serving as a threat multiplier. It can be a catalyst for violent conflict and a threat to international security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> A meta-analysis of over 50 quantitative studies that examine the link between climate and conflict found that "for each 1 standard deviation (1σ) change in climate toward warmer temperatures or more extreme rainfall, median estimates indicate that the frequency of interpersonal violence rises 4% and the frequency of intergroup conflict rises 14%".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The IPCC has suggested that the disruption of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_migrant" title="Environmental migrant">environmental migration</a> may serve to exacerbate conflicts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> though they are less confident of the role of increased resource scarcity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilbanks_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-wilbanks-11">[11]</a></sup> Of course, climate change does not always lead to violence, and conflicts are often caused by multiple interconnected factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>A variety of experts have warned that climate change may lead to increased conflict.  The <a href="/wiki/Military_Advisory_Board" title="Military Advisory Board">Military Advisory Board</a>, a panel of retired U.S. generals and admirals, predicted that global warming will serve as a "threat multiplier" in already volatile regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Strategic_and_International_Studies" title="Center for Strategic and International Studies">Center for Strategic and International Studies</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_a_New_American_Security" title="Center for a New American Security">Center for a New American Security</a>, two Washington think tanks, have reported that flooding "has the potential to challenge regional and even national identities," leading to "armed conflict over resources."  They indicate that the greatest threat would come from "large-scale migrations of people — both inside nations and across existing national borders."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>  However, other researchers have been more skeptical: One study found no statistically meaningful relationship between climate and conflict using data from Europe between the years 1000 and 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>The link between climate change and security is a concern for authorities across the world, including <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_77" title="Group of 77">G77</a> group of developing nations.  Climate change's impact as a security threat is expected to hit developing nations particularly hard. In Britain, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Foreign Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Beckett" title="Margaret Beckett">Margaret Beckett</a> has argued that "An unstable climate will exacerbate some of the core drivers of conflict, such as migratory pressures and competition for resources."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
The links between the human impact of climate change and the threat of violence and armed conflict are particularly important because multiple destabilizing conditions are affected simultaneously.
</p><p>Experts have suggested links to climate change in several major conflicts:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/War_in_Darfur" title="War in Darfur">War in Darfur</a>, where sustained drought encouraged conflict between herders and farmers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Syrian_Civil_War" title="Syrian Civil War">Syrian Civil War</a>, preceded by the displacement of 1.5 million people due to drought-induced crop and livestock failure<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist_insurgency_in_Nigeria" title="Islamist insurgency in Nigeria">Islamist insurgency in Nigeria</a>, which exploited natural resource shortages to fuel anti-government sentiment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Somali_Civil_War" title="Somali Civil War">Somali Civil War</a>, in which droughts and extreme high temperatures have been linked to violence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Additionally, researchers studying ancient climate patterns (<a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>) have shown that long-term fluctuations of war frequency and population changes have followed cycles of temperature change since the preindustrial era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> A 2016 study finds that "drought can contribute to sustaining conflict, especially for agriculturally dependent groups and politically excluded groups in very poor countries. These results suggest a reciprocal nature–society interaction in which violent conflict and environmental shock constitute a vicious circle, each phenomenon increasing the group’s vulnerability to the other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_impacts">Social impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Social impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_poverty" title="Climate change and poverty">Climate change and poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_gender" title="Climate change and gender">Climate change and gender</a></div>
<p>The consequences of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_poverty" title="Climate change and poverty">climate change and poverty</a> are not distributed uniformly within communities. Individual and social factors such as gender, age, education, ethnicity, geography and language lead to differential <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability" title="Vulnerability">vulnerability</a> and capacity to adapt to the effects of climate change. Climate change effects such as hunger, poverty and diseases like diarrhea and malaria, disproportionately impact children; about 90 percent of malaria and diarrhea deaths are among young children. Children are also 14–44 percent more likely to die from environmental factors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> again leaving them the most vulnerable.Those in urban areas will be affected by lower air quality and overcrowding, and will struggle the most to better their situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_effects_of_extreme_weather">Social effects of extreme weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Social effects of extreme weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_costliest_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of costliest Atlantic hurricanes">List of costliest Atlantic hurricanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Physical impacts of climate change">Physical impacts of climate change</a></div>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> explains, "recent increase in societal impact from tropical cyclones has largely been caused by rising concentrations of population and infrastructure in coastal regions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Pielke <i>et al.</i> (2008) normalized mainland U.S. hurricane damage from 1900 to 2005 to 2005 values and found no remaining trend of increasing absolute damage. The 1970s and 1980s were notable because of the extremely low amounts of damage compared to other decades. The decade 1996–2005 has the second most damage among the past 11 decades, with only the decade 1926–1935 surpassing its costs. The most damaging single storm is the <a href="/wiki/1926_Miami_hurricane" title="1926 Miami hurricane">1926 Miami hurricane</a>, with $157 billion of normalized damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>The American <i>Insurance Journal</i> predicted that "catastrophe losses should be expected to double roughly every 10 years because of increases in construction costs, increases in the number of structures and changes in their characteristics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The Association of British Insurers has stated that limiting carbon emissions would avoid 80% of the projected additional annual cost of tropical cyclones by the 2080s. The cost is also increasing partly because of building in exposed areas such as coasts and floodplains. The ABI claims that reduction of the vulnerability to some inevitable effects of climate change, for example through more resilient buildings and improved flood defences, could also result in considerable cost-savings in the longterm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_settlement">Human settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Human settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">Sea level rise</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_sea_level" title="Future sea level">Future sea level</a></div>
<p>A major challenge for human settlements is <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, indicated by ongoing observation and research of rapid declines in ice-mass balance from both Greenland and Antarctica. Estimates for 2100 are at least twice as large as previously estimated by IPCC AR4, with an upper limit of about two meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copenhagen_Diagnosis_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Copenhagen_Diagnosis-90">[90]</a></sup> Depending on regional changes, increased precipitation patterns can cause more flooding or extended drought stresses water resources.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coasts_and_low-lying_areas">Coasts and low-lying areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Coasts and low-lying areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For historical reasons to do with <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, many of the world's largest and most prosperous cities are on the <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coast</a>. In developing countries, the poorest often live on <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a>, because it is the only available space, or fertile agricultural land. These settlements often lack infrastructure such as <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">dykes</a> and early warning systems. Poorer communities also tend to lack the insurance, savings, or access to credit needed to recover from disasters.
</p><p>In a journal paper, Nicholls and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Tol" title="Richard Tol">Tol</a> (2006) considered the effects of sea level rise:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The most vulnerable future worlds to sea-level rise appear to be the A2 and B2 [IPCC] <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">scenarios</a>, which primarily reflects differences in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socio-economic" title="Socio-economic">socio-economic</a> situation (coastal population, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">Gross Domestic Product</a> (GDP) and GDP/capita), rather than the magnitude of sea-level rise. Small islands and deltaic settings stand out as being more vulnerable as shown in many earlier analyses. Collectively, these results suggest that human societies will have more choice in how they respond to sea-level rise than is often assumed. However, this conclusion needs to be tempered by recognition that we still do not understand these choices and significant impacts remain possible.</p></blockquote>
<p>The IPCC reported that socioeconomic impacts of climate change in coastal and low-lying areas would be overwhelmingly adverse. The following impacts were projected with very high confidence:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Coastal and low-lying areas would be exposed to increasing risks including coastal erosion due to climate change and sea level rise.</li>
<li>By the 2080s, millions of people would experience floods every year due to sea level rise. The numbers affected were projected to be largest in the densely populated and low-lying mega-deltas of Asia and Africa; and smaller islands were judged to be especially vulnerable.</li></ul>
<p>A study in the April 2007 issue of <i>Environment and Urbanization</i> reports that 634 million people live in coastal areas within 30 feet (9.1 m) of sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>  The study also reported that about two thirds of the world's cities with over five million people are located in these low-lying coastal areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_sector">Energy sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Energy sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Oil.2C_coal_and_natural_gas"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil,_coal_and_natural_gas">Oil, coal and natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Oil, coal and natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oil and natural gas infrastructure is vulnerable to the effects of climate change and the increased risk<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of disasters such as <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a> and long-term <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">increases in sea level</a>. Minimising these risks by building in less disaster prone areas, can be expensive and impossible in countries with coastal locations or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_state" title="Island state">island states</a>. All <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a> depend on water to cool them.  Not only is there increased demand for fresh water, but climate change can increase the likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_shortage" title="Water shortage">fresh water shortages</a>. Another impact for thermal power plants, is that increasing the temperatures in which they operate reduces their efficiency and hence their output. The source of oil often comes from areas prone to high natural disaster risks; such as tropical storms, hurricanes, cyclones, and floods. An example is <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>'s impact on oil extraction in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, as it destroyed 126 oil and gas platforms and damaged 183 more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, previously pristine arctic areas will now be available for <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">resource extraction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear">Nuclear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Nuclear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate change, along with extreme weather and natural disasters can affect nuclear power plants in a similar way to those using oil, coal, and natural gas. However, the impact of water shortages on nuclear power plants cooled by rivers will be greater than on other thermal power plants.  This is because old reactor designs with water-cooled cores must run at lower internal temperatures and thus, paradoxically, must dump more heat to the environment to produce a given amount of electricity. This situation has forced some nuclear reactors to be shut down and will do so again unless the cooling systems of these plants are enhanced to provide more capacity.  Nuclear power supply was diminished by low river ﬂow rates and droughts, which meant rivers had reached the maximum temperatures for cooling. Such shutdowns happened in France during the 2003 and 2006 heat waves.  During the heat waves, 17 reactors had to limit output or shut down. 77% of French electricity is produced by nuclear power; and in 2009 a similar situation created a 8GW shortage, and forced the French government to import electricity. Other Cases have been reported from Germany, where extreme temperatures have reduced nuclear power production 9 times due to high temperatures between 1979 and 2007. In particular: 
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Unterweser_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Unterweser Nuclear Power Plant">Unterweser nuclear power plant</a> reduced output by 90% between June and September 2003.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Isar_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Isar Nuclear Power Plant">Isar nuclear power plant</a> cut production by 60% for 14 days due to excess river temperatures and low stream ﬂow in the river Isar in 2006.</li></ul>
<p>Similar events have happened elsewhere in Europe during those same hot summers. Many scientists agree that if <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> continues, this disruption is likely to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectricity">Hydroelectricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hydroelectricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changes in the amount of river flow will correlate with the amount of energy produced by a dam. Lower river flows because of drought, climate change, or upstream dams and diversions, will reduce the amount of live storage in a reservoir; therefore reducing the amount of water that can be used for hydroelectricity. The result of diminished river flow can be a power shortage in areas that depend heavily on hydroelectric power. The risk of flow shortage may increase as a result of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Studies from the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> in the United States suggests that modest climate changes (such as a 2 degree change in Celsius that could result in a 10% decline in precipitation), might reduce river run-off by up to 40%. <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in particular, is vulnerable due to its having reliance on hydroelectricity as increasing temperatures, lower water ﬂow, and alterations in the rainfall regime, could reduce total energy production by 7% annually by the end of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_94-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific evidence for links between global warming and the increasing cost of natural disasters due to weather events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fivethirtyeight032114_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-fivethirtyeight032114-96">[96]</a></sup> is weak, but, nevertheless, prominent mainstream environmental spokesmen such as Barack Obama and Al Gore have emphasized the possible connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peilke6_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peilke6-97">[97]</a></sup> For the most part increased costs due to events such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fivethirtyeight031915_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-fivethirtyeight031915-98">[98]</a></sup> are due to increased exposure to loss resulting from building insured facilities in vulnerable locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peilke21_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peilke21-99">[99]</a></sup> This information has been denounced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">Paul Krugman</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT032314_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT032314-100">[100]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/ThinkProgress" title="ThinkProgress">ThinkProgress</a> as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">climate change denial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thinkprogress031914_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-thinkprogress031914-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance">Insurance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Insurance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An industry directly affected by the risks of climate change is the <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> According to a 2005 report from the Association of British Insurers, limiting carbon emissions could avoid 80% of the projected additional annual cost of tropical cyclones by the 2080s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> A June 2004 report by the Association of British Insurers declared "Climate change is not a remote issue for future generations to deal with; it is, in various forms here already, impacting on insurers' businesses now."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> The report noted that weather-related risks for households and property were already increasing by 2–4% per year due to the changing weather conditions, and claims for storm and flood damages in the UK had doubled to over £6 billion over the period from 1998–2003 compared to the previous five years. The results are rising insurance premiums, and the risk that in some areas <a href="/wiki/Flood_insurance" title="Flood insurance">flood insurance</a> will become unaffordable for those in the lower income brackets.
</p><p>Financial institutions, including the world's two largest insurance companies: <a href="/wiki/Munich_Re" title="Munich Re">Munich Re</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Re" title="Swiss Re">Swiss Re</a>, warned in a 2002 study that "the increasing frequency of severe climatic events, coupled with social trends could cost almost 150 billion <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a> each year in the next decade".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> These costs would burden customers, taxpayers, and the insurance industry, with increased costs related to insurance and disaster relief.
</p><p>In the United States, insurance losses have also greatly increased. It has been shown that a 1% climb in annual precipitation can increase catastrophe loss by as much as 2.8%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-choifisher_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-choifisher-106">[106]</a></sup> Gross increases are mostly attributed to increased population and property values in <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability" title="Vulnerability">vulnerable</a> coastal areas; though there was also an increase in frequency of weather-related events like heavy rainfalls since the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_humans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roads, airport runways, railway lines and pipelines, (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_pipeline" title="Oil pipeline">oil pipelines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_main" title="Water main">water mains</a> etc.) may require increased maintenance and renewal as they become subject to greater temperature variation. Regions already adversely affected include areas of <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>, which are subject to high levels of <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>, resulting in buckling roads, sunken foundations, and severely cracked runways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
