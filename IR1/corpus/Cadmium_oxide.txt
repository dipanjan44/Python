<p><b>Cadmium oxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">Cd</a>O. It is one of the main precursors to other cadmium compounds. It crystallizes in a cubic rocksalt lattice like <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral" title="Octahedral">octahedral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It occurs naturally as the rare mineral monteponite. Cadmium oxide can be found as a colorless amorphous powder or as brown or red crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189-12">[12]</a></sup> Cadmium oxide is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" title="N-type semiconductor">n-type semiconductor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> with a band gap of 2.18 eV (2.31 eV) at room temperature (298 K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_structure">Production and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since cadmium compounds are often found in association with <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ores, cadmium oxide is a common by-product of zinc <a href="/wiki/Refining_(metallurgy)" title="Refining (metallurgy)">refining</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is produced by burning elemental cadmium in air. Pyrolysis of other cadmium compounds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_nitrate" title="Cadmium nitrate">nitrate</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a>, also affords this oxide. When pure, it is red, but CdO is unusual in being available in many differing colours due to its tendency to form <a href="/wiki/Colour_centre" title="Colour centre">defect structures</a> resulting from anion vacancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Cadmium oxide is prepared commercially by oxidizing cadmium vapor in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium oxide is used in cadmium plating baths, electrodes for storage batteries, cadmium salts, catalyst, ceramic glazes, phosphors, and nematocide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189-12">[12]</a></sup> Major uses for cadmium oxide are as an ingredient for electroplating baths, and in pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transparent_conductor">Transparent conductor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transparent conductor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CdO is used as a transparent conductive material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which was prepared as a <a href="/wiki/Transparent_conducting_film" title="Transparent conducting film">transparent conducting film</a> as early as 1907 by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Baedeker_(scientist)" title="Karl Baedeker (scientist)">Karl Baedeker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Cadmium oxide in the form of thin films has been used in applications such as photodiodes, phototransistors, photovoltaic cells, transparent electrodes, liquid crystal displays, IR detectors, and anti reflection coatings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> CdO microparticles undergo bandgap excitation when exposed to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV-A</a> light and is also selective in phenol photodegradation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadmium_plating">Cadmium plating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cadmium plating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most commercial electroplating of cadmium is done by electrodeposition from cyanide baths. These cyanide baths consist of cadmium oxide and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_cyanide" title="Sodium cyanide">sodium cyanide</a> in water, which likely form <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_cyanide" title="Cadmium cyanide">cadmium cyanide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. A typical formula is 32 g/L cadmium oxide and 75 g/L sodium cyanide. The cadmium concentration may vary by as much as 50%. Brighteners are usually added to the bath and the plating is done at room temperature with high purity cadmium anodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">Reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CdO is a basic oxide and is thus attacked by aqueous acids to give solutions of [Cd(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>. Upon treatment with strong alkaline solutions, <span class="chemf nowrap">[Cd(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> forms.
A thin coat of cadmium oxide forms on the surface of cadmium in moist air at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis,_Richard_J._1997,_p._189-12">[12]</a></sup> Cadmium will oxidize at room temperatures to form CdO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103-23">[23]</a></sup> Cadmium vapor and steam will form CdO and hydrogen in a reversible reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Clifford_A._Hampel_1954,_p._87-103-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
