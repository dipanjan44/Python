<p><b>Cerium(IV) oxide</b>, also known as <b>ceric oxide</b>, <b>ceric dioxide</b>, <b>ceria</b>, <b>cerium oxide</b> or <b>cerium dioxide</b>,  is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metal" title="Rare-earth metal">rare-earth metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>. It is a pale yellow-white powder with the chemical formula CeO<sub>2</sub>.  It is an important commercial product and an intermediate in the purification of the element from the ores.  The distinctive property of this material is its reversible conversion to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_oxide" title="Nonstoichiometric oxide">nonstoichiometric oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cerium occurs naturally as a mixture with other rare-earth elements in its principal ores <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bastnaesite" title="Bastnaesite">bastnaesite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>.  After extraction of the metal ions into aqueous base, Ce is separated from that mixture by addition of an oxidant followed by adjustment of the pH.  This step exploits the low solubility of CeO<sub>2</sub> and the fact that other rare-earth elements resist oxidation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cerium(IV) oxide is formed by the <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cerium_oxalate" title="Cerium oxalate">cerium oxalate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cerium_hydroxide" title="Cerium hydroxide">cerium hydroxide</a>.
</p><p>Cerium also forms <a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_oxide" title="Cerium(III) oxide">cerium(III) oxide</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">Ce<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which is unstable and will oxidize to cerium(IV) oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_defect_behavior">Structure and defect behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and defect behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

At high temperatures it releases oxygen to give a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric, anion deficient form</a> that retains the fluorite lattice.  This material has the formula CeO<sub>(2−<i>x</i>)</sub>, where 0 &lt; <i>x</i> &lt; 0.28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayes-4">[4]</a></sup> The value of <i>x</i> depends on both the temperature and the oxygen partial pressure. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defect_chemistry">Defect chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Defect chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the most stable <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> phase of ceria, it exhibits several defects depending on partial pressure of oxygen or stress state of the material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary defects of concern are oxygen vacancies and small polarons (electrons localized on cerium cations). Increases in the number of oxygen defects increases the diffusion rate of oxide in the lattice as reflected in an increase in <a href="/wiki/Ionic_conductivity_(solid_state)" title="Ionic conductivity (solid state)">ionic conductivity</a>.  These factors recommend ceria as a solid electrolyte in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-oxide_fuel_cell" title="Solid-oxide fuel cell">solid-oxide fuel cells</a>. Undoped and doped ceria also exhibit high electronic conductivity at low partial pressures of oxygen due to reduction of the cerium ion leading to the formation of small <a href="/wiki/Polaron" title="Polaron">polarons</a>.  Since the oxygen atoms in a ceria crystal occur in planes, diffusion of these anions is facile. The diffusion rate increases as the defect concentration increases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polishing_applications">Polishing applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polishing applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal application of ceria is for polishing, especially <a href="/wiki/Chemical-mechanical_planarization" title="Chemical-mechanical planarization">chemical-mechanical planarization</a> (CMP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>  For this purpose, it has displaced many other oxides that were previously used, such as <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconia" title="Zirconia">zirconia</a>. For hobbyists, it is also known as "opticians' rouge".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CeO<sub>2</sub> is used to decolorize glass by converting green-tinted ferrous impurities to nearly colorless ferric oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cerium oxide has found use in <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared filters</a>, as an oxidizing species in <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a> and as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">incandescent mantles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysis">Catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The interconvertibility of CeO<sub><i>x</i></sub> materials is the basis of the use of ceria for an oxidation catalyst.  One small but illustrative use is its use in the walls of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-cleaning_ovens" title="Self-cleaning ovens">self-cleaning ovens</a> as a hydrocarbon oxidation catalyst during the high-temperature cleaning process.  Another small scale but famous example is its role in oxidation of natural gas in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">gas mantles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1059" data-file-width="1017" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/220px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/330px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/440px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg" title="실제 크기로"></a></div>A glowing <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Company" title="Coleman Company">Coleman</a> <a href="/wiki/White_gas" title="White gas">white gas</a> lantern mantle.  The glowing element is mainly <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">ThO<sub>2</sub></a> doped with CeO<sub>2</sub>, heated by the Ce-catalyzed oxidation of the natural gas with air.</div></div></div>
<p>Ceria has been used as a sensor in <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a> in automotive applications, controlling the air-exhaust ratio to reduce <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_conduction">Mixed conduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mixed conduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the significant <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electronic</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">conduction</a> of cerium oxide, it is well suited to be used as a <a href="/wiki/Mixed_conductor" title="Mixed conductor">mixed conductor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPG_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPG-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_cells">Fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ceria is of interest as a material for <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_fuel_cell" title="Solid oxide fuel cell">solid oxide fuel cells</a> (SOFCs) because of its relatively high oxygen <a href="/wiki/Fast_ion_conductor" title="Fast ion conductor">ion conductivity</a> (i.e. oxygen atoms readily move through it) at intermediate temperatures (500–650 °C) and  lower association enthalpy compared to Zirconia system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_splitting">Water splitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water splitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide%E2%80%93cerium(III)_oxide_cycle" title="Cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle">cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle</a> or CeO<sub>2</sub>/Ce<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> cycle is a two step <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">water splitting</a> process based on cerium(IV) oxide and <a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_oxide" title="Cerium(III) oxide">cerium(III) oxide</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
