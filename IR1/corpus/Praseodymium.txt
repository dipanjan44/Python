<p><b>Praseodymium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Pr</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 59. It is the third member of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series and is traditionally considered to be one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metal" title="Rare-earth metal">rare-earth metals</a>. Praseodymium is a soft, silvery, malleable and ductile <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, valued for its magnetic, electrical, chemical, and optical properties. It is too reactive to be found in native form, and pure praseodymium metal slowly develops a green oxide coating when exposed to air.
</p><p>Praseodymium always occurs naturally together with the other rare-earth metals. It is the fourth most common rare-earth element, making up 9.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> of the Earth's crust, an abundance similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>. In 1841, Swedish chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Mosander" title="Carl Gustav Mosander">Carl Gustav Mosander</a> extracted a rare-earth oxide residue he called <a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a> from a residue he called "lanthana", in turn separated from <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> salts. In 1885, the Austrian chemist <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> separated didymium into two elements that gave salts of different colours, which he named praseodymium and <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>. The name praseodymium comes from the Greek <i>prasinos</i> (πράσινος), meaning "green", and <i>didymos</i> (δίδυμος), "twin".
</p><p>Like most <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth elements</a>, praseodymium most readily forms the +3 oxidation state, which is the only stable state in aqueous solution, although the +4 oxidation state is known in some solid compounds and, uniquely among the lanthanides, the +5 oxidation state is attainable in matrix-isolation conditions. Aqueous praseodymium ions are yellowish-green, and similarly praseodymium results in various shades of yellow-green when incorporated into glasses. Many of praseodymium's industrial uses involve its ability to filter yellow light from light sources.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Praseodymium is the third member of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series. In the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>, it appears between the lanthanides <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> to its left and <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> to its right, and above the <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> <a href="/wiki/Protactinium" title="Protactinium">protactinium</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> metal with a hardness comparable to that of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup> Its 59 electrons are arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">configuration</a> [Xe]4f<sup>3</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>; theoretically, all five outer electrons can act as <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a>, but the use of all five requires extreme conditions and normally, praseodymium only gives up three or sometimes four electrons in its compounds. Praseodymium is the first of the lanthanides to have an electron configuration conforming to the <a href="/wiki/Aufbau_principle" title="Aufbau principle">Aufbau principle</a>, which predicts the 4f orbitals to have a lower energy level than the 5d orbitals; this does not hold for lanthanum and cerium, because the sudden contraction of the 4f orbitals does not happen until after lanthanum, and is not strong enough at cerium to avoid occupying the 5d subshell. Nevertheless, solid praseodymium takes on the [Xe]4f<sup>2</sup>5d<sup>1</sup>6s<sup>2</sup> configuration, with one electron in the 5d subshell like all the other trivalent lanthanides (all but <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>, which are divalent in the metallic state).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1232_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1232-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like most lanthanides, praseodymium usually only uses three electrons as valence electrons, as afterwards the remaining 4f electrons are too strongly bound: this is because the 4f orbitals penetrate the most through the inert xenon core of electrons to the nucleus, followed by 5d and 6s, and this increases with higher ionic charge. Praseodymium nevertheless can continue losing a fourth and even occasionally a fifth valence electron because it comes very early in the lanthanide series, where the nuclear charge is still low enough and the 4f subshell energy high enough to allow the removal of further valence electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1235_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1235-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, similarly to the other early trivalent lanthanides, praseodymium has a <a href="/wiki/Close-packing_of_equal_spheres" title="Close-packing of equal spheres">double hexagonal close-packed</a> crystal structure at room temperature. At about 560 °C, it transitions to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centered_cubic" title="Face-centered cubic">face-centered cubic</a> structure, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure appears shortly before the melting point of 935 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Praseodymium, like all of the lanthanides (except lanthanum, ytterbium, and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>, which have no unpaired 4f electrons), is paramagnetic at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Unlike some other rare-earth metals, which show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> ordering at low temperatures, praseodymium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> at all temperatures above 1 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-jackson-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_praseodymium" title="Isotopes of praseodymium">Isotopes of praseodymium</a></div>
<p>Praseodymium has only one stable and naturally occurring isotope, <sup>141</sup>Pr. It is thus a <a href="/wiki/Mononuclidic_element" title="Mononuclidic element">mononuclidic element</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weight</a> can be determined with high precision as it is a constant of nature. This isotope has 82 neutrons, a <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(physics)" title="Magic number (physics)">magic number</a> that confers additional stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-10">[10]</a></sup> This isotope is produced in stars through the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-</a> and <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-processes</a> (slow and rapid neutron capture, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>All other praseodymium isotopes have half-lives under a day (and most under a minute), with the single exception of <sup>143</sup>Pr with a half-life of 13.6 days. Both <sup>143</sup>Pr and <sup>141</sup>Pr occur as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission products</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. The primary decay mode of isotopes lighter than <sup>141</sup>Pr is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_plus_decay" title="Beta plus decay">inverse beta decay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cerium" title="Isotopes of cerium">isotopes of cerium</a>, while that of heavier isotopes is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_neodymium" title="Isotopes of neodymium">isotopes of neodymium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Praseodymium metal tarnishes slowly in air, forming a <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spalling</a> oxide layer like <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> rust; a centimetre-sized sample of praseodymium metal corrodes completely in about a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It burns readily at 150 °C to form <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium_(III,IV)_oxide" title="Praseodymium (III,IV) oxide">praseodymium (III,IV) oxide</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">nonstoichiometric compound</a> approximating to Pr<sub>6</sub>O<sub>11</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>12 Pr + 11 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Pr<sub>6</sub>O<sub>11</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This may be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium(III)_oxide" title="Praseodymium(III) oxide">praseodymium(III) oxide</a> (Pr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) with hydrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1238_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1238-14">[14]</a></sup> The dark-coloured <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Praseodymium(IV) oxide (page does not exist)">praseodymium(IV) oxide</a>, PrO<sub>2</sub>, is the most oxidised product of the combustion of praseodymium and is only obtained by reaction of praseodymium metal with pure oxygen at 400 °C and 282 bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1238_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1238-14">[14]</a></sup> The reactivity of praseodymium conforms to <a href="/wiki/Periodic_trends" title="Periodic trends">periodic trends</a>, as it is one of the first and thus one of the largest lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1235_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1235-7">[7]</a></sup> At 1000 °C, many praseodymium oxides with composition PrO<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub> exist as disordered, nonstoichiometric phases with 0 &lt; <i>x</i> &lt; 0.25, but at 400–700 °C the oxide defects are instead ordered, creating phases of the general formula Pr<sub><i>n</i></sub>O<sub>2<i>n</i>−2</sub> with <i>n</i> = 4, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12, and ∞. These phases PrO<sub><i>y</i></sub> are sometimes labelled α and β′ (nonstoichiometric), β (<i>y</i> = 1.833), δ (1.818), ε (1.8), ζ (1.778), ι (1.714), θ, and σ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood643_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood643-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Praseodymium is quite electropositive and reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form praseodymium(III) hydroxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Pr (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Pr(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Praseodymium metal reacts with all the halogens to form trihalides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Pr (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 PrF<sub>3</sub> (s) [green]</dd>
<dd>2 Pr (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 PrCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [green]</dd>
<dd>2 Pr (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 PrBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [green]</dd>
<dd>2 Pr (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 PrI<sub>3</sub> (s)</dd></dl>
<p>The tetrafluoride, PrF<sub>4</sub>, is also known, and is produced by reacting a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium(III)_fluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Praseodymium(III) fluoride (page does not exist)">praseodymium(III) fluoride</a> with fluorine gas, producing Na<sub>2</sub>PrF<sub>6</sub>, following which sodium fluoride is removed from the reaction mixture with liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1240_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1240-16">[16]</a></sup> Additionally, praseodymium forms a bronze diiodide; like the diiodides of lanthanum, cerium, and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>, it is a praseodymium(III) <a href="/wiki/Electride" title="Electride">electride</a> compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1240_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1240-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Praseodymium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_(color)" title="Chartreuse (color)">chartreuse</a> Pr<sup>3+</sup> ions, which exist as [Pr(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1242_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1242-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Pr (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Pr<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Dissolving praseodymium(IV) compounds in water results in solutions containing the yellow Pr<sup>4+</sup> ions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SroorEdelmann2012_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-SroorEdelmann2012-18">[18]</a></sup> because of the high positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_reduction_potential" title="Standard reduction potential">standard reduction potential</a> of the Pr<sup>4+</sup>/Pr<sup>3+</sup> couple at +3.2 V, these ions are unstable in aqueous solution, oxidising water and being reduced to Pr<sup>3+</sup>. The value for the Pr<sup>3+</sup>/Pr couple is −2.35 V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1232_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1232-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although praseodymium(V) in the bulk state is unknown, the existence of praseodymium in its +5 oxidation state (with the stable electron configuration of the preceding noble gas <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>) under noble-gas matrix isolation conditions was reported in 2016.  The species assigned to the +5 state were identified as [PrO<sub>2</sub>]<sup>+</sup>, its O<sub>2</sub> and Ar adducts, and PrO<sub>2</sub>(η<sup>2</sup>-O<sub>2</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organopraseodymium compounds are very similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organolanthanide_compound" title="Organolanthanide compound">those of the other lanthanides</a>, as they all share an inability to undergo π backbonding. They are thus mostly restricted to the mostly ionic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienide" title="Cyclopentadienide">cyclopentadienides</a> (isostructural with those of lanthanum) and the σ-bonded simple alkyls and aryls, some of which may be polymeric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1248_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1248-20">[20]</a></sup> The coordination chemistry of praseodymium is largely that of the large, electropositive Pr<sup>3+</sup> ion, and is thus largely similar to those of the other early lanthanides La<sup>3+</sup>, Ce<sup>3+</sup>, and Nd<sup>3+</sup>. For instance, like lanthanum, cerium, and neodymium, praseodymium nitrates form both 4:3 and 1:1 complexes with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18-crown-6" title="18-crown-6">18-crown-6</a>, whereas the middle lanthanides from <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> can only form the 4:3 complex and the later lanthanides from <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> cannot successfully coordinate to all the ligands. Such praseodymium complexes have high but uncertain coordination numbers and poorly defined stereochemistry, with exceptions resulting from exceptionally bulky ligands such as the tricoordinate [Pr{N(SiMe<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>}<sub>3</sub>]. There are also a few mixed oxides and fluorides involving praseodymium(IV), but it does not have an appreciable coordination chemistry in this oxidation state like its neighbour cerium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1244_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1244-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1268" data-file-width="1002" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/200px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/300px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/400px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> (1858–1929), discoverer of praseodymium in 1885.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1751, the Swedish mineralogist <a href="/wiki/Axel_Fredrik_Cronstedt" title="Axel Fredrik Cronstedt">Axel Fredrik Cronstedt</a> discovered a heavy mineral from the mine at <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4s" title="Bastnäs">Bastnäs</a>, later named <a href="/wiki/Cerite" title="Cerite">cerite</a>. Thirty years later, the fifteen-year-old <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vilhelm_Hisinger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vilhelm Hisinger (page does not exist)">Vilhelm Hisinger</a>, from the family owning the mine, sent a sample of it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Scheele" title="Carl Scheele">Carl Scheele</a>, who did not find any new elements within. In 1803, after Hisinger had become an ironmaster, he returned to the mineral with <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> and isolated a new oxide, which they named <i>ceria</i> after the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>, which had been discovered two years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley120_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley120-22">[22]</a></sup> Ceria was simultaneously and independently isolated in Germany by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1424_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1424-23">[23]</a></sup> Between 1839 and 1843, ceria was shown to be a mixture of oxides by the Swedish surgeon and chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a>, who lived in the same house as Berzelius; he separated out two other oxides, which he named <i>lanthana</i> and <i>didymia</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-24">[24]</a></sup> He partially decomposed a sample of <a href="/wiki/Cerium_nitrate" title="Cerium nitrate">cerium nitrate</a> by roasting it in air and then treating the resulting oxide with dilute <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>. The metals that formed these oxides were thus named <i>lanthanum</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> While lanthanum turned out to be a pure element, didymium was not and turned out to be only a mixture of all the stable early lanthanides from praseodymium to <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>, as had been suspected by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Delafontaine" title="Marc Delafontaine">Marc Delafontaine</a> after spectroscopic analysis, though he lacked the time to pursue its separation into its constituents. The heavy pair of <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> and europium were only removed in 1879 by <a href="/wiki/Paul-%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a> and it was not until 1885 that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> separated didymium into praseodymium and neodymium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lost_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lost-26">[26]</a></sup> Since neodymium was a larger constituent of didymium than praseodymium, it kept the old name with disambiguation, while praseodymium was distinguished by the leek-green colour of its salts (Greek πρασιος, "leek green").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup> The composite nature of didymium had previously been suggested in 1882 by <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Brauner" title="Bohuslav Brauner">Bohuslav Brauner</a>, who did not experimentally pursue its separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lost_p40_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lost_p40-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Praseodymium is not particularly rare, making up 9.1 mg/kg of the Earth's crust. This value is between those of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> (13 mg/kg) and <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> (9 mg/kg), and makes praseodymium the fourth-most abundant of the lanthanides, behind cerium (66 mg/kg), neodymium (40 mg/kg), and lanthanum (35 mg/kg); it is less abundant than the <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth elements</a> <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> (31 mg/kg) and <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> (25 mg/kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup> Instead, praseodymium's classification as a rare-earth metal comes from its rarity relative to "common earths" such as lime and magnesia, the few known minerals containing it for which extraction is commercially viable, as well as the length and complexity of extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-29">[29]</a></sup> Although not particularly rare, praseodymium is never found as a dominant rare earth in praseodymium-bearing minerals. It is always preceded by cerium, lanthanum and usually also by neodymium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg"><img alt="Monazite acid cracking process.svg" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="1160" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/730px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/1095px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/1460px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png 2x" width="730"/></a></div></div>
<p>The Pr<sup>3+</sup> ion is similar in size to the early lanthanides of the cerium group (those from lanthanum up to <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>) that immediately follow in the periodic table, and hence it tends to occur along with them in <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> (M<sup>III</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> (M<sup>III</sup>CO<sub>3</sub>F), where M refers to all the rare-earth metals except scandium and the radioactive <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a> (mostly Ce, La, and Y, with somewhat less Nd and Pr).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup> Bastnäsite is usually lacking in <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and the heavy lanthanides, and the purification of the light lanthanides from it is less involved. The ore, after being crushed and ground, is first treated with hot concentrated sulfuric acid, evolving carbon dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicon_tetrafluoride" title="Silicon tetrafluoride">silicon tetrafluoride</a>. The product is then dried and leached with water, leaving the early lanthanide ions, including lanthanum, in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The procedure for monazite, which usually contains all the rare earths, as well as thorium, is more involved. Monazite, because of its magnetic properties, can be separated by repeated electromagnetic separation. After separation, it is treated with hot concentrated sulfuric acid to produce water-soluble sulfates of rare earths. The acidic filtrates are partially neutralised with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> to pH 3–4, during which thorium precipitates as a hydroxide and is removed. The solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths to their insoluble <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalates</a>, the oxalates are converted to oxides by annealing, and the oxides are dissolved in nitric acid. This last step excludes one of the main components, <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>, whose oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-29">[29]</a></sup> Care must be taken when handling some of the residues as they contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radium-228" title="Radium-228"><sup>228</sup>Ra</a>, the daughter of <sup>232</sup>Th, which is a strong gamma emitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Praseodymium may then be separated from the other lanthanides via ion-exchange chromatography, or by using a solvent such as <a href="/wiki/Tributyl_phosphate" title="Tributyl phosphate">tributyl phosphate</a> where the solubility of Ln<sup>3+</sup> increases as the atomic number increases. If ion-exchange chromatography is used, the mixture of lanthanides is loaded into one column of cation-exchange resin and Cu<sup>2+</sup> or Zn<sup>2+</sup> or Fe<sup>3+</sup> is loaded into the other. An aqueous solution of a complexing agent, known as the eluant (usually triammonium edtate), is passed through the columns, and Ln<sup>3+</sup> is displaced from the first column and redeposited in a compact band at the top of the column before being re-displaced by <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>. The <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> of formation for Ln(edta·H) complexes increases along the lanthanides by about one quarter from Ce<sup>3+</sup> to Lu<sup>3+</sup>, so that the Ln<sup>3+</sup> cations descend the development column in a band and are fractionated repeatedly, eluting from heaviest to lightest. They are then precipitated as their insoluble oxalates, burned to form the oxides, and then reduced to the metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Praseodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leo Moser (son of Ludwig Moser, founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moser_Glass" title="Moser Glass">Moser Glassworks</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Karlovy_Vary" title="Karlovy Vary">Karlovy Vary</a> in the Czech Republic, not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Leo_Moser" title="Leo Moser">the mathematician of the same name</a>) investigated the use of praseodymium in glass colouration in the late 1920s, yielding a yellow-green glass given the name "Prasemit". However, at that time far cheaper colourants could give a similar colour, so Prasemit was not popular, few pieces were made, and examples are now extremely rare. Moser also blended praseodymium with neodymium to produce "Heliolite" glass ("Heliolit" in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>), which was more widely accepted. The first enduring commercial use of purified praseodymium, which continues today, is in the form of a yellow-orange "Praseodymium Yellow" stain for ceramics, which is a solid solution in the <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_silicate" title="Zirconium(IV) silicate">zircon</a> lattice.  This stain has no hint of green in it; by contrast, at sufficiently high loadings, praseodymium glass is distinctly green rather than pure yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>As the lanthanides are so similar, praseodymium can substitute for most other lanthanides without significant loss of function, and indeed many applications such as <a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">mischmetal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferrocerium" title="Ferrocerium">ferrocerium</a> alloys involve variable mixes of several lanthanides, including small quantities of praseodymium. The following more modern applications involve praseodymium specifically, or at least praseodymium in a small subset of the lanthanides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>In combination with neodymium, another rare-earth element, praseodymium is used to create high-power magnets notable for their strength and durability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAMGOLD_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAMGOLD-33">[33]</a></sup>  In general, most alloys of the cerium-group rare earths (lanthanum through samarium) with 3d <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> give extremely stable magnets that are often used in small equipment, such as motors, printers, watches, headphones, loudspeakers, and magnetic storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>As an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloying</a> agent with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> to create high-strength metals that are used in <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a>; <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> and neodymium are also viable substitutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium is present in the rare-earth mixture whose fluoride forms the core of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_arc_light" title="Carbon arc light">carbon arc lights</a>, which are used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Movie_studio" title="Movie studio">motion picture industry</a> for <a href="/wiki/Studio" title="Studio">studio</a> lighting and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_projector" title="Image projector">projector</a> lights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley423_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley423-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> give <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a> a yellow color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium is used to color ceramics yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium is a component of <a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a> glass, which is used to make certain types of <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welder</a>'s and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_blowing" title="Glass blowing">glass blower</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Goggles" title="Goggles">goggles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Silicate crystals doped with praseodymium ions have been used to <a href="/wiki/Slow_light" title="Slow light">slow a light pulse</a> down to a few hundred meters per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANUPressStopLight_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANUPressStopLight-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium alloyed with <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> (PrNi<sub>5</sub>) has such a strong <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_refrigeration#The_magnetocaloric_effect" title="Magnetic refrigeration">magnetocaloric effect</a> that it has allowed scientists to approach within one thousandth of a degree of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley423_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley423-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>Doping praseodymium in <a href="/wiki/Fluoride_glass" title="Fluoride glass">fluoride glass</a> allows it to be used as a single-mode fiber <a href="/wiki/Optical_amplifier" title="Optical amplifier">optical amplifier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Praseodymium oxide in solid solution with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceria" title="Ceria">ceria</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ceria-zirconia" title="Ceria-zirconia">ceria-zirconia</a> has been used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pr<sup>3+</sup> ions are used as activators in some red, green, blue, and ultraviolet phosphors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>The early lanthanides have been found to be essential to some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanotrophic" title="Methanotrophic">methanotrophic</a> bacteria living in <a href="/wiki/Mudpot" title="Mudpot">volcanic mudpots</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Methylacidiphilum_fumariolicum" title="Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum">Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum</a></i>: lanthanum, cerium, praseodymium, and neodymium are about equally effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Praseodymium is otherwise not known to have a biological role in any other organisms, but is not very toxic either. Intravenous injection of rare earths into animals has been known to impair liver function, but the main side effects from inhalation of rare-earth oxides in humans come from radioactive <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> impurities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-32">[32]</a></sup>
