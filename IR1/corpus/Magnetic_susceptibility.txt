<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Magnetic susceptibility</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, the <b>magnetic susceptibility</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">susceptibilis</i>, "receptive"; denoted <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;"><span class="nounderlines"><a href="/wiki/Chi_(letter)" title="Chi (letter)">χ</a></span></span>) is one measure of the magnetic properties of a material.  The susceptibility indicates whether a material is attracted into or repelled out of a magnetic field, which in turn has implications for practical applications.  Quantitative measures of the magnetic susceptibility also provide insights into the structure of materials, providing insight into <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy levels</a>.
</p><p>If the magnetic susceptibility is greater than zero, the substance is said to be "<a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetic</a>"; the magnetization of the substance is higher than that of empty space.  If the magnetic susceptibility is less than zero, the substance is "<a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a>"; it tends to exclude a magnetic field from its interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mathematically it is the ratio of magnetization <span class="texhtml"><b>M</b></span> (magnetic moment per unit volume) to the applied magnetizing field intensity <span class="texhtml"><b>H</b></span>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_susceptibility">Volume susceptibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Volume susceptibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnetic susceptibility is a dimensionless proportionality constant that indicates the degree of <a href="/wiki/Magnetization" title="Magnetization">magnetization</a> of a material in response to an applied <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. A related term is <b>magnetizability</b>, the proportion between <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic flux density</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A closely related parameter is the <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism)" title="Permeability (electromagnetism)">permeability</a>, which expresses the total magnetization of material and volume.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paramagnetism_and_diamagnetism">Paramagnetism and diamagnetism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paramagnetism and diamagnetism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">χ</span> is positive, a material can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>. In this case, the magnetic field in the material is strengthened by the induced magnetization. Alternatively, if <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">χ</span> is negative, the material is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>. In this case, the magnetic field in the material is weakened by the induced magnetization. Generally, nonmagnetic materials are said to be para- or diamagnetic because they do not possess permanent magnetization without external magnetic field. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">Ferromagnetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetism" title="Ferrimagnetism">ferrimagnetic</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> materials possess permanent magnetization even without external magnetic field and do not have a well defined zero-field susceptibility.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_measurement">Experimental measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Experimental measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Volume magnetic susceptibility is measured by the force change felt upon a substance when a magnetic field gradient is applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Early measurements are made using the <a href="/wiki/Gouy_balance" title="Gouy balance">Gouy balance</a> where a sample is hung between the poles of an electromagnet. The change in weight when the electromagnet is turned on is proportional to the susceptibility. Today, high-end measurement systems use a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductive" title="Superconductive">superconductive</a> magnet. An alternative is to measure the force change on a strong compact magnet upon insertion of the sample. This system, widely used today, is called the <a href="/wiki/Evans_balance" title="Evans balance">Evans balance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For liquid samples, the susceptibility can be measured from the dependence of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a> frequency of the sample on its shape or orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
Another method using NMR techniques measures the magnetic field distortion around a sample immersed in water inside an MR scanner. This method is highly accurate for diamagnetic materials with susceptibilities similar to water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wapler_JMR_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wapler_JMR-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tensor_susceptibility">Tensor susceptibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tensor susceptibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>where <span class="texhtml"><i>χ</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>d</i></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>ij</i></sub></span></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensor</a> derived from <a href="/wiki/Partial_derivative" title="Partial derivative">partial derivatives</a> of components of <span class="texhtml"><b>M</b></span> with respect to components of <span class="texhtml"><b>H</b></span>. When the <a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a> of the material parallel to an applied field is the smaller of the two, the differential susceptibility is a function of the applied field and self interactions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anisotropy" title="Magnetic anisotropy">magnetic anisotropy</a>. When the material is not <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(magnetic)" title="Saturation (magnetic)">saturated</a>, the effect will be nonlinear and dependent upon the <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain wall</a> configuration of the material.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_frequency_domain">In the frequency domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In the frequency domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the magnetic susceptibility is measured in response to an <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> magnetic field (i.e. a magnetic field that varies sinusoidally), this is called <i>AC susceptibility</i>. AC susceptibility (and the closely related "AC permeability") are <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex number</a> quantities, and various phenomena, such as resonance, can be seen in AC susceptibility that cannot in constant-field (<a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a>) susceptibility. In particular, when an AC field is applied perpendicular to the detection direction (called the "transverse susceptibility" regardless of the frequency), the effect has a peak at the <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic_resonance" title="Ferromagnetic resonance">ferromagnetic resonance</a> frequency of the material with a given static applied field. Currently, this effect is called the <i>microwave permeability</i> or <i>network ferromagnetic resonance</i> in the literature. These results are sensitive to the <a href="/wiki/Domain_wall_(magnetism)" title="Domain wall (magnetism)">domain wall</a> configuration of the material and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddy_currents" title="Eddy currents">eddy currents</a>.
</p><p>In terms of <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic_resonance" title="Ferromagnetic resonance">ferromagnetic resonance</a>, the effect of an AC-field applied along the direction of the magnetization is called <i>parallel pumping</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_confusion_in_published_data">Sources of confusion in published data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_susceptibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sources of confusion in published data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/CRC_Handbook_of_Chemistry_and_Physics" title="CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics">CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics</a> has one of the only published magnetic susceptibility tables. Some of the data (e.g., for <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>) is listed as cgs, which has caused confusion to some readers. "cgs" is an abbreviation of <i>centimeters–grams–seconds</i>; it represents the form of the units, but cgs does not specify units. Correct units of magnetic susceptibility in cgs is cm<sup>3</sup>/mol or cm<sup>3</sup>/g. <b>Molar susceptibility</b> and <b>mass susceptibility</b> are both listed in the CRC.  Some table have listed magnetic susceptibility of diamagnets as positives. It is important to check the header of the table for the correct units and sign of magnetic susceptibility readings.
</p>
