<p><b>Population Action International</b> (<b>PAI</b>) is an international, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organization</a> that uses research and advocacy to improve global access to <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive health</a> care.  Its mission is to "ensure that every person has the right and access to sexual and reproductive health, so that humanity and the natural environment can exist in balance with fewer people living in poverty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  PAI's headquarters is in Washington, D.C.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population Action International was founded in 1965 as the Population Crisis Committee by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Henry_Draper,_Jr." title="William Henry Draper, Jr.">William Henry Draper, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lammot_du_Pont_Copeland" title="Lammot du Pont Copeland">Lammot du Pont Copeland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Moore_(businessman)" title="Hugh Moore (businessman)">Hugh Moore</a>. PAI’s early successes include playing a role in the establishment of the Office of Population within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Agency_for_International_Development" title="U.S. Agency for International Development">U.S. Agency for International Development</a> (USAID), the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> (UNFPA) and raising funds for the <a href="/wiki/International_Planned_Parenthood_Federation" title="International Planned Parenthood Federation">International Planned Parenthood Federation</a> (IPPF).
PAI participated in the 1994 <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_Population_and_Development" title="International Conference on Population and Development">International Conference on Population and Development</a> in Cairo, which called for universal access to a range of population-related programs. It has participated in the UN Commission on Population and Development; in 2010, PAI president Suzanne Ehlers was a U.S. delegate to the commission.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Profile">Profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PAI promotes economic development, health and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_sustainability" title="Environmental sustainability">environmental sustainability</a> through funding for family-planning and sexual and reproductive health services worldwide. The organization monitors the impact of U.S. policies and programs overseas, and fosters the development of United States and international policy on population and reproductive health issues. Internationally, PAI provides partner organizations with financial resources and technical assistance.
</p><p>PAI's research and analysis focus on key issues in reproductive-health policy: improving access to reproductive health care, funding, and mobilizing political will to support family planning. It conducts demographic research on women’s empowerment, political and economic stability and governance. PAI tracks funding for family planning and reproductive-health services in developing countries, and engages in research examining the impact of population change on the environment and its implications for human security and sustainable economic development.
</p><p>PAI's advocacy involves mobilizing political and financial support for family-planning programs and sexual and reproductive health and rights. It focuses on highlighting the links between population, family planning, gender equality and <a href="/wiki/Global_issue" title="Global issue">global issues</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">poverty reduction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_security" title="Human security">human security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, geopolitical conflict and environmental sustainability.
</p><p>In the United States, PAI works with domestic policymakers to strengthen U.S. reproductive-health policies and funding for programs that mitigate poverty and rapid population growth.
It monitors the United Nations, the World Bank and other multilateral organizations to assess progress achieved on the development and implementation of international population and reproductive-health policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The president of PAI is Suzanne Ehlers, who has worked with PAI for a number of years and previously served as interim president. Ehlers served as a U.S. delegate to the U.N. Commission on Population and Development in April 2010. Previously, she was a grants officer at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wallace_Global_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wallace Global Fund (page does not exist)">Wallace Global Fund</a> and served as a <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a> volunteer in the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>.
</p><p>Past presidents of PAI include Amy Coen (1997–2010), Hugo Hoogenboom (1995–1997) and J. Joseph Speidel (1987–1995). Under Speidel, PAI nearly doubled its staff and budget, increased media exposure in the U.S. and abroad, and began to train and sustain overseas advocacy groups. He also led PAI’s team during the 1994 <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_Population_and_Development" title="International Conference on Population and Development">International Conference on Population and Development</a> (ICPD) in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. During Coen's tenure, PAI worked to move U.S. policy to fortify the reproductive-health advocacy movement in other countries and the links between population and reproductive health and global issues such as climate change.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiatives">Initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproductive-health_supplies"><span id="Reproductive_Health_Supplies_Initiative"></span>Reproductive-health supplies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reproductive-health supplies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To meet the growing need for reproductive-health supplies, PAI joined the Interim Working Group (IWG) on Reproductive Health Commodity Security in 2001 with partners John Snow, Inc. (JSI), the Program for Appropriate Technology and Health (PATH) and the Wallace Global Fund. The coalition met in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 2001 and devised a "Call to Action" plan with the goals of raising awareness, increasing support and proposing solutions to the crisis in reproductive health supplies in two phases (a supply initiative and a project Resources Mobilization Awareness:
</p>
<ul><li>The supply-initiative phase resulted in an increase in media coverage of the RH-supplies issue, increased availability of advocacy resources and materials, improved participation and investment by key stakeholders and partners and increased numbers of donor governments contributing to RH supplies and increased volume of funding.</li>
<li>Project RMA focuses on specific countries and regional bodies to create a tangible financial and political commitment to sustained availability of reproductive health supplies in developing countries. Project RMA's partners include PAI, the International Planned Parenthood Foundation and the <a href="/wiki/German_Foundation_for_World_Population" title="German Foundation for World Population">German Foundation for World Population</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change"><span id="Climate_Change_Initiative"></span>Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PAI’s climate-change initiative examines and assesses the relationships among population, gender and climate change. The goals of the initiative are to strengthen the understanding of the influence of population on <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions; demonstrate how demographic variables relate to climate-change vulnerability, and expand the concept of climate change resilience by highlighting gender, fertility, and reproductive-health dimensions. PAI partners with climate-focused organizations, including the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Global_Change_Research_Institute" title="Joint Global Change Research Institute">Joint Global Change Research Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth"><span id="Youth_Initiative"></span>Youth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Youth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PAI's youth initiative works to highlight the sexual and reproductive-health and -rights needs of young people in the U.S. and abroad through the youth-led Young People's Initiative (YPI). The YPI aims to reach diverse audiences to promote improved sexual reproductive health and rights outcomes for young people. Goals and activities of the young people's work group include funding to youth-led initiatives in developing countries and U.S university tours and documentary presentations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications_and_documentaries"><span id="Documentaries"></span>Publications and documentaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_Action_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Publications and documentaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PAI publishes annual reports that provide an overview of the programs, research, accomplishments, finances, and future goals that the organization has undertaken the previous fiscal year. PAI also has financed and sponsored several documentaries centering on key population issues such as gender relations, HIV/AIDS, family planning and reproductive health. They are:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Empty Handed: Responding the Demand for Contraceptives</i>: Examines women's lack of access to reproductive-health supplies in sub-Saharan Africa and the impact it has on their lives. It documents the challenges at each level of the reproductive-health supply chain and identifies areas in need of improvement. <i>Empty Handed</i> was released at the Women Deliver Conference in Washington, DC on June 8, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>The Silent Partner: HIV in Marriage</i>: Examines the risk of HIV transmission in marriage and the health and cultural challenges facing married women. The 12-minute documentary, filmed in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, is intended to inform and provoke discussion of harmful gender and societal norms that put women and couples at risk for <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>. It is also intended for advocacy to mobilize political and financial support for sexual and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive health</a> and rights to achieve broader social, economic and gender equity for everyone. <i>The Silent Partner</i> premiered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nairobi,_Kenya" title="Nairobi, Kenya">Nairobi, Kenya</a> on November 19, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Abstaining from Reality</i>: Filmed in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, the documentary provides a snapshot of the Bush administration’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstinence-only" title="Abstinence-only">abstinence-only</a> approach to HIV prevention as a requirement for its global HIV/AIDS assistance. <i>Abstaining from Reality</i> examines how ideologically driven programs can endanger the lives of people they ostensibly protect. The film urges a balanced, comprehensive approach to preventing HIV infections by providing full and accurate information and a range of services that empower individuals to make informed decisions. <i>Abstaining from Reality</i> was accepted by the 2007 United Nations Association Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Finding Balance—Forests and Family Planning in Madagascar</i>: Poverty and rapid population growth have driven the destruction of almost 90 percent of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>, home to thousands of unique plant and animal species. The documentary journeys to the edge of a rapidly disappearing world, where population growth continues to fuel the cycle of poverty and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_in_Madagascar" title="Deforestation in Madagascar">deforestation</a>. Interviews with local women reveal their desire to have fewer children and underscore the critical need for family planning services in remote areas. This short documentary brings the links between population growth and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_destruction" title="Environmental destruction">environmental destruction</a> into focus, and explores what one local organization is doing about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
