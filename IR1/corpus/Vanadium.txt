<p><b>Vanadium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>V</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 23. It is a hard, silvery-grey, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>. The elemental metal is rarely found in nature, but once isolated artificially, the formation of an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> layer (<a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a>) somewhat stabilizes the free metal against further <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Manuel_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Andrés Manuel del Río">Andrés Manuel del Río</a> discovered compounds of vanadium in 1801 in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> by analyzing a new <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>-bearing mineral he called "brown lead", and presumed its qualities were due to the presence of a new element, which he named <i>erythronium</i> (derived from Greek for "red") since, upon heating, most of the <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> turned red. Four years later, however, he was (erroneously) convinced by other scientists that erythronium was identical to <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">Chlorides</a> of vanadium were generated in 1830 by <a href="/wiki/Nils_Gabriel_Sefstr%C3%B6m" title="Nils Gabriel Sefström">Nils Gabriel Sefström</a> who thereby proved that a new element was involved, which he named "vanadium" after the Scandinavian goddess of beauty and fertility, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanad%C3%ADs" title="Vanadís">Vanadís</a> (<a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a>). Both names were attributed to the wide range of colors found in vanadium compounds. Del Rio's lead mineral was later renamed <a href="/wiki/Vanadinite" title="Vanadinite">vanadinite</a> for its vanadium content. In 1867 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Enfield_Roscoe" title="Henry Enfield Roscoe">Henry Enfield Roscoe</a> obtained the pure element.
</p><p>Vanadium occurs naturally in about 65 <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> deposits. It is produced in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> from steel smelter <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a>; other countries produce it either from magnetite directly, flue dust of heavy oil, or as a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> mining. It is mainly used to produce specialty <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> such as <a href="/wiki/High-speed_steel" title="High-speed steel">high-speed tool steels</a>. The most important industrial vanadium compound, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium pentoxide</a>, is used as a catalyst for the production of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.
</p><p>Large amounts of vanadium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> are found in a few organisms, possibly as a <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">toxin</a>. The oxide and some other salts of vanadium have moderate toxicity. Particularly in the ocean, vanadium is used by some life forms as an active center of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_bromoperoxidase" title="Vanadium bromoperoxidase">vanadium bromoperoxidase</a> of some ocean <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vanadium was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> by <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Manuel_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Andrés Manuel del Río">Andrés Manuel del Río</a>, a Spanish-Mexican mineralogist, in 1801. Del Río extracted the element from a sample of Mexican "brown lead" ore, later named <a href="/wiki/Vanadinite" title="Vanadinite">vanadinite</a>. He found that its salts exhibit a wide variety of colors, and as a result he named the element <i>panchromium</i> (Greek: παγχρώμιο "all colors"). Later, Del Río renamed the element <i>erythronium</i> (Greek: ερυθρός "red") because most of the salts turned red upon heating. In 1805, the French chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Victor_Collet-Descotils" title="Hippolyte Victor Collet-Descotils">Hippolyte Victor Collet-Descotils</a>, backed by del Río's friend Baron <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>, incorrectly declared that del Río's new element was only an impure sample of <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>. Del Río accepted Collet-Descotils' statement and retracted his claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cintas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cintas-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1831, the Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Nils_Gabriel_Sefstr%C3%B6m" title="Nils Gabriel Sefström">Nils Gabriel Sefström</a> rediscovered the element in a new oxide he found while working with <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ores</a>. Later that same year, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> confirmed del Río's earlier work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sefs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sefs-4">[4]</a></sup> Sefström chose a name beginning with V, which had not been assigned to any element yet. He called the element <i>vanadium</i> after <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_names_of_Freyja" title="List of names of Freyja">Vanadís</a></i> (another name for the <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse</a> <a href="/wiki/Vanir" title="Vanir">Vanr</a> goddess <a href="/wiki/Freyja" title="Freyja">Freyja</a>, whose attributes include beauty and fertility), because of the many beautifully colored <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> it produces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sefs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sefs-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1831, the geologist <a href="/wiki/George_William_Featherstonhaugh" title="George William Featherstonhaugh">George William Featherstonhaugh</a> suggested that vanadium should be renamed "<i>rionium</i>" after del Río, but this suggestion was not followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1910Ford-T.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="670" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1910Ford-T.jpg/220px-1910Ford-T.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1910Ford-T.jpg/330px-1910Ford-T.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1910Ford-T.jpg/440px-1910Ford-T.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1910Ford-T.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_T" title="Model T">Model T</a> made use of vanadium steel in its <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">chassis</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The isolation of vanadium metal proved difficult. In 1831, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Berzelius</a> reported the production of the metal, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Enfield_Roscoe" title="Henry Enfield Roscoe">Henry Enfield Roscoe</a> showed that Berzelius had in fact produced the nitride, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_nitride" title="Vanadium nitride">vanadium nitride</a> (VN). Roscoe eventually produced the metal in 1867 by reduction of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(II)_chloride" title="Vanadium(II) chloride">vanadium(II) chloride</a>, VCl<sub>2</sub>, with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roscoe_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roscoe-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1927, pure vanadium was produced by reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" title="Vanadium pentoxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marden_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marden-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first large-scale industrial use of vanadium was in the <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> alloy chassis of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Ford Model T</a>, inspired by French race cars. Vanadium steel allowed for reduced weight while simultaneously increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> (ca. 1905).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>German chemist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wolfgang_Martin_Henze" title="Friedrich Wolfgang Martin Henze">Martin Henze</a> discovered vanadium in the <a href="/wiki/Hemovanadin" title="Hemovanadin">hemovanadin</a> proteins found in <a href="/wiki/Blood_cell" title="Blood cell">blood cells</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Coelom" title="Coelom">coelomic</a> cells) of <a href="/wiki/Ascidiacea" title="Ascidiacea">Ascidiacea</a> (sea squirts) in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michibata2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-michibata2002-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium-bar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="800" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Vanadium-bar.jpg/220px-Vanadium-bar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Vanadium-bar.jpg/330px-Vanadium-bar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Vanadium-bar.jpg/440px-Vanadium-bar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium-bar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High-purity (99.95%) vanadium cuboids, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam_technology" title="Electron beam technology">ebeam remelted</a> and macro-etched</div></div></div>
<p>Vanadium is a medium-hard, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, steel-blue metal. It is electrically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductive" title="Conductive">conductive</a> and thermally <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulating</a>. Some sources describe vanadium as "soft", perhaps because it is ductile, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a> and not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Vanadium is harder than most metals and steels (see <a href="/wiki/Hardnesses_of_the_elements_(data_page)" title="Hardnesses of the elements (data page)">Hardnesses of the elements (data page)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron#Mechanical_properties" title="Iron">iron</a>). It has good resistance to <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> and it is stable against <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup> It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidized</a> in air at about 933 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (660 °C, 1220 °F), although an oxide <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a> layer forms even at room temperature.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_vanadium" title="Isotopes of vanadium">Isotopes of vanadium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring vanadium is composed of one stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>51</sup>V, and one radioactive isotope, <sup>50</sup>V. The latter has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 1.5×10<sup>17</sup> years and a natural abundance of 0.25%. <sup>51</sup>V has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_spin" title="Nuclear spin">nuclear spin</a> of ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>7</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>, which is useful for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-14">[14]</a></sup> Twenty-four artificial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been characterized, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> from 40 to 65. The most stable of these isotopes are <sup>49</sup>V with a half-life of 330 days, and <sup>48</sup>V with a half-life of 16.0 days. The remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives shorter than an hour, most below 10 seconds. At least four isotopes have <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">metastable excited states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">Electron capture</a> is the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> for isotopes lighter than <sup>51</sup>V. For the heavier ones, the most common mode is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. The electron capture reactions lead to the formation of element 22 (<a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>) isotopes, while beta decay leads to element 24 (<a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>) isotopes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Vanadium_compounds" title="Category:Vanadium compounds">Category:Vanadium compounds</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3005" data-file-width="2003" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg/170px-Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg/255px-Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg/340px-Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadiumoxidationstates.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From left: [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> (lilac), [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> (green), [VO(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> (blue) and [VO(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> (yellow).</div></div></div>
<p>The chemistry of vanadium is noteworthy for the accessibility of the four adjacent <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> 2–5. In <a href="/wiki/Metal_ions_in_aqueous_solution" title="Metal ions in aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>, vanadium forms <a href="/wiki/Metal_aquo_complex" title="Metal aquo complex">metal aquo complexes</a> of which the colours are lilac [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>, green [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup>, blue [VO(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>, yellow VO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>. Vanadium(II) compounds are reducing agents, and vanadium(V) compounds are oxidizing agents. Vanadium(IV) compounds often exist as <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_ion" title="Vanadyl ion">vanadyl</a> derivatives, which contain the VO<sup>2+</sup> center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_metavanadate" title="Ammonium metavanadate">Ammonium vanadate(V)</a> (NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>) can be successively reduced with elemental <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> to obtain the different colors of vanadium in these four oxidation states. Lower oxidation states occur in compounds such as V(CO)<sub>6</sub>, <span class="chemf nowrap">[V(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and substituted derivatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most commercially important compound is <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium pentoxide</a>. It is used as a catalyst for the production of sulfuric acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup> This compound oxidizes <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) to the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">trioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>). In this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redox_reaction" title="Redox reaction">redox reaction</a>, sulfur is oxidized from +4 to +6, and vanadium is reduced from +5 to +4:
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + SO<sub>2</sub>  →  2 VO<sub>2</sub> + SO<sub>3</sub><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The catalyst is regenerated by oxidation with air:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 VO<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub>  →  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Similar oxidations are used in the production of maleic anhydride, phthalic anhydride, and several other bulk organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_redox_battery" title="Vanadium redox battery">vanadium redox battery</a> utilizes all four oxidation states; one electrode uses the +5/+4 couple and the other uses the +3/+2 couple. Conversion of these oxidation states is illustrated by the reduction of a strongly acidic solution of a vanadium(V) compound with zinc dust or amalgam. The initial yellow color characteristic of the pervanadyl ion [VO<sub>2</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup> is replaced by the blue color of [VO(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>, followed by the green color of [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> and then the violet color of [V(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxyanions">Oxyanions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oxyanions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Decavanadate_polyhedra.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1520" data-file-width="1929" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/220px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/330px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/440px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Decavanadate_polyhedra.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decavanadate" title="Decavanadate">decavanadate</a> structure</div></div></div>
<p>In aqueous solution, vanadium(V) forms an extensive family of <a href="/wiki/Oxyanion" title="Oxyanion">oxyanions</a>. The interrelationships in this family are described by the <a href="/wiki/Predominance_diagram" title="Predominance diagram">predominance diagram</a>, which shows at least 11 species, depending on pH and concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The tetrahedral orthovanadate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>, is the principal species present at pH 12-14. Similar in size and charge to phosphorus(V), vanadium(V) also parallels its chemistry and crystallography. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_orthovanadate" title="Sodium orthovanadate">Orthovanadate</a> V<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_crystallography" title="Protein crystallography">protein crystallography</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> to study the <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> of phosphate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The tetrathiovanadate [VS<sub>4</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> is analogous to the orthovanadate ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>At lower pH values, the monomer [HVO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> and dimer [V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>]<sup>−</sup> are formed, with the monomer predominant at vanadium concentration of less than c. 10<sup>−2</sup>M (pV &gt; 2, where pV is equal to the minus value of the logarithm of the total vanadium concentration/M). The formation of the divanadate ion is analogous to the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dichromate" title="Dichromate">dichromate</a> ion. As the pH is reduced, further protonation and condensation to <a href="/wiki/Vanadate" title="Vanadate">polyvanadates</a> occur: at pH 4-6 [H<sub>2</sub>VO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>−</sup> is predominant at pV greater than ca. 4, while at higher concentrations trimers and tetramers are formed. Between pH 2-4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decavanadate" title="Decavanadate">decavanadate</a> predominates, its formation from orthovanadate is represented by this condensation reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>10 [VO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>3−</sup>  +  24 H<sup>+</sup>  →  [V<sub>10</sub>O<sub>28</sub>]<sup>6−</sup>  +  12 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>In decavanadate, each V(V) center is surrounded by six oxide <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-13">[13]</a></sup> Vanadic acid, H<sub>3</sub>VO<sub>4</sub> exists only at very low concentrations because protonation of the tetrahedral species [H<sub>2</sub>VO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>−</sup> results in the preferential formation of the octahedral [VO<sub>2</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup> species. In strongly acidic solutions, pH&lt;2. [VO<sub>2</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup> is the predominant species, while the oxide V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> precipitates from solution at high concentrations. The oxide is formally the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_anhydride" title="Inorganic anhydride">inorganic anhydride</a> of vanadic acid. The structures of many <a href="/wiki/Vanadate" title="Vanadate">vanadate</a> compounds have been determined by X-ray crystallography.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="496" data-file-width="484" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg/220px-VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg/330px-VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg/440px-VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VinwaterPourbaixdiagram2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">Pourbaix diagram</a> for vanadium in water</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">Pourbaix diagram</a> for vanadium in water, which shows the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> potentials between various vanadium species in different oxidation states, is also complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium(V) forms various peroxo complexes, most notably in the active site of the vanadium-containing <a href="/wiki/Bromoperoxidase" title="Bromoperoxidase">bromoperoxidase</a> enzymes. The species VO(O)<sub>2</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> is stable in acidic solutions. In alkaline solutions, species with 2, 3 and 4 peroxide groups are known; the last forms violet salts with the formula M<sub>3</sub>V(O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>4</sub> nH<sub>2</sub>O (M = Li, Na, etc.), in which the vanadium has an 8-coordinate dodecahedral structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halide_derivatives">Halide derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Halide derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Twelve binary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halides" title="Halides">halides</a>, compounds with the formula VX<sub>n</sub> (n=2..5), are known. VI<sub>4</sub>, VCl<sub>5</sub>, VBr<sub>5</sub>, and VI<sub>5</sub> do not exist or are extremely unstable. In combination with other reagents, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium(IV)_chloride" title="Vanadium(IV) chloride">VCl<sub>4</sub></a> is used as a catalyst for polymerization of <a href="/wiki/Diene" title="Diene">dienes</a>. Like all binary halides, those of vanadium are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acidic</a>, especially those of V(IV) and V(V). Many of the halides form octahedral complexes with the formula VX<sub><i>n</i></sub>L<sub>6−<i>n</i></sub> (X = halide; L = other ligand).
</p><p>Many vanadium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhalide" title="Oxyhalide">oxyhalides</a> (formula VO<sub>m</sub>X<sub>n</sub>) are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The oxytrichloride and oxytrifluoride (<a href="/wiki/Vanadium_oxytrichloride" title="Vanadium oxytrichloride">VOCl<sub>3</sub></a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium_oxytrifluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vanadium oxytrifluoride (page does not exist)">VOF<sub>3</sub></a>) are the most widely studied. Akin to POCl<sub>3</sub>, they are volatile, adopt tetrahedral structures in the gas phase, and are Lewis acidic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_compounds">Coordination compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coordination compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="723" data-file-width="1100" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/220px-Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/330px-Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/440px-Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadyl-acetylacetonate-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Ball-and-stick_model" title="Ball-and-stick model">ball-and-stick model</a> of VO<sub>5</sub>(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<p>Complexes of vanadium(II) and (III) are relatively exchange inert and reducing. Those of V(IV) and V(V) are oxidants. Vanadium ion is rather large and some complexes achieve coordination numbers greater than 6, as is the case in [V(CN)<sub>7</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>. Oxovanadium(V) also forms 7 coordinate coordination complexes with tetradentate ligands and peroxides and these complexes are used for oxidative brominations and thioether oxidations. The coordination chemistry of V<sup>4+</sup> is dominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadyl" title="Vanadyl">vanadyl</a> center, VO<sup>2+</sup>, which binds four other ligands strongly and one weakly (the one trans to the vanadyl center). An example is <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_acetylacetonate" title="Vanadyl acetylacetonate">vanadyl acetylacetonate</a> (V(O)(O<sub>2</sub>C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>). In this complex, the vanadium is 5-coordinate, square pyramidal, meaning that a sixth ligand, such as pyridine, may be attached, though the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_constant" title="Association constant">association constant</a> of this process is small. Many 5-coordinate vanadyl complexes have a trigonal bypyramidal geometry, such as VOCl<sub>2</sub>(NMe<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The coordination chemistry of V<sup>5+</sup> is dominated by the relatively stable dioxovanadium coordination complexes which are often formed by aerial oxidation of the vanadium(IV) precursors indicating the stability of the +5 oxidation state and ease of interconversion between the +4 and +5 states.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organometallic_compounds">Organometallic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organometallic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organovanadium_chemistry" title="Organovanadium chemistry">Organovanadium chemistry</a></div>
<p>Organometallic chemistry of vanadium is well developed, although it has mainly only academic significance. <a href="/wiki/Vanadocene_dichloride" title="Vanadocene dichloride">Vanadocene dichloride</a> is a versatile starting reagent and even finds some applications in organic chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilkinson_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilkinson-25">[25]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_carbonyl" title="Vanadium carbonyl">Vanadium carbonyl</a>, V(CO)<sub>6</sub>, is a rare example of a paramagnetic <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyl</a>. Reduction yields V<span class="chemf nowrap">(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexacarbonylchromium" title="Hexacarbonylchromium">Cr(CO)<sub>6</sub></a>), which may be further reduced with sodium in liquid ammonia to yield V<span class="chemf nowrap">(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> (isoelectronic with Fe(CO)<sub>5</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe">Universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements#Abundance_of_elements_in_the_Universe" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">cosmic abundance</a> of vanadium in the universe is 0.0001%, making the element nearly as common as <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dieter_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dieter-28">[28]</a></sup> Vanadium is detected <a href="/wiki/Optical_spectrometer" title="Optical spectrometer">spectroscopically</a> in light from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and sometimes in the light from other <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Earth.27s_crust"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_crust">Earth's crust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Earth's crust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Vanadate_minerals" title="Category:Vanadate minerals">Category:Vanadate minerals</a>.</div>
<p>Vanadium is the 20th most abundant element in the earth's crust;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> metallic vanadium is rare in nature (known as the mineral vanadium, <a href="/wiki/Native_vanadium" title="Native vanadium">native vanadium</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> but vanadium compounds occur naturally in about 65 different <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. Economically significant examples include <a href="/wiki/Patr%C3%B3nite" title="Patrónite">patrónite</a> (VS<sub>4</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vanadinite" title="Vanadinite">vanadinite</a> (<span class="nowrap">Pb<sub>5</sub>(VO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>Cl</span>), and <a href="/wiki/Carnotite" title="Carnotite">carnotite</a> (<span class="nowrap">K<sub>2</sub>(UO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(VO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O</span>). Much of the world's vanadium production is sourced from vanadium-bearing <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a> bodies. Vanadium is mined mostly in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, north-western <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. In 2013 these three countries mined more than 97% of the 79,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of produced vanadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium is also present in <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a> and in deposits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a> sands. In crude oil, concentrations up to 1200 ppm have been reported. When such oil products are burned, traces of vanadium may cause <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> in engines and boilers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> An estimated 110,000 tonnes of vanadium per year are released into the atmosphere by burning fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_ion" title="Vanadyl ion">vanadyl ion</a> is abundant in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>, having an average concentration of 30 <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration#Units" title="Molar concentration">nM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dieter_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dieter-28">[28]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a> <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">springs</a> also contain the ion in high concentrations. For example, springs near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Fuji" title="Mount Fuji">Mount Fuji</a> contain as much as 54 <a href="/wiki/Microgram" title="Microgram">μg</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">liter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dieter_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dieter-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FerroVanadium.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="402" data-file-width="800" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/FerroVanadium.jpg/220px-FerroVanadium.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/FerroVanadium.jpg/330px-FerroVanadium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/FerroVanadium.jpg/440px-FerroVanadium.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FerroVanadium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ferrovanadium chunks</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="786" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg/220px-Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg/330px-Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg/440px-Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_crystal_vakuum_sublimed.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vacuum sublimed vanadium <a href="/wiki/Dendrite_(crystal)" title="Dendrite (crystal)">dendritic</a> crystals (99.9%)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3588" data-file-width="4698" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/220px-Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/330px-Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/440px-Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium_crystal_bar_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Crystal_bar_process" title="Crystal bar process">Crystal-bar</a> vanadium, showing different textures and surface oxidation; 99.95%-pure cube for comparison</div></div></div>
<p>Most vanadium is used as a <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> alloy called <a href="/wiki/Ferrovanadium" title="Ferrovanadium">ferrovanadium</a>.  Ferrovanadium is produced directly by reducing a mixture of vanadium oxide, iron oxides and iron in an electric furnace. The vanadium ends up in <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a> produced from vanadium-bearing magnetite. Depending on the ore used, the slag contains up to 25% of vanadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium metal is obtained by a multistep process that begins with the roasting of crushed ore with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">NaCl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub></a> at about 850 °C to give <a href="/wiki/Sodium_metavanadate" title="Sodium metavanadate">sodium metavanadate</a> (NaVO<sub>3</sub>). An aqueous extract of this solid is acidified to give "red cake", a polyvanadate salt, which is reduced with <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> metal. As an alternative for small-scale production, vanadium pentoxide is reduced with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>. Many other methods are also in use, in all of which vanadium is produced as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproduct" title="Byproduct">byproduct</a> of other processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-37">[37]</a></sup> Purification of vanadium is possible by the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_bar_process" title="Crystal bar process">crystal bar process</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Anton_Eduard_van_Arkel" title="Anton Eduard van Arkel">Anton Eduard van Arkel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hendrik_de_Boer" title="Jan Hendrik de Boer">Jan Hendrik de Boer</a> in 1925. It involves the formation of the metal iodide, in this example <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(III)_iodide" title="Vanadium(III) iodide">vanadium(III) iodide</a>, and the subsequent decomposition to yield pure metal:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 V + 3 I<sub>2</sub> ⇌ 2 VI<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Knarre.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2275" data-file-width="2304" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Knarre.jpg/170px-Knarre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Knarre.jpg/255px-Knarre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Knarre.jpg/340px-Knarre.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Knarre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tool made from vanadium steel</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Approximately 85% of vanadium produced is used as <a href="/wiki/Ferroalloy#Ferrovanadium" title="Ferroalloy">ferrovanadium</a> or as a <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> additive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-37">[37]</a></sup> The considerable increase of strength in steel containing small amounts of vanadium was discovered in the early 20th century. Vanadium forms stable nitrides and carbides, resulting in a significant increase in the strength of steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandler_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chandler-39">[39]</a></sup> From that time on, vanadium steel was used for applications in <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axles</a>, bicycle frames, <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshafts</a>, gears, and other critical components. There are two groups of vanadium steel alloys. Vanadium high-carbon steel alloys contain 0.15% to 0.25% vanadium, and <a href="/wiki/High-speed_steel" title="High-speed steel">high-speed tool steels</a> (HSS) have a vanadium content of 1% to 5%. For high-speed tool steels, a hardness above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rockwell_hardness" title="Rockwell hardness">HRC</a> 60 can be achieved. HSS steel is used in <a href="/wiki/Surgical_instrument" title="Surgical instrument">surgical instruments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">Powder-metallurgic</a> alloys contain up to 18% percent vanadium. The high content of vanadium carbides in those alloys increases wear resistance significantly. One application for those alloys is tools and knives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium stabilizes the beta form of titanium and increases the strength and temperature stability of titanium. Mixed with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> alloys, it is used in <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>, high-speed airframes and <a href="/wiki/Dental_implant" title="Dental implant">dental implants</a>. The most common alloy for seamless tubing is <a href="/wiki/Titanium_alloy#Grades_of_titanium" title="Titanium alloy">Titanium 3/2.5</a> containing 2.5% vanadium, the titanium alloy of choice in the aerospace, defense and bicycle industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Another common alloy, primarily produced in sheets, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanium_6AL-4V" title="Titanium 6AL-4V">Titanium 6AL-4V</a>, a titanium alloy with 6% aluminium and 4% vanadium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several vanadium alloys show superconducting behavior. The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A15_phase" title="A15 phase">A15 phase</a> superconductor was a vanadium compound, V<sub>3</sub>Si, which was discovered in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium-gallium" title="Vanadium-gallium">Vanadium-gallium</a> tape is used in <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> magnets (17.5 <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">teslas</a> or 175,000 <a href="/wiki/Gauss_(unit)" title="Gauss (unit)">gauss</a>). The structure of the superconducting A15 phase of V<sub>3</sub>Ga is similar to that of the more common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-tin" title="Niobium-tin">Nb<sub>3</sub>Sn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobium-titanium" title="Niobium-titanium">Nb<sub>3</sub>Ti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been proposed that a small amount, 40 to 270 ppm, of vanadium in <a href="/wiki/Wootz_steel" title="Wootz steel">Wootz steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damascus_steel" title="Damascus steel">Damascus steel</a> significantly improved the strength of the product, though the source of the vanadium is unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium(V)_oxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="321" data-file-width="316" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Vanadium%28V%29_oxide.jpg/170px-Vanadium%28V%29_oxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Vanadium%28V%29_oxide.jpg/255px-Vanadium%28V%29_oxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/eb/Vanadium%28V%29_oxide.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vanadium(V)_oxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">Vanadium(V) oxide</a> is a catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a> for producing sulfuric acid</div></div></div>
<p>Vanadium compounds are used extensively as catalysts;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> for example, the most common oxide of vanadium, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in manufacturing sulfuric acid by the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and as an oxidizer in <a href="/wiki/Maleic_anhydride" title="Maleic anhydride">maleic anhydride</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Vanadium pentoxide is used in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubber_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubber-50">[50]</a></sup>  Vanadium is an important component of mixed metal oxide catalysts used in the oxidation of propane and propylene to acrolein, acrylic acid or the ammoxidation of propylene to acrylonitrile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In service, the oxidation state of vanadium changes dynamically and reversibly with the oxygen and the steam content of the reacting feed mixture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>  Another oxide of vanadium, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_dioxide" title="Vanadium dioxide">vanadium dioxide</a> VO<sub>2</sub>, is used in the production of glass coatings, which blocks <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> (and not visible light) at a specific temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Vanadium oxide can be used to induce color centers in <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a> to create simulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandrite" title="Alexandrite">alexandrite</a> jewelry, although alexandrite in nature is a <a href="/wiki/Chrysoberyl" title="Chrysoberyl">chrysoberyl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_redox_battery" title="Vanadium redox battery">Vanadium redox battery</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow battery</a>, is an electrochemical cell consisting of aqueous vanadium ions in different oxidation states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RychcikSkyllas-Kazacos1988_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-RychcikSkyllas-Kazacos1988-59">[59]</a></sup> Batteries of the type were first proposed in the 1930s and developed commercially from the 1980s onwards. Cells use +5 and +2 formal oxidization state ions, and (as of 2016)  are used commercially for small scale (c. 0.1 - 10 MW, 0.1 - 100 GJ) <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">grid energy storage</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vanadate" title="Vanadate">Vanadate</a> can be used for protecting steel against rust and corrosion by <a href="/wiki/Conversion_coating" title="Conversion coating">conversion coating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Vanadium foil is used in <a href="/wiki/Cladding_(metalworking)" title="Cladding (metalworking)">cladding</a> titanium to steel because it is compatible with both iron and titanium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The moderate <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">thermal neutron-capture cross-section</a> and the short half-life of the isotopes produced by neutron capture makes vanadium a suitable material for the inner structure of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fusion_reactor" title="Fusion reactor">fusion reactor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed">Proposed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Proposed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lithium vanadium oxide has been proposed for use as a high energy density anode for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_Ion" title="Lithium Ion">lithium ion batteries</a>, at 745 Wh/L when paired with a <a href="/wiki/Lithium_cobalt_oxide" title="Lithium cobalt oxide">lithium cobalt oxide</a> cathode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Vanadium phosphates have been proposed as the cathode in the <a href="/wiki/Lithium_vanadium_phosphate_battery" title="Lithium vanadium phosphate battery">lithium vanadium phosphate battery</a>, another type of lithium ion battery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Health benefits of vanadium and its potential as an anticancer agent have been reviewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Vanadium is more important in marine environments than terrestrial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VBrPOactsite.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="439" data-file-width="284" height="340" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/VBrPOactsite.png/220px-VBrPOactsite.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/VBrPOactsite.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VBrPOactsite.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Active site of the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_bromoperoxidase" title="Vanadium bromoperoxidase">vanadium bromoperoxidase</a>, which produces the preponderance of naturally-occurring <a href="/wiki/Organobromine_compound" title="Organobromine compound">organobromine compounds</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1932" data-file-width="2415" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg/220px-Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg/330px-Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg/440px-Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bluebell_tunicates_Nick_Hobgood.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tunicate" title="Tunicate">Tunicates</a> such as this bluebell tunicate contain vanadium as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanabin" title="Vanabin">vanabin</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg/220px-Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg/330px-Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg/440px-Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amanita_muscaria_3_vliegenzwammen_op_rij.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Amanita_muscaria" title="Amanita muscaria">Amanita muscaria</a></i> contains <a href="/wiki/Amavadin" title="Amavadin">amavadin</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vanadoenzymes">Vanadoenzymes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Vanadoenzymes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of species of marine <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> produce <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_bromoperoxidase" title="Vanadium bromoperoxidase">vanadium bromoperoxidase</a> as well as the closely related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroperoxidase" title="Chloroperoxidase">chloroperoxidase</a> (which may use a <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> or vanadium cofactor) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Iodoperoxidase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Iodoperoxidase (page does not exist)">iodoperoxidases</a>. The bromoperoxidase produces an estimated 1–2 million tons of <a href="/wiki/Bromoform" title="Bromoform">bromoform</a> and 56,000 tons of <a href="/wiki/Bromomethane" title="Bromomethane">bromomethane</a> annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Most naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Organobromine_compound" title="Organobromine compound">organobromine compounds</a> are produced by this enzyme,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> catalyzing the following reaction (R-H is hydrocarbon substrate):
</p>
<dl><dd>R-H  +  Br<sup>−</sup>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>  →  R-Br  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  +  OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_nitrogenase" title="Vanadium nitrogenase">vanadium nitrogenase</a> is used by some <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing</a> micro-organisms, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Azotobacter" title="Azotobacter">Azotobacter</a></i>. In this role, vanadium replaces more common <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, and gives the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenase" title="Nitrogenase">nitrogenase</a> slightly different properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vanadium_accumulation_in_tunicates_and_ascidians">Vanadium accumulation in tunicates and ascidians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Vanadium accumulation in tunicates and ascidians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vanadium is essential to <a href="/wiki/Ascidiacea" title="Ascidiacea">ascidians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunicate" title="Tunicate">tunicates</a>, where it is stored in the highly acidified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuoles" title="Vacuoles">vacuoles</a> of certain blood cell types, designated "vanadocytes". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanabin" title="Vanabin">Vanabins</a> (vanadium binding proteins) have been identified in the cytoplasm of such cells. The concentration of vanadium in the blood of ascidians is as much as ten million times higher<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (April 2014)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> than the surrounding seawater, which normally contains 1 to 2 µg/l.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The function of this vanadium concentration system and these vanadium-bearing proteins is still unknown, but the vanadocytes are later deposited just under the outer surface of the tunic where they may deter <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Fungi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Amanita_muscaria" title="Amanita muscaria">Amanita muscaria</a></i> and related species of macrofungi accumulate vanadium (up to 500 mg/kg in dry weight). Vanadium is present in the <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complex</a> <a href="/wiki/Amavadin" title="Amavadin">amavadin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> in fungal fruit-bodies. The biological importance of the accumulation is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Toxic or <a href="/wiki/Peroxidase" title="Peroxidase">peroxidase</a> enzyme functions have been suggested.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammals">Mammals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Mammals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deficiencies in vanadium result in reduced growth in rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The U.S. Institute of Medicine has not confirmed that vanadium is an essential nutrient for humans, so neither a Recommended Dietary Intake nor an Adequate Intake have been established. Dietary intake is estimated at 6 to 18 µg/day, with less than 5% absorbed. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolerable_upper_intake_levels" title="Tolerable upper intake levels">Tolerable Upper Intake Level</a> (UL) of dietary vanadium, beyond which adverse effects may occur, is set at 1.8 mg/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vanadyl sulfate as a dietary supplement has been researched as a means of increasing insulin sensitivity or otherwise improving glycemic control in people who are diabetic. Some of the trials had significant treatment effects, but were deemed as being of poor study quality. The amounts of vanadium used in these trials (30 to 150 mg) far exceeded the safe upper limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2008_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2008-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The conclusion of the systemic review was "There is no rigorous evidence that oral vanadium supplementation improves glycaemic control in type 2 diabetes. The routine use of vanadium for this purpose cannot be recommended."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2008_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2008-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">astrobiology</a>, it has been suggested that discrete vanadium accumulations on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> could be a potential microbial <a href="/wiki/Biosignature" title="Biosignature">biosignature</a>, when used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">Raman spectroscopy</a> and morphology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biosignature_Vanadium_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biosignature_Vanadium-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanadium_Craig_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanadium_Craig-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All vanadium compounds should be considered toxic. Tetravalent <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_sulfate" title="Vanadyl sulfate">VOSO<sub>4</sub></a> has been reported to be at least 5 times more toxic than trivalent V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set an exposure limit of 0.05 mg/m<sup>3</sup> for vanadium pentoxide dust and 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> for vanadium pentoxide fumes in workplace air for an 8-hour workday, 40-hour work week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-85">[85]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has recommended that 35 mg/m<sup>3</sup> of vanadium be considered immediately dangerous to life and health, that is, likely to cause permanent health problems or death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium compounds are poorly absorbed through the gastrointestinal system. Inhalation of vanadium and vanadium compounds results primarily in adverse effects on the respiratory system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ress_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-ress-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Quantitative data are, however, insufficient to derive a subchronic or chronic inhalation reference dose. Other effects have been reported after oral or inhalation exposures on blood parameters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> liver,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> neurological development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and other organs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> in rats.
</p><p>There is little evidence that vanadium or vanadium compounds are reproductive toxins or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogens" title="Teratogens">teratogens</a>. Vanadium pentoxide was reported to be carcinogenic in male rats and in male and female mice by inhalation in an NTP study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ress_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-ress-87">[87]</a></sup> although the interpretation of the results has recently been disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> The carcinogenicity of vanadium has not been determined by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vanadium traces in <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuels</a> are the main fuel component in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_temperature_corrosion" title="High temperature corrosion">high temperature corrosion</a>. During combustion, vanadium oxidizes and reacts with sodium and sulfur, yielding <a href="/wiki/Vanadate" title="Vanadate">vanadate</a> compounds with melting points as low as 530 °C, which attack the <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation layer</a> on steel and render it susceptible to corrosion. The solid vanadium compounds also abrade engine components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
