<p><b>Disulfur dioxide</b>, <b>dimeric sulfur monoxide</b> or <b>SO dimer</b> is an oxide of sulfur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The solid is unstable with a lifetime of a few seconds at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Disulfur dioxide adopts cis-planar structure with C<sub>2v</sub> symmetry. The S-O bond length is 145.8 pm, shorter than in <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide" title="Sulfur monoxide">sulfur monoxide</a>. The S-S bond length is 202.45 pm and the OSS angle is 112.7°.  S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> has a dipole moment of 3.17 D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lovas_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lovas-4">[4]</a></sup> an asymmetric top molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atm3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-atm3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thorwirth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thorwirth-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide" title="Sulfur monoxide">Sulfur monoxide</a> (SO) converts to disulfur dioxide (S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) spontaneously and reversibly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lovas_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-lovas-4">[4]</a></sup> So the substance can be generated by methods that produce sulfur monoxide. Disulfur dioxide has also been formed by an electric discharge in <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thorwirth_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thorwirth-5">[5]</a></sup> Another laboratory procedure is to react oxygen atoms with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_oxysulfide" title="Carbon oxysulfide">carbon oxysulfide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> vapour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng1999_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng1999-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although most forms of elemental sulfur (S<sub>8</sub> and other rings and chains) do not combine with SO<sub>2</sub>, atomic sulfur does so to form sulfur monoxide, which dimerizes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murakami2003_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murakami2003-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>S + SO<sub>2</sub> <big>→</big> S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> ⇌   2SO</dd></dl>
<p>Disulfur dioxide is also produced upon a microwave discharge in sulfur dioxide diluted in helium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Field2005_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Field2005-8">[8]</a></sup> At a pressure of 0.1 mm Hg,five percent of the result is S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PujapandaBalaram_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PujapandaBalaram-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Disulfur dioxide is formed transiently when <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and oxygen undergo <a href="/wiki/Flash_photolysis" title="Flash photolysis">flash photolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ionisation energy of disulfur dioxide is 9.93±0.02  eV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheng1999_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng1999-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Disulfur dioxide absorbs at 320–400 nm, as observed of the Venusian atmosphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frandsen2016_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frandsen2016-11">[11]</a></sup> and is believed to have contributed to the greenhouse effect on that planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyUser_Cbc.ca_November_11_2016c_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MyUser_Cbc.ca_November_11_2016c-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although disulfur dioxide exists in equilibrium with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide" title="Sulfur monoxide">sulfur monoxide</a>, it also reacts with sulfur monoxide to form sulfur dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_monoxide" title="Disulfur monoxide">disulfur monoxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Field2005_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Field2005-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herron_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herron-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complexes">Complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> can be a <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a> with transition metals. It binds in the η<sup>2</sup>-S,S' position with both sulfur atoms linked to the metal atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halcrow1994_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halcrow1994-14">[14]</a></sup> This was first shown in 2003. The bis-(trimethylphosphine) <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thiirane_S-oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thiirane S-oxide (page does not exist)">thiirane S-oxide</a> complex of platinum, when heated in toluene at 110 °C loses ethylene, and forms a complex with S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: (Ph<sub>3</sub>P)<sub>2</sub>PtS<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lorenz1986_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenz1986-15">[15]</a></sup> Iridium atoms can also form a complex: <i>cis</i>-[(dppe)<sub>2</sub>IrS<sub>2</sub>]Cl with sodium periodate oxidises to [(dppe)<sub>2</sub>IrS<sub>2</sub>O] and then to [(dppe)<sub>2</sub>IrS<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>], with dppe being <a href="/wiki/1,2-Bis(diphenylphosphino)ethane" title="1,2-Bis(diphenylphosphino)ethane">1,2-Bis(diphenylphosphino)ethane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid1975_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid1975-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nagata_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nagata-17">[17]</a></sup> This substance has the S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in a cis position.  The same conditions can make a trans complex, but this contains two separate SO radicals instead. The iridium complex can be decomposed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triphenyl_phosphine" title="Triphenyl phosphine">triphenyl phosphine</a> to form triphenyl phosphine oxide, and triphenyl phosphine sulfide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid1975_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid1975-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anion">Anion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> anion has been observed in the gas phase.  It may adopt a trigonal shape akin to SO<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clements2002_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clements2002-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Solar_System">In the Solar System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Disulfur_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In the Solar System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a some evidence that disulfur dioxide may be a small component in the atmosphere of Venus, and that it may substantially contribute of the planet's severe greenhouse effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frandsen2016_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frandsen2016-11">[11]</a></sup> It is not found in any substantive quantity in Earth's atmosphere.
</p>
