<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_heat_and_internal_energy">Relation to heat and internal energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relation to heat and internal energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heat is energy transferred spontaneously from a hotter to a colder system or body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speyer_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leland_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-leland-2">[2]</a></sup> Heat is energy in transfer, not a property of any one system, or 'contained' within it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speyer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-1">[1]</a></sup> On the other hand, internal energy is a property of a system. In an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>, the internal energy is the statistical mean of the gas particles' kinetic energy, and it is this kinetic motion that is the source and the effect of the transfer of heat across a system's boundary. For this reason, the term "thermal energy" is sometimes used synonymously with <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a>. (Heat and work depend on the way in which an energy transfer occurred, whereas internal energy is a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">property of the state of a system</a> and can thus be understood even without knowing how the energy got there.) The term "thermal energy" is also applied to the energy carried by a heat flow,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, although this quantity can also simply be called heat or amount of heat.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an 1847 lecture titled "On Matter, Living Force, and Heat", <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> characterised various terms that are closely related to thermal energy and heat. He identified the terms <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> as forms of heat each affecting distinct physical phenomena, namely the potential and kinetic energy of particles, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He described latent energy as the energy of interaction in a given configuration of particles, i.e. a form of <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a>, and the sensible heat as an energy affecting temperature measured by the thermometer due to the thermal energy, which he called the living force.
</p>
