<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atmospheric thermodynamics</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Atmospheric thermodynamics</b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>-to-work transformations (and their reverse) that take place in the earth's atmosphere and manifest as weather or climate.  Atmospheric thermodynamics use the laws of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_thermodynamics" title="Classical thermodynamics">classical thermodynamics</a>,  to describe and explain such phenomena as the properties of moist air, the formation of clouds, atmospheric convection, boundary layer meteorology, and vertical instabilities in the atmosphere. Atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_diagrams" title="Thermodynamic diagrams">thermodynamic diagrams</a> are used as tools in the forecasting of storm development.  Atmospheric thermodynamics forms a basis for cloud microphysics and convection <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parametrization_(climate)" title="Parametrization (climate)">parameterizations</a> used in numerical weather models and is used in many climate considerations, including convective-equilibrium climate models.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_thermodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The atmosphere is an example of a non-equilibrium system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Atmospheric thermodynamics describes the effect of buoyant forces that cause the rise of less dense (warmer) air, the descent of more dense air, and the transformation of water from liquid to vapor (evaporation) and its condensation. Those dynamics are modified by the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">force of the pressure gradient</a> and that motion is modified by the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a>.  The tools used include the law of energy conservation, the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>, specific heat capacities, the assumption of <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropic processes</a> (in which <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> is a constant), and moist <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic processes</a> (during which no energy is transferred as heat). Most of tropospheric gases are treated as ideal gases and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, with its ability to change phase from vapor, to liquid, to solid, and back is considered as one of the most important trace components of air.
</p><p>Advanced topics are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_transitions" title="Phase transitions">phase transitions</a> of water, homogeneous and in-homogeneous nucleation, effect of dissolved substances on cloud condensation, role of supersaturation on formation of ice crystals and cloud droplets. Considerations of moist air and cloud theories typically involve various temperatures, such as equivalent potential temperature, wet-bulb and virtual temperatures. Connected areas are energy, momentum, and <a href="/wiki/Mass_transfer" title="Mass transfer">mass transfer</a>, turbulence interaction between air particles in clouds, convection, dynamics of tropical cyclones, and large scale dynamics of the atmosphere.
</p><p>The major role of atmospheric thermodynamics is expressed in terms of adiabatic and diabatic forces acting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">air parcels</a> included in <a href="/wiki/Primitive_equations" title="Primitive equations">primitive equations</a> of air motion either as grid resolved or subgrid parameterizations. These equations form a basis for the numerical weather and climate predictions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_thermodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 19th century thermodynamicists such as <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Sadi Carnot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius" title="Rudolf Clausius">Rudolf Clausius</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Clapeyron" title="Émile Clapeyron">Émile Clapeyron</a> developed mathematical models on the dynamics of fluid bodies and vapors related to the combustion and pressure cycles of atmospheric steam engines; one example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_equation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron equation">Clausius–Clapeyron equation</a>.  In 1873, thermodynamicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willard_Gibbs" title="Willard Gibbs">Willard Gibbs</a> published "Graphical Methods in the Thermodynamics of Fluids."
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skew-T.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="747" data-file-width="838" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Skew-T.gif/220px-Skew-T.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Skew-T.gif/330px-Skew-T.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Skew-T.gif/440px-Skew-T.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Skew-T.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thermodynamic diagram developed in the 19th century is still used to calculate quantities such as convective available potential energy or air stability.</div></div></div>
<p>These sorts of foundations naturally began to be applied towards the development of theoretical models of atmospheric thermodynamics which drew the attention of the best minds.  Papers on atmospheric thermodynamics appeared in the 1860s that treated such topics as dry and moist <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic processes</a>. In 1884 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rudolf_Hertz" title="Heinrich Rudolf Hertz">Heinrich Hertz</a> devised first atmospheric thermodynamic diagram (<a href="/wiki/Emagram" title="Emagram">emagram</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Pseudo-adiabatic process was coined by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_von_Bezold" title="Wilhelm von Bezold">von Bezold</a> describing air as it is lifted, expands, cools, and eventually precipitates its water vapor;  in 1888 he published voluminous work entitled "On the thermodynamics of the atmosphere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1911 von <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Alfred Wegener</a> published a book "Thermodynamik der Atmosphäre", Leipzig, J. A. Barth.
From here the development of atmospheric thermodynamics as a branch of science began to take root. The term "atmospheric thermodynamics", itself, can be traced to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_W._Very" title="Frank W. Very">Frank W. Verys</a> 1919 publication:  "The radiant properties of the earth from the standpoint of atmospheric thermodynamics" (Occasional scientific papers of the Westwood Astrophysical Observatory). By the late 1970s various textbooks on the subject began to appear.  Today, atmospheric thermodynamics is an integral part of weather forecasting.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_thermodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hadley_Circulation">Hadley Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_thermodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hadley Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hadley Circulation can be considered as a heat engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Hadley circulation is identified with rising of warm and moist air in the equatorial region with the descent of colder air in the subtropics corresponding to a thermally driven direct circulation, with consequent net production of kinetic energy. The thermodynamic efficiency of the Hadley system, considered as a heat engine, has been relatively constant over the 1979~2010 period, averaging 2.6%. Over the same interval, the power generated by the Hadley regime has risen at an average rate of about 0.54 TW per yr; this reflects an increase in energy input to the system consistent with the observed trend in the tropical sea surface temperatures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclone_Carnot_cycle">Tropical cyclone Carnot cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_thermodynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclone Carnot cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anvil_convection.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="540" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Anvil_convection.jpg/300px-Anvil_convection.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Anvil_convection.jpg/450px-Anvil_convection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Anvil_convection.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anvil_convection.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Air is being moistened as it travels toward convective system. Ascending motion in a deep convective core produces air expansion, cooling, and condensation. Upper level outflow visible as an anvil cloud is eventually descending conserving mass (rysunek – Robert Simmon).</div></div></div>
<p>The thermodynamic behavior of a hurricane can be modelled as a heat engine <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that operates between the heat reservoir of the sea at a temperature of about 300K (27 °C) and the heat sink of the tropopause at a temperature of about 200K (−72 °C) and in the process converts heat energy into mechanical energy of winds. Parcels of air traveling close to the sea surface take up heat and water vapor, the warmed air rises and expands and cools as it does so causes condensation and precipitation. The rising air, and condensation, produces circulatory winds that are propelled by the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a>, which whip up waves and increase the amount of warm moist air that powers the cyclone. Both a decreasing temperature in the upper troposphere or an increasing temperature of the atmosphere close to the surface will increase the maximum winds observed in hurricanes. When applied to hurricane dynamics it defines a Carnot heat engine cycle and predicts maximum hurricane intensity.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron relation">Clausius–Clapeyron relation</a> shows how the water-holding capacity of the atmosphere increases by about 8% per Celsius increase in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. (It does not directly depend on other parameters like the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>.) </p>
