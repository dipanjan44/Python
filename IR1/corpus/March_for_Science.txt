<p>The <b>March for Science</b> (formerly known as the <b>Scientists' March on Washington</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indepToOppose_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-indepToOppose-6">[6]</a></sup> is an international series of rallies and marches held on <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a>. The inaugural march was held on April 22, 2017 in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and more than 600 other cities across the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170422a_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170422a-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170422b_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170422b-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-map1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M4Sofficialsite_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-M4Sofficialsite-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M4Sbadforscience_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-M4Sbadforscience-11">[11]</a></sup> According to organizers, the march is a <a href="/wiki/Nonpartisanism" title="Nonpartisanism">non-partisan</a> movement to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and the role it plays in everyday lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M4Sofficialsitemission_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-M4Sofficialsitemission-12">[12]</a></sup> The goals of the marches and rallies were to emphasize that science upholds the <a href="/wiki/Common_good" title="Common good">common good</a> and to call for <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_policy" title="Evidence-based policy">evidence-based policy</a> in the public's best interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M4Sbadforscience_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-M4Sbadforscience-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21goals_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-21goals-13">[13]</a></sup> The March for Science organizers, using crowd science techniques, estimated global attendance at 1.07 million, with 100,000 participants estimated for the main March in Washington, D.C., 70,000 in Boston, 60,000 in Chicago, 50,000 in Los Angeles, 50,000 in San Francisco,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Science_Behind_the_March_for_Science_Crowd_Estimates_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Science_Behind_the_March_for_Science_Crowd_Estimates-14">[14]</a></sup> 14,000 in Phoenix, and 11,000 in Berlin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organizers announced plans for a <a href="/wiki/March_for_Science_2018" title="March for Science 2018">second March for Science</a> to be held April 14, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  More than 70 satellite events around the world have already registered to participate in the 2nd annual event, including New York City,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Abuja, Nigeria,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Baraut, India<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The March for Science organizers and supporters say that support for science should be nonpartisan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahuja_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahuja-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossman_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rossman-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rathernotmarch_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-rathernotmarch-22">[22]</a></sup> The march is being organized by scientists<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VOA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-VOA-1">[1]</a></sup> skeptical of the agenda of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_administration" title="Trump administration">Trump administration</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossman_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rossman-21">[21]</a></sup> and critical of Trump administration policies widely viewed as hostile to science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn-23">[23]</a></sup> The march's website states that an "American government that ignores science to pursue ideological agendas endangers the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahuja_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ahuja-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossman_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rossman-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Particular issues of <a href="/wiki/Science_policy" title="Science policy">science policy</a> raised by the marchers include support for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evidence-based_policymaking" title="Evidence-based policymaking">evidence-based policymaking</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn-23">[23]</a></sup> as well as support for government <a href="/wiki/Funding_of_science" title="Funding of science">funding for scientific research</a>, <a href="/wiki/Open_government" title="Open government">government transparency</a>, and government acceptance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus_on_climate_change" title="Scientific consensus on climate change">scientific consensus on climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Level_of_support_for_evolution" title="Level of support for evolution">evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahuja_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ahuja-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossman_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rossman-21">[21]</a></sup> The march is part of growing political activity by American scientists in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2016" title="United States presidential election, 2016">November 2016 elections</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2017_Women%27s_March" title="2017 Women's March">2017 Women's March</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rathernotmarch_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-rathernotmarch-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Robert_N._Proctor" title="Robert N. Proctor">Robert N. Proctor</a>, a historian of science at Stanford University, stated that the March for Science was "pretty unprecedented in terms of the scale and breadth of the scientific community that's involved" and was rooted in "a broader perception of a massive attack on sacred notions of truth that are sacred to the scientific community."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Donald_Trump">Donald Trump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Donald Trump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> referred to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> as a hoax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> As a presidential candidate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucky_Tran_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucky_Tran-27">[27]</a></sup> he promised to resume construction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keystone_XL_Pipeline" title="Keystone XL Pipeline">Keystone XL Pipeline</a> and roll back <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) regulations adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_administration" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>After Trump's election, his <a href="/wiki/Presidential_transition_of_Donald_Trump" title="Presidential transition of Donald Trump">transition team</a> sought out specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> (DOE) employees who had worked on climate change during the Obama administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Prior to Trump's inauguration, many climate scientists began downloading climate data from government websites that they feared might be deleted by the Trump administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Other actions taken or promised by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_administration" title="Trump administration">Trump administration</a> inspired the march, including pulling out of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NetburnFrontier_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NetburnFrontier-31">[31]</a></sup> the stances of his <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Donald_Trump" title="Cabinet of Donald Trump">Cabinet nominees</a>, the freezing of research grants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Gag_order" title="Gag order">gag order</a> placed on scientists in the EPA regarding dissemination of their research findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWPGoingToProtest_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TWPGoingToProtest-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In February 2017, <a href="/wiki/William_Happer" title="William Happer">William Happer</a>, a possible Trump science advisor with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_scientists_opposing_the_mainstream_scientific_assessment_of_global_warming" title="List of scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming">skeptical views on human caused global warming</a>, described an area of climate science as "really more like a cult" and its practitioners "glassy-eyed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ScienceInsider" title="ScienceInsider">ScienceInsider</a></i> reported Trump's first budget request as "A grim budget day for U.S. science" because it contained major funding cuts to <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a>'s research and satellite programs, the EPA's Office of Research and Development, the DOE's <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science" title="Office of Science">Office of Science</a> and energy programs, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">U.S. Geological Survey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>, and other science agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_solidarity">International solidarity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International solidarity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>International sister marches were planned for countries around the world. These both supported American scientists and climate scientists more generally, and protested against other impingements on academic freedom internationally, such as government action against the <a href="/wiki/Central_European_University" title="Central European University">Central European University</a> in Hungary and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_educational_institutions_closed_in_the_2016_Turkish_purges" title="List of educational institutions closed in the 2016 Turkish purges">closure of educational institutes</a> and dismissal of academics in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2016%E2%80%9317_Turkish_purges" title="2016–17 Turkish purges">2016–17 Turkish purges</a>, as well as local issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning_and_participants">Planning and participants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Planning and participants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:30%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"There needs to be a Scientists' March on Washington."</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">-Beaverteeth92's original proposal on Reddit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>A major source of inspiration behind the planning of the march was the <a href="/wiki/2017_Women%27s_March" title="2017 Women's March">2017 Women's March</a> of January 21, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The specific idea to create a march originated from a Reddit discussion thread about the removal of references to climate change from the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWPGoingToProtest_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-TWPGoingToProtest-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In the discussion, an anonymous poster named "Beaverteeth92" made a comment regarding the need for a "Scientist's March on Washington".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throwaway_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-throwaway-41">[41]</a></sup> Dozens of Reddit users responded positively to the proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throwaway_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-throwaway-41">[41]</a></sup> Jonathan Berman, a postdoctoral fellow at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_Health_Science_Center" title="University of Texas Health Science Center">University of Texas Health Science Center</a> and a participant in the original conversation, created a Facebook page, Twitter feed and website to organize a march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWPGoingToProtest_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-TWPGoingToProtest-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-throwaway_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-throwaway-41">[41]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> group grew from 200 members to 300,000 in less than a week,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWPGoingToProtest_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-TWPGoingToProtest-2">[2]</a></sup> growing to 800,000 members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  Individual scientists have both applauded and criticized this development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>It was announced on March 30 that <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nye" title="Bill Nye">Bill Nye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mona_Hanna-Attisha" title="Mona Hanna-Attisha">Mona Hanna-Attisha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lydia_Villa-Komaroff" title="Lydia Villa-Komaroff">Lydia Villa-Komaroff</a> would headline the march, and serve as honorary co-chairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaplanNye_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-KaplanNye-4">[4]</a></sup> The protest was set to occur on <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn_2017_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn_2017-45">[45]</a></sup> with satellite rallies planned in hundreds of cities across the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-map1-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the annual meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> (AAAS), the largest scientific organization in the US, scientists held the "Rally to Stand Up for Science" at <a href="/wiki/Copley_Square" title="Copley Square">Copley Square</a>, Boston, on February 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The same month, the AAAS announced its support for the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaplanNye_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-KaplanNye-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghosh_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ghosh-48">[48]</a></sup> By mid-March, some 100 science organizations endorsed the March for Science, including many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_societies" title="Scientific societies">scientific societies</a>. Endorsers of the march included the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Geographers" title="American Association of Geographers">American Association of Geographers</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Physical_Anthropologists" title="American Association of Physical Anthropologists">American Association of Physical Anthropologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Neuroscience" title="Society for Neuroscience">Society for Neuroscience</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Society_for_Freshwater_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Society for Freshwater Science (page does not exist)">Society for Freshwater Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" title="American Statistical Association">American Statistical Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Psychological_Science" title="Association for Psychological Science">Association for Psychological Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Sociological_Association" title="American Sociological Association">American Sociological Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_Society" title="Electrochemical Society">Electrochemical Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entomological_Society_of_America" title="Entomological Society of America">Entomological Society of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/California_Academy_of_Sciences" title="California Academy of Sciences">California Academy of Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium">Monterey Bay Aquarium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>The University of Delaware Center for Political Communication conducted a survey of 1,040 members of March for Science Facebook groups or pages from March 31 to April 18 to study their motivations for joining the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bothum_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bothum-50">[50]</a></sup> Respondents cited the following as reasons for marching:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bothum_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bothum-50">[50]</a></sup>

<p>The primary march in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> began at 10 AM with a rally and teach-in on the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a>, featuring speeches by concerned citizens alternating with scientists and engineers; including <a href="/wiki/Denis_Hayes" title="Denis Hayes">Denis Hayes</a>, co-founder of the first Earth Day in 1970 and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nye" title="Bill Nye">Bill Nye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planb_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-planb-52">[52]</a></sup> No politicians spoke at the rally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planb_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-planb-52">[52]</a></sup> At 2 PM the crowd of thousands, in spite of the steady rain throughout the day, proceeded down <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Avenue" title="Constitution Avenue">Constitution Avenue</a> to 3rd Street, NW between the <a href="/wiki/National_Mall" title="National Mall">National Mall</a> and the west front of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planb_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-planb-52">[52]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support">Support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 26, 2017, U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a> of Vermont expressed his support for the march, congratulating "those scientists and researchers who are fighting back".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> U.S. Representative Bill Foster of Illinois, a physicist and the only current member of Congress with a Ph.D. in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a> field, will join the march, "not as a Democratic member of Congress, but as a scientist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gacher_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gacher-57">[57]</a></sup> Foster said that he viewed the march as political, but not partisan, saying, "if you see a specific policy that is inconsistent with the known principles of science, every citizen who is also a scientist should speak out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gacher_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gacher-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February the AAAS and other science groups announced their support for the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KaplanNye_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-KaplanNye-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rush_Holt_Jr." title="Rush Holt Jr.">Rush Holt Jr.</a>, the chief executive officer of the AAAS, expressed support for scientist involvement in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghosh_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ghosh-48">[48]</a></sup> Holt also emphasizes the importance of "appreciation for and understanding of science in the general population".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"What’s so interesting is it’s the first time, I think, anybody can point to in decades where there has been a spontaneous effort to defend the idea of science. It’s not a march pro or con GMOs or pro or con nuclear power. It’s about the value of science and the power of evidence. People are understandably and correctly outraged that in so many areas of public policy ideology is crowding out evidence, that evidence seems to be optional in the fashioning of public policy, and that you have officials using phrases like <i>alternative fact</i>."– Rush Holt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_for_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of scientists voiced concerns over the march. <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_James_Gates" title="Sylvester James Gates">Sylvester James Gates</a> warned that "such a politically charged event might send a message to the public that scientists are driven by ideology more than by evidence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLOOMsome_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLOOMsome-59">[59]</a></sup> Climate change denier <a href="/wiki/William_Happer" title="William Happer">William Happer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> stated that "there's no reason to assume the president is against science" while regarding the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLOOMsome_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLOOMsome-59">[59]</a></sup> Writing in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Young" title="Robert S. Young">Robert S. Young</a> argued that the march will "reinforce the narrative from skeptical conservatives that scientists are an interest group and politicize their data, research and findings for their own ends" and that it would be better for scientists to "march into local civic groups, churches, county fairs and, privately, into the offices of elected officials."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Matthew Nisbet writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Skeptical_Inquirer" title="Skeptical Inquirer">Skeptical Inquirer</a></i> magazine right after the first march in 2017, states that it isn't the least educated but the "best educated and most scientifically literate who are prone to biased reasoning and false beliefs about contentious science issues". In his opinion this will mean that the March will only deepen "partisan differences, while jeopardizing trust and impartiality and credibility of scientists". Regardless of assurances by the March's organizers that this would be non-political, it clearly was. Nisbet feels that confidence in scientists is strong, and they should "use this capital wisely and effectively".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nisbet_2017_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nisbet_2017-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Responding to criticism surrounding the political nature of the march, meteorologist and columnist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Holthaus" title="Eric Holthaus">Eric Holthaus</a> wrote that the scientific field "has always been political" and referred to the example of Galileo Galilei's <a href="/wiki/Galileo_affair" title="Galileo affair">confrontation with the political order</a>. Holthaus wrote that the scientists must also protest when "truth itself is being called into question".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Discussing science’s role in policy and government, Rush Holt points out a <a href="/wiki/Fallacy" title="Fallacy">fallacy</a> in viewing science and politics as philosophically incompatible: "The ethic in the profession is that you stick to your science, and if you’re interested in how science affects public policy or public questions, just let the facts speak for themselves. Of course, there’s a fallacy there, too. Facts are, by themselves, voiceless."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>San Francisco Lead Organizer Kristen Ratan debated <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Coyne" title="Jerry Coyne">Jerry Coyne</a> on <a href="/wiki/KQED" title="KQED">KQED's</a> Forum<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> regarding his criticism of the March and remarked that the millennial generation is just finding its feet with regard to activism and should be encouraged. Ratan also distinguished between being political and being partisan and suggested that while the March for Science is a political act, it is by no means partisan, which implies blind allegiance to one party over another. Ratan reiterated that the March For Science supports evidence-based policy-making regardless of party or affiliation.
</p>
