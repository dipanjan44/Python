<p><b>Nitrogen dioxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. It is one of several <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is an intermediate in the industrial synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, millions of tons of which are produced each year which is used primary in production of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>. At higher temperatures it is a reddish-brown <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> that has a characteristic sharp, biting odor and is a prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" title="Air pollutant">air pollutant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAmain_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAmain-8">[8]</a></sup> Nitrogen dioxide is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>, bent molecule with C<sub>2v</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">point group symmetry</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nitrogen dioxide is a reddish-brown gas above 21.2 °C (70.2 °F; 294.3 K) with a pungent, acrid odor, becomes a yellowish-brown liquid below 21.2 °C (70.2 °F; 294.3 K), and converts to the colorless dinitrogen tetroxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) below −11.2 °C (11.8 °F; 261.9 K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PGCH_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-PGCH-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bond</a> length between the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> atom and the oxygen atom is 119.7 <a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">pm</a>. This bond length is consistent with a <a href="/wiki/Bond_order" title="Bond order">bond order</a> between one and two.
</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, O<sub>3</sub>, the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_state" title="Electronic state">electronic state</a> of nitrogen dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Doublet_state" title="Doublet state">doublet state</a>, since nitrogen has one unpaired electron,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which decreases the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_effect" title="Alpha effect">alpha effect</a> compared with <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> and creates a weak bonding interaction with the oxygen lone pairs. The lone electron in <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> also means that this compound is a <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">free radical</a>, so the formula for nitrogen dioxide is often written as <sup>•</sup><span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>The reddish-brown color is a consequence of preferential absorption of light in the blue (400 – 500 nm), although the absorption extends throughout the visible (at shorter wavelengths) and into the infrared (at longer wavelengths). Absorption of light at wavelengths shorter than about 400 nm results in photolysis (to form NO + O, atomic oxygen); in the atmosphere the addition of O atom so formed to O<sub>2</sub> results in ozone formation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_reactions">Preparation and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nitrogen dioxide typically arises via the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> by oxygen in air:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NO + <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Nitrogen dioxide is formed in most <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> processes using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidant" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a>. At elevated temperatures <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> combines with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO</span></dd></dl>
<p>In the laboratory, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> can be prepared in a two-step procedure where dehydration of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_pentoxide" title="Dinitrogen pentoxide">dinitrogen pentoxide</a>, which subsequently undergoes thermal decomposition:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></dd>
<dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> → 4 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The thermal decomposition of some metal nitrates also affords <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">Pb(NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 PbO + 4 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Alternatively, reduction of concentrated nitric acid by metal (such as copper).
</p>
<dl><dd>4 <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + Cu → <span class="chemf nowrap">Cu(NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></dd></dl>
<p>Or finally by adding concentrated nitric acid over tin; hydrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" title="Tin dioxide">tin dioxide</a> is produced as byproduct.
</p>
<dl><dd>4 HNO<sub>3</sub> + Sn → H<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>3</sub> + 4 NO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_reactions">Main reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_thermal_properties">Basic thermal properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Basic thermal properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> exists in equilibrium with the colourless gas <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>):
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>The equilibrium is characterized by <span class="nowrap">Δ<i>H</i> = −57.23 kJ/mol</span>, which is exothermic. NO<sub>2</sub> is favored at higher temperatures, while at lower temperatures, dinitrogen tetroxide (N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) predominates. <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">Dinitrogen tetroxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) can be obtained as a white solid with melting point −11.2 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-10">[10]</a></sup> NO<sub>2</sub> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> due to its unpaired electron, while N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>.
</p><p>The chemistry of nitrogen dioxide has been investigated extensively. At 150 °C, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> decomposes with release of oxygen via an endothermic process (<span class="nowrap">Δ<i>H</i> = 14 kJ/mol</span>):
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 NO + <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_oxidizer">As an oxidizer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As an oxidizer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As suggested by the weakness of the N–O bond, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is a good oxidizer. Consequently, it will combust, sometimes explosively, with many compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysis">Hydrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hydrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>It <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyses</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">nitrous acid</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf <p>This reaction is one step in the <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_process" title="Ostwald process">Ostwald process</a> for the industrial production of nitric acid from ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This reaction is negligibly slow at low concentrations of NO<sub>2</sub> characteristic of the ambient atmosphere, although it does proceed upon NO<sub>2</sub> uptake to surfaces. Such surface reaction is thought to produce gaseous <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">HNO<sub>2</sub></a> (often written as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HONO" title="HONO">HONO</a>) in outdoor and indoor environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_from_decomposition_of_nitric_acid">Formation from decomposition of nitric acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Formation from decomposition of nitric acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The nitrogen dioxide so formed confers the characteristic yellow color often exhibited by this acid.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_nitrates">Conversion to nitrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conversion to nitrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_nitrites">Conversion to nitrites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conversion to nitrites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is introduced into the environment by natural causes, including entry from the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, bacterial respiration, volcanos, and lightning. These sources make <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> a <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">trace gas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a>, where it plays a role in absorbing <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> and regulating the chemistry of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, especially in determining <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is used as an intermediate in the manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, as a nitrating agent in manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_explosive" title="Chemical explosive">chemical explosives</a>, as a polymerization inhibitor for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrylates" title="Acrylates">acrylates</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Flour_bleaching_agent" title="Flour bleaching agent">flour bleaching agent</a>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>223</span></sup> and as a room temperature sterilization agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mechanism_Overview,_June_2012_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mechanism_Overview,_June_2012-15">[15]</a></sup> It is also used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuel</a>, for example in <a href="/wiki/Red_fuming_nitric_acid" title="Red fuming nitric acid">red fuming nitric acid</a>; it was used in the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan rockets</a>, to launch <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Project Gemini</a>, in the maneuvering thrusters of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, and in unmanned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_probes" title="Space probes">space probes</a> sent to various planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChemWorld_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChemWorld-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human-caused_sources_and_exposure">Human-caused sources and exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Human-caused sources and exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the general public, the most prominent sources of <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engines" title="Internal combustion engines">internal combustion engines</a> burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAmain_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPAmain-8">[8]</a></sup> Outdoors, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> can be a result of traffic from motor vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indoors, exposure arises from cigarette smoke,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATSDR_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATSDR-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_heater" title="Kerosene heater">kerosene heaters</a> and stoves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Workers in industries where <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is used are also exposed and are at risk for <a href="/wiki/Occupational_lung_disease" title="Occupational lung disease">occupational lung diseases</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIOSH" title="NIOSH">NIOSH</a> has set exposure limits and safety standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PGCH_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-PGCH-6">[6]</a></sup> Astronauts in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">Apollo–Soyuz Test Project</a> were almost killed when <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> was accidentally vented into the cabin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChemWorld_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChemWorld-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Farmworker" title="Farmworker">Agricultural workers</a> can be exposed to <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> arising from grain decomposing in silos; chronic exposure can lead to lung damage in a condition called "<a href="/wiki/Occupational_lung_disease#Silo-filler.27s_disease" title="Occupational lung disease">Silo-filler's disease</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, nitrogen dioxide was also produced by atmospheric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_test" title="Nuclear test">nuclear tests</a>, and was responsible for the reddish colour of <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_cloud" title="Mushroom cloud">mushroom clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide_poisoning" title="Nitrogen dioxide poisoning">Nitrogen dioxide poisoning</a></div>
<p>Gaseous <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> diffuses into the epithelial lining fluid (ELF) of the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_epithelium" title="Respiratory epithelium">respiratory epithelium</a> and dissolves, and chemically reacts with antioxidant and lipid molecules in the ELF; <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>'s health effects are caused by the reaction products or their metabolites, which are <a href="/wiki/Reactive_nitrogen_species" title="Reactive nitrogen species">reactive nitrogen species</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen species</a> that can drive <a href="/wiki/Bronchoconstriction" title="Bronchoconstriction">bronchoconstriction</a>, inflammation, reduced immune response, and may have effects on the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAassess2016_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAassess2016-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:No2toxpathwaysEPA.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="1182" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/No2toxpathwaysEPA.png/220px-No2toxpathwaysEPA.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/No2toxpathwaysEPA.png/330px-No2toxpathwaysEPA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/No2toxpathwaysEPA.png/440px-No2toxpathwaysEPA.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:No2toxpathwaysEPA.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pathways indicated by a dotted line are those for which evidence is limited to findings from experimental animal studies, while evidence from controlled human exposure studies is available for pathways indicated by a solid line. <p>Acute harm due to <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> exposure is only likely to arise in occupational settings. Direct exposure to the skin can cause irritations and burns. Only very high concentrations of the gaseous form cause immediate distress: 10–20 ppm can cause mild irritation of the nose and throat, 25–50 ppm can cause edema leading to <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, and levels above 100 ppm can cause death due to asphyxiation from fluid in the lungs. There are often no symptoms at the time of exposure other than transient cough, fatigue or nausea, but over hours inflammation in the lungs causes edema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToxnetHH_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ToxnetHH-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>For skin or eye exposure, the affected area is flushed with saline. For inhalation, oxygen is administered, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronchodilators" title="Bronchodilators">bronchodilators</a> may be administered, and if there are signs of <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobinemia" title="Methemoglobinemia">methemoglobinemia</a>, a condition that arises when nitrogen-based compounds affect the <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> in red blood cells, <a href="/wiki/Methylene_blue" title="Methylene blue">methylene blue</a> may be administered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is classified as an <a href="/wiki/List_of_extremely_hazardous_substances" title="List of extremely hazardous substances">extremely hazardous substance</a> in the United States as defined in Section 302 of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Planning_and_Community_Right-to-Know_Act" title="Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act">Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act</a> (42 U.S.C. 11002), and it is subject to strict reporting requirements by facilities which produce, store, or use it in significant quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov-right-know_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov-right-know-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects_of_NO2_exposure">Health effects of <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Health effects of NO2 exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide_poisoning" title="Nitrogen dioxide poisoning">Nitrogen dioxide poisoning</a></div>
<p>For the public, chronic exposure to <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> can cause respiratory effects including airway inflammation in healthy people and increased respiratory symptoms in people with asthma. <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> creates <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> which causes eye irritation and exacerbates respiratory conditions, leading to increased visits to emergency departments and hospital admissions for respiratory issues, especially asthma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAHealth_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAHealth-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effects of toxicity on health have been examined using questionnaires and inperson interviews in an effort to understand the relationship between (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  and asthma. The influence of indoor air pollutants on health is important because the majority of people in the world spend more than 80% of their time indoors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup> The amount of time spent indoors depends upon on several factors including geographical region, job activities, and gender among other variables.  Additionally, because home insulation is improving, this can result in greater retention of indoor air pollutants, such as (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup>  With respect to geographic region, the prevalence of asthma has ranged from 2 to 20% with no clear indication as to what’s driving the difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup> This may be a result of the “hygiene hypothesis” or "western lifestyle” that captures the notions of homes that are well insulated and with fewer inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup>  Another study examined the relationship between nitrogen exposure in the home and respiratory symptoms  and found a statistically significant odds ratio of 2.23 (95% CI: 1.06, 4.72) among those with a medical diagnosis of asthma and gas stove exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>A major source of indoor exposure to (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  is from the use of gas stoves for cooking or heating in homes.  According to the 2000 census, over half of US households use gas stoves<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and indoor exposure levels of (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  are, on average, at least three times higher in homes with gas stoves compared to electric stoves with the highest levels being in multifamily homes. Exposure to (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  is especially harmful for children with asthma. Research has shown that children with asthma who live in homes with gas stoves have greater risk of respiratory symptoms such as wheezing, cough and chest tightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAbasic_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAbasic-33">[33]</a></sup>  Additionally, gas stove use was associated with reduced lung function in girls with asthma, although this association was not found in boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  Using ventilation when operating gas stoves may reduce the risk of respiratory symptoms in children with asthma.
</p><p>In a cohort study with inner-city minority African American Baltimore children to determine if there was a relationship between (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  and asthma for children aged 2 to 6 years old, with an existing medical diagnosis of asthma, and one asthma related visit. Families of lower socioeconomic status were more likely to have gas stoves in their homes. The study concluded that higher levels of (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  within a home were linked to a greater level of respiratory symptoms among the study population.  This further exemplifies that (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)  toxicity is dangerous for children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoiding_NO2_toxicity">Avoiding <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Avoiding NO2 toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While using a gas stove, it is advised to also use ventilation. Studies show that in homes with gas stoves, if ventilation is used while using gas stoves, then children have lower odds of asthma, wheezing and bronchitis as compared to children in homes that never used ventilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> If venting isn’t possible, then replacing gas stoves with electric stove could be another option. Replacing gas stoves with electric ranges could greatly reduce the exposure to indoor NO<sub>2</sub> and improve the respiratory function of children with asthma. It is important to keep gas stoves and heaters in good repair so they are not polluting extra NO<sub>2</sub>. 2015 International Residential Code that requires that vent hoods are used for all stoves and set standards for residential buildings. This requires that all range hoods have a vent that discharges outside. You can also prevent NO<sub>2</sub> exposure by avoiding cigarette smoking and not idling your car whenever possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_limits">Environmental limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nitrogen_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The U.S. EPA has set safety levels for environmental exposure to <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> at 100 ppb, averaged over one hour, and 53 ppb, averaged annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAmain_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPAmain-8">[8]</a></sup> As of February 2016, no area of the US was out of compliance with these limits and concentrations ranged between 10–20 ppb, and annual average ambient NO<sub>2</sub> concentrations, as measured at area-wide monitors, have decreased by more than 40% since 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAbasic_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPAbasic-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> concentrations in vehicles and near roadways are appreciably higher than those measured at monitors in the current network. In fact, in-vehicle concentrations can be 2–3 times higher than measured at nearby area-wide monitors. Near-roadway (within about 50 metres (160 ft)) concentrations of NO<sub>2</sub> have been measured to be approximately 30 to 100% higher than concentrations away from roadways. Individuals who spend time on or near major roadways can experience short-term NO<sub>2</sub> exposures considerably higher than measured by the current network. Approximately 16% of U.S. housing units are located within 300 feet (91 m) of a major highway, railroad, or airport (approximately 48 million people). Studies show a connection between breathing elevated short-term NO<sub>2</sub> concentrations, and increased visits to emergency departments and hospital admissions for respiratory issues, especially asthma. NO<sub>2</sub> exposure concentrations near roadways are of particular concern for susceptible individuals, including asthmatics, children, and the elderly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAHealth_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPAHealth-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>For limits in other countries see the table in the <i><a href="/wiki/Ambient_air_quality_criteria" title="Ambient air quality criteria">Ambient air quality criteria</a></i> article.
</p>
