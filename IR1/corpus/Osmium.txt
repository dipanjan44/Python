<p><b>Osmium</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> ὀσμή <i>osme</i>, "smell") is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Os</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 76. It is a hard, brittle, bluish-white <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> that is found as a <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">trace element</a> in alloys, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> ores. Osmium is the densest naturally occurring element, with an experimentally measured (using x-ray crystallography) <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004225900000000000♠</span>22.59 g/cm<sup>3</sup></span>. Manufacturers use its <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> with platinum, <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>, and other platinum-group metals to make <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain pen</a> <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)#Nib_tipping" title="Nib (pen)">nib tipping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_contacts" title="Electrical contacts">electrical contacts</a>, and in other applications that require extreme durability and <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaynes20114.25_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaynes20114.25-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">element's abundance</a> in the Earth's crust is among the rarest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osmium_1-crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="289" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Osmium_1-crop.jpg/170px-Osmium_1-crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Osmium_1-crop.jpg/255px-Osmium_1-crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/00/Osmium_1-crop.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osmium_1-crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Osmium, remelted pellet</div></div></div>
<p>Osmium has a blue-gray tint and is the densest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_element" title="Stable element">stable element</a>, approximately twice the density of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHaynes20114.25_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHaynes20114.25-3">[3]</a></sup> and slightly denser than <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Densities_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Densities-6">[6]</a></sup> Calculations of density from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a> data may produce the most reliable data for these elements, giving a value of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004225620000000000♠</span>22.562<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.009 <a href="/wiki/Gram_per_cubic_centimetre" title="Gram per cubic centimetre">g/cm<sup>3</sup></a></span> for iridium versus <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004225870000000000♠</span>22.587<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.009 g/cm<sup>3</sup></span> for osmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Densest_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Densest-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Osmium is a hard but brittle <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that remains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lustrous" title="Lustrous">lustrous</a> even at high temperatures. It has a very low <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">compressibility</a>. Correspondingly, its <a href="/wiki/Bulk_modulus" title="Bulk modulus">bulk modulus</a> is extremely high, reported between <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002395000000000000♠</span>395</span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011462000000000000♠</span>462 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">GPa</a></span>, which rivals that of <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011443000000000000♠</span>443 GPa</span>). The hardness of osmium is moderately high at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009400000000000000♠</span>4 GPa</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Because of its <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a>, brittleness, low <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> (the lowest of the platinum-group metals), and very high <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elements_by_melting_point" title="List of elements by melting point">fourth highest</a> of all elements, after only <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>), solid osmium is difficult to machine, form or work.
</p>

<p>Osmium forms compounds with <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> ranging from −2 to +8. The most common oxidation states are +2, +3, +4, and +8. The +8 oxidation state is notable for being the highest attained by any chemical element aside from iridium's +9<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and is encountered only in <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selig_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-selig-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hassium" title="Hassium">hassium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The oxidation states −1 and −2 represented by the two reactive compounds <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>[Os<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">13</sub></span>]</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>[Os(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]</span> are used in the synthesis of osmium <a href="/wiki/Cluster_chemistry#Transition_metal_carbonyl_clusters" title="Cluster chemistry">cluster compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common compound exhibiting the +8 oxidation state is <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>. This toxic compound is formed when powdered osmium is exposed to air. It is a very volatile, water-soluble, pale yellow, crystalline solid with a strong smell. Osmium powder has the characteristic smell of osmium tetroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mager_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-mager-19">[19]</a></sup> Osmium tetroxide forms red osmates <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> upon reaction with a base. With <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, it forms the nitrido-osmates <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holle_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holle-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffith_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffith-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Osmium tetroxide boils at 130 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> and is a powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizing</a> agent. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Osmium_dioxide" title="Osmium dioxide">osmium dioxide</a> (OsO<sub>2</sub>) is black, non-volatile, and much less reactive and toxic.
</p><p>Only two osmium compounds have major applications: osmium tetroxide for <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a> tissue in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_microscopy" title="Electron microscopy">electron microscopy</a> and for the oxidation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, and the non-volatile osmates for <a href="/wiki/Sharpless_asymmetric_dihydroxylation" title="Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation">organic oxidation reactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bozzola_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bozzola-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Osmium pentafluoride (OsF<sub>5</sub>) is known, but osmium trifluoride (OsF<sub>3</sub>) has not yet been synthesized. The lower oxidation states are stabilized by the larger halogens, so that the trichloride, tribromide, triiodide, and even diiodide are known. The oxidation state +1 is known only for osmium iodide (OsI), whereas several carbonyl complexes of osmium, such as <a href="/wiki/Triosmium_dodecacarbonyl" title="Triosmium dodecacarbonyl">triosmium dodecacarbonyl</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Os<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sub></span></span>), represent oxidation state 0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holle_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holle-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffith_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griffith-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, the lower oxidation states of osmium are stabilized by <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a> that are good σ-donors (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amines" title="Amines">amines</a>) and π-acceptors (<a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic_compound" title="Heterocyclic compound">heterocycles</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>). The higher oxidation states are stabilized by strong σ- and π-donors, such as <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite its broad range of compounds in numerous oxidation states, osmium in bulk form at ordinary temperatures and pressures resists attack by all acids and alkalis, including <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_osmium" title="Isotopes of osmium">Isotopes of osmium</a></div>
<p>Osmium has seven naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, six of which are stable: <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">184</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">187</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">188</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">189</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">190</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, and (most abundant) <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">192</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>. <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">186</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span> undergoes <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> with such a long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7015200000000000000♠</span>(2.0<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>1.1)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>15</sup></span> years, approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005140000000000000♠</span>140<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> times the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a>, that for practical purposes it can be considered stable. Alpha decay is predicted for all seven naturally occurring isotopes, but it has been observed only for <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">186</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>, presumably due to very long half-lives. It is predicted that <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">184</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">192</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span> can undergo <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a> but this radioactivity has not been observed yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">187</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span> is the descendant of <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">187</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span><a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">Re</a></span> (half-life <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018143902655999999♠</span>4.56<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> years</span>) and is used extensively in dating terrestrial as well as <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteoric</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhenium-osmium_dating" title="Rhenium-osmium dating">rhenium-osmium dating</a>). It has also been used to measure the intensity of continental weathering over geologic time and to fix minimum ages for stabilization of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a> roots of continental <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a>. This decay is a reason why rhenium-rich minerals are abnormally rich in <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">187</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Os</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> However, the most notable application of Os isotopes in geology has been in conjunction with the abundance of iridium, to characterise the layer of <a href="/wiki/Shocked_quartz" title="Shocked quartz">shocked quartz</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary">Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary</a> that marks the extinction of the non-avian <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> 65 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alvarez_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alvarez-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Osmium was discovered in 1803 by <a href="/wiki/Smithson_Tennant" title="Smithson Tennant">Smithson Tennant</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The discovery of osmium is intertwined with that of platinum and the other metals of the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a>. Platinum reached Europe as <i>platina</i> ("small silver"), first encountered in the late 17th century in silver mines around the <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Chocó Department">Chocó Department</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The discovery that this metal was not an alloy, but a distinct new element, was published in 1748.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
Chemists who studied platinum dissolved it in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a> (a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acids</a>) to create soluble salts. They always observed a small amount of a dark, insoluble residue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Proust" title="Joseph Louis Proust">Joseph Louis Proust</a> thought that the residue was <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Collet-Descotils" title="Victor Collet-Descotils">Victor Collet-Descotils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Fran%C3%A7ois,_comte_de_Fourcroy" title="Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy">Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin" title="Louis Nicolas Vauquelin">Louis Nicolas Vauquelin</a> also observed iridium in the black platinum residue in 1803, but did not obtain enough material for further experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup> Later the two French chemists Antoine-François Fourcroy and Nicolas-Louis Vauquelin identified a metal in a platinum residue they called ‘<i>ptène</i>’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1803, <a href="/wiki/Smithson_Tennant" title="Smithson Tennant">Smithson Tennant</a> analyzed the insoluble residue and concluded that it must contain a new metal. Vauquelin treated the powder alternately with alkali and acids<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-35">[35]</a></sup> and obtained a volatile new oxide, which he believed was of this new metal—which he named <i>ptene</i>, from the Greek word <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">πτηνος</span> (ptènos) for winged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griffith_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-griffith-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, Tennant, who had the advantage of a much larger amount of residue, continued his research and identified two previously undiscovered elements in the black residue, iridium and osmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-35">[35]</a></sup> He obtained a yellow solution (probably of <i>cis</i>–[Os(OH)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>) by reactions with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> at red heat. After acidification he was able to distill the formed OsO<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griffith_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-griffith-36">[36]</a></sup> He named it osmium after <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>osme</i> meaning "a smell", because of the ashy and smoky smell of the volatile <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weeks_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-weeks-38">[38]</a></sup> Discovery of the new elements was documented in a letter to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> on June 21, 1804.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hunt_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-hunt-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">Uranium</a> and osmium were early successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a> reaction of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, giving enough yield to make the process economically successful. At the time, a group at <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bosch" title="Carl Bosch">Carl Bosch</a> bought most of the world's supply of osmium to use as a catalyst. Shortly thereafter, in 1908, cheaper catalysts based on iron and iron oxides were introduced by the same group for the first pilot plants, removing the need for the expensive and rare osmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nowadays osmium is obtained primarily from the processing of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-YB-2006_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-YB-2006-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Platinum_nuggets.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="439" data-file-width="816" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Platinum_nuggets.jpg/220px-Platinum_nuggets.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Platinum_nuggets.jpg/330px-Platinum_nuggets.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Platinum_nuggets.jpg/440px-Platinum_nuggets.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Platinum_nuggets.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Native platinum containing traces of the other <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> metals</div></div></div>
<p>Osmium is one of the even-numbered elements, which puts it in the upper half of <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">elements commonly found in space</a>. It is, however, the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">least abundant</a> stable element in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, with an average mass fraction of 50 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts per trillion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wede_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-wede-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Osmium is found in nature as an uncombined element or in natural <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>; especially the iridium–osmium alloys, <a href="/wiki/Osmiridium" title="Osmiridium">osmiridium</a> (osmium rich), and <a href="/wiki/Osmiridium" title="Osmiridium">iridosmium</a> (iridium rich).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-35">[35]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> deposits, the platinum group metals occur as <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a> (i.e., (Pt,Pd)S)), <a href="/wiki/Telluride_(chemistry)" title="Telluride (chemistry)">tellurides</a> (e.g., PtBiTe), <a href="/wiki/Antimonide" title="Antimonide">antimonides</a> (e.g., PdSb), and <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenides</a> (e.g., PtAs<sub>2</sub>); in all these compounds platinum is exchanged by a small amount of iridium and osmium. As with all of the platinum group metals, osmium can be found naturally in alloys with nickel or <a href="/wiki/Native_copper" title="Native copper">copper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within Earth's crust, osmium, like iridium, is found at highest concentrations in three types of geologic structure: igneous deposits (crustal intrusions from below), <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact craters</a>, and deposits reworked from one of the former structures. The largest known primary reserves are in the <a href="/wiki/Bushveld_Igneous_Complex" title="Bushveld Igneous Complex">Bushveld Igneous Complex</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirk-pt_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirk-pt-44">[44]</a></sup> though the large copper–nickel deposits near <a href="/wiki/Norilsk#Norilsk-Talnakh_nickel_deposits" title="Norilsk">Norilsk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Basin" title="Sudbury Basin">Sudbury Basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> are also significant sources of osmium. Smaller reserves can be found in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirk-pt_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-kirk-pt-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> deposits used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> people in the <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Chocó Department">Chocó Department</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> are still a source for platinum group metals. The second large alluvial deposit was found in the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>, Russia, which is still mined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-YB-2006_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-YB-2006-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osmium_cluster.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1892" data-file-width="2060" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Osmium_cluster.jpg/220px-Osmium_cluster.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Osmium_cluster.jpg/330px-Osmium_cluster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Osmium_cluster.jpg/440px-Osmium_cluster.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osmium_cluster.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Osmium <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, grown by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_transport_reaction" title="Chemical transport reaction">chemical vapor transport</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Osmium is obtained commercially as a by-product from <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> mining and processing. During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_extraction_techniques#Electrorefining" title="Copper extraction techniques">electrorefining of copper</a> and nickel, noble metals such as silver, gold and the platinum group metals, together with non-metallic elements such as <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> settle to the bottom of the cell as <i>anode mud</i>, which forms the starting material for their extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2008-summary_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2008-summary-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinYb2006_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-MinYb2006-47">[47]</a></sup> Separating the metals requires that they first be brought into solution. Several methods can achieve this, depending on the separation process and the composition of the mixture. Two representative methods are fusion with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_peroxide" title="Sodium peroxide">sodium peroxide</a> followed by dissolution in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>, and dissolution in a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirk-pt_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-kirk-pt-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann-pt_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-pt-48">[48]</a></sup> Osmium, ruthenium, rhodium and iridium can be separated from platinum, gold and base metals by their insolubility in aqua regia, leaving a solid residue. Rhodium can be separated from the residue by treatment with molten <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bisulfate" title="Sodium bisulfate">sodium bisulfate</a>. The insoluble residue, containing Ru, Os and Ir, is treated with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a>, in which Ir is insoluble, producing water-soluble Ru and Os salts. After oxidation to the volatile oxides, <span class="chemf nowrap">RuO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> is separated from <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> by precipitation of (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>RuCl<sub>6</sub> with ammonium chloride.
</p><p>After it is dissolved, osmium is separated from the other platinum group metals by distillation or extraction with organic solvents of the volatile osmium tetroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The first method is similar to the procedure used by Tennant and Wollaston. Both methods are suitable for industrial scale production. In either case, the product is reduced using hydrogen, yielding the metal as a powder or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_sponge" title="Metal sponge">sponge</a> that can be treated using <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">powder metallurgy</a> techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neither the producers nor the United States Geological Survey published any production amounts for osmium. In 1971, estimations of the United States production of osmium as a byproduct of copper refining was 2000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">troy ounces</a> (62 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appraisal_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appraisal-51">[51]</a></sup>  In 2017, the estimated US import of osmium for consumption was 90 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the volatility and extreme toxicity of its oxide, osmium is rarely used in its pure state, but is instead often alloyed with other metals for high-wear applications. Osmium alloys such as <a href="/wiki/Osmiridium" title="Osmiridium">osmiridium</a> are very hard and, along with other platinum-group metals, are used in the tips of <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain pens</a>, instrument pivots, and electrical contacts, as they can resist wear from frequent operation. They were also used for the tips of <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">phonograph</a> <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">styli</a> during the late 78 <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">rpm</a> and early "<a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">LP</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">45</a>" record era, circa 1945 to 1955. Osmium-alloy tips were significantly more durable than steel and chromium needle points, but wore out far more rapidly than competing, and costlier, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> tips, so they were discontinued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">Osmium tetroxide</a> has been used in <a href="/wiki/Fingerprint" title="Fingerprint">fingerprint</a> detection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and in staining <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fatty</a> tissue for optical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_microscopy" title="Electron microscopy">electron microscopy</a>. As a strong oxidant, it cross-links lipids mainly by reacting with unsaturated carbon–carbon bonds and thereby both fixes <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">biological membranes</a> in place in tissue samples and simultaneously stains them. Because osmium atoms are extremely electron-dense, osmium staining greatly enhances image contrast in <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">transmission electron microscopy</a> (TEM) studies of biological materials. Those carbon materials have otherwise very weak TEM contrast (see image).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bozzola_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bozzola-23">[23]</a></sup> Another osmium compound, osmium ferricyanide (OsFeCN), exhibits similar fixing and staining action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The tetroxide and its derivative  <a href="/wiki/Potassium_osmate" title="Potassium osmate">potassium osmate</a> are important oxidants in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>. For the <a href="/wiki/Sharpless_asymmetric_dihydroxylation" title="Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation">Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation</a>, which uses osmate for the conversion of a <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bond</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">vicinal</a> <a href="/wiki/Diol" title="Diol">diol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Barry_Sharpless" title="Karl Barry Sharpless">Karl Barry Sharpless</a> was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> OsO<sub>4</sub> is very expensive for this use, so KMnO<sub>4</sub> is often used instead, even though the yields are less for this cheaper chemical reagent.
</p><p>In 1898 an Austrian chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Auer von Welsbach</a> developed the Oslamp with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_filament" title="Electrical filament">filament</a> made of osmium, which he introduced commercially in 1902. After only a few years, osmium was replaced by the more stable metal <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>. Tungsten has the highest melting point among all metals, and its use in light bulbs increases the luminous efficacy and life of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp">incandescent lamps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griffith_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-griffith-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The light bulb manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Osram" title="Osram">Osram</a> (founded in 1906, when three German companies, Auer-Gesellschaft, AEG and Siemens &amp; Halske, combined their lamp production facilities) derived its name from the elements of <b>os</b>mium and Wolf<b>ram</b> (the latter is German for tungsten).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>, powdered osmium effectively absorbs hydrogen atoms. This could make osmium a potential candidate for a metal-hydride battery electrode. However, osmium is expensive and would react with potassium hydroxide, the most common battery electrolyte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Osmium has high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> range of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>; for example, at 600 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> osmium has a reflectivity twice that of gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> This high reflectivity is desirable in space-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet-visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy">UV spectrometers</a>, which have reduced mirror sizes due to space limitations. Osmium-coated mirrors were flown in several space missions aboard the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, but it soon became clear that the oxygen radicals in the <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> are abundant enough to significantly deteriorate the osmium layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The only known clinical use of osmium is <a href="/wiki/Synovectomy" title="Synovectomy">synovectomy</a> in arthritic patients in Scandinavia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> It involves the local administration of osmium tetroxide (OsO<sub>4</sub>), which is a highly toxic compound. The lack of reports of long-term side effects suggest that osmium itself can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocompatible" title="Biocompatible">biocompatible</a>, though this depends on the osmium compound administered. In 2011, osmium(VI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and osmium(II)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> compounds were reported to show anticancer activity <i>in vivo</i>, it indicated a promising future for using osmium compounds as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticancer" title="Anticancer">anticancer</a> drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metallic osmium is harmless<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>  but finely divided metallic osmium is <a href="/wiki/Pyrophoricity" title="Pyrophoricity">pyrophoric</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appraisal_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Appraisal-51">[51]</a></sup> and reacts with oxygen at room temperature, forming volatile osmium tetroxide. Some osmium compounds are also converted to the tetroxide if oxygen is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appraisal_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-Appraisal-51">[51]</a></sup> This makes osmium tetroxide the main source of contact with the environment.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">Osmium tetroxide</a> is highly volatile and penetrates skin readily, and is very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> by inhalation, ingestion, and skin contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToxOs_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-ToxOs-69">[69]</a></sup> Airborne low concentrations of osmium tetroxide vapor can cause <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lung</a> congestion and <a href="/wiki/Human_skin" title="Human skin">skin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eye</a> damage, and should therefore be used in a <a href="/wiki/Fume_hood" title="Fume hood">fume hood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mager_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-mager-19">[19]</a></sup> Osmium tetroxide is rapidly reduced to relatively inert compounds by e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated" title="Polyunsaturated">polyunsaturated</a> <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">vegetable oils</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Corn_oil" title="Corn oil">corn oil</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Osmium is usually sold as a minimum 99.9% pure powder. Like other precious metals, it is measured by <a href="/wiki/Troy_weight" title="Troy weight">troy weight</a> and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grams" title="Grams">grams</a>. Its price in 2018 is about $1,000 per troy oz, depending on the quantity, its purity and its supplier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
