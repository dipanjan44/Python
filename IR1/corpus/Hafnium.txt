<p><b>Hafnium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Hf</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 72. A <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">lustrous</a>, silvery gray, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetravalence" title="Tetravalence">tetravalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>, hafnium chemically resembles <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> and is found in many zirconium <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. Its existence was <a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev</a> in 1869, though it was not identified until 1923, by Coster and Hevesy, making it the last <a href="/wiki/Stable_nuclide" title="Stable nuclide">stable</a> element to be discovered. Hafnium is named after <i>Hafnia</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name for <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen#Etymology" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, where it was discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Authier2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Authier2013-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hafnium is used in filaments and electrodes.  Some <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> fabrication processes use its oxide for <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> at 45 nm and smaller feature lengths. Some <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> used for special applications contain hafnium in combination with <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>.
</p><p>Hafnium's large <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a> cross-section makes it a good material for <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> absorption in <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a>, but at the same time requires that it be removed from the neutron-transparent corrosion-resistant <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_alloy" title="Zirconium alloy">zirconium alloys</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_bits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Hafnium_bits.jpg/220px-Hafnium_bits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Hafnium_bits.jpg/330px-Hafnium_bits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Hafnium_bits.jpg/440px-Hafnium_bits.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_bits.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pieces of hafnium</div></div></div>
<p>Hafnium is a shiny, silvery, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>-resistant and chemically similar to zirconium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup> (due to its having the same number of <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a>, being in the same group, but also to <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry" title="Relativistic quantum chemistry">relativistic effects</a>; the expected expansion of atomic radii from period 5 to 6 is almost exactly cancelled out by the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a>). The physical properties of hafnium metal samples are markedly affected by zirconium impurities, especially the nuclear properties, as these two elements are among the most difficult to separate because of their chemical similarity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>A notable physical difference between these metals is their <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, with zirconium having about one-half the density of hafnium. The most notable <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear</a> properties of hafnium are its high thermal <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron-capture cross-section</a> and that the nuclei of several different hafnium isotopes readily absorb two or more <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> apiece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup> In contrast with this, zirconium is practically transparent to thermal neutrons, and it is commonly used for the metal components of nuclear reactors – especially the cladding of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_rod" title="Nuclear fuel rod">nuclear fuel rods</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_characteristics">Chemical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Hafnium_compounds" title="Category:Hafnium compounds">Category:Hafnium compounds</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium(IV)_oxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="391" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Hafnium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg/220px-Hafnium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Hafnium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg/330px-Hafnium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/78/Hafnium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium(IV)_oxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hafnium dioxide</div></div></div>
<p>Hafnium reacts in air to form a <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">protective film</a> that inhibits further <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>.  The metal is not readily attacked by acids but can be oxidized with <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> or it can be burnt in air.  Like its sister metal zirconium, finely divided hafnium can ignite spontaneously in air. The metal is resistant to concentrated <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a>.
</p><p>The chemistry of hafnium and zirconium is so similar that the two cannot be separated on the basis of differing chemical reactions. The melting points and boiling points of the compounds and the <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in solvents are the major differences in the chemistry of these twin elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_hafnium" title="Isotopes of hafnium">Isotopes of hafnium</a></div>
<p>At least 34 isotopes of hafnium have been observed, ranging in mass number from 153 to 186.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-9">[9]</a></sup> The five stable isotopes are in the range of 176 to 180. The radioactive isotopes' <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-lives</a> range from only 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">ms</a> for <sup>153</sup>Hf,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-9">[9]</a></sup> to 2.0 petayears (10<sup>15</sup> years) for the most stable one, <sup>174</sup>Hf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> <sup>178m2</sup>Hf was at the <a href="/wiki/Hafnium_controversy" title="Hafnium controversy">center of a controversy</a> for several years regarding its potential use as a weapon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="422" data-file-width="513" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg/220px-Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg/330px-Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg/440px-Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zirc%C3%A3o.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zircon crystal (2×2 cm) from Tocantins, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hafnium is estimated to make up about 5.8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s upper <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> by mass. It does not exist as a free element on Earth, but is found combined in <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> with zirconium in natural <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>, ZrSiO<sub>4</sub>, which usually has about 1–4% of the Zr replaced by Hf. Rarely, the Hf/Zr ratio increases during crystallization to give the isostructural mineral <a href="/wiki/Hafnon" title="Hafnon">hafnon</a> (Hf,Zr)SiO<sub>4</sub>, with atomic Hf &gt; Zr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> An obsolete name for a variety of zircon containing unusually high Hf content is <i>alvite</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>A major source of zircon (and hence hafnium) ores is <a href="/wiki/Heavy_mineral_sands_ore_deposits" title="Heavy mineral sands ore deposits">heavy mineral sands ore deposits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatites</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbonatite" title="Carbonatite">carbonatite</a> intrusions, particularly the Crown Polymetallic Deposit at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Weld" title="Mount Weld">Mount Weld</a>, Western Australia. A potential source of hafnium is trachyte tuffs containing rare zircon-hafnium silicates <a href="/wiki/Eudialyte" title="Eudialyte">eudialyte</a> or armstrongite, at <a href="/wiki/Dubbo" title="Dubbo">Dubbo</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hafnium reserves have been infamously estimated to last under 10 years by one source if the world population increases and demand grows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In reality, since hafnium occurs with zirconium, hafnium can always be a byproduct of zirconium extraction to the extent that the low demand requires.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3592" data-file-width="4256" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/220px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/330px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/440px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Melted tip of a hafnium consumable electrode used in an <a href="/wiki/Electron-beam_additive_manufacturing" title="Electron-beam additive manufacturing">electron beam</a> <a href="/wiki/Electron-beam_furnace" title="Electron-beam furnace">remelting furnace</a>, a 1 cm cube, and an oxidized hafnium electron beam-remelted ingot (left to right)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Heavy_mineral_sands_ore_deposits" title="Heavy mineral sands ore deposits">heavy mineral sands ore deposits</a> of the titanium ores <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> yield most of the mined zirconium, and therefore also most of the hafnium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconium is a good nuclear fuel-rod cladding metal, with the desirable properties of a very low neutron capture cross-section and good chemical stability at high temperatures. However, because of hafnium's neutron-absorbing properties, hafnium impurities in zirconium would cause it to be far less useful for nuclear-reactor applications. Thus, a nearly complete separation of zirconium and hafnium is necessary for their use in nuclear power. The production of hafnium-free zirconium is the main source for hafnium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3582" data-file-width="8976" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg/220px-Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg/330px-Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg/440px-Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_pellets_with_a_thin_oxide_layer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hafnium oxidized  ingots which exhibit <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_optics" title="Thin-film optics">thin film optical</a> effects.</div></div></div>
<p>The chemical properties of hafnium and zirconium are nearly identical, which makes the two difficult to separate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larsen_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larsen-15">[15]</a></sup> The methods first used — <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a> of ammonium fluoride salts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1924a_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1924a-16">[16]</a></sup> or the fractional distillation of the chloride<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1924b_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1924b-17">[17]</a></sup> — have not proven suitable for an industrial-scale production. After zirconium was chosen as material for nuclear reactor programs in the 1940s, a separation method had to be developed. Liquid-liquid extraction processes with a wide variety of solvents were developed and are still used for the production of hafnium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hend_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hend-18">[18]</a></sup> About half of all hafnium metal manufactured is produced as a by-product of zirconium refinement. The end product of the separation is hafnium(IV) chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS1952_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1952-19">[19]</a></sup> The purified hafnium(IV) chloride is converted to the metal by reduction  with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, as in the <a href="/wiki/Kroll_process" title="Kroll process">Kroll process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilb_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilb-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>HfCl<sub>4</sub> + 2 Mg (1100 °C) → 2 MgCl<sub>2</sub> + Hf</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Further purification is effected by a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_transport_reaction" title="Chemical transport reaction">chemical transport reaction</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Crystal_bar_process" title="Crystal bar process">Arkel and de Boer</a>: In a closed vessel, hafnium reacts with <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> at temperatures of 500 °C, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafnium(IV)_iodide" title="Hafnium(IV) iodide">hafnium(IV) iodide</a>; at a tungsten filament of 1700 °C the reverse reaction happens, and the iodine and hafnium are set free. The hafnium forms a solid coating at the tungsten filament, and the iodine can react with additional hafnium, resulting in a steady turn over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1925_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1925-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Hf + 2 I<sub>2</sub> (500 °C) → HfI<sub>4</sub></dd>
<dd>HfI<sub>4</sub> (1700 °C) → Hf + 2 I<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">Chemical compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radii</a> of hafnium(IV) (0.78 angstroms) is almost the same  as that of  <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>(IV) (0.79 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">angstroms</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanl72_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-lanl72-22">[22]</a></sup> Consequently, compounds of hafnium(IV) and zirconium(IV) have very similar chemical and physical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanl72_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-lanl72-22">[22]</a></sup> Hafnium and zirconium tend to occur together in nature and the similarity of their ionic radii makes their chemical separation rather difficult. Hafnium tends to form <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic compounds</a> in the oxidation state of +4. <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">Halogens</a> react with it to form hafnium tetrahalides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanl72_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-lanl72-22">[22]</a></sup> At higher temperatures, hafnium reacts with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanl72_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-lanl72-22">[22]</a></sup> Some compounds of hafnium in lower oxidation states are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafnium(IV)_chloride" title="Hafnium(IV) chloride">Hafnium(IV) chloride</a> and hafnium(IV) iodide have some applications in the production and purification of hafnium metal.  They are volatile solids with polymeric structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-7">[7]</a></sup> These tetrachlorides are precursors to various <a href="/wiki/Organozirconium_chemistry" title="Organozirconium chemistry">organohafnium compounds</a> such as hafnocene dichloride and tetrabenzylhafnium.
</p><p>The white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafnium_oxide" title="Hafnium oxide">hafnium oxide</a> (HfO<sub>2</sub>), with a melting point of 2812 °C and a boiling point of roughly 5100 °C, is very similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconia" title="Zirconia">zirconia</a>, but slightly more basic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafnium_carbide" title="Hafnium carbide">Hafnium carbide</a> is the most <a href="/wiki/Refraction_(metallurgy)" title="Refraction (metallurgy)">refractory</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary compound</a> known, with a melting point over 3890 °C, and hafnium nitride is the most refractory of all known metal nitrides, with a melting point of 3310 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanl72_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-lanl72-22">[22]</a></sup> This has led to proposals that hafnium or its carbides might be useful as construction materials that are subjected to very high temperatures. The mixed carbide <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_hafnium_carbide" title="Tantalum hafnium carbide">tantalum hafnium carbide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Ta<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>HfC<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>) possesses the highest melting point of any currently known compound, 4215 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Recent supercomputer simulations suggest a hafnium alloy with a melting point of 4400 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hong2015_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-hong2015-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moseley_step_ladder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1750" data-file-width="1363" height="334" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Moseley_step_ladder.jpg/260px-Moseley_step_ladder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Moseley_step_ladder.jpg/390px-Moseley_step_ladder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Moseley_step_ladder.jpg/520px-Moseley_step_ladder.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moseley_step_ladder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photographic recording of the characteristic <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> emission lines of some elements</div></div></div>
<p>In his report on <i>The Periodic Law of the Chemical Elements</i>, in 1869, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a> had implicitly <a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">predicted the existence</a> of a heavier analog of titanium and zirconium. At the time of his formulation in 1871, Mendeleev believed that the elements were ordered by their <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic masses</a> and placed <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> (element 57) in the spot below zirconium. The exact placement of the elements and the location of missing elements was done by determining the specific weight of the elements and comparing the chemical and physical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy" title="X-ray spectroscopy">X-ray spectroscopy</a> done by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> in 1914 showed a direct dependency between <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral line</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effective_nuclear_charge" title="Effective nuclear charge">effective nuclear charge</a>. This led to the nuclear charge, or <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> of an element, being used to ascertain its place within the periodic table. With this method, Moseley determined the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanides" title="Lanthanides">lanthanides</a> and showed the gaps in the atomic number sequence at numbers 43, 61, 72, and 75.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The discovery of the gaps led to an extensive search for the missing elements. In 1914, several people claimed the discovery after Henry Moseley predicted the gap in the periodic table for the then-undiscovered element 72.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a> asserted that he found element 72 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a> in 1907 and published his results on <i>celtium</i> in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Neither the spectra nor the chemical behavior he claimed matched with the element found later, and therefore his claim was turned down after a long-standing controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mel_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mel-30">[30]</a></sup> The controversy was partly because the chemists favored the chemical techniques which led to the discovery of <i>celtium</i>, while the physicists relied on the use of the new X-ray spectroscopy method that proved that the substances discovered by Urbain did not contain element 72.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mel_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mel-30">[30]</a></sup> By early 1923, several physicists and chemists such as <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and Charles R. Bury<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> suggested that element 72 should resemble zirconium and therefore was not part of the rare earth elements group. These suggestions were based on Bohr's theories of the atom, the X-ray spectroscopy of Moseley, and the chemical arguments of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Paneth" title="Friedrich Paneth">Friedrich Paneth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Encouraged by these suggestions and by the reappearance in 1922 of Urbain's claims that element 72 was a rare earth element discovered in 1911, <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Coster" title="Dirk Coster">Dirk Coster</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_von_Hevesy" title="Georg von Hevesy">Georg von Hevesy</a> were motivated to search for the new element in zirconium ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Hafnium was discovered by the two in 1923 in Copenhagen, Denmark, validating the original 1869 prediction of Mendeleev.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It was ultimately found in <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> in Norway through X-ray spectroscopy analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hev1503_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-hev1503-38">[38]</a></sup> The place where the discovery took place led to the element being named for the Latin name for "Copenhagen", <i>Hafnia</i>, the home town of <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scerri_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scerri-39">[39]</a></sup> Today, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen_Faculty_of_Science" title="University of Copenhagen Faculty of Science">Faculty of Science</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a> uses in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seal</a> a stylized image of the hafnium atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hafnium was separated from zirconium through repeated recrystallization of the double <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> fluorides by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Valdemar_Thal_Jantzen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Valdemar Thal Jantzen (page does not exist)">Valdemar Thal Jantzen</a> and von Hevesey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1924a_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1924a-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anton_Eduard_van_Arkel" title="Anton Eduard van Arkel">Anton Eduard van Arkel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hendrik_de_Boer" title="Jan Hendrik de Boer">Jan Hendrik de Boer</a> were the first to prepare metallic hafnium by passing hafnium tetraiodide vapor over a heated <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> filament in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1924b_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1924b-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ark1925_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ark1925-21">[21]</a></sup> This process for differential purification of zirconium and hafnium is still in use today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1923, four predicted elements were still missing from the periodic table: 43 (<a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>) and 61 (<a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>) are radioactive elements and are only present in trace amounts in the environment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> thus making elements 75 (<a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>) and 72 (hafnium) the last two unknown non-radioactive elements. Since rhenium was discovered in 1908, hafnium was the last element with stable isotopes to be discovered.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the hafnium produced is used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Control_rod" title="Control rod">control rods</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hend_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hend-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several details contribute to the fact that there are only a few technical uses for hafnium: First, the close similarity between hafnium and zirconium makes it possible to use zirconium for most of the applications; second, hafnium was first available as pure metal after the use in the nuclear industry for hafnium-free zirconium in the late 1950s. Furthermore, the low abundance and difficult separation techniques necessary make it a scarce commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASTM_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-ASTM-6">[6]</a></sup> When the demand for zirconium dropped following the Fukushima disaster, the price of hafnium increased sharply from around $500–600/kg in 2014 to around $1000/kg in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_reactors">Nuclear reactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nuclear reactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nuclei of several hafnium isotopes can each absorb multiple neutrons. This makes hafnium a good material for use in the control rods for nuclear reactors. Its neutron-capture cross-section is about 600 times that of zirconium. (Other elements that are good neutron-absorbers for control rods are <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>.) Excellent mechanical properties and exceptional corrosion-resistance properties allow its use in the harsh environment of <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hend_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hend-18">[18]</a></sup> The German research reactor <a href="/wiki/Forschungsreaktor_M%C3%BCnchen_II" title="Forschungsreaktor München II">FRM II</a> uses hafnium as a neutron absorber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FRMII_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FRMII-43">[43]</a></sup> It is also common in military reactors, particularly in US naval reactors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schemel1977_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schemel1977-44">[44]</a></sup> but seldom found in civilian ones, the first core of the <a href="/wiki/Shippingport_Atomic_Power_Station" title="Shippingport Atomic Power Station">Shippingport Atomic Power Station</a> (a conversion of a naval reactor) being a notable exception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YanHino2011_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-YanHino2011-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2369" data-file-width="2349" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/220px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/330px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/440px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hafnium-containing rocket nozzle of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a> in the lower right corner</div></div></div>
<p>Hafnium is used in <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> with <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>, and other metals. An alloy used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket" title="Liquid rocket">liquid rocket</a> thruster nozzles, for example the main engine of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Modules</a>, is C103 which consists of 89% niobium, 10% hafnium and 1% titanium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hightemp_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-hightemp-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Small additions of hafnium increase the adherence of protective oxide scales on nickel-based alloys. It improves thereby the <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance especially under cyclic temperature conditions that tend to break oxide scales by inducing thermal stresses between the bulk material and the oxide layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microprocessors">Microprocessors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Microprocessors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hafnium-based compounds are employed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gate_(transistor)" title="Gate (transistor)">gate</a> insulators in the 45 nm generation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a> from <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Hafnium oxide-based compounds are practical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-k_dielectric" title="High-k dielectric">high-k dielectrics</a>, allowing reduction of the gate leakage current which improves performance at such scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotope_geochemistry">Isotope geochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Isotope geochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Isotopes of hafnium and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> (along with <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>) are also used in <a href="/wiki/Isotope_geochemistry" title="Isotope geochemistry">isotope geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geochronology" title="Geochronology">geochronological</a> applications, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutetium-hafnium_dating" title="Lutetium-hafnium dating">lutetium-hafnium dating</a>. It is often used as a tracer of isotopic evolution of <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">Earth’s mantle</a> through time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> This is because <sup>176</sup>Lu decays to <sup>176</sup>Hf with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of approximately 37 billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In most geologic materials, <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> is the dominant host of hafnium (&gt;10,000 ppm) and is often the focus of hafnium studies in <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Hafnium is readily substituted into the zircon <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal lattice</a>, and is therefore very resistant to hafnium mobility and contamination. Zircon also has an extremely low Lu/Hf ratio, making any correction for initial lutetium minimal. Although the Lu/Hf system can be used to calculate a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nd_model_ages" title="Nd model ages">model age</a>", i.e. the time at which it was derived from a given isotopic reservoir such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depleted-mantle_model" title="Depleted-mantle model">depleted mantle</a>, these "ages" do not carry the same geologic significance as do other geochronological techniques as the results often yield isotopic mixtures and thus provide an average age of the material from which it was derived.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">Garnet</a> is another mineral that contains appreciable amounts of hafnium to act as a geochronometer. The high and variable Lu/Hf ratios found in garnet make it useful for dating <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphic</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its heat resistance and its affinity to oxygen and nitrogen, hafnium is a good scavenger for oxygen and nitrogen in gas-filled and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lamp" title="Incandescent lamp">incandescent lamps</a>. Hafnium is also used as the electrode in <a href="/wiki/Plasma_cutting" title="Plasma cutting">plasma cutting</a> because of its ability to shed electrons into air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>The high energy content of <sup>178m2</sup>Hf was the concern of a <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a>-funded program in the US. This program determined that the possibility of using a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> of hafnium (the above-mentioned <sup>178m2</sup>Hf) to construct high-yield weapons with X-ray triggering mechanisms—an application of <a href="/wiki/Induced_gamma_emission" title="Induced gamma emission">induced gamma emission</a>—was infeasible because of its expense. See <a href="/wiki/Hafnium_controversy" title="Hafnium controversy">Hafnium controversy</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Care needs to be taken when <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machining</a> hafnium because it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a>—fine particles can spontaneously combust when exposed to air. Compounds that contain this metal are rarely encountered by most people. The pure metal is not considered toxic, but hafnium compounds should be handled as if they were toxic because the ionic forms of metals are normally at greatest risk for toxicity, and limited animal testing has been done for hafnium compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to hafnium in the workplace by breathing it in, swallowing it, skin contact, and eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">Permissible exposure limit</a>) for exposure to hafnium and hafnium compounds in the workplace as TWA 0.5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set the same <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL). At levels of 50 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, hafnium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
