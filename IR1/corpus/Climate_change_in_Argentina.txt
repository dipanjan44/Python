<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Argentina</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noaa-argentina.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="792" data-file-width="612" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Noaa-argentina.gif/225px-Noaa-argentina.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Noaa-argentina.gif/338px-Noaa-argentina.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Noaa-argentina.gif/450px-Noaa-argentina.gif 2x" width="225"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noaa-argentina.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Absolute maximum temperatures recorded in Argentina during the December 2013 heat wave. This heat wave was the longest and the most intense in Argentina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>According to scientists, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is predicted to have significant effects on the <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Argentina" title="Climate of Argentina">climate of Argentina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>30</span></sup> Though temperatures have increased at a slower rate than the global average, nonetheless these impacts have occurred in many areas. If these trends continue, it is predicted that climate change will exacerbate existing natural disasters such as increasing the intensity and frequency of floods or create new ones.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_trends">Observed trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observed trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation">Precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There has been an increase in annual precipitation in almost all of Argentina, particularly in the northeast and central parts of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metoffice_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metoffice-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup> Since 1970, precipitation has increased by 10% in the northeast while in parts of <a href="/wiki/La_Pampa_Province" title="La Pampa Province">La Pampa Province</a> and western parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_province" title="Buenos Aires province">Buenos Aires province</a>, it has increased by 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>86</span></sup> The highest increases in the precipitation (from the period 1960–2010) have occurred in the eastern parts of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima3da_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima3da-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> In contrast, the Andean part of Patagonia along with the <a href="/wiki/Cuyo_(Argentina)" title="Cuyo (Argentina)">Cuyo</a> region has seen a decrease in precipitation, leading to a reduction in river flow in the last 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima3da_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima3da-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> Some parts of Patagonia in the Andes have seen a 30%–50% reduction in precipitation since the middle of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, since the middle of the 20th century, the 600 mm (24 in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isohyet" title="Isohyet">isohyet</a> in the south and the 800 mm (31 in) isohyet in the north, which approximately form the boundaries in which agricultural production is possible had moved more than 100 km (62 mi) to the west, expanding possible agricultural production to these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>86,87</span></sup> Although an increase in precipitation will expand agricultural production to the west in areas that were previously too dry and will benefit hydroelectric energy owing to the increase in river flow, there will be an increase the frequency of extreme precipitation events such as flooding, hail and strong winds, which can destroy these agricultural fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29–30</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> These trends were observed with an increase in the river–stream flows in most of the country excluding rivers originating from the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, and increase in extreme precipitation events that led to considerable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">socioeconomic</a> losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25,87</span></sup> These extreme precipitation events such as flooding, and hail have occurred more frequently in the east,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>30</span></sup> leading to the destruction of agricultural land in these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>88</span></sup> The increase in precipitation has led to more variability in precipitation from year to year in the northern parts of the country, with a higher risk of prolonged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droughts" title="Droughts">droughts</a>, disfavoring agriculture in these regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>88</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperatures">Temperatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Temperatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mean temperatures have increased by 0.5 °C (0.90 °F) from 1901–2012, which is slightly lower than the global average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Temperatures in the Andean part of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> have increased by more than 1 °C (1.80 °F), which has caused the retreat of almost all of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciers" title="Glaciers">glaciers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>30</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> This affects water availability to <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> areas of the country that depend on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lanacioncc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-lanacioncc-6">[6]</a></sup> Higher temperatures can reduce winter snowfall, causing river flow to decrease (less water available), which can reduce hydroelectric energy production; losses of up to 40% had been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> In the last half of the 20th century, the lack of snow at the highest peaks in the Cuyo region has impacted agriculture and <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">viticulture</a> production due to less water available in the rivers (a reduction in 50% of river flow).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larraquy2014_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larraquy2014-7">[7]</a></sup> Outside Patagonia, mean temperatures have increased at a smaller rate since the increase in minimum temperatures is counteracted by the decrease in maximum temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>89</span></sup> The increase in minimum temperatures in much of Argentina outside Patagonia is attributed to the increase in the concentration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> in the atmosphere while the decrease in maximum temperatures is a consequence of higher precipitation, which is associated with higher cloud coverage and evaporation, processes that tend to reduce maximum temperatures north of 40<sup>o</sup>S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>89</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima3da_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima3da-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup> In Patagonia, the increase in mean temperatures is higher than the increase in minimum temperatures and is due to the change in atmospheric circulation, not just only the increase in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima3da_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima3da-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Ozone depletion</a> has played a major role in changing the atmospheric circulation patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> There has been a decrease in the days with frost, and increases in the number of hot nights throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima3da_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima3da-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_waves" title="Heat waves">Heat waves</a>, which is defined as 3 consecutive days in which the temperature exceeds the 90th <a href="/wiki/Percentile" title="Percentile">percentile</a> with respect to the 1961–1990 period have become more common and intense between 1961–2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_trends_and_impacts">Projected trends and impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Projected trends and impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the next 2 or 3 decades (2016–2035), mean temperatures are predicted to increase by 0.5 to 1.0 °C (0.90 to 1.80 °F) under the two scenarios (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">RCP</a> 4.5 and RCP 8.5) from the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Under the RCP 4.5 scenario, temperatures will increase by 1.0 °C (1.80 °F) throughout the country, though this increase will be more pronounced in the northwest where temperatures would increase by 2 to 2.5 °C (3.60 to 4.50 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> In the more severe RCP 8.5 scenario, the projected increased in temperatures will be higher, reaching 3.5 °C (6.30 °F) in the northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> In both scenarios, the projected warming will be more pronounced during the summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> The predicted trend for precipitation is not as clear as the one for temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>92</span></sup> In the northern and central regions, precipitation is predicted to increase while in much of central–western Argentina and Patagonia, precipitation is predicted to decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> Because Argentina is potentially vulnerable to climate change, such projected changes based on the models could enhance current or create new <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">problems associated with climate change</a> on Argentina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Agriculture will be affected by climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> The decrease in precipitation that has been observed in the Andes will be predicted to continue to decrease, affecting hydroelectric energy even more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>33</span></sup> Glaciers are predicted to continue to recede and melt or in some areas, disappear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> It is predicted that the Cuyo region could face a potential water crisis due to an increase in water demand owing to a reduction in river streamflows and higher <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> from a combination of lower precipitation and higher temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>95</span></sup> Higher temperatures will cause the snow cover to melt earlier in the year, causing a raise in river flow in the spring months and a drop in summer, which is when water demand is the highest for agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> The higher water demand would lead to higher groundwater use for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> which drives up the costs for irrigation, cause the deterioration in the water quality, and lead to the eventual depletion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquifers" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>95</span></sup> In northern Patagonia, a similar situation is projected in which negative impacts can occur in the future for fruit and vegetable growing owing to a reduction in available water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> It is predicted that between 2020–2040, the river flow in the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(Argentina)" title="Colorado River (Argentina)">Colorado River</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chubut_river" title="Chubut river">Chubut river</a> would decrease by 20% due to more irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>97</span></sup> Numerous studies have indicated that the productivity of wheat, soybean and maize will not change that much by the middle of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> This is because while crop production may increase in the southern and western parts of the Pampas, it will decrease in the northern parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup> In the north and central parts of the country, the higher temperatures projected for this region leads to higher evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>94</span></sup> Combined with little precipitation change for this region, it is probable that it would become more <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>94</span></sup> In areas that normally have a dry winter, a higher evaporation would intensify droughts which would disfavour agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>88,94</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_areas">Urban areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Urban areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Higher temperatures can negatively affect urban areas by affecting the provision of services such as water and energy by increasing the demand for these services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>94</span></sup> Heat waves, such as the one in 2013–2014 during the summer could become more frequent and intense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larraquy2014_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Larraquy2014-7">[7]</a></sup> These heat waves can impact agricultural production while in urban areas, it places more demand on energy needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>94</span></sup> Intense precipitation events could become more common, leading to negative consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclimatico2009_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclimatico2009-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>33</span></sup> Argentina, with much of its population living in urban areas is vulnerable to intense precipitation events since many cities are located near a body of water (rivers, lakes and oceans), which increases the likelihood of suffering from these intense precipitation events such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>95</span></sup> Climate change could extend the habitats of <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> carrying tropical diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> spread southwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In much of the coastal regions of Argentina, it will not suffer permanent flooding and loss of land associated with <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> since much of the coastal regions are high cliffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>97</span></sup> Nonetheless, potential agricultural land could be lost if sea levels rise by 1 m (3.3 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metoffice_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metoffice-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>67</span></sup> Sea level rise will impact the country through an increase in the frequency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_surges" title="Storm surges">storm surges</a> in coastal areas, including <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> and a study has suggested that Buenos Aires could be impacted significantly by sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barros2014_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-Barros2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metoffice_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Metoffice-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>67</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambioclima2da_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-cambioclima2da-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>98</span></sup>
</p>
