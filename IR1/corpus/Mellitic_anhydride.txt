<p><b>Mellitic anhydride</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Acid_anhydride" title="Acid anhydride">anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mellitic_acid" title="Mellitic acid">mellitic acid</a>, is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the formula C<sub>12</sub>O<sub>9</sub>.
</p><p>Mellitic anhydride is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>  (<a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon" title="Oxocarbon">oxocarbon</a>), like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_suboxide" title="Carbon suboxide">C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a>. It is a white sublimable solid, apparently obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justus_Liebig" title="Justus Liebig">Justus Liebig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> in 1830 in their study of <a href="/wiki/Mellite" title="Mellite">mellite</a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honey_stone" title="Honey stone">honey stone</a>"); they assigned it the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Empiric_formula&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Empiric formula (page does not exist)">empiric formula</a> C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The substance was properly characterized in 1913 by H. Meyer and K. Steiner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It retains the aromatic character of the benzene ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
