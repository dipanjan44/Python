<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Photovoltaics</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1625" data-file-width="2161" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg/220px-SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg/330px-SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg/440px-SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SoSie%2BSoSchiff_Ansicht.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Settlement" title="Solar Settlement">Solar Settlement</a>, a sustainable housing community project in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freiburg" title="Freiburg">Freiburg</a>, Germany.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2532" data-file-width="3232" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/220px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/330px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/440px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photovoltaic SUDI shade is an autonomous and mobile station in France that provides energy for electric cars using solar energy.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ROSSA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2032" data-file-width="3060" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/ROSSA.jpg/220px-ROSSA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/ROSSA.jpg/330px-ROSSA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/ROSSA.jpg/440px-ROSSA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ROSSA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar panels on the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Photovoltaics</b> (<b>PV</b>) is the conversion of light into <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconducting_material" title="Semiconducting material">semiconducting materials</a> that exhibit the <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect" title="Photovoltaic effect">photovoltaic effect</a>, a phenomenon studied in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>.
</p><p>A  <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic system</a> employs <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a>, each comprising a number of <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>, which generate electrical power.  PV installations may be ground-mounted, <a href="/wiki/Rooftop_photovoltaic_power_station" title="Rooftop photovoltaic power station">rooftop</a> mounted or wall mounted.  The mount may be fixed, or use a <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">solar tracker</a> to follow the sun across the sky.
</p><p>Solar PV has specific advantages as an energy source: once installed, its operation generates no pollution and no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a>, it shows simple scalability in respect of power needs and silicon has large availability in the Earth’s crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayumi2017_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayumi2017-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>PV systems have the major disadvantage that the power output works best with direct sunlight, so about 10-25% is lost if a tracking system is not used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_tracker-pro&amp;con_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_tracker-pro&amp;con-2">[2]</a></sup> Dust, clouds, and other obstructions in the atmosphere also diminish the power output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Another important issue is the concentration of the production in the hours corresponding to main insolation, which do not usually match the peaks in demand in human activity cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayumi2017_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mayumi2017-1">[1]</a></sup> Unless current societal patterns of consumption and electrical networks adjust to this scenario, electricity still needs to be stored for later use or made up by other power sources, usually hydrocarbons.
</p><p>Photovoltaic systems have long been used in specialized applications, and stand-alone and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grid-connected_PV_system" title="Grid-connected PV system">grid-connected PV systems</a> have been in use since the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reconsidering_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-reconsidering-5">[5]</a></sup> They were first mass-produced in 2000, when German environmentalists and the <a href="/wiki/Eurosolar" title="Eurosolar">Eurosolar</a> organization got government funding for a ten thousand roof program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_Power_for_the_World:_What_You_Wanted_to_Know_about_Photovoltaics_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_Power_for_the_World:_What_You_Wanted_to_Know_about_Photovoltaics-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Advances in technology and increased manufacturing scale have in any case reduced the cost, increased the reliability, and increased the efficiency of photovoltaic installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reconsidering_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-reconsidering-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Net_metering" title="Net metering">Net metering</a> and financial incentives, such as preferential <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariffs</a> for solar-generated electricity, have supported solar PV installations in many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> More than <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_by_country" title="Solar power by country">100 countries</a> now use solar PV.
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind powers</a>, PV is the third renewable energy source in terms of global capacity. At the end of 2016, worldwide installed PV capacity increased to more than 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatts" title="Gigawatts">gigawatts</a> (GW),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA-PVPS-2016_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEA-PVPS-2016-9">[9]</a></sup> covering approximately two percent of global <a href="/wiki/Electric_energy_consumption" title="Electric energy consumption">electricity demand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_China" title="Solar power in China">China</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Japan" title="Solar power in Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_United_States" title="Solar power in the United States">United States</a>, is the fastest growing market, while <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Germany" title="Solar power in Germany">Germany</a> remains the world's largest producer, with solar PV providing seven percent of annual domestic electricity consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014-11">[11]</a></sup> With current technology (as of 2013), photovoltaics <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon#Energy_payback_time" title="Crystalline silicon">recoups</a> the energy needed to manufacture them in 1.5 years in Southern Europe and 2.5 years in Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "photovoltaic" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> φῶς (<i>phōs</i>) meaning "light", and from "volt", the unit of electro-motive force, the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>, which in turn comes from the last name of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Volta" title="Alessandro Volta">Alessandro Volta</a>, inventor of the battery (<a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">electrochemical cell</a>). The term "photo-voltaic" has been in use in English since 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cells">Solar cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solar cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">Solar cell</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_cell.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="341" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Solar_cell.png/220px-Solar_cell.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Solar_cell.png/330px-Solar_cell.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/90/Solar_cell.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_cell.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells">Solar cells</a> generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> directly from <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="739" data-file-width="1500" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png/220px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png/330px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png/440px-SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SolarGIS-Solar-map-World-map-en.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Average insolation. Note that this is for a horizontal surface. Solar panels are normally propped up at an angle and receive more energy per unit area.</div></div></div>
<p>Photovoltaics are best known as a method for generating electric power by using <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> to convert energy from the sun into a flow of electrons by the <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect" title="Photovoltaic effect">photovoltaic effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solar cells produce direct current electricity from sunlight which can be used to power equipment or to <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">recharge a battery</a>. The first practical application of photovoltaics was to power orbiting satellites and other <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, but today the majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">photovoltaic modules</a> are used for grid connected power generation. In this case an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverter_(electrical)" title="Inverter (electrical)">inverter</a> is required to convert the DC to AC. There is a smaller market for off-grid power for remote dwellings, <a href="/wiki/Electric_boat" title="Electric boat">boats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">recreational vehicles</a>, electric cars, roadside emergency telephones, <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cathodic_protection" title="Cathodic protection">cathodic protection</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a>.
</p><p>Photovoltaic power generation employs <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> composed of a number of <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> containing a photovoltaic material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jac_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-jac-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Copper_in_renewable_energy#Photovoltaic_system_configurations" title="Copper in renewable energy">Copper solar cables</a> connect modules (module cable), arrays (array cable), and sub-fields. Because of the growing demand for <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources, the manufacturing of solar cells and <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic arrays</a> has advanced considerably in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_PV_market_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-German_PV_market-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renewableenergyaccess.com_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-renewableenergyaccess.com-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-technologyreview.com_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-technologyreview.com-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solar photovoltaic power generation has long been seen as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy">clean energy</a> technology which draws upon the planet’s most plentiful and widely distributed renewable energy source – the sun.  Cells require protection from the environment and are usually packaged tightly in solar panels.
</p><p>Photovoltaic power capacity is measured as maximum power output under standardized test conditions (STC) in "W<sub>p</sub>" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-peak" title="Watt-peak">watts peak</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The actual power output at a particular point in time may be less than or greater than this standardized, or "rated", value, depending on geographical location, time of day, weather conditions, and other factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Solar photovoltaic array <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factors</a> are typically under 25%, which is lower than many other industrial sources of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_developments">Current developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For best performance, terrestrial PV systems aim to maximize the time they face the sun. <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">Solar trackers</a> achieve this by moving PV panels to follow the sun. The increase can be by as much as 20% in winter and by as much as 50% in summer. Static mounted systems can be optimized by analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">sun path</a>. Panels are often set to latitude tilt, an angle equal to the latitude, but performance can be improved by adjusting the angle for summer or winter. Generally, as with other semiconductor devices, temperatures above room temperature reduce the performance of photovoltaics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of solar panels may also be mounted vertically above each other in a tower, if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenith_distance" title="Zenith distance">zenith distance</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is greater than zero, and the tower can be turned horizontally as a whole and each panels additionally around a horizontal axis. In such a tower the panels can follow the Sun exactly. Such a device may be described as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turntable_ladder#Turntable_ladder" title="Turntable ladder">ladder</a> mounted on a turnable disk. Each step of that ladder is the middle axis of a rectangular <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panel</a>. In case the zenith distance of the Sun reaches zero, the "ladder" may be rotated to the north or the south to avoid a solar panel producing a shadow on a lower solar panel. Instead of an exactly vertical tower one can choose a tower with an axis directed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_star" title="Polar star">polar star</a>, meaning that it is parallel to the rotation axis of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. In this case the angle between the axis and the Sun is always larger than 66 degrees. During a day it is only necessary to turn the panels around this axis to follow the Sun. Installations may be ground-mounted (and sometimes integrated with farming and grazing)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huliq.com_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-huliq.com-24">[24]</a></sup> or built into the roof or walls of a building (<a href="/wiki/Building-integrated_photovoltaics" title="Building-integrated photovoltaics">building-integrated photovoltaics</a>).
</p><p>Another recent development involves the makeup of solar cells. <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">Perovskite</a> is a very inexpensive material which is being used to replace the expensive <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">crystalline silicon</a> which is still part of a standard PV cell build to this day. Michael Graetzel, Director of the Laboratory of Photonics and Interfaces at EPFL says, "Today, efficiency has peaked at 18 percent, but it's expected to get even higher in the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn.com-25">[25]</a></sup> This is a significant claim, as 20% efficiency is typical among solar panels which use more expensive materials.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">Efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell_efficiency" title="Solar cell efficiency">Solar cell efficiency</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1555" data-file-width="2800" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/280px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/420px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/560px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Best Research-Cell Efficiencies</div></div></div>
<p>Electrical efficiency (also called conversion efficiency) is a contributing factor in the selection of a photovoltaic system. However, the most efficient solar panels are typically the most expensive, and may not be commercially available. Therefore, selection is also driven by cost efficiency and other factors.
</p><p>The electrical efficiency of a PV cell is a <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical property</a> which represents how much electrical power a cell can produce for a given <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">insolation</a>. The basic expression for maximum efficiency of a photovoltaic cell is given by the ratio of output power to the incident solar power (radiation flux times area)
</p>

<p>The efficiency is measured under ideal laboratory conditions and represents the maximum achievable efficiency of the PV material. Actual efficiency is influenced by the output Voltage, current, junction temperature, light intensity and spectrum.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The most efficient type of solar cell to date is a multi-junction concentrator solar cell with an efficiency of 46.0%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Institute_for_Solar_Energy_Systems_ISE" title="Fraunhofer Institute for Solar Energy Systems ISE">Fraunhofer ISE</a> in December 2014. The highest efficiencies achieved without concentration include a material by <a href="/wiki/Sharp_Corporation" title="Sharp Corporation">Sharp Corporation</a> at 35.8% using a proprietary triple-junction manufacturing technology in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and Boeing Spectrolab (40.7% also using a triple-layer design). The US company <a href="/wiki/SunPower" title="SunPower">SunPower</a> produces cells that have an efficiency of 21.5%, well above the market average of 12–18%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is an ongoing effort to increase the conversion efficiency of PV cells and modules, primarily for competitive advantage. In order to increase the efficiency of solar cells, it is important to choose a semiconductor material with an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> that matches the solar spectrum. This will enhance the electrical and optical properties. Improving the method of charge collection is also useful for increasing the efficiency. There are several groups of materials that are being developed. Ultrahigh-efficiency devices (η&gt;30%)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> are made by using GaAs and GaInP2 semiconductors with multijunction tandem cells. High-quality, single-crystal silicon materials are used to achieve high-efficiency, low cost cells (η&gt;20%).
</p><p>Recent developments in Organic photovoltaic cells (OPVs) have made significant advancements in power conversion efficiency from 3% to over 15% since their introduction in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> To date, the highest reported power conversion efficiency ranges from 6.7% to 8.94% for small molecule, 8.4%–10.6% for polymer OPVs, and 7% to 21% for perovskite OPVs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>  OPVs are expected to play a major role in the PV market. Recent improvements have increased the efficiency and lowered cost, while remaining environmentally-benign and renewable.
</p><p>Several companies have begun embedding <a href="/wiki/Power_optimizer" title="Power optimizer">power optimizers</a> into PV modules called <a href="/wiki/Smart_module" title="Smart module">smart modules</a>. These modules perform <a href="/wiki/Maximum_power_point_tracking" title="Maximum power point tracking">maximum power point tracking</a> (MPPT) for each module individually, measure performance data for monitoring, and provide additional safety features. Such modules can also compensate for shading effects, wherein a shadow falling across a section of a module causes the electrical output of one or more strings of cells in the module to decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the major causes for the decreased performance of cells is overheating. The efficiency of a solar cell declines by about 0.5% for every 1 degree Celsius increase in temperature. This means that a 100 degree increase in surface temperature could decrease the efficiency of a solar cell by about half. Self-cooling solar cells are one solution to this problem. Rather than using energy to cool the surface, pyramid and cone shapes can be formed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>, and attached to the surface of a solar panel. Doing so allows visible light to reach the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>, but reflects <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> rays (which carry heat).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Solar photovoltaics is growing rapidly and worldwide installed capacity reached about 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">gigawatts</a> (GW) by the end of 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA-PVPS-2016_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEA-PVPS-2016-9">[9]</a></sup> Since 2000, installed capacity has seen a growth factor of about 57.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haegel_2017_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haegel_2017-36">[36]</a></sup> The total power output of the world’s PV capacity in a calendar year in 2014 is now beyond 200 TWh of electricity. This represents 1% of worldwide electricity demand. More than 100 <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_by_country" title="Solar power by country">countries</a> use solar PV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ren21-2011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-ren21-2011-37">[37]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Solar power in the People's Republic of China">China</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Japan" title="Solar power in Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_United_States" title="Solar power in the United States">United States</a> is now the fastest growing market, while <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Germany" title="Solar power in Germany">Germany</a> remains the world's largest producer, contributing more than 7% to its national electricity demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-iea-pvps-snapshot-1992-2014-11">[11]</a></sup> Photovoltaics is now, after hydro and wind power, the third most important renewable energy source in terms of globally installed capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epia-2012_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-epia-2012-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several market research and financial companies foresee record-breaking global installation of more than 50 GW in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHS-2015-estimate_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-IHS-2015-estimate-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deutsche-2015-forecast-feb15_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-deutsche-2015-forecast-feb15-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercom-mar-2015_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-mercom-mar-2015-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> China is predicted to take the lead from Germany and to become the world's largest producer of PV power by installing another targeted 17.8 GW in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CleanTechnica_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-CleanTechnica-43">[43]</a></sup> India is expected to install 1.8 GW, doubling its annual installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mercom-mar-2015_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-mercom-mar-2015-41">[41]</a></sup> By 2018, worldwide photovoltaic capacity is projected to doubled or even triple to 430 GW. Solar Power Europe (formerly known as EPIA) also estimates that photovoltaics will meet 10% to 15% of Europe's energy demand in 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epia-2013-market-report_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-epia-2013-market-report-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epia-2013_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-epia-2013-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017 a study in <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a> estimated that by 2030 global PV installed capacities will be between 3,000 and 10,000 GW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haegel_2017_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haegel_2017-36">[36]</a></sup> The EPIA/<a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> Solar Generation Paradigm Shift Scenario (formerly called Advanced Scenario) from 2010 shows that by the year 2030, 1,845 GW of PV systems could be generating approximately 2,646 TWh/year of electricity around the world. Combined with <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy use efficiency</a> improvements, this would represent the electricity needs of more than 9% of the world's population. By 2050, over 20% of all electricity could be provided by photovoltaics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epia_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-epia-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Liebreich" title="Michael Liebreich">Michael Liebreich</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P.#New_Energy_Finance" title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg New Energy Finance</a>, anticipates a tipping point for solar energy. The costs of power from wind and solar are already below those of conventional electricity generation in some parts of the world, as they have fallen sharply and will continue to do so. He also asserts, that the electrical grid has been greatly expanded worldwide, and is ready to receive and distribute electricity from renewable sources. In addition, worldwide electricity prices came under strong pressure from renewable energy sources, that are, in part, enthusiastically embraced by consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg-leibreich-2014_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-leibreich-2014-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a> sees a "second gold rush" for the photovoltaic industry to come. <a href="/wiki/Grid_parity" title="Grid parity">Grid parity</a> has already been reached in at least 19 markets by January 2014. Photovoltaics will prevail beyond <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariffs</a>, becoming more competitive as deployment increases and prices continue to fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2014 <a href="/wiki/Barclays" title="Barclays">Barclays</a> downgraded bonds of U.S. utility companies. Barclays expects more competition by a growing self-consumption due to a combination of decentralized PV-systems and residential <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">electricity storage</a>. This could fundamentally change the utility's business model and transform the system over the next ten years, as prices for these systems are predicted to fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts_of_photovoltaic_technologies">Environmental impacts of photovoltaic technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of photovoltaic technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><b>Types of impacts</b></i>
</p><p>While solar photovoltaic (PV) cells are promising for clean energy production, their deployment is hindered by production costs, material availability, and toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Data required to investigate their impact are sometimes affected by a rather large amount of uncertainty. The values of human labor and water consumption, for example, are not precisely assessed due to the lack of systematic and accurate analyses in the scientific literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayumi2017_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mayumi2017-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life cycle assessment</a> (LCA) is one method of determining environmental impacts from PV. Many studies have been done on the various types of PV including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_generation_solar_cell" title="First generation solar cell">first generation</a>, second generation, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Generation_Solar_Cell" title="Third Generation Solar Cell">third generation</a>. Usually these PV LCA studies select a cradle to gate system boundary because often at the time the studies are conducted, it is a new technology not commercially available yet and their required balance of system components and disposal methods are unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>A traditional LCA can look at many different impact categories ranging from <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a>, eco-toxicity, human toxicity, water depletion, and many others.
</p><p>Most LCAs of PV have focused on two categories: carbon dioxide equivalents per kWh and energy pay-back time (EPBT). The  EPBT is defined as " the time needed to compensate for the total renewable- and non-renewable- primary energy required during the life cycle of a PV system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-53">[53]</a></sup> A 2015 review of EPBT from first and second generation PV<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-54">[54]</a></sup> suggested that there was greater variation in embedded energy than in efficiency of the cells implying that it was mainly the embedded energy that needs to reduce to have a greater reduction in EPBT. One difficulty in determining impacts due to PV is to determine if the wastes are released to the air, water, or soil during the manufacturing phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-55">[55]</a></sup> Research is underway to try to understand emissions and releases during the lifetime of PV systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><b>Impacts from first-generation PV</b></i>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">Crystalline silicon</a> modules are the most extensively studied PV type in terms of LCA since they are the most commonly used. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mono-crystalline_silicon_cell" title="Mono-crystalline silicon cell">Mono-crystalline silicon</a> photovoltaic systems (mono-si) have an average efficiency of 14.0%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-56">[56]</a></sup> The cells tend to follow a structure of front electrode, anti-reflection film, n-layer, p-layer, and back electrode, with the sun hitting the front electrode. EPBT ranges from 1.7 to 2.7 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-57">[57]</a></sup> The cradle to gate of CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh ranges from 37.3 to 72.2 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Techniques to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-crystalline_silicon" title="Multi-crystalline silicon">multi-crystalline silicon</a> (multi-si) photovoltaic cells are simpler and cheaper than mono-si, however tend to make less efficient cells, an average of 13.2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-56">[56]</a></sup> EPBT ranges from 1.5 to 2.6 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-57">[57]</a></sup> The cradle to gate of CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh ranges from 28.5 to 69 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-58">[58]</a></sup> Some studies have looked beyond EPBT and GWP to other environmental impacts. In one such study, conventional energy mix in Greece was compared to multi-si PV and found a 95% overall reduction in impacts including carcinogens, eco-toxicity, acidification, eutrophication, and eleven others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><b>Impacts from second generation</b></i>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride_photovoltaics" title="Cadmium telluride photovoltaics">Cadmium telluride</a> (CdTe) is one of the fastest-growing <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_solar_cell" title="Thin-film solar cell">thin film based solar cells</a> which are collectively known as second generation devices. This new thin film device also shares similar performance restrictions (<a href="/wiki/Shockley%E2%80%93Queisser_limit" title="Shockley–Queisser limit">Shockley-Queisser efficiency limit</a>) as conventional Si devices but promises to lower the cost of each device by both reducing material and energy consumption during manufacturing. Today the global market share of CdTe is 5.4%, up from 4.7% in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-:4-55">[55]</a></sup> This technology’s highest power conversion efficiency is 21%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The cell structure includes glass substrate (around 2 mm), transparent conductor layer, CdS buffer layer (50–150 nm), CdTe absorber and a metal contact layer.
</p><p>CdTe PV systems require less energy input in their production than other commercial PV systems per unit electricity production. The average CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh is around 18 grams (cradle to gate). CdTe has the fastest EPBT of all commercial PV technologies, which varies between 0.3 and 1.2 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Copper_indium_gallium_selenide" title="Copper indium gallium selenide">Copper Indium Gallium Diselenide</a> (CIGS) is a thin film solar cell based on the copper indium diselenide (CIS) family of chalcopyrite <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>. CIS and CIGS are often used interchangeably within the CIS/CIGS community. The cell structure includes soda lime glass as the substrate, Mo layer as the back contact, CIS/CIGS as the absorber layer, cadmium sulfide (CdS) or Zn (S,OH)x  as the buffer layer, and ZnO:Al as the front contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> CIGS is approximately 1/100th the thickness of conventional silicon solar cell technologies. Materials necessary for assembly are readily available, and are less costly per watt of solar cell. CIGS based solar devices resist performance degradation over time and are highly stable in the field.
</p><p>Reported global warming potential impacts of CIGS range from 20.5 – 58.8 grams CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh of electricity generated for different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_irradiation" title="Solar irradiation">solar irradiation</a> (1,700 to 2,200 kWh/m<sup>2</sup>/y) and power conversion efficiency (7.8 – 9.12%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> EPBT ranges from 0.2 to 1.4 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-:8-61">[61]</a></sup> while harmonized value of EPBT was found 1.393 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-54">[54]</a></sup> Toxicity is an issue within the buffer layer of CIGS modules because it contains cadmium and gallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> CIS modules do not contain any heavy metals.
</p><p><i><b>Impacts from third generation</b></i>
</p><p>Third-generation PVs are designed to combine the advantages of both the first and second generation devices and they do not have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shockley-Queisser_limit" title="Shockley-Queisser limit">Shockley-Queisser limit</a>, a theoretical limit for first and second generation PV cells. The thickness of a third generation device is less than 1 µm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>One emerging alternative and promising technology is based on an organic-inorganic hybrid solar cell made of methylammonium lead halide perovskites. <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_solar_cell" title="Perovskite solar cell">Perovskite PV cells</a> have progressed rapidly over the past few years and have become one of the most attractive areas for PV research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celik_et_al_2016_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Celik_et_al_2016-66">[66]</a></sup> The cell structure includes a metal back contact (which can be made of Al, Au or Ag), a hole transfer layer (spiro-MeOTAD, P3HT, PTAA, CuSCN, CuI, or NiO), and absorber layer (CH<sub>3</sub>NH<sub>3</sub>PbIxBr<sub>3</sub>-x, CH<sub>3</sub>NH<sub>3</sub>PbIxCl<sub>3</sub>-x or CH<sub>3</sub>NH<sub>3</sub>PbI<sub>3</sub>), an electron transport layer (TiO, ZnO, Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> or SnO<sub>2</sub>) and a top contact layer (fluorine doped tin oxide or tin doped indium oxide).
</p><p>There are a limited number of published studies to address the environmental impacts of perovskite solar cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celik_et_al_2016_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Celik_et_al_2016-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-:10-68">[68]</a></sup> The major environmental concern is the lead used in the absorber layer. Due to the instability of perovskite cells lead may eventually be exposed to fresh water during the use phase. These LCA  studies looked at human and ecotoxicity of perovskite solar cells and found they were  surprisingly low and may not be an environmental issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-:9-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-:10-68">[68]</a></sup>  Global warming potential of perovskite PVs were found to be in the range of 24–1500 grams CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh electricity production. Similarly, reported EPBT of the published paper range from 0.2 to 15 years. The large range of reported values highlight the uncertainties associated with these studies. Celik et al. (2016) critically discussed the assumptions made in perovskite PV LCA studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celik_et_al_2016_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-Celik_et_al_2016-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two new promising thin film technologies are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_zinc_tin_sulfide" title="Copper zinc tin sulfide">copper zinc tin sulfide</a> (Cu<sub>2</sub>ZnSnS<sub>4</sub> or CZTS),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zinc_phosphide" title="Zinc phosphide">zinc phosphide</a> (Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup> and single-walled carbon nano-tubes (SWCNT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celik,_I._2017_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Celik,_I._2017-69">[69]</a></sup> These thin films are currently only produced in the lab but may be commercialized in the future. The manufacturing of CZTS and (Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub>) processes are expected to be similar to those of current thin film technologies of CIGS and CdTe, respectively. While the absorber layer of SWCNT PV is expected to be synthesized with CoMoCAT method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> by  Contrary to established thin films such as CIGS and CdTe, CZTS,  Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub>, and SWCNT PVs are made from earth abundant, nontoxic materials and have the potential to produce more electricity annually than the current worldwide consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> While CZTS and Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub> offer good promise for these reasons, the specific environmental implications of their commercial production are not yet known. Global warming potential of CZTS and Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub> were found 38 and 30 grams CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh while their corresponding EPBT were found 1.85 and 0.78 years, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup> Overall, CdTe and Zn<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub> have similar environmental impacts but can slightly outperform CIGS and CZTS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_52-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-52">[52]</a></sup> Celik et al. performed the first LCA study on environmental impacts of SWCNT PVs, including a laboratory-made 1% efficient device and an aspirational 28% efficient four-cell tandem device and interpreted the results by using mono-Si as a reference point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celik,_I._2017_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Celik,_I._2017-69">[69]</a></sup> the results show that compared to monocrystalline Si (mono-Si), the environmental impacts from 1% SWCNT was ∼18 times higher due mainly to the short lifetime of three years. However, even with the same short lifetime, the 28% cell had lower environmental impacts than mono-Si.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Organic_solar_cell" title="Organic solar cell">Organic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymer_solar_cell" title="Polymer solar cell">polymer photovoltaic</a> (OPV) are a relatively new area of research. The tradition OPV cell structure layers consist of a semi-transparent electrode, electron blocking layer, tunnel junction, holes blocking layer, electrode, with the sun hitting the transparent electrode. OPV replaces silver with carbon as an electrode material lowering manufacturing cost and making them more environmentally friendly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> OPV are flexible, low weight, and work well with roll-to roll manufacturing for mass production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> OPV uses "only abundant elements coupled to an extremely low embodied energy through very low processing temperatures using only ambient processing conditions on simple printing equipment enabling energy pay-back times".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-75">[75]</a></sup> Current efficiencies range from 1–6.5%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> however theoretical analyses show promise beyond 10% efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many different configurations of OPV exist using different materials for each layer. OPV technology rivals existing PV technologies in terms of EPBT even if they currently present a shorter operational lifetime. A 2013 study analyzed 12 different configurations all with 2% efficiency, the EPBT ranged from 0.29–0.52 years for 1 m² of PV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The average CO<sub>2</sub>-eq/kWh for OPV is 54.922 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table style="float: right; margin: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td><div class="timeline-wrapper"><map name="timeline_7930fe3479de11db173727163d3e7105"></map><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/timeline/7930fe3479de11db173727163d3e7105.png" usemap="#timeline_7930fe3479de11db173727163d3e7105"/></div>
<p>Source: Apricus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apricus_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Apricus-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>There have been major changes in the underlying costs, industry structure and market prices of solar photovoltaics technology, over the years, and gaining a coherent picture of the shifts occurring across the industry value chain globally is a challenge. This is due to: "the rapidity of cost and price changes, the complexity of the PV supply chain, which involves a large number of manufacturing processes, the balance of system (BOS) and installation costs associated with complete PV systems, the choice of different distribution channels, and differences between regional markets within which PV is being deployed". Further complexities result from the many different policy support initiatives that have been put in place to facilitate photovoltaics commercialisation in various countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reconsidering_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-reconsidering-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The PV industry has seen dramatic drops in module prices since 2008. In late 2011, factory-gate prices for crystalline-silicon photovoltaic modules dropped below the $1.00/W mark. The $1.00/W installed cost, is often regarded in the PV industry as marking the achievement of <a href="/wiki/Grid_parity" title="Grid parity">grid parity</a> for PV. Technological advancements, manufacturing process improvements, and industry re-structuring, mean that further price reductions are likely in coming years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reconsidering_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-reconsidering-5">[5]</a></sup> As of 2017 power-purchase agreement prices for solar farms below $0.05/kWh are common in the United States and the lowest bids in several international countries were about $0.03/kWh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haegel_2017_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haegel_2017-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Financial_incentives_for_photovoltaics" title="Financial incentives for photovoltaics">Financial incentives for photovoltaics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariffs</a>, have often been offered to electricity consumers to install and operate solar-electric generating systems. Government has sometimes also offered incentives in order to encourage the PV industry to achieve the <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a> needed to compete where the cost of PV-generated electricity is above the cost from the existing grid. Such policies are implemented to promote national or territorial <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy independence</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> job creation and reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" title="Carbon emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a> which cause climate change. Due to economies of scale solar panels get less costly as people use and buy more—as manufacturers increase production to meet demand, the cost and price is expected to drop in the years to come.
</p><p>Solar cell efficiencies vary from 6% for amorphous silicon-based solar cells to 44.0% with multiple-junction <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentrated_photovoltaics" title="Concentrated photovoltaics">concentrated photovoltaics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Solar cell energy conversion efficiencies for commercially available photovoltaics are around 14–22%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Concentrated photovoltaics (CPV) may reduce cost by concentrating up to 1,000 suns (through magnifying lens) onto a smaller sized photovoltaic cell. However, such concentrated solar power requires sophisticated heat sink designs, otherwise the photovoltaic cell overheats, which reduces its efficiency and life. To further exacerbate the concentrated cooling design, the heat sink must be passive, otherwise the power required for active cooling would reduce the overall efficiency and economy.
</p><p>Crystalline silicon solar cell prices have fallen from $76.67/Watt in 1977 to an estimated $0.74/Watt in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1-83">[83]</a></sup> This is seen as evidence supporting <a href="/wiki/Swanson%27s_law" title="Swanson's law">Swanson's law</a>, an observation similar to the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moore%27s_Law" title="Moore's Law">Moore's Law</a> that states that solar cell prices fall 20% for every doubling of industry capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2011, the price of PV modules has fallen by 60% since the summer of 2008, according to Bloomberg New Energy Finance estimates, putting solar power for the first time on a competitive footing with the retail price of electricity in a number of sunny countries; an alternative and consistent price decline figure of 75% from 2007 to 2012 has also been published,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wells2012_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-wells2012-84">[84]</a></sup> though it is unclear whether these figures are specific to the United States or generally global. The levelised cost of electricity (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCOE" title="LCOE">LCOE</a>) from PV is competitive with conventional electricity sources in an expanding list of geographic regions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Branker_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Branker-85">[85]</a></sup> particularly when the time of generation is included, as electricity is worth more during the day than at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> There has been fierce competition in the supply chain, and further improvements in the levelised cost of energy for solar lie ahead, posing a growing threat to the dominance of fossil fuel generation sources in the next few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-invest2011_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-invest2011-87">[87]</a></sup> As time progresses, renewable energy technologies generally get cheaper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> while fossil fuels generally get more expensive:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The less solar power costs, the more favorably it compares to conventional power, and the more attractive it becomes to utilities and energy users around the globe. Utility-scale solar power can now be delivered in California at prices well below $100/MWh ($0.10/kWh) less than most other peak generators, even those running on low-cost natural gas. Lower solar module costs also stimulate demand from consumer markets where the cost of solar compares very favorably to retail electric rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="649" data-file-width="729" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg/300px-Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg/450px-Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg/600px-Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Price_history_of_silicon_PV_cells_since_1977.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_per_watt" title="Price per watt">Price per watt</a> history for conventional (<a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">c-Si</a>) solar cells since 1977.</div></div></div>
<p>As of 2011, the cost of PV has fallen well below that of nuclear power and is set to fall further. The average retail price of solar cells as monitored by the Solarbuzz group fell from $3.50/watt to $2.43/watt over the course of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natint_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-natint-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>For large-scale installations, prices below $1.00/watt were achieved. A module price of 0.60 Euro/watt ($0.78/watt) was published for a large scale 5-year deal in April 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>By the end of 2012, the "best in class" module price had dropped to $0.50/watt, and was expected to drop to $0.36/watt by 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many locations, PV has reached grid parity, which is usually defined as PV production costs at or below retail electricity prices (though often still above the power station prices for coal or gas-fired generation without their distribution and other costs). However, in many countries there is still a need for more access to capital to develop PV projects. To solve this problem <a href="/wiki/Securitization" title="Securitization">securitization</a> has been proposed and used to accelerate development of solar photovoltaic projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/SolarCity" title="SolarCity">SolarCity</a> offered, the first U.S. <a href="/wiki/Asset-backed_security" title="Asset-backed security">asset-backed security</a> in the solar industry in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p>Photovoltaic power is also generated during a time of day that is close to peak demand (precedes it) in electricity systems with high use of air conditioning. More generally, it is now evident that, given a carbon price of $50/ton, which would raise the price of coal-fired power by 5c/kWh, solar PV will be cost-competitive in most locations. The declining price of PV has been reflected in rapidly growing installations, totaling about 23 GW in 2011. Although some consolidation is likely in 2012, due to support cuts in the large markets of Germany and Italy, strong growth seems likely to continue for the rest of the decade. Already, by one estimate, total investment in renewables for 2011 exceeded investment in carbon-based electricity generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natint_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-natint-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of self consumption payback time is calculated based on how much electricity is not brought from the grid. Additionally, using PV solar power to charge DC batteries, as used in Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles and Electric Vehicles, leads to greater efficiencies. Traditionally, DC generated electricity from solar PV must be converted to AC for buildings, at an average 10% loss during the conversion. An additional efficiency loss occurs in the transition back to DC for battery driven devices and vehicles, and using various interest rates and energy price changes were calculated to find present values that range from $2,057 to $8,213 (analysis from 2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, in Germany with electricity prices of 0.25 euro/kWh and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">Insolation</a> of 900 kWh/kW one kW<sub>p</sub> will save 225 euro per year and with installation cost of 1700 euro/kW<sub>p</sub> means that the system will pay back in less than 7 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_photovoltaics_companies" title="List of photovoltaics companies">List of photovoltaics companies</a></div>
<p>Overall the
manufacturing process of creating solar photovoltaics is simple in that it does
not require the culmination of many complex or moving parts. Because of the
solid state nature of PV systems they often have relatively long lifetimes,
anywhere from 10 to 30 years. To increase electrical output of a PV
system, the manufacturer must simply add more photovoltaic components and
because of this economies of scale are important for manufacturers as costs
decrease with increasing output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>While there are many types of PV systems known to be effective, crystalline silicon PV accounted for around 90% of the worldwide production of PV in 2013. Manufacturing silicon PV systems has several steps. First, polysilicon is processed from mined quartz until it is very pure (semi-conductor grade). This is melted down when small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, a group III element, are added to make a p-type semiconductor rich in electron holes. Typically using a seed crystal, an ingot of this solution is grown from the liquid polycrystalline. The ingot may also be cast in a mold. Wafers of this semiconductor material are cut from the bulk material with wire saws, and then go through surface etching before being cleaned. Next, the wafers are placed into a phosphorus vapor deposition furnace which lays a very thin layer of phosphorus, a group V element, which creates an n-type semiconducting surface. To reduce energy losses, an anti-reflective coating is added to the surface, along with electrical contacts. After finishing the cell, cells are connected via electrical circuit according to the specific application and prepared for shipping and installation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crystalline silicon photovoltaics are only one type of PV, and while they represent the majority of solar cells produced currently there are many new and promising technologies that have the potential to be scaled up to meet future energy needs. As of 2018, crystalline silicon cell technology serves as the basis for several PV module types, including monocrystalline, multicrystalline, mono PERC, and bifacial. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another newer technology, thin-film PV, are manufactured by depositing semiconducting layers on substrate in vacuum. The substrate is often glass or stainless-steel, and these semiconducting layers are made of many types of materials including cadmium telluride (CdTe), copper indium diselenide (CIS), copper indium gallium diselenide (CIGS), and amorphous silicon (a-Si). After being deposited onto the substrate the semiconducting layers are separated and connected by electrical circuit by laser-scribing. Thin-film photovoltaics now make up around 20% of the overall production of PV because of the reduced materials requirements and cost to manufacture modules consisting of thin-films as compared to silicon-based wafers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other emerging PV technologies include organic, dye-sensitized, quantum-dot, and Perovskite photovoltaics. OPVs fall into the thin-film category of manufacturing, and typically operate around the 12% efficiency range which is lower than the 12–21% typically seen by silicon based PVs. Because organic photovoltaics require very high purity and are relatively reactive they must be encapsulated which vastly increases cost of manufacturing and meaning that they are not feasible for large scale up. Dye-sensitized PVs are similar in efficiency to OPVs but are significantly easier to manufacture. However these dye-sensitized photovoltaics present storage problems because the liquid electrolyte is toxic and can potentially permeate the plastics used in the cell. Quantum dot solar cells are quantum dot sensitized DSSCs and are solution processed meaning they are potentially scalable, but currently they peak at 12% efficiency. Perovskite solar cells are a very efficient solar energy converter and have excellent optoelectric properties for photovoltaic purposes, but they are expensive and difficult to manufacture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photovoltaic_systems">Photovoltaic systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Photovoltaic systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">Photovoltaic system</a></div>
<p>A photovoltaic system, or solar PV system is a power system designed to supply usable solar power by means of photovoltaics. It consists of an arrangement of several components, including solar panels to absorb and directly convert sunlight into electricity, a solar inverter to change the electric current from DC to AC, as well as mounting, cabling and other electrical accessories. PV systems range from small, <a href="/wiki/Rooftop_photovoltaic_power_station" title="Rooftop photovoltaic power station">roof-top mounted</a> or <a href="/wiki/Building-integrated_photovoltaics" title="Building-integrated photovoltaics">building-integrated</a> systems with capacities from a few to several tens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatts" title="Kilowatts">kilowatts</a>, to large utility-scale <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_station" title="Photovoltaic power station">power stations</a> of hundreds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatts" title="Megawatts">megawatts</a>. Nowadays, most PV systems are <a href="/wiki/Grid-connected_photovoltaic_power_system" title="Grid-connected photovoltaic power system">grid-connected</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Stand-alone_power_system" title="Stand-alone power system">stand-alone</a> systems only account for a small portion of the market.
</p>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Rooftop and building integrated systems</dt></dl></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="1097" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg/220px-SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg/330px-SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg/440px-SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SolarFachwerkhaus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rooftop PV on half-timbered house</div></div></div>
<dl><dd>Photovoltaic arrays are often associated with buildings: either integrated into them, mounted on them or mounted nearby on the ground. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rooftop_PV_system" title="Rooftop PV system">Rooftop PV systems</a> are most often retrofitted into existing buildings, usually mounted on top of the existing roof structure or on the existing walls. Alternatively, an array can be located separately from the building but connected by cable to supply power for the building. <a href="/wiki/Building-integrated_photovoltaics" title="Building-integrated photovoltaics">Building-integrated photovoltaics</a> (BIPV) are increasingly incorporated into the roof or walls of new domestic and industrial buildings as a principal or ancillary source of electrical power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Roof tiles with integrated PV cells are sometimes used as well. Provided there is an open gap in which air can circulate, rooftop mounted solar panels can provide a passive cooling effect on buildings during the day and also keep accumulated heat in at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Typically, residential rooftop systems have small capacities of around 5–10 kW, while commercial rooftop systems often amount to several hundreds of kilowatts. Although rooftop systems are much smaller than ground-mounted utility-scale power plants, they account for most of the worldwide installed capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epia-2014_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-epia-2014-106">[106]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Concentrator photovoltaics</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Concentrator_photovoltaics" title="Concentrator photovoltaics">Concentrator photovoltaics</a> (CPV) is a photovoltaic technology that contrary to conventional flat-plate PV systems uses lenses and curved mirrors to focus sunlight onto small, but highly efficient, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-junction" title="Multi-junction">multi-junction</a> (MJ) solar cells. In addition, CPV systems often use <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">solar trackers</a> and sometimes a cooling system to further increase their efficiency. Ongoing research and development is rapidly improving their competitiveness in the utility-scale segment and in areas of high solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Photovoltaic thermal hybrid solar collector</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_thermal_hybrid_solar_collector" title="Photovoltaic thermal hybrid solar collector">Photovoltaic thermal hybrid solar collector</a> (PVT) are systems that convert solar radiation into <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">thermal</a> and electrical energy. These systems combine a solar PV cell, which converts sunlight into electricity, with a <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar thermal collector</a>, which captures the remaining energy and removes waste heat from the PV module. The capture of both electricity and heat allow these devices to have higher <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">exergy</a> and thus be more overall energy efficient than solar PV or solar thermal alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Power stations</dt></dl></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Topaz_Solar_Farm,_California_Valley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg/220px-Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg/330px-Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg/440px-Topaz_Solar_Farm%2C_California_Valley.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Topaz_Solar_Farm,_California_Valley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of the <a href="/wiki/Topaz_Solar_Farm" title="Topaz Solar Farm">Topaz Solar Farm</a></div></div></div>
<dl><dd>Many utility-scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_farm" title="Solar farm">solar farms</a> have been constructed all over the world. As of 2015, the 579-megawatt (MW<sub>AC</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Solar_Star" title="Solar Star">Solar Star</a> is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_power_station" title="Photovoltaic power station">photovoltaic power station</a>, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Sunlight_Solar_Farm" title="Desert Sunlight Solar Farm">Desert Sunlight Solar Farm</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Topaz_Solar_Farm" title="Topaz Solar Farm">Topaz Solar Farm</a>, both with a capacity of 550 MW<sub>AC</sub>, constructed by US-company <a href="/wiki/First_Solar" title="First Solar">First Solar</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride_photovoltaics" title="Cadmium telluride photovoltaics">CdTe modules</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_solar_cell" title="Thin film solar cell">thin-film</a> PV technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rew11_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-rew11-109">[109]</a></sup> All three power stations are located in the Californian desert. Many solar farms around the world are integrated with agriculture and some use innovative solar tracking systems that follow the sun's daily path across the sky to generate more electricity than conventional fixed-mounted systems. There are no fuel costs or emissions during operation of the power stations.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Rural electrification</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">Developing countries</a> where many villages are often more than five kilometers away from grid power are increasingly using photovoltaics. In remote locations in India a rural lighting program has been providing solar powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> lighting to replace kerosene lamps. The solar powered lamps were sold at about the cost of a few months' supply of kerosene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Cuba is working to provide solar power for areas that are off grid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> More complex applications of off-grid solar energy use include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3D_printers" title="3D printers">3D printers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RepRap" title="RepRap">RepRap</a> 3D printers have been solar powered with photovoltaic technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> which enables <a href="/wiki/Distributed_manufacturing" title="Distributed manufacturing">distributed manufacturing</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>. These are areas where the social costs and benefits offer an excellent case for going solar, though the lack of profitability has relegated such endeavors to humanitarian efforts. However, in 1995 solar <a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">rural electrification</a> projects had been found to be difficult to sustain due to unfavorable economics, lack of technical support, and a legacy of ulterior motives of north-to-south technology transfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Standalone systems</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Until a decade or so ago, PV was used frequently to power calculators and novelty devices. Improvements in integrated circuits and low power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">liquid crystal displays</a> make it possible to power such devices for several years between battery changes, making PV use less common. In contrast, solar powered remote fixed devices have seen increasing use recently in locations where significant connection cost makes grid power prohibitively expensive. Such applications include <a href="/wiki/Solar_lamp" title="Solar lamp">solar lamps</a>, water pumps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parking_meter" title="Parking meter">parking meters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emergency_telephone" title="Emergency telephone">emergency telephones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trash_compactor" title="Trash compactor">trash compactors</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> temporary traffic signs, charging stations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> and remote guard posts and signals.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Floatovoltaics</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd>In May 2008, the Far Niente Winery in Oakville, CA pioneered the world's first "floatovoltaic" system by installing 994 photovoltaic solar panels onto 130 pontoons and floating them on the winery's irrigation pond. The floating system generates about 477 kW of peak output and when combined with an array of cells located adjacent to the pond is able to fully offset the winery's electricity consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The primary benefit of a floatovoltaic system is that it avoids the need to sacrifice valuable land area that could be used for another purpose. In the case of the Far Niente Winery, the floating system saved three-quarters of an acre that would have been required for a land-based system. That land area can instead be used for agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> Another benefit of a floatovoltaic system is that the panels are kept at a lower temperature than they would be on land, leading to a higher efficiency of solar energy conversion. The floating panels also reduce the amount of water lost through evaporation and inhibit the growth of algae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>In transport</dt></dl></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="956" data-file-width="1474" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg/220px-Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg/330px-Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg/440px-Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Impulse_SI2_pilote_Bertrand_Piccard_Payerne_November_2014.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Solar_Impulse" title="Solar Impulse">Solar Impulse</a> 2, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_aircraft" title="Solar aircraft">solar aircraft</a></div></div></div>
<dl><dd>PV has traditionally been used for electric power in space. PV is rarely used to provide motive power in transport applications, but is being used increasingly to provide auxiliary power in boats and cars. Some automobiles are fitted with solar-powered air conditioning to limit interior temperatures on hot days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> A self-contained <a href="/wiki/Solar_vehicle" title="Solar vehicle">solar vehicle</a> would have limited power and utility, but a solar-charged <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> allows use of solar power for transportation. Solar-powered cars, boats<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> and airplanes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> have been demonstrated, with the most practical and likely of these being <a href="/wiki/Solar_car" title="Solar car">solar cars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> The Swiss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_aircraft" title="Solar aircraft">solar aircraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Impulse_2" title="Solar Impulse 2">Solar Impulse 2</a>, achieved the <a href="/wiki/Flight_endurance_record" title="Flight endurance record">longest non-stop solo flight in history</a> and completed the first solar-powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_circumnavigation" title="Aerial circumnavigation">aerial circumnavigation</a> of the globe in 2016.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Telecommunication and signaling</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Solar PV power is ideally suited for telecommunication applications such as local telephone exchange, radio and TV broadcasting, microwave and other forms of electronic communication links. This is because, in most telecommunication application, storage batteries are already in use and the electrical system is basically DC. In hilly and mountainous terrain, radio and TV signals may not reach as they get blocked or reflected back due to undulating terrain. At these locations, <a href="/wiki/Low-power_broadcasting" title="Low-power broadcasting">low power transmitters (LPT)</a> are installed to receive and retransmit the signal for local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Spacecraft applications</dt></dl></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg/220px-Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg/330px-Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg/440px-Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Illumination_test_on_one_of_Juno%27s_solar_panels.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Part of <a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">Juno</a>'s solar array</div></div></div>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">Solar panels on spacecraft</a> are usually the sole source of power to run the sensors, active heating and cooling, and communications.  A battery stores this energy for use when the solar panels are in shadow.  In some, the power is also used for <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft propulsion</a>—<a href="/wiki/Electrically_powered_spacecraft_propulsion" title="Electrically powered spacecraft propulsion">electric propulsion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA11_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA11-130">[130]</a></sup>  Spacecraft were one of the earliest applications of photovoltaics, starting with the silicon solar cells used on the <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_1" title="Vanguard 1">Vanguard 1</a> satellite, launched by the US in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> Since then, solar power has been used on missions ranging from the <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">MESSENGER</a> probe to Mercury, to as far out in the solar system as the <a href="/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft)" title="Juno (spacecraft)">Juno</a> probe to Jupiter. The largest solar power system flown in space is the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_system_of_the_International_Space_Station" title="Electrical system of the International Space Station">electrical system of the International Space Station</a>. To increase the power generated per kilogram, typical spacecraft solar panels use high-cost, high-efficiency, and close-packed rectangular <a href="/wiki/Multi-junction_solar_cell" title="Multi-junction solar cell">multi-junction solar cells</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> (GaAs) and other semiconductor materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA11_130-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA11-130">[130]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Specialty Power Systems</dt></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Photovoltaics may also be incorporated as energy conversion devices for objects at elevated temperatures and with preferable radiative emissivities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_Combustion" title="Heterogeneous Combustion">heterogeneous combustors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 122 <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(power)#petawatt_(1015_watts)" title="Orders of magnitude (power)">PW</a> of sunlight reaching the Earth's surface is plentiful—almost 10,000 times more than the 13 TW equivalent of average power consumed in 2005 by humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smil_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smil-133">[133]</a></sup> This abundance leads to the suggestion that it will not be long before solar energy will become the world's primary energy source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Additionally, solar electric generation has the highest power density (global mean of 170 W/m<sup>2</sup>) among renewable energies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smil_133-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smil-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solar power is pollution-free during use, which enables it to cut down on pollution when it is substituted for other energy sources. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> estimated that 52,000 people per year die prematurely in the U.S. from coal-fired power plant pollution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> and all but one of these deaths could be prevented from using PV to replace coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> Production end-wastes and emissions are manageable using existing pollution controls. End-of-use recycling technologies are under development<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> and policies are being produced that encourage recycling from producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>PV installations can operate for 100 years or even more<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> with little maintenance or intervention after their initial set-up, so after the initial <a href="/wiki/Capital_cost" title="Capital cost">capital cost</a> of building any solar power plant, <a href="/wiki/Operating_cost" title="Operating cost">operating costs</a> are extremely low compared to existing power technologies.
</p><p>Grid-connected solar electricity can be used locally thus reducing transmission/distribution losses (transmission losses in the US were approximately 7.2% in 1995).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p><p>Compared to fossil and nuclear energy sources, very little research money has been invested in the development of solar cells, so there is considerable room for improvement. Nevertheless, experimental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_efficiency_solar_cells" title="High efficiency solar cells">high efficiency solar cells</a> already have efficiencies of over 40% in case of concentrating photovoltaic cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> and efficiencies are rapidly rising while mass-production costs are rapidly falling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some states of the United States, much of the investment in a home-mounted system may be lost if the home-owner moves and the buyer puts less value on the system than the seller. The city of <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley</a> developed an innovative financing method to remove this limitation, by adding a tax assessment that is transferred with the home to pay for the solar panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> Now known as <a href="/wiki/PACE_financing" title="PACE financing">PACE</a>, Property Assessed Clean Energy, 30 U.S. states have duplicated this solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is evidence, at least in California, that the presence of a home-mounted solar system can actually increase the value of a home. According to a paper published in April 2011 by the Ernest Orlando Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory titled An Analysis of the Effects of Residential Photovoltaic Energy Systems on Home Sales Prices in California:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The research finds strong evidence that homes with PV systems in California have sold for a premium over comparable homes without PV systems. More specifically, estimates for average PV premiums range from approximately $3.9 to $6.4 per installed watt (DC) among a large number of different model specifications, with most models coalescing near $5.5/watt. That value corresponds to a premium of approximately $17,000 for a relatively new 3,100 watt PV system (the average size of PV systems in the study).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photovoltaics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Pollution and Energy in Production</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>PV has been a well-known method of generating clean, emission free electricity. PV systems are often made of PV modules and inverter (changing DC to AC). PV modules are mainly made of PV cells, which has no fundamental difference to the material for making computer chips. The process of producing PV cells (computer chips) is energy intensive and involves highly poisonous and environmental toxic chemicals. There are few PV manufacturing plants around the world producing PV modules with energy produced from PV. This measure greatly reduces the carbon footprint during the manufacturing process. Managing the chemicals used in the manufacturing process is subject to the factories' local laws and regulations.
</p>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Impact on Electricity Network</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>With the increasing levels of rooftop photovoltaic systems, the energy flow becomes 2-way. When there is more local generation than consumption, electricity is exported to the grid. However, electricity network traditionally is not designed to deal with the 2- way energy transfer. Therefore, some technical issues may occur. For example, in Queensland Australia, there have been more than 30% of households with rooftop PV by the end of 2017. The famous Californian 2020 duck curve appears very often for a lot of communities from 2015 onwards. An over-voltage issue may come out as the electricity flows from these PV households back to the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> There are solutions to manage the over voltage issue, such as regulating PV inverter power factor, new voltage and energy control equipment at electricity distributor level, re-conductor the electricity wires, demand side management, etc. There are often limitations and costs related to these solutions.
</p>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Implication onto Electricity Bill Management and Energy Investment</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>There is no silver bullet in electricity or energy demand and bill management, because customers (sites) have different specific situations, e.g. different comfort/convenience needs, different electricity tariffs, or different usage patterns. Electricity tariff may have a few elements, such as daily access and metering charge, energy charge (based on kWh, MWh) or peak demand charge (e.g. a price for the highest 30min energy consumption in a month). PV is a promising option for reducing energy charge when electricity price is reasonably high and continuously increasing, such as in Australia and Germany. However, for sites with peak demand charge in place, PV may be less attractive if peak demands mostly occur in the late afternoon to early evening, for example residential communities. Overall, energy investment is largely an economical decision and it is better to make investment decisions based on systematical evaluation of options in operational improvement, energy efficiency, onsite generation and energy storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup>
</p>
