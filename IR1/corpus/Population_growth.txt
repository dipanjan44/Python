<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Population growth</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a>, <b>population growth</b> is the increase in the number of individuals in a <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>.
</p><p>Global human population growth amounts to around 83 million annually,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or 1.1% per year. The <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">global population</a> has grown from 1 billion in 1800 to 7.616 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in 2018. It is expected to keep growing, and estimates have put the total population at 8.6 billion by mid-2030, 9.8 billion by mid-2050 and 11.2 billion by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Many nations with rapid population growth have low standards of living, whereas many nations with low rates of population growth have high standards of living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population began growing rapidly in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> early in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">industrial revolution</a> of the late 18th century. The reasons for the "Modern Rise of Population"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-6">[6]</a></sup> were particularly investigated by the British health scientist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_McKeown_(physician)" title="Thomas McKeown (physician)">Thomas McKeown</a> (1912-1988). In his publications, McKeown challenged four theories about the population growth: 
</p>
<ol><li>McKeown stated that the growth in Western population, particularly surging in the 19th century, was not so much caused by an increase in <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a>, but largely by a decline of <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality</a> particularly of childhood mortality followed by <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">infant mortality</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The decline of mortality could largely be attributed to rising standards of living, whereby McKeown put most emphasis on improved nutritional status,</li>
<li>His most controversial idea, at least his most disputed idea, was that he questioned the effectiveness of public health measures, including sanitary reforms, vaccination and quarantine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The sometime fierce disputes that his publication provoked around the "McKeown thesis", have overshadowed his more important and largely unchallenged argument that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curative_medicine" title="Curative medicine">curative medicine</a> measures played little role in mortality decline, not only prior to the mid-20th century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-7">[7]</a></sup> but also until well into the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">[10]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Although the McKeown thesis has been heavily disputed, recent studies have confirmed the value of his ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> His work is pivotal for present day thinking about population growth, birth control, public health and medical care. McKeown had a major influence on many population researchers, such as health economists and Nobel prize winners <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fogel" title="Robert Fogel">Robert W. Fogel</a> (1993) and <a href="/wiki/Angus_Deaton" title="Angus Deaton">Angus Deaton</a> (2015). The latter considered McKeown as 'the founder of <a href="/wiki/Social_medicine" title="Social medicine">social medicine</a>'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:14_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:14-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_growth_rate">Population growth rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population growth rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "population growth rate" is the rate at which the number of individuals in a population increases in a given time period, expressed as a fraction of the initial population. Specifically, population growth rate refers to the change in population over a unit time period, often expressed as a percentage of the number of individuals in the population at the beginning of that period. This can be written as the formula, valid for a sufficiently small time interval:

<p>A positive growth rate indicates that the population is increasing, while a negative growth rate indicates that the population is decreasing. A growth ratio of zero indicates that there were the same number of individuals at the beginning and end of the period—a growth rate may be zero even when there are significant changes in the <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_rate" title="Death rate">death rates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigration_rate" title="Immigration rate">immigration rates</a>, and age distribution between the two times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>A related measure is the <a href="/wiki/Net_reproduction_rate" title="Net reproduction rate">net reproduction rate</a>. In the absence of migration, a net reproduction rate of more than 1 indicates that the population of females is increasing, while a net reproduction rate less than one (<a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">sub-replacement fertility</a>) indicates that the population of females is decreasing.
</p><p>Most populations do not grow exponentially, rather they follow a <a href="/wiki/Logistic_function" title="Logistic function">logistic model</a>. Once the population has reached its <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>, it will stabilize and the exponential curve will level off towards the carrying capacity, which is usually when a population has depleted most its <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 2017, the estimated annual growth rate was 1.1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U.S._Census_Bureau,_January_2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Census_Bureau,_January_2010-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA_World_Factbook" title="CIA World Factbook">CIA World Factbook</a> gives the world annual birthrate, mortality rate, and growth rate as 1.86%, 0.78%, and 1.08% respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The last 100 years have seen a massive fourfold increase in the population, due to <a href="/wiki/History_of_medicine#Modern_medicine" title="History of medicine">medical advances</a>, lower mortality rates, and an increase in agricultural productivity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> made possible by the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>.
</p><p>The annual increase in the number of living humans peaked at 88.0 million in 1989, then slowly declined to 73.9 million in 2003, after which it rose again to 75.2 million in 2006. In 2017, the human population increased by 83 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U.S._Census_Bureau,_January_2010_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Census_Bureau,_January_2010-16">[16]</a></sup> Generally, developed nations have seen a decline in their growth rates in recent decades, though annual growth rates remain above 2% in poverty-stricken countries of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, and also in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some countries the <a href="/wiki/Population_decline" title="Population decline">population is declining</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, mainly due to low <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">fertility rates</a>, high death rates and <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, growth is slowing due to the high number of AIDS-related deaths. Some <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> countries might also experience population decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Japan's population began decreasing in 2005; it now has the highest standard of living in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Division" title="United Nations Population Division">United Nations Population Division</a> projects world population to reach 11.2 billion by the end of the 21st century, but <a href="/wiki/Sanjeev_Sanyal" title="Sanjeev Sanyal">Sanjeev Sanyal</a> has argued that global fertility will fall below the replacement rate in the 2020s and that world population will peak below 9 billion by 2050, followed by a long decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanjeev_Sanyal_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanjeev_Sanyal-22">[22]</a></sup> A 2014 study in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> concludes that the global population will reach 11 billion by 2100, with a 70% chance of continued growth into the 22nd century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Many of the world's countries, including many in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a>, have seen a sharp rise in population since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The fear is that high population numbers are putting further strain on natural resources, food supplies, fuel supplies, employment, housing, etc. in some of the less fortunate countries. For example, the population of <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> has ultimately grown from 6,279,921 in 1993 to 10,329,208 in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cia_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-cia-25">[25]</a></sup> further straining its resources. <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">DRC</a> are witnessing a similar growth in population.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_by_region">Growth by region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Growth by region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population growth rates vary by world region, with the highest growth rates in Sub-Saharan Africa and the lowest in Europe. For example, from 1950 to 2010, Sub-Saharan African grew over three and a half times, from about 186 million to 856 million. On the other hand, Europe only increased by 35%, from 547 million in 1950 to 738 million in 2010. As a result of these varying population growths, Sub-Saharan Africa changed from 7.4% of world population in 1950 to 12.4% in 2010, while Europe declined from 22% to 11% in the same time period.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Into_the_future">Into the future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Into the future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Projections_of_population_growth" title="Projections of population growth">Projections of population growth</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="547" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/250px-Population_curve.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/375px-Population_curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/500px-Population_curve.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Estimated size of human population from 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a> to 2000 CE.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="407" data-file-width="604" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg/250px-Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg/375px-Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg/500px-Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Growthbydevelopedvslessdeveloped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The majority of world population growth today is occurring in less developed countries.</div></div></div>
<p>According to the UN's 2017 revision to its population projections, world population is projected to reach 11.2 billion by 2100 compared to 7.6 billion in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> In 2011, Indian economist Sanjeev Sanyal disputed the UN's figures and argued that birth rates will fall below replacement rates in the 2020s. According to his projections, population growth will be only sustained till the 2040s by rising longevity, but will peak below 9 bn by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanjeev_Sanyal_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sanjeev_Sanyal-22">[22]</a></sup> Conversely, a 2014 paper by demographers from several universities and the United Nations Population Division projected that the world's population would reach about 10.9 billion in 2100 and continue growing thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science-86">[86]</a></sup> One of its authors, Adrian Raftery, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a> professor of statistics and of sociology, says "The consensus over the past 20 years or so was that world population, which is currently around 7 billion, would go up to 9 billion and level off or probably decline. We found there’s a 70 percent probability the world population will not stabilize this century. Population, which had sort of fallen off the world’s agenda, remains a very important issue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
