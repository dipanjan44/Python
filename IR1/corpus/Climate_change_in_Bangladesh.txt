<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Bangladesh</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a></b> in <b><a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a></b> is a pressing issue. According to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i>, Bangladesh is one the most vulnerable nations to the impacts of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun-2010_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braun-2010-1">[1]</a></sup>Bangladesh being located on the Tropic of Cancer receives fairly direct radiation throughout the year &amp; maintains relatively high temperature.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bangladesh lies at the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmaputra" title="Brahmaputra">Brahmaputra</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meghna" title="Meghna">Meghna</a> (GBM) river system. Bangladesh is watered by a total of 57 trans-boundary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivers" title="Rivers">rivers</a> flowing to it: 54 from neighbouring <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and three from <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>. The country, which has no control of <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_transport_model" title="Hydrological transport model">water flows</a> and volume, drains to the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>. Coupled with the high level of widespread <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and increasing <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>, limited adaptive <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">capacity</a>, and poorly funded, ineffective local governance have made the region one of the most adversely affected on the planet. There are an estimated one thousand people in each square kilometre, with the national population increasing by two million people each year. Almost half the population is in poverty (defined as <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a> of US$1.25 per person a day). The population lacks the resources to respond to <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Bangladesh" title="Government of Bangladesh">government</a> cannot help them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunny-2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sunny-2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup>
</p><p>In the 2017 edition of <a href="/wiki/Germanwatch" title="Germanwatch">Germanwatch</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_Risk" title="Climate Risk">Climate Risk</a> Index</i>, Bangladesh was judged to be the sixth hardest hit by climate calamities of 180 nations during the period 1996–2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is projected that, by 2020, from 500 to 750 million people will be affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_stress" title="Water stress">water stress</a> caused by climate change around the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming#Coastal_regions" title="Regional effects of global warming">Low-lying coastal regions</a>, such as Bangladesh, are vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> and the increased occurrence of intense, extreme weather conditions such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a> of 2007–2009, as well as the melting of polar ice. In most countries like Bangladesh, yields from rain-fed agriculture could be reduced to 50 percent by 2020.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For a country with increasing <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_hunger" title="World hunger">hunger</a>, this will have an adverse effect on <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>. Although effects of climate change are highly variable, by 2030, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> could lose 10 percent of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> yields, while neighboring states like <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> could experience a 50 percent reduction in crop yield.
</p><p>As a result of all this, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> would need to prepare for long-term adaptation, which could be as drastic as changing sowing dates due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasonal_variation" title="Seasonal variation">seasonal variations</a>, introducing different varieties and species, to practicing novel water supply and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunny-2011_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sunny-2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>230</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_security">Food security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Food security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">Food security</a></div>
<p>With a larger population facing losses in arable lands, climate change poses an acute risk to the already <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnourished population</a> of Bangladesh. Although the country has managed to increase its production of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> since the nation's birth — from 10 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_tons" title="Metric tons">metric tons</a> (MT) to over 30 MT — around 30 percent of the population is still malnourished. Now more than five million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectares" title="Hectares">hectares</a> of land are irrigated, almost fourfold that in 1990. Even though modern rice varieties have been introduced in three-fourths of the total rice irrigation area, the sudden shift in population increase is putting strains on the production. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Climate change threatens the agricultural economy</a>, which, although it counts for just 20 percent of GDP, contributes to over half the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_force" title="Labor force">labor force</a>. In 2007, after a series of floods and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Sidr" title="Cyclone Sidr">Cyclone Sidr</a>, food security was severely threatened. Given the country's infrastructure and <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">disaster response mechanisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop yields</a> worsened. The loss of rice production was estimated at around two million metric tons (MT), which could potentially feed 10 million people. This was the single most important catalyst of the 2008 price increases, which led to around 15 million people going without much food. This was further worsened by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Aila" title="Cyclone Aila">cyclone Allia</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_and_international_policies">National and international policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National and international policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Given the frequent climate change-based catastrophes, Bangladesh needs to enhance food security by drafting and implementing new policies such as the 2006 National Sausage Policy. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO) supported this policy through the "National Food Policy Capacity Strengthening Program" (NFPCSP). There is also an initiative for the start of a "Food Security Country Investment Plan" enabling the country to secure around US$52 million under the "Global Agriculture and Food Security Program" (GAFSP), making it <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>'s first recipient. More work and better implementation from the government is necessary for activities to reach fruitful outcomes. Already, 11 ministries and governmental agencies are involved in this integrated endeavour. In the aftermath of the "East Pakistan Coastal Embankment plan" (CEP) in the mid-20th century, Bangladesh has recently started work on the "Master Plan for the South". The southern coastal area is vulnerable to the ill-effects of global climate. Crops, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> of the southern delta are threatened. There are plans for a US$3 billion multi-purpose bridge named "Padma" to transform the agricultural sector in the region. The government estimates a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> increase of around two percent as a result of the project.
</p><p>In an effort to achieve <a href="/wiki/Developing_country#List_of_emerging_and_developing_economies" title="Developing country">middle income country</a> status by 2021, the government is focusing on increasing agriculture production, productivity, water management techniques, surface water infrastructure, irrigation, fisheries, and promoting poultry and dairy development. <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuels</a> fit into this scenario by providing energy for agriculture.  In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Bangladesh" title="Government of Bangladesh">Ministry of Agriculture</a> provided a 30 percent subsidy to diesel to power irrigation for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a>, further proposing a 7,750 million <a href="/wiki/Bangladeshi_taka" title="Bangladeshi taka">BDT</a> disbursement to help almost a million farmers with fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunny-2011_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sunny-2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>354</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_policies">Mitigation policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mitigation policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bangladesh loses land to rising sea levels, but gains land from sediment deposits. The effects of sea level rise and land accretion in Bangladesh are highly regional and variegated. Natural land accretion, paired with targeted policies to secure such land for farming use has the potential to partially mitigate the effects of land lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brammer-2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brammer-2014-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_developed_country" title="Least developed country">Least developed country</a> (LDC), Bangladesh is exempt from any responsibility to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_emissions" title="GHG emissions">GHG emissions</a>, which are the primary cause of global warming. But lately this has been the rallying factor for policy makers to give off higher amounts of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">emissions</a> in nearly all sectors with disregard for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. Large developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_nations" title="Industrial nations">industrial nations</a> are emitting increasing quantities of greenhouse gases (GHG). The country cannot go far in their struggle with reducing emissions and fighting global warming with the considerable scantily supported funding and help it receives from the international community. There exist plans such as the "National Action Plan on Adaptation" (NAPA) of 2005, and the "Bangladesh Climate Change Strategy and Action Plan" (BCCSAP) of 2009.
</p><p>BCCSAP states that an integrated approach is necessary and the only way to gain sustainability is where economic and social development is pursued to the exclusion of disaster management, as one major calamity will destroy any socio-economic gains. Around 40–45 percent of GHG emissions are required to be reduced by 2020 and 90–95 percent by 2050. This is using the 1990 GHG concentration levels as a benchmark. With higher population and rapid industrialization, Bangladesh should be on its way to developing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-carbon" title="Low-carbon">low-carbon</a> path given it initially receives significant financial and technical support from the international community and national goals of economic growth and social development is not hampered. But a more holistic short-term plan is also necessary. Bangladesh has established the <a class="external text" href="http://www.china.org.cn/environment/2009-11/16/content_18894737.htm" rel="nofollow">Bangladesh Climate Change Trust Fund</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.bcct.gov.bd" rel="nofollow">BCCTF</a>) and the <a class="external text" href="http://banglawire.com/blog/2011/10/23/bangladesh-climate-change-resilience-fund-bccrf/" rel="nofollow">Bangladesh Climate Change Resilience Fund</a> (BCCRF) allocating US$200 million and cumulating around further US$114 million respectively. Although 3000 cyclone shelters were constructed with over 40,000 trained volunteers and 10,000 km of embankments erected, Bangladesh should not only place emphasis on capacity building and disaster management but also institutional and infrastructure strengthening, development of research and low carbon technologies in order to create an inclusive and truly <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">comprehensive mitigation scheme</a>. Even though it is agreed that the willingness and cooperation of the current UNFCCC parties (194 member states as of 2011) is necessary to help the nation, funds like the Special Climate and LDC, Adaptation Fund should be easily made available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunny-2011_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sunny-2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>133</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_aid_and_funding">Foreign aid and funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Bangladesh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Foreign aid and funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various countries have pledged to provide funding for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">adaptation and mitigation</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nations" title="Developing nations">developing nations</a>, such as Bangladesh. The accord committed up to US$30 billion of immediate short term funding over the 2010-2012 period from developed to developing countries to support their action in climate change mitigation. This funding is available for developing nations to <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">build their capacity</a> to reduce <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">emissions</a> and responds to impacts of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Furthermore, this funding will be balanced between mitigation and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> adaptation in various sectors including forestry, science, technology and capacity building. Moreover, the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Accord" title="Copenhagen Accord">Copenhagen Accord</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP_15" title="COP 15">COP 15</a>) also pledges US$100 million of public and private <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a> by 2020, mostly to developing nations.
</p><p>Another misconception is that this accord will divert funding from <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> reduction. The private sector alone contributes more than 85 percent of current investments for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon_economy" title="Low carbon economy">low carbon economy</a>. In order to maximize any future contributions from this sector, the public sector needs to overcome the political and bureaucratic barriers the private sector has to face towards a low carbon future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunny-2011_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sunny-2011-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>72</span></sup>
</p>
