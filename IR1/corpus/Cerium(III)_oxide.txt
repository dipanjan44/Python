<p><b>Cerium(III) oxide</b>, also known as <b>cerium oxide</b>, <b>cerium trioxide</b>, <b>cerium sesquioxide</b>, <b>cerous oxide</b> or <b>dicerium trioxide</b>, is an oxide of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metal" title="Rare-earth metal">rare-earth metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>. It has <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> Ce<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and is gold-yellow in color.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine_and_exhaust_catalysts">Engine and exhaust catalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Engine and exhaust catalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cerium oxide is used as a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> for the minimisation of CO emissions in the exhaust gases from motor vehicles. 
</p><p>When there is a shortage of oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a> is reduced by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> to cerium(III) oxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">CeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + CO → <span class="chemf nowrap">Ce<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>When there is an oxygen surplus, the process is reversed and cerium(III) oxide is oxidized to cerium(IV) oxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">Ce<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 4 <span class="chemf nowrap">CeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> applications for cerium(III) oxide are, as a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> for the oxidation of CO and <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a> emissions in the exhaust gases from motor vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and secondly, cerium oxide finds use as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_additive" title="Fuel additive">fuel additive</a> to <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuels</a>, which results in increased <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a> and decreased hydrocarbon derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a> emissions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> however the health effects of the cerium oxide bearing engine exhaust is a point of study and dispute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_splitting">Water splitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water splitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide%E2%80%93cerium(III)_oxide_cycle" title="Cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle">cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle</a> or CeO<sub>2</sub>/Ce<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> cycle is a two step <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">water splitting</a> process based on <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a> and cerium(III) oxide for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illumination">Illumination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Illumination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cerium(III) oxide combined with <a href="/wiki/Tin(II)_oxide" title="Tin(II) oxide">tin(II) oxide</a> (SnO) in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> form is used for illumination with UV light. It absorbs light with a wavelength of 320 nm and emits light with a wavelength of 412 nm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  This combination of cerium(III) oxide and tin(II) oxide is rare, and obtained only with difficulty on a laboratory scale.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cerium(III) oxide is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> at approximately 1,400 °C (2,550 °F) to make air stable cerium(III) oxide.
</p>
