<p><b>Svante August Arrhenius</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ʊ/: 'u' in 'push'">ʊ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> 19 February 1859 – 2 October 1927) was a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a>. Originally a <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, but often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>, Arrhenius was one of the founders of the science of <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical chemistry</a>. He received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize for Chemistry">Nobel Prize for Chemistry</a> in 1903, becoming the first <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> Nobel laureate, and in 1905 became director of the Nobel Institute where he remained until his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-2">[2]</a></sup> His lasting contributions to science are exemplified and memorialized by the <a href="/wiki/Arrhenius_equation" title="Arrhenius equation">Arrhenius equation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhenius_acid" title="Arrhenius acid">Arrhenius definition of an acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> <a href="/wiki/Arrhenius_(lunar_crater)" title="Arrhenius (lunar crater)">Arrhenius</a>, the mountain of <a href="/wiki/Arrheniusfjellet" title="Arrheniusfjellet">Arrheniusfjellet</a> and the Arrhenius Labs at  <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_University" title="Stockholm University">Stockholm University</a>, all named after him.  He was the first to use basic principles of physical chemistry to calculate estimates of the extent to which increases in atmospheric carbon dioxide increase Earth's surface temperature, leading <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">David Keeling</a> to conclude that human-caused carbon dioxide emissions are large enough to cause global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baum_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baum-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arrhenius was born on 19 February 1859 at Vik (also spelled Wik or Wijk), near <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a>, Sweden, the son of Svante Gustav and Carolina Thunberg Arrhenius. His father had been a <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">land surveyor</a> for <a href="/wiki/Uppsala_University" title="Uppsala University">Uppsala University</a>, moving up to a supervisory position. At the age of three, Arrhenius taught himself to read without the encouragement of his parents, and by watching his father's addition of numbers in his account books, became an <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetical</a> <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">prodigy</a>. In later life, Arrhenius enjoyed using masses of data to discover mathematical relationships and laws.
</p><p>At age eight, he entered the local cathedral school, starting in the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_grade" title="Fifth grade">fifth grade</a>, distinguishing himself in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and graduating as the youngest and most able student in 1876.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionic_disassociation">Ionic disassociation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ionic disassociation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the University of Uppsala, he was dissatisfied with the chief instructor of physics and the only faculty member who could have supervised him in chemistry, <a href="/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve" title="Per Teodor Cleve">Per Teodor Cleve</a>, so he left to study at the Physical Institute of the Swedish Academy of Sciences in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> under the physicist <a href="/wiki/Erik_Edlund" title="Erik Edlund">Erik Edlund</a> in 1881.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>His work focused on the <a href="/wiki/Conductivity_(electrolytic)" title="Conductivity (electrolytic)">conductivities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>. In 1884, based on this work, he submitted a 150-page dissertation on electrolytic conductivity to Uppsala for the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">doctorate</a>. It did not impress the professors, among whom was Cleve, and he received a fourth-class degree, but upon his defense it was reclassified as third-class. Later, extensions of this very work would earn him the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates_in_Chemistry" title="List of Nobel laureates in Chemistry">1903 Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arrhenius put forth 56 theses in his 1884 dissertation, most of which would still be accepted today unchanged or with minor modifications. The most important idea in the dissertation was his explanation of the fact that solid crystalline salts disassociate into paired charged particles when dissolved, for which he would win the 1903 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Arrhenius' explanation was that in forming a solution, the salt disassociates into charged particles, to which <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> had given the name <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> many years earlier. Faraday's belief had been that ions were produced in the process of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>, that is, an external direct current source of electricity was necessary to form ions. Arrhenius proposed that, even in the absence of an electric current, aqueous solutions of salts contained ions.
He thus proposed that chemical reactions in solution were reactions between ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-columbia-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncBrit_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncBrit-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciBio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciBio-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The dissertation did not impress the professors at Uppsala, but Arrhenius sent it to a number of scientists in Europe who were developing the new science of <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical chemistry</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius" title="Rudolf Clausius">Rudolf Clausius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._H._van_%27t_Hoff" title="J. H. van 't Hoff">J. H. van 't Hoff</a>. 
They were far more impressed, and Ostwald even came to Uppsala to persuade Arrhenius to join his research team. Arrhenius declined, however, as he preferred to stay in Sweden for a while (his father was very ill and would die in 1885) and had received an appointment at Uppsala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-columbia-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncBrit_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EncBrit-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciBio_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SciBio-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In an extension of his <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic theory</a> Arrhenius proposed definitions for <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>, in 1884. He believed that acids were substances that produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> and that bases were substances that produce hydroxide ions in solution.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_period">Middle period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1885 Arrhenius next received a travel grant from the Swedish Academy of Sciences, which enabled him to study with Ostwald in <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>), with <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Kohlrausch_(physicist)" title="Friedrich Kohlrausch (physicist)">Friedrich Kohlrausch</a> in <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann" title="Ludwig Boltzmann">Ludwig Boltzmann</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graz,_Austria" title="Graz, Austria">Graz, Austria</a>, and with van 't Hoff in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.
</p><p>In 1889 Arrhenius explained the fact that most reactions require added heat energy to proceed by formulating the concept of <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a>, an energy barrier that must be overcome before two molecules will react. The <a href="/wiki/Arrhenius_equation" title="Arrhenius equation">Arrhenius equation</a> gives the quantitative basis of the relationship between the activation energy and the rate at which a reaction proceeds.
</p><p>In 1891 he became a lecturer at the Stockholm University College (<i>Stockholms Högskola</i>, now <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_University" title="Stockholm University">Stockholm University</a>), being promoted to professor of physics (with much opposition) in 1895, and <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a> in 1896.
</p><p>He was married twice, first to his former pupil Sofia Rudbeck (1894 to 1896), with whom he had one son <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olof_Vilhelm_Arrhenius" title="fr:Olof Vilhelm Arrhenius">Olof Arrhenius</a>, and then to Maria Johansson (1905 to 1927), with whom he had two daughters and a son.
</p><p>About 1900, Arrhenius became involved in setting up the Nobel Institutes and the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prizes</a>. He was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a> in 1901. For the rest of his life, he would be a member of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Committee" title="Nobel Committee">Nobel Committee</a> on Physics and a de facto member of the Nobel Committee on Chemistry.  He used his positions to arrange prizes for his friends (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobus_van%27t_Hoff" title="Jacobus van't Hoff">Jacobus van't Hoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_William_Richards" title="Theodore William Richards">Theodore Richards</a>) and to attempt to deny them to his enemies (<a href="/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich" title="Paul Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walther_Nernst" title="Walther Nernst">Walther Nernst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  In 1901 Arrhenius was elected to the Swedish Academy of Sciences, against strong opposition. In 1903 he became the first Swede to be awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in chemistry">Nobel Prize in chemistry</a>. 
In 1905, upon the founding of the Nobel Institute for Physical Research at Stockholm, he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Rector_(academia)" title="Rector (academia)">rector</a> of the institute, the position where he remained until retirement in 1927. He was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_1910" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 1910">Foreign Member of the Royal Society (ForMemRS) in 1910</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1911 he won the first Willard Gibbs Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
"  In 1912, he was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1919 he became foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2701" data-file-width="1699" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg/220px-Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg/330px-Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg/440px-Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius_family_grave.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arrhenius' family grave in <a href="/wiki/Uppsala" title="Uppsala">Uppsala</a></div></div></div>
<p>Eventually, Arrhenius' theories became generally accepted and he turned to other scientific topics. In 1902 he began to investigate <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiological</a> problems in terms of chemical theory. He determined that reactions in living organisms and in the test tube followed the same laws.
</p><p>In 1904 he delivered at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> a course of lectures, the object of which was to illustrate the application of the methods of physical chemistry to the study of the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxins" title="Toxins">toxins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antitoxins" title="Antitoxins">antitoxins</a>, and which were published in 1907 under the title <i>Immunochemistry</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
He also turned his attention to <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> (the origin of <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>), <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, accounting for the birth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a> by interstellar collision. 
He considered <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> as accounting for <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>, the solar <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">aurora borealis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">zodiacal light</a>.
</p><p>He thought life might have been carried from planet to planet by the transport of <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">spores</a>, the theory now known as <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  He thought of the idea of a <a href="/wiki/Universal_language" title="Universal language">universal language</a>, proposing a modification of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>.
</p><p>He was a board member for the <i>Swedish Society for Racial Hygiene</i> (founded 1909), which endorsed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mendelism" title="Mendelism">mendelism</a> at the time, and contributed to the topic of contraceptives around 1910. However, until 1938 information and sale of contraceptives was prohibited in Sweden. Around 1930, conservative members of the society helped to establish <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenic</a> policies in Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Stein" title="Gordon Stein">Gordon Stein</a> wrote that Svante Arrhenius was an atheist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In his last years he wrote both textbooks and popular books, trying to emphasize the need for further work on the topics he discussed. In September 1927 he came down with an attack of acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">intestinal</a> <a href="/wiki/Catarrh" title="Catarrh">catarrh</a> and died on 2 October. He was buried in Uppsala.
</p><p>Arrhenius was the grandfather of bacteriologist <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Wold" title="Agnes Wold">Agnes Wold</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_effect">Greenhouse effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Svante_Arrhenius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greenhouse effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In developing a theory to explain the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>, Arrhenius, in 1896, was the first to use basic principles of physical chemistry to calculate estimates of the extent to which increases in atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) will increase Earth's surface temperature through the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baum_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baum-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CarbonicAcid_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CarbonicAcid-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> These calculations led him to conclude that human-caused CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, from fossil-fuel burning and other combustion processes, are large enough to cause global warming. This conclusion has been extensively tested, winning a place at the core of modern climate science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Arrhenius, in this work, built upon the prior work of other famous scientists, including <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Claude_Pouillet" title="Claude Pouillet">Claude Pouillet</a>. Arrhenius wanted to determine whether greenhouse gases could contribute to the explanation of the temperature variation between glacial and inter-glacial periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Arrhenius used infrared observations of the moon — by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Washington_Very" title="Frank Washington Very">Frank Washington Very</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pierpont_Langley" title="Samuel Pierpont Langley">Samuel Pierpont Langley</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Observatory" title="Allegheny Observatory">Allegheny Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> — to calculate how much of infrared (heat) radiation is captured by CO<sub>2</sub> and water (H<sub>2</sub>O) vapour in Earth's atmosphere. Using 'Stefan's law' (better known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a>), he formulated what he referred to as a 'rule'. 
In its original form, Arrhenius' rule reads as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>if the quantity of carbonic acid increases in geometric progression, the augmentation of the temperature will increase nearly in arithmetic progression.</i></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Here, Arrhenius refers to CO<sub>2</sub> as carbonic acid (which refers only to the aqueous form H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> in modern usage). The following formulation of Arrhenius' rule is still in use today:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walter_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walter-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solvay_conference,_1922.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1469" data-file-width="1640" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg/220px-Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg/330px-Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg/440px-Solvay_conference%2C_1922.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solvay_conference,_1922.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arrhenius at the first Solvay conference on chemistry in 1922 in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Based on information from his colleague <a href="/wiki/Arvid_H%C3%B6gbom" title="Arvid Högbom">Arvid Högbom</a>, Arrhenius was the first person to predict that emissions of carbon dioxide from the burning of fossil fuels and other combustion processes were large enough to cause global warming. In his calculation Arrhenius included the feedback from changes in water vapor as well as latitudinal effects, but he omitted clouds, convection of heat upward in the atmosphere, and other essential factors. His work is currently seen less as an accurate quantification of global warming than as the first demonstration that increases in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> will cause global warming, everything else being equal.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1017" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg/220px-Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg/330px-Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg/440px-Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Svante_Arrhenius_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Svante Arrhenius (1909)</div></div></div>
<p>Arrhenius' absorption values for CO<sub>2</sub> and his conclusions met criticism by <a href="/wiki/Knut_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Knut Ångström">Knut Ångström</a> in 1900, who published the first modern infrared absorption spectrum of CO<sub>2</sub> with two absorption bands, and published experimental results that seemed to show that absorption of infrared radiation by the gas in the atmosphere was already "saturated" so that adding more could make no difference. Arrhenius replied strongly in 1901 (<i>Annalen der Physik</i>), dismissing the critique altogether. He touched on the subject briefly in a technical book titled <i>Lehrbuch der kosmischen Physik</i> (1903). He later wrote <i>Världarnas utveckling</i> (1906) (German: <i>Das Werden der Welten</i> [1907], English: <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=1t45AAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover" rel="nofollow"><i>Worlds in the Making</i></a> [1908]) directed at a general audience, where he suggested that the human emission of CO<sub>2</sub> would be strong enough to prevent the world from entering a new ice age, and that a warmer earth would be needed to feed the rapidly increasing population:  
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"To a certain extent the temperature of the earth's surface, as we shall presently see, is conditioned by the properties of the atmosphere surrounding it, and particularly by the permeability of the latter for the rays of heat." (p46)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"That the atmospheric envelopes limit the heat losses from the planets had been suggested about 1800 by the great French physicist Fourier. His ideas were further developed afterwards by Pouillet and Tyndall. Their theory has been styled the hot-house theory, because they thought that the atmosphere acted after the manner of the glass panes of hot-houses." (p51)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"If the quantity of carbonic acid [ CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O <b>→</b> H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> (carbonic acid) ] in the air should sink to one-half its present percentage, the temperature would fall by about 4°; a diminution to one-quarter would reduce the temperature by 8°. On the other hand, any doubling of the percentage of carbon dioxide in the air would raise the temperature of the earth's surface by 4°; and if the carbon dioxide were increased fourfold, the temperature would rise by 8°." (p53)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Although the sea, by absorbing carbonic acid, acts as a regulator of huge capacity, which takes up about five-sixths of the produced carbonic acid, we yet recognize that the slight percentage of carbonic acid in the atmosphere may by the advances of industry be changed to a noticeable degree in the course of a few centuries." (p54)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Since, now, warm ages have alternated with glacial periods, even after man appeared on the earth, we have to ask ourselves: Is it probable that we shall in the coming geological ages be visited by a new ice period that will drive us from our temperate countries into the hotter climates of Africa? There does not appear to be much ground for such an apprehension. The enormous combustion of coal by our industrial establishments suffices to increase the percentage of carbon dioxide in the air to a perceptible degree." (p61)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"We often hear lamentations that the coal stored up in the earth is wasted by the present generation without any thought of the future, and we are terrified by the awful destruction of life and property which has followed the volcanic eruptions of our days. We may find a kind of consolation in the consideration that here, as in every other case, there is good mixed with the evil. By the influence of the increasing percentage of carbonic acid in the atmosphere, we may hope to enjoy ages with more equable and better climates, especially as regards the colder regions of the earth, ages when the earth will bring forth much more abundant crops than at present, for the benefit of rapidly propagating mankind." (p63)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>At this time, the accepted consensus explanation is that, historically, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> has set the timing for ice ages, with CO<sub>2</sub> acting as an essential <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">amplifying feedback</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, CO<sub>2</sub> releases since the industrial revolution have increased CO<sub>2</sub> to a level not found since 10 to 15 million years ago, when the global average surface temperature was up to 11 °F (6 °C) warmer than now and almost all ice had melted, raising world sea-levels to about 100 feet higher than today's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arrhenius estimated based on the CO<sub>2</sub> levels at his time, that reducing levels by 0.62 – 0.55 would decrease temperatures by 4–5 °C (Celsius) and an increase of 2.5 to 3 times of CO<sub>2</sub> would cause a temperature rise of 8–9 °C in the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CarbonicAcid_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-CarbonicAcid-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In his book <i>Worlds in the Making</i> he described the "hot-house" theory of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
