<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Albedo</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Albedo_(disambiguation)" title="Albedo (disambiguation)">Albedo (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albedo-e_hg.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2220" data-file-width="1588" height="405" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Albedo-e_hg.svg/290px-Albedo-e_hg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Albedo-e_hg.svg/435px-Albedo-e_hg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Albedo-e_hg.svg/580px-Albedo-e_hg.svg.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Albedo-e_hg.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The percentage of <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffusely reflected</a> <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> relative to various surface conditions</div></div></div>
<p><b>Albedo</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">albedo</i>, meaning "whiteness") is the measure of the <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffuse reflection</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">solar radiation</a> out of the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> received by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">astronomical body</a> (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> like <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>). It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless_number" title="Dimensionless number">dimensionless</a> and measured on a scale from 0 (corresponding to a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> that absorbs all incident radiation) to 1 (corresponding to a body that reflects all incident radiation).
</p><p>Surface albedo is defined as the ratio of irradiance reflected to the irradiance received by a surface. The proportion reflected is not only determined by properties of the surface itself, but also by the spectral and angular distribution of solar radiation reaching the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These factors vary with atmospheric composition, geographic location and time (see <a href="/wiki/Position_of_the_Sun" title="Position of the Sun">position of the Sun</a>). While bi-hemispherical <a href="/wiki/Reflectance" title="Reflectance">reflectance</a> is calculated for a single angle of incidence (i.e., for a given position of the Sun), albedo is the directional integration of reflectance over all solar angles in a given period. The temporal resolution may range from seconds (as obtained from flux measurements) to daily, monthly, or annual averages.
</p><p>Unless given for a specific wavelength (spectral albedo), albedo refers to the entire spectrum of solar radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to measurement constraints, it is often given for the spectrum in which most solar energy reaches the surface (between 0.3 and 3 μm). This spectrum includes <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light</a> (0.39–0.7 μm), which explains why surfaces with a low albedo appear dark (e.g., trees absorb most radiation), whereas surfaces with a high albedo appear bright (e.g., snow reflects most radiation).
</p><p>Albedo is an important concept in <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, and environmental management (e.g., as part of the <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) program for sustainable rating of buildings). The average albedo of the Earth from the upper atmosphere, its <i>planetary albedo</i>, is 30–35% because of <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud cover</a>, but widely varies locally across the surface because of different geological and environmental features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term albedo was introduced into optics by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Lambert" title="Johann Heinrich Lambert">Johann Heinrich Lambert</a> in his 1760 work <i><a href="/wiki/Photometria" title="Photometria">Photometria</a></i>.
</p>

<p>Any albedo in visible light falls within a range of about 0.9 for fresh snow to about 0.04 for charcoal, one of the darkest substances. Deeply shadowed cavities can achieve an effective albedo approaching the zero of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a>. When seen from a distance, the ocean surface has a low albedo, as do most forests, whereas desert areas have some of the highest albedos among landforms. Most land areas are in an albedo range of 0.1 to 0.4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhysicsWorld_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PhysicsWorld-10">[10]</a></sup> The average albedo of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is about 0.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goode_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goode-11">[11]</a></sup> This is far higher than for the ocean primarily because of the contribution of clouds.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2740" data-file-width="2075" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png/220px-Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png/330px-Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png/440px-Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceres_2003_2004_clear_sky_total_sky_albedo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>2003–2004 mean annual clear-sky and total-sky albedo</div></div></div>
<p>Earth's surface albedo is regularly estimated via <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation" title="Earth observation">Earth observation</a> satellite sensors such as <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MODIS" title="MODIS">MODIS</a> instruments on board the <a href="/wiki/Terra_(satellite)" title="Terra (satellite)">Terra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aqua_(satellite)" title="Aqua (satellite)">Aqua</a> satellites, and the CERES instrument on the <a href="/wiki/Suomi_NPP" title="Suomi NPP">Suomi NPP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joint_Polar_Satellite_System" title="Joint Polar Satellite System">JPSS</a>. As the amount of reflected radiation is only measured for a single direction by satellite, not all directions, a mathematical model is used to translate a sample set of satellite reflectance measurements into estimates of <a href="/wiki/Directional-hemispherical_reflectance" title="Directional-hemispherical reflectance">directional-hemispherical reflectance</a> and bi-hemispherical reflectance (e.g.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-12">[12]</a></sup>). These calculations are based on the <a href="/wiki/Bidirectional_reflectance_distribution_function" title="Bidirectional reflectance distribution function">bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF)</a>, which describes how the reflectance of a given surface depends on the view angle of the observer and the solar angle. BDRF can facilitate translations of observations of reflectance into albedo.
</p><p>Earth's average surface temperature due to its albedo and the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> is currently about 15 °C. If Earth were frozen entirely (and hence be more reflective), the average temperature of the planet would drop below −40 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washington_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-washington-13">[13]</a></sup> If only the continental land masses became covered by glaciers, the mean temperature of the planet would drop to about 0 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clim-past_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-clim-past-14">[14]</a></sup> In contrast, if the entire Earth was covered by water — a so-called aquaplanet — the average temperature on the planet would rise to almost 27 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Robin_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Robin-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White-sky_and_black-sky_albedo">White-sky and black-sky albedo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: White-sky and black-sky albedo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Directional-hemispherical_reflectance" title="Directional-hemispherical reflectance">Directional-hemispherical reflectance</a> is sometimes referred to as black-sky albedo and <a href="/wiki/Bi-hemispherical_reflectance" title="Bi-hemispherical reflectance">bi-hemispherical reflectance</a> as white-sky albedo. These terms are important because they allow the albedo to be calculated for any given illumination conditions from a knowledge of the intrinsic properties of the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BlueskyAlbedo_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-BlueskyAlbedo-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_albedo">Astronomical albedo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Astronomical albedo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The albedos of <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">satellites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planets</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> can be used to infer much about their properties. The study of albedos, their dependence on wavelength, lighting angle ("phase angle"), and variation in time comprises a major part of the astronomical field of <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometry</a>. For small and far objects that cannot be resolved by telescopes, much of what we know comes from the study of their albedos. For example, the absolute albedo can indicate the surface ice content of outer <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> objects, the variation of albedo with phase angle gives information about <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> properties, whereas unusually high radar albedo is indicative of high metal content in <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a>, a moon of Saturn, has one of the highest known albedos of any body in the Solar System, with 99% of EM radiation reflected. Another notable high-albedo body is <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a>, with an albedo of 0.96.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sicardy_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-sicardy-17">[17]</a></sup> Many small objects in the outer Solar System<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tnoalbedo_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-tnoalbedo-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a> have low albedos down to about 0.05.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astalbedo_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-astalbedo-19">[19]</a></sup> A typical <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">comet nucleus</a> has an albedo of 0.04.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dark_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-dark-20">[20]</a></sup> Such a dark surface is thought to be indicative of a primitive and heavily <a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">space weathered</a> surface containing some <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>.
</p><p>The overall albedo of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is measured to be around 0.136,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CERESmoon_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-CERESmoon-21">[21]</a></sup> but it is strongly directional and non-Lambertian, displaying also a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_effect" title="Opposition effect">opposition effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medkeff_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-medkeff-22">[22]</a></sup> Although such reflectance properties are different from those of any terrestrial terrains, they are typical of the <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> surfaces of airless Solar System bodies.
</p><p>Two common albedos that are used in astronomy are the (V-band) <a href="/wiki/Geometric_albedo" title="Geometric albedo">geometric albedo</a> (measuring brightness when illumination comes from directly behind the observer) and the <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">Bond albedo</a> (measuring total proportion of electromagnetic energy reflected). Their values can differ significantly, which is a common source of confusion.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_terrestrial_albedo_effects">Examples of terrestrial albedo effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples of terrestrial albedo effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illumination">Illumination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Illumination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albedo is not directly dependent on illumination because changing the amount of incoming light proportionally changes the amount of reflected light, except in circumstances where a change in illumination induces a change in the Earth's surface at that location (e.g. through albedo-temperature feedback). That said, albedo and illumination both vary by latitude. Albedo is highest near the poles and lowest in the subtropics, with a local maximum in the tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winston_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winston-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insolation_effects">Insolation effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Insolation effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The intensity of albedo temperature effects depend on the amount of albedo and the level of local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> (solar irradiance); high albedo areas in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">antarctic</a> regions are cold due to low insolation, where areas such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, which also have a relatively high albedo, will be hotter due to high insolation.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">Tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-tropical" title="Sub-tropical">sub-tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> areas have low albedo, and are much hotter than their <a href="/wiki/Temperate_forest" title="Temperate forest">temperate forest</a> counterparts, which have lower insolation. Because insolation plays such a big role in the heating and cooling effects of albedo, high insolation areas like the tropics will tend to show a more pronounced fluctuation in local temperature when local albedo changes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Arctic regions notably release more heat back into space than what they absorb, effectively cooling the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. This has been a concern since arctic ice and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> has been melting at higher rates due to higher temperatures, creating regions in the arctic that are notably darker (being water or ground which is darker color) and reflects less heat back into space. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">feedback loop</a> results in a reduced albedo effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_weather">Climate and weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate and weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albedo affects <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> by determining how much <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> a planet absorbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The uneven heating of Earth from albedo variations between land, ice, or ocean surfaces can drive <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Albedo.E2.80.93temperature_feedback"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Albedo–temperature_feedback">Albedo–temperature feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Albedo–temperature feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When an area's albedo changes due to snowfall, a snow–temperature <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> results. A layer of snowfall increases local albedo, reflecting away sunlight, leading to local cooling. In principle, if no outside temperature change affects this area (e.g., a warm <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a>), the raised albedo and lower temperature would maintain the current snow and invite further snowfall, deepening the snow–temperature feedback. However, because local <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> is dynamic due to the change of <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, eventually warm air masses and a more direct angle of sunlight (higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>) cause melting. When the melted area reveals surfaces with lower albedo, such as grass or soil, the effect is reversed: the darkening surface lowers albedo, increasing local temperatures, which induces more melting and thus reducing the albedo further, resulting in still more heating.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snow">Snow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Snow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Snow albedo is highly variable, ranging from as high as 0.9 for freshly fallen snow, to about 0.4 for melting snow, and as low as 0.2 for dirty snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Over <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> snow albedo averages a little more than 0.8. If a marginally snow-covered area warms, snow tends to melt, lowering the albedo, and hence leading to more snowmelt because more radiation is being absorbed by the snowpack (the ice–albedo <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>).
</p><p>Just as fresh snow has a higher albedo than does dirty snow, the albedo of snow-covered sea ice is far higher than that of sea water. Sea water absorbs more solar radiation than would the same surface covered with reflective snow. When sea ice melts, either due to a rise in sea temperature or in response to increased solar radiation from above, the snow-covered surface is reduced, and more surface of sea water is exposed, so the rate of energy absorption increases. The extra absorbed energy heats the sea water, which in turn increases the rate at which sea ice melts. As with the preceding example of snowmelt, the process of melting of sea ice is thus another example of a positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Both positive feedback loops have long been recognized as important to the modern theory of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cryoconite" title="Cryoconite">Cryoconite</a>, powdery windblown <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> containing soot, sometimes reduces albedo on glaciers and ice sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat._Geo_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nat._Geo-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The dynamical nature of albedo in response to positive feedback, together with the effects of small errors in the measurement of albedo, can lead to large errors in energy estimates. Because of this, in order to reduce the error of energy estimates, it is important to measure the albedo of snow-covered areas through remote sensing techniques rather than applying a single value for albedo over broad regions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Small-scale_effects">Small-scale effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Small-scale effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albedo works on a smaller scale, too. In sunlight, dark clothes absorb more heat and light-coloured clothes reflect it better, thus allowing some control over body temperature by exploiting the albedo effect of the colour of external clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ranknfile-ue_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ranknfile-ue-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_photovoltaic_effects">Solar photovoltaic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solar photovoltaic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albedo can affect the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a> output of solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic devices</a>. For example, the effects of a spectrally responsive albedo are illustrated by the differences between the spectrally weighted albedo of solar photovoltaic technology based on hydrogenated amorphous silicon (a-Si:H) and crystalline silicon (c-Si)-based compared to traditional spectral-integrated albedo predictions. Research showed impacts of over 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> More recently, the analysis was extended to the effects of spectral bias due to the specular reflectivity of 22 commonly occurring surface materials (both human-made and natural) and analyzes the albedo effects on the performance of seven photovoltaic materials covering three common photovoltaic system topologies: industrial (solar farms), commercial flat rooftops and residential pitched-roof applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trees">Trees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Trees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because forests generally have a low albedo, (the majority of the ultraviolet and <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> is absorbed through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>), some scientists have suggested that greater heat absorption by trees could offset some of the carbon benefits of afforestation (or offset the negative climate impacts of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>). In the case of evergreen forests with seasonal snow cover albedo reduction may be great enough for deforestation to cause a net cooling effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Trees also impact climate in extremely complicated ways through <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>. The water vapor causes cooling on the land surface, causes heating where it condenses, acts a strong greenhouse gas, and can increase albedo when it condenses into clouds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Scientists generally treat evapotranspiration as a net cooling impact, and the net climate impact of albedo and evapotranspiration changes from deforestation depends greatly on local climate <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In seasonally snow-covered zones, winter albedos of treeless areas are 10% to 50% higher than nearby forested areas because snow does not cover the trees as readily. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciduous_trees" title="Deciduous trees">Deciduous trees</a> have an albedo value of about 0.15 to 0.18 whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coniferous_trees" title="Coniferous trees">coniferous trees</a> have a value of about 0.09 to 0.15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mmutrees_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-mmutrees-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre" title="Hadley Centre">Hadley Centre</a> have investigated the relative (generally warming) effect of albedo change and (cooling) effect of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> on planting forests. They found that new forests in tropical and midlatitude areas tended to cool; new forests in high latitudes (e.g., Siberia) were neutral or perhaps warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Betts_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Betts-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_reflectivity.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Water_reflectivity.jpg/290px-Water_reflectivity.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Water_reflectivity.jpg/435px-Water_reflectivity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Water_reflectivity.jpg/580px-Water_reflectivity.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_reflectivity.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reflectivity of smooth water at 20 °C (refractive index=1.333)</div></div></div>
<p>Water reflects light very differently from typical terrestrial materials. The reflectivity of a water surface is calculated using the <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_equations" title="Fresnel equations">Fresnel equations</a> (see graph).
</p><p>At the scale of the wavelength of light even wavy water is always smooth so the light is reflected in a locally <a href="/wiki/Specular_reflection" title="Specular reflection">specular manner</a> (not <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffusely</a>). The glint of light off water is a commonplace effect of this. At small <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">angles of incident</a> light, <a href="/wiki/Waviness" title="Waviness">waviness</a> results in reduced reflectivity because of the steepness of the reflectivity-vs.-incident-angle curve and a locally increased average incident angle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fresnel_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fresnel-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the reflectivity of water is very low at low and medium angles of incident light, it becomes very high at high angles of incident light such as those that occur on the illuminated side of Earth near the <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">terminator</a> (early morning, late afternoon, and near the poles). However, as mentioned above, waviness causes an appreciable reduction. Because light specularly reflected from water does not usually reach the viewer, water is usually considered to have a very low albedo in spite of its high reflectivity at high angles of incident light.
</p><p>Note that white caps on waves look white (and have high albedo) because the water is foamed up, so there are many superimposed bubble surfaces which reflect, adding up their reflectivities. Fresh 'black' ice exhibits Fresnel reflection.
Snow on top of this sea ice increases the albedo to 0.9.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clouds">Clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cloud_albedo" title="Cloud albedo">Cloud albedo</a> has substantial influence over atmospheric temperatures. Different types of clouds exhibit different reflectivity, theoretically ranging in albedo from a minimum of near 0 to a maximum approaching 0.8. "On any given day, about half of Earth is covered by clouds, which reflect more sunlight than land and water. Clouds keep Earth cool by reflecting sunlight, but they can also serve as blankets to trap warmth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livescience_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-livescience-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Albedo and climate in some areas are affected by artificial clouds, such as those created by the <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a> of heavy commercial airliner traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> A study following the burning of the Kuwaiti oil fields during Iraqi occupation showed that temperatures under the burning oil fires were as much as 10 °C colder than temperatures several miles away under clear skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvard_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvard-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerosol_effects">Aerosol effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Aerosol effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">Aerosols</a> (very fine particles/droplets in the atmosphere) have both direct and indirect effects on Earth's radiative balance. The direct (albedo) effect is generally to cool the planet; the indirect effect (the particles act as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a> and thereby change cloud properties) is less certain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-girda_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-girda-50">[50]</a></sup> As per <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOMINICK_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOMINICK-51">[51]</a></sup> the effects are:
</p>
<blockquote>
<ul><li><i>Aerosol direct effect.</i> Aerosols directly scatter and absorb radiation. The scattering of radiation causes atmospheric cooling, whereas absorption can cause atmospheric warming.</li>
<li><i>Aerosol indirect effect.</i> Aerosols modify the properties of clouds through a subset of the aerosol population called <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a>. Increased nuclei concentrations lead to increased cloud droplet number concentrations, which in turn leads to increased cloud albedo, increased light scattering and radiative cooling (<i>first indirect effect</i>), but also leads to reduced precipitation efficiency and increased lifetime of the cloud (<i>second indirect effect</i>).</li></ul>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_carbon">Black carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Black carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another albedo-related effect on the climate is from <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> particles. The size of this effect is difficult to quantify: the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> estimates that the global mean radiative forcing for black carbon aerosols from fossil fuels is +0.2 W m<sup>−2</sup>, with a range +0.1 to +0.4 W m<sup>−2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-girda_1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-girda_1-52">[52]</a></sup> Black carbon is a bigger cause of the melting of the polar ice cap in the Arctic than carbon dioxide due to its effect on the albedo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_activities">Human activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Human activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Human activities (e.g., deforestation, farming, and urbanization) change the albedo of various areas around the globe. However, quantification of this effect on the global scale is difficult.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_albedo">Other types of albedo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albedo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other types of albedo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Single-scattering_albedo" title="Single-scattering albedo">Single-scattering albedo</a> is used to define scattering of electromagnetic waves on small particles. It depends on properties of the material (<a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>); the size of the particle or particles; and the wavelength of the incoming radiation.
</p>
