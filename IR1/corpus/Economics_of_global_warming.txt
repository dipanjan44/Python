<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Economics of global warming</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>economics of global warming</b> concerns the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> aspects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>; this can inform <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">policies</a> that governments might consider in response. A number of factors make this a difficult problem from both economic and political perspectives: it is a long-term, intergenerational problem;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_long_term_climate_change_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_long_term_climate_change-1">[1]</a></sup> benefits and costs are distributed unequally both within and across countries; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_global_warming" title="Scientific opinion on global warming">scientific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion_on_global_warming" title="Public opinion on global warming">public</a> opinions may diverge.
</p><p>One of the most important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_attribution_of_climate_change_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_attribution_of_climate_change-2">[2]</a></sup> Around 20% of carbon dioxide which is emitted <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">due to human activities</a> can remain in <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">the atmosphere</a> for many thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_co2_lifetime_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-meehl_co2_lifetime-3">[3]</a></sup> The long time scales and <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> associated with global warming have led analysts to develop "<a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">scenarios</a>" of future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environmental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_change" title="Social change">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic</a> changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections-4">[4]</a></sup> These scenarios can help governments understand the potential consequences of their decisions.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">impacts of climate change</a> include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loss_of_biodiversity" title="Loss of biodiversity">loss of biodiversity</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, increased frequency and severity of some <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidification of the oceans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Economists have attempted to quantify these impacts in monetary terms, but these assessments can be controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decanio_iam_limitations_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-decanio_iam_limitations-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policy_responses_to_global_warming" title="Policy responses to global warming">policy responses to global warming</a> are to reduce greenhouse gas emissions (<a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">to adapt</a> to the impacts of global warming (e.g., by building <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a> in response to sea level rise). Another policy response which has recently received greater attention is <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a> of the climate system (e.g. injecting <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> to reflect sunlight away from the Earth's surface).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the responses to the uncertainties of global warming is to adopt a strategy of sequential decision making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making-9">[9]</a></sup> This strategy recognizes that decisions on global warming need to be made with incomplete <a href="/wiki/Information_economics" title="Information economics">information</a>, and that decisions in the near term will have potentially <a href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_global_warming" title="Long-term effects of global warming">long-term impacts</a>. Governments might choose to use <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> as part of their policy response to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yohe_risk_management_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-yohe_risk_management-10">[10]</a></sup> For instance, a risk-based approach can be applied to climate impacts which are difficult to quantify in economic terms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yohe_risk_management_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-yohe_risk_management-10">[10]</a></sup> e.g., the impacts of global warming on <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a>.
</p><p>Analysts have assessed global warming in relation to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sustainable development considers how future generations might be affected by the actions of the current generation. In some areas, policies designed to address global warming may contribute positively towards other development objectives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_sustainability_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-parry_sustainability-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sathaye_mitigation_and_development_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-sathaye_mitigation_and_development-13">[13]</a></sup> In other areas, the <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> of global warming policies may divert <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a> away from other socially and environmentally beneficial <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investments</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_cost" title="Opportunity cost">opportunity costs</a> of climate change policy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parry_sustainability_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-parry_sustainability-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sathaye_mitigation_and_development_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-sathaye_mitigation_and_development-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scenarios">Scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">Climate change scenario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios" title="Climate change mitigation scenarios">Climate change mitigation scenarios</a></div>
<p>One of the economic aspects of climate change is producing <a href="/wiki/Scenario_analysis" title="Scenario analysis">scenarios</a> of future economic development. Future economic developments can, for example, affect how <a href="/wiki/Climate_change,_industry_and_society#Vulnerability" title="Climate change, industry and society">vulnerable</a> society is to future climate change,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  what the future <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">impacts of climate change</a> might be, as well as the level of future GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_scenarios">Emissions scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emissions scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In scenarios designed to project future GHG emissions, economic projections, e.g., changes in future income levels, will often necessarily be combined with other projections that affect emissions, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Projections_of_population_growth" title="Projections of population growth">future population</a> levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivers_of_GHG_emissions_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-drivers_of_GHG_emissions-16">[16]</a></sup>  Since these future changes are highly uncertain, one approach is that of <a href="/wiki/Scenario_analysis" title="Scenario analysis">scenario analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections-4">[4]</a></sup>  In scenario analysis, scenarios are developed that are based on differing assumptions of future development patterns. An example of this are the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">"SRES" emissions scenarios</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC). The SRES scenarios project a wide range of possible future emissions levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  The SRES scenarios are "baseline" or "non-intervention" scenarios, in that they assume no specific policy measures to control future GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  The different SRES scenarios contain widely differing assumptions of future social and economic changes. For example, the SRES "A2" emissions scenario projects a future population level of 15 billion people in the year 2100, but the SRES "B1" scenario projects a lower population level of 7 billion people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  The SRES scenarios were not assigned probabilities by the IPCC, but some authors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  have argued that particular SRES scenarios are more likely to occur than others.
</p><p>Some analysts have developed scenarios that project a continuation of current policies into the future. These scenarios are sometimes called "business-as-usual" scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Experts who work on scenarios tend to prefer the term "projections" to "forecasts" or "predictions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahmad_2001_projections_v_predictions_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ahmad_2001_projections_v_predictions-23">[23]</a></sup> This distinction is made to emphasize the point that probabilities are not assigned to the scenarios,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahmad_2001_projections_v_predictions_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ahmad_2001_projections_v_predictions-23">[23]</a></sup> and that future emissions depend on decisions made both now and into the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world_energy_outlook_2009_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-world_energy_outlook_2009-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>75</span></sup>
</p><p>Another approach is that of <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_analysis" title="Uncertainty analysis">uncertainty analysis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-webster_2002_types_of_emissions_projections-4">[4]</a></sup> where analysts attempt to estimate the probability of future changes in emission levels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_futures_scenarios">Global futures scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global futures scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_Scenario_Group" title="Global Scenario Group">Global Scenario Group</a></div>
<p>"Global futures" scenarios can be thought of as stories of possible futures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  They allow for the description of factors which are difficult to quantify but are important in affecting future GHG emissions. The IPCC Third Assessment Report (Morita <i>et al.</i>, 2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  includes an assessment of 124 global futures scenarios. These scenarios project a wide range of possible futures. Some are pessimistic, for example, 5 scenarios project the future breakdown of human society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  Others are optimistic, for example, in 5 other scenarios, future advances in technology solve most or all of humanity's problems. Most scenarios project increasing damage to the natural environment, but many scenarios also project this trend to reverse in the long-term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the scenarios, Morita <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions-29">[29]</a></sup>  found no strong patterns in the relationship between economic activity and GHG emissions. By itself, this relationship is not proof of <a href="/wiki/Correlation_does_not_imply_causation" title="Correlation does not imply causation">causation</a>, and is only reflective of the scenarios that were assessed.
</p><p>In the assessed scenarios, economic growth is compatible with increasing or decreasing GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions-29">[29]</a></sup> In the latter case, emissions growth is mediated by increased <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy efficiency</a>, shifts to non-fossil energy sources, and/or shifts to a post-industrial <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">(service-based)</a> economy. Most scenarios projecting rising GHGs also project low levels of government intervention in the economy. Scenarios projecting falling GHGs generally have high levels of government intervention in the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-morita_2001_economic_growth_and_emissions-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_affecting_emissions_growth">Factors affecting emissions growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Factors affecting emissions growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_PAT" title="I PAT">I PAT</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Changes_in_components_of_the_Kaya_identity_between_1971-2009._Includes_global_energy-related_carbon_dioxide_emissions,_world_population,_world_GDP_per_capita,_energy_intensity_of_world_GDP_and_carbon_intensity_of_world_energy_use.png"><img alt="refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="812" data-file-width="1092" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Changes_in_components_of_the_Kaya_identity_between_1971-2009._Includes_global_energy-related_carbon_dioxide_emissions%2C_world_population%2C_world_GDP_per_capita%2C_energy_intensity_of_world_GDP_and_carbon_intensity_of_world_energy_use.png/220px-thumbnail.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Changes_in_components_of_the_Kaya_identity_between_1971-2009._Includes_global_energy-related_carbon_dioxide_emissions%2C_world_population%2C_world_GDP_per_capita%2C_energy_intensity_of_world_GDP_and_carbon_intensity_of_world_energy_use.png/330px-thumbnail.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Changes_in_components_of_the_Kaya_identity_between_1971-2009._Includes_global_energy-related_carbon_dioxide_emissions%2C_world_population%2C_world_GDP_per_capita%2C_energy_intensity_of_world_GDP_and_carbon_intensity_of_world_energy_use.png/440px-thumbnail.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Changes_in_components_of_the_Kaya_identity_between_1971-2009._Includes_global_energy-related_carbon_dioxide_emissions,_world_population,_world_GDP_per_capita,_energy_intensity_of_world_GDP_and_carbon_intensity_of_world_energy_use.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Changes in components of the Kaya identity between 1971 and 2009. Includes global energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions, world population, world GDP per capita, energy intensity of world GDP and carbon intensity of world energy use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea_2011_co2_emissions_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-iea_2011_co2_emissions-30">[30]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Historically, growth in GHG emissions have been driven by economic development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>169</span></sup>  One way of understanding trends in GHG emissions is to use the <a href="/wiki/Kaya_identity" title="Kaya identity">Kaya identity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivers_of_GHG_emissions_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-drivers_of_GHG_emissions-16">[16]</a></sup>  The Kaya identity breaks down emissions growth into the effects of changes in human population, economic affluence, and technology:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drivers_of_GHG_emissions_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-drivers_of_GHG_emissions-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>177</span></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions from energy ≡
</p>
<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;">Population × (<a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) per head of population) × (energy use / GDP) × (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions / energy use)</div>
<p>GDP per person (or "<a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a>") is used as a measure of economic affluence, and changes in technology are described by the other two terms: (energy use / GDP) and (energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions / energy use). These two terms are often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Energy_intensity" title="Energy intensity">energy intensity</a> of GDP" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_intensity" title="Carbon intensity">carbon intensity</a> of energy", respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy_intensity_and_carbon_intensity_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-energy_intensity_and_carbon_intensity-32">[32]</a></sup>  Note that the abbreviated term "carbon intensity" may also refer to the carbon intensity of GDP, i.e., (energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions / GDP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy_intensity_and_carbon_intensity_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-energy_intensity_and_carbon_intensity-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reductions in the energy intensity of GDP and/or carbon intensity of energy will tend to reduce energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-stern_projecting_GHG_emissions-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>177</span></sup> Increases in population and/or GDP per capita will tend to increase energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions.  If, however, energy intensity of GDP or carbon intensity of energy were reduced to zero (i.e., complete decarbonization of the energy system), increases in population or GDP per capita would not lead to an increase in energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions.
</p><p>The graph on the right shows changes in global energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions between 1971 and 2009. Also plotted are changes in world population, world GDP per capita, energy intensity of world GDP, and carbon intensity of world energy use. Over this time period, reductions in energy intensity of GDP and carbon intensity of energy use have been unable to offset increases in population and GDP per capita. Consequently, energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions have increased. Between 1971 and 2009, energy-related <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions grew on average by about 2.8% per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea_2011_co2_emissions_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-iea_2011_co2_emissions-30">[30]</a></sup>  Population grew on average by about 2.1% per year and GDP per capita by 2.6% per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea_2011_co2_emissions_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-iea_2011_co2_emissions-30">[30]</a></sup> Energy intensity of GDP on average fell by about 1.1% per year, and carbon intensity of energy fell by about 0.2% per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea_2011_co2_emissions_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-iea_2011_co2_emissions-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions">Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Equity_and_GHG_emissions">Equity and GHG emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Equity and GHG emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Regional_and_national_attribution_of_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas § Regional and national attribution of emissions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation#Sharing" title="Climate change mitigation">Climate change mitigation § Sharing</a></div>
<p>In considering GHG emissions, there are a number of areas where equity is important. In common language equity means "the quality of being impartial" or "something that is fair and just."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> One example of the relevance of equity to GHG emissions are the different ways in which emissions can be measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_1996_equity_emissions_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_1996_equity_emissions-34">[34]</a></sup>  These include the total <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Annual_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">annual emissions</a> of one country, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Cumulative_and_historical_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">cumulative emissions</a> measured over long time periods (sometimes measured over more than 100 years), average emissions per person in a country (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_capita_emissions" title="Per capita emissions">per capita emissions</a>), as well as measurements of energy intensity of GDP, carbon intensity of GDP, or carbon intensity of energy use (<a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Factors_affecting_emissions_growth" title="Economics of global warming">discussed earlier</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_1996_equity_emissions_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_1996_equity_emissions-34">[34]</a></sup> Different indicators of emissions provide different insights relevant to climate change policy, and have been an important issue in international climate change negotiations (e.g., see <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol#Negotiations" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol#Negotiations</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Developed countries' past contributions to climate change were in the process of economically developing to their current level of prosperity; developing countries are attempting to rise to this level, this being one cause of their increasing greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSRN_India_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSRN_India-36">[36]</a></sup>  Equity is an issue in GHG emissions scenarios, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sonali_P._Chitre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sonali P. Chitre (page does not exist)">Sonali P. Chitre</a> argues, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emerging_markets" title="Emerging markets">Emerging markets</a> countries, such as India and China, often would rather analyze <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_capita_emissions" title="Per capita emissions">Per capita emissions</a> instead of committing to aggregate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_reduction" title="Emissions reduction">Emissions reduction</a> because of historical contributions by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialized_nations" title="Industrialized nations">Industrialized nations</a> to the climate change crisis, under the principle of <a href="/wiki/Common_But_Differentiated_Responsibilities" title="Common But Differentiated Responsibilities">Common But Differentiated Responsibilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> For example, the scenarios used in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_First_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC First Assessment Report">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's (IPCC) First Assessment Report</a> of 1990 were criticized by Parikh (1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parikh_criticism_of_IPCC_scenarios_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-parikh_criticism_of_IPCC_scenarios-38">[38]</a></sup> Parikh (1992)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parikh_criticism_of_IPCC_scenarios_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-parikh_criticism_of_IPCC_scenarios-38">[38]</a></sup> argued that the stabilization scenarios contained in the Report "stabilize the lifestyles of the rich and adversely affect the development of the poor". The IPCC's later "<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a>" scenarios, published in 2000, explicitly explore scenarios with a narrowing income gap (<a href="/wiki/Convergence_(economics)" title="Convergence (economics)">convergence</a>) between the developed and developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  Projections of convergence in the SRES scenarios have been criticized for lacking objectivity (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defra" title="Defra">Defra</a>/<a href="/wiki/HM_Treasury" title="HM Treasury">HM Treasury</a>, 2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_projections">Emissions projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Emissions projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">Climate change scenario</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="1059" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png/220px-Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png/330px-Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png/440px-Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Projected_total_carbon_dioxide_emissions_between_2000-2100_using_the_six_illustrative_SRES_marker_scenarios.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Projected total carbon dioxide emissions between 2000–2100 using the six illustrative <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">"SRES"</a> marker scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Changes in future greenhouse gas emission levels are highly uncertain, and a wide range of quantitative emission projections have been produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Rogner <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogner_emissions_projections_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogner_emissions_projections-43">[43]</a></sup> assessed these projections. Some of these projections aggregate anthropogenic emissions into a single figure as a "<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a>" (CO<sub>2</sub>-eq). By 2030, baseline scenarios projected an increase in greenhouse emissions (the F-gases, <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and CO<sub>2</sub>, measured in CO<sub>2</sub>-eq)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> of between 25% and 90%, relative to the 2000 level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogner_emissions_projections_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-rogner_emissions_projections-43">[43]</a></sup> For CO<sub>2</sub> only, two-thirds to three-quarters of the increase in emissions was projected to come from developing countries, although the average <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Regional_and_national_attribution_of_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">per capita</a> CO<sub>2</sub> emissions in developing country regions were projected to remain substantially lower than those in developed country regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogner_emissions_projections_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-rogner_emissions_projections-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2100, CO<sub>2</sub>-eq projections ranged from a 40% reduction to an increase in emissions of 250% above their levels in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogner_emissions_projections_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-rogner_emissions_projections-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concentrations_and_temperatures">Concentrations and temperatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Concentrations and temperatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As mentioned earlier, impacts of climate change are determined more by the concentration of GHGs in the atmosphere than annual GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munasinghe_1996_cost_benefit_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-munasinghe_1996_cost_benefit-45">[45]</a></sup> Changes in the atmospheric concentrations of the individual GHGs are given in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.
</p><p>Rogner <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> reported that the then-current estimated total atmospheric concentration of long-lived GHGs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> was around 455 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts-per-million</a> (ppm) CO<sub>2</sub>-eq (range: 433-477 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq). The effects of <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land-use" title="Land-use">land-use</a> changes (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>) reduced the physical effect (the <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>) of this to 311 to 435 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq, with a central estimate of about 375 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq. The 2011 estimate of CO<sub>2</sub>-eq concentrations (the long-lived GHGs, made up of CO<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span>), <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbon</a>-12 (CFC-12), CFC-11, and fifteen other <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogenated</a> gases)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> is 473 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq (NOAA, 2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_co2eq_conc_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-butler_co2eq_conc-49">[49]</a></sup> The NOAA (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_co2eq_conc_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-butler_co2eq_conc-49">[49]</a></sup> estimate excludes the overall cooling effect of aerosols (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sulfate_aerosol" title="Sulfate aerosol">sulfate</a>).
</p><p>Six of the SRES emissions scenarios have been used to project possible future changes in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> For the six illustrative SRES scenarios, IPCC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations-50">[50]</a></sup> projected the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in the year 2100 as ranging between 540 and 970 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts-per-million</a> (ppm) . Uncertainties such as the removal of carbon from the atmosphere by "<a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">sinks</a>" (e.g., forests) increase the projected range to between 490 and 1,260 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_2001_sres_concentrations-50">[50]</a></sup> This compares to a <a href="/wiki/Pre-industrial_society" title="Pre-industrial society">pre-industrial</a> (taken as the year 1750) concentration of 280 ppm, and a concentration of 390.5 ppm in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indicative_probabilities_of_exceeding_various_increases_in_global_mean_temperature_(relative_to_the_pre-industrial_level)_for_stabilization_levels_of_400,_450,_500,_550,_650_and_750_ppmv_carbon_dioxide_equivalent.png"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="806" data-file-width="1159" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Indicative_probabilities_of_exceeding_various_increases_in_global_mean_temperature_%28relative_to_the_pre-industrial_level%29_for_stabilization_levels_of_400%2C_450%2C_500%2C_550%2C_650_and_750_ppmv_carbon_dioxide_equivalent.png/350px-thumbnail.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Indicative_probabilities_of_exceeding_various_increases_in_global_mean_temperature_%28relative_to_the_pre-industrial_level%29_for_stabilization_levels_of_400%2C_450%2C_500%2C_550%2C_650_and_750_ppmv_carbon_dioxide_equivalent.png/525px-thumbnail.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Indicative_probabilities_of_exceeding_various_increases_in_global_mean_temperature_%28relative_to_the_pre-industrial_level%29_for_stabilization_levels_of_400%2C_450%2C_500%2C_550%2C_650_and_750_ppmv_carbon_dioxide_equivalent.png/700px-thumbnail.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indicative_probabilities_of_exceeding_various_increases_in_global_mean_temperature_(relative_to_the_pre-industrial_level)_for_stabilization_levels_of_400,_450,_500,_550,_650_and_750_ppmv_carbon_dioxide_equivalent.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Indicative probabilities of exceeding various increases in global mean temperature for different stabilization levels of atmospheric GHG concentrations.</div></div></div>
<p>Atmospheric GHG concentrations can be related to changes in global mean temperature by the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_risks_of_global_warming_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-stern_risks_of_global_warming-53">[53]</a></sup> Projections of future global warming are affected by different estimates of climate sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> For a given increase in the atmospheric concentration of GHGs, high estimates of climate sensitivity suggest that relatively more future warming will occur, while low estimates of climate sensitivity suggest that relatively less future warming will occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_risks_of_global_warming_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-stern_risks_of_global_warming-53">[53]</a></sup> Lower values would correspond with less severe climate impacts, while higher values would correspond with more severe impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_climate_sensitivity_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_climate_sensitivity-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the scientific literature, there is sometimes a focus on "best estimate" or "likely" values of climate sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> However, from a <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> perspective (discussed <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Risk" title="Economics of global warming">below</a>), values outside of "likely" ranges are relevant, because, though these values are less probable, they could be associated with more severe climate impacts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_climate_sensitivity_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider_climate_sensitivity-55">[55]</a></sup> (the statistical definition of <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> = probability of an impact × magnitude of the impact).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Analysts have also looked at how uncertainty over climate sensitivity affects economic estimates of climate change impacts. Hope (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> for example, found that uncertainty over the climate sensitivity was the most important factor in determining the <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Marginal_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">social cost of carbon</a> (an economic measure of climate change impacts).
</p>
<h2><span id="Cost.E2.80.93benefit_analysis"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost–benefit_analysis">Cost–benefit analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cost–benefit analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Standard <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93benefit_analysis" title="Cost–benefit analysis">cost–benefit analysis</a> (CBA)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup> (also referred to as a monetized cost–benefit framework)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearce_monetized_cba_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-pearce_monetized_cba-60">[60]</a></sup> can be applied to the problem of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_general_issues_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_general_issues-61">[61]</a></sup> This requires (1) the valuation of costs and benefits using <a href="/wiki/Willingness_to_pay" title="Willingness to pay">willingness to pay</a> (WTP) or willingness to accept (WTA) compensation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearce_monetized_cba_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-pearce_monetized_cba-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ahmad_cost_benefit_analysis_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-ahmad_cost_benefit_analysis-64">[64]</a></sup> as a measure of value,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup> and (2) a criterion for accepting or rejecting proposals:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>For (1), in CBA where WTP/WTA is used, climate change impacts are aggregated into a monetary value,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearce_monetized_cba_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-pearce_monetized_cba-60">[60]</a></sup> with environmental impacts converted into <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> equivalents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_consumption_and_certainty_equivalents_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-arrow_consumption_and_certainty_equivalents-65">[65]</a></sup> and risk accounted for using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Certainty_equivalent" title="Certainty equivalent">certainty equivalents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_consumption_and_certainty_equivalents_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-arrow_consumption_and_certainty_equivalents-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Values over time are then discounted to produce their equivalent <a href="/wiki/Present_value" title="Present value">present values</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The valuation of costs and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benefits_of_global_warming" title="Benefits of global warming">benefits of climate change</a> can be controversial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> because some climate change impacts are difficult to assign a value to, e.g., ecosystems and human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decanio_iam_limitations_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-decanio_iam_limitations-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> It is also impossible to know the preferences of future generations, which affects the valuation of costs and benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_canio_2007_climate_change_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_canio_2007_climate_change-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> Another difficulty is quantifying the risks of future climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>For (2), the standard criterion is the (Kaldor-Hicks)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_canio_2007_climate_change_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_canio_2007_climate_change-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compensation_principle" title="Compensation principle">compensation principle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup> According to the compensation principle, so long as those benefiting from a particular project compensate the losers, and there is still something left over, then the result is an unambiguous gain in welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-4"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup> If there are no mechanisms allowing compensation to be paid, then it is necessary to assign weights to particular individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_standard_cba_59-5"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_standard_cba-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the mechanisms for compensation is impossible for this problem: mitigation might benefit future generations at the expense of current generations, but there is no way that future generations can compensate current generations for the costs of mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_canio_2007_climate_change_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-de_canio_2007_climate_change-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> On the other hand, should future generations bear most of the costs of climate change, compensation to them would not be possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_general_issues_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_general_issues-61">[61]</a></sup> Another transfer for compensation exists between regions and populations. If, for example, some countries were to benefit from future climate change but others lose out, there is no guarantee that the winners would compensate the losers;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_general_issues_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_general_issues-61">[61]</a></sup> similarly, if some countries were to benefit from reducing climate change but others lose out, there would likewise be no guarantee that the winners would compensate the losers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Cost.E2.80.93benefit_analysis_and_risk"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost–benefit_analysis_and_risk">Cost–benefit analysis and risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cost–benefit analysis and risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a cost–benefit analysis, an acceptable <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> means that the benefits of a climate policy outweigh the costs of the policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup> The standard rule used by public and private decision makers is that a risk will be acceptable if the expected <a href="/wiki/Net_(economics)" title="Net (economics)">net</a> <a href="/wiki/Present_value" title="Present value">present value</a> is positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup> The expected value is the mean of the distribution of expected outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> In other words, it is the average expected outcome for a particular decision. This criterion has been justified on the basis that:
</p>
<ul><li>a policy's benefits and costs have known <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probabilities</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-3"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_agent" title="Economic agent">economic agents</a> (people and organizations) can diversify their own risk through <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> and other markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-4"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>On the first point, probabilities for climate change are difficult to calculate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-5"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup> Also, some impacts, such as those on human health and biodiversity, are difficult to value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-6"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup> On the second point, it has been suggested that insurance could be bought against climate change risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-7"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup> In practice, however, there are difficulties in implementing the necessary policies to diversify climate change risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits_71-8"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes_measuring_costs_and_benefits-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>

This contrasts with a strategy in which no action is taken until research resolves all key uncertainties.</div></div></div>
<p>One of the problems of climate change are the large <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainties</a> over the potential impacts of climate change, and the costs and benefits of actions taken in response to climate change, e.g., in reducing GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Two related ways of thinking about the problem of climate change decision-making in the presence of uncertainty are iterative <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and sequential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">decision making</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toth_decision_uncertainty_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-toth_decision_uncertainty-78">[78]</a></sup> Considerations in a risk-based approach might include, for example, the potential for low-probability, worst-case climate change impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>An approach based on sequential decision making recognises that, over time, decisions related to climate change can be revised in the light of improved <a href="/wiki/Information_economics" title="Information economics">information</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making-9">[9]</a></sup> This is particularly important with respect to climate change, due to the long-term nature of the problem. A near-term <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedging</a> strategy concerned with reducing future climate impacts might favour stringent, near-term emissions reductions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toth_decision_uncertainty_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-toth_decision_uncertainty-78">[78]</a></sup> As stated earlier, carbon dioxide accumulates in the atmosphere, and to stabilize the atmospheric concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>, emissions would need to be drastically reduced from their present level (refer to diagram opposite).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_stablize_ghg_conc_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_stablize_ghg_conc-73">[73]</a></sup> Stringent near-term emissions reductions allow for greater future flexibility with regard to a low stabilization target, e.g., 450 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts-per-million</a> (ppm) CO<sub>2</sub>. To put it differently, stringent near-term emissions abatement can be seen as having an <a href="/wiki/Option_time_value#Option_value" title="Option time value">option value</a> in allowing for lower, long-term stabilization targets. This option may be lost if near-term emissions abatement is less stringent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the other hand, a view may be taken that points to the benefits of improved information over time. This may suggest an approach where near-term emissions abatement is more modest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Another way of viewing the problem is to look at the potential irreversibility of future climate change impacts (e.g., damages to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_ecosystems" title="Climate change and ecosystems">ecosystems</a>) against the irreversibility of making investments in efforts to reduce emissions (see also <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#Irreversible_impacts_and_policy" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">Economics of climate change mitigation#Irreversible impacts and policy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg_sequential_decision_making-9">[9]</a></sup> Overall, a range of arguments can be made in favour of policies where emissions are reduced stringently or modestly in the near-term (see: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#The_mitigation_portfolio" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">Economics of climate change mitigation#The mitigation portfolio</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resilient_and_adaptive_strategies">Resilient and adaptive strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Resilient and adaptive strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">strategic management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Operations_research" title="Operations research">operations research</a></div>
<p>Granger Morgan <i>et al.</i> (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_decision_making_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_decision_making-74">[74]</a></sup> suggested two related decision-making management strategies that might be particularly appealing when faced with high uncertainty. The first were resilient strategies. This seeks to identify a range of possible future circumstances, and then choose approaches that work reasonably well across all the range. The second were adaptive strategies. The idea here is to choose strategies that can be improved as more is learned as the future progresses. Granger Morgan <i>et al.</i> (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_decision_making_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_decision_making-74">[74]</a></sup> contrasted these two approaches with the cost–benefit approach, which seeks to find an optimal strategy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portfolio_theory">Portfolio theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Portfolio theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An example of a strategy that is based on risk is <a href="/wiki/Modern_portfolio_theory" title="Modern portfolio theory">portfolio theory</a>. This suggests that a reasonable response to uncertainty is to have a wide portfolio of possible responses. In the case of climate change, mitigation can be viewed as an effort to reduce the chance of climate change impacts (Goldemberg <i>et al.</i>, 1996, p. 24).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup> Adaptation acts as insurance against the chance that unfavourable impacts occur. The risk associated with these impacts can also be spread. As part of a policy portfolio, climate research can help when making future decisions. Technology research can help to lower future costs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optimal_choices_and_risk_aversion">Optimal choices and risk aversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Optimal choices and risk aversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#Decision_analysis" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">Economics of climate change mitigation § Decision analysis</a></div>
<p>The optimal result of <a href="/wiki/Decision_analysis" title="Decision analysis">decision analysis</a> depends on how "optimal" is defined (Arrow <i>et al.</i>, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_1996_decision_analysis_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-arrow_1996_decision_analysis-83">[83]</a></sup> See also the section on <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Trade_offs" title="Economics of global warming">trade offs</a>). Decision analysis requires a selection criterion to be specified. In a decision analysis based on monetized cost–benefit analysis (CBA), the optimal policy is evaluated in economic terms. The optimal result of monetized CBA maximizes net benefits. Another type of decision analysis is <a href="/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Cost-effectiveness analysis">cost-effectiveness analysis</a>. Cost-effectiveness analysis aims to minimize net costs.
</p><p>Monetized CBA may be used to decide on the policy objective, e.g., how much emissions should be allowed to grow over time. The benefits of emissions reductions are included as part of the assessment.
</p><p>Unlike monetized CBA, cost-effectiveness analysis does not suggest an optimal climate policy. For example, cost-effectiveness analysis may be used to determine how to stabilize atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations at lowest cost. However, the actual choice of stabilization target (e.g., 450 or 550 ppm <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a>), is not "decided" in the analysis.
</p><p>The choice of selection criterion for decision analysis is subjective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_1996_decision_analysis_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-arrow_1996_decision_analysis-83">[83]</a></sup> The choice of criterion is made outside of the analysis (it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exogenous" title="Exogenous">exogenous</a>). One of the influences on this choice on this is attitude to risk. <a href="/wiki/Risk_aversion" title="Risk aversion">Risk aversion</a> describes how willing or unwilling someone is to take risks. Evidence indicates that most, but not all, individuals prefer certain outcomes to uncertain ones. Risk-averse individuals prefer decision criteria that reduce the chance of the worst possible outcome, while risk-seeking individuals prefer decision criteria that maximize the chance of the best possible outcome. In terms of returns on investment, if society as a whole is risk-averse, we might be willing to accept some investments with negative expected returns, e.g., in mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Such investments may help to reduce the possibility of future climate damages or the costs of adaptation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_views">Alternative views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Alternative views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Reasons_for_concern" title="Reasons for concern">Reasons for concern</a></div>
<p>As stated, there is considerable uncertainty over decisions regarding climate change, as well as different attitudes over how to proceed, e.g., attitudes to risk and valuation of climate change impacts. Risk management can be used to evaluate policy decisions based a range of criteria or viewpoints, and is not restricted to the results of particular type of analysis, e.g., monetized CBA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Some authors have focused on a <i>disaggregated analysis</i> of climate change impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> "Disaggregated" refers to the choice to assess impacts in a variety of indicators or units, e.g., changes in agricultural yields and loss of biodiversity. By contrast, monetized CBA converts all impacts into a common unit (money), which is used to assess changes in <a href="/wiki/Welfare_economics" title="Welfare economics">social welfare</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_insurance">International insurance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: International insurance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditional insurance works by transferring risk to those better able or more willing to bear risk, and also by the pooling of risk (Goldemberg <i>et al.</i>, 1996, p. 25).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup> Since the risks of climate change are, to some extent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlated" title="Correlated">correlated</a>, this reduces the effectiveness of pooling. However, there is reason to believe that different regions will be affected differently by climate change. This suggests that pooling might be effective. Since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> appear to be potentially most at risk from the effects of climate change, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a> could provide insurance against these risks.
</p><p>A study carried out by David R. Easterling et al. identified societal impacts in the United States.  Losses caused by catastrophes, defined by the property insurance industry as storms causing insured losses over $5 million in the year of occurrence, have grown steadily in the United States from about $100 million annually in the 1950s to $6 billion per year in the 1990s, and the annual number of catastrophes grew from 10 in the 1950s to 35 in the 1990s.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>Authors have pointed to several reasons why <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a> insurance markets cannot adequately cover risks associated with climate change (Arrow <i>et al.</i>, 1996, p. 72).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-arrow-89">[89]</a></sup> For example, there is no international market where individuals or countries can insure themselves against losses from climate change or related climate change policies.
</p><p><b>Financial markets for risk</b>
</p><p>There are several options for how insurance could be used in responding to climate change (Arrow <i>et al.</i>, 1996, p. 72).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arrow_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-arrow-89">[89]</a></sup> One response could be to have binding agreements between countries. Countries suffering greater-than-average climate-related losses would be assisted by those suffering less-than-average losses. This would be a type of mutual insurance contract. Another approach would be to trade "risk <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a>" among countries. These securities would amount to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betting" title="Betting">betting</a> on particular climate outcomes.
</p><p>These two approaches would allow for a more efficient distribution of climate change risks. They would also allow for different beliefs over future climate outcomes. For example, it has been suggested that these markets might provide an objective test of the honesty of a particular country's beliefs over climate change. Countries that honestly believe that climate change presents little risk would be more prone to hold securities against these risks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Economic impacts of climate change">Economic impacts of climate change</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_of_impacts">Distribution of impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Distribution of impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate change impacts can be measured as an economic cost (Smith <i>et al.</i>, 2001, pp. 936–941).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith-90">[90]</a></sup> This is particularly well-suited to market impacts, that is impacts that are linked to market transactions and directly affect GDP. Monetary measures of non-market impacts, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">impacts on human health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, are more difficult to calculate. Other difficulties with impact estimates are listed below:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Knowledge gaps</b>: Calculating distributional impacts requires detailed geographical knowledge, but these are a major source of uncertainty in <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a>.</li>
<li><b>Vulnerability</b>: Compared with developed countries, there is a limited understanding of the potential <a href="/wiki/Market_sector" title="Market sector">market sector</a> impacts of climate change in developing countries.</li>
<li><b>Adaptation</b>: The future level of adaptive capacity in human and natural systems to climate change will affect how society will be impacted by climate change. Assessments may under- or overestimate adaptive capacity, leading to under- or overestimates of positive or negative impacts.</li>
<li><b>Socioeconomic trends</b>: Future predictions of development affect estimates of future climate change impacts, and in some instances, different estimates of development trends lead to a reversal from a predicted positive, to a predicted negative, impact (and <i>vice versa</i>).</li></ul>
<p>In a literature assessment, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001, pp. 957–958) concluded, with medium confidence, that:
</p>
<ul><li>climate change would increase income inequalities between and within countries.</li>
<li>a small increase in global mean temperature (up to 2 °C, measured against 1990 levels) would result in net negative market sector impacts in many developing countries and net positive market sector impacts in many developed countries.</li></ul>
<p>With high confidence, it was predicted that with a medium (2–3 °C) to high level of warming (greater than 3 °C), negative impacts would be exacerbated, and net positive impacts would start to decline and eventually turn negative.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">Water scarcity</a> due to climate change is predicted to decrease global gross domestic product (<a href="/wiki/Gross_world_product" title="Gross world product">gross world product</a>) according to a recent report by the World Bank, which predicts that this "severe hit" will spur conflict and migration across the Middle East, Central Asia, and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aggregate_impacts">Aggregate impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Aggregate impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aggregating impacts adds up the total impact of climate change across sectors and/or regions (IPCC, 2007a, p. 76).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccsr_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipccsr-92">[92]</a></sup> In producing aggregate impacts, there are a number of difficulties, such as predicting the ability of societies to adapt climate change, and estimating how future economic and social development will progress (Smith <i>et al.</i>, 2001, p. 941).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-smith-90">[90]</a></sup> It is also necessary for the researcher to make subjective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_judgement" title="Value judgement">value judgements</a> over the importance of impacts occurring in different economic sectors, in different regions, and at different times.
</p><p>Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001, p. 958) assessed the literature on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Aggregate impacts of climate change">aggregate impacts of climate change</a>. With medium confidence, they concluded that a small increase in global average temperature (up to 2 °C, measured against 1990 levels) would result in an aggregate market sector impact of plus or minus a few percent of world GDP. Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001) found that for a small to medium (2-3 °C) global average temperature increase, some studies predicted small net positive market impacts. Most studies they assessed predicted net damages beyond a medium temperature increase, with further damages for greater (more than 3 °C) temperature rises.
</p><p><b>Comparison with SRES projections</b>
</p><p>IPCC (2001, p. 74) compared their literature assessment of the aggregate market sector impacts of climate change against projections of future increases in global mean temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Temperature projections were based on the six illustrative SRES emissions scenarios. Projections for the year 2025 ranged from 0.4 to 1.1 °C. For 2050, projections ranged from 0.8 to 2.6 °C, and for 2100, 1.4 to 5.8 °C. These temperature projections correspond to atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations of 405–460 ppm for the year 2025, 445–640 ppm for 2050, and 540–970 ppm for 2100.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_and_vulnerability">Adaptation and vulnerability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Adaptation and vulnerability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation to global warming</a></div>
<p>IPCC (2007a) defined adaptation (to climate change) as "[initiatives] and measures to reduce the vulnerability of natural and human systems against actual or expected climate change effects" (p. 76).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccsr_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipccsr-92">[92]</a></sup> Vulnerability (to climate change) was defined as "the degree to which a system is susceptible to, and unable to cope with, adverse effects of climate change, including climate variability and extremes" (p. 89).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomous_and_planned_adaptation">Autonomous and planned adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Autonomous and planned adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Autonomous adaptation are adaptations that are reactive to climatic stimuli, and are done as a matter of course without the intervention of a public agency. Planned adaptation can be reactive or anticipatory, i.e., undertaken before impacts are apparent. Some studies suggest that human systems have considerable capacity to adapt autonomously (Smit <i>et al.</i>, 2001:890).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smit_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-smit-94">[94]</a></sup> Others point to constraints on autonomous adaptation, such as limited information and access to resources (p. 890). Smit <i>et al.</i> (2001:904) concluded that relying on autonomous adaptation to climate change would result in substantial ecological, social, and economic costs. In their view, these costs could largely be avoided with planned adaptation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_and_benefits">Costs and benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Costs and benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A literature assessment by Adger <i>et al.</i> (2007:719) concluded that there was a lack of comprehensive, global cost and benefit estimates for adaptation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adger_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-adger-95">[95]</a></sup> Studies were noted that provided cost estimates of adaptation at regional level, e.g., for sea-level rise. A number of adaptation measures were identified as having high benefit-cost ratios.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptive_capacity">Adaptive capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Adaptive capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adaptive capacity is the ability of a system to adjust to climate change. Smit <i>et al.</i> (2001:895–897) described the determinants of adaptive capacity:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smit_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-smit-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Economic resources</b>: Wealthier nations are better able to bear the costs of adaptation to climate change than poorer ones.</li>
<li><b>Technology</b>: Lack of technology can impede adaptation.</li>
<li><b>Information and skills</b>: Information and trained personnel are required to assess and implement successful adaptation options.</li>
<li><b>Social infrastructure</b></li>
<li><b>Institutions</b>: Nations with well-developed social <a href="/wiki/Institutional_economics" title="Institutional economics">institutions</a> are believed to have greater adaptive capacity than those with less effective institutions, typically developing nations and economies in transition.</li>
<li><b>Equity</b>: Some believe that adaptive capacity is greater where there are government institutions and arrangements in place that allow equitable access to resources.</li></ul>
<p>Smit <i>et al.</i> (2001) concluded that:
</p>
<ul><li>countries with limited economic resources, low levels of technology, poor information and skills, poor infrastructure, unstable or weak institutions, and inequitable empowerment and access to resources have little adaptive capacity and are highly vulnerable to climate change (p. 879).</li>
<li>developed nations, broadly speaking, have greater adaptive capacity than developing regions or countries in economic transition (p. 897).</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancing_adaptive_capacity">Enhancing adaptive capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Enhancing adaptive capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Smit <i>et al.</i> (2001:905) concluded that enhanced adaptive capacity would reduce vulnerability to climate change. In their view, activities that enhance adaptive capacity are essentially equivalent to activities that promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smit_94-2"><a href="#cite_note-smit-94">[94]</a></sup> These activities include (p. 899):
</p>
<ul><li>improving access to resources</li>
<li>reducing <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a></li>
<li>lowering inequities of resources and wealth among groups</li>
<li>improving <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and information</li>
<li>improving infrastructure</li>
<li>improving institutional capacity and efficiency</li></ul>
<p>Goklany (1995) concluded that promoting free trade – e.g., through the removal of international trade barriers –  could enhance adaptive capacity and contribute to economic growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With high confidence, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001:957–958) concluded that developing countries would tend to be more vulnerable to climate change than developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_90-2"><a href="#cite_note-smith-90">[90]</a></sup> Based on then-current development trends, Smith <i>et al.</i> (2001:940–941) predicted that few developing countries would have the capacity to efficiently adapt to climate change.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Africa</b>: In a literature assessment, Boko <i>et al.</i> (2007:435) concluded, with high confidence, that Africa's major economic sectors had been vulnerable to observed climate variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> This vulnerability was judged to have contributed to Africa's weak adaptive capacity, resulting in Africa having high vulnerability to future climate change. It was thought likely that projected sea-level rise would increase the socio-economic vulnerability of African coastal cities.</li>
<li><b>Asia</b>: Lal <i>et al.</i> (2001:536) reviewed the literature on adaptation and vulnerability. With medium confidence, they concluded that climate change would result in the degradation of <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> in boreal Asia, worsening the vulnerability of climate-dependent sectors, and affecting the region's economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Australia and New Zealand</b>: Hennessy <i>et al.</i> (2007:509) reviewed the literature on adaptation and vulnerability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> With high confidence, they concluded that in Australia and New Zealand, most human systems had considerable adaptive capacity. With medium confidence, some Indigenous communities were judged to have low adaptive capacity.</li>
<li><b>Europe</b>: In a literature assessment, Kundzewicz <i>et al.</i> (2001:643) concluded, with very high confidence, that the adaptation potential of socioeconomic systems in Europe was relatively high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> This was attributed to Europe's high GNP, stable growth, stable population, and well-developed political, institutional, and technological support systems.</li>
<li><b>Latin America</b>: In a literature assessment, Mata <i>et al.</i> (2001:697) concluded that the adaptive capacity of socioeconomic systems in Latin America was very low, particularly in regard to extreme weather events, and that the region's vulnerability was high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Polar regions</b>: Anisimov <i>et al.</i> (2001, pp. 804–805) concluded that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
<ul><li>within the Antarctic and Arctic, at localities where water was close to melting point, socioeconomic systems were particularly vulnerable to climate change.</li>
<li>the Arctic would be extremely vulnerable to climate change. Anisimov <i>et al.</i> (2001) predicted that there would be major ecological, sociological, and economic impacts in the region.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Small islands</b>: Mimura <i>et al.</i> (2007, p. 689) concluded, with very high confidence, that small islands were particularly vulnerable to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> Partly this was attributed to their low <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a> and the high costs of adaptation in proportion to their GDP.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_and_sectors">Systems and sectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Systems and sectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Coasts and low-lying areas</b>: According to Nicholls <i>et al.</i> (2007, p. 336), societal vulnerability to climate change is largely dependent on development status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Developing countries lack the necessary financial resources to relocate those living in low-lying coastal zones, making them more vulnerable to climate change than developed countries. With high confidence, Nicholls <i>et al.</i> (2007, p. 317) concluded that on vulnerable coasts, the costs of adapting to climate change are lower than the potential damage costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Industry, settlements and society</b>:
<ul><li>At the scale of a large nation or region, at least in most <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialized</a> economies, the economic value of sectors with low vulnerability to climate change greatly exceeds that of sectors with high vulnerability (Wilbanks <i>et al.</i>, 2007, p. 366).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Additionally, the capacity of a large, complex economy to absorb climate-related impacts, is often considerable. Consequently, estimates of the aggregate damages of climate change – ignoring possible abrupt climate change – are often rather small as a percentage of economic production. On the other hand, at smaller scales, e.g., for a small country, sectors and societies might be highly vulnerable to climate change. Potential climate change impacts might therefore amount to very severe damages.</li>
<li>Wilbanks <i>et al.</i> (2007, p. 359) concluded, with very high confidence, that vulnerability to climate change depends considerably on specific geographic, sectoral and social contexts. In their view, these vulnerabilities are not reliably estimated by large-scale aggregate modelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">economics of climate change mitigation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">low-carbon economy</a></div>
<p>Mitigation of climate change involves actions that are designed to limit the amount of long-term climate change (Fisher <i>et al.</i>, 2007:225).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fisher_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-108">[108]</a></sup> Mitigation may be achieved through the reduction of GHG emissions or through the enhancement of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">sinks</a> that absorb GHGs, e.g., forests.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_public_goods">International public goods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: International public goods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The atmosphere is an international <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a>, and GHG emissions are an international <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a> (Goldemberg <i>et al.</i>, 1996:21, 28, 43).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-3"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup> A change in the quality of the atmosphere does not affect the welfare of all individuals equally. In other words, some individuals may benefit from climate change, while others may lose out. This uneven distribution of potential climate change impacts, plus the uneven distribution of emissions globally, make it difficult to secure a global agreement to reduce emissions (Halsnæs <i>et al.</i>, 2007:127).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsnaes_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-halsnaes-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National">National</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: National">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Both climate and non-climate policies can affect emissions growth. Non-climate policies that can affect emissions are listed below (Bashmakov <i>et al.</i>, 2001:409-410):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bashmakov_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-bashmakov-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Market-orientated reforms</b> can have important impacts on energy use, energy efficiency, and therefore GHG emissions.</li>
<li><b>Price and subsidy policies</b>: Many countries provide <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidies</a> for activities that impact emissions, e.g., subsidies in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agriculture_subsidy" title="Agriculture subsidy">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_subsidies" title="Energy subsidies">energy</a> sectors, and indirect subsidies for transport.</li>
<li><b>Market liberalization</b>: Restructuring of <a href="/wiki/Energy_market" title="Energy market">energy markets</a> has occurred in several countries and regions. These policies have mainly been designed to increase competition in the market, but they can have a significant impact on emissions.</li></ul>
<p>There are a number of policies that might be used to mitigate climate change, including *The Green Marshall Plan (<a class="external free" href="http://nb.referata.com/w/index.php?title=Green_Marshall_Plan&amp;action=edit" rel="nofollow">http://nb.referata.com/w/index.php?title=Green_Marshall_Plan&amp;action=edit</a>)  which calls for global central bank money creation to fund green infrastructure,
(Bashmakov <i>et al.</i>, 2001:412–422):
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">Regulatory</a> standards, such as fuel-efficiency standards for cars (Creutzig et al., 2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></li>
<li>Market-based instruments, such as emissions taxes and tradable permits.</li>
<li>Voluntary agreements between public agencies and industry.</li>
<li>Informational instruments, e.g., to increase public awareness of climate change.</li>
<li>Use of subsidies and financial incentives, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">feed-in tariffs</a> for renewable energy (Gupta <i>et al.</i>, 2007:762).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></li>
<li>Removal of subsidies, e.g., for coal mining and burning (Barker <i>et al.</i>, 2001:567–568).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-barker-113">[113]</a></sup></li>
<li>Demand-side management, which aims to reduce energy demand through energy audits, product labelling, etc.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> to the UNFCCC sets out legally binding emission reduction commitments for the "Annex B" countries (Verbruggen, 2007, p. 817).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> The Protocol defines three international policy instruments ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexibility_mechanisms" title="Flexibility mechanisms">Flexibility Mechanisms</a>") which can be used by the Annex B countries to meet their emission reduction commitments. According to Bashmakov <i>et al.</i> (2001:402), use of these instruments could significantly reduce the costs for Annex B countries in meeting their emission reduction commitments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bashmakov_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-bashmakov-110">[110]</a></sup></li>
<li>Other possible policies include internationally coordinated <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon taxes</a> and/or regulation (Bashmakov <i>et al.</i>, 2001:430).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finance">Finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> estimates that US$197 billion is required by states in the developing world above and beyond the underlying investments needed by various sectors regardless of climate considerations, this is twice the amount promised by the developed world at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UN_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="UN Framework Convention on Climate Change">UN Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) Cancún Agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-115">[115]</a></sup> Thus, a new method is being developed to help ensure that funding is available for climate change mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-115">[115]</a></sup> This involves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_leverage" title="Financial leverage">financial leveraging</a>, whereby public financing is used to encourage private investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI1_115-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODI1-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_estimates">Cost estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Cost estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a literature assessment by Barker <i>et al.</i> (2007b:622), mitigation cost estimates depend critically on the baseline (in this case, a reference scenario that the alternative scenario is compared with), the way costs are modelled, and assumptions about future government policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker2007_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-barker2007-116">[116]</a></sup> Fisher <i>et al.</i> (2007:204–206)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fisher_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-fisher-108">[108]</a></sup> (summarized by IPCC, 2007b:11)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg3_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-wg3-117">[117]</a></sup> estimated macroeconomic costs in 2030 for multi-gas mitigation (reducing emissions of carbon dioxide and other GHGs, such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>) as between a 3% decrease in global GDP to a small increase, relative to baseline. This was for an emissions pathway consistent with atmospheric stabilization of GHGs between 445 and 710 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq. In 2050, the estimated costs for stabilization between 710 and 445 ppm CO<sub>2</sub>-eq ranged between a 1% gain to a 5.5% decrease in global GDP, relative to baseline. These cost estimates were supported by a moderate amount of evidence and much agreement in the literature (IPCC, 2007b:11,18).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg3_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-wg3-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Macroeconomic cost estimates made by Fisher <i>et al.</i> (2007:204) were mostly based on models that assumed transparent markets, no transaction costs, and perfect implementation of cost-effective policy measures across all regions throughout the 21st century. According to Fisher <i>et al.</i> (2007), relaxation of some or all these assumptions would lead to an appreciable increase in cost estimates. On the other hand, IPCC (2007b:8)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg3_117-2"><a href="#cite_note-wg3-117">[117]</a></sup> noted that cost estimates could be reduced by allowing for accelerated technological learning, or the possible use of carbon tax/emission permit revenues to reform national tax systems.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Regional costs</b> were estimated as possibly being significantly different from the global average. Regional costs were found to be largely dependent on the assumed stabilization level and baseline scenario.</li>
<li><b>Sectoral costs</b>: In a literature assessment, Barker <i>et al.</i> (2001:563–564), predicted that the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewables</a> sector could potentially benefit from mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker_113-1"><a href="#cite_note-barker-113">[113]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> (and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil</a>) industry was predicted to potentially lose substantial proportions of output relative to a baseline scenario, with energy-intensive sectors, such as heavy <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemicals</a>, facing higher costs.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_and_mitigation">Adaptation and mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Adaptation and mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The distribution of benefits from adaptation and mitigation policies are different in terms of damages avoided (Toth <i>et al.</i>, 2001:653).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toth_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-toth-118">[118]</a></sup> Adaptation activities mainly benefit those who implement them, while mitigation benefits others who may not have made mitigation investments. Mitigation can therefore be viewed as a global public good, while adaptation is either a <a href="/wiki/Private_good" title="Private good">private good</a> in the case of autonomous adaptation, or a national or regional public good in the case of public sector policies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paying_for_an_international_public_good">Paying for an international public good</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Paying for an international public good">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Economists generally agree on the following two principles (Goldemberg, <i>et al.</i>., 1996:29):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-4"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>For the purposes of analysis, it is possible to separate equity from efficiency. This implies that all emitters, regardless of whether they are rich or poor, should pay the full social costs of their actions. From this perspective, corrective (Pigouvian) taxes should be applied uniformly (see <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax#Economic_theory" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax#Economic theory</a>).</li>
<li>It is inappropriate to redress all equity issues through climate change policies. However, climate change itself should not aggravate existing inequalities between different regions.</li></ul>
<p>Some early studies suggested that a uniform carbon tax would be a fair and efficient way of reducing emissions (Banuri <i>et al.</i>, 1996, pp. 103–104).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equity_and_Social_Considerations_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Equity_and_Social_Considerations-119">[119]</a></sup> A carbon tax is a Pigouvian tax, and taxes fuels based on their carbon content (Hoeller and Wallin, 1991, p. 92).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> A literature assessment by Banuri <i>et al.</i> (1996:103–104)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equity_and_Social_Considerations_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Equity_and_Social_Considerations-119">[119]</a></sup> summarized criticisms of such a system:
</p>
<ul><li>A carbon tax would impose different burdens on countries due to existing differences in tax structures, resource endowments, and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equity_and_Social_Considerations_119-2"><a href="#cite_note-Equity_and_Social_Considerations-119">[119]</a></sup></li>
<li>Most observers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quote_from_Banuri_et_al_1996_on_a_uniform_carbon_tax_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quote_from_Banuri_et_al_1996_on_a_uniform_carbon_tax-121">[121]</a></sup> argue that such a tax would not be fair because of differences in historical emissions and current wealth.</li>
<li>A uniform carbon tax would not be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pareto_efficient" title="Pareto efficient">Pareto efficient</a> unless lump sum transfers were made between countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equity_and_Social_Considerations_119-3"><a href="#cite_note-Equity_and_Social_Considerations-119">[119]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pareto_efficiency" title="Pareto efficiency">Pareto efficiency</a> requires that the carbon tax would not make any countries worse off than they would be without the tax (Chichilnisky and Heal, 1994, p. 445;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> Tol, 2001, p. 72).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> Also, at least one country would need to be better off.</li></ul>
<p>An alternative approach to having a Pigouvian tax is one based on property rights. A practical example of this would be a system of emissions trading, which is essentially a privatization of the atmosphere (Hepburn, 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> The idea of using property rights in response to an externality was put forward by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coase" title="Coase">Coase</a> (1960). Coase's model of social cost assumes a situation of equal bargaining power among participants and equal costs of making the bargain (Toth <i>et al.</i>., 2001:668).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toth_118-1"><a href="#cite_note-toth-118">[118]</a></sup> Assigning property rights can be an efficient solution. This is based on the assumption that there are no bargaining/transaction costs involved in buying or selling these property rights, and that buyers and sellers have perfect information available when making their decisions.
</p><p>If these assumptions are correct, efficiency is achieved regardless of how property rights are allocated. In the case of emissions trading, this suggests that equity and efficiency can be addressed separately: equity is taken care of in the allocation of emission permits, and efficiency is promoted by the market system. In reality, however, markets do not live up to the ideal conditions that are assumed in Coase's model, with the result that there may be trade-offs between efficiency and equity (Halsnæs <i>et al.</i>, 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency_and_equity">Efficiency and equity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Efficiency and equity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>No scientific consensus exists on who should bear the burden of adaptation and mitigation costs (Goldemberg <i>et al.</i>., 1996:29).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldemberg_72-5"><a href="#cite_note-goldemberg-72">[72]</a></sup> Several different arguments have been made over how to spread the costs and benefits of taxes or systems based on emissions trading.
</p><p>One approach considers the problem from the perspective of who benefits most from the public good. This approach is sensitive to the fact that different preferences exist between different income classes. The public good is viewed in a similar way as a private good, where those who use the public good must pay for it. Some people will benefit more from the public good than others, thus creating inequalities in the absence of benefit taxes. A difficulty with public goods is determining who exactly benefits from the public good, although some estimates of the distribution of the costs and benefits of global warming have been made – <a href="#Distribution_of_impacts">see above</a>. Additionally, this approach does not provide guidance as to how the surplus of benefits from climate policy should be shared.
</p><p>A second approach has been suggested based on economics and the <a href="/wiki/Social_welfare_function" title="Social welfare function">social welfare function</a>. To calculate the social welfare function requires an aggregation of the impacts of climate change policies and climate change itself across all affected individuals. This calculation involves a number of complexities and controversial equity issues (Markandya <i>et al.</i>, 2001:460).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> For example, the monetization of certain impacts on human health. There is also controversy over the issue of benefits affecting one individual offsetting negative impacts on another (Smith <i>et al.</i>., 2001:958).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_90-3"><a href="#cite_note-smith-90">[90]</a></sup> These issues to do with equity and aggregation cannot be fully resolved by economics (Banuri <i>et al.</i>., 1996:87).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Equity_and_Social_Considerations_119-4"><a href="#cite_note-Equity_and_Social_Considerations-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>On a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a> basis, which has traditionally been used in welfare economics, an argument can be made for richer countries taking on most of the burdens of mitigation (Halsnæs <i>et al.</i>, 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> However, another result is possible with a different modeling of impacts. If an approach is taken where the interests of poorer people have lower weighting, the result is that there is a much weaker argument in favour of mitigation action in rich countries. Valuing climate change impacts in poorer countries less than domestic climate change impacts (both in terms of policy and the impacts of climate change) would be consistent with observed spending in rich countries on foreign aid (Hepburn, 2005;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> Helm, 2008:229).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of the social welfare function, the different results depend on the elasticity of marginal utility. A declining marginal utility of consumption means that a poor person is judged to benefit more from increases in consumption relative to a richer person. A constant marginal utility of consumption does not make this distinction, and leads to the result that richer countries should mitigate less.
</p><p>A third approach looks at the problem from the perspective of who has contributed most to the problem. Because the industrialized countries have contributed more than two-thirds of the stock of human-induced GHGs in the atmosphere, this approach suggests that they should bear the largest share of the costs. This stock of emissions has been described as an "environmental debt" (Munasinghe <i>et al.</i>, 1996, p. 167).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> In terms of efficiency, this view is not supported. This is because efficiency requires incentives to be forward-looking, and not retrospective (Goldemberg et al., 1996, p. 29). The question of historical responsibility is a matter of <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>. Munasinghe <i>et al.</i> (1996, p. 167) suggested that developed countries could address the issue by making side-payments to developing countries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_offs">Trade offs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Trade offs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Integrated_assessment_modelling" title="Integrated assessment modelling">integrated assessment modelling</a></div>
<p>It is often argued in the literature that there is a trade-off between adaptation and mitigation, in that the resources committed to one are not available for the other (Schneider <i>et al.</i>, 2001:94).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> This is debatable in practice because the people who bear emission reduction costs or benefits are often different from those who pay or benefit from adaptation measures.
</p><p>There is also a trade off in how much damage from climate change should be avoided. The assumption that it is always possible to trade off different outcomes is viewed as problematic by many people (Halsnæs <i>et al.</i>, 2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> For example, a trade off might exist between economic growth and damages faced by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a>.
</p><p>Some of the literature has pointed to difficulties in these kinds of assumptions. For instance, there may be aversion at any price towards losing particular species. It has also been suggested that low-probability, extreme outcomes are overweighted when making choices. This is related to climate change, since the possibility of future <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt changes</a> in the climate or the Earth system cannot be ruled out. For example, if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheet</a> was to disintegrate, it could result in a sea level rise of 4–6 meters over several centuries.
</p><p><b>Cost–benefit analysis</b>
</p><p>In a cost–benefit analysis, the trade offs between climate change impacts, adaptation, and mitigation are made explicit. Cost–benefit analyses of climate change are produced using integrated assessment models (IAMs), which incorporate aspects of the natural, social, and economic sciences.
</p><p>In an IAM designed for cost–benefit analysis, the costs and benefits of impacts, adaptation and mitigation are converted into monetary estimates. Some view the monetization of costs and benefits as controversial (see <a href="/wiki/Economic_impacts_of_climate_change#Aggregate_impacts" title="Economic impacts of climate change">Economic impacts of climate change#Aggregate impacts</a>). The "optimal" levels of mitigation and adaptation are then resolved by comparing the marginal costs of action with the marginal benefits of avoided climate change damages (Toth <i>et al.</i>, 2001:654).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toth_118-2"><a href="#cite_note-toth-118">[118]</a></sup> The decision over what "optimal" is depends on subjective value judgements made by the author of the study (Azar, 1998).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many uncertainties that affect cost–benefit analysis, for example, sector- and country-specific damage functions (Toth <i>et al.</i>, 2001:654). Another example is with adaptation. The options and costs for adaptation are largely unknown, especially in developing countries.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A common finding of cost–benefit analysis is that the optimum level of emissions reduction is modest in the near-term, with more stringent abatement in the longer-term (Stern, 2007:298;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Heal, 2008:20;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> Barker, 2008).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barker2008_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-barker2008-136">[136]</a></sup> This approach might lead to a warming of more than 3 °C above the pre-industrial level (<a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, 2010:8).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> In most models, benefits exceed costs for stabilization of GHGs leading to warming of 2.5 °C. No models suggest that the optimal policy is to do nothing, i.e., allow "business-as-usual" emissions.
</p><p>Along the efficient emission path calculated by <a href="/wiki/William_Nordhaus" title="William Nordhaus">Nordhaus</a> and Boyer (2000) (referred to by Fisher <i>et al.</i>., 2007), the long-run global average temperature after 500 years increases by 6.2 °C above the 1900 level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> Nordhaus and Boyer (2000) stated their concern over the potentially large and uncertain impacts of such a large environmental change. It should be noted that the projected temperature in this IAM, like any other, is subject to scientific uncertainty (e.g., the relationship between concentrations of GHGs and global mean temperature, which is called the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>). Projections of future atmospheric concentrations based on emission pathways are also affected by scientific uncertainties, e.g., over how carbon sinks, such as forests, will be affected by future climate change. Klein <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that there were few high quality studies in this area, and placed low confidence in the results of cost–benefit analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hof <i>et al.</i> (2008) (referred to by <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, 2010:8) examined the sensitivity of the optimal climate target to assumptions about the time horizon, climate sensitivity, mitigation costs, likely damages, and discount rates. The optimal target was defined as the concentration that would result in the lowest reduction in the present value (i.e., discounted) of global consumption. A set of assumptions that included a relatively high climate sensitivity (i.e., a relatively large global temperature increase for a given increase in GHGs), high damages, a long time horizon, low discount rates (i.e., future consumption is valued relatively highly), and low mitigation costs, produced an optimum peak in the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>e at 540 parts per million (ppm). Another set of assumptions that assumed a lower climate sensitivity (lower global temperature increase), lower damages, a shorter time horizon, and a higher discount rate (present consumption is valued relatively more highly), produced an optimum peaking at 750 ppm.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Strengths">Strengths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Strengths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In spite of various uncertainties or possible criticisms of cost–benefit analysis, it does have several strengths:
</p>
<ul><li>It offers an internally consistent and global comprehensive analysis of impacts (Smith <i>et al.</i>, 2001:955).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith_90-4"><a href="#cite_note-smith-90">[90]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sensitivity analysis allows critical assumptions in the analysis to be changed. This can identify areas where the value of information is highest and where additional research might have the highest payoffs (Downing, <i>et al.</i>, 2001:119).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup></li>
<li>As uncertainty is reduced, the integrated models used in producing cost–benefit analysis might become more realistic and useful.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geoengineering">Geoengineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Geoengineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Geoengineering</a> are technological efforts to stabilize the climate system by direct intervention in the Earth-atmosphere-system's energy balance (Verbruggen, 2007, p. 815).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> The intent of geoengineering is to reduce the amount of global warming (the observed trend of increased global average temperature (NRC, 2008, p. 2)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> IPCC (2007b:15) concluded that reliable cost estimates for geoengineering options had not been published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg3_117-3"><a href="#cite_note-wg3-117">[117]</a></sup> This finding was based on medium agreement in the literature and limited evidence.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_reports_considering_economics_of_climate_change">Major reports considering economics of climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Major reports considering economics of climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) has produced several reports where the economics literature on climate change is assessed. In 1995, the IPCC produced its <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">second</a> set of assessment reports on climate change. Working Group III of the IPCC produced a report on the "Economic and Social Dimensions of Climate Change." In the later <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">third</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">fourth</a> IPCC assessments, published in 2001 and 2007 respectively, the assessment of the economics literature is divided across two reports produced by IPCC Working Groups II and III. In 2011 IPCC Working Group III published a Special Report on <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Sources_and_Climate_Change_Mitigation" title="Renewable Energy Sources and Climate Change Mitigation">Renewable Energy Sources and Climate Change Mitigation</a>.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stern_review" title="Stern review">Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change</a> is a 700-page report released for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" title="Her Majesty's Government">British government</a> on 30 October 2006, by economist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_Stern" title="Nicholas Stern">Nicholas Stern</a>, chair of the <a href="/wiki/Grantham_Research_Institute_on_Climate_Change_and_the_Environment" title="Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment">Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment</a> at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a>. The report discusses the effect of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> on the world economy.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Garnaut_Climate_Change_Review" title="Garnaut Climate Change Review">Garnaut Climate Change Review</a> was a study by Professor <a href="/wiki/Ross_Garnaut" title="Ross Garnaut">Ross Garnaut</a>, commissioned by then Opposition Leader,  <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Rudd" title="Kevin Rudd">Kevin Rudd</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> and by the Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States_and_Territories_of_Australia" title="States and Territories of Australia">State and Territory</a> Governments on 30 April 2007. After his election on 24 November 2007 <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister of Australia</a> <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Rudd" title="Kevin Rudd">Kevin Rudd</a> confirmed the participation of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">Commonwealth Government</a> in the Review.
</p><p>A report by the United Nations Environment Program and the World Trade Organization "provides an overview of the key linkages between trade and climate change based on a review of available literature and a survey of relevant national policies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p>
