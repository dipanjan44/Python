<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental ethics</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the academic journal, see <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Ethics_(journal)" title="Environmental Ethics (journal)">Environmental Ethics (journal)</a>.</div>

<p><b>Environmental ethics</b> is the part of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_philosophy" title="Environmental philosophy">environmental philosophy</a> which considers extending the traditional boundaries of ethics from solely including humans to including the non-human world. It exerts influence on a large range of disciplines including <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sociology" title="Environmental sociology">environmental sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecotheology" title="Ecotheology">ecotheology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_geography" title="Integrated geography">environmental geography</a>.
</p><p>There are many ethical decisions that human beings make with respect to the environment. For example:
</p>
<ul><li>Should humans continue to <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clear cut</a> forests for the sake of human consumption?</li>
<li>Why should humans continue to propagate its species, and life itself? <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bioethics_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bioethics-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Should humans continue to make <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">gasoline-powered vehicles</a>?</li>
<li>What environmental obligations do humans need to keep for future generations?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>Is it right for humans to  knowingly cause the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of a species for the convenience of humanity?</li>
<li>How should humans best use and conserve the space environment to secure and expand life?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeding_Book_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seeding_Book-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>What role can Planetary Boundaries play in reshaping the human-earth relationship?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The academic field of environmental ethics grew up in response to the work of scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a> and events such as the first <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a> in 1970, when environmentalists started urging philosophers to consider the philosophical aspects of environmental problems. Two papers published in <i>Science</i> had a crucial impact: <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Townsend_White_Jr." title="Lynn Townsend White Jr.">Lynn White</a>'s "The Historical Roots of our Ecologic Crisis" (March 1967)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Hardin" title="Garrett Hardin">Garrett Hardin</a>'s "The Tragedy of the Commons" (December 1968).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Also influential was Garett Hardin's later essay called "Exploring New Ethics for Survival", as well as an essay by <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/A_Sand_County_Almanac" title="A Sand County Almanac">A Sand County Almanac</a></i>, called "The Land Ethic," in which Leopold explicitly claimed that the roots of the ecological crisis were philosophical (1949).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first international academic journals in this field emerged from North America in the late 1970s and early 1980s – the US-based journal <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Ethics_(journal)" title="Environmental Ethics (journal)">Environmental Ethics</a></i> in 1979 and the Canadian-based journal <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Trumpeter:_Journal_of_Ecosophy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Trumpeter: Journal of Ecosophy (page does not exist)">The Trumpeter: Journal of Ecosophy</a></i> in 1983. The first British based journal of this kind, <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Values" title="Environmental Values">Environmental Values</a></i>, was launched in 1992.
</p>

<h2><span id="Marshall.27s_categories"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Marshall's_categories">Marshall's categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Marshall's categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some scholars have tried to categorise the various ways the natural environment is valued. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Marshall_(New_Zealand_author)" title="Alan Marshall (New Zealand author)">Alan Marshall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_A._Smith" title="Michael A. Smith">Michael Smith</a> are two examples of this, as cited by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Vardy_(theologian)" title="Peter Vardy (theologian)">Peter Vardy</a> in "The Puzzle of Ethics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> According to Marshall, three general ethical approaches have emerged over the last 40 years: Libertarian Extension, the Ecologic Extension and Conservation Ethics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libertarian_extension">Libertarian extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Libertarian extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marshall’s Libertarian extension echoes a civil liberty approach (i.e. a commitment to extend equal rights to all members of a community). In environmentalism, though, the community is generally thought to consist of non-humans as well as humans.
</p><p>Andrew Brennan was an advocate of ecologic humanism (eco-humanism), the argument that all ontological entities, animate and in-animate, can be given ethical worth purely on the basis that they exist. The work of <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a> and his collaborator Sessions also falls under the libertarian extension, although they preferred the term "<a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a>". Deep ecology is the argument for the intrinsic value or inherent worth of the environment – the view that it is valuable in itself. Their argument, incidentally, falls under both the libertarian extension and the ecologic extension.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Singer" title="Peter Singer">Peter Singer</a>'s work can be categorized under Marshall's 'libertarian extension'. He reasoned that the "expanding circle of moral worth" should be redrawn to include the rights of non-human animals, and to not do so would be guilty of <a href="/wiki/Speciesism" title="Speciesism">speciesism</a>. Singer found it difficult to accept the argument from intrinsic worth of a-biotic or "non-sentient" (non-conscious) entities, and concluded in his first edition of "Practical Ethics" that they should not be included in the expanding circle of moral worth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This approach is essentially then, bio-centric. However, in a later edition of "Practical Ethics" after the work of Næss and Sessions, Singer admits that, although unconvinced by deep ecology, the argument from intrinsic value of non-sentient entities is plausible, but at best problematic. Singer advocated a humanist ethics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecologic_extension">Ecologic extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecologic extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alan Marshall's category of ecologic extension places emphasis not on
human rights but on the recognition of the fundamental interdependence 
of all biological (and some abiological) entities and their essential 
diversity. Whereas Libertarian Extension can be thought of as flowing 
from a political reflection of the natural world, ecologic extension is 
best thought of as a scientific reflection of the natural world. 
Ecological Extension is roughly the same classification of Smith's 
eco-holism, and it argues for the intrinsic value inherent in collective
ecological entities like ecosystems or the global environment as a 
whole entity. Holmes Rolston, among others, has taken this approach.
</p><p>This category might include <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia hypothesis</a>; the theory that the planet earth alters its geo-physiological structure over time in order to ensure the continuation of an equilibrium of evolving organic and inorganic matter. The planet is characterized as a unified, <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> entity with ethical worth of which the human race is of no particular significance in the long run.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_ethics">Conservation ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marshall's category of 'conservation ethics' is an extension of use-value into the non-human biological world. It focuses only on the worth of the environment in terms of its utility or usefulness to humans. It contrasts the intrinsic value ideas of 'deep ecology', hence is often referred to as 'shallow ecology', and generally argues for the preservation of the environment on the basis that it has extrinsic value – instrumental to the welfare of human beings. Conservation is therefore a means to an end and purely concerned with mankind and inter-generational considerations. It could be argued that it is this ethic that formed the underlying arguments proposed by Governments at the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto summit</a> in 1997 and three agreements reached in Rio in 1992.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humanist_theories">Humanist theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Humanist theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peter Singer advocated the preservation of "world heritage sites," unspoilt parts of the world that acquire a "scarcity value" as they diminish over time. Their preservation is a bequest for future generations as they have been inherited from human's ancestors and should be passed down to future generations so they can have the opportunity to decide whether to enjoy unspoilt countryside or an entirely urban landscape. A good example of a world heritage site would be the tropical rainforest, a very specialist ecosystem that has taken centuries to evolve. Clearing the rainforest for farmland often fails due to soil conditions, and once disturbed, can take thousands of years to regenerate.
</p>

<p>The Christian world view sees the universe as created by God, and humankind accountable to God for the use of the resources entrusted to humankind. Ultimate values are seen in the light of being valuable to God.  This applies both in breadth of scope -  caring for people (Matthew 25) and environmental issues, e.g. environmental health (Deuteronomy 22.8; 23.12-14) - and dynamic motivation,  the love of Christ controlling (2 Corinthians 5.14f) and dealing with the underlying spiritual disease of sin, which shows itself in selfishness and thoughtlessness. In many countries this relationship of accountability is symbolised at harvest thanksgiving. (B.T. Adeney : Global Ethics in New Dictionary of Christian Ethics and Pastoral Theology 1995 Leicester)
</p><p>Abrahamic religious scholars have used theology to motivate the public. <a href="/wiki/John_L._O%27Sullivan" title="John L. O'Sullivan">John L. O'Sullivan</a>, who coined the term <a href="/wiki/Manifest_destiny" title="Manifest destiny">Manifest destiny</a>, and other influential people like him used Abrahamic ideologies to encourage action. These religious scholars, columnists and politicians historically have used these ideas and continue to do so to justify the consumptive tendencies of a young America around the time of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. In order to solidify the understanding that God had intended for humankind to use earths natural resources, environmental writers and religious scholars alike proclaimed that humans are separate from nature, on a higher order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Those that may critique this point of view may ask the same question that John Muir asks ironically in a section of his novel <i>A Thousand Mile Walk to the Gulf</i>, <i>why are there so many dangers in the natural world in the form of poisonous plants, animals and natural disasters</i>, The answer is that those creatures are a result of <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a>'s sins in the garden of Eden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the turn of the 20th century, the application of theology in environmentalism diverged into two schools of thought. The first system of understanding holds religion as the basis of environmental stewardship. The second sees the use of theology as a means to rationalize the unmanaged consumptions of natural resources. <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Townsend_White_Jr." title="Lynn Townsend White Jr.">Lynn White</a> and Calvin DeWitt represent each side of this dichotomy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> personified nature as an inviting place away from the loudness of urban centers. "For Muir and the growing number of Americans who shared his views, Satan's home had become God's Own Temple."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The use of Abrahamic religious allusions assisted Muir and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> to create support for some of the first public nature preserves.
</p><p>Authors like Terry Tempest Williams as well as John Muir build on the idea that "...God can be found wherever you are, especially outside. Family worship was not just relegated to Sunday in a chapel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> References like these assist the general public to make a connection between paintings done at the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River_School" title="Hudson River School">Hudson River School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams" title="Ansel Adams">Ansel Adams</a>' photographs, along with other types of media, and their religion or spirituality. Placing intrinsic value upon nature through theology is a fundamental idea of <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropocentrism">Anthropocentrism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anthropocentrism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">Anthropocentrism</a></div>
<p>Anthropocentrism is the position that humans are the most important or critical element in any given situation; that the human race must always be its own primary concern. Detractors of anthropocentrism argue that the Western tradition biases homo sapiens when considering the environmental ethics of a situation and that humans evaluate their environment or other organisms in terms of the utility for them (see <a href="/wiki/Speciesism" title="Speciesism">speciesism</a>). Many argue that all environmental studies should include an assessment of the intrinsic value of non-human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In fact, based on this very assumption, a philosophical article has explored recently the possibility of humans' willing extinction as a gesture toward other beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The authors refer to the idea as a <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiment</a> that should not be understood as a call for action.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a> reasoned that if humans were to look at things objectively, they would discover that everything in the universe has a unique value. Likewise, it is possible that a human-centred or anthropocentric/androcentric ethic is not an accurate depiction of reality, and there is a bigger picture that humans may or may not be able to understand from a human perspective.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Vardy_(theologian)" title="Peter Vardy (theologian)">Peter Vardy</a> distinguished between two types of anthropocentrism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A strong anthropocentric ethic argues that humans are at the center of reality and it is right for them to be so. Weak anthropocentrism, however, argues that reality can only be interpreted from a human point of view, thus humans have to be at the centre of reality as they see it.
</p><p>Another point of view has been developed by Bryan Norton, who has become one of the essential actors of environmental ethics by launching environmental pragmatism, now one of its leading trends. Environmental pragmatism refuses to take a stance in disputes between defenders of anthropocentrist and non-anthropocentrist ethics. Instead, Norton distinguishes between <i>strong anthropocentrism</i> and <i>weak-or-extended-anthropocentrism</i> and argues that the former  must underestimate the diversity of instrumental values humans may derive from the natural world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent view relates anthropocentrism to the future of life. <a href="/wiki/Biotic_ethics" title="Biotic ethics">Biotic ethics</a> are based on the human identity as part of gene/protein organic life whose effective purpose is self-propagation. This implies a human purpose to secure and propagate life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bioethics_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bioethics-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeding_Book_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seeding_Book-4">[4]</a></sup> Humans are central because only they can secure life beyond the duration of the Sun, possibly for trillions of eons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biotic_ethics" title="Biotic ethics">Biotic ethics</a> values life itself, as embodied in biological structures and processes. Humans are special because they can secure the future of life on cosmological scales. In particular, humans can continue sentient life that enjoys its existence, adding further motivation to propagate life. Humans can secure the future of  life, and this future can give human existence a cosmic purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bioethics_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bioethics-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeding_Book_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Seeding_Book-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_of_the_field">Status of the field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_ethics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status of the field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only after 1990 did the field gain institutional recognition at programs such as <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montana" title="University of Montana">University of Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green_State_University" title="Bowling Green State University">Bowling Green State University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Texas" title="University of North Texas">University of North Texas</a>. In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Schumacher_College" title="Schumacher College">Schumacher College</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dartington" title="Dartington">Dartington</a>, England, was founded and now provides an MSc in Holistic Science.
</p><p>These programs began to offer a master's degree with a specialty in environmental ethics/philosophy. Beginning in 2005 the Department of Philosophy and Religion Studies at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Texas" title="University of North Texas">University of North Texas</a> offered a PhD program with a concentration in environmental ethics/philosophy.
</p><p>In Germany, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Greifswald" title="University of Greifswald">University of Greifswald</a> has recently established an international program in Landscape Ecology &amp; Nature Conservation with a strong focus on environmental ethics. In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maximilian_University_of_Munich" title="Ludwig Maximilian University of Munich">University of Munich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson_Center_for_Environment_and_Society" title="Rachel Carson Center for Environment and Society">Rachel Carson Center for Environment and Society</a>, an international, interdisciplinary center for research and education in the environmental humanities.
</p>
