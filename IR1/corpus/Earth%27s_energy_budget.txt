<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Earth's energy budget</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1530" data-file-width="1980" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg/300px-The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg/450px-The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg/600px-The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The-NASA-Earth%27s-Energy-Budget-Poster-Radiant-Energy-System-satellite-infrared-radiation-fluxes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth's climate is largely determined by the planet's <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> budget, <i>i.e.</i>, the balance of incoming and outgoing <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. It is measured by satellites and shown in W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_CERES_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_CERES-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Earth's energy budget</b> accounts for the balance between the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> Earth receives from the Sun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the energy Earth radiates back into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> after having been distributed throughout the five components of Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_system" title="Climate system">climate system</a> and having thus powered the so-called <b>Earth’s heat engine</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  This system is made up of earth's <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">ice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_Atmosphere" title="Earth's Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">rocky crust</a>, and all <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">living things</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_glossary_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_glossary-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quantifying changes in these amounts is required to accurately model the Earth's climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SatObsEva_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SatObsEva-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Received <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> is unevenly distributed over the planet, because the Sun heats equatorial regions more than polar regions. The atmosphere and ocean work non-stop to even out solar heating imbalances through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> of surface water, convection, rainfall, winds, and ocean circulation. Earth is very close to being in <a href="/wiki/Radiative_equilibrium" title="Radiative equilibrium">radiative equilibrium</a>, the situation where the incoming solar energy is balanced by an equal flow of heat to space; under that condition, global temperatures will be <i>relatively</i> stable. Globally, over the course of the year, the Earth system—land surfaces, oceans, and atmosphere—absorbs and then radiates back to space an average of about 240 watts of solar power per square meter. Anything that increases or decreases the amount of incoming or outgoing energy will change global temperatures in response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindsey_2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindsey_2009-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, Earth's energy balance and heat fluxes depend on many factors, such as atmospheric composition (mainly aerosols and greenhouse gases), the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (reflectivity) of surface properties, cloud cover and vegetation and land use patterns.
</p><p>Changes in surface temperature due to Earth's energy budget do not occur instantaneously, due to the <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>. The net heat flux is buffered primarily by becoming part of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">ocean's heat content</a>, until a new equilibrium state is established between radiative forcings and the climate response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_budget">Energy budget</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Energy budget">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg/250px-Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg/375px-Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg/500px-Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_heat_balance_Sankey_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Sankey_diagram" title="Sankey diagram">Sankey diagram</a> illustrating the Earth's energy budget described in this section — line thickness is linearly proportional to relative amount of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy_budget_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-energy_budget-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In spite of the enormous transfers of energy into and from the Earth, it maintains a relatively constant temperature because, as a whole, there is little net gain or loss: Earth emits via atmospheric and terrestrial radiation (shifted to longer electromagnetic wavelengths) to space about the same amount of energy as it receives via insolation (all forms of electromagnetic radiation).
</p><p>To quantify Earth's <i>heat budget</i> or <i>heat balance</i>, let the insolation received at the top of the atmosphere be 100 units (100 units = about 1,360 watts per square meter facing the sun), as shown in the accompanying illustration. Called the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> of Earth, around 35 units are reflected back to space: 27 from the top of clouds, 2 from snow and ice-covered areas, and 6 by other parts of the atmosphere. The 65 remaining units are absorbed: 14 within the atmosphere and 51 by the Earth’s surface. These 51 units are radiated to space in the form of terrestrial radiation: 17 directly radiated to space and 34 absorbed by the atmosphere (19 through latent heat of condensation, 9 via convection and turbulence, and 6 directly absorbed). The 48 units absorbed by the atmosphere (34 units from terrestrial radiation and 14 from insolation) are finally radiated back to space. These 65 units (17 from the ground and 48 from the atmosphere) balance the 65 units absorbed from the sun in order to maintain zero net gain of energy by the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy_budget_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-energy_budget-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Incoming_radiant_energy_.28shortwave.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Incoming_radiant_energy_(shortwave)">Incoming radiant energy (shortwave)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Incoming radiant energy (shortwave)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The total amount of energy received per second at the top of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> (TOA) is measured in <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> and is given by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a> times the cross-sectional area of the Earth. Because the surface area of a sphere is four times the cross-sectional surface area of a sphere (i.e. the area of a circle), the average TOA flux is one quarter of the solar constant and so is approximately 340 W/m².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_CERES_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_CERES-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Since the absorption varies with location as well as with diurnal, seasonal and annual variations, the numbers quoted are long-term averages, typically averaged from multiple satellite measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_CERES_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_CERES-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the ~340 W/m² of solar radiation received by the Earth, an average of ~77 W/m² is reflected back to space by clouds and the atmosphere and ~23 W/m² is reflected by the surface <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, leaving ~240 W/m² of solar energy input to the Earth's energy budget. This gives the earth a mean net albedo of 0.29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_CERES_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_CERES-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Earth.27s_internal_heat_and_other_small_effects"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_internal_heat_and_other_small_effects">Earth's internal heat and other small effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Earth's internal heat and other small effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_internal_heat_budget" title="Earth's internal heat budget">geothermal heat flux</a> from the Earth's interior is estimated to be 47 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terawatt" title="Terawatt">terawatts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-10">[10]</a></sup> This comes to 0.087 watt/square metre, which represents only 0.027% of Earth's total energy budget at the surface, which is dominated by 173,000 terawatts of incoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">Human production of energy</a> is even lower, at an estimated 18 TW.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">Photosynthesis</a> has a larger effect: <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic_efficiency" title="Photosynthetic efficiency">photosynthetic efficiency</a> turns up to 2% of incoming sunlight into <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">biomass</a>, for a total photosynthetic productivity of earth between ~1500–2250 TW (~1%+/-0.26% solar energy hitting the Earth's surface).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLoS_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-PLoS-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other minor sources of energy are usually ignored in these calculations, including accretion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust" title="Interplanetary dust">interplanetary dust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, light from stars other than the Sun and the thermal radiation from space. Earlier, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> had claimed that deep space radiation was significant in a paper often cited as the first on the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Longwave_radiation">Longwave radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Longwave radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">Outgoing longwave radiation</a></div>
<p>Longwave radiation is usually defined as outgoing <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> energy leaving the planet. However, the atmosphere absorbs parts initially, or cloud cover can reflect radiation. Generally, heat energy is transported between the planet's surface layers (land and ocean) to the atmosphere, transported via <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> fluxes or <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">conduction</a>/<a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_CERES_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_CERES-1">[1]</a></sup>  Ultimately, energy is radiated in the form of longwave <a href="/wiki/Infrared#Earth_as_an_infrared_emitter" title="Infrared">infrared radiation</a> back into space.
</p><p>Recent satellite observations indicate additional precipitation, which is sustained by increased energy leaving the surface through evaporation (the latent heat flux), offsetting increases in longwave flux to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SatObsEva_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SatObsEva-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Earth.27s_energy_imbalance"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_energy_imbalance">Earth's energy imbalance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earth's energy imbalance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If the incoming energy flux is not equal to the outgoing energy flux, net heat is added to or lost by the planet (if the incoming flux is larger or smaller than the outgoing respectively).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_measurement">Indirect measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indirect measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An imbalance must show in something on Earth warming or cooling (depending on the direction of the imbalance), and the ocean being the larger <a href="/wiki/Thermal_reservoir" title="Thermal reservoir">thermal reservoir</a> on Earth, is a prime candidate for measurements.
</p><p>Earth's energy imbalance measurements provided by <a href="/wiki/Argo_(oceanography)" title="Argo (oceanography)">Argo floats</a> have detected an accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">ocean heat content</a> (OHC). The estimated imbalance was measured during a deep <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">solar minimum</a> of 2005–2010 to be 0.58 ± 0.15 W/m².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imbalance_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imbalance-14">[14]</a></sup> This level of detail cannot be inferred directly from measurements of surface energy fluxes, which have combined uncertainties of the order of ± 17 W/m².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_measurement">Direct measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Direct measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> indirectly measure the energy absorbed and radiated by Earth and by inference the energy imbalance. The <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth_Radiation_Budget_Satellite" title="Earth Radiation Budget Satellite">Earth Radiation Budget Experiment</a> (ERBE) project involves three such satellites: the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Radiation_Budget_Satellite" title="Earth Radiation Budget Satellite">Earth Radiation Budget Satellite</a> (ERBS), launched October 1984; NOAA-9, launched December 1984; and NOAA-10, launched September 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today NASA's satellite instruments, provided by CERES, part of the NASA's <a href="/wiki/Earth_Observing_System" title="Earth Observing System">Earth Observing System</a> (EOS), are designed to measure both solar-reflected and Earth-emitted radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_greenhouse_effect">Natural greenhouse effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Natural greenhouse effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget,_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_(NASA).png"><img alt="refer to caption and image description" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png/250px-Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png/375px-Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png/500px-Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget%2C_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_%28NASA%29.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_showing_the_Earth%27s_energy_budget,_which_includes_the_greenhouse_effect_(NASA).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing the energy budget of Earth's atmosphere, which includes the greenhouse effect</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">Greenhouse effect</a></div>
<p>The major <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmospheric</a> gases (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>) are transparent to incoming sunlight but are also transparent to outgoing thermal (<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>) radiation. However, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor#Water_vapor_in_Earth.27s_atmosphere" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and other trace gases are opaque to many <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of thermal radiation. The Earth's surface radiates the net equivalent of 17 percent of the incoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar energy</a> in the form of thermal infrared. However, the amount that directly escapes to space is only about 12 percent of incoming solar energy. The remaining fraction, 5 to 6 percent, is absorbed by the atmosphere by <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="107" data-file-width="720" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png/500px-CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png 1.5x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric gases only absorb some wavelengths of energy but are transparent to others. The absorption patterns of water vapor (blue peaks) and carbon dioxide (pink peaks) overlap in some wavelengths. Carbon dioxide is not as strong a greenhouse gas as water vapor, but it absorbs energy in wavelengths (12–15 micrometres) that water vapor does not, partially closing the "window" through which heat radiated by the surface would normally escape to space. (Illustration NASA, Robert Rohde)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_climate_forcings_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_climate_forcings-19">[19]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>When greenhouse gas molecules absorb thermal infrared energy, their temperature rises. Those gases then radiate an increased amount of thermal infrared energy in all directions. Heat radiated upward continues to encounter greenhouse gas molecules; those molecules also absorb the heat, and their temperature rises and the amount of heat they radiate increases. The atmosphere thins with <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>, and at roughly 5–6 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a>, the concentration of greenhouse gases in the overlying atmosphere is so thin that heat can escape to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because greenhouse gas molecules radiate infrared energy in all directions, some of it spreads downward and ultimately returns to the Earth's surface, where it is absorbed. The Earth's surface temperature is thus higher than it would be if it were heated only by direct solar heating. This supplemental heating is the natural greenhouse effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-lindsey_greenhouse_effect_p6-18">[18]</a></sup> It is as if the Earth is covered by a blanket that allows high frequency radiation (sunlight) to enter, but slows the rate at which the low frequency infrared radiant energy emitted by the Earth leaves.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_sensitivity">Climate sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">Radiative forcing</a></div>
<p>A change in the incident radiated portion of the energy budget is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">Climate sensitivity</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">steady state</a> change in the equilibrium temperature as a result of changes in the energy budget.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_forcings_and_global_warming">Climate forcings and global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_energy_budget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate forcings and global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="678" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif/300px-NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif/450px-NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif/600px-NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA_Hansen_Aerosol_effect_on_expected_energy_imbalance_Earth_budget.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Expected Earth energy imbalance for three choices of aerosol climate forcing. Measured imbalance, close to 0.6 W/m², implies that aerosol forcing is close to −1.6 W/m². (Credit: NASA/GISS)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imbalance_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Imbalance-14">[14]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Climate forcings are changes that cause temperatures to rise or fall, disrupting the energy balance. Natural climate forcings include changes in the Sun's brightness, <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a> (small variations in the shape of Earth's orbit and its axis of rotation that occur over thousands of years) and volcanic eruptions that inject light-reflecting particles as high as the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Man-made forcings include particle pollution (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>) that absorb and reflect incoming sunlight; <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, which changes how the surface reflects and absorbs sunlight; and the rising concentration of atmospheric carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases, which decreases the rate at which heat is radiated to space.
</p><p>A forcing can trigger <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedbacks</a> that intensify (<a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>) or weaken (<a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>) the original forcing. For example, <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">loss of ice</a> at the poles, which makes them less reflective, causes greater absorption of energy and so increases the rate at which the ice melts, is an example of a positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_climate_forcings_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_climate_forcings-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The observed planetary energy imbalance during the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_24" title="Solar cycle 24">recent solar minimum</a> shows that solar forcing of climate, although natural and significant, is overwhelmed by anthropogenic climate forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> scientists reported that to stop <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> content would have to be reduced to 350 ppm or less, assuming all other climate forcings were fixed. The impact of anthropogenic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a> has not been quantified, but individual aerosol types are thought to have substantial heating and cooling effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imbalance_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Imbalance-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
