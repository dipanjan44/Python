<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_and_sectors">Systems and sectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Systems and sectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_human_health" title="Effects of climate change on human health">Effects of climate change on human health</a></div>
<p>Humans are exposed to climate change through changing weather patterns (temperature, precipitation, sea-level rise and more frequent extreme events) and indirectly through changes in water, air and food quality and changes in ecosystems, agriculture, industry and settlements and the economy (Confalonieri <i>et al</i>., 2007:393).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> According to a literature assessment by Confalonieri <i>et al</i>. (2007:393), the effects of climate change to date have been small, but are projected to progressively increase in all countries and regions.
</p><p>With high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al</i>. (2007:393) concluded that climate change had altered the seasonal distribution of some allergenic <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> species. With medium confidence, they concluded that climate change had:
</p>
<ul><li>altered the distribution of some infectious disease vectors</li>
<li>increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heatwave" title="Heatwave">heatwave</a>-related deaths</li></ul>
<p>With high confidence, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> (2007d:48) projected that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4syn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4syn-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the health status of millions of people would be affected through, for example, increases in <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>; increased deaths, diseases and injury due to extreme weather events; increased burden of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrhoea" title="Diarrhoea">diarrhoeal</a> diseases; increased frequency of cardio-<a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">respiratory diseases</a> due to high concentrations of ground-level ozone in urban areas related to climate change; and altered spatial distribution of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a>.</li>
<li>climate change would bring some benefits in temperate areas, such as fewer deaths from cold exposure, and some mixed effects such as changes in range and transmission potential of malaria in Africa. Overall, IPCC (2007d:48) expected that benefits would be outweighed by negative health effects of rising temperatures, especially in developing countries.</li></ul>
<p>With very high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007:393) concluded that economic development was an important component of possible adaptation to climate change. Economic growth on its own, however, was not judged to be sufficient to insulate the world's population from disease and injury due to climate change. The manner in which economic growth occurs was judged to be important, along with how the benefits of growth are distributed in society. Examples of other important factors in determining the health of populations were listed as: education, health care, and public-health infrastructure.
</p><p>According to a 2009 journal paper by <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">UCL</a> academics, climate change and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> pose the biggest threat to human health in the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Malnutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) projected that malnutrition would increase due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup> This link is associated with climate variability and change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drought_nutrition_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-drought_nutrition-6">[6]</a></sup> Drought reduces variety in <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diets</a> and reduces overall <a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">consumption</a>. This can lead to <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrient</a> deficiencies.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (Campbell-Lendrum <i>et al.</i>, 2003)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_projections_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-who_projections-7">[7]</a></sup>  conducted a regional and global assessment to quantify the amount of premature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morbidity" title="Morbidity">morbidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortality</a> due to a range of factors, including climate change. Projections were made over future climate change impacts. Limited adjustments for adaptation were included in the estimates based on these projections. Projected relative risks attributable to climate change in 2030 varied by health outcome and region. Risks were largely negative, with most of the projected disease burden due to increases in diarrhoeal disease and malnutrition. These increases were primarily in low-income populations already experiencing a large burden of disease.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_events">Extreme events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extreme events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) projected that climate change would increase the number of people suffering from death, disease and injury from <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Floods and weather disasters</dt></dl>
<p>Floods are low-probability, high-impact events that can overwhelm physical infrastructure and human communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Health_impacts_of_wind,_storms_and_floods_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Health_impacts_of_wind,_storms_and_floods-8">[8]</a></sup> Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) reported that major storm and flood disasters have occurred in the last two decades.
</p><p>The impacts of weather disasters is considerable and unequally distributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Health_impacts_of_wind,_storms_and_floods_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Health_impacts_of_wind,_storms_and_floods-8">[8]</a></sup> For example, natural disasters have been shown to result in increased domestic violence against - and <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">post-traumatic stress disorders</a> in – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women" title="Women">women</a>. In terms of deaths and populations affected, floods and tropical cyclones have the greatest impact in South Asia and Latin America. Vulnerability to weather disasters depends on the attributes of the person at risk, including where they live and their age, as well as other social and environmental factors. High-density populations in low-lying coastal regions experience a high health burden from weather disasters.
</p>
<dl><dt>Heatwaves</dt></dl>
<p>Hot days, hot nights and heatwaves have become more frequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Heatwaves are associated with marked short-term increases in mortality. For example, in August 2003, a heatwave in Europe resulted in excess mortality in the range of 35,000 total deaths.
</p><p>Heat-related morbidity and mortality is projected to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heat_and_cold_related_mortality_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-heat_and_cold_related_mortality-10">[10]</a></sup> The health burden could be relatively small for moderate heatwaves in temperate regions, because deaths occur primarily in susceptible persons.
</p>
<dl><dt>Drought</dt></dl>
<p>The effects of drought on health include deaths, malnutrition, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">respiratory diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drought_nutrition_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-drought_nutrition-6">[6]</a></sup> Countries within the "<a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">Meningitis</a> Belt" in semi-arid sub-Saharan Africa experience the highest <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(epidemiology)" title="Endemic (epidemiology)">endemicity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> frequency of <a href="/wiki/Meningococcal_disease" title="Meningococcal disease">meningococcal</a> meningitis in Africa, although other areas in the Rift Valley, the Great Lakes, and southern Africa are also affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The spatial distribution, intensity, and seasonality of meningococcal (epidemic) meningitis appear to be strongly linked to climate and environmental factors, particularly drought. The cause of this link is not fully understood.
</p>
<dl><dt>Fires</dt></dl>
<p>In some regions, changes in temperature and precipitation are projected to increase the frequency and severity of fire events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Forest and bush fires cause burns, damage from smoke inhalation and other injuries.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_of_disease">Spread of disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spread of disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_disease" title="Tropical disease">Tropical disease</a></div>
<p>Global warming may extend the favourable zones for <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> conveying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious disease</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile virus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In poorer countries, this may simply lead to higher incidence of such diseases. In richer countries, where such diseases have been eliminated or kept in check by <a href="/wiki/Vaccination" title="Vaccination">vaccination</a>, draining swamps and using pesticides, the consequences may be felt more in economic than health terms. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) says global warming could lead to a major increase in insect-borne diseases in Britain and Europe, as northern Europe becomes warmer, ticks—which carry <a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">encephalitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">lyme disease</a>—and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandflies" title="Sandflies">sandflies</a>—which carry <a href="/wiki/Visceral_leishmaniasis" title="Visceral leishmaniasis">visceral leishmaniasis</a>—are likely to move in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, malaria has always been a common threat in European past, with the last epidemic occurring in the Netherlands during the 1950s. In the United States, Malaria has been endemic in as much as 36 states (including <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, North Dakota, Michigan and New York) until the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> By 1949, the country was declared free of malaria as a significant public health problem, after more than 4,650,000 house <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> spray applications had been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The World Health Organisation estimates 150,000 deaths annually "as a result of climate change", of which half are in the Asia-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In April 2008, it reported that, as a result of increased temperatures, the number of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> infections is expected to increase in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_(geography)" title="Highland (geography)">highland</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Infectious_disease_vectors">Infectious disease vectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infectious disease vectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>With high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup>  projected that climate change would continue to change the range of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious disease</a> vectors. <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">Vector-borne diseases</a> (VBD) are infections transmitted by the bite of infected <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> species, such as <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tick" title="Tick">ticks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triatominae" title="Triatominae">triatomine</a> bugs, <a href="/wiki/Sandfly" title="Sandfly">sandflies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Black_fly" title="Black fly">blackflies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> There is some evidence of climate-change-related shifts in the distribution of tick vectors of disease, of some (non-malarial) mosquito vectors in Europe and North America. Climate change has also been implicated in changes in the breeding and migration dates of several bird species. Several species of wild bird can act as carriers of human <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> as well as of vectors of infectious agents.
</p>
<h6><span class="mw-headline" id="Dengue">Dengue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dengue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h6>
<p>With low confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that climate change would increase the number of people at risk of dengue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">Dengue</a> is the world's most important vector-borne viral disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Several studies have reported associations between dengue and climate, however, these associations are not entirely consistent.
</p>

<h6><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaria">Malaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Malaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h6>
<p>The spatial distribution, intensity of transmission, and seasonalty of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> is influenced by climate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-saharan_Africa" title="Sub-saharan Africa">Sub-saharan Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Rainfall can be a limiting factor for mosquito populations and there is some evidence of reductions in transmission associated with decadal decreases in rainfall. The effects of observed climate change on the geographical distribution of malaria and its transmission intensity in highland regions remains controversial. There is no clear evidence that malaria has been affected by climate change in South America or in continental regions of the Russian Federation. There is still much uncertainty about the potential impact of climate change on malaria at local and global scales.
</p>

<p>A paper by researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a> published in <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a> in May 2010 concluded that claims that a warming climate has led to more widespread disease and death due to malaria are largely at odds with the evidence, and that "<i>predictions of an intensification of malaria in a warmer world, based on extrapolated empirical relationships or biological mechanisms, must be set against a context of a century of warming that has seen marked global declines in the disease and a substantial weakening of the global correlation between malaria endemicity and climate.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h6><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_infectious_diseases">Other infectious diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other infectious diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h6>
<p>There is good evidence that diseases transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> sometimes increase during heavy rainfall and flooding because of altered patterns of human-pathogen-rodent contact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h6><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections">Projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h6>
<p>With very high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup>  concluded that climate change would have mixed effects on malaria. Malaria is a complex disease to model and all of the published models assessed by Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) had limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parametrization_(climate)" title="Parametrization (climate)">parametrization</a> of some key factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Parametrization is used in <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a> because the resolution of models is insufficient to resolve some physical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Given this limitation, models assessed by Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007) projected that, particularly in Africa, climate change would be associated with geographical expansions of the areas suitable for <i>Plasmodium falciparum</i> malaria in some regions, and contractions in other regions. Projections also suggested that some regions would experience a longer season of transmission. Projections suggested expansions in vector species that carry dengue for parts of Australia and New Zealand.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Diarrhoeal_diseases">Diarrhoeal diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diarrhoeal diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>With medium confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup> concluded that climate change would increase the burden of diarrhoeal diseases. Childhood mortality due to diarrhoea in low-income countries, especially in Sub-Saharan Africa, remains high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This is despite improvements in care. Several studies have shown that transmission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">enteric</a> pathogens is higher during the rainy season. Some studies have found that higher temperature was strongly associated with increased episodes of diarrhoeal disease in adults and children in Peru. Campbell-Lendrum <i>et al.</i> (2003)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who_projections_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-who_projections-7">[7]</a></sup> projected that climate change would increase the burden of diarrhoeal diseases in low-income regions by approximately 2 to 5% in 2020.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground-level_ozone">Ground-level ozone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ground-level ozone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With high confidence, Confalonieri <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-health_summary_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-health_summary-5">[5]</a></sup> projected that climate change would increase cardio-respiratory morbidity and mortality associated with ground-level <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>. Ground-level ozone is both naturally occurring and is the primary constituent of urban <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Ozone in smog is formed through chemical reactions involving <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> and other compounds. The reaction is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_reaction" title="Photochemical reaction">photochemical reaction</a>, meaning that it involves <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, and occurs in the presence of bright sunshine and high temperatures. Exposure to elevated concentrations of ozone is associated with increased hospital admissions for <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, allergic <a href="/wiki/Rhinitis" title="Rhinitis">rhinitis</a> and other respiratory diseases, and with premature mortality.
</p><p>Background levels of ground-level ozone have risen since pre-industrial times because of increasing emissions of methane, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and nitrogen oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This trend is expected to continue into the mid-21st century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold-waves">Cold-waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cold-waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">Cold-waves</a> continue to be a problem in northern latitudes, where very low temperatures can be reached in a few hours and extend over long periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Reductions in cold-deaths due to climate change are projected to be greater than increases in heat-related deaths in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heat_and_cold_related_mortality_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-heat_and_cold_related_mortality-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="959" data-file-width="649" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/220px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/330px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg/440px-NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAAprecipitationtrend.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Precipitation during the 20th century and up through 2008 during global warming, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> estimating an observed trend over that period of 1.87% global precipitation increase per century.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_effects_of_temperature_rise">Direct effects of temperature rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Direct effects of temperature rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most direct effect of climate change on humans might be the impacts of hotter temperatures themselves. Extreme high temperatures increase the number of people who die on a given day for many reasons: people with heart problems are vulnerable because one's cardiovascular system must work harder to keep the body cool during hot weather, heat exhaustion, and some respiratory problems increase. Global warming could mean more <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular diseases</a>, doctors warn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Higher air temperature also increase the concentration of ozone at ground level. In the lower atmosphere, ozone is a harmful pollutant. It damages lung tissues and causes problems for people with asthma and other lung diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising temperatures have two opposing direct effects on <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortality</a>: higher temperatures in winter reduce deaths from cold; higher temperatures in summer increase heat-related deaths. The net local impact of these two direct effects depends on the current climate in a particular area. Palutikof <i>et al.</i> (1996) calculate that in England and Wales for a 1 °C temperature rise the reduced deaths from cold outweigh the increased deaths from heat, resulting in a reduction in annual average mortality of 7000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> while Keatinge <i>et al.</i> (2000) "suggest that any increases in mortality due to increased temperatures would be outweighed by much larger short term declines in cold related mortalities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keatinge2000_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keatinge2000-38">[38]</a></sup> Cold-related deaths are far more numerous than heat-related deaths in the United States, Europe, and almost all countries outside the tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> During 1979–1999, a total of 3,829 deaths in the United States were associated with excessive heat due to weather conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> while in that same period a total of 13,970 deaths were attributed to <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In Europe, mean annual heat related mortalities are 304 in north <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, 445 in Athens, and 40 in London, while cold related mortalities are 2457, 2533, and 3129 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keatinge2000_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keatinge2000-38">[38]</a></sup> According to Keatinge <i>et al.</i> (2000), "populations in Europe have adjusted successfully to mean summer temperatures ranging from 13.5°C to 24.1°C, and can be expected to adjust to global warming predicted for the next half century with little sustained increase in heat related mortality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keatinge2000_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keatinge2000-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>A government report shows decreased mortality due to recent warming and predicts increased mortality due to future warming in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">2003 European heat wave</a> killed 22,000–35,000 people, based on normal mortality rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schär2004_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schär2004-43">[43]</a></sup> Peter A. Stott from the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a> estimated with 90% confidence that past human influence on climate was responsible for at least half the risk of the 2003 European summer heat-wave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stott2004_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stott2004-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Pediatrics" title="American Academy of Pediatrics">American Academy of Pediatrics</a> issued the policy statement <i>Global Climate Change and Children's Health</i>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Anticipated direct health consequences of climate change include injury and death from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_weather_event" title="Extreme weather event">extreme weather events</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>, increases in climate-sensitive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a>, increases in <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution#Health_effects" title="Air pollution">air pollution–related illness</a>, and more heat-related, potentially fatal, illness. Within all of these categories, children have increased vulnerability compared with other groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>On 2008-04-29, a <a href="/wiki/UNICEF_UK" title="UNICEF UK">UNICEF UK</a> Report found that global warming is already reducing the quality of the world's most vulnerable children's lives and making it more difficult to meet the UN <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>. Global warming will reduce access to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_water" title="Clean water">clean water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food supplies</a>, particularly in Africa and Asia. Disasters, violence and disease are expected to be more frequent and intense, making the future of the world's poorest children more bleak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits_of_human_survivability">Limits of human survivability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change,_industry_and_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Limits of human survivability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some areas of the world would start to surpass the <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet-bulb temperature</a> limit of human survivability with global warming of about 6.7 °C (12 °F) while a warming of 11.7 °C (21 °F) would put half of the world's population in an uninhabitable environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily-WetBulb_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily-WetBulb-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS-WetBulb_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-WetBulb-48">[48]</a></sup> In practice, the survivable limit of global warming in these areas is probably lower and in practice, some areas may experience lethal wet-bulb temperatures even earlier, because this study conservatively projected the survival limit for persons who are out of the sun, in gale-force winds, doused with water, wearing no clothing, and not working.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS-WetBulb_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-WetBulb-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The combined effects of global warming may have particularly harsh effects on people and countries without the resources to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigate</a> those effects. This may slow <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poverty_reduction" title="Poverty reduction">poverty reduction</a>, and make it harder to achieve the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2004 the Working Group on Climate Change and Development, a coalition of development and environment <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a>, issued a report
<a class="external text" href="http://www.iied.org/pubs/display.php?o=9512IIED" rel="nofollow">Up in Smoke</a> on the effects of climate change on development. This report, and the July 2005 report <a class="external text" href="http://www.iied.org/pubs/display.php?o=9560IIED" rel="nofollow">Africa - Up in Smoke?</a> predicted increased hunger and disease due to decreased rainfall and severe weather events, particularly in Africa. These are likely to have severe impacts on development for those affected.
</p><p>In a literature assessment, Yohe <i>et al</i>. (2007:813) concluded that climate change would very likely make it more difficult for nations to achieve the MDGs for the middle of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the short-term, it was judged very likely that climate change (as attributed with high confidence to human activities) would not be a significant extra impediment to nations reaching their 2015 Millennium Development Targets.
</p>
