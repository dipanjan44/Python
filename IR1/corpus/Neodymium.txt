<p><b>Neodymium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Nd</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 60. It is a soft silvery metal that <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnishes</a> in air. Neodymium was discovered in 1885 by the Austrian chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>. It is present in significant quantities in the ore minerals <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>. Neodymium is not found naturally in metallic form or unmixed with other <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, and it is usually refined for general use. Although neodymium is classed as a <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare earth</a>, it is a fairly common element, no rarer than <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and is widely distributed in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of the world's commercial neodymium is <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mined</a> in China.
</p><p>Neodymium <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> were first commercially used as glass dyes in 1927, and they remain a popular additive in glasses. The color of neodymium compounds—due to the Nd<sup>3+</sup> ion—is often a reddish-purple but it changes with the type of lighting, due to the interaction of the sharp light absorption bands of neodymium with ambient light enriched with the sharp visible emission bands of mercury, trivalent europium or terbium. Some neodymium-doped glasses are also used in <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> that emit <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> with <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> between 1047 and 1062 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a>. These have been used in extremely-high-power applications, such as experiments in <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement fusion</a>.
</p><p>Neodymium is also used with various other <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrate</a> crystals, such as <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_aluminium_garnet" title="Yttrium aluminium garnet">yttrium aluminium garnet</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nd:YAG_laser" title="Nd:YAG laser">Nd:YAG laser</a>. This laser usually emits infrared at a <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of about 1064 nanometers. The Nd:YAG laser is one of the most commonly used <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a>.
</p><p>Another important use of neodymium is as a component in the alloys used to make high-strength <a href="/wiki/Neodymium_magnet" title="Neodymium magnet">neodymium magnets</a>—powerful permanent magnets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These magnets are widely used in such products as microphones, professional loudspeakers, in-ear headphones, high performance hobby DC electric motors, and computer hard disks, where low magnet mass (or volume) or strong magnetic fields are required. Larger neodymium magnets are used in high-power-versus-weight <a href="/wiki/Electric_motor" title="Electric motor">electric motors</a> (for example in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_cars" title="Hybrid cars">hybrid cars</a>) and generators (for example <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbine</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generators</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-reu-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neodymium, a <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, was present in the classical <a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">mischmetal</a> at a concentration of about 18%. Metallic neodymium has a bright, silvery metallic luster, but as one of the more reactive <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> rare-earth <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, it quickly <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizes</a> in ordinary air. The <a href="/wiki/Neodymium(III)_oxide" title="Neodymium(III) oxide">oxide</a> layer that forms then peels off, exposing the metal to further oxidation. Thus, a <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">centimeter</a>-sized sample of neodymium completely oxidizes within a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neodymium commonly exists in two <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropic</a> forms, with a transformation from a double hexagonal to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure taking place at about 863 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neodymium metal tarnishes slowly in air and it burns readily at about 150 °C to form <a href="/wiki/Neodymium(III)_oxide" title="Neodymium(III) oxide">neodymium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Nd + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Nd<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Neodymium is a quite electropositive element, and it reacts slowly with cold water, but quite quickly with hot water to form neodymium(III) hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Nd (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Nd(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Neodymium metal reacts vigorously with all the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Nd (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 NdF<sub>3</sub> (s) [a violet substance]</dd>
<dd>2 Nd (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 NdCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [a mauve substance]</dd>
<dd>2 Nd (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 NdBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [a violet substance]</dd>
<dd>2 Nd (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 NdI<sub>3</sub> (s) [a green substance]</dd></dl>
<p>Neodymium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions that contain the lilac Nd(III) ion. These exist as a [Nd(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Nd (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Nd<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<li>sulfides: neodymium(II) sulfide (NdS), neodymium(III) sulfide (Nd<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>)</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_neodymium" title="Isotopes of neodymium">Isotopes of neodymium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring neodymium is a mixture of five stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, <sup>142</sup>Nd, <sup>143</sup>Nd, <sup>145</sup>Nd, <sup>146</sup>Nd and <sup>148</sup>Nd, with <sup>142</sup>Nd being the most abundant (27.2% of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">natural abundance</a>), and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a>, <sup>144</sup>Nd and <sup>150</sup>Nd. In all, 31 radioisotopes of neodymium have been detected as of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, with the most stable radioisotopes being the naturally occurring ones: <sup>144</sup>Nd (<a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>) of 2.29×10<sup>15</sup> years) and <sup>150</sup>Nd (<a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a>, <i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 7×10<sup>18</sup> years, approximately). All of the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives that are shorter than eleven days, and the majority of these have half-lives that are shorter than 70 seconds. Neodymium also has 13 known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, with the most stable one being <sup>139<i>m</i></sup>Nd (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 5.5 hours), <sup>135<i>m</i></sup>Nd (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 5.5 minutes) and <sup>133<i>m</i>1</sup>Nd (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> ~70 seconds).
</p><p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay modes</a> before the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>142</sup>Nd, are <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positron_decay" title="Positron decay">positron decay</a>, and the primary mode after is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_minus_decay" title="Beta minus decay">beta minus decay</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> before <sup>142</sup>Nd are element Pr (<a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>) isotopes and the primary products after are element Pm (<a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>) isotopes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1268" data-file-width="1002" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/200px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/300px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/400px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> (1858–1929), the discoverer of neodymium</div></div></div>
<p>Neodymium was discovered by Baron <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>, an Austrian <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1885.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He separated neodymium, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>, from a material known as <a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a> by means of fractional crystallization of the double ammonium nitrate tetrahydrates from <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, while following the separation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic" title="Spectroscopic">spectroscopic</a> analysis; however, it was not isolated in relatively pure form until 1925. The name neodymium is derived from the Greek words <i>neos</i> (νέος), new, and <i>didymos</i> (διδύμος), twin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks1932_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks1932-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Double nitrate crystallization was the means of commercial neodymium purification until the 1950s. Lindsay Chemical Division was the first to commercialize large-scale ion-exchange purification of neodymium. Starting in the 1950s, high purity (above 99%) neodymium was primarily obtained through an <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> process from <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, a mineral rich in rare-earth elements. The metal itself is obtained through <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of its <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halide</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>. Currently, most neodymium is extracted from bastnäsite, (Ce,La,Nd,Pr)CO<sub>3</sub>F, and purified by solvent extraction. Ion-exchange purification is reserved for preparing the highest purities (typically &gt;99.99%). The evolving technology, and improved purity of commercially available neodymium oxide, was reflected in the appearance of neodymium glass that resides in collections today. Early neodymium glasses made in the 1930s have a more reddish or orange tinge than modern versions which are more cleanly purple, due to the difficulties in removing the last traces of praseodymium in the era when manufacturing relied upon fractional crystallization technology.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk,_Ural.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1038" data-file-width="1564" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg/220px-Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg/330px-Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg/440px-Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk%2C_Ural.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bastnaesite_-_Kischtimsk,_Ural.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">Bastnäsite</a></div></div></div>
<p>Neodymium is rarely found in nature as a free element, but rather it occurs in ores such as <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> (these are mineral group names rather than single mineral names) that contain small amounts of all rare-earth metals. In these minerals neodymium is rarely dominant (as in the case of lanthanum), with cerium being the most abundant lanthanide; some exceptions include monazite-(Nd) and kozoite-(Nd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The main mining areas are in China, the United States, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, India, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, and Australia. The reserves of neodymium are estimated at about eight million tonnes. Although it belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth metals</a>, neodymium is not rare at all. Its abundance in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s crust is about 38 mg/kg, which is the second highest among rare-earth elements, following <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>. The world's production of neodymium was about 7,000 tonnes in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup> The bulk of current production is from China. As of  January 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the Chinese government has imposed strategic material controls on the element, raising some concerns in consuming countries and causing skyrocketing prices of neodymium and other rare-earth metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As of late 2011, 99% pure neodymium was traded in world markets for US$300 to US$350 per kilogram, down from the mid-2011 peak of US$500/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The price of neodymium oxide fell from $200/kg in 2011 to $40 in 2015, largely due to illegal production in China which circumvented government restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The uncertainty of pricing and availability have caused companies (particularly Japanese ones) to create permanent magnets and associated electric motors with fewer rare-earth metals; however, so far they have been unable to eliminate the need for neodymium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neodymium is typically 10–18% of the rare-earth content of commercial deposits of the light rare-earth-element minerals bastnäsite and monazite.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With neodymium compounds being the most strongly colored for the trivalent lanthanides, it can occasionally dominate the coloration of rare-earth minerals when competing chromophores are absent. It usually gives a pink coloration. Outstanding examples of this include monazite crystals from the <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/Llallagua" title="Llallagua">Llallagua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ancylite" title="Ancylite">ancylite</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mont_Saint-Hilaire" title="Mont Saint-Hilaire">Mont Saint-Hilaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>; or <a href="/wiki/Lanthanite" title="Lanthanite">lanthanite</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saucon_Township,_Northampton_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Lower Saucon Township, Northampton County, Pennsylvania">Saucon Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, United States. As with neodymium glasses, such minerals change their colors under the differing lighting conditions. The absorption bands of neodymium interact with the visible <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">emission spectrum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mercury-vapor_lamp" title="Mercury-vapor lamp">mercury vapor</a>, with the unfiltered shortwave UV light causing neodymium-containing minerals to reflect a distinctive green color. This can be observed with monazite-containing sands or bastnäsite-containing ore.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Neodymium has an unusually large specific <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> at liquid-helium temperatures, so is useful in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryocoolers" title="Cryocoolers">cryocoolers</a>.</li>
<li>Probably because of similarities to Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Nd<sup>3+</sup> has been reported<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> to promote plant growth. Rare-earth-element compounds are frequently used in China as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93neodymium_dating" title="Samarium–neodymium dating">Samarium–neodymium dating</a> is useful for determining the age relationships of rocks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and meteorites.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnets">Magnets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Magnets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neodymag.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="1211" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Neodymag.jpg/220px-Neodymag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Neodymag.jpg/330px-Neodymag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Neodymag.jpg/440px-Neodymag.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neodymag.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neodymium magnet on a <a href="/wiki/Mu-metal" title="Mu-metal">mu-metal</a> bracket from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">hard drive</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neodymium_magnet" title="Neodymium magnet">Neodymium magnets</a> (actually an alloy, Nd<sub>2</sub>Fe<sub>14</sub>B) are the strongest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnet" title="Permanent magnet">permanent magnets</a> known. A neodymium magnet of a few grams can lift a thousand times its own weight. These magnets are cheaper, lighter, and stronger than <a href="/wiki/Samarium%E2%80%93cobalt_magnet" title="Samarium–cobalt magnet">samarium–cobalt magnets</a>. However, they are not superior in every aspect, as neodymium-based magnets lose their magnetism at lower temperatures and tend to rust, while samarium–cobalt magnets do not.
</p><p>Neodymium magnets appear in products such as <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphones</a>, professional <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a>, in-ear <a href="/wiki/Headphones" title="Headphones">headphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass guitar</a> <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)" title="Pickup (music technology)">pick-ups</a>, and computer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">hard disks</a> where low mass, small volume, or strong magnetic fields are required. Neodymium magnet electric motors have also been responsible for the development of purely electrical model aircraft within the first decade of the 21st century, to the point that these are displacing internal combustion–powered models internationally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Likewise, due to this high magnetic capacity per weight, neodymium is used in the electric motors of hybrid and electric automobiles, and in the electricity generators of some designs of commercial wind turbines (only wind turbines with "permanent magnet" generators use neodymium). For example, drive electric motors of each <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a> require one kilogram (2.2 pounds) of neodymium per vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reu_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-reu-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neodymium_doped_lasers">Neodymium doped lasers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Neodymium doped lasers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YAG2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="253" data-file-width="402" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/YAG2.svg/310px-YAG2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/YAG2.svg/465px-YAG2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/YAG2.svg/620px-YAG2.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YAG2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neodymium ions in various types of ionic crystals, and also in glasses, act as a laser gain medium, typically emitting 1064 nm light from a particular atomic transition in the neodymium ion, after being "pumped" into excitation from an external source</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laser_glass_slabs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="442" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Laser_glass_slabs.jpg/310px-Laser_glass_slabs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2f/Laser_glass_slabs.jpg 1.5x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laser_glass_slabs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neodymium doped glass slabs used in extremely powerful lasers for <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement fusion</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yag-rod.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yag-rod.jpg/220px-Yag-rod.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yag-rod.jpg/330px-Yag-rod.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f9/Yag-rod.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yag-rod.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nd:YAG laser rod</div></div></div>
<p>Certain transparent materials with a small concentration of neodymium <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> can be used in <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gain_medium" title="Gain medium">gain media</a> for infrared wavelengths (1054–1064 nm), e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nd:YAG_laser" title="Nd:YAG laser">Nd:YAG</a> (yttrium aluminium garnet), <a href="/wiki/Neodymium-doped_yttrium_lithium_fluoride" title="Neodymium-doped yttrium lithium fluoride">Nd:YLF</a> (yttrium lithium fluoride), <a href="/wiki/Neodymium-doped_yttrium_orthovanadate" title="Neodymium-doped yttrium orthovanadate">Nd:YVO<sub>4</sub></a> (yttrium orthovanadate), and Nd:glass. Neodymium-doped crystals (typically Nd:YVO<sub>4</sub>) generate high-powered infrared laser beams which are converted to green laser light in commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPSS" title="DPSS">DPSS</a> hand-held lasers and <a href="/wiki/Laser_pointer" title="Laser pointer">laser pointers</a>.
</p><p>The current laser at the UK <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Weapons_Establishment" title="Atomic Weapons Establishment">Atomic Weapons Establishment</a> (AWE), the HELEN (High Energy Laser Embodying Neodymium) 1-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terawatt" title="Terawatt">terawatt</a> neodymium-glass laser, can access the midpoints of pressure and temperature regions and is used to acquire data for modeling on how density, temperature, and pressure interact inside warheads. HELEN can create plasmas of around 10<sup>6</sup> <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>, from which opacity and transmission of radiation are measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neodymium glass <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a> are used in extremely high power (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E11_W#1_terawatt" title="1 E11 W">terawatt</a> scale), high energy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">megajoules</a>) multiple beam systems for <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement fusion</a>. Nd:glass lasers are usually <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_optics" title="Nonlinear optics">frequency tripled</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Optical_frequency_multiplier" title="Optical frequency multiplier">third harmonic</a> at 351 nm in laser fusion devices.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neodymium_glass_for_other_applications">Neodymium glass for other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neodymium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Neodymium glass for other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2421" data-file-width="3468" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg/220px-Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg/330px-Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg/440px-Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neodymium_glass_light_bulb_under_fluorescent_and_incandescent_light.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A neodymium glass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulb</a>, with the base and inner coating removed, under two different types of light: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent" title="Fluorescent">fluorescent</a> on the left, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent" title="Incandescent">incandescent</a> on the right.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neodymium-glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="581" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Neodymium-glass.jpg/220px-Neodymium-glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Neodymium-glass.jpg/330px-Neodymium-glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Neodymium-glass.jpg/440px-Neodymium-glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neodymium-glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neodymium-colored glass</div></div></div>
<p>Neodymium <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> (Nd:glass) is produced by the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Neodymium(III)_oxide" title="Neodymium(III) oxide">neodymium oxide</a> (Nd<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) in the glass melt. Usually in daylight or <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent</a> light neodymium glass appears lavender, but it appears pale blue under <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent</a> lighting. Neodymium may be used to color <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> in delicate shades ranging from pure violet through wine-red and warm gray.
</p><p>The first commercial use of purified neodymium was in glass coloration, starting with experiments by Leo Moser in November 1927. The resulting "Alexandrite" glass remains a signature color of the Moser glassworks to this day. Neodymium glass was widely emulated in the early 1930s by American glasshouses, most notably Heisey, Fostoria ("wisteria"), Cambridge ("heatherbloom"), and Steuben ("wisteria"), and elsewhere (e.g. Lalique, in France, or Murano). Tiffin's "twilight" remained in production from about 1950 to 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Current sources include glassmakers in the Czech Republic, the United States, and China.
</p><p>The sharp absorption bands of neodymium cause the glass color to change under different lighting conditions, being reddish-purple under <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> or yellow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_light" title="Incandescent light">incandescent light</a>, but blue under white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_light" title="Fluorescent light">fluorescent lighting</a>, or greenish under <a href="/wiki/Trichromacy" title="Trichromacy">trichromatic</a> lighting. This color-change phenomenon is highly prized by collectors. In combination with <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> or <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, beautiful red colors result. Since neodymium coloration depends upon "<a href="/wiki/Forbidden_mechanism" title="Forbidden mechanism">forbidden</a>" f-f transitions deep within the atom, there is relatively little influence on the color from the chemical environment, so the color is impervious to the thermal history of the glass. However, for the best color, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-containing impurities need to be minimized in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> used to make the glass. The same forbidden nature of the f-f transitions makes <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth</a> colorants less intense than those provided by most d-transition elements, so more has to be used in a glass to achieve the desired color intensity. The original Moser recipe used about 5% of neodymium oxide in the glass melt, a sufficient quantity such that Moser referred to these as being "rare-earth doped" glasses. Being a strong base, that level of neodymium would have affected the melting properties of the glass, and the <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">lime</a> content of the glass might have had to be adjusted accordingly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Light transmitted through neodymium glasses shows unusually sharp <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">absorption bands</a>; the glass is used in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical work</a> to produce sharp bands by which <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral lines</a> may be calibrated. Another application is the creation of selective astronomical filters to reduce the effect of light pollution from sodium and fluorescent lighting while passing other colours, especially dark red hydrogen-alpha emission from nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Neodymium is also used to remove the green color caused by <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> contaminants from glass.
</p><p>Neodymium is a component of "<a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a>" (referring to mixture of salts of neodymium and <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>) used for coloring glass to make <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welder</a>'s and glass-blower's goggles; the sharp absorption bands obliterate the strong sodium emission at 589 nm.  The similar absorption of the yellow mercury emission line at 578 nm is the principal cause of the blue color observed for neodymium glass under traditional white-fluorescent lighting.
</p><p>Neodymium and didymium glass are used in color-enhancing filters in indoor photography, particularly in filtering out the yellow hues from incandescent lighting.
</p><p>Similarly, neodymium glass is becoming widely used more directly in <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulbs</a>. These lamps contain neodymium in the glass to filter out yellow light, resulting in a whiter light which is more like sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of neodymium in automobile rear-view mirrors, to reduce the glare at night, has been patented.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Similar to its use in glasses, neodymium salts are used as a colorant for <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<p>Neodymium metal dust is combustible and therefore an explosion hazard. Neodymium compounds, as with all rare-earth metals, are of low to moderate toxicity; however, its toxicity has not been thoroughly investigated. Neodymium dust and salts are very irritating to the eyes and <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a>, and moderately irritating to skin. Breathing the dust can cause lung <a href="/wiki/Embolism" title="Embolism">embolisms</a>, and accumulated exposure damages the liver. Neodymium also acts as an <a href="/wiki/Anticoagulant" title="Anticoagulant">anticoagulant</a>, especially when given intravenously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Neodymium magnets have been tested for medical uses such as magnetic braces and bone repair, but <a href="/wiki/Biocompatibility" title="Biocompatibility">biocompatibility</a> issues have prevented widespread application. Commercially available magnets made from neodymium are exceptionally strong, and can attract each other from large distances. If not handled carefully, they come together very quickly and forcefully, causing injuries. For example, there is at least one documented case of a person losing a fingertip when two magnets he was using snapped together from 50 cm away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another risk of these powerful magnets is that if more than one magnet is ingested, they can pinch soft tissues in the gastrointestinal tract. This has led to at least 1,700 emergency room visits and necessitated the recall of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buckyball_(toy)" title="Buckyball (toy)">Buckyballs line of toys</a>, which were construction sets of small neodymium magnets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
