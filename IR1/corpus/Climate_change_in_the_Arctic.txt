<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in the Arctic</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The effects of global warming in the Arctic</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1512" data-file-width="1439" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg/220px-ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg/330px-ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg/440px-ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArcticYearlongTempAnom_HR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The image above shows where average air temperatures (October 2010 – September 2011) were up to 2 degrees Celsius above (red) or below (blue) the long-term average (1981–2010).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1005" data-file-width="720" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png/220px-Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png/330px-Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png/440px-Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Sea_Ice_Minimum_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The maps above compare the Arctic ice minimum extents from 2012 (top) and 1984 (bottom). In 1984 the sea ice extent was roughly the average of the minimum from 1979 to 2000, and so was a typical year.  The minimum sea ice extent in 2012 was roughly half of that average.</div></div></div>
<p>The effects of  <b>global warming in the Arctic</b>, or <b>climate change in the Arctic</b> include rising air and water temperatures, loss of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>, and melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a> with a related <a href="/wiki/Cold_blob_(North_Atlantic)" title="Cold blob (North Atlantic)">cold temperature anomaly</a>, observed since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-From_2_Satellites,_the_Big_Picture_on_Ice_Melt_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-From_2_Satellites,_the_Big_Picture_on_Ice_Melt-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-3">[3]</a></sup> Related impacts include ocean circulation changes, increased input of freshwater,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and ocean acidification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Indirect effects through potential climate teleconnections to mid latitudes may result in a greater frequency of extreme weather events (flooding, fires and drought),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> ecological, biological and phenology changes, biological migrations and extinctions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> natural resource stresses and as well as human health, displacement and security issues. Potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">methane releases</a> from the region, especially through the thawing of <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_clathrates" title="Methane clathrates">methane clathrates</a>, may occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Presently, the Arctic is warming twice as fast compared to the rest of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The pronounced warming signal, the <a href="/wiki/Polar_amplification" title="Polar amplification">amplified response of the Arctic</a> to global warming, is often seen as a leading indicator of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. The melting of Greenland's ice sheet is linked to polar amplification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> According to a study published in 2016, about 0.5◦C of the warming in the Arctic has been attributed to reductions in sulfate aerosols in Europe since 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_temperatures">Rising temperatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rising temperatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, "warming in the Arctic, as indicated by daily maximum and minimum temperatures, has been as great as in any other part of the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_wg2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_wg2-14">[14]</a></sup>  The period of 1995–2005 was the warmest decade in the Arctic since at least the 17th century, with temperatures 2 °C (3.6 °F) above the 1951–1990 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Przybylak_2007_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Przybylak_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> Some regions within the Arctic have warmed even more rapidly, with <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and western Canada's temperature rising by 3 to 4 °C (5.40 to 7.20 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACIA_2004_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACIA_2004-16">[16]</a></sup> This warming has been caused not only by the rise in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> concentration, but also the  deposition of <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> on Arctic ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quinn_et_al._2007_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quinn_et_al._2007-17">[17]</a></sup> A 2013 article published in <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Research_Letters" title="Geophysical Research Letters">Geophysical Research Letters</a> has shown that temperatures in the region haven't been as high as they currently are since at least 44,000 years ago and perhaps as long as 120,000 years ago. The authors conclude that "anthropogenic increases in greenhouse gases have led to unprecedented regional warmth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arctic_amplification">Arctic amplification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arctic amplification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_amplification" title="Arctic amplification">Arctic amplification</a></div>
<p>The poles of the Earth are more sensitive to any change in the planet's climate than the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>.  In the face of ongoing global warming, the poles are warming faster than lower latitudes.  The primary cause of this phenomenon is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>, whereby melting ice uncovers darker land or ocean beneath, which then absorbs more sunlight, causing more heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RCAmp_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-RCAmp-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature09051_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature09051-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black-22">[22]</a></sup> The loss of the Arctic sea ice may represent a <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping point</a> in global warming, when <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">'runaway' climate change</a> starts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005gl025080_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005gl025080-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2004gc000854_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-2004gc000854-24">[24]</a></sup> but on this point the science is not yet settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arctic_summer_sea_ice_loss_may_not_'tip'_over_the_edge_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arctic_summer_sea_ice_loss_may_not_'tip'_over_the_edge-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenman2009_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisenman2009-26">[26]</a></sup> According to a 2015 study, based on computer modelling of aerosols in the atmosphere, up to 0.5 degrees Celsius of the warming observed in the Arctic between 1980 and 2005 is due to aerosol reductions in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_carbon">Black carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Black carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">Black carbon</a> deposits (from the exhaust system of marine engines that often run on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunker_fuel" title="Bunker fuel">bunker fuel</a>) reduce the albedo when deposited on snow and ice, and thus accelerate the effect of the melting of snow and sea ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 2015 study, reductions in black carbon emissions and other minor greenhouse gases, by roughly 60 percent, could cool the Arctic up to 0.2 °C by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_sea_ice">Decline of sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline of sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a></div>
<p>Sea ice is currently in decline in area, extent, and volume and may cease to exist sometime during the 21st century.  Sea ice area refers to the total area covered by ice, whereas sea ice extent is the area of ocean with at least 15% sea ice, while the volume is the total amount of ice in the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_extent_and_area">Changes in extent and area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Changes in extent and area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seaice-1870-part-2009.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="840" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/220px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/330px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Seaice-1870-part-2009.png/440px-Seaice-1870-part-2009.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seaice-1870-part-2009.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1870–2009 Northern Hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Measurement_of_sea_ice#Sea_ice_extent" title="Measurement of sea ice">sea ice extent</a> in million square kilometers. Blue shading indicates the pre-satellite era; data then is less reliable. In particular, the near-constant level extent in autumn up to 1940 reflects lack of data rather than a real lack of variation.</div></div></div>
<p>Reliable <a href="/wiki/Measurement_of_sea_ice" title="Measurement of sea ice">measurement of sea ice</a> edges began with the satellite era in the late 1970s. Before this time, sea ice area and extent were monitored less precisely by a combination of ships, buoys and aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whither_Arctic_sea_ice?_A_clear_signal_of_decline_regionally,_seasonally_and_extending_beyond_the_satellite_record_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whither_Arctic_sea_ice?_A_clear_signal_of_decline_regionally,_seasonally_and_extending_beyond_the_satellite_record-31">[31]</a></sup> The data show a long-term negative trend in recent years, attributed to global warming, although there is also a considerable amount of variation from year to year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASASeesArcticOcean_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASASeesArcticOcean-32">[32]</a></sup> Some of this variation may be related to effects such as the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>, which may itself be related to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Arctic_and_Antarctic_Oscillations_and_their_Projected_Changes_Under_Global_Warming_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Arctic_and_Antarctic_Oscillations_and_their_Projected_Changes_Under_Global_Warming-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Arctic sea ice September minimum extent (i.e., area with at least 15% sea ice coverage) reached new record lows in 2002, 2005, 2007, and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The 2007 melt season let to a minimum 39% below the 1979–2000 average, and for the first time in human memory, the fabled <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> opened completely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsidc_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsidc-35">[35]</a></sup> The dramatic 2007 melting surprised and concerned scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Remarkable_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Remarkable-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_-_Monitoring_Sea_Ice_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_-_Monitoring_Sea_Ice-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3375" data-file-width="3000" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png/220px-Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png/330px-Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png/440px-Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oldest_Arctic_Sea_Ice_is_Disappearing.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea ice coverage in 1980 (bottom) and 2012 (top), as observed by passive microwave sensors on NASA’s Nimbus-7 satellite and by the Special Sensor Microwave Imager/Sounder (SSMIS) from the Defense Meteorological Satellite Program (DMSP). Multi-year ice is shown in bright white, while average sea ice cover is shown in light blue to milky white. The data shows the ice cover for the period of 1 November through 31 January in their respective years.</div></div></div>
<p>From 2008 to 2011, Arctic sea ice minimum extent was higher than 2007, but it did not return to the levels of previous years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsidc1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsidc1-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arcticseaiceextent_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arcticseaiceextent-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2012 however, the 2007 record low was broken in late August with three weeks still left in the melt season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsidc2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsidc2-40">[40]</a></sup> It continued to fall, bottoming out on 16 September 2012 at 3.41 million square kilometers (1.32 million square miles), or 760,000 square kilometers (293,000 square miles) below the previous low set on 18 September 2007 and 50% below the 1979–2000 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsidc3_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsidc3-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_Arctic_sea_ice_minimum_confirmed_by_NSIDC_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Record_Arctic_sea_ice_minimum_confirmed_by_NSIDC-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rate of the decline in entire Arctic ice coverage is accelerating. From 1979–1996, the average per decade decline in entire ice coverage was a 2.2% decline in ice extent and a 3% decline in ice area. For the decade ending 2008, these values have risen to 10.1% and 10.7%, respectively. These are comparable to the September to September loss rates in year-round ice (i.e., perennial ice, which survives throughout the year), which averaged a retreat of 10.2% and 11.4% per decade, respectively, for the period 1979–2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accelerated_decline_in_Arctic_sea_ice_cover_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Accelerated_decline_in_Arctic_sea_ice_cover-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_volume">Changes in volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changes in volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="516" data-file-width="688" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/220px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/330px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg/440px-Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plot_arctic_sea_ice_volume.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seasonal variation and long-term decrease of Arctic sea ice volume as determined by measurement backed numerical modelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zhangrothrock1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-zhangrothrock1-44">[44]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The sea ice thickness field and accordingly the ice volume and mass, is much more difficult to determine than the extension. Exact measurements can be made only at a limited number of points. Because of large variations in ice and snow thickness and consistency air- and spaceborne-measurements have to be evaluated carefully.  Nevertheless, the studies made support the assumption of a dramatic decline in ice age and thickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arcticseaiceextent_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arcticseaiceextent-39">[39]</a></sup> While the Arctic ice area and extent show an accelerating downward trend, arctic ice volume shows an even sharper decline than the ice coverage. Since 1979, the ice volume has shrunk by 80% and in just the past decade the volume declined by 36% in the autumn and 9% in the winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="An_end_to_summer_sea_ice.3F"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="An_end_to_summer_sea_ice?">An end to summer sea ice?</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: An end to summer sea ice?">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The IPCC's Fourth Assessment Report in 2007 summarized the current state of sea ice projections: "the projected reduction [in global sea ice cover] is accelerated in the Arctic, where some models project summer sea ice cover to disappear entirely in the high-emission A2 scenario in the latter part of the 21st century.″ <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_Change_2007:_The_Physical_Science_Basis._Contribution_of_Working_Group_I_to_the_Fourth_Assessment_Report_of_the_Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change,_Chapter_10_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_Change_2007:_The_Physical_Science_Basis._Contribution_of_Working_Group_I_to_the_Fourth_Assessment_Report_of_the_Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change,_Chapter_10-46">[46]</a></sup> However, current <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a> frequently underestimate the rate of sea ice retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007gl029703_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-2007gl029703-47">[47]</a></sup> A summertime ice-free Arctic would be unprecedented in recent geologic history, as currently <a href="/wiki/Scientific_evidence" title="Scientific evidence">scientific evidence</a> does not indicate an ice-free polar sea anytime in the last 700,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overpeck_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-overpeck-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ottera_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ottera-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_ocean" title="Arctic ocean">Arctic ocean</a> will likely be free of summer <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> before the year 2100, but many different dates have been projected. One study suggests 2060–2080<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngeo467_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngeo467-50">[50]</a></sup> and another suggests 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arctic_Largely_Ice_Free_in_Summer_Within_Ten_Years?_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arctic_Largely_Ice_Free_in_Summer_Within_Ten_Years?-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> A 2013 study showed that simply extending summertime ice melting trends into the future in a straight line predicts an ice-free summertime Arctic as early as by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permafrost_thaw">Permafrost thaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Permafrost thaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG/220px-Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG/330px-Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG/440px-Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_Permafrost1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rapidly thawing Arctic permafrost and coastal erosion on the Beaufort Sea, Arctic Ocean, near Point Lonely, AK. Photo Taken in August 2013</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="4088" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/220px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/330px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/440px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Permafrost thaw ponds on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a></div>
<p>This century, thawing of the various types of Arctic permafrost could release large amounts of carbon into the atmosphere. It has been estimated that about two-thirds of released carbon escapes to the atmosphere as carbon dioxide, originating primarily from ancient ice deposits along the ~7,000 kilometer long coastline of the East Siberian Arctic Shelf (ESAS) and shallow subsea permafrost. Following thaw, collapse and erosion of coastline and seafloor deposits may accelerate with Arctic amplification of climate warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate models suggest that during periods of rapid sea-ice loss, temperatures could increase as far as 1,450 km (900 mi) inland, accelerating the rate of terrestrial permafrost thaw, with consequential effects on carbon and methane release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yale_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-yale-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucar.edu_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucar.edu-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2018, modeling of the permafrost carbon feedback has focused on gradual surface thawing, models have yet to account for deeper soil layers. A new study used field observations, radiocarbon dating, and remote sensing to account for <a href="/wiki/Thermokarst" title="Thermokarst">thermokarst</a> lakes, the authors concluded that, "..methane and carbon dioxide emissions from abrupt thaw beneath thermokarst lakes will more than double radiative forcing from circumpolar permafrost-soil carbon fluxes this century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsea_permafrost">Subsea permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Subsea permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">Clathrate gun hypothesis</a></div>
<p>Subsea permafrost occurs beneath the seabed and exists in the continental shelves of the polar regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This source of methane is different from methane clathrates, but contributes to the overall outcome and feedbacks.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">Sea ice</a> serves to stabilise methane deposits on and near the shoreline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005gl022751_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005gl022751-60">[60]</a></sup> preventing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> breaking down and venting into the water column and eventually reaching the atmosphere. From sonar measurements in recent years researchers quantified the density of bubbles emanating from the subsea permafrost into the Ocean (a process called ebullition), and found that 100–630 mg methane per square meters is emitted daily along the East Siberian Shelf, into the water column. They also found that during storms, methane levels in the water column drop dramatically, when wind-driven air-sea gas exchange accelerates the ebullition process into the atmosphere. This observed pathway suggests that methane from seabed permafrost will progress rather slowly, instead of abrupt changes. However, Arctic cyclones, fueled by <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and further accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere could contribute to more release from this methane cache, which is really important for the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> An update to the mechanisms of this permafrost degradation, implying the possibility of being close to an acceleration of methane release was published in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_vegetation">Changes in vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Changes in vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg/220px-Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg/330px-Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg/440px-Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bloody_Falls_Campsite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bloody_Falls" title="Bloody Falls">Bloody Falls</a> in July 2007.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_(WH).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1250" data-file-width="2000" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png/220px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png/330px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png/440px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28WH%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_(WH).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Western Hemisphere Arctic Vegetation Index Trend</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_(EH).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1250" data-file-width="2000" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png/220px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png/330px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png/440px-Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_%28EH%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic_Vegetation_Index_Trend_(EH).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eastern Hemisphere Vegetation Index Trend</div></div></div>
<p>Changes in vegetation are associated with the increases in landscape scale <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Methane_is_a_greenhouse_gas_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Methane_is_a_greenhouse_gas-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The growing season has lengthened in the far northern latitudes, bringing major changes to plant communities in tundra and boreal (also known as taiga) ecosystems.
</p><p>For decades, NASA and NOAA satellites have continuously monitored vegetation from space. The Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) and Advanced Very High-Resolution Radiometer (AVHRR) instruments measure the intensity of visible and near-infrared light reflecting off of plant leaves. Scientists use the information to calculate the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), an indicator of photosynthetic activity or “greenness” of the landscape.
</p><p>The maps above show the Arctic Vegetation Index Trend between July 1982 and December 2011 in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>. Shades of green depict areas where plant productivity and abundance increased; shades of brown show where photosynthetic activity declined.  The maps show a ring of greening in the treeless tundra ecosystems of the circumpolar Arctic—the northernmost parts of Canada, Russia, and Scandinavia. Tall shrubs and trees started to grow in areas that were previously dominated by tundra grasses. The researchers concluded that plant growth had increased by 7 to 10 percent overall.
</p><p>However, boreal forests, particularly those in North America, showed a different response to warming. Many boreal forests greened, but the trend was not as strong as it was for tundra of the circumpolar Arctic. In North America, some boreal forests actually experienced “browning” (less photosynthetic activity) over the study period. Droughts, forest fire activity, animal and insect behavior, industrial pollution, and a number of other factors may have contributed to the browning.
</p><p>"Satellite data identify areas in the boreal zone that are warmer and drier and other areas that are warmer and wetter," explained co-author Ramakrishna Nemani of NASA’s Ames Research Center. "Only the warmer and wetter areas support more growth."
</p><p>"We found more plant growth in the boreal zone from 1982 to 1992 than from 1992 to 2011, because water limitations were encountered in the later two decades of our study," added co-author Sangram Ganguly of the Bay Area Environmental Research Institute and NASA Ames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Greening_Arctic_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Greening_Arctic-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>The less severe winters in tundra areas allow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Alder" title="Alder">alders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Betula_nana" title="Betula nana">dwarf birch</a> to replace <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichens</a>. The impact on mosses and lichens is unclear as there exist very few studies at species level, also climate change is more likely to cause increased fluctuation and more frequent extreme events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The feedback effect of shrubs on the tundra's permafrost is unclear. In the winter they trap more snow which insulates the permafrost from extreme cold spells, but in the summer they shade the ground from direct sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>  The warming is likely to cause changes in the plant communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Except for an increase in shurbs, warming may also cause a decline in cushion plants such as moss campion. Since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cushion_plants" title="Cushion plants">cushion plants</a> act as facilitator species across <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a> and fill important roles in severe environments this could cause cascading effects in the ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Rising summer temperature melts on Canada's Baffin Island have revealed moss previously covered which has not seen daylight in 44,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduction of sea ice has boosted the productivity of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> by about twenty percent over the past thirty years. However, the effect on <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a> is unclear, since the larger types of phytoplankton, which are the preferred food source of most <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, do not appear to have increased as much as the smaller types. So far, Arctic phytoplankton have not had a significant impact on the global carbon cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In summer, the melt ponds on young and thin ice have allowed sunlight to penetrate the ice, in turn allowing phytoplankton to bloom in unexpected concentrations, although it is unknown just how long this phenomenon has been occurring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unprecedented_Blooms_of_Ocean_Plant_Life_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unprecedented_Blooms_of_Ocean_Plant_Life-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_for_animals">Changes for animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Changes for animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_marine_mammals" title="Effects of global warming on marine mammals">Effects of global warming on marine mammals</a></div>
<p>The northward shift of the <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subarctic climate</a> zone is allowing animals that are adapted to that climate to move into the far north, where they are replacing species that are more adapted to a pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_climate" title="Arctic climate">Arctic climate</a>. Where the Arctic species are not being replaced outright, they are often <a href="/wiki/Grizzly%E2%80%93polar_bear_hybrid" title="Grizzly–polar bear hybrid">interbreeding</a> with their southern relations. Among slow-breeding <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> species, this often has the effect of reducing the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a>. Another concern is the spread of <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infectious diseases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phocine_distemper_virus" title="Phocine distemper virus">phocine distemper virus</a>, to previously untouched populations. This is a particular danger among <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a> who were previously segregated by sea ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polar_Bear_Habitat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="879" data-file-width="981" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polar_Bear_Habitat.png/220px-Polar_Bear_Habitat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polar_Bear_Habitat.png/330px-Polar_Bear_Habitat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polar_Bear_Habitat.png/440px-Polar_Bear_Habitat.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polar_Bear_Habitat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Projected change in polar bear habitat from 2001–2010 to 2041–2050</div></div></div>
<p>3 April 2007, the <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Federation" title="National Wildlife Federation">National Wildlife Federation</a> urged the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> to place <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Protection_For_Polar_Bears_Urged_By_National_Wildlife_Federation_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Protection_For_Polar_Bears_Urged_By_National_Wildlife_Federation-73">[73]</a></sup>
Four months later, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> completed a year-long study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uncertainty_in_Climate_Model_Projections_of_Arctic_Sea_Ice_Decline:_An_Evaluation_Relevant_to_Polar_Bears_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uncertainty_in_Climate_Model_Projections_of_Arctic_Sea_Ice_Decline:_An_Evaluation_Relevant_to_Polar_Bears-74">[74]</a></sup> which concluded in part that the floating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> will continue its rapid shrinkage over the next 50 years, consequently wiping out much of the polar bear <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. The bears would disappear from Alaska, but would continue to exist in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_Archipelago" title="Canadian Arctic Archipelago">Canadian Arctic Archipelago</a> and areas off the northern Greenland coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warming_Is_Seen_as_Wiping_Out_Most_Polar_Bears_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warming_Is_Seen_as_Wiping_Out_Most_Polar_Bears-75">[75]</a></sup> Secondary ecological effects are also resultant from the shrinkage of sea ice; for example, <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a> are denied their historic length of seal hunting season due to late formation and early thaw of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pack_ice" title="Pack ice">pack ice</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Melting_of_the_Greenland_Ice_Sheet">Melting of the Greenland Ice Sheet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Melting of the Greenland Ice Sheet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_Albedo_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1584" data-file-width="1438" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Greenland_Albedo_Change.png/220px-Greenland_Albedo_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Greenland_Albedo_Change.png/330px-Greenland_Albedo_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Greenland_Albedo_Change.png/440px-Greenland_Albedo_Change.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_Albedo_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Albedo Change on Greenland</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1392" data-file-width="1268" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg/220px-Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg/330px-Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg/440px-Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_Ice_Mass_Trend.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenland_Ice_Sheet" title="Greenland Ice Sheet">Greenland Ice Sheet</a> Mass Trend 2003–2005</div></div></div>
<p>Models predict a sea-level contribution of about 5 centimetres (2 in) from melting in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> during the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-76">[76]</a></sup> It is also predicted that Greenland will become warm enough by 2100 to begin an almost complete melt during the next 1,000 years or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climatology:_threatened_loss_of_the_Greenland_ice-sheet_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climatology:_threatened_loss_of_the_Greenland_ice-sheet-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regional_Sea_Level_Change_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Sea_Level_Change-78">[78]</a></sup> In early July 2012, 97% percent of the Ice Sheet experienced some form of surface melt including the summits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice thickness measurements from the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a> satellite indicate that ice mass loss is accelerating. For the period 2002–2009, the rate of loss increased from −137 Gt/yr to −286 Gt/yr, with an acceleration of −30 gigatonnes per year per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Increasing_rates_of_ice_mass_loss_from_the_Greenland_and_Antarctic_ice_sheets_revealed_by_GRACE_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Increasing_rates_of_ice_mass_loss_from_the_Greenland_and_Antarctic_ice_sheets_revealed_by_GRACE-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_ocean_circulation">Effect on ocean circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effect on ocean circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_anoxic_event" title="Ocean anoxic event">Ocean anoxic event</a></div>
<p>Although this is now thought unlikely in the near future, it has also been suggested that there could be a <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a>, similar to that which is believed to have driven the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a> event. There is also potentially a possibility of a more general disruption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" title="Ocean circulation">ocean circulation</a>, which may lead to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_anoxic_event" title="Ocean anoxic event">ocean anoxic event</a>, although these are believed to be much more common in the distant past. It is unclear whether the appropriate pre-conditions for such an event exist today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_claims">Territorial claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Territorial claims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_the_Arctic" title="Territorial claims in the Arctic">Territorial claims in the Arctic</a></div>
<p>Growing evidence that global warming is shrinking polar ice has added to the urgency of several nations' <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_the_Arctic" title="Territorial claims in the Arctic">Arctic territorial claims</a> in hopes of establishing resource development and new shipping lanes, in addition to protecting sovereign rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Danish Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Per_Stig_M%C3%B8ller" title="Per Stig Møller">Per Stig Møller</a> and Greenland's Premier <a href="/wiki/Hans_Enoksen" title="Hans Enoksen">Hans Enoksen</a> invited <a href="/wiki/Foreign_minister" title="Foreign minister">foreign ministers</a> from Canada, Norway, Russia and the United States to <a href="/wiki/Ilulissat" title="Ilulissat">Ilulissat, Greenland</a> for a summit in May 2008 to discuss how to divide borders in the changing Arctic region, and a discussion on more cooperation against climate change affecting the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUX_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUX-82">[82]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean_Conference" title="Arctic Ocean Conference">Arctic Ocean Conference</a>, Foreign Ministers and other officials representing the five countries announced the <a href="/wiki/Ilulissat_Declaration" title="Ilulissat Declaration">Ilulissat Declaration</a> on 28 May 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conference_in_Ilulissat,_Greenland:_Landmark_political_declaration_on_the_future_of_the_Arctic_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conference_in_Ilulissat,_Greenland:_Landmark_political_declaration_on_the_future_of_the_Arctic-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Ilulissat_Declaration_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Ilulissat_Declaration-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_impacts">Social impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Social impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>People are affecting the geographic space of the Arctic and the Arctic is affecting the population. Much of the climate change in the Arctic can be attributed to humans influences on the atmosphere, such as an increased <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>  caused by the increase in CO<sub>2</sub> due to the burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Climate change is having a direct impact on the people that live in the Arctic, as well as other societies around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>The warming environment presents challenges to local communities such as the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>. Hunting, which is a major way of survival for some small communities, will be changed with increasing temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethz.ch_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-ethz.ch-87">[87]</a></sup> The reduction of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> will cause certain species populations to decline or even become extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup> In good years, some communities are fully employed by the commercial harvest of certain animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethz.ch_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-ethz.ch-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since October 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> The harvest of different animals fluctuates each year and with the rise of temperatures it is likely to continue changing and creating issues for Inuit hunters. Unsuspected changes in river and snow conditions will cause herds of animals, including reindeer, to change migration patterns, <a href="/wiki/Birth#Cattle" title="Birth">calving</a> grounds, and <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> availability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-2"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other forms of transportation in the Arctic have seen negative impacts from the current warming, with some transportation routes and pipelines on land being disrupted by the melting of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-3"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup> Many Arctic communities rely on frozen roadways to transport supplies and travel from area to area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-4"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup> The changing landscape and unpredictability of weather is creating new challenges in the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Transpolar_Sea_Route" title="Transpolar Sea Route">Transpolar Sea Route</a> is a future Arctic shipping lane running from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean across the center of the Arctic Ocean. The route is also sometimes called Trans-Arctic Route. In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Passage" title="Northeast Passage">Northeast Passage</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Sea_Route" title="Northern Sea Route">Northern Sea Route</a>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North-West_Passage" title="North-West Passage">North-West Passage</a> it largely avoids the territorial waters of Arctic states and lies in international high seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>Governments and private industry have shown a growing interest in the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Major new shipping lanes are opening up: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Sea_Route" title="Northern Sea Route">the northern sea route</a> had 34 passages in 2011 while the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> had 22 traverses, more than any time in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Shipping companies may benefit from the shortened distance of these northern routes. Access to natural resources will increase, including valuable minerals and offshore oil and gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-5"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup> Finding and controlling these resources will be difficult with the continually moving ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-6"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup> Tourism may also increase as less sea ice will improve safety and accessibility to the Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateImpactAssessment_86-7"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateImpactAssessment-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>The melting of Arctic ice caps is likely to increase traffic in and the commercial viability of the Northern Sea Route. One study, for instance, projects, "remarkable shifts in trade flows between Asia and Europe, diversion of trade within Europe, heavy shipping traffic in the Arctic and a substantial drop in Suez traffic. Projected shifts in trade also imply substantial pressure on an already threatened Arctic ecosystem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National">National</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: National">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Individual countries within the Arctic zone, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>), <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>) conduct independent research through a variety of organizations and agencies, public and private, such as Russia's <a href="/wiki/Arctic_and_Antarctic_Research_Institute" title="Arctic and Antarctic Research Institute">Arctic and Antarctic Research Institute</a>. Countries who do not have Arctic claims, but are close neighbors, conduct Arctic research as well, such as the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Arctic_and_Antarctic_Administration" title="Chinese Arctic and Antarctic Administration">Chinese Arctic and Antarctic Administration</a> (CAA).  The United States's <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) produces an <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Report_Card" title="Arctic Report Card">Arctic Report Card</a> annually, containing <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed</a> information on recent observations of environmental conditions in the Arctic relative to historical records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_the_Arctic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>International cooperative research between nations has become increasingly important:
</p>
<ul><li>Arctic <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> is summarized by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) in its series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC#Assessment_reports" title="IPCC">Assessment Reports</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Climate_Impact_Assessment" title="Arctic Climate Impact Assessment">Arctic Climate Impact Assessment</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA) launched <a href="/wiki/CryoSat#CryoSat_II" title="CryoSat">CryoSat-2</a> on 8 April 2010. It provides satellite data on Arctic ice cover change rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-esa-95">[95]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Arctic_Buoy_Program" title="International Arctic Buoy Program">International Arctic Buoy Program</a>: deploys and maintains buoys that provide real-time position, pressure, temperature, and interpolated ice velocity data</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Arctic_Research_Center" title="International Arctic Research Center">International Arctic Research Center</a>: Main participants are the United States and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Arctic_Science_Committee" title="International Arctic Science Committee">International Arctic Science Committee</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental" title="Non-governmental">non-governmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a> (NGO) with diverse membership, including 23 countries from 3 continents.</li>
<li>'Role of the Arctic Region', in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/International_Polar_Year" title="International Polar Year">International Polar Year</a>, was the focus of the second international conference on Global Change Research, held in <a href="/wiki/Nyn%C3%A4shamn" title="Nynäshamn">Nynäshamn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, October 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Environmental_Change_–_The_Role_of_the_Arctic_Region_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Environmental_Change_–_The_Role_of_the_Arctic_Region-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E_SF,_VR,_FORMAS_sign_MOU_to_promote_Global_Environmental_Change_Research_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-E_SF,_VR,_FORMAS_sign_MOU_to_promote_Global_Environmental_Change_Research-97">[97]</a></sup></li>
<li>SEARCH (<a href="/wiki/Study_of_Environmental_Arctic_Change" title="Study of Environmental Arctic Change">Study of Environmental Arctic Change</a>): A research framework originally promoted by several US agencies; an international extension is ISAC (the International Study of Arctic Change<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>).</li></ul>
