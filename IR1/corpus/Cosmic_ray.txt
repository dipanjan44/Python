<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cosmic ray</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">High-energy particle, mainly originating outside the Solar system</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Cosmic radiation" redirects here. For some other types of cosmic radiation, see <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation" title="Cosmic background radiation">Cosmic background radiation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cosmic_background_(disambiguation)" title="Cosmic background (disambiguation)">Cosmic background (disambiguation)</a>. </p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="400" height="432" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg/360px-Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg/540px-Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg/720px-Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg.png 2x" width="360"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosmic_ray_flux_versus_particle_energy.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cosmic <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> versus particle energy</div></div></div>
<p><b>Cosmic rays</b> are <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">high-energy radiation</a>, mainly originating outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and even from distant galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Upon impact with the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>, cosmic rays can produce <a href="/wiki/Air_shower_(physics)" title="Air shower (physics)">showers of secondary particles</a> that sometimes reach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_surface" title="Earth's surface">surface</a>. Composed primarily of high-energy <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" title="Atomic nuclei">atomic nuclei</a>, they are of uncertain origin. Data from the <a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">Fermi Space Telescope</a> (2013)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ackermann-2013_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ackermann-2013-3">[3]</a></sup> have been interpreted as evidence that a significant fraction of primary cosmic rays originate from the <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> explosions of stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinholster-2013_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pinholster-2013-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">Active galactic nuclei</a> also appear to produce cosmic rays, based on observations of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> from <a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">blazar</a> <a href="/wiki/TXS_0506%2B056" title="TXS 0506+056">TXS 0506+056</a> in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>ray</i> is somewhat of a misnomer due to a historical accident, as cosmic rays were at first, and wrongly, thought to be mostly <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>. In common scientific usage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> high-energy particles with intrinsic mass are known as "cosmic" rays, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a>, which are quanta of electromagnetic radiation (and so have no intrinsic mass) are known by their common names, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a></i>, depending on their <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Massive_cosmic_rays_compared_to_photons">Massive cosmic rays compared to photons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Massive cosmic rays compared to photons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In current usage, the term <i>cosmic ray</i> almost exclusively refers to <a href="/wiki/Massive_particle" title="Massive particle">massive particles</a> – those that have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> – as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, which have no rest mass. Massive particles have additional, <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic</a>, mass-energy when they are moving, due to <a href="/wiki/Mass_in_special_relativity" title="Mass in special relativity">relativistic effects</a>. Through this process, some particles acquire tremendously high mass-energies. These are significantly higher than the <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a> of even the highest-energy photons detected to date. The energy of the massless photon depends solely on <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">frequency</a>, not speed, as photons always travel at the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">same speed</a>. At the higher end of the energy spectrum, relativistic kinetic energy is the main source of the mass-energy of cosmic rays.
</p><p>The highest-energy <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermionic</a> cosmic rays detected to date, such as the <a href="/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle" title="Oh-My-God particle">Oh-My-God particle</a>, had an energy of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7020300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>20</sup> <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a></span>, while the highest-energy gamma rays to be observed, <a href="/wiki/Very-high-energy_gamma_ray" title="Very-high-energy gamma ray">very-high-energy gamma rays</a>, are photons with energies of up to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6995160217648700000♠</span>10<sup>14</sup> eV</span>. Hence, the highest-energy detected fermionic cosmic rays are about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006300000000000000♠</span>3<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>6</sup></span> times as energetic as the highest-energy detected cosmic photons.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of primary cosmic rays, which originate outside of Earth's atmosphere, about 99% are the nuclei of well-known atoms (stripped of their electron shells), and about 1% are solitary electrons (similar to <a href="/wiki/Beta_particle" title="Beta particle">beta particles</a>). Of the nuclei, about 90% are simple <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> (i.e., hydrogen nuclei); 9% are <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>, identical to helium nuclei; and 1% are the nuclei of heavier elements, called <a href="/wiki/HZE_ions" title="HZE ions">HZE ions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goddard-2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-goddard-2012-8">[8]</a></sup> A very small fraction are stable particles of <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiprotons</a>. The precise nature of this remaining fraction is an area of active research. An active search from Earth orbit for anti-alpha particles has failed to detect them.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cosmic rays attract great interest practically, due to the damage they inflict on microelectronics and life outside the protection of an atmosphere and magnetic field, and scientifically, because the energies of the most energetic <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray" title="Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray">ultra-high-energy cosmic rays</a> (UHECRs) have been observed to approach <span class="nowrap">3 × 10<sup>20</sup> eV</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nerlich-2011_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nerlich-2011-9">[9]</a></sup> about 40 million times the energy of particles accelerated by the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhc1-cern_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-lhc1-cern-10">[10]</a></sup> One can show that such enormous energies might be achieved by means of the <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_mechanism_of_acceleration" title="Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration">centrifugal mechanism of acceleration</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">active galactic nuclei</a>. At 50<span class="nowrap"> </span>J,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaensler-2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaensler-2013-11">[11]</a></sup> the highest-energy ultra-high-energy cosmic rays have energies comparable to the kinetic energy of a 90-kilometre-per-hour (56 mph) baseball. As a result of these discoveries, there has been interest in investigating cosmic rays of even greater energies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Most cosmic rays, however, do not have such extreme energies; the energy distribution of cosmic rays peaks on 0.3 gigaelectronvolts (4.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−11</sup> J).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hyperphysics-2012_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hyperphysics-2012-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactivity</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Becquerel" title="Henri Becquerel">Henri Becquerel</a> in 1896, it was generally believed that atmospheric electricity, <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>, was caused only by <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> from radioactive elements in the ground or the radioactive gases or isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a> they produce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malley2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malley2011-14">[14]</a></sup> Measurements of ionization rates at increasing heights above the ground during the decade from 1900 to 1910 showed a decrease that could be explained as due to absorption of the ionizing radiation by the intervening air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-North2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-North2008-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
In 1909, <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Wulf" title="Theodor Wulf">Theodor Wulf</a> developed an <a href="/wiki/Electrometer" title="Electrometer">electrometer</a>, a device to measure the rate of ion production inside a hermetically sealed container, and used it to show higher levels of radiation at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a> than at its base. However, his paper published in <i><a href="/wiki/Physikalische_Zeitschrift" title="Physikalische Zeitschrift">Physikalische Zeitschrift</a></i> was not widely accepted. In 1911, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Pacini" title="Domenico Pacini">Domenico Pacini</a> observed simultaneous variations of the rate of ionization over a lake, over the sea, and at a depth of 3 metres from the surface. Pacini concluded from the decrease of radioactivity underwater that a certain part of the ionization must be due to sources other than the radioactivity of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pacini_measurement.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="997" data-file-width="1375" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pacini_measurement.jpg/160px-Pacini_measurement.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pacini_measurement.jpg/240px-Pacini_measurement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Pacini_measurement.jpg/320px-Pacini_measurement.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pacini_measurement.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pacini makes a measurement in 1910.</div></div></div><p>
In 1912, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Francis_Hess" title="Victor Francis Hess">Victor Hess</a> carried three enhanced-accuracy Wulf electrometers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HessNobelPresSp_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-HessNobelPresSp-17">[17]</a></sup> to an altitude of 5,300 metres in a <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">free balloon</a> flight. He found the ionization rate increased approximately fourfold over the rate at ground level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HessNobelPresSp_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-HessNobelPresSp-17">[17]</a></sup> Hess ruled out the Sun as the radiation's source by making a balloon ascent during a near-total eclipse. With the moon blocking much of the Sun's visible radiation, Hess still measured rising radiation at rising altitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HessNobelPresSp_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-HessNobelPresSp-17">[17]</a></sup> He concluded that "The  results  of  the    observations seem most likely to be explained by the assumption that radiation of very high penetrating power enters from above into our atmosphere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1913–1914, <a href="/wiki/Werner_Kolh%C3%B6rster" title="Werner Kolhörster">Werner Kolhörster</a> confirmed Victor Hess's earlier results by measuring the increased ionization enthalpy rate at an altitude of 9 km.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HessKol.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HessKol.jpg/220px-HessKol.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HessKol.jpg/330px-HessKol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HessKol.jpg/440px-HessKol.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HessKol.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Increase of ionization with altitude as measured by Hess in 1912 (left) and by Kolhörster (right)</div></div></div><p> Hess received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> in 1936 for his discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Hess balloon flight took place on 7 August 1912. By sheer coincidence, exactly 100 years later on 7 August 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> rover used its <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">Radiation Assessment Detector</a> (RAD) instrument to begin measuring the radiation levels on another planet for the first time. On 31 May 2013, NASA scientists reported that a possible <a href="/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars" title="Human mission to Mars">manned mission to Mars</a> may involve a greater <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation risk</a> than previously believed, based on the amount of <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">energetic particle radiation</a> detected by the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">RAD</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> while traveling from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in 2011–2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531a_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531b_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531b-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20130530_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20130530-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hessballon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1018" data-file-width="876" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/220px-Hessballon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/330px-Hessballon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Hessballon.jpg/440px-Hessballon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hessballon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hess lands after his balloon flight in 1912.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bruno_Rossi" title="Bruno Rossi">Bruno Rossi</a> wrote that:
</p>
<blockquote><p>In the late 1920s and early 1930s the technique of self-recording electroscopes carried by balloons into the highest layers of the atmosphere or sunk to great depths under water was brought to an unprecedented degree of perfection by the German physicist <a href="/wiki/Erich_Regener" title="Erich Regener">Erich Regener</a> and his group. To these scientists we owe some of the most accurate measurements ever made of cosmic-ray ionization as a function of altitude and depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> stated in 1931 that "thanks to the fine experiments of Professor Millikan and the even more far-reaching experiments of Professor Regener, we have now got for the first time, a curve of absorption of these radiations in water which we may safely rely upon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1920s, the term <i>cosmic rays</i> was coined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Millikan" title="Robert Millikan">Robert Millikan</a> who made measurements of ionization due to cosmic rays from deep under water to high altitudes and around the globe. Millikan believed that his measurements proved that the primary cosmic rays were gamma rays; i.e., energetic photons. And he proposed a theory that they were produced in interstellar space as by-products of the fusion of hydrogen atoms into the heavier elements, and that secondary <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> were produced in the atmosphere by <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">Compton scattering</a> of gamma rays. But then, sailing from <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> to the Netherlands in 1927, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Clay" title="Jacob Clay">Jacob Clay</a> found evidence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clay-1927_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-clay-1927-26">[26]</a></sup> later confirmed in many experiments, of a variation of cosmic ray intensity with latitude, which indicated that the primary cosmic rays are deflected by the geomagnetic field and must therefore be charged particles, not photons. In 1929, <a href="/wiki/Walther_Bothe" title="Walther Bothe">Bothe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Werner_Kolh%C3%B6rster" title="Werner Kolhörster">Kolhörster</a> discovered charged cosmic-ray particles that could penetrate 4.1 cm of gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bothe-korlhorster-1929_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bothe-korlhorster-1929-27">[27]</a></sup> Charged particles of such high energy could not possibly be produced by photons from Millikan's proposed interstellar fusion process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 1930, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Rossi" title="Bruno Rossi">Bruno Rossi</a> predicted a difference between the intensities of cosmic rays arriving from the east and the west that depends upon the charge of the primary particles—the so-called "east-west effect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossi-1930_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-rossi-1930-28">[28]</a></sup> Three independent experiments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnson-1933_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-1933-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alvarez-compton-1933_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-alvarez-compton-1933-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossi-1934_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-rossi-1934-31">[31]</a></sup> found that the intensity is, in fact, greater from the west, proving that most primaries are positive. During the years from 1930 to 1945, a wide variety of investigations confirmed that the primary cosmic rays are mostly protons, and the secondary radiation produced in the atmosphere is primarily electrons, photons and <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a>. In 1948, observations with nuclear emulsions carried by balloons to near the top of the atmosphere showed that approximately 10% of the primaries are helium nuclei (<a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>) and 1% are heavier nuclei of the elements such as carbon, iron, and lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freier-1948_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-freier-1948-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freier-1948b_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-freier-1948b-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>During a test of his equipment for measuring the east-west effect, Rossi observed that the rate of near-simultaneous discharges of two widely separated <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counters</a> was larger than the expected accidental rate. In his report on the experiment, Rossi wrote "... it seems that once in a while the recording equipment is struck by very extensive showers of particles, which causes coincidences between the counters, even placed at large distances from one another."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossi-1934b_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-rossi-1934b-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1937 <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Victor_Auger" title="Pierre Victor Auger">Pierre Auger</a>, unaware of Rossi's earlier report, detected the same phenomenon and investigated it in some detail. He concluded that high-energy primary cosmic-ray particles interact with air nuclei high in the atmosphere, initiating a cascade of secondary interactions that ultimately yield a shower of electrons, and photons that reach ground level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rmp11_3_4_288_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-rmp11_3_4_288-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Soviet physicist Sergey Vernov was the first to use <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosondes</a> to perform cosmic ray readings with an instrument carried to high altitude by a balloon. On 1 April 1935, he took measurements at heights up to 13.6 kilometres using a pair of <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counters</a> in an anti-coincidence circuit to avoid counting secondary ray showers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Homi_J._Bhabha" title="Homi J. Bhabha">Homi J. Bhabha</a> derived an expression for the probability of scattering positrons by electrons, a process now known as <a href="/wiki/Bhabha_scattering" title="Bhabha scattering">Bhabha scattering</a>. His classic paper, jointly with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Heitler" title="Walter Heitler">Walter Heitler</a>, published in 1937 described how primary cosmic rays from space interact with the upper atmosphere to produce particles observed at the ground level. Bhabha and Heitler explained the cosmic ray shower formation by the cascade production of gamma rays and positive and negative electron pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BhabhaHeitler1937_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-BhabhaHeitler1937-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_distribution">Energy distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energy distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Measurements of the energy and arrival directions of the ultra-high-energy primary cosmic rays by the techniques of <i>density sampling</i> and <i>fast timing</i> of extensive air showers were first carried out in 1954 by members of the Rossi Cosmic Ray Group at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossi-1961_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-rossi-1961-40">[40]</a></sup> The experiment employed eleven <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillation detectors</a> arranged within a circle 460 metres in diameter on the grounds of the Agassiz Station of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College_Observatory" title="Harvard College Observatory">Harvard College Observatory</a>. From that work, and from many other experiments carried out all over the world, the energy spectrum of the primary cosmic rays is now known to extend beyond 10<sup>20</sup> eV. A huge air shower experiment called the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Auger Project</a> is currently operated at a site on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pampa" title="Pampa">pampas</a> of Argentina by an international consortium of physicists, led by <a href="/wiki/James_Cronin" title="James Cronin">James Cronin</a>, winner of the 1980 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Andrew_Watson" title="Alan Andrew Watson">Alan Watson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>. Their aim is to explore the properties and arrival directions of the very highest-energy primary cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auger-website_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-auger-website-41">[41]</a></sup> The results are expected to have important implications for particle physics and cosmology, due to a theoretical <a href="/wiki/Greisen%E2%80%93Zatsepin%E2%80%93Kuzmin_limit" title="Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit">Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit</a> to the energies of cosmic rays from long distances (about 160 million light years) which occurs above 10<sup>20</sup> eV because of interactions with the remnant photons from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> origin of the universe.
</p><p>High-energy gamma rays (&gt;50<span class="nowrap"> </span>MeV photons) were finally discovered in the primary cosmic radiation by an MIT experiment carried on the OSO-3 satellite in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kraushaar-1972_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-kraushaar-1972-42">[42]</a></sup> Components of both galactic and extra-galactic origins were separately identified at intensities much less than 1% of the primary charged particles. Since then, numerous satellite gamma-ray observatories have mapped the gamma-ray sky. The most recent is the Fermi Observatory, which has produced a map showing a narrow band of gamma ray intensity produced in discrete and diffuse sources in our galaxy, and numerous point-like extra-galactic sources distributed over the celestial sphere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_cosmic_rays">Sources of cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sources of cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early speculation on the sources of cosmic rays included a 1934 proposal by Baade and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky" title="Fritz Zwicky">Zwicky</a> suggesting cosmic rays originated from supernovae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaadeZwicky1934_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaadeZwicky1934-43">[43]</a></sup> A 1948 proposal by <a href="/wiki/Horace_W._Babcock" title="Horace W. Babcock">Horace W. Babcock</a> suggested that magnetic variable stars could be a source of cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-babcock-1948_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-babcock-1948-44">[44]</a></sup> Subsequently, in 1951, Y. Sekido <i>et al.</i> identified the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a> as a source of cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sediko-1951_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-sediko-1951-45">[45]</a></sup> Since then, a wide variety of potential sources for cosmic rays began to surface, including <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei" title="Active galactic nuclei">active galactic nuclei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmicraysources_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-cosmicraysources-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="546" data-file-width="824" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/220px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/330px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/440px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sources of ionizing radiation in interplanetary space.</div></div></div>
<p>Later experiments have helped to identify the sources of cosmic rays with greater certainty. In 2009, a paper presented at the <a href="/wiki/International_Cosmic_Ray_Conference" title="International Cosmic Ray Conference">International Cosmic Ray Conference</a> (ICRC) by scientists at the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a> showed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-high_energy_cosmic_ray" title="Ultra-high energy cosmic ray">ultra-high energy cosmic rays</a> (UHECRs) originating from a location in the sky very close to the <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxy</a> <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a>, although the authors specifically stated that further investigation would be required to confirm Cen A as a source of cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auger-2009-1_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-auger-2009-1-47">[47]</a></sup> However, no correlation was found between the incidence of gamma-ray bursts and cosmic rays, causing the authors to set upper limits as low as 3.4 × 10<sup>−6</sup> <a href="/wiki/Erg" title="Erg">erg</a>·cm<sup>−2</sup> on the flux of <span class="nowrap">1 GeV – 1 TeV</span> cosmic rays from gamma-ray bursts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auger-2009-2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-auger-2009-2-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, supernovae were said to have been "pinned down" as a source of cosmic rays, a discovery made by a group using data from the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moskowitz-2009_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-moskowitz-2009-49">[49]</a></sup> This analysis, however, was disputed in 2011 with data from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_for_Antimatter_Matter_Exploration_and_Light-nuclei_Astrophysics" title="Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics">PAMELA</a>, which revealed that "spectral shapes of [hydrogen and helium nuclei] are different and cannot be described well by a single power law", suggesting a more complex process of cosmic ray formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piccoza-2011_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-piccoza-2011-50">[50]</a></sup> In February 2013, though, research analyzing data from <i><a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">Fermi</a></i> revealed through an observation of neutral pion decay that supernovae were indeed a source of cosmic rays, with each explosion producing roughly 3 × 10<sup>42</sup> – 3 × 10<sup>43</sup><span class="nowrap"> </span><a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a> of cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ackermann-2013_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ackermann-2013-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pinholster-2013_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pinholster-2013-4">[4]</a></sup> However, supernovae do not produce all cosmic rays, and the proportion of cosmic rays that they do produce is a question which cannot be answered without further study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jha-2013_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-jha-2013-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cosmic rays can be divided into two types, <b>galactic cosmic rays</b> (<b>GCR</b>), high-energy particles originating outside the solar system, and <b><a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">solar energetic particles</a></b>, high-energy particles (predominantly protons) emitted by the sun, primarily in <a href="/wiki/Solar_particle_event" title="Solar particle event">solar particle events</a>. However, the term "cosmic ray" is often used to refer to only the GCR flux. Despite the nomenclature <i>galactic</i>, GCRs may originate within or outside the galaxy (as discussed in the source section above).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Collision.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="850" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Atmospheric_Collision.svg/400px-Atmospheric_Collision.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Atmospheric_Collision.svg/600px-Atmospheric_Collision.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Atmospheric_Collision.svg/800px-Atmospheric_Collision.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Collision.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Primary cosmic particle collides with a molecule of atmosphere.</div></div></div>
<p>Cosmic rays originate as primary cosmic rays, which are those originally produced in various astrophysical processes. Primary cosmic rays are composed primarily of protons and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a> (99%), with a small amount of heavier nuclei (~1%) and an extremely minute proportion of <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiprotons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goddard-2012_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-goddard-2012-8">[8]</a></sup> Secondary cosmic rays, caused by a decay of primary cosmic rays as they impact an atmosphere, include <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a>. Of these four, the latter three were first detected in cosmic rays.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_cosmic_rays">Primary cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Primary cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Primary cosmic rays primarily originate from outside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">Solar system</a> and sometimes even the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. When they interact with Earth's atmosphere, they are converted to secondary particles. The mass ratio of helium to hydrogen nuclei, 28%, is similar to the primordial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elemental_abundance" title="Elemental abundance">elemental abundance</a> ratio of these elements, 24%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The remaining fraction is made up of the other heavier nuclei that are typical nucleosynthesis end products, primarily <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>. These nuclei appear in cosmic rays in much greater abundance (~1%) than in the solar atmosphere, where they are only about 10<sup>−11</sup> as abundant as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. Cosmic rays made up of charged nuclei heavier than helium are called <a href="/wiki/HZE_ions" title="HZE ions">HZE ions</a>. Due to the high charge and heavy nature of HZE ions, their contribution to an astronaut's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_dose" title="Radiation dose">radiation dose</a> in space is significant even though they are relatively scarce.
</p><p>This abundance difference is a result of the way secondary cosmic rays are formed. Carbon and oxygen nuclei collide with interstellar matter to form <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> in a process termed <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation" title="Cosmic ray spallation">cosmic ray spallation</a>. Spallation is also responsible for the abundances of <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> in cosmic rays produced by collisions of iron and nickel nuclei with <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koch-2012_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-koch-2012-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_cosmic_ray_antimatter">Primary cosmic ray antimatter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Primary cosmic ray antimatter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Magnetic_Spectrometer" title="Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer">Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer</a></div>
<p>Satellite experiments have found evidence of <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a> and a few antiprotons in primary cosmic rays, amounting to less than 1% of the particles in primary cosmic rays. These do not appear to be the products of large amounts of antimatter from the Big Bang, or indeed complex antimatter in the universe. Rather, they appear to consist of only these two elementary particles, newly made in energetic processes.
</p><p>Preliminary results from the presently operating <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Magnetic_Spectrometer" title="Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer">Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer</a> (<i>AMS-02</i>) on board the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> show that positrons in the cosmic rays arrive with no directionality. In September, 2014, new results with almost twice as much data were presented in a talk at CERN and published in Physical Review Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> A new measurement of positron fraction up to 500 GeV was reported, showing that positron fraction peaks at a maximum of about 16% of total electron+positron events, around an energy of <span class="nowrap">275±32 GeV</span>. At higher energies, up to 500<span class="nowrap"> </span>GeV, the ratio of positrons to electrons begins to fall again. The absolute flux of positrons also begins to fall before 500<span class="nowrap"> </span>GeV, but peaks at energies far higher than electron energies, which peak about 10<span class="nowrap"> </span>GeV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> These results on interpretation have been suggested to be due to positron production in annihilation events of massive <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrevltrs413_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-physrevltrs413-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cosmic ray antiprotons also have a much higher average energy than their normal-matter counterparts (protons). They arrive at Earth with a characteristic energy maximum of 2 GeV, indicating their production in a fundamentally different process from cosmic ray protons, which on average have only one-sixth of the energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is no evidence of complex antimatter atomic nuclei, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihelium" title="Antihelium">antihelium</a> nuclei (i.e., anti-alpha particles), in cosmic rays. These are actively being searched for. A prototype of the <i>AMS-02</i> designated <i>AMS-01</i>, was flown into space aboard the <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a> on <a href="/wiki/STS-91" title="STS-91">STS-91</a> in June 1998. By not detecting any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihelium#Antihelium" title="Antihelium">antihelium</a> at all, the <i>AMS-01</i> established an upper limit of <span class="nowrap">1.1 × 10<sup>−6</sup></span> for the antihelium to helium <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_cosmic_rays">Secondary cosmic rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Secondary cosmic rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When cosmic rays enter the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> they collide with <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, mainly oxygen and nitrogen. The interaction produces a cascade of lighter particles, a so-called <a href="/wiki/Air_shower_(physics)" title="Air shower (physics)">air shower</a> secondary radiation that rains down, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">x-rays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a>, protons, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> All of the produced particles stay within about one degree of the primary particle's path.
</p><p>Typical particles produced in such collisions are <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> and charged <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a> such as positive or negative <a href="/wiki/Pion" title="Pion">pions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaon" title="Kaon">kaons</a>. Some of these subsequently decay into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muons" title="Muons">muons</a>, which are able to reach the surface of the Earth, and even penetrate for some distance into shallow mines. The muons can be easily detected by many types of particle detectors, such as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_chamber" title="Cloud chamber">cloud chambers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bubble_chamber" title="Bubble chamber">bubble chambers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scintillation_(physics)" title="Scintillation (physics)">scintillation</a> detectors. The observation of a secondary shower of particles in multiple detectors at the same time is an indication that all of the particles came from that event.
</p><p>Cosmic rays impacting other planetary bodies in the Solar System are detected indirectly by observing high-energy <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> emissions by gamma-ray telescope. These are distinguished from radioactive decay processes by their higher energies above about 10 MeV.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic-ray_flux">Cosmic-ray flux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cosmic-ray flux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1453" data-file-width="3175" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/400px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/600px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/800px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An overview of the space environment shows the relationship between the solar activity and galactic cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> of incoming cosmic rays at the upper atmosphere is dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>, and the energy of the cosmic rays. At distances of ~94 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> from the Sun, the solar wind undergoes a transition, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Termination_shock" title="Termination shock">termination shock</a>, from supersonic to subsonic speeds. The region between the termination shock and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a> acts as a barrier to cosmic rays, decreasing the flux at lower energies (≤<span class="nowrap"> </span>1 GeV) by about 90%. However, the strength of the solar wind is not constant, and hence it has been observed that cosmic ray flux is correlated with solar activity.
</p><p>In addition, the Earth's magnetic field acts to deflect cosmic rays from its surface, giving rise to the observation that the flux is apparently dependent on <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth angle</a>.
</p><p>The combined effects of all of the factors mentioned contribute to the flux of cosmic rays at Earth's surface. </p>

<p>In the past, it was believed that the cosmic ray flux remained fairly constant over time. However, recent research suggests one-and-a-half- to two-fold millennium-timescale changes in the cosmic ray flux in the past forty thousand years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>The magnitude of the energy of cosmic ray flux in interstellar space is very comparable to that of other deep space energies: cosmic ray energy density averages about one electron-volt per cubic centimetre of interstellar space, or ~1<span class="nowrap"> </span>eV/cm<sup>3</sup>, which is comparable to the energy density of visible starlight at 0.3<span class="nowrap"> </span>eV/cm<sup>3</sup>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_magnetic_fields" title="Galactic magnetic fields">galactic magnetic field</a> energy density (assumed 3 microgauss) which is ~0.25<span class="nowrap"> </span>eV/cm<sup>3</sup>, or the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> (CMB) radiation energy density at ~ 0.25<span class="nowrap"> </span>eV/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_methods">Detection methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Detection methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VERITAS_array.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="955" data-file-width="2678" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/VERITAS_array.jpg/600px-VERITAS_array.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/VERITAS_array.jpg/900px-VERITAS_array.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/VERITAS_array.jpg/1200px-VERITAS_array.jpg 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VERITAS_array.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/VERITAS" title="VERITAS">VERITAS</a> array of air Cherenkov telescopes.</div></div></div>
<p>There are several ground-based methods of detecting cosmic rays currently in use. The first detection method is called the air Cherenkov telescope, designed to detect low-energy (&lt;200 GeV) cosmic rays by means of analyzing their <a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation" title="Cherenkov radiation">Cherenkov radiation</a>, which for cosmic rays are gamma rays emitted as they travel faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in their medium, the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-losalamos-2002_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-losalamos-2002-67">[67]</a></sup> While these telescopes are extremely good at distinguishing between background radiation and that of cosmic-ray origin, they can only function well on clear nights without the Moon shining, and have very small fields of view and are only active for a few percent of the time. Another Cherenkov telescope uses water as a medium through which particles pass and produce Cherenkov radiation to make them detectable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michiganstate_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-michiganstate-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1017" data-file-width="900" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png/250px-PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png/375px-PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png/500px-PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA17601-Comparisons-RadiationExposure-MarsTrip-20131209.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of radiation doses, including the amount detected on the trip from Earth to Mars by the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">RAD</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">MSL</a> (2011 – 2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531a_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531b_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531b-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20130530_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20130530-23">[23]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Extensive air shower (EAS) arrays, a second detection method, measure the charged particles which pass through them. EAS arrays measure much higher-energy cosmic rays than air Cherenkov telescopes, and can observe a broad area of the sky and can be active about 90% of the time. However, they are less able to segregate background effects from cosmic rays than can air Cherenkov telescopes. EAS arrays employ plastic <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillators</a> in order to detect particles.
</p><p>Another method was developed by Robert Fleischer, <a href="/wiki/P._Buford_Price" title="P. Buford Price">P. Buford Price</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_M._Walker_(physicist)" title="Robert M. Walker (physicist)">Robert M. Walker</a> for use in high-altitude balloons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FPW_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FPW-69">[69]</a></sup> In this method, sheets of clear plastic, like 0.25 <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexan" title="Lexan">Lexan</a> polycarbonate, are stacked together and exposed directly to cosmic rays in space or high altitude. The nuclear charge causes chemical bond breaking or <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> in the plastic. At the top of the plastic stack the ionization is less, due to the high cosmic ray speed. As the cosmic ray speed decreases due to deceleration in the stack, the ionization increases along the path. The resulting plastic sheets are "etched" or slowly dissolved in warm caustic <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> solution, that removes the surface material at a slow, known rate. The caustic sodium hydroxide dissolves the plastic at a faster rate along the path of the ionized plastic. The net result is a conical etch pit in the plastic. The etch pits are measured under a high-power microscope (typically 1600× oil-immersion), and the etch rate is plotted as a function of the depth in the stacked plastic.
</p><p>This technique yields a unique curve for each atomic nucleus from 1 to 92, allowing identification of both the charge and energy of the cosmic ray that traverses the plastic stack. The more extensive the ionization along the path, the higher the charge. In addition to its uses for cosmic-ray detection, the technique is also used to detect nuclei created as products of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>.
</p><p>A fourth method involves the use of <a href="/wiki/Cloud_chamber" title="Cloud chamber">cloud chambers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chess-2006_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-chess-2006-70">[70]</a></sup> to detect the secondary muons created when a pion decays. Cloud chambers in particular can be built from widely available materials and can be constructed even in a high-school laboratory. A fifth method, involving <a href="/wiki/Bubble_chamber" title="Bubble chamber">bubble chambers</a>, can be used to detect cosmic ray particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chu-1970_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-chu-1970-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another method detects the light from nitrogen fluorescence caused by the excitation of nitrogen in the atmosphere by the shower of particles moving through the atmosphere. This method allows for accurate detection of the direction from which the cosmic ray came.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, the <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> devices in pervasive <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> cameras have been proposed as a practical distributed network to detect air showers from ultra-high-energy cosmic rays (UHECRs) which is at least comparable with that of conventional cosmic ray detectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">app</a>, which is currently in beta and accepting applications, is CRAYFIS (Cosmic RAYs Found In Smartphones).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_atmospheric_chemistry">Changes in atmospheric chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Changes in atmospheric chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cosmic rays ionize the nitrogen and oxygen molecules in the atmosphere, which leads to a number of chemical reactions. Cosmic rays are also responsible for the continuous production of a number of <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">unstable isotopes</a> in the Earth's atmosphere, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a>, via the reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>n + <sup>14</sup>N → p + <sup>14</sup>C</dd></dl>
<p>Cosmic rays kept the level of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trumbore_2000_41–59_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trumbore_2000_41–59-76">[76]</a></sup> in the atmosphere roughly constant (70 tons) for at least the past 100,000 years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> until the beginning of above-ground nuclear weapons testing in the early 1950s. This is an important fact used in <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_ambient_radiation">Role in ambient radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Role in ambient radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cosmic rays constitute a fraction of the annual radiation exposure of human beings on the Earth, averaging 0.39<span class="nowrap"> </span>mSv out of a total of 3<span class="nowrap"> </span>mSv per year (13% of total background) for the Earth's population. However, the background radiation from cosmic rays increases with altitude, from 0.3<span class="nowrap"> </span>mSv per year for sea-level areas to 1.0<span class="nowrap"> </span>mSv per year for higher-altitude cities, raising cosmic radiation exposure to a quarter of total background radiation exposure for populations of said cities. Airline crews flying long distance high-altitude routes can be exposed to 2.2<span class="nowrap"> </span>mSv of extra radiation each year due to cosmic rays, nearly doubling their total exposure to ionizing radiation.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_electronics">Effect on electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Effect on electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cosmic rays have sufficient energy to alter the states of circuit components in <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, causing transient errors to occur (such as corrupted data in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" title="Random access memory">electronic memory devices</a> or incorrect performance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPU" title="CPU">CPUs</a>) often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Soft_error" title="Soft error">soft errors</a>." This has been a problem in <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> at extremely high-altitude, such as in <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, but with <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> becoming smaller and smaller, this is becoming an increasing concern in ground-level electronics as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Studies by <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> in the 1990s suggest that computers typically experience about one cosmic-ray-induced error per 256 megabytes of <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">RAM</a> per month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> To alleviate this problem, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_Corporation" title="Intel Corporation">Intel Corporation</a> has proposed a cosmic ray detector that could be integrated into future high-density <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessors</a>, allowing the processor to repeat the last command following a cosmic-ray event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, data corruption in a flight control system caused an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330</a> airliner to twice <a href="/wiki/Qantas_Flight_72" title="Qantas Flight 72">plunge hundreds of feet</a>, resulting in injuries to multiple passengers and crew members. Cosmic rays were investigated among other possible causes of the data corruption, but were ultimately ruled out as being very unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">Radiation hardening</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_to_aerospace_travel">Significance to aerospace travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Significance to aerospace travel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays" title="Health threat from cosmic rays">Health threat from cosmic rays</a></div>
<p>Galactic cosmic rays are one of the most important barriers standing in the way of plans for interplanetary travel by crewed spacecraft. Cosmic rays also pose a threat to electronics placed aboard outgoing probes. In 2010, a malfunction aboard the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> space probe was credited to a single flipped bit, probably caused by a cosmic ray. Strategies such as physical or magnetic shielding for spacecraft have been considered in order to minimize the damage to electronics and human beings caused by cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globus-2002_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-globus-2002-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atkinson-2005_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-atkinson-2005-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>Flying 12 kilometres (39,000 ft) high, passengers and crews of <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">jet airliners</a> are exposed to at least 10 times the cosmic ray dose that people at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> receive. Aircraft flying <a href="/wiki/Polar_route" title="Polar route">polar routes</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_pole" title="Geomagnetic pole">geomagnetic poles</a> are at particular risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_lightning">Role in lightning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Role in lightning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cosmic rays have been implicated in the triggering of electrical breakdown in <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>. It has been proposed that essentially all lightning is triggered through a relativistic process, "<a href="/wiki/Runaway_breakdown" title="Runaway breakdown">runaway breakdown</a>", seeded by cosmic ray secondaries. Subsequent development of the lightning discharge then occurs through "conventional breakdown" mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postulated_role_in_climate_change">Postulated role in climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Postulated role in climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A role for cosmic rays in climate was suggested by <a href="/wiki/Edward_P._Ney" title="Edward P. Ney">Edward P. Ney</a> in 1959<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NATURE_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-NATURE-93">[93]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Dickinson" title="Robert E. Dickinson">Robert E. Dickinson</a> in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dickinson-1975_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-dickinson-1975-94">[94]</a></sup> It has been postulated that cosmic rays may have been responsible for major climatic change and mass-extinction in the past. According to Adrian Mellott and Mikhail Medvedev, 62-million-year cycles in biological marine populations correlate with the motion of the Earth relative to the galactic plane and increases in exposure to cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The researchers suggest that this and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> bombardments deriving from local <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> could have affected <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mutation_rate" title="Mutation rate">mutation rates</a>, and might be linked to decisive alterations in the Earth's climate, and to the <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass-extinctions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>Danish physicist <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Svensmark" title="Henrik Svensmark">Henrik Svensmark</a> has controversially argued that because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a> modulates the cosmic ray flux on Earth, they would consequently affect the rate of cloud formation and hence be an indirect cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Svensmark is one of several scientists <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_scientists_opposing_the_mainstream_scientific_assessment_of_global_warming" title="List of scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming">outspokenly opposed to the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming</a>, leading to concerns that the proposition that cosmic rays are connected to global warming could be ideologically biased rather than scientifically based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>  Other scientists have vigorously criticized Svensmark for sloppy and inconsistent work: one example is adjustment of cloud data that understates error in lower cloud data, but not in high cloud data;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> another example is "incorrect handling of the physical data" resulting in graphs that do not show the correlations they claim to show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Despite Svensmark's assertions, galactic cosmic rays have shown no statistically significant influence on changes in cloud cover,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> and demonstrated to have no causal relationship to changes in global temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
