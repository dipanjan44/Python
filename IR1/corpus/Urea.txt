<p><b>Urea</b>, also known as <b>carbamide</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> CO(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>. This <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a> has two <a href="/wiki/Amidogen" title="Amidogen">–NH<sub>2</sub></a> groups joined by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> (C=O) <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>.
</p><p>Urea serves an important role in the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>-containing compounds by animals and is the main nitrogen-containing substance in the <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>. It is a colorless, odorless solid, highly soluble in water, and practically non-toxic (<a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub style="font-size:-1">50</sub></a> is 15 g/kg for rats)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  Dissolved in water, it is neither <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">alkaline</a>.  The body uses it in many processes, most notably <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_waste#Nitrogen_wastes" title="Metabolic waste">nitrogen excretion</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> forms it by combining two <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> molecules (NH<sub>3</sub>) with a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) molecule in the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a>. Urea is widely used in <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> as a source of nitrogen and is an important <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemical industry</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6hler_synthesis" title="Wöhler synthesis">discovery</a> in 1828 that urea can be produced from inorganic starting materials was an important conceptual milestone in chemistry. It showed for the first time that a substance previously known only as a byproduct of life could be synthesized in the laboratory without biological starting materials thereby contradicting the widely held doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg/220px-Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg/330px-Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg/440px-Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Urea_process_plant_UFFL_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A process plant in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> that commercially produces urea as fertilizer by using <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> as the main raw material.</div></div></div>
<p>More than 90% of world industrial production of urea is destined for use as a nitrogen-release fertilizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Urea has the highest nitrogen content of all solid nitrogenous fertilizers in common use. Therefore, it has the lowest transportation costs per unit of nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a>.
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> bacteria possess the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Urease" title="Urease">urease</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzes</a> conversion of urea to <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (NH<sub>3</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ion (NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>) and bicarbonate ion (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>). Thus urea fertilizers rapidly transform to the ammonium form in soils. Among the soil bacteria known to carry urease, some ammonia-oxidizing bacteria (AOB), such as species of <i><a href="/wiki/Nitrosomonas" title="Nitrosomonas">Nitrosomonas</a></i>, can also assimilate the carbon dioxide the reaction releases to make biomass via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a>, and harvest energy by oxidizing ammonia (the other product of urease) to <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a>, a process termed  nitrification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Nitrite-oxidizing bacteria, especially <i><a href="/wiki/Nitrobacter" title="Nitrobacter">Nitrobacter</a></i>, oxidize nitrite to nitrate, which is extremely mobile in soils because of its negative charge and is a major cause of water pollution from agriculture.  Ammonium and nitrate are readily absorbed by plants, and are the dominant sources of nitrogen for plant growth.  Urea is also used in many multi-component solid fertilizer formulations.  Urea is highly soluble in water and is therefore also very suitable for use in fertilizer solutions (in combination with ammonium nitrate: UAN), e.g., in 'foliar feed' fertilizers. For fertilizer use, granules are preferred over prills because of their narrower particle size distribution, which is an advantage for mechanical application.
</p><p>The most common impurity of synthetic urea is <a href="/wiki/Biuret" title="Biuret">biuret</a>, which impairs plant growth.
</p><p>Urea is usually spread at rates of between 40 and 300 kg/ha but rates vary. Smaller applications incur lower losses due to leaching. During summer, urea is often spread just before or during rain to minimize losses from <a href="/wiki/Ammonia_volatilization_from_urea" title="Ammonia volatilization from urea">volatilization</a> (a process wherein nitrogen is lost to the atmosphere as ammonia gas).
</p><p>Because of the high nitrogen concentration in urea, it is very important to achieve an even spread. The application equipment must be correctly calibrated and properly used. Drilling must not occur on contact with or close to seed, due to the risk of germination damage. Urea dissolves in water for application as a spray or through irrigation systems.
</p><p>In grain and cotton crops, urea is often applied at the time of the last cultivation before planting. In high rainfall areas and on sandy soils (where nitrogen can be lost through leaching) and where good in-season rainfall is expected, urea can be side- or top-dressed during the growing season. Top-dressing is also popular on pasture and forage crops. In cultivating sugarcane, urea is side-dressed after planting, and applied to each <a href="/wiki/Ratooning" title="Ratooning">ratoon</a> crop.
</p><p>In irrigated crops, urea can be applied dry to the soil, or dissolved and applied through the irrigation water. Urea dissolves in its own weight in water, but becomes increasingly difficult to dissolve as the concentration increases. Dissolving urea in water is endothermic—the solution temperature falls when urea dissolves.
</p><p>As a practical guide, when preparing urea solutions for <a href="/wiki/Fertigation" title="Fertigation">fertigation</a> (injection into irrigation lines), dissolve no more than 3 g urea per 1 L water.
</p><p>In foliar sprays, urea concentrations of between 0.5% and 2.0% are often used in horticultural crops. Low-<a href="/wiki/Biuret" title="Biuret">biuret</a> grades of urea are often indicated.
</p><p>Urea absorbs moisture from the atmosphere and therefore is typically stored either in closed or sealed bags on pallets or, if stored in bulk, under cover with a tarpaulin. As with most solid fertilizers, storage in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area is recommended.
</p><p>Overdose or placing urea near seed is harmful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_industry">Chemical industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea is a raw material for the manufacture of two main classes of materials: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urea-formaldehyde_resin" title="Urea-formaldehyde resin">urea-formaldehyde resins</a> and urea-melamine-formaldehyde used in marine <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosives">Explosives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Explosives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea can be used to make <a href="/wiki/Urea_nitrate" title="Urea nitrate">urea nitrate</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_material#High_explosives" title="Explosive material">high explosive</a> that is used industrially and as part of some <a href="/wiki/Improvised_explosive_device" title="Improvised explosive device">improvised explosive devices</a>.  It is a stabilizer in <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_material" title="Explosive material">explosives</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automobile_systems">Automobile systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Automobile systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea is used in <a href="/wiki/Selective_non-catalytic_reduction" title="Selective non-catalytic reduction">SNCR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">SCR</a> reactions to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">NO<sub>x</sub></a> <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> in <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gases</a> from <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> from <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">Diesel</a>, dual fuel, and lean-burn <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> engines. The <a href="/wiki/BlueTec" title="BlueTec">BlueTec</a> system, for example, injects a water-based urea solution into the exhaust system. The ammonia produced by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of the urea reacts with the nitrogen oxide emissions and is converted into nitrogen and water within the catalytic converter. Trucks and cars using these catalytic converters need to carry a supply of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust_fluid" title="Diesel exhaust fluid">diesel exhaust fluid</a>, a solution of urea in water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_uses">Laboratory uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Laboratory uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea in concentrations up to 10 <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration#Units" title="Molar concentration">M</a> is a powerful <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denaturant</a> as it disrupts the noncovalent bonds in the proteins. This property can be exploited to increase the solubility of some proteins.
A mixture of urea and <a href="/wiki/Choline_chloride" title="Choline chloride">choline chloride</a> is used as a <a href="/wiki/Deep_eutectic_solvent" title="Deep eutectic solvent">deep eutectic solvent</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Ionic_liquid" title="Ionic liquid">ionic liquid</a>.
</p><p>Urea can in principle serve as a hydrogen source for subsequent power generation in <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>.  Urea present in urine/wastewater can be used directly (though bacteria normally quickly degrade urea.)  Producing hydrogen by electrolysis of urea solution occurs at a lower voltage (0.37 V) and thus consumes less energy than the electrolysis of water (1.2 V).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urea in concentrations up to 8 M can be used to make fixed brain tissue transparent to visible light while still preserving fluorescent signals from labeled cells. This allows for much deeper imaging of neuronal processes than previously obtainable using conventional one photon or two photon confocal microscopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_use">Medical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Urea-containing_cream" title="Urea-containing cream">Urea-containing creams</a> are used as topical <a href="/wiki/Dermatology" title="Dermatology">dermatological</a> products to promote <a href="/wiki/Fluid_replacement" title="Fluid replacement">rehydration</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>. Urea 40% is indicated for <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xerosis" title="Xerosis">xerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onychomycosis" title="Onychomycosis">onychomycosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosis" title="Ichthyosis">ichthyosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eczema" title="Eczema">eczema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keratosis" title="Keratosis">keratosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keratoderma" title="Keratoderma">keratoderma</a>, corns, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calluses" title="Calluses">calluses</a>. If covered by an <a href="/wiki/Occlusive_dressing" title="Occlusive dressing">occlusive dressing</a>, 40% urea preparations may also be used for nonsurgical <a href="/wiki/Debridement" title="Debridement">debridement</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nail_(anatomy)" title="Nail (anatomy)">nails</a>.  Urea 40% "dissolves the intercellular matrix"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> of the nail plate. Only diseased or dystrophic nails are removed, as there is no effect on healthy portions of the nail.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This drug is also used as an earwax removal aid.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Urea has also been studied as a <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretic</a>. It was first used Dr. W. Friedrich in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In a 2010 study of ICU patients, urea was used to treat euvolemic <a href="/wiki/Hyponatremia" title="Hyponatremia">hyponatremia</a> and was found a safe, inexpensive, and simple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Saline_(medicine)" title="Saline (medicine)">saline</a>, urea injection has previously been used to perform <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Blood_urea_nitrogen" title="Blood urea nitrogen">blood urea nitrogen</a> (BUN) test is a measure of the amount of nitrogen in the blood that comes from urea.  It is used as a marker of <a href="/wiki/Renal_function" title="Renal function">renal function</a>, though it is inferior to other markers such as <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a> because blood urea levels are influenced by other factors such as diet and dehydration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urea labeled with <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a> is used in the <a href="/wiki/Urea_breath_test" title="Urea breath test">urea breath test</a>, which is used to detect the presence of the bacteria <i><a href="/wiki/Helicobacter_pylori" title="Helicobacter pylori">Helicobacter pylori</a></i> (<i>H. pylori</i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> of humans, associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcers</a>.  The test detects the characteristic enzyme <a href="/wiki/Urease" title="Urease">urease</a>, produced by <i>H. pylori</i>, by a reaction that produces ammonia from urea.  This increases the pH (reduces acidity) of the stomach environment around the bacteria.  Similar bacteria species to <i>H. pylori</i> can be identified by the same test in animals such as <a href="/wiki/Ape" title="Ape">apes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Big_cat" title="Big cat">big cats</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverse_effects">Adverse effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adverse effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Urea can be irritating to skin, eyes, and the respiratory tract. Repeated or prolonged contact with urea in fertilizer form on the skin may cause <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>High concentrations in the blood can be damaging.  Ingestion of low concentrations of urea, such as are found in typical human <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>, are not dangerous with additional water ingestion within a reasonable time-frame. Many animals (e.g., dogs) have a much more concentrated urine and it contains a higher urea amount than normal human urine; this can prove dangerous as a source of liquids for consumption in a life-threatening situation (such as in a desert).
</p><p>Urea can cause <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal blooms</a> to produce toxins, and its presence in the runoff from fertilized land may play a role in the increase of toxic blooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The substance decomposes on heating above melting point, producing toxic gases, and reacts violently with strong oxidants, nitrites, inorganic chlorides, chlorites and perchlorates, causing fire and explosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiology">Physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amino acids from ingested food that are not used for the synthesis of proteins and other biological substances — or produced from catabolism of muscle protein — are oxidized by the body as an alternative source of energy, yielding urea and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The oxidation pathway starts with the removal of the amino group by a <a href="/wiki/Transaminase" title="Transaminase">transaminase</a>; the amino group is then fed into the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a>. The first step in the conversion of amino acids from protein into <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_waste#Nitrogen_wastes" title="Metabolic waste">metabolic waste</a> in the liver is removal of the alpha-amino nitrogen, which results in <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. Because ammonia is toxic, it is excreted immediately by fish, converted into <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a> by birds, and converted into urea by mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ammonia (NH<sub>3</sub>) is a common byproduct of the metabolism of nitrogenous compounds. Ammonia is smaller, more volatile and more mobile than urea.  If allowed to accumulate, ammonia would raise the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> in cells to toxic levels. Therefore, many organisms convert ammonia to urea, even though this synthesis has a net energy cost.  Being practically neutral and highly soluble in water, urea is a safe vehicle for the body to transport and excrete excess nitrogen.
</p><p>Urea is synthesized in the body of many organisms as part of the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a>, either from the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> or from <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>.  In this cycle, <a href="/wiki/Amino" title="Amino">amino</a> groups donated by ammonia and L-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate" title="Aspartate">aspartate</a> are converted to urea, while L-<a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">ornithine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citrulline" title="Citrulline">citrulline</a>, L-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argininosuccinate" title="Argininosuccinate">argininosuccinate</a>, and L-<a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> act as intermediates.  Urea production occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and is regulated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-acetylglutamate" title="N-acetylglutamate">N-acetylglutamate</a>.  Urea is then dissolved into the blood (in the <a href="/wiki/Reference_ranges_for_blood_tests" title="Reference ranges for blood tests">reference range</a> of 2.5 to 6.7 mmol/liter) and further transported and excreted by the kidney as a component of <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>. In addition, a small amount of urea is excreted (along with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> and water) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweat" title="Sweat">sweat</a>.
</p><p>In water, the amine groups undergo slow displacement by water molecules, producing ammonia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_ion" title="Ammonium ion">ammonium ion</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicarbonate_ion" title="Bicarbonate ion">bicarbonate ion</a>.  For this reason, old, stale urine has a stronger odor than fresh urine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">Humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Renal_urea_handling" title="Renal urea handling">cycling of and excretion of urea by the kidneys</a> is a vital part of mammalian metabolism. Besides its role as carrier of waste nitrogen, urea also plays a role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange_system" title="Countercurrent exchange system">countercurrent exchange system</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephrons" title="Nephrons">nephrons</a>, that allows for re-absorption of water and critical ions from the excreted <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>. Urea is reabsorbed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_medullary_collecting_duct" title="Inner medullary collecting duct">inner medullary collecting ducts</a> of the nephrons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boron837_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-boron837-24">[24]</a></sup> thus raising the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmolarity" title="Osmolarity">osmolarity</a> in the medullary <a href="/wiki/Interstitium" title="Interstitium">interstitium</a> surrounding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_descending_limb_of_the_loop_of_Henle" title="Thin descending limb of the loop of Henle">thin descending limb of the loop of Henle</a>, which makes the water reabsorb.
</p><p>By action of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urea_transporter_2" title="Urea transporter 2">urea transporter 2</a>, some of this reabsorbed urea eventually flows back into the thin descending limb of the tubule,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> through the collecting ducts, and into the excreted urine.  The body uses this mechanism, which is controlled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antidiuretic_hormone" title="Antidiuretic hormone">antidiuretic hormone</a>, to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperosmotic" title="Hyperosmotic">hyperosmotic</a> urine—i.e., urine with a higher concentration of dissolved substances than the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. This mechanism  is important to prevent the loss of water, maintain <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a>, and maintain a suitable concentration of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> ions in the blood plasma.
</p><p>The equivalent nitrogen content (in <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a>) of urea (in <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mmol</a>) can be estimated by the conversion factor 0.028 g/mmol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Furthermore, 1 gram of nitrogen is roughly equivalent to 6.25 grams of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, and 1 gram of protein is roughly equivalent to 5 grams of <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> tissue. In situations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle_wasting" title="Muscle wasting">muscle wasting</a>, 1 mmol of excessive urea in the urine (as measured by urine volume in litres multiplied by urea concentration in mmol/l) roughly corresponds to a muscle loss of 0.67 gram.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_species">Other species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">aquatic</a> organisms the most common form of nitrogen waste is ammonia, whereas land-dwelling organisms convert the toxic ammonia to either urea or <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>.  Urea is found in the urine of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, as well as some fish. Birds and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saurian" title="Saurian">saurian</a> reptiles have a different form of nitrogen metabolism that requires less water, and leads to nitrogen excretion in the form of uric acid.  It is noteworthy that <a href="/wiki/Tadpole" title="Tadpole">tadpoles</a> excrete ammonia but shift to urea production during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphosis_(biology)" title="Metamorphosis (biology)">metamorphosis</a>.  Despite the generalization above, the urea pathway has been documented not only in mammals and amphibians but in many other organisms as well, including birds, <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>, insects, plants, <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Urea is readily quantified by a number of different methods, such as the diacetyl monoxime colorimetric method, and the Berthelot reaction (after initial conversion of urea to ammonia via urease).  These methods are amenable to high throughput instrumentation, such as automated flow injection analyzers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and 96-well micro-plate spectrophotometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_compounds">Related compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Related compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ureas" title="Ureas">ureas</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ureas" title="Ureas">Ureas</a> describes a <i>class</i> of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> that share the same functional group, a carbonyl group attached to two organic amine residues: RR'N—CO—NRR'. Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbamide_peroxide" title="Carbamide peroxide">carbamide peroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allantoin" title="Allantoin">allantoin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydantoin" title="Hydantoin">hydantoin</a>. Ureas are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Biuret" title="Biuret">biurets</a> and related in structure to <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbamate" title="Carbamate">carbamates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbodiimide" title="Carbodiimide">carbodiimides</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thiocarbamide" title="Thiocarbamide">thiocarbamides</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Urea was first discovered in urine in 1727 by the Dutch scientist <a href="/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave" title="Herman Boerhaave">Herman Boerhaave</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> although this discovery is often attributed to the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Rouelle" title="Hilaire Rouelle">Hilaire Rouelle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Boerhaave used the following steps to isolate urea:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Boiled off water, resulting in a substance similar to fresh cream</li>
<li>Used filter paper to squeeze out remaining liquid</li>
<li>Waited a year for solid to form under an oily liquid</li>
<li>Removed the oily liquid</li>
<li>Dissolved the solid in water</li>
<li>Used <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry)" title="Recrystallization (chemistry)">recrystallization</a> to tease out the urea</li></ol>
<p>In 1828, the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> obtained urea artificially by treating <a href="/wiki/Silver_cyanate" title="Silver cyanate">silver cyanate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>AgNCO + NH<sub>4</sub>Cl → (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO + AgCl</dd></dl>
<p>This was the first time an organic compound was artificially synthesized from inorganic starting materials, without the involvement of living organisms.  The results of this experiment implicitly discredited <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a> — the theory that the chemicals of living organisms are fundamentally different from those of inanimate matter.  This insight was important for the development of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>. His discovery prompted Wöhler to write triumphantly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Berzelius</a>: "I must tell you that I can make urea without the use of kidneys, either man or dog. <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_cyanate" title="Ammonium cyanate">Ammonium cyanate</a> is urea." In fact, this was incorrect. These are two different chemicals, which are in chemical equilibrium heavily favoring urea under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_conditions" title="Standard conditions">standard conditions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Regardless, with his discovery, Wöhler secured a place among the pioneers of organic chemistry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Urea is produced on an industrial scale: In 2012, worldwide production capacity was approximately 184 million tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_methods">Industrial methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Industrial methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For use in industry, urea is produced from synthetic <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.  As large quantities of carbon dioxide are produced during the ammonia manufacturing process as a byproduct from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> (predominantly natural gas, less often petroleum derivatives), or occasionally from coal, urea production plants are almost always located adjacent to the site where the ammonia is manufactured. Although natural gas is both the most economical and the most widely available ammonia plant feedstock, plants using it do not produce quite as much carbon dioxide from the process as is needed to convert their entire ammonia output into urea. In recent years new technologies such as the KM-CDR process<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> have been developed to recover supplementary carbon dioxide from the combustion exhaust gases produced in the fired reforming furnace of the ammonia synthesis gas plant, allowing operators of stand-alone nitrogen fertilizer complexes to avoid the need to handle and market ammonia as a separate product and also to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions to the atmosphere.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1217" data-file-width="1513" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif/lossy-page1-220px-THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif/lossy-page1-330px-THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif/lossy-page1-440px-THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.071_Urea_Plant.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Urea plant using ammonium carbamate briquettes, Fixed Nitrogen Research Laboratory, ca. 1930</div></div></div>
<p>The basic process, developed in 1922, is also called the Bosch–Meiser urea process after its discoverers. Various commercial urea processes are characterized by the conditions under which urea forms and the way that unconverted reactants are further processed.  The process consists of two main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_reaction" title="Equilibrium reaction">equilibrium reactions</a>, with incomplete conversion of the reactants.  The first is <b>carbamate formation</b>: the fast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> reaction of liquid ammonia with gaseous carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) at high temperature and pressure to form <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_carbamate" title="Ammonium carbamate">ammonium carbamate</a> (H<sub>2</sub>N-COONH<sub>4</sub>):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NH<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> ⇌ H<sub>2</sub>N-COONH<sub>4</sub>         (ΔH= -117kJ/mol at 110 atm and 160°C) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-41">[41]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The second is <b>urea conversion</b>: the slower <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> decomposition of ammonium carbamate into urea and water:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>N-COONH<sub>4</sub> ⇌ (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O         (ΔH= +15.5 kJ/mol at 160-180°C) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-41">[41]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The overall conversion of NH<sub>3</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub> to urea is exothermic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> the reaction heat from the first reaction driving the second. Like all chemical equilibria, these reactions behave according to <a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier's principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>, and the conditions that most favour carbamate formation have an unfavourable effect on the urea conversion equilibrium. The process conditions are, therefore, a compromise: the ill-effect on the first reaction of the high temperature (around 190 °C) needed for the second is compensated for by conducting the process under high pressure (140–175 bar), which favours the first reaction. Although it is necessary to compress gaseous carbon dioxide to this pressure, the ammonia is available from the ammonia plant in liquid form, which can be pumped into the system much more economically. To allow the slow urea formation reaction time to reach equilibrium a large reaction space is needed, so the synthesis reactor in a large urea plant tends to be a massive pressure vessel.
</p><p>Because the urea conversion is incomplete, the product must be separated from unchanged ammonium carbamate. In early "straight-through" urea plants this was done by letting down the system pressure to atmospheric to let the carbamate decompose back to ammonia and carbon dioxide. Originally, because it was not economic to recompress the ammonia and carbon dioxide for recycle, the ammonia at least would be used for the manufacture of other products, for example <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>. (The carbon dioxide was usually wasted.) Later process schemes made recycling unused ammonia and carbon dioxide practical. This was accomplished by depressurizing the reaction solution in stages (first to 18–25 bar and then to 2–5 bar) and passing it at each stage through a steam-heated <i>carbamate decomposer</i>, then recombining the resultant carbon dioxide and ammonia in a falling-film <i>carbamate condenser</i> and pumping the carbamate solution into the previous stage.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="The_stripping_concept">The stripping concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The stripping concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The "total recycle" concept has two main disadvantages. The first is the complexity of the flow scheme and, consequently, the amount of process equipment needed. The second is the amount of water recycled in the carbamate solution, which has an adverse effect on the equilibrium in the urea conversion reaction and thus on overall plant efficiency. The stripping concept, developed in the early 1960s by <a href="/wiki/Stamicarbon" title="Stamicarbon">Stamicarbon</a> in The Netherlands, addressed both problems. It also improved heat recovery and reuse in the process.
</p><p>The position of the equilibrium in the carbamate formation/decomposition depends on the product of the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressures</a> of the reactants. In the total recycle processes, carbamate decomposition is promoted by reducing the overall pressure, which reduces the partial pressure of both ammonia and carbon dioxide. It is possible, however, to achieve a similar effect without lowering the overall pressure—by suppressing the partial pressure of just one of the reactants. Instead of feeding carbon dioxide gas directly to the reactor with the ammonia, as in the total recycle process, the stripping process first routes the carbon dioxide through a stripper (a carbamate decomposer that operates under full system pressure and configured to provide maximum gas-liquid contact). This flushes out free ammonia, reducing its partial pressure over the liquid surface and carrying it directly to a carbamate condenser (also under full system pressure). From there, reconstituted ammonium carbamate liquor passes directly to the reactor. That eliminates the medium-pressure stage of the total recycle process altogether.
</p><p>The stripping concept was such a major advance that competitors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snamprogetti" title="Snamprogetti">Snamprogetti</a>—now Saipem—(Italy), the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montedison" title="Montedison">Montedison</a> (Italy), <a href="/wiki/Toyo_Engineering_Corporation" title="Toyo Engineering Corporation">Toyo Engineering Corporation</a> (Japan), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Urea_Casale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Urea Casale (page does not exist)">Urea Casale</a> (Switzerland) all developed versions of it. Today, effectively all new urea plants use the principle, and many total recycle urea plants have converted to a stripping process. No one has proposed a radical alternative to the approach. The main thrust of technological development today, in response to industry demands for ever larger individual plants, is directed at re-configuring and re-orientating major items in the plant to reduce size and overall height of the plant, and at meeting challenging environmental performance targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_reactions">Side reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Side reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>It is fortunate that the urea conversion reaction is slow.  If it were not it would go into reverse in the stripper. As it is, succeeding stages of the process must be designed to minimize residence times, at least until the temperature reduces to the point where the reversion reaction is very slow.
</p><p>Two reactions produce impurities. <a href="/wiki/Biuret" title="Biuret">Biuret</a> is formed when two molecules of urea combine with the loss of a molecule of ammonia.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NH<sub>2</sub>CONH<sub>2</sub>   →   H<sub>2</sub>NCONHCONH<sub>2</sub>  +  NH<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Normally this reaction is suppressed in the synthesis reactor by maintaining an excess of ammonia, but after the stripper, it occurs until the temperature is reduced. Biuret is undesirable in fertilizer urea because it is toxic to crop plants, although to what extent depends on the nature of the crop and the method of application of the urea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> (Biuret is actually welcome in urea when is used as a cattle feed supplement).
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Isocyanic_acid" title="Isocyanic acid">Isocyanic acid</a></b> results from the thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_cyanate" title="Ammonium cyanate">ammonium cyanate</a>, which is in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibrium</a> with urea:
</p>
<dl><dd>NH<sub>2</sub>CONH<sub>2</sub>   →   NH<sub>4</sub>NCO   →   HNCO  +  NH<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is at its worst when the urea solution is heated at low pressure, which happens when the solution is concentrated for prilling or granulation (see below). The reaction products mostly volatilize into the overhead vapours, and recombine when these condense to form urea again, which contaminates the process condensate.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrosion">Corrosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Corrosion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Ammonium carbamate solutions are notoriously corrosive to metallic construction materials, even more resistant forms of stainless steel—especially in the hottest parts of the plant such as the stripper. Traditionally corrosion has been minimized (although not eliminated) by continuously injecting a small amount of oxygen (as air) into the plant to establish and maintain a passive oxide layer on exposed stainless steel surfaces. Because the carbon dioxide feed is recovered from ammonia synthesis gas, it contains traces of hydrogen that can mingle with passivation air to form an explosive mixture if allowed to accumulate.
</p><p>In the mid 1990s two duplex (ferritic-austenitic) stainless steels were introduced (DP28W, jointly developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toyo_Engineering" title="Toyo Engineering">Toyo Engineering</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Metals_Industries" title="Sumitomo Metals Industries">Sumitomo Metals Industries</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and Safurex, jointly developed by <a href="/wiki/Stamicarbon" title="Stamicarbon">Stamicarbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandvik" title="Sandvik">Sandvik Materials Technology</a> (Sweden).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>) These let manufactures drastically reduce the amount of passivation oxygen. In theory, they could operate with no oxygen.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Saipem" title="Saipem">Saipem</a> now uses either zirconium stripper tubes, or bimetallic tubes with a titanium body (cheaper but less erosion-resistant) and a metallurgically bonded internal zirconium lining. These tubes are fabricated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATI_Wah_Chang" title="ATI Wah Chang">ATI Wah Chang</a> (USA) using its Omegabond technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finishing">Finishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Finishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Urea can be produced as <a href="/wiki/Prill" title="Prill">prills</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/granule" title="wikt:granule">granules</a>, pellets, crystals, and solutions.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_forms">Solid forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Solid forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>For its main use as a fertilizer urea is mostly marketed in solid form, either as <a href="/wiki/Prill" title="Prill">prills</a> or granules. The advantage of prills is that, in general, they can be produced more cheaply than granules and that the technique was firmly established in industrial practice long before a satisfactory urea granulation process was commercialized.  However, on account of the limited size of particles that can be produced with the desired degree of sphericity and their low crushing and impact strength, the performance of prills during bulk storage, handling and use is generally (with some exceptions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>) considered inferior to that of granules.
</p><p>High-quality compound fertilizers containing nitrogen co-granulated with other components such as phosphates have been produced routinely since the beginnings of the modern fertilizer industry, but on account of the low melting point and hygroscopic nature of urea it took courage to apply the same kind of technology to granulate urea on its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> But at the end of the 1970s three companies began to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluidized-bed" title="Fluidized-bed">fluidized-bed</a> granulation. The first in the field was Nederlandse Stikstof Maatschappij, which later became part of Hydro Agri (now <a href="/wiki/Yara_International" title="Yara International">Yara International</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Yara eventually sold this technology to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uhde_GmbH&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uhde GmbH (page does not exist)">Uhde GmbH</a>, whose <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uhde_Fertilizer_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uhde Fertilizer Technology (page does not exist)">Uhde Fertilizer Technology</a> (UFT) subsidiary now markets it. Around the same time <a href="/wiki/Toyo_Engineering_Corporation" title="Toyo Engineering Corporation">Toyo Engineering Corporation</a> developed its spouted-bed process, comprising a fluidized bed deliberately agitated to produce turbulent ebullation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stamicarbon" title="Stamicarbon">Stamicarbon</a> also undertook development work on its own fluidized-bed granulation system, using film sprays rather than atomizing sprays to introduce the urea melt, but shelved it until the 1990s, when there was for a time considerable doubt about the commercial future of the Hydro (UFT) process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> As a result, the Stamicarbon technology is now commercialized and highly successful. More recently, Urea Casale introduced a different fluidized-bed granulation system: the urea is sprayed in laterally from the side walls of the granulator instead of from the bottom. This organizes the bed into two cylindrical masses contrarotating on parallel longitudinal axes. The raw product is uniform enough to not require screens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surprisingly, perhaps, considering the product particles not spherical, pastillation using a Rotoform steel-belt pastillator is gaining ground as a urea particle-forming process as a result of development work by <a href="/wiki/Stamicarbon" title="Stamicarbon">Stamicarbon</a> in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Sandvik" title="Sandvik">Sandvik Process Systems</a> (Germany). Single-machine capacity is limited to 175 t/d, but the machines are simple and need little maintenance, specific power consumption is much lower than for granulation, and the product is very uniform. The robustness of the product appears to make up for its non-spherical shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="UAN_solutions">UAN solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: UAN solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In admixture, the combined solubility of ammonium nitrate and urea is so much higher than that of either component alone that it is possible to obtain a stable solution (known as <a href="/wiki/UAN" title="UAN">UAN</a>) with a total nitrogen content (32%) approaching that of solid ammonium nitrate (33.5%), though not, of course, that of urea itself (46%). Given the ongoing safety and security concerns surrounding fertilizer-grade solid ammonium nitrate, UAN provides a considerably safer alternative without entirely sacrificing the agronomic properties that make ammonium nitrate more attractive than urea as a fertilizer in areas with short growing seasons. It is also more convenient to store and handle than a solid product and easier to apply accurately to the land by mechanical means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_preparation">Laboratory preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Laboratory preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ureas in the more general sense can be accessed in the laboratory by reaction of <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a> with primary or secondary <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, proceeding through an <a href="/wiki/Isocyanate" title="Isocyanate">isocyanate</a> intermediate. Non-symmetric ureas can be accessed by reaction of primary or secondary amines with an isocyanate.
</p><p>Also, urea is produced when <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a> reacts with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>COCl<sub>2</sub> + 4 NH<sub>3</sub>   →   (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO + 2 NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</dd></dl>
<p>Urea is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproduct" title="Byproduct">byproduct</a> of converting alkyl halides to <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> via a S-alkylation of <a href="/wiki/Thiourea" title="Thiourea">thiourea</a>. Such reactions proceed via the intermediacy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isothiouronium_salt" title="Isothiouronium salt">isothiouronium salts</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>RX + CS(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> → RSCX(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>X</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>RSCX(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>X + MOH → RSH + (NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO + MX</dd></dl>
<p>In this reaction R is alkyl group, X is halogen and M is alkali metal.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_process">Historical process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Historical process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea was first noticed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Boerhaave" title="Hermann Boerhaave">Hermann Boerhaave</a> in the early 18th century from evaporates of urine. In 1773, <a href="/wiki/Hilaire_Rouelle" title="Hilaire Rouelle">Hilaire Rouelle</a> obtained crystals containing urea from human urine by evaporating it and treating it with alcohol in successive filtrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> This method was aided by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a>'s discovery that urine treated by concentrated nitric acid precipitated crystals. <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Fran%C3%A7ois,_comte_de_Fourcroy" title="Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy">Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin" title="Louis Nicolas Vauquelin">Louis Nicolas Vauquelin</a> discovered in 1799 that the nitrated crystals were identical to Rouelle's substance and invented the term "urea."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berzelius" title="Berzelius">Berzelius</a> made further improvements to its purification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and finally <a href="/wiki/William_Prout" title="William Prout">William Prout</a>, in 1817, succeeded in obtaining and determining the chemical composition of the pure substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In the evolved procedure, urea was precipitated as urea nitrate by adding strong nitric acid to urine. To purify the resulting crystals, they were dissolved in boiling water with charcoal and filtered. After cooling, pure crystals of urea nitrate form. To reconstitute the urea from the nitrate, the crystals are dissolved in warm water, and <a href="/wiki/Barium_carbonate" title="Barium carbonate">barium carbonate</a> added. The water is then evaporated and anhydrous alcohol added to extract the urea. This solution is drained off and evaporated, leaving pure urea.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_and_crystal_structure">Molecular and crystal structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Molecular and crystal structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The urea molecule is planar in the crystal structure, but the geometry around the nitrogens is pyramidal in the gas-phase minimum-energy structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In solid urea, the oxygen center is engaged in two N-H-O <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a>. The resulting dense and energetically favourable hydrogen-bond network is probably established at the cost of efficient molecular packing: The structure is quite open, the ribbons forming tunnels with square cross-section.  The carbon in urea is described as sp<sup>2</sup> hybridized, the C-N bonds have significant double bond character, and the carbonyl oxygen is basic compared to, say, <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>. Urea's high aqueous solubility reflects its ability to engage in extensive hydrogen bonding with water.
</p><p>By virtue of its tendency to form porous frameworks, urea has the ability to trap many organic compounds.  In these so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrates</a>, the organic "guest" molecules are held in channels formed  by interpenetrating helices composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen-bonded</a> urea molecules. This behaviour can be used to separate mixtures, e.g., in the production of <a href="/wiki/Aviation_fuel" title="Aviation fuel">aviation fuel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lubricating_oil" title="Lubricating oil">lubricating oils</a>, and in the separation of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>.
</p><p>As the helices are interconnected, all helices in a crystal must have the same <a href="/wiki/Stereoisomerism" title="Stereoisomerism">molecular handedness</a>. This is determined when the crystal is nucleated and can thus be forced by seeding.  The resulting crystals have been used to separate <a href="/wiki/Racemic_mixture" title="Racemic mixture">racemic mixtures</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urea dissolved in water is in equilibrium with the isomeric <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_cyanate" title="Ammonium cyanate">ammonium cyanate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The resulting activity of the <a href="/wiki/Isocyanic_acid" title="Isocyanic acid">isocyanic acid</a> ions do result in carbamylation (formation of long-chain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbamide" title="Carbamide">carbamides</a>, liberating ammonia molecule as byproduct) of proteins if proteins are present in the solution too. The carbamylation reaction may occurs at elevated temperatures even without catalysts. At room temperature, water solutions of urea are prone to same decomposition reaction in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Urease" title="Urease">urease</a>. The isomerization of urea in solution at room temperature without catalysts is a slow process (taking days to reach equilibrium), and freshly prepared, unheated solutions had negligible carbamylation rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Urea reacts with alcohols to form <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_carbamate" title="Ethyl carbamate">urethanes</a>.
</p><p>Urea reacts with <a href="/wiki/Malonic_acid" title="Malonic acid">malonic</a> esters to make <a href="/wiki/Barbituric_acid" title="Barbituric acid">barbituric acids</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_pronunciation">Etymology and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Etymology and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Urea</i> is <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> from French <i>urée</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> οὖρον <i>ouron</i>, "urine".
</p>
