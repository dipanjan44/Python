<p><b>Lanthanum</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>La</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 57. It is a <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">soft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, silvery-white <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that tarnishes rapidly when exposed to air and is soft enough to be cut with a knife. It is the eponym of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, a group of 15 similar elements between lanthanum and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>, of which lanthanum is the first and the prototype. It is also sometimes considered the first element of the 6th-period <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metals" title="Transition metals">transition metals</a> and is traditionally counted among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a>. The usual <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> is +3. Lanthanum has no biological role in humans but is essential to some bacteria. It is not particularly toxic to humans but does show some antimicrobial activity.
</p><p>Lanthanum usually occurs together with <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> and the other rare earth elements. Lanthanum was first found by the Swedish chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Mosander" title="Carl Gustav Mosander">Carl Gustav Mosander</a> in 1839 as an impurity in <a href="/wiki/Cerium_nitrate" title="Cerium nitrate">cerium nitrate</a> – hence the name <i>lanthanum</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>λανθάνειν</i> (<i>lanthanein</i>), meaning "to lie hidden". Although it is classified as a rare earth element, lanthanum is the 28th most abundant element in the Earth's crust, almost three times as abundant as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. In minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, lanthanum composes about a quarter of the lanthanide content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is extracted from those minerals by a process of such complexity that pure lanthanum metal was not isolated until 1923.
</p><p>Lanthanum compounds have numerous applications as <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysts</a>, additives in glass, carbon arc lamps for studio lights and projectors, ignition elements in <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">lighters</a> and torches, <a href="/wiki/Hot_cathode" title="Hot cathode">electron cathodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">GTAW</a> electrodes, and other things. <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_carbonate" title="Lanthanum carbonate">Lanthanum carbonate</a> is used as a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate_binder" title="Phosphate binder">phosphate binder</a> in cases of <a href="/wiki/Hyperphosphatemia" title="Hyperphosphatemia">renal failure</a>. It is also an element in the 6th period and in the 3rd group.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lanthanum is the first element and prototype of the lanthanide series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[a]</a></sup> In the periodic table, it appears to the right of the <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a> and to the left of the lanthanide cerium. Lanthanum is often considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Group_3_element" title="Group 3 element">group 3 element</a>, along with its lighter <a href="/wiki/Congener_(chemistry)" title="Congener (chemistry)">congeners</a> <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> and its heavier congener, the radioactive <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1102_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1102-7">[6]</a></sup> although this classification is sometimes disputed. Similarly to scandium, yttrium, and actinium, the 57 electrons of a lanthanum atom are arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">configuration</a> [Xe]5d<sup>1</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>, with three valence electrons outside the noble gas core. In chemical reactions, lanthanum almost always gives up these three valence electrons from the 5d and 6s <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell#Subshells" title="Electron shell">subshells</a> to form the +3 oxidation state, achieving the stable configuration of the preceding noble gas <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1106_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1106-8">[7]</a></sup> Some lanthanum(II) compounds are also known, but they are much less stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the lanthanides, lanthanum is exceptional as it does not have any 4f electrons; indeed, the sudden contraction and lowering of energy of the 4f orbital that is important for the chemistry of the lanthanides only begins to happen at cerium. Thus it is only very weakly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>, unlike the strongly paramagnetic later lanthanides (with the exceptions of the last two, <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>, where the 4f shell is completely full).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Furthermore, since the melting points of the trivalent lanthanides are related to the extent of hybridisation of the 6s, 5d, and 4f electrons, lanthanum has the second-lowest (after cerium) melting point among all the lanthanides: 920 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The lanthanides become harder as the series is traversed: as expected, lanthanum is a soft metal. Lanthanum has a relatively high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> of 615 nΩm at room temperature; in comparison, the value for the good conductor aluminium is only 26.50 nΩm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1429_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1429-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup> Lanthanum is the least volatile of the lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radio_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-radio-14">[13]</a></sup> Like most of the lanthanides, lanthanum has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal crystal structure</a> at room temperature. At 310 °C, lanthanum changes to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centered_cubic" title="Face-centered cubic">face-centered cubic</a> structure, and at 865 °C, it changes to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As expected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_trend" title="Periodic trend">periodic trends</a>, lanthanum has the largest <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic radius</a> of the lanthanides and the stable group 3 elements. Hence, it is the most reactive among them, tarnishing slowly in air and burning readily to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanum(III)_oxide" title="Lanthanum(III) oxide">lanthanum(III) oxide</a>, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, which is almost as basic as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1105_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1105-15">[14]</a></sup> A centimeter-sized sample of lanthanum will corrode completely in a year as its oxide <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spalls</a> off like iron <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, instead of forming a protective oxide coating like <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and lanthanum's lighter congeners scandium and yttrium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> Lanthanum reacts with the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> at room temperature to form the trihalides, and upon warming will form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary compounds</a> with the nonmetals nitrogen, carbon, sulfur, phosphorus, boron, selenium, silicon and arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1106_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1106-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup> Lanthanum reacts slowly with water to form <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_hydroxide" title="Lanthanum hydroxide">lanthanum(III) hydroxide</a>, La(OH)<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-17">[16]</a></sup> In dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, lanthanum readily forms the aquated tripositive ion <span class="nowrap">[La(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup></span>: this is colorless in aqueous solution since La<sup>3+</sup> has no f electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-17">[16]</a></sup> Lanthanum is the strongest and <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">hardest</a> base among the lanthanides and group 3 elements, which is again expected from its being the largest of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1434_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1434-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_lanthanum" title="Isotopes of lanthanum">Isotopes of lanthanum</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring lanthanum is made up of two isotopes, the stable <sup>139</sup>La and the <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial long-lived radioisotope</a> <sup>138</sup>La. <sup>139</sup>La is by far the most abundant, making up 99.910% of natural lanthanum: it is produced in the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a> (slow <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> capture, which occurs in low- to medium-mass stars) and the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a> (rapid neutron capture, which occurs in core-collapse <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-19">[18]</a></sup> The very rare isotope <sup>138</sup>La is one of the few primordial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odd-odd_nuclei" title="Odd-odd nuclei">odd-odd nuclei</a>, with a long half-life of 1.05×10<sup>11</sup> years: it is one of the proton-rich <a href="/wiki/P-nuclei" title="P-nuclei">p-nuclei</a> which cannot be produced in the s- or r-processes. <sup>138</sup>La, along with the even rarer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantalum-180m" title="Tantalum-180m"><sup>180m</sup>Ta</a>, is produced in the ν-process, where <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> interact with stable nuclei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nu-process_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-nu-process-20">[19]</a></sup> All other lanthanum isotopes are <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">synthetic</a>: with the exception of <sup>137</sup>La with a half-life of about 60,000 years, all of them have half-lives less than a day, and most have half-lives less than a minute. The isotopes <sup>139</sup>La and <sup>140</sup>La occur as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission products</a> of uranium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_oxide" title="Lanthanum oxide">Lanthanum oxide</a> is a white solid that can be prepared by direct reaction of its constituent elements. Due to the large size of the La<sup>3+</sup> ion, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> adopts a hexagonal 7-coordinate structure that changes to the 6-coordinate structure of <a href="/wiki/Scandium_oxide" title="Scandium oxide">scandium oxide</a> (Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Yttrium(III)_oxide" title="Yttrium(III) oxide">yttrium oxide</a> (Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) at high temperature. When it reacts with water, lanthanum hydroxide is formed: a lot of heat is evolved in the reaction and a hissing sound is heard. Lanthanum hydroxide will react with atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to form the basic carbonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1107_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1107-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lanthanum fluoride is insoluble in water and can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis" title="Qualitative inorganic analysis">qualitative</a> test for the presence of La<sup>3+</sup>. The heavier halides are all very soluble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deliquescent" title="Deliquescent">deliquescent</a> compounds. The anhydrous halides are produced by direct reaction of their elements, as heating the hydrates causes hydrolysis: for example, heating hydrated LaCl<sub>3</sub> produces LaOCl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1107_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1107-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lanthanum reacts exothermically with hydrogen to produce the dihydride LaH<sub>2</sub>, a black, <a href="/wiki/Pyrophoricity" title="Pyrophoricity">pyrophoric</a>, brittle, conducting compound with the <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fukai_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fukai-22">[21]</a></sup> This is a non-stoichiometric compound, and further absorption of hydrogen is possible, with a concomitant loss of electrical conductivity, until the more salt-like LaH<sub>3</sub> is reached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1107_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1107-21">[20]</a></sup> Like LaI<sub>2</sub> and LaI, LaH<sub>2</sub> is probably an <a href="/wiki/Electride" title="Electride">electride</a> compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1107_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1107-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the large ionic radius and great electropositivity of La<sup>3+</sup>, there is not much covalent contribution to its bonding and hence it has a limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordination chemistry</a>, like yttrium and the other lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1108_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1108-23">[22]</a></sup> Lanthanum oxalate does not dissolve very much in alkali-metal oxalate solutions, and [La(acac)<sub>3</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>] decomposes around 500 °C. Oxygen is the most common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donor_atom" title="Donor atom">donor atom</a> in lanthanum complexes, which are mostly ionic and often have high coordination numbers over 6: 8 is the most characteristic, forming <a href="/wiki/Square_antiprism" title="Square antiprism">square antiprismatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snub_disphenoid" title="Snub disphenoid">dodecadeltahedral</a> structures. These high-coordinate species, reaching up to coordination number 12 with the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelating_ligand" title="Chelating ligand">chelating ligands</a> such as in La<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·9H<sub>2</sub>O, often have a low degree of symmetry because of stereo-chemical factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1108_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1108-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lanthanum chemistry tends not to involve π bonding due to the electron configuration of the element: thus its organometallic chemistry is quite limited. The best characterized organolanthanum compounds are the <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">cyclopentadienyl complex</a> La(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, which is produced by reacting anhydrous LaCl<sub>3</sub> with NaC<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub> in <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a>, and its methyl-substituted derivatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1110_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1110-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1902" data-file-width="1560" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg/200px-Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg/300px-Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg/400px-Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mosander_Carl_Gustav_bw.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a>, discoverer of lanthanum</div></div></div>
<p>In 1751, the Swedish mineralogist <a href="/wiki/Axel_Fredrik_Cronstedt" title="Axel Fredrik Cronstedt">Axel Fredrik Cronstedt</a> discovered a heavy mineral from the mine at <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4s" title="Bastnäs">Bastnäs</a>, later named <a href="/wiki/Cerite" title="Cerite">cerite</a>. Thirty years later, the fifteen-year-old <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vilhelm_Hisinger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vilhelm Hisinger (page does not exist)">Vilhelm Hisinger</a>, from the family owning the mine, sent a sample of it to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Scheele" title="Carl Scheele">Carl Scheele</a>, who did not find any new elements within. In 1803, after Hisinger had become an ironmaster, he returned to the mineral with <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> and isolated a new oxide which they named <i>ceria</i> after the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>, which had been discovered two years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> Ceria was simultaneously independently isolated in Germany by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1424_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1424-26">[25]</a></sup> Between 1839 and 1843, ceria was shown to be a mixture of oxides by the Swedish surgeon and chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a>, who lived in the same house as Berzelius: he separated out two other oxides which he named <i>lanthana</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymia</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-27">[26]</a></sup> He partially decomposed a sample of <a href="/wiki/Cerium_nitrate" title="Cerium nitrate">cerium nitrate</a> by roasting it in air and then treating the resulting oxide with dilute <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Since lanthanum's properties differed only slightly from those of cerium, and occurred along with it in its salts, he named it from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>λανθάνειν</i> [lanthanein] (lit. <i>to lie hidden</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1424_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1424-26">[25]</a></sup> Relatively pure lanthanum metal was first isolated in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum is the third-most abundant of all the lanthanides, making up 39 mg/kg of the Earth's crust, behind <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> at 41.5 mg/kg and cerium at 66.5 mg/kg. It is almost three times as abundant as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in the Earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Despite being among the so-called "rare earth metals", lanthanum is thus not rare at all, but it is historically so named because it is rarer than "common earths" such as lime and magnesia, and historically only a few deposits were known. Lanthanum is considered a rare earth metal because the process to mine it is difficult, time-consuming, and expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup> Lanthanum is rarely the dominant lanthanide found in the rare earth minerals, and in their chemical formulae it is usually preceded by cerium. Rare examples of La-dominant minerals are monazite-(La) and lanthanite-(La).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:732px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg"><img alt="Monazite acid cracking process.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="1160" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/730px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/1095px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg/1460px-Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg.png 2x" width="730"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monazite_acid_cracking_process.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div></div>
<p>The La<sup>3+</sup> ion is similarly-sized to the early lanthanides of the cerium group (those up to <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>) that immediately follow in the periodic table, and hence it tends to occur along with them in <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> (M<sup>III</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> (M<sup>III</sup>CO<sub>3</sub>F), where M refers to all the rare earth metals except scandium and the radioactive <a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a> (mostly Ce, La, and Y).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1103_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1103-31">[30]</a></sup> Bastnäsite is usually lacking in <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and the heavy lanthanides, and the purification of the light lanthanides from it is less involved. The ore, after being crushed and ground, is first treated with hot concentrated sulfuric acid, evolving carbon dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silicon_tetrafluoride" title="Silicon tetrafluoride">silicon tetrafluoride</a>: the product is then dried and leached with water, leaving the early lanthanide ions, including lanthanum, in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1426_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1426-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The procedure for monazite, which usually contains all the rare earths as well as thorium, is more involved. Monazite, because of its magnetic properties, can be separated by repeated electromagnetic separation. After separation, it is treated with hot concentrated sulfuric acid to produce water-soluble sulfates of rare earths. The acidic filtrates are partially neutralized with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> to pH 3-4. Thorium precipitates out of solution as hydroxide and is removed. After that, the solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths to their insoluble <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalates</a>. The oxalates are converted to oxides by annealing. The oxides are dissolved in nitric acid that excludes one of the main components, <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>, whose oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub>. Lanthanum is separated as a double salt with ammonium nitrate by crystallization. This salt is relatively less soluble than other rare earth double salts and therefore stays in the residue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup> Care must be taken when handling some of the residues as they contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radium-228" title="Radium-228"><sup>228</sup>Ra</a>, the daughter of <sup>232</sup>Th, which is a strong gamma emitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1426_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1426-32">[31]</a></sup> Lanthanum is relatively easy to extract as it has only one neighbouring lanthanide, cerium, which can be removed by making use of its ability to be oxidised to the +4 state; thereafter, lanthanum may be separated out by the historical method of <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a> of La(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·2NH<sub>4</sub>NO<sub>3</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O, or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> techniques when higher purity is desired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1426_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1426-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lanthanum metal is obtained from its oxide by heating it with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> or fluoride and hydrofluoric acid at 300-400 °C to produce the chloride or fluoride:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 NH<sub>4</sub>Cl → 2 LaCl<sub>3</sub> + 6 NH<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>This is followed by reduction with alkali or alkaline earth metals in vacuum or argon atmosphere:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>LaCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 Li → La + 3 LiCl</dd></dl>
<p>Also, pure lanthanum can be produced by electrolysis of molten mixture of anhydrous LaCl<sub>3</sub> and NaCl or KCl at elevated temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1059" data-file-width="1017" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/220px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/330px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg/440px-Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glowing_gas_mantle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Company" title="Coleman Company">Coleman</a> <a href="/wiki/White_gas" title="White gas">white gas</a> lantern mantle burning at full brightness</div></div></div>
<p>The first historical application of lanthanum was in gas lantern <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">mantles</a>. Carl Auer von Welsbach used a mixture of 60% <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, 20% lanthanum oxide, and 20% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a>, which he called <i>Actinophor</i> and patented in 1885. The original mantles gave a green-tinted light and were not very successful, and his first company, which established a factory in <a href="/wiki/Atzgersdorf" title="Atzgersdorf">Atzgersdorf</a> in 1887, failed in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="It seems that EB1911 confirms Carl Auer von Welsbach patented the process in 1880s but none of the specific details mentioned in this paragraph are in the EB1911 (August 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Modern uses of lanthanum include:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaB6HotCathode.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/LaB6HotCathode.jpg/220px-LaB6HotCathode.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/LaB6HotCathode.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaB6HotCathode.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><span class="chemf nowrap">LaB<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> hot cathode</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zblan_transmit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="634" data-file-width="1000" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/220px-Zblan_transmit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/330px-Zblan_transmit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/440px-Zblan_transmit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zblan_transmit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of infrared transmittance of ZBLAN glass and silica</div></div></div>
<ul><li>One material used for anodic material of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-metal_hydride_battery" title="Nickel-metal hydride battery">nickel-metal hydride batteries</a> is <span class="chemf nowrap">La(Ni<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3.6</sub></span>Mn<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0.4</sub></span>Al<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0.3</sub></span>Co<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0.7</sub></span>)</span>. Due to high cost to extract the other lanthanides, a <a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">mischmetal</a> with more than 50% of lanthanum is used instead of pure lanthanum.  The compound is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermetallics" title="Intermetallics">intermetallic</a> component of the <span class="chemf nowrap">AB<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd>As most hybrid cars use nickel-metal hydride batteries, massive quantities of lanthanum are required for the production of hybrid automobiles. A typical hybrid automobile battery for a <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a> requires 10 to 15 kilograms (22 to 33 lb) of lanthanum. As engineers push the technology to increase fuel efficiency, twice that amount of lanthanum could be required per vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Hydrogen sponge alloys can contain lanthanum. These alloys are capable of storing up to 400 times their own volume of hydrogen gas in a reversible adsorption process. Heat energy is released every time they do so; therefore these alloys have possibilities in energy conservation systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mischmetal" title="Mischmetal">Mischmetal</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> alloy used in lighter flints, contains 25% to 45% lanthanum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2-39">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_oxide" title="Lanthanum oxide">Lanthanum oxide</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_hexaboride" title="Lanthanum hexaboride">boride</a> are used in electronic <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> as <a href="/wiki/Hot_cathode" title="Hot cathode">hot cathode</a> materials with strong emissivity of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>. Crystals of <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_hexaboride" title="Lanthanum hexaboride">LaB<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></a></span> are used in high-brightness, extended-life, thermionic electron emission sources for <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hall-effect_thruster" title="Hall-effect thruster">Hall-effect thrusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_trifluoride" title="Lanthanum trifluoride">Lanthanum trifluoride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">LaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) is an essential component of a heavy fluoride glass named <a href="/wiki/ZBLAN" title="ZBLAN">ZBLAN</a>. This glass has superior transmittance in the infrared range and is therefore used for fiber-optical communication systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rutg_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-rutg-41">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cerium-doped <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum(III)_bromide" title="Lanthanum(III) bromide">lanthanum bromide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum(III)_bromide" title="Lanthanum(III) bromide">lanthanum chloride</a> are the recent inorganic <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillators</a>, which have a combination of high light yield, best energy resolution, and fast response. Their high yield converts into superior energy resolution; moreover, the light output is very stable and quite high over a very wide range of temperatures, making it particularly attractive for high-temperature applications. These scintillators are already widely used commercially in detectors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrons" title="Neutrons">neutrons</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_arc_lamp" title="Carbon arc lamp">Carbon arc lamps</a> use a mixture of rare earth elements to improve the light quality. This  application, especially by the <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">motion picture</a> industry for studio lighting and projection, consumed about 25% of the rare-earth compounds produced until the phase out of carbon arc lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanum(III)_oxide" title="Lanthanum(III) oxide">Lanthanum(III) oxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">La<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) improves the alkali resistance of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and is used in making special optical glasses, such as infrared-absorbing glass, as well as <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>, because of the high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> and low dispersion of rare-earth glasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup> Lanthanum oxide is also used as a grain-growth additive during the liquid-phase <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_nitride" title="Silicon nitride">silicon nitride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_diboride" title="Zirconium diboride">zirconium diboride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>Small amounts of lanthanum added to <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> improves its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleability" title="Malleability">malleability</a>, resistance to impact, and <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a>, whereas addition of lanthanum to <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> decreases its hardness and sensitivity to temperature variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-13">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Small amounts of lanthanum are present in many pool products to remove the phosphates that feed algae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lanthanum oxide additive to tungsten is used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">gas tungsten arc welding</a> electrodes, as a substitute for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> thorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>Various compounds of lanthanum and other rare-earth elements (oxides, chlorides, etc.) are components of various catalysis, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleum_cracking" title="Petroleum cracking">petroleum cracking</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lanthanum-barium <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> is used to estimate age of rocks and ores, though the technique has limited popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_carbonate" title="Lanthanum carbonate">Lanthanum carbonate</a> was approved as a medication (Fosrenol, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shire_Pharmaceuticals" title="Shire Pharmaceuticals">Shire Pharmaceuticals</a>) to absorb excess <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> in cases of <a href="/wiki/Hyperphosphatemia" title="Hyperphosphatemia">end-stage renal failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fosrenol_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-fosrenol-50">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lanthanum fluoride is used in phosphor lamp coatings. Mixed with europium fluoride, it is also applied in the crystal membrane of <a href="/wiki/Fluoride_selective_electrode" title="Fluoride selective electrode">fluoride ion-selective electrodes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-9">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Like <a href="/wiki/Horseradish_peroxidase" title="Horseradish peroxidase">horseradish peroxidase</a>, lanthanum is used as an electron-dense tracer in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lanthanum-modified bentonite (or <a href="/wiki/Phoslock" title="Phoslock">phoslock</a>) is used to remove phosphates from water in lake treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum has no known biological role in humans. The element is very poorly absorbed after oral administration and when injected its elimination is very slow. <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_carbonate" title="Lanthanum carbonate">Lanthanum carbonate</a> (Fosrenol) was approved as a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate_binder" title="Phosphate binder">phosphate binder</a> to absorb excess phosphate in cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_stage_renal_disease" title="End stage renal disease">end stage renal disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fosrenol_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-fosrenol-50">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>While lanthanum has pharmacological effects on several receptors and ion channels, its specificity for the <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-Aminobutyric acid">GABA</a> receptor is unique among trivalent cations. Lanthanum acts at the same modulatory site on the <a href="/wiki/GABA_receptor" title="GABA receptor">GABA receptor</a> as <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, a known negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allosteric" title="Allosteric">allosteric</a> modulator. The lanthanum cation La<sup>3+</sup> is a positive allosteric modulator at native and recombinant GABA receptors, increasing open channel time and decreasing desensitization in a subunit configuration dependent manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lanthanum is an essential cofactor for the methanol dehydrogenase of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanotrophic" title="Methanotrophic">methanotrophic</a> bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Methylacidiphilum_fumariolicum" title="Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum">Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum</a></i> SolV, although the great chemical similarity of the lanthanides means that it may be substituted with cerium, praseodymium, or neodymium without ill effects, and with the smaller samarium, europium, or gadolinium giving no side effects other than slower growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Lanthanum has a low to moderate level of toxicity and should be handled with care. The injection of lanthanum solutions produces <a href="/wiki/Hyperglycemia" title="Hyperglycemia">hyperglycemia</a>, low blood pressure, degeneration of the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">hepatic</a> alterations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iqbal_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iqbal-56">[55]</a></sup> The application in carbon arc light led to the exposure of people to rare earth element oxides and fluorides, which sometimes led to <a href="/wiki/Pneumoconiosis" title="Pneumoconiosis">pneumoconiosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> As the La<sup>3+</sup> ion is similar in size to the Ca<sup>2+</sup> ion, it is sometimes used as an easily traced substitute for the latter in medical studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-59">[58]</a></sup> Lanthanum, like the other lanthanides, is known to affect human metabolism, lowering cholesterol levels, blood pressure, appetite, and risk of blood coagulation. When injected into the brain, it acts as a painkiller, similarly to <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> and other opiates, though the mechanism behind this is still unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-59">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
