<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sea level rise</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the current and future rise in sea level associated with <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. For general discussion of sea level, see <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">Sea level</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The current long-term trend for sea levels to rise mainly in response to global warming.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="940" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg/400px-Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg/600px-Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg/800px-Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evolution-of-GMSL-time-series-from-six-different-groups%E2%80%99.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global mean sea level evolution from different datasets (1993–2018).</div></div></div>
<p>At least since 1880, the average global <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> has been rising. This is due mostly to anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> that is driving the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> of seawater while melting land-based <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mengel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mengel-1">[1]</a></sup> This trend is expected to accelerate during the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>62</span></sup>
</p><p>Projecting future sea level has always been challenging, due to our imperfect understanding of many aspects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_system" title="Climate system">climate system</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climate research</a> leads to improved <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">computer models</a>, projections have consistently increased.  For example, in 2007 the high end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) projections through 2099 was less than 2 feet (0.61 m),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but in their 2014 report the high end was considered to be about 3 feet (0.91 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A number of later studies have concluded that 2.0 to 2.7 metres (6 ft 7 in to 8 ft 10 in) rise this century is "physically plausible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The contributions to <b>sea level rise</b> since 1993, based on 2018 figures, divide into ocean <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> (42%), melting of temperate glaciers (21%), <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> (15%) and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> (8%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCRP2018_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WCRP2018-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sea level rise will not be the same at every location on earth, with some locations even getting a drop in sea levels. Local factors include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> effects, and <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> of the land, tides, currents and storms. Sea level rise is expected to continue for centuries. Because of <a href="/wiki/Climate_inertia" title="Climate inertia">long response times</a> for parts of the climate system, it has been estimated that we are committed to a sea-level rise of approximately 2.3 metres (7.5 ft) within the next 2,000 years for each <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> degree of temperature rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sea level rises <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans" title="Effects of climate change on humans">can considerably influence human populations</a> in coastal and island regions and natural environments like <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg1-5-5-1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wg1-5-5-1-8">[8]</a></sup> Widespread <a href="/wiki/Coastal_flood" title="Coastal flood">coastal flooding</a> would be expected if several degrees of warming is sustained for millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr-9">[9]</a></sup> For example, sustained global warming of more than 2 C° relative to pre-industrial levels could lead to eventual sea level rise of about 1–4 metres (3.3–13 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Societies can respond to sea level rise in three different ways: retreat, accommodate and protect. Sometimes these adaptation strategies go hand in hand, but at other times choices have to be made between different strategies. Ecosystems that adapt to rising sea levels by moving inland might not always be able to do so, due to natural or man made barriers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_changes_in_sea_level">Past changes in sea level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Past changes in sea level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="526" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/220px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/330px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/440px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Changes in sea level since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial episode</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Past_sea_level" title="Past sea level">Past sea level</a></div><p>Understanding past sea level is important for the analysis of current and future changes. In the recent geological past changes in land ice and thermal expansion from increased temperatures are the dominant reasons of sea level rise. The last time the Earth was 2 °C warmer than the pre-industrial temperatures, sea levels were at least 5 metres (16 ft) higher than present. This was during the last <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a>, when the earth warming was caused by slow changes in the <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">orbital forcing</a>. The warming was sustained over a period of thousands of years and the magnitude of the rise in sea level implied a large contribution from the Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1139</span></sup>
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">last glacial maximum</a> about 20,000 years ago, the sea level has risen by more than 125 metres (410 ft), with rates varying from less than a mm/year to 40+ mm/year, as a result of melting ice sheets over Canada and Eurasia. Rapid disintegration of ice sheets led to so called '<a href="/wiki/Meltwater_pulse_1A" title="Meltwater pulse 1A">meltwater pulses</a>', periods during which sea level rose rapidly. The rate of rise started to slow down 8.2 thousand years before present; the sea level was almost constant in the last 2,500 years, before the recent rising trend starting approximately in 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_measurement">Sea level measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sea level measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To get precise measurements for sea level, researchers studying the state of frozen water and the ocean on our planet factor in ongoing <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">deformations of the solid Earth</a>, in particular due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_isostatic_adjustment" title="Glacial isostatic adjustment">landmasses still rising from past ice masses retreating</a>. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">Earth gravitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">rotation</a> have to be accounted for. These factors are dependent on the different layers which make up the Earth (<a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a>), and the order in which land-based ice melts. Because the involved processes, which are collectively known as the <i>Sea-level equation</i>, change very slowly, on time scales of thousands of years, they are considered to be constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCRP2018_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-WCRP2018-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellites">Satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1280" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg/220px-TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg/330px-TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg/440px-TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TOPEX-JasonSeries2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jason-1" title="Jason-1">Jason-1</a> continued the sea surface measurements started by TOPEX/Poseidon. It was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Surface_Topography_Mission" title="Ocean Surface Topography Mission">Ocean Surface Topography Mission</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jason-2" title="Jason-2">Jason-2</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/Jason-3" title="Jason-3">Jason-3</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="404" data-file-width="653" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg/220px-NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg/330px-NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg/440px-NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Satellite-sea-level-rise-observations-1993-April-2018.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite measurements of sea level, in millimeters, 1993–2018 (April).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1337" data-file-width="2425" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png/220px-NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png/330px-NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png/440px-NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_sea_level_trend_1993_2010.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea level trends from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_altimetry" title="Satellite altimetry">satellite altimetry</a> (1993–2012)</div></div></div>
<p>Since the 1992 launch of <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">TOPEX/Poseidon</a>, altimetric satellites have been recording the change in sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Those satellites can measure the hills and valleys in the sea caused by currents and detect trends in their height. To measure the distance to the sea surface, the satellite sends a microwave pulse to the ocean's surface and records the time it takes to return. A <a href="/wiki/Microwave_radiometer" title="Microwave radiometer">microwave radiometer</a> corrects any delay that may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Combining this data with the precise location of the spacecraft makes it possible to determine sea-surface height to within a few centimeters (about one inch).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Current rates of sea level rise from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_altimetry" title="Satellite altimetry">satellite altimetry</a> have been estimated to be 3.0 ± 0.4 millimetres (0.118 ± 0.016 in) per year for the period 1993–2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> Earlier satellite measurements were previously at odds with <a href="/wiki/Tide_gauge" title="Tide gauge">tide gauge</a> measurements. A small calibration error for the Topex/Poseidon satellite discovered in 2015 was identified as the cause of this mismatch. It had caused a slight overestimation of the 1992–2005 sea levels, which masked the ongoing sea level rise acceleration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>With satellites it is possible to capture regional variations in sea level rise well. In the 1993–2012 period for instance, sea level rose substantially in the western tropical Pacific. The sharp rise in this area has been linked to increasing <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a>, which occur when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Decadal_Oscillation" title="Pacific Decadal Oscillation">Pacific Decadal Oscillation</a> (PDO) and the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) change from one state to the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The PDO is a basin-wide climate pattern consisting of two phases, each commonly lasting 10 to 30 years, while the ENSO has a shorter period of 2 to 7 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tide_gauges">Tide gauges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tide gauges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another important source of sea-level observations comes from the global network of tide gauges. In contrast to the satellite record, this record has a lot of spatial and temporal gaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup> Coverage of tide gauges started primarily in the Northern Hemisphere, with data for the Southern Hemisphere remained scarce up to the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">[18]</a></sup> The longest running sea-level measurements, NAP or <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Ordnance_Datum" title="Amsterdam Ordnance Datum">Amsterdam Ordnance Datum</a> established in 1675, are recorded in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Netherlands" title="The Netherlands">the Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In Australia, record collection is also quite extensive, including measurements by an amateur meteorologist beginning in 1837 and measurements taken from a sea-level benchmark struck on a small cliff on the Isle of the Dead<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> near the <a href="/wiki/Port_Arthur,_Tasmania" title="Port Arthur, Tasmania">Port Arthur</a> convict settlement in 1841.
</p><p>This network was used, in combination with satellite altimeter data, to establish that global mean sea-level rose 19.5 cm (7.7 in) between 1870 and 2004 at an average rate of about 1.44 mm/yr (1.7 mm/yr during the 20th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-church_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-church-21">[21]</a></sup> This is an important confirmation of climate change simulations, predicting that SLR will accelerate in response to global warming. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, data collected by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) show the current global mean sea level trend to be 3.2 mm (0.13 in) per year, a doubling of the rate during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some regional differences are also visible in the tide gauge data. Some of the recorded regional differences are due to differences in the actual sea level, while other are due to vertical land movements. In the United States for instance, considerable variation is found because some land areas are rising and some are sinking. Over the past 100 years, the rate of sea level rise varied from an increase of about 0.36 inches (9.1 mm) per year along the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> Coast due to land sinking, to a drop of a few inches per decade in parts of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a>. The rate of sea level rise increased during the 1993–2003 period compared with the longer-term average (1961–2003), although it is unclear whether the faster rate reflected a short-term variation or an increase in the long-term trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sheet_dynamics" title="Ice sheet dynamics">Ice sheet dynamics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1764" data-file-width="1196" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/170px-Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/255px-Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg/340px-Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Corp2400_-_Flickr_-_NOAA_Photo_Library.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up of <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a>, the largest ice shelf of Antarctica, about the size of France and up to several hundred metres thick.</div></div></div>
<p>There are three main contributions to sea level rise. Oceans <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">expand</a> if they are warming, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> at high altitudes melt and the total mass of ice sheets decreases. Sea level rise in the last 150 years was dominated by retreat of glaciers and expansion of the ocean, but the contributions of the two large ice sheets (Greenland and Antarctica) is expected to increase in the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mengel_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mengel-1">[1]</a></sup> The ice sheets store most of the land ice (∼99.5%), with a sea-level equivalent (SLE) of 7.4 m (24 ft) for Greenland and 58.3 m (191 ft) for Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCRP2018_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-WCRP2018-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Each year about 8 mm (0.31 in) of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation#Measurement" title="Precipitation">precipitation (liquid equivalent)</a> falls on the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">ice sheets in Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a>, mostly as snow, which accumulates and over time forms glacial ice. Much of this precipitation began as water vapor evaporated from the ocean surface. To a first approximation, the same amount of water appeared to return to the ocean in <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a> and from ice melting at the edges. Scientists previously had estimated which is greater, ice going in or coming out, called the <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">glacier mass balance</a>, important because a nonzero balance causes changes in global sea level. The rate of ice loss is accelerating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_heating">Ocean heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ocean heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">Ocean heat content</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_Heat_Content_(2012).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="381" data-file-width="566" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png/220px-Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png/330px-Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png/440px-Ocean_Heat_Content_%282012%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_Heat_Content_(2012).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ocean heat content (OHC), <a class="external text" href="http://www.nodc.noaa.gov/OC5/3M_HEAT_CONTENT/" rel="nofollow">NOAA</a> 2012</div></div></div>
<p>In terms of heat content, it is the world ocean that dominates the atmospheric climate. The oceans store more than 90% of the heat in Earth's climate system and act as a buffer against the effects of climate change. For instance, an average temperature increase of the entire world ocean by 0.01 °C may seem small, but in fact it represents a very large increase in heat content. If all the heat associated with this anomaly was instantaneously transferred to the entire global atmosphere it would increase the average temperature of the atmosphere by approximately 10<span class=""> </span>°C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Thus, a small change in the mean temperature of the ocean represents a very large change in the total heat content of the climate system. Of course, when the ocean gains heat the water expands and this represents a component of global sea-level rise.
</p><p>The thermal expansion of water increases with temperature and pressure of the water. Hence, cold <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> water will expand less for a given increase in temperature compared to warm tropical water. Because different climate models have slightly different patterns of ocean heating, they do not agree fully on the predictions for the contribution of ocean heating on sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antarctica">Antarctica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Antarctica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1749" data-file-width="2834" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png/220px-Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png/330px-Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png/440px-Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_shelf_ice_hg.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Processes around an Antarctic ice shelf</div></div></div>
<p>The large volume of ice on the Antarctic continent stores around 70% of the world's fresh water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks-28">[28]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Antarctica#Ice_mass_and_global_sea_level" title="Antarctica">Antarctic ice sheet mass balance</a> is affected by snowfall accumulations, and ice discharge along the periphery. Under the influence of global warming, melt at the base of the ice sheet increases. Simultaneously, the capacity of the atmosphere to carry precipitation increases with temperature so that precipitation, in the form of snowfall, increases. Furthermore, the additional snowfall causes increased ice flow which leads to further loss of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Different satellite methods for measuring ice mass and change are in good agreement, and combining methods leads to more certainty how the <a href="/wiki/East_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="East Antarctic Ice Sheet">East Antarctic Ice Sheet</a>, the <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="West Antarctic Ice Sheet">West Antarctic Ice Sheet</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Peninsula" title="Antarctic Peninsula">Antarctic Peninsula</a> evolve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMBIE2012_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMBIE2012-30">[30]</a></sup> A 2018 <a href="/wiki/Systematic_review" title="Systematic review">systematic review</a> study estimated that ice loss across the entire continent was 43 gigatons (Gt) per year on average during the period from 1992 to 2002, but has accelerated to an average of 220 Gt per year during the five years from 2012 to 2017. Most of the melt comes from the West Antarctic Ice Sheet, but the Antarctic Peninsula also positively contributes. The East Antarctic Ice Sheet does not contribute much and scientists are not able to determine whether it gains or loses mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMBIE2018_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMBIE2018-31">[31]</a></sup> The sea-level budget from Antarctica has been estimated to be 0.25 mm (0.0098 in) per year from 1993–2005, and 0.42 mm (0.017 in) per year from 2005 to 2015. All datasets generally show an acceleration of mass loss from the Antarctic ice-sheet, but with year-to-year variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCRP2018_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-WCRP2018-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Antarctica">East Antarctica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: East Antarctica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The world's largest potential source of sea level rise is the East Antarctic Ice Sheet, which holds enough ice to raise global sea levels by 53.3 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Satellite observations suggest the overall mass balance of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet was relatively steady or slightly positive for much of the period from 1992–2017,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> with the notable exception of <a href="/wiki/Totten_Glacier" title="Totten Glacier">Totten Glacier</a>, which has steadily lost mass in recent decades <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChenWilson2008_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChenWilson2008-34">[34]</a></sup> in response to ocean warming<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and possibly a reduction in local sea ice cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Totten Glacier is the primary outlet of the <a href="/wiki/Aurora_Subglacial_Basin" title="Aurora Subglacial Basin">Aurora Subglacial Basin</a>, which together with the <a href="/wiki/Wilkes_Basin" title="Wilkes Basin">Wilkes Basin</a> are the two major ice reservoirs in East Antarctica exposed to potential rapid collapse through <a href="/wiki/Marine_ice_sheet_instability" title="Marine ice sheet instability">marine ice sheet instability</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Antarctica">West Antarctica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: West Antarctica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>West Antarctica is currently experiencing a net outflow of glacial ice, which will increase global sea level over time. A review of the scientific studies looking at data from 1992 to 2017 suggests an increase in the melt from around 53 ± 29 Gt of ice per year to 159 ± 26 Gt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMBIE2018_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMBIE2018-31">[31]</a></sup> Significant acceleration of outflow glaciers in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea_Embayment" title="Amundsen Sea Embayment">Amundsen Sea Embayment</a> may have contributed to this increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RignotBamber2008_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-RignotBamber2008-38">[38]</a></sup> The data showed that the <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a> sector of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet was discharging 250 cubic kilometres (60 cu mi) of ice every year, which was 60% more than the precipitation accumulation in the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">catchment</a> areas. This alone was sufficient to raise the sea level at 0.24 mm (0.0094 in) per year. Further, thinning rates for the glaciers studied in 2002–2003 had increased over the values measured in the early 1990s.
</p><p>Annual temperatures based on <a href="/wiki/Byrd_Station" title="Byrd Station">Byrd Station</a> (central West Antarctica) from 1958 to 2010 increased linear by 2.4 ± 1.2 °C, the study authors note, "West Antarctica as one of the fastest-warming regions globally. In contrast to previous studies, we report statistically significant warming [...] particularly in December–January, the peak of the melting season. A continued rise in summer temperatures could lead to more frequent and extensive episodes of surface melting of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Two types of instability are at play in West Antarctica. The first one is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Ice_Sheet_Instability" title="Marine Ice Sheet Instability">Marine Ice Sheet Instability</a>, the bedrock on which parts of the ice sheet rest is moving deeper inland. This means that when a part of the ice sheet melts, a thicker part of the ice sheet is exposed, which may lead to additional ice loss. Secondly, melting of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelfs</a>, the floating extensions of the ice sheet, leads to a process named the Marine Ice Cliff Instability<i>.</i> Because they function as a <a href="/wiki/Buttress" title="Buttress">buttress</a> to the ice sheet, their melt leads to additional ice flow. Melt of ice shelfs is accelerated when surface melt creates <a href="/wiki/Crevasse" title="Crevasse">crevasses</a> and these crevasses cause fracturing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since most of the <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a> underlying the West Antarctic Ice Sheet lies well below sea level and ocean waters are warming, the ice sheet is becoming less stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollard2015_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard2015-41">[41]</a></sup> A rapid collapse of West Antarctic Ice Sheet could raise sea level by 3.3 metres (11 ft) at an unknown rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamber2009_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber2009-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thwaites_glacier" title="Thwaites glacier">Thwaites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pine_Island_glacier" title="Pine Island glacier">Pine Island</a> glaciers have been identified to be potentially prone to these processes, since both glaciers bedrock <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> gets deeper farther inland, exposing them to more warm water intrusion at the grounding line, and with the continued melt, retreat, eventually raising global sea levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland">Greenland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Greenland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="2000" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg/170px-Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg/255px-Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg/340px-Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenland_ssi_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> 2007 melt anomaly, measured as the difference between the number of days on which melting occurred in 2007 compared to the average annual melting days from 1988–2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Most ice on Greenland is part of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a> which rises to an average of 2.135 kilometres (1.327 mi). The rest of the ice on Greenland is part of isolated glaciers and ice caps.
</p><p>The sources contributing to sea level rise from Greenland are from ice sheet melting (70%) and from glacier calving (30%). Dust, <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>, and microbes and algae living on parts of the ice sheet further enhance melting by darkening its surface and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation#Surface_effects" title="Thermal radiation">thus absorbing more</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a>; these regions grew by 12% between 2000 and 2012, and are likely to expand further. Estimates on future contribution to sea level rise from Greenland range from 0.3 to 3 metres (1 ft 0 in to 9 ft 10 in), for the year 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of Greenland's largest outlet glaciers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakobshavn_Isbr%C3%A6" title="Jakobshavn Isbræ">Jakobshavn Isbræ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kangerlussuaq_Glacier" title="Kangerlussuaq Glacier">Kangerlussuaq Glacier</a> have seen an acceleration in how fast they are flowing into the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> It was shown that this acceleration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outlet_glacier" title="Outlet glacier">outlet glaciers</a> had mostly taken place on the Southern part of Greenland (66 N in 1996), but had spread further north (70 N) in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The contribution of the Greenland ice sheet on sea level over the next couple of centuries can be very high due to a self-reinforcing cycle (a so-called <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>). After an initial period of melting, the height of the ice sheet will have lowered. As air temperature increases closer to the sea surface, more melt starts to occur. This melting may further be accelerated because the color of ice is darker while it is melting. There is a threshold in surface warming beyond which a partial or near-complete melting of the Greenland ice sheet occurs. Different research has put this threshold value as low as 1.0 °C, and definitely 4.0 °C, above pre-industrial temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1170</span></sup>
</p><p>Accounting for the Greenland ice sheet, its peripheral glaciers and ice caps, contributions to current sea level rise have been estimated to be 43%. A study published in 2017, concluded that Greenland’s glaciers and ice caps crossed an irreversible tipping point in 1997, and will continue to melt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Results based on the existing literature found estimates for the Greenland ice sheet and its glaciers and ice caps, they were the largest contributor to the observed sea level rise from <i>land ice</i> sources (excluding thermal expansion), combined accounting for 71 percent, or 1.32 mm per year during the 2012–2016 period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers">Glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">Glacier mass balance</a></div>
<p>Mountain glaciers include only a minor fraction of all water bound up in glaciers ice (&lt;1%), compared to a bigger portion in Greenland and Antarctica (99%). Still, mountain glaciers have contributed appreciably to historical sea level rise and are set to contribute a smaller, but still significant fraction of sea level rise in the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The roughly 200,000 glaciers on earth are spread out across all continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-56">[56]</a></sup> Different glaciers respond differently to increasing temperatures. For instance, valley glaciers that have a shallow slope already retreat under mild warming. Every glacier has a height above which there is net gain in mass and under which the glacier loses mass. If that height changes a bit, this has large consequences for glaciers with a shallow slope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>345</span></sup> A large set of glaciers drain into the ocean and ice loss can therefore increase when ocean temperatures increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Observational and modelling studies of <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">mass loss from glaciers and ice caps</a> indicate a contribution to sea-level rise of 0.2–0.4 mm/yr, averaged over the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Over the 21st century, this is expected to rise, with glaciers contributing 7 to 24 centimetres (3 to 9 in) to global sea levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1165</span></sup> Glaciers contributed around 40% to sea-level rise during the 20th century, with estimates for the 21st century of around 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCRP2018_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-WCRP2018-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_ice">Sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sea ice melt has a very small contribution to global sea level rise. According to <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_principle" title="Archimedes' principle">Archimedes' principle</a>, sea ice that melts does not take up more volume than it had in the form of sea ice or icebergs. However, this only holds true in the case that the salinity of the sea ice and sea water are equal. This assumption is not valid in the case of melting sea ice, where the sea ice contains less salt than sea water. Fresh water has a larger volume compared to salt water, and as such there can be a small contribution of sea ice melt. In the case that all floating <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icebergs" title="Icebergs">icebergs</a> melt, the sea levels would rise only by about 4 cm (1.6 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NoerdlingerOthers2007a_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-NoerdlingerOthers2007a-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_water_storage">Land water storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Land water storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations,-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="599" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg/220px-Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg/330px-Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg/440px-Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations%2C-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trends-in-land-water-storage-from-GRACE-observations,-April-2002-to-November-2014.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trends in land water storage from <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a> observations in gigatons per year, April 2002 to November 2014 (glaciers and ice sheets are excluded).</div></div></div>
<p>Humans impact how much water is stored on land. Building <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dams</a> prevents large masses of water from flowing into the sea and therefore increases the storage of water on land. On the other hand humans extract water from <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">underground reservoirs</a> for <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food production</a> leading to rising seas. Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">hydrological cycle</a> is influenced by climate change and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, which can lead to further positive and negative contributions to sea level rise. In the 20th century, these processes roughly balanced, but dam building has slowed down and is expected to stay low for the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1155</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are broadly two ways of modelling sea level rise and making future <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model#Projections" title="General circulation model">projections</a>. On the one hand, scientists use process-based modelling, where all relevant and well-understood physical processes are included in a physical model. An <a href="/wiki/Ice-sheet_model" title="Ice-sheet model">ice-sheet model</a> is used to calculate the contributions of ice sheets and a <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">general circulation model</a> is used to compute the rising sea temperature and its expansion. A disadvantage of this method is that not all relevant processes might be understood to a sufficient level. Alternatively, some scientist use semi-empirical techniques that use geological data from the past to determine likely sea level responses to a warming world in addition to some basic physical modelling. Semi-empiral modelling relies on sophisticated statistical techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mengel_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mengel-1">[1]</a></sup> This type of modelling was partially motivated by the fact that in the 2007 IPCC report, most physical models underestimated the amount of sea level rise compared to observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) has made predictions of sea level changes to the year 2100, using the available scientific literature. Their projections are based on the contributors to sea level rise, but do exclude some processes that are less understood. In the case of rapid cuts in emission (the so-called RCP2.6 scenario), the IPCC deem it likely that the sea level will rise to 26–55 cm (10–22 in) with a 67% <a href="/wiki/Confidence_interval" title="Confidence interval">confidence interval</a>. The higher value should thus not be read as an upper limit, which can be substantially higher. For a scenario with very high emissions, the IPCC project the sea level to rise to 52–98 cm (20–39 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc13_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc13-10">[10]</a></sup> Compared to the previous IPCC estimate, more sea level rise is expected for similar scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Projections assessed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrcpro_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrcpro-63">[63]</a></sup> suggest possible sea level rise over the 21st century of between 56 and 200 cm (22 and 79 in). The NRC describes the IPCC projections as "conservative".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrcpro_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrcpro-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Rignot" title="Eric Rignot">Rignot</a> and others projected a rise of 32 centimetres (13 in) by 2050. Their projection included increased contributions from the Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets. Use of two completely different approaches reinforced the Rignot projection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romm10Mar2011_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Romm10Mar2011-65">[65]</a></sup> Other estimates suggest that for the same period, global mean sea level could rise by 0.2 to 2.0 m (8 in to 6 ft 7 in), relative to the mean sea level in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parris_sea_level_rise_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-parris_sea_level_rise-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Third <a href="/wiki/National_Climate_Assessment" title="National Climate Assessment">National Climate Assessment</a> (NCA), released May 6, 2014, projected a sea level rise of 1 to 4 feet (0.3 to 1 m) by 2100. Decision makers who are particularly susceptible to risk may wish to use a wider range of scenarios from 20 to 200 cm (8 to 80 in) by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sea_Level_Rise_Key_Message_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sea_Level_Rise_Key_Message-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2016 study concluded that based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIS_5e" title="MIS 5e">past climate change</a> data, sea level rise could accelerate exponentially in the coming decades, with a doubling time of 10, 20 or 40 years, respectively, raising the ocean by several meters, in 50, 100 or 200 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen2016_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen2016-68">[68]</a></sup> However, Greg Holland from the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a>, who reviewed the study, noted: “There is no doubt that the sea level rise, within the IPCC, is a very conservative number, so the truth lies somewhere between IPCC and Jim<i>.</i>”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>One 2017 study's scenario, assuming high <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> use for combustion and strong economic growth during this century, projects sea level rise of up to 1.32 metres (4.3 ft) on average — and an extreme scenario with as much as 1.89 metres (6.2 ft), by 2100. This could mean rapid sea level rise of up to 19 millimeters per year by the end of the century. The study also concluded that the Paris climate agreement emissions scenario, if met, would result in a median 0.52 metres (1.7 ft) of sea level rise by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Estimates of future sea level were also produced in the 20th century. For instance, Hansen et al. 1981, published the study <i>Climate impact of increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide</i>, and predicted that anthropogenic carbon dioxide warming and its potential effects on climate in the 21st century could cause a sea level rise of 5 to 6 m (16 to 20 ft), from melting of the West Antarctic ice-sheet alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="The Hansen paper references a different paper for this statement (https://www.nature.com/articles/271321a0). (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_2100">After 2100</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: After 2100">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_global_warming" title="Long-term effects of global warming">Long-term effects of global warming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="2048" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg/220px-6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg/330px-6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg/440px-6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:6m_Sea_Level_Rise.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the Earth with a long-term 6-metre (20 ft) sea level rise represented in red (uniform distribution, actual sea level rise will vary regionally).</div></div></div>
<p>There is a widespread consensus that substantial long-term sea-level rise will continue for centuries to come even if the temperature stabilizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrccon_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrccon-73">[73]</a></sup> IPCC <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">AR4</a> estimated that at least a partial deglaciation of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a>, and possibly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheet</a>, would occur given a global average temperature increase of 1–4 °C  (relative to temperatures over the years 1990–2000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> This estimate was given about a 50% chance of being correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The estimated timescale was centuries to millennia, and would contribute 4 to 6 metres (13 to 20 ft) or more to sea levels over this period.
</p><p>Melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland ice sheet</a> could contribute an additional 4 to 7.5 m (10 to 20 ft) over many thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2011_long_term_slr-9">[9]</a></sup> It has been estimated that we are already committed to a sea-level rise of approximately 2.3 m (7 ft 7 in) for each degree of temperature rise within the next 2,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Warming beyond the 2 °C target would potentially lead to rates of sea-level rise dominated by ice loss from <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Continued carbon dioxide emissions from fossil fuel sources could cause additional tens of metres of sea level rise, over the next millennia, and ultimately melt the entire Antarctic ice sheet, causing about 58 m (190 ft) of sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>After 500 years, sea-level rise from thermal expansion alone may have reached only half of its eventual level, which models suggest may lie within ranges of 0.5 to 2 m (1 ft 8 in to 6 ft 7 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-solomon-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising sea levels will cause flooding and may erase entire cities. In a study published by <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a>, the entire state of <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> could be completely wiped out by 2500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_sea_level_rise">Regional sea level rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Regional sea level rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">Subsidence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">isostatic rebound</a>, gravitational effects of changing ice masses, and spatially varying patterns of warming lead to differences in sea level rise around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many ports, urban conglomerations, and agricultural regions are built on <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a>, where subsidence of land contributes to a substantially increased <i>effective</i> sea level rise. This is caused by both unsustainable extraction of groundwater (in some places also by extraction of oil and gas), and by <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a> and other flood management practices that prevent accumulation of sediments from compensating for the natural settling of deltaic soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> In many deltas, this results in subsidence ranging from several millimeters per year up to possibly 25 centimeters per year in parts of the Ciliwung delta (<a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Total anthropogenic-caused subsidence in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine-Meuse-Scheldt_delta" title="Rhine-Meuse-Scheldt delta">Rhine-Meuse-Scheldt delta</a> (Netherlands) is estimated at 3 to 4 m (9.8 to 13.1 ft), over 3 m (9.8 ft) in urban areas of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">Mississippi River Delta</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>), and over 9 m (30 ft) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento-San_Joaquin_River_Delta" title="Sacramento-San Joaquin River Delta">Sacramento-San Joaquin River Delta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Atlantic is set to warm at a faster pace than the Pacific. This has consequences for Europe and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._East_Coast" title="U.S. East Coast">U.S. East Coast</a>, which may receive a sea level rise 3–4 times the global average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The downturn of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Atlantic meridional overturning circulation">Atlantic meridional overturning circulation</a> (AMOC) has been also tied to extreme regional sea level rise on the US Northeast Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">Regional effects of global warming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png/220px-Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png/330px-Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png/440px-Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Major_cities_threatened_by_sea_level_rise.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of major cities of the world most vulnerable to sea level rise</div></div></div>
<p>Current and future climate change is set to have a number of impacts, particularly on <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> systems. Such impacts include increased <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>, higher <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm-surge</a> flooding, inhibition of <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a> processes, more extensive coastal inundation, changes in surface <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> and groundwater characteristics, increased loss of property and coastal habitats, increased flood risk and potential loss of life, loss of non-monetary cultural resources and values, impacts on agriculture and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> through decline in soil and water quality, and loss of tourism, recreation, and transportation functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>356</span></sup>
</p><p>Many of these impacts are detrimental — especially for the three-quarters of the world's poor who depend on agriculture systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Owing to the great diversity of coastal environments; regional and local differences in projected relative sea level and climate changes; and differences in the resilience and adaptive capacity of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, sectors, and countries, the impacts will be highly variable in time and space. <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">River deltas</a> and small island states are particularly vulnerable to sea-level rise.
</p><p>Globally tens of millions of people will be displaced in the latter decades of the century if greenhouse gases are not reduced drastically. Many coastal areas have large population growth, which results in more people at risk from sea level rise. The rising seas pose both a direct risk: unprotected homes can be flooded, and indirect threats of higher storm surges, tsunamis and king tides. Asia has the largest population at risk from sea level with countries such as <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> having very densely populated coastal areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The effects of displacement are very dependent on how successful governments will be in implementing defenses against the rising sea, with concerns for the poorerst countries such as sub-Saharan countries and island nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_areas">Coastal areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Coastal areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ten per cent of the world's population live in coastal areas that are less than 10 metres (33 ft) above sea level. Furthermore, two thirds of the world's cities with over five million people are located in these low-lying coastal areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Future sea level rise could lead to potentially catastrophic difficulties for shore-based communities in the next centuries: for example, many major cities such as <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> already need storm-surge defenses, and will need more if the sea level rises; they also face issues such as <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Egyptian city <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> faces a similar situation, where hundreds of thousands people living in the low-lying areas may already have to be relocated in the coming decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-92">[92]</a></sup> However, modest increases in sea level are likely to be offset when cities adapt by constructing sea walls or through relocating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> has been listed as "the number-one most vulnerable city worldwide" in terms of potential damage to property from storm-related flooding and sea-level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS62013_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS62013-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising seas has also been tied to an increased risk from <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a>, potentially affecting coastal cities in the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Food production in coastal areas is affected by rising sea levels as well. Due to flooding and salt water intrusion into the soil, the salinity of agricultural lands near the sea increases, posing problems for crops that are not salt-resistant. Furthermore, salt intrusion in fresh irrigation water poses a second problem for crops that are irrigated. Newly developed salt-resistant crop variants are currently more expensive than the crops they are set to replace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-96">[96]</a></sup> The farmland in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile Delta</a> is affected by salt water flooding<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-92">[92]</a></sup>, and there is now more salt in the soil and irrigation water in the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Delta" title="Red River Delta">Red River Delta</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a> in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-96">[96]</a></sup> Bangladesh and China are affected in a similar way, particularly their rice production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Island_nations">Island nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Island nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Small_Island_States" title="Alliance of Small Island States">Alliance of Small Island States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Small_Island_Developing_States" title="Small Island Developing States">Small Island Developing States</a></div><p><a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">Atolls</a> and low-lying coastal areas on islands are particularly vulnerable to sea level rise. Possible impacts include <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>, floodings and salt intrusion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salinization" title="Soil salinization">into soils</a> and freshwater. It is difficult to assess how much of past erosion and floods have been caused by sea level change, compared to other environmental events such as hurricanes. Adaptation to sea level rise is costly for small island nation as a large portion of their population lives in areas that are at risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TWSeaLevelRise1.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/TWSeaLevelRise1.gif/220px-TWSeaLevelRise1.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/TWSeaLevelRise1.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TWSeaLevelRise1.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic animation of projected sea level rise around <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>, and other low-lying countries are among the areas that are at the highest level of risk. At current rates, sea level would be high enough to make the Maldives uninhabitable by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">Geomorphological</a> events such as storms tend to have larger impacts on reef island than sea level rise, for instance at one of the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>. These effects include the immediate erosion and subsequent regrowth process that may vary in length from decades to centuries, even resulting in land areas larger than pre-storm values. With an expected rise in the frequency and intensity of storms, they may become more significant in determining island shape and size than sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Five of the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> have disappeared due to the combined effects of sea level rise and stronger trade winds that were pushing water into the Western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case all islands of an island nation become uninhabitable or completely submerged by the sea, the states themselves would also become dissolved. Once this happens, all rights on the surrounding area (sea) are removed. This area can be huge as rights extend to a radius of 224 nautical miles (415 km; 258 mi) around the entire island state. Any resources, such as fossil oil, minerals and metals, within this area can be freely dug up by anyone and sold without needing to pay any commission to the (now dissolved) island state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystems">Ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coastal ecosystems are facing drastic changes as a consequence of rising sea levels. Many systems might ultimately be lost when sea levels rise too much or too fast. Some ecosystems can move land inward with the high-water mark, but many are prevented from migrating due to natural or man-made barriers. This 'coastal squeeze' could result in the loss of habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">mudflats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mangroves" title="Mangroves">Mangroves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_marsh" title="Tidal marsh">tidal marshes</a> adjust to rising sea levels by building vertically using accumulated <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>. If sea level rise is too rapid, they will not be able to keep up and will instead be submerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> As both ecosystems protect against storm surges, waves and tsunamis, losing them makes the effects of sea level rise worse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Human activities, such as dam building, may restrict sediment supplies to wetlands, and thereby prevent natural adaptation processes. The loss of some <a href="/wiki/Tidal_marsh" title="Tidal marsh">tidal marshes</a> is unavoidable as a consequence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p><p>When seawater reaches inland, problems related to contaminated soils may occur. Also, fish, birds, and coastal plants could lose parts of their habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> Coral, important for bird and fish life, needs to grow vertically to remain close to the sea surface in order to get enough energy from sunlight. It has so far been able to keep up the vertical growth with the rising seas, but might not be able to do so in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> In 2016, it was reported that the <a href="/wiki/Bramble_Cay_melomys" title="Bramble Cay melomys">Bramble Cay melomys</a>, which lived on a <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> island, had probably become extinct because of inundation due to sea level rises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">Adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level_rise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">Adaptation to global warming</a></div>

<p>Adaptation options to sea level rise can be broadly classified into <i>retreat, accommodate</i> and <i>protect</i>. Retreating is moving people and <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> to less exposed areas and preventing further development in areas that are at risk. This type of adaptation is potentially disruptive, as displacement of people might lead to tensions. Accommodation options are measurements that make societies more flexible to sea level rise. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">cultivation</a> of food crops that tolerate a high salt content in the soil and making new <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">building standards</a> which require building to be built higher and have less damage in the case a flood does occur. Finally, areas can be protected by the construction of dams, dikes and by improving natural defenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>These adaptation options can be further divided into <i>hard</i> and <i>soft</i>. Hard adaptation relies mostly on capital-intensive human-built infrastructure and involves large-scale changes to human societies and ecological systems. Because of its large scale, it is often not flexible. Soft adaptation involves strengthening natural defenses and adaptation strategies in local communities and the use of simple and modular technology, which can be locally owned. The two types of adaptation might be complementary or mutually exclusive. The building of a dike (hard adaptation) for instance destroys the natural dune system and dune nourishment will not be possible anymore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_113-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many countries are developing concrete plans for adaptation. An example is the extension of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Delta_Works" title="Delta Works">Delta Works</a>. In 2008, the Dutch <i>Delta Commission</i>, advised in a report that the Netherlands would need a massive new building program to strengthen the country's water defenses against the anticipated <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> for the next 190 years. This included drawing up worst-case plans for evacuations. The plan also included more than €100 billion (US$113 billion) in new spending through to the year 2100 to implement precautionary measures, such as broadening coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunes" title="Dunes">dunes</a> and strengthening sea and river <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyke_(construction)" title="Dyke (construction)">dikes</a>. The commission said the country must plan for a rise in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> up to 1.3 metres (4 ft 3 in) by 2100 and plan for a 2-4 m rise by 2200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> About a quarter of the Netherlands lies beneath sea level, while more than 50% of the nation's area would be inundated by tidal floods if it did not have an <a href="/wiki/Flood_control_in_the_Netherlands" title="Flood control in the Netherlands">extensive levee system</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="New York City Panel on Climate Change">New York City Panel on Climate Change</a> (NPCC) is an effort to prepare the New York City area for climate change. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_Beach" title="Miami Beach">Miami Beach</a> is spending $500 million from 2015 to 2020 to address sea-level rise. Actions include a pump drainage system, and raising of roadways and sidewalks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> U.S. coastal cities also conduct so called <i>beach nourishment</i>, also known as <i>beach replenishment</i>, where new beach sand is trucked in and added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClimateCentral2012_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClimateCentral2012-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Options that have been proposed to assist island nations to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adapt</a> to rising sea level include abandoning islands, building dikes, and "building upwards."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
