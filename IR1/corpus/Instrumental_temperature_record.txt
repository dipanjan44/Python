<p>The <b>instrumental temperature record</b> provides the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate system</a> from the historical network of <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a> measurements of surface air temperatures and ocean surface temperatures. Data are collected at thousands of meteorological stations, buoys and ships around the globe. The longest-running temperature record is the <a href="/wiki/Central_England_temperature" title="Central England temperature">Central England temperature</a> data series, which starts in 1659. The longest-running quasi-global record starts in 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In recent decades more extensive sampling of ocean temperatures at various depths have begun allowing estimates of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">ocean heat content</a> but these do not form part of the global surface temperature datasets.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_warming_and_trends">Total warming and trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Total warming and trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global average and combined land and ocean surface temperature, show a warming of 0.85 [0.65 to 1.06] °C, in the period 1880 to 2012, based on multiple independently produced datasets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This gives a trend of 0.064 ± 0.015 °C per decade over that period. The trend is faster for land than ocean, faster for Arctic regions, and faster since the 1970s than the longer period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warming_in_the_instrumental_temperature_record">Warming in the instrumental temperature record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Warming in the instrumental temperature record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the observed warming occurred in two periods: around 1900 to around 1940 and around 1970 onwards;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the cooling/plateau from 1940 to 1970 has been mostly attributed to <a href="/wiki/Sulfate#Environmental_effects" title="Sulfate">sulphate aerosol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Some of the temperature variations over this time period may also be due to ocean circulation patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Attribution of the temperature change to natural or anthropogenic (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">human-induced</a>) factors is an important question: see <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">attribution of recent climate change</a>.
</p><p>Land air temperatures are rising faster than sea surface temperatures. Over 1979 to 2012 the trend for land was about 0.254 ± 0.050 °C per decade per CruTemp4 or 0.273 ± 0.047 per GHCN while the trend for sea surface temperatures is about 0.072 ± 0.024 °C per decade per HadISST to 0.124 ± 0.030 °C per decade per HadSST3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>For 1979 to 2012, the linear warming trend for combined land and sea temperatures has been 0.155 °C [0.122 to 0.188 °C] per decade according to AR5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC Fourth Assessment Report found that the instrumental temperature record for the past century included urban heat island effects but that these were primarily local, having a negligible influence on global temperature trends (less than 0.006 °C per decade over land and zero over the oceans).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trenberth_summary_of_observations_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-trenberth_summary_of_observations-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Uncertainties in the temperature record, e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island_effect" title="Urban heat island effect">urban heat island effect</a>, are discussed further in a later <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record#Robustness_of_evidence" title="Instrumental temperature record">section</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Warmest_periods">Warmest periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Warmest periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warmest_years">Warmest years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Warmest years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1033" data-file-width="1422" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/220px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/330px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/440px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> graph of global annual temperature anomalies, 1950–2012</div></div></div>
<p>In January 2017, several scientific agencies around the world, including <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-NOAA2016_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-NOAA2016-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_2017_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_2017-12">[12]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> in the United Kingdom, named 2016 the warmest year recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetOfficePR_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetOfficePR-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2017_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_2017-14">[14]</a></sup>  This marked the third consecutive year reaching a new record temperature, the first time since the current warming trend began in the 1970s that three years in a row were record highs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_2017_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_2017-12">[12]</a></sup>  2016's record meant that 16 of the 17 warmest years have occurred since 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_2017_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt_2017-12">[12]</a></sup> 2017 being the third hottest year on record meant that 17 of the last 18 warmest years have occurred since 2000.
</p><p>While record-breaking years can attract considerable public interest, individual years are less significant than the overall trend. Some climatologists have criticized the attention that the popular press gives to "warmest year" statistics; for example, <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Schmidt" title="Gavin Schmidt">Gavin Schmidt</a> stated "the long-term trends or the expected sequence of records are far more important than whether any single year is a record or not."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gavin_Schmidt_201501_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gavin_Schmidt_201501-15">[15]</a></sup>  Of the 2015 and 2016 records, Schmidt stated that the <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9316_El_Ni%C3%B1o_event" title="2014–16 El Niño event">2014–16 El Niño event</a> was "a factor ... but both 2015 and 2016 would have been records even without it"; he attributed about 90% of the warming in 2016 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Consistent with Schmidt's comment, the NASA / NOAA announcement stated that "[g]lobally-averaged temperatures in 2016 were 1.78 degrees Fahrenheit (0.99 degrees Celsius) warmer than the mid-20th century mean" and that the impact of El Niño warming was estimated to have "increased the annual global temperature anomaly for 2016 by 0.2 degrees Fahrenheit (0.12 degrees Celsius)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-NOAA2016_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-NOAA2016-11">[11]</a></sup>  Comments from climate scientists reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> showed strong agreement in attributing the warming primarily to anthropogenic climate change, with some contribution from El Niño warming, though there were differing views on the significance of individual records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Deke_Arndt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Deke Arndt (page does not exist)">Deke Arndt</a> leads the monitoring group at NOAA's <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Information" title="National Centers for Environmental Information">National Centers for Environmental Information</a> and offered an analogy in a report on <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a>: "The long-term warming is a lot like riding up an escalator over time. The longer you're on the escalator, the higher you go. And the El Niño phenomenon is like jumping up and down while you're on the escalator."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  Arndt also stated that the "long-term warming is driven almost entirely by greenhouse gases."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Stott" title="Peter Stott">Peter Stott</a>, acting director of the Met Office, noted the influence of the El Niño event on 2016 temperatures but also stated that "the main contributor to warming over the last 150 years is human influence on climate from increasing greenhouse gases in the atmosphere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetOfficePR_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetOfficePR-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_2017_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_2017-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tim_Osborn" title="Tim Osborn">Tim Osborn</a>, Director of Research at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_East_Anglia" title="University of East Anglia">University of East Anglia</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Climatic_Research_Unit" title="Climatic Research Unit">Climatic Research Unit</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> agreed stating that "[m]ultiple lines of independent evidence confirm that the planet has warmed over the last 150 years: warmer oceans, warmer land, warmer lower atmosphere and melting ice. This long-term trend is the main cause for the record warmth of 2015 and 2016, surpassing all previous years – even ones with strong El Niño events."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetOfficePR_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-MetOfficePR-13">[13]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_global_temperature">Influences on global temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences on global temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="510" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png/300px-Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png/450px-Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/af/Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Short_Instrumental_Temperature_Record.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global surface temperature change for the period 1980–2004. The blue line is the monthly average, the black line is the annual average and the red line is the 5-year running average. Data source: <a class="external free" href="http://www.cru.uea.ac.uk/" rel="nofollow">http://www.cru.uea.ac.uk/</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gases</a> trap outgoing radiation warming the atmosphere which in turn warms the land.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> generally tends to increase global temperatures. <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a>, on the other hand, usually causes years which are cooler than the short-term average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-27">[27]</a></sup> El Niño is the warm phase of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) and La Niña the cold phase.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">Aerosols</a> diffuse incoming radiation generally cooling the planet. Volcanoes are the largest source but there are also anthropogenic sources. There are several other effects such as clouds. Some aerosols like carbon black have warming effects.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">Land use</a> change like deforestation can increase greenhouse gases through burning <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">Albedo</a> can also be changed.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">Incoming solar radiation</a> varies very slightly, with the main variation controlled by the approximately 11-year <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar magnetic activity cycle</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolute_temperatures_v._anomalies">Absolute temperatures v. anomalies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Absolute temperatures v. anomalies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Records of global average surface temperature are usually presented as <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anomaly" title="wikt:anomaly">anomalies</a> rather than as absolute temperatures. A temperature anomaly is measured against a reference value or long-term average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> For example, if the reference value is 15 °C, and the measured temperature is 17 °C, then the temperature anomaly is +2 °C (i.e., 17 °C −15 °C).
</p><p>Temperature anomalies are useful for deriving average surface temperatures because they tend to be highly correlated over large distances (of the order of 1000 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In other words, anomalies are representative of temperature changes over large areas and distances. By comparison, absolute temperatures vary markedly over even short distances.
</p><p>The Earth's average surface absolute temperature for the 1961–1990 period has been derived by spatial interpolation of average observed near-surface air temperatures from over the land, oceans and sea ice regions, with a best estimate of 14 °C (57.2 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jonesreview_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-jonesreview-30">[30]</a></sup> The estimate is uncertain, but probably lies within 0.5 °C of the true value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jonesreview_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-jonesreview-30">[30]</a></sup> Given the difference in uncertainties between this absolute value and any annual anomaly, it's not valid to add them together to imply a precise absolute value for a specific year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_record_from_1850">Global record from 1850</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Global record from 1850">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The period for which reasonably reliable instrumental records of near-surface temperature exist with quasi-global coverage is generally considered to begin around 1850. Earlier records exist, but with sparser coverage and less standardized instrumentation.
</p><p>The temperature data for the record come from measurements from land stations and ships. On land, temperature sensors are kept in a <a href="/wiki/Stevenson_screen" title="Stevenson screen">Stevenson screen</a> or a <a href="/wiki/MMTS_(meteorology)" title="MMTS (meteorology)">maximum minimum temperature system</a> (MMTS). The sea record consists of surface ships taking sea temperature measurements from engine inlets or buckets. The land and marine records can be compared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Land and sea measurement and instrument calibration is the responsibility of <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization#Current_member_states" title="World Meteorological Organization">national meteorological services</a>. Standardization of methods is organized through the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (and formerly through its predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/International_Meteorological_Organization" title="International Meteorological Organization">International Meteorological Organization</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOSGuide_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOSGuide-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most meteorological observations are taken for use in weather forecasts. Centers such as <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">ECMWF</a> show <a class="external text" href="http://www.ecmwf.int/products/forecasts/d/overview/monitoring/coverage/dcover!ssmi!00!pop!od!oper!w_coverage!latest!obs/" rel="nofollow">instantaneous map of their coverage</a>; or the Hadley Centre show the coverage for the <a class="external text" href="http://www.meto.gov.uk/research/hadleycentre/CR_data/Annual/HadCRUTanm_2000.gif" rel="nofollow">average of the year 2000</a>. Coverage for earlier in the 20th and 19th centuries would be significantly less. While temperature changes vary both in size and direction from one location to another, the numbers from different locations are combined to produce an estimate of a global average change.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Robustness_of_evidence">Robustness of evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Robustness of evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific consensus that climate is changing</a> and that greenhouse gases emitted by human activities are the primary driver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joint_statement_on_climate_change_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-joint_statement_on_climate_change-34">[34]</a></sup> The scientific consensus is reflected, for example, by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC), an international body which summarizes existing science, and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joint_statement_on_climate_change_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-joint_statement_on_climate_change-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The methods used to derive the principal estimates of global surface temperature trends — HadCRUT3, NOAA and NASA/GISS — are largely independent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_reports_and_assessments">Other reports and assessments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other reports and assessments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_(NCADAC).png"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1174" data-file-width="1561" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png/350px-Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png/525px-Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png/700px-Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_%28NCADAC%29.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Global_warming._Short-term_variations_versus_a_long-term_trend_(NCADAC).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This graph shows how short-term variations occur in the global temperature record. Image source: NCADAC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">U.S. National Academy of Sciences</a>, both in its 2002 report to President George W. Bush, and in later publications, has strongly endorsed evidence of an average global temperature increase in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NA_brief_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NA_brief-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The preliminary results of an assessment carried out by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Earth_Surface_Temperature" title="Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature">Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature</a> group and made public in October 2011, found that over the past 50 years the land surface warmed by 0.911 °C, and their results mirrors those obtained from earlier studies carried out by the NOAA, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre" title="Hadley Centre">Hadley Centre</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s GISS.  The study addressed concerns raised by "skeptics"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily1011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily1011-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> including urban heat island effect, "poor"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily1011_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily1011-37">[37]</a></sup> station quality, and the "issue of data selection bias"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily1011_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily1011-37">[37]</a></sup> and found that these effects did not bias the results obtained from these earlier studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDaily1011_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-SciDaily1011-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian1011_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian1011-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcBlack_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcBlack-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Econ1011_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Econ1011-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_climate_variability_and_global_warming">Internal climate variability and global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Internal climate variability and global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#Internal_climate_variability_and_global_warming" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change § Internal climate variability and global warming</a></div>
<p>One of the issues that has been raised in the media is the view that global warming "stopped in 1998".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p1_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p1-43">[43]</a></sup>
This view ignores the presence of internal climate variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p1_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p1-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met_office_internal_variability_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-met_office_internal_variability-44">[44]</a></sup> Internal climate variability is a result of complex interactions between components of the climate system, such as the <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(physics)" title="Coupling (physics)">coupling</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> An example of internal climate variability is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p1_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p1-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met_office_internal_variability_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-met_office_internal_variability-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_El_Ni%C3%B1o_event" title="1997–98 El Niño event">El Niño in 1998</a> was particularly strong, possibly one of the strongest of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p1_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p1-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cooling between 2006 and 2008, for instance, was likely driven by La Niña, the opposite of El Niño conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p3_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p3-46">[46]</a></sup> The area of cooler-than-average sea surface temperatures that defines La Niña conditions can push global temperatures downward, if the phenomenon is strong enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p3_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p3-46">[46]</a></sup> Even accounting for the presence of internal climate variability, recent years rank among the warmest on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott_variability_p2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-scott_variability_p2-47">[47]</a></sup> For example, every year of the 2000s was warmer than the 1990 average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-State2009p5_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-State2009p5-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_temperature">Regional temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Regional temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NCDC_temperature_trend.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="991" data-file-width="2005" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/NCDC_temperature_trend.png/300px-NCDC_temperature_trend.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/NCDC_temperature_trend.png/450px-NCDC_temperature_trend.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/NCDC_temperature_trend.png/600px-NCDC_temperature_trend.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NCDC_temperature_trend.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1901–2011 global temperature trend</div></div></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:308px;max-width:308px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_Land.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1000" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/NOAA_Land.svg/150px-NOAA_Land.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/NOAA_Land.svg/225px-NOAA_Land.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/NOAA_Land.svg/300px-NOAA_Land.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Global land temperature anomaly, 1880–2012</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_Ocean.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1000" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/NOAA_Ocean.svg/150px-NOAA_Ocean.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/NOAA_Ocean.svg/225px-NOAA_Ocean.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/NOAA_Ocean.svg/300px-NOAA_Ocean.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Global ocean temperature anomaly, 1880–2012</div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>Temperature trends from 1901 are positive over most of the world's surface except for Atlantic Ocean south of Greenland, the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">southeastern United States</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. Warming is strongest over inland areas in Asia and North America as well as south-eastern Brazil and some areas in the South Atlantic and Indian oceans.
</p><p>Since 1979 temperature increase is considerably stronger over land while cooling has been observed over some oceanic regions in the Pacific Ocean and Southern Hemisphere; the spatial pattern of ocean temperature trend in those regions is possibly related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Decadal_Oscillation" title="Pacific Decadal Oscillation">Pacific Decadal Oscillation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_oscillation" title="Antarctic oscillation">Southern Annular Mode</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Seasonal temperature trends are positive over most of the globe but weak cooling is observed over the mid latitudes of the southern ocean but also over eastern Canada in spring because of strengthening of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a>. Warming is stronger over northern Europe, China and North America in winter, Europe and Asia interior in spring, Europe and north Africa in summer and northern North America, Greenland and eastern Asia in autumn.
Enhanced warming over north Eurasia is partly linked to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Northern Annular Mode</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polyakov_2003_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polyakov_2003-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu_2007_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu_2007-50">[50]</a></sup> while in the southern hemisphere the trend toward stronger westerlies over the Southern ocean favoured a cooling over much of Antarctica with the exception of the Antarctic Peninsula where strong westerlies decrease cold air outbreaks from the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThompsonSolomon_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ThompsonSolomon-51">[51]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Peninsula" title="Antarctic Peninsula">Antarctic Peninsula</a> has warmed by 2.5 °C (4.5 °F) in the past five decades at Bellingshausen Station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_temperature_records">Satellite temperature records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Satellite temperature records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">Satellite temperature measurements</a></div>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:304px;max-width:304px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:302px;max-width:302px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Satellite_Temperatures.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="1590" data-file-width="2400" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Satellite_Temperatures.png/300px-Satellite_Temperatures.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Satellite_Temperatures.png/450px-Satellite_Temperatures.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Satellite_Temperatures.png/600px-Satellite_Temperatures.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Comparison of ground-based (blue) and satellite-based (red: <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama_in_Huntsville" title="University of Alabama in Huntsville">University of Alabama in Huntsville</a>; green: RSS) records of global surface temperature change from 1979–2009. Linear trends plotted since 1982.</div></div></div></div>
<p>The most recent climate model simulations give a range of results for changes in global-average temperature. Some models show more warming in the troposphere than at the surface, while a slightly smaller number of simulations show the opposite behaviour. There is no fundamental inconsistency among these model results and observations at the global scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>The satellite records used to show much smaller warming trends for the troposphere which were considered to disagree with model prediction; however, following revisions to the satellite records, the trends are now similar.
</p><p>The IPCC fifth assessment report concluded "assessment of the large body of studies comparing various long-term radiosonde and MSU products since AR4 is hampered by data set version changes, and inherent data uncertainties. These factors substantially limit the ability to draw robust and consistent inferences from such studies about the true long-term trends or the value of different data products".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> Cooperative Observer Program has established minimum standards regarding the instrumentation, siting, and reporting of surface temperature stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The observing systems available are able to detect year-to-year temperature variations such as those caused by El Niño or volcanic eruptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCSP_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-CCSP-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect is very small, estimated to account for less than <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273152000000000♠</span>0.002 °C</span> of warming per decade since 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brooks investigated Historical Climate Network (USHCN) sites in Indiana in 2005, and assigned 16% of the sites an ‘excellent’ rating, 59% a ‘good’ rating, 12.5% a ‘fair’ rating, and 12.5% ‘poor’ rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> A 2006 study analyzed 366 U.S. surface stations; results indicate relatively few significant temperature trends, and these are generally evenly divided between warming and cooling trends. 95% of the stations displayed a warming trend after land use/land cover changes took place, and the authors noted "this does not necessarily imply that the changes are the causative factor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Another study that same year documented examples of well and poorly sited monitoring stations in the United States, including ones near buildings, roadways, and air conditioning exhausts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another study concluded in 2006, that existing empirical techniques for validating the local and regional consistency of temperature data are adequate to identify and remove biases from station records, and that such corrections allow information about long-term trends to be preserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> A study in 2013 also found that urban bias can be accounted for, and when all available station data is divided into rural and urban, that both temperature sets are broadly consistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_surface_and_ocean_datasets">Global surface and ocean datasets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Global surface and ocean datasets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="595" data-file-width="900" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png/300px-GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png/450px-GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png/600px-GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHCN_Temperature_Stations.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the land-based long-term monitoring stations included in the <a href="/wiki/Global_Historical_Climatology_Network" title="Global Historical Climatology Network">Global Historical Climatology Network</a>. Colors indicate the length of the temperature record available at each site.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Temperature_measurement" title="Temperature measurement">Temperature measurement</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) maintains the <a href="/wiki/Global_Historical_Climatology_Network" title="Global Historical Climatology Network">Global Historical Climatology Network</a> (GHCN-Monthly) data base containing historical temperature, precipitation, and pressure data for thousands of land stations worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GHCN_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-GHCN-63">[63]</a></sup> Also, NOAA's <a href="/wiki/National_Climatic_Data_Center" title="National Climatic Data Center">National Climatic Data Center</a> (NCDC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> of surface temperature measurements maintains a global temperature record since 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/HadCRUT" title="HadCRUT">HadCRUT</a>, a collaboration between the <a href="/wiki/University_of_East_Anglia" title="University of East Anglia">University of East Anglia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Climatic_Research_Unit" title="Climatic Research Unit">Climatic Research Unit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a>
</p><p>NASA's <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Institute_for_Space_Studies" title="Goddard Institute for Space Studies">Goddard Institute for Space Studies</a> maintains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GISTEMP" title="GISTEMP">GISTEMP</a>.
</p><p>More recently the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Earth_Surface_Temperature" title="Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature">Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature</a> dataset. These datasets are updated frequently, and are generally in close agreement.
