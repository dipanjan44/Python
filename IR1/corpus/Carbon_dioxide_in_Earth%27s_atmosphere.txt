<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) is an important <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">trace gas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>. It is an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, a biogeochemical cycle in which <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is exchanged between the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>, soil, rocks and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Plants</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoautotroph" title="Photoautotroph">photoautotrophs</a> use solar energy to produce carbohydrate from atmospheric carbon dioxide and water by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. Almost all other organisms depend on carbohydrate derived from photosynthesis as their primary source of energy and carbon compounds.  CO<sub>2</sub> absorbs and emits <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation at <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of 4.26 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a> (asymmetric stretching <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">vibrational mode</a>) and 14.99 µm (bending vibrational mode) and consequently is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> that plays a vital role in regulating <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface temperature through the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere were as high as 4,000  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> (ppm) during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian period </a> about 500 million years ago to as low as 180 ppm during the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a> of the last two million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge2013_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge2013-2">[2]</a></sup> Estimates based on reconstructed temperature records suggests that the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> during the last 420 million years ago was with ~2000 ppm highest during the <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> (∼400 Myrs ago) and <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> (220–200 Myrs ago), with a few maximum estimates ranging up to ∼3,700±1,600 ppm (215 Myrs ago).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Global annual mean CO<sub>2</sub> concentration has increased by more than 45% since the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, from 280 ppm during the 10,000 years up to the mid-18th century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge2013_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge2013-2">[2]</a></sup> to 410 ppm as of mid-2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_CO2annual_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_CO2annual-5">[5]</a></sup> The present concentration is the highest in the last 800,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deep_ice_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-deep_ice-6">[6]</a></sup> and possibly even the last 20 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grida_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grida-7">[7]</a></sup> The increase has been caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_greenhouse_gases" title="Anthropogenic greenhouse gases">human activities</a>, particularly the burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etheridge1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Etheridge1996-8">[8]</a></sup> This increase of CO<sub>2</sub> and other long-lived greenhouse gases in Earth's atmosphere has produced the current episode of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.  About 30–40% of the CO<sub>2</sub> released by humans into the atmosphere dissolves into oceans, rivers and lakes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millero95_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-millero95-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feely04_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feely04-10">[10]</a></sup> which has produced <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_concentration">Current concentration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current concentration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">Atmospheric methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holocene#Climate" title="Holocene">Holocene climate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quaternary#Climate" title="Quaternary">Quaternary climate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="600" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png/250px-Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png/375px-Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png/500px-Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Dioxide_400kyr.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations over the last 400,000 years</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="212" data-file-width="391" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg/250px-Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg/375px-Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Future-climate-forcing-potentially-without-precedent-in-the-last-420-million-years.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mosaic sample of reconstructed CO2 concentrations during the past 420 million years, visit <a class="external text" href="https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms14845/figures/4" rel="nofollow">Nature</a> for full graph.</div></div></div>
<p>Over the past 400,000 years, CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations have shown several cycles of variation from about 180 parts per million during the deep glaciations of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> to 280 parts per million during the interglacial periods. Following the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration has increased to over 400 parts per million and continues to increase,  causing the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> 
The daily average concentration of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> at <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a> first exceeded 400 ppm on 10 May 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-13">[13]</a></sup> although this concentration had already been reached in the Arctic in June 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It currently constitutes about 0.041% by volume of the atmosphere, (equal to 410 ppm) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carbon_Trends_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carbon_Trends-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which corresponds to approximately 3200 gigatons of CO<sub>2</sub>, containing approximately 870 gigatons of carbon. Each part per million by volume of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere thus represents approximately 2.13 gigatonnes of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The global mean CO<sub>2</sub> concentration is currently rising at a rate of approximately 2 ppm/year and accelerating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carbon_Trends_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carbon_Trends-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carbon_budget_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-carbon_budget-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atmospheric_Carbon_Dioxide_Growth_Rate_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atmospheric_Carbon_Dioxide_Growth_Rate-22">[22]</a></sup> There is an annual fluctuation of about 3–9 ppm which is negatively correlated with the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>'s growing season. The Northern Hemisphere dominates the annual cycle of CO<sub>2</sub> concentration because it has much greater land area and plant biomass than the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. Concentrations reach a peak in May as the Northern Hemisphere spring greenup begins, and decline to a minimum in October, near the end of the growing season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atmospheric_Carbon_Dioxide_Growth_Rate_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atmospheric_Carbon_Dioxide_Growth_Rate-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since global warming is attributed to increasing atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases such as CO<sub>2</sub>, scientists closely monitor atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations and their impact on the present-day biosphere.  The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> wrote that the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere is this high "for the first time in 55 years of measurement—and probably more than 3 million years of Earth history."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The current concentration may be the highest in the last 20 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grida_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grida-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  April 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the average monthly level of CO2 in Earth's atmosphere exceeded 410 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_concentration">Past concentration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Past concentration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a></div>

<p>Carbon dioxide concentrations have varied widely over the Earth's 4.54 billion year history. Carbon dioxide is believed to have been present in Earth's first atmosphere, shortly after Earth's formation. Earth's second atmosphere emerged after the lighter gases, hydrogen and helium, escaped to space or like oxygen were bound up in molecules and is thought to have consisted largely of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and inert gases<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> produced by outgassing from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, supplemented by gases<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> produced during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_heavy_bombardment" title="Late heavy bombardment">late heavy bombardment</a> of Earth by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">asteroids</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The production of free oxygen by <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacterial</a> photosynthesis eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">oxygen catastrophe</a> that ended Earth's second atmosphere and brought about the Earth's third atmosphere (the modern atmosphere) 2.4 billion years before the present. Carbon dioxide concentrations dropped from 4,000 parts per million during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian period </a> about 500 million years ago to as low as 180 parts per million during the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a> of the last two million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge2013_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge2013-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drivers_of_ancient-Earth_carbon_dioxide_concentration">Drivers of ancient-Earth carbon dioxide concentration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drivers of ancient-Earth carbon dioxide concentration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">Biogeochemical cycle</a></div>
<p>On long timescales, atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration is determined by the balance among <a href="/wiki/Geochemical_cycle" title="Geochemical cycle">geochemical processes</a> including organic carbon burial in sediments, silicate rock <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>. The net effect of slight imbalances in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> over tens to hundreds of millions of years has been to reduce atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. On a timescale of billions of years, such downward trend appears bound to continue indefinitely as occasional massive historical releases of buried carbon due to volcanism will become less frequent (as earth mantle cooling and progressive exhaustion of  <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">internal radioactive heat</a> proceed further). The rates of these processes are extremely slow; hence they are of no relevance to the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration over the next hundreds or thousands of years.
</p><p>In billion-year timescales, it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_of_the_Earth" title="Future of the Earth">predicted</a> that plant, and therefore animal, life on land will die off altogether, since by that time most of the remaining carbon in the atmosphere will be sequestered underground, and natural releases of CO<sub>2</sub> by radioactivity-driven tectonic activity will have continued to slow down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The loss of plant life would also result in the eventual loss of oxygen. Some microbes are capable of photosynthesis at concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> of a few parts per million and so the last life forms would probably disappear finally due to the rising temperatures and loss of the atmosphere when the <a href="/wiki/Sun#Earth's_fate" title="Sun">sun</a> becomes a red giant some four billion years from now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_ancient-Earth_carbon_dioxide_concentration">Measuring ancient-Earth carbon dioxide concentration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measuring ancient-Earth carbon dioxide concentration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">climate reconstruction proxies</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_Petit_data.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/250px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/375px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/500px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_Petit_data.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graph of CO<sub>2</sub> (green), reconstructed temperature (blue) and dust (red) from the Vostok ice core for the past 420,000 years</div></div></div>
<p>The most direct method for measuring atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations for periods before instrumental sampling is to measure bubbles of air (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_inclusions" title="Fluid inclusions">fluid or gas inclusions</a>) trapped in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> ice sheets. The most widely accepted of such studies come from a variety of Antarctic cores and indicate that atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations were about 260–280 ppmv immediately before industrial emissions began and did not vary much from this level during the preceding 10,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annum" title="Annum">years</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The longest <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> record comes from East Antarctica, where ice has been sampled to an age of 800,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deep_ice_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-deep_ice-6">[6]</a></sup> During this time, the atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration has varied between 180–210 ppm during <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>, increasing to 280–300 ppm during warmer <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The beginning of human agriculture during the current <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> epoch may have been strongly connected to the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> increase after the last ice age ended, a <a href="/wiki/CO2_fertilization_effect" title="CO2 fertilization effect">fertilization effect</a> raising plant biomass growth and reducing <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomatal</a> conductance requirements for CO<sub>2</sub> intake, consequently reducing transpiration water losses and increasing water usage efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Various <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy measurements</a> have been used to attempt to determine atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations millions of years in the past. These include <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> ratios in certain types of marine sediments, and the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> observed on fossil plant leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is evidence for high CO<sub>2</sub> volume concentrations between 200 and 150 million years ago of over 3,000 ppm, and between 600 and 400 million years ago of over 6,000 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grida_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grida-7">[7]</a></sup> In more recent times, atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration continued to fall after about 60 million years ago. About 34 million years ago, the time of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene%E2%80%93Oligocene_extinction_event" title="Eocene–Oligocene extinction event">Eocene–Oligocene extinction event</a> and when the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheet</a> started to take its current form, CO<sub>2</sub> is found to have been about 760 ppm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and there is geochemical evidence that concentrations were less than 300 ppm by about 20 million years ago. Carbon dioxide decrease, with a tipping point of 600 ppm, was the primary agent forcing Antarctic glaciation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Low CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations may have been the stimulus that favored the evolution of <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4</a> plants, which increased greatly in abundance between 7 and 5 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194-32">[32]</a></sup> Based on an analysis of fossil leaves, Wagner et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> argued that atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations during the last 7,000–10,000 year period were significantly higher than 300 ppm and contained substantial variations that may be correlated to climate variations. Others have disputed such claims, suggesting they are more likely to reflect calibration problems than actual changes in CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Relevant to this dispute is the observation that Greenland ice cores often report higher and more variable CO<sub>2</sub> values than similar measurements in Antarctica. However, the groups responsible for such measurements (e.g. H. J Smith et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>) believe the variations in Greenland cores result from <i>in situ</i> decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> dust found in the ice. When dust concentrations in Greenland cores are low, as they nearly always are in Antarctic cores, the researchers report good agreement between measurements of Antarctic and Greenland CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide_and_the_greenhouse_effect">Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the greenhouse effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the greenhouse effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">Faint young Sun paradox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_green_house_effect.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="834" data-file-width="1198" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/The_green_house_effect.svg/250px-The_green_house_effect.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/The_green_house_effect.svg/375px-The_green_house_effect.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/The_green_house_effect.svg/500px-The_green_house_effect.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_green_house_effect.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pictogram of the greenhouse effect</div></div></div>
<p>Earth’s natural <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> makes life as we know it possible and carbon dioxide plays a significant role in providing for the relatively warm temperature that the planet enjoys. The greenhouse effect is a process by which thermal radiation from a planetary atmosphere warms the planet's surface beyond the temperature it would have in the absence of its atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccar4syr_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipccar4syr-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc-AR4WG1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-AR4WG1-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Without the greenhouse effect, the Earth's temperature would be about −18 °C (-0.4 °F) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC4_ch01_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC4_ch01-42">[42]</a></sup> compared to Earth's actual surface temperature of approximately 14 °C (57.2 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide is believed to have played an important effect in regulating Earth's temperature throughout its 4.7 billion year history. Early in the Earth's life, scientists have found evidence of liquid water indicating a warm world even though the Sun's output is believed to have only been 70% of what it is today. It has been suggested by scientists that higher carbon dioxide concentrations in the early Earth's atmosphere might help explain this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faint_young_sun_paradox" title="Faint young sun paradox">faint young sun paradox</a>. When Earth first formed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> may have contained more greenhouse gases and CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations may have been higher, with estimated <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> as large as 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> (10 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>), because there was no bacterial <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduce</a> the gas to carbon compounds and oxygen. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>, a very active greenhouse gas which reacts with oxygen to produce CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapor, may have been more prevalent as well, with a mixing ratio of 10<sup>−4</sup> (100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> by volume).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pavlov_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlov-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="600" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/250px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/375px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/500px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contribution of natural factors and human activities to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> (RF) of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_radiative_forcing_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_radiative_forcing-46">[46]</a></sup> RF values are for year 2005, relative to pre-industrial (1750).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_radiative_forcing_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_radiative_forcing-46">[46]</a></sup> The contribution of solar irradiance to RF is 5% the value of the combined RF due to increases in the atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_solar_activity_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_solar_activity-47">[47]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Though water is responsible for most (about 36-70%) of the total greenhouse effect, the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Role_of_water_vapor" title="Greenhouse gas">role of water vapor</a> as a greenhouse gas depends on temperature. On Earth, carbon dioxide is the most relevant, direct anthropologically influenced greenhouse gas. Carbon dioxide is often mentioned in the context of its increased influence as a greenhouse gas since the pre-industrial (1750) era. In the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> the increase in CO<sub>2</sub> was estimated to be responsible for 1.82 W·m<sup>2</sup> of the 2.63 W·m<sup>2</sup> change in radiative forcing on Earth (about 70%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> increasing ground temperature was first published by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius#Greenhouse_effect" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> in 1896.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arrhenius_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arrhenius-49">[49]</a></sup> The increased radiative forcing due to increased CO<sub>2</sub> in the Earth's atmosphere is based on the physical properties of CO<sub>2</sub> and the non-saturated absorption windows where CO<sub>2</sub> absorbs outgoing long-wave energy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide_and_the_carbon_cycle">Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">Carbon cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_cycle" title="Atmospheric carbon cycle">Atmospheric carbon cycle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2192" data-file-width="2600" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/250px-Carbon_cycle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/375px-Carbon_cycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/500px-Carbon_cycle.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This diagram of the fast carbon cycle shows the movement of carbon between land, atmosphere, and oceans in billions of tons of carbon per year. Yellow numbers are natural fluxes, red are human contributions in billions of tons of carbon per year. White numbers indicate stored carbon.</div></div></div>
<p>Atmospheric carbon dioxide plays an integral role in the Earth's carbon cycle whereby carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere by some natural processes such as <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and deposition of carbonates, to form limestones for example, and added back to the atmosphere by other natural processes such as <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a> and the acid dissolution of carbonate deposits. There are two broad carbon cycles on Earth: the fast carbon cycle and the slow carbon cycle. The fast carbon cycle refers to movements of carbon between the environment and living things in the biosphere whereas the slow carbon cycle involves the movement of carbon between the atmosphere, oceans, soil, rocks, and volcanism. Both carbon cycles are intrinsically interconnected and atmospheric gaseous carbon dioxide facilitates the carbon cycle.
</p><p>Natural sources of atmospheric carbon dioxide include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic matter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildfires" title="Wildfires">wildfires</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a> processes of living <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic organisms</a>. Man-made sources of carbon dioxide include the burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> for heating, <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">power generation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, as well as some industrial processes such as cement making. It is also produced by various <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> convert carbon dioxide to <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> by a process called <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. They gain the energy needed for this reaction from absorption of sunlight by <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> and other pigments. Oxygen, produced as a by-product of photosynthesis, is released into the atmosphere and subsequently used for respiration by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophic" title="Heterotrophic">heterotrophic</a> organisms and other plants, forming a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">cycle</a>.
</p><p>Most sources of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions are natural, and are balanced to various degrees by natural CO<sub>2</sub> sinks. For example, the natural decay of organic material in forests and grasslands and the action of forest fires results in the release of about 439 gigatonnes of carbon dioxide every year, while new growth entirely counteracts this effect, absorbing 450 gigatonnes per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Although the initial carbon dioxide in the atmosphere of the young Earth was produced by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a>, modern volcanic activity releases only 130 to 230 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">megatonnes</a> of carbon dioxide each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> These natural sources are nearly balanced by natural sinks, physical and biological processes which remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. For example, some is directly removed from the atmosphere by land plants for <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and it is soluble in water forming <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>. There is a large natural flux of CO<sub>2</sub> into and out of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In the pre-industrial era these fluxes were largely in balance. Currently about 57% of human-emitted CO<sub>2</sub> is removed by the biosphere and oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RERLCO2_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-RERLCO2-54">[54]</a></sup> From pre-industrial era to 2010, the terrestrial biosphere represented a net source of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> prior to 1940, switching subsequently to a net sink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RERLCO2_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-RERLCO2-54">[54]</a></sup> The ratio of the increase in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> to emitted CO<sub>2</sub> is known as the <i>airborne fraction</i> (Keeling et al., 1995); this varies for short-term averages and is typically about 45% over longer (5 year) periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RERLCO2_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-RERLCO2-54">[54]</a></sup> Estimated carbon in global terrestrial vegetation increased from approximately 740 billion tons in 1910 to 780 billion tons in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>

<p>Carbon dioxide in the Earth's atmosphere is essential to life and to the present planetary biosphere. Over the course of Earth's geologic history CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations have played a role in biological evolution. The first photosynthetic organisms probably <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> early in the <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_history_of_life" title="Evolutionary history of life">evolutionary history of life</a> and most likely used <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> as sources of electrons, rather than water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16453059_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16453059-56">[56]</a></sup> Cyanobacteria appeared later, and the excess oxygen they produced contributed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_catastrophe" title="Oxygen catastrophe">oxygen catastrophe</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18468984_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18468984-57">[57]</a></sup> which rendered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolution_of_multicellularity" title="Evolution of multicellularity">evolution of complex life</a> possible. In recent geologic times, low CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations below 600 parts per million might have been the stimulus that favored the evolution of <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4</a> plants which increased greatly in abundance between 7 and 5 million years ago over plants that use the less efficient <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C3</a> metabolic pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Osborne,_C._P._2006_173–194-32">[32]</a></sup> At current atmospheric pressures photosynthesis shuts down when atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations fall below 150 ppm and 200 ppm although some microbes can extract carbon from the air at much lower concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Today, the average rate of energy capture by photosynthesis globally is approximately 130 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terawatts" title="Terawatts">terawatts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10670014_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10670014-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitmarsh_Govindjee_1999_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitmarsh_Govindjee_1999-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steger_2005_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steger_2005-62">[62]</a></sup> which is about six times larger than the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_energy_resources_and_consumption" title="World energy resources and consumption">power consumption of human civilization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIA_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-63">[63]</a></sup> Photosynthetic organisms also convert around 100–115 thousand million metric tonnes of carbon into <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9657713_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9657713-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-ps-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1027" data-file-width="830" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg/250px-Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg/375px-Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg/500px-Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photosynthesis changes sunlight into chemical energy, splits water to liberate O<sub>2</sub>, and fixes CO<sub>2</sub> into sugar.</div></div></div>
<p>Photosynthetic organisms are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoautotroph" title="Photoautotroph">photoautotrophs</a>, which means that they are able to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthesize</a> food directly from CO<sub>2</sub> and water using energy from light. However, not all organisms that use light as a source of energy carry out photosynthesis, since <i><a href="/wiki/Photoheterotroph" title="Photoheterotroph">photoheterotrophs</a></i> use organic compounds, rather than CO<sub>2</sub>, as a source of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bryantfrigaard_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-bryantfrigaard-66">[66]</a></sup> In plants, algae and cyanobacteria, photosynthesis releases oxygen. This is called <i>oxygenic photosynthesis</i>. Although there are some differences between oxygenic photosynthesis in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>, the overall process is quite similar in these organisms. However, there are some types of bacteria that carry out <a href="/wiki/Anoxygenic_photosynthesis" title="Anoxygenic photosynthesis">anoxygenic photosynthesis</a>, which consumes CO<sub>2</sub> but does not release oxygen.
</p><p>Carbon dioxide is converted into sugars in a process called <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon fixation</a>. Carbon fixation is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reaction, so photosynthesis needs to supply both the source of energy to drive this process and the electrons needed to convert CO<sub>2</sub> into a <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a>. This addition of the electrons is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction_(chemistry)" title="Reduction (chemistry)">reduction reaction</a>. In general outline and in effect, photosynthesis is the opposite of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, in which glucose and other compounds are oxidized to produce CO<sub>2</sub> and water, and to release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> chemical energy to drive the organism's <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. However, the two processes take place through a different sequence of chemical reactions and in different cellular compartments.
</p><p>Most organisms that utilize photosynthesis to produce oxygen use <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light</a> to do so, although at least three use shortwave <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> or, more specifically, far-red radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_increased_CO2_on_plants_and_crops">Effects of increased CO<sub>2</sub> on plants and crops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects of increased CO2 on plants and crops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 1993 review of scientific greenhouse studies found that a doubling of CO<sub>2</sub> concentration would stimulate the growth of 156 different plant species by an average of 37%. Response varied significantly by species, with some showing much greater gains and a few showing a loss. For example, a 1979 greenhouse study found that with doubled CO<sub>2</sub> concentration the dry weight of 40-day-old cotton plants doubled, but the dry weight of 30-day-old maize plants increased by only 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to greenhouse studies, field and satellite measurements attempt to understand the effect of increased CO<sub>2</sub> in more natural environments. In <a href="/wiki/Free-air_concentration_enrichment" title="Free-air concentration enrichment">free-air carbon dioxide enrichment</a> (FACE) experiments plants are grown in field plots and the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration of the surrounding air is artificially elevated. These experiments generally use lower CO<sub>2</sub> levels than the greenhouse studies.  They show lower gains in growth than greenhouse studies, with the gains depending heavily on the species under study. A 2005 review of 12 experiments at 475-600 ppm showed an average gain of 17% in crop yield, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legumes" title="Legumes">legumes</a> typically showing a greater response than other species and <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4 plants</a> generally showing less. The review also stated that the experiments have their own limitations. The studied CO<sub>2</sub> levels were lower, and most of the experiments were carried out in temperate regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Satellite measurements found increasing <a href="/wiki/Leaf_area_index" title="Leaf area index">leaf area index</a> for 25% to 50% of Earth's vegetated area Earth over the past 35 years, providing evidence for a positive <a href="/wiki/CO2_fertilization_effect" title="CO2 fertilization effect">CO<sub>2</sub> fertilization effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2017 article states that increased CO<sub>2</sub> levels have a negative impact on the nutritional quality of various human <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">food crops</a>, by increasing the levels of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, while decreasing the levels of important nutrients such as <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_deficiency" title="Zinc deficiency">zinc</a>. Crops experiencing a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a> include <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide_and_the_oceanic_carbon_cycle">Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the oceanic carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atmospheric carbon dioxide and the oceanic carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_carbon_cycle" title="Oceanic carbon cycle">Oceanic carbon cycle</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">Solubility pump</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="720" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/250px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/375px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/500px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Air-sea exchange of CO<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<p>The Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> contain a large amount of CO<sub>2</sub> in the form of bicarbonate and carbonate ions — much more than the amount in the atmosphere. The bicarbonate is produced in reactions between rock, water, and carbon dioxide. One example is the dissolution of calcium carbonate:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + CO<sub>2</sub> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">Ca<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Reactions like this tend to buffer changes in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. Since the right side of the reaction produces an acidic compound, adding CO<sub>2</sub> on the left side decreases the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of seawater, a process which has been termed <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> (pH of the ocean becomes more acidic although the pH value remains in the alkaline range). Reactions between CO<sub>2</sub> and non-carbonate rocks also add bicarbonate to the seas. This can later undergo the reverse of the above reaction to form carbonate rocks, releasing half of the bicarbonate as CO<sub>2</sub>. Over hundreds of millions of years, this has produced huge quantities of carbonate rocks.
</p><p>Ultimately, most of the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by human activities will dissolve in the ocean;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arch05_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-arch05-74">[74]</a></sup> however, the rate at which the ocean will take it up in the future is less certain.
Even if equilibrium is reached, including dissolution of carbonate minerals, the increased concentration of bicarbonate and decreased or unchanged concentration of carbonate ion will give rise to a higher concentration of un-ionized carbonic acid and dissolved CO<sub>2</sub>. This, along with higher temperatures, would mean a higher equilibrium concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in the air.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_CO2_emissions"><span id="Anthropogenic_CO2_increase"></span> Anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic CO2 emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>
<p>While CO<sub>2</sub> absorption and release is always happening as a result of natural processes, the recent rise in CO<sub>2</sub> levels in the atmosphere is known to be mainly due to human (anthropogenic) activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gosh03_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gosh03-75">[75]</a></sup> There are four ways human activity, especially fossil fuel burning, is known to have caused the rapid increase in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> over the last few centuries. 1) Various national statistics accounting for fossil fuel consumption, combined with knowledge of how much atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> is produced per unit of fossil fuel (e.g. liter of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline#Energy_content" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> 2) By examining the ratio of various carbon isotopes in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gosh03_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gosh03-75">[75]</a></sup> The burning of long-buried fossil fuels releases CO<sub>2</sub> containing carbon of different isotopic ratios to those of living plants, enabling distinction between natural and human-caused contributions to CO<sub>2</sub> concentration. 3) Higher atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations in the Northern Hemisphere, where most of the world's population lives (and emissions originate from), compared to the southern hemisphere. This difference has increased as anthropogenic emissions have increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeelingPiper2011_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-KeelingPiper2011-77">[77]</a></sup> 4) Atmospheric O<sub>2</sub> levels are decreasing in Earth's atmosphere as it reacts with the carbon in fossil fuels to form CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BenderHo2005_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-BenderHo2005-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Burning fossil fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> is the leading cause of increased <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> CO<sub>2</sub>; <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> is the second major cause. In 2010, 9.14 gigatonnes of carbon (GtC, equivalent to 33.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigatonne" title="Gigatonne">gigatonnes</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> or about 4.3 ppm in Earth's atmosphere) were released from fossil fuels and cement production worldwide, compared to 6.15 GtC in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-79">[79]</a></sup> In addition, land use change contributed 0.87 GtC in 2010, compared to 1.45 GtC in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peters_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peters-79">[79]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1997_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="1997 Southeast Asian haze">1997, human-caused Indonesian peat fires</a> were estimated to have released between 13% and 40% of the average carbon emissions caused by the burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> around the world in a single year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> In the period 1751 to 1900, about 12 GtC were released as CO<sub>2</sub> to the atmosphere from burning of fossil fuels, whereas from 1901 to 2013 the figure was about 380 GtC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Anthropogenic carbon emissions exceed the amount that can be taken up or balanced out by natural sinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BallantyneAlden2012_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-BallantyneAlden2012-88">[88]</a></sup> As a result, carbon dioxide has gradually accumulated in the atmosphere, and as of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, its concentration is almost 43% above pre-industrial levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-13">[13]</a></sup> Various techniques have been proposed for removing excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">carbon dioxide sinks</a>. Currently about half of the carbon dioxide released from the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">burning of fossil fuels</a> is not absorbed by vegetation and the oceans and remains in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>Excess CO<sub>2</sub> emitted since the pre-industrial era is projected to remain in the atmosphere for centuries to millennia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArcherEby2009_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArcherEby2009-90">[90]</a></sup> even after emissions stop.  Even if human carbon dioxide emissions were to completely cease, atmospheric temperatures are not expected to decrease significantly for thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ongoing_measurements_of_atmospheric_CO2">Ongoing measurements of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ongoing measurements of atmospheric CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TCCON" title="TCCON">TCCON</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space-based_measurements_of_carbon_dioxide" title="Space-based measurements of carbon dioxide">Space-based measurements of carbon dioxide</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="502" data-file-width="631" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg/250px-Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg/375px-Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg/500px-Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Global_distribution_of_Carbon_Dioxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carbon Dioxide observations from 2005 to 2014 showing the seasonal variations and the difference between northern and southern hemispheres</div></div></div>
<p>The first reproducibly accurate measurements of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> were from flask sample measurements made by <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Dave Keeling</a> at <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">Caltech</a> in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> A few years later in March 1958 the first ongoing measurements were started by Keeling at <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a>. Measurements at Mauna Loa have been ongoing since then. Now measurements are made at many sites globally. Additional measurement techniques are also used as well. Many measurement sites are part of larger global networks. Global network data are often made publicly available on the conditions of proper acknowledgment according to the respective data user policies.
</p><p>There are several surface measurement (including flasks and continuous in situ) networks including <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a>/<a href="/wiki/Earth_System_Research_Laboratory" title="Earth System Research Laboratory">ERSL</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> WDCGG,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> and RAMCES.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>  The NOAA/ESRL Baseline Observatory Network, and the <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a> Network <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> data are hosted at the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Dioxide_Information_Analysis_Center" title="Carbon Dioxide Information Analysis Center">CDIAC</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">ORNL</a>. The World Data Centre for Greenhouse Gases (WDCGG), part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Atmospheric_Watch" title="Global Atmospheric Watch">GAW</a>, data are hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">JMA</a>. The Reseau Atmospherique de Mesure des Composes an Effet de Serre database (RAMCES) is part of <a href="/wiki/Institute_Pierre_Simon_Laplace" title="Institute Pierre Simon Laplace">IPSL</a>.
</p><p>From these measurements, further products are made which integrate data from the various sources. These products also address issues such as data discontinuity and sparseness. GLOBALVIEW-CO2 is one of these products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ongoing ground-based total column measurements began more recently. Column measurements typically refer to an averaged column amount denoted X<sub>CO2</sub>, rather than a surface only measurement. These measurements are made by the <a href="/wiki/Total_Carbon_Column_Observing_Network" title="Total Carbon Column Observing Network">TCCON</a>. These data are also hosted on the CDIAC, and made publicly available according to the data use policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>Satellite measurements are also a recent addition to atmospheric X<sub>CO2</sub> measurements. <a href="/wiki/SCIAMACHY" title="SCIAMACHY">SCIAMACHY</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">ESA's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENVISAT" title="ENVISAT">ENVISAT</a> made global column X<sub>CO2</sub> measurements from 2002-2012. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Infrared_Sounder" title="Atmospheric Infrared Sounder">AIRS</a> aboard NASA’s <a href="/wiki/Aqua_(satellite)" title="Aqua (satellite)">Aqua satellite</a> makes global X<sub>CO2</sub> measurements and was launched shortly after ENVISAT in 2012. More recent satellites have significantly improved the data density and precision of global measurements. Newer missions have higher spectral and spatial resolutions. <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA's</a> <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_Gases_Observing_Satellite" title="Greenhouse Gases Observing Satellite">GOSAT</a> was the first dedicated GHG monitoring satellite to successfully achieve orbit in 2009. NASA's <a href="/wiki/Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory_2" title="Orbiting Carbon Observatory 2">OCO-2</a> launched in 2014 was the second. Various other satellites missions to measure atmospheric X<sub>CO2</sub> are planned.
</p>
