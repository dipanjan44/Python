<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water conservation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:211px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water-conservation-stamp-1960.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="135" data-file-width="209" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Water-conservation-stamp-1960.jpg" width="209"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water-conservation-stamp-1960.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>United States 1960 postal stamp advocating water conservation.</div></div></div>
<p>Water needs to be conserved because it is the most basic need and life without water is practically not possible.
</p><p><b>Water conservation</b> includes all the policies, strategies and activities to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainably" title="Sustainably">sustainably</a> manage the <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, to protect the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and to meet the current and future human  <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a>. Population, household size, and growth and affluence all affect how much water is used. Factors such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> have increased pressures on natural water resources especially in <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> and agricultural <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many US cities have already implemented policies aimed at water conservation, with much success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The goals of water conservation efforts include:
</p>
<ul><li>Ensuring <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">availability of water</a> for future generations where the withdrawal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> does not exceed its natural replacement rate.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">Energy conservation</a> as water pumping, delivery and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> facilities consume a significant amount of energy. In some regions of the world over 15% of total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_consumption" title="Electricity consumption">electricity consumption</a> is devoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_management" title="Water management">water management</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">Habitat conservation</a> where minimizing human water use helps to preserve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater_habitat" title="Freshwater habitat">freshwater habitats</a> for local wildlife and migrating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterfowl" title="Waterfowl">waterfowl</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategies">Strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key activities that benefit water conservation(save water) are as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>Any beneficial reduction in <a href="/wiki/Drying" title="Drying">water loss</a>, use and waste of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Avoiding any damage to <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.</li>
<li>Improving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_management" title="Water management">water management</a> practices that reduce the use or enhance the beneficial use of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vickers,_Amy_2002_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vickers,_Amy_2002-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>One strategy in water conservation is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_water_harvesting" title="Rain water harvesting">rain water harvesting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup> Digging ponds, lakes, canals, expanding the water reservoir, and installing rain water catching ducts and filtration systems on homes are different methods of harvesting rain water. Many people in many countries keep clean containers so they can boil it and drink it, which is useful to supply water to the needy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup> Harvested and filtered rain water can be used for toilets, home gardening, lawn irrigation, and small scale agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another strategy in water conservation is protecting <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> resources. When precipitation occurs, some infiltrates the soil and goes underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-8">[8]</a></sup> Water in this saturation zone is called groundwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:22-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groundwater_contamination" title="Groundwater contamination">Contamination of groundwater</a> causes the groundwater water supply to not be able to be used as resource of fresh drinking water and the natural regeneration of contaminated groundwater can takes years to replenish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:32-9">[9]</a></sup> Some examples of potential sources of groundwater contamination include storage tanks, <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic</a> systems, uncontrolled <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_contaminants" title="Atmospheric contaminants">atmospheric contaminants</a>, chemicals, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_salt" title="Road salt">road salts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:32-9">[9]</a></sup> Contamination of groundwater decreases the replenishment of available freshwater so taking preventative measures by protecting groundwater resources from contamination is an important aspect of water conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>An additional strategy to water conservation is practicing sustainable methods of utilizing groundwater resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup>  Groundwater flows due to gravity and eventually discharges into streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:22-8">[8]</a></sup> Excess pumping of groundwater leads to a decrease in groundwater levels and if continued it can exhaust the resource.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-:12-7">[7]</a></sup> Ground and surface waters are connected and overuse of groundwater can reduce and, in extreme examples, diminish the water supply of lakes, rivers, and streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:32-9">[9]</a></sup> In coastal regions, over pumping groundwater can increase saltwater intrusion which results in the contamination of  groundwater water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-:32-9">[9]</a></sup> Sustainable use of groundwater is essential in water conservation.
</p><p>A fundamental component to water conservation strategy is communication and education outreach of different water programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-10">[10]</a></sup> Developing communication that educates science to land managers, policy makers, farmers, and the general public is another important strategy utilized in water conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-10">[10]</a></sup> Communication of the science of how water systems work is an important aspect when creating a management plan to conserve that system and is often used for ensuring the right management plan to be put into action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:02-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_solutions">Social solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VineyardDrip.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="1500" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/VineyardDrip.JPG/220px-VineyardDrip.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/VineyardDrip.JPG/330px-VineyardDrip.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/VineyardDrip.JPG/440px-VineyardDrip.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VineyardDrip.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">Drip irrigation</a> system in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a></div></div></div>
<p>Water conservation programs involved in social solutions are typically initiated at the local level, by either municipal <a href="/wiki/Water_industry" title="Water industry">water utilities</a> or regional governments.  Common strategies include public <a href="/wiki/Outreach" title="Outreach">outreach</a> campaigns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> tiered water rates (charging progressively higher prices as water use increases), or restrictions on outdoor water use such as lawn watering and car washing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Cities in dry climates often require or encourage the installation of <a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">xeriscaping</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_landscaping" title="Natural landscaping">natural landscaping</a> in new homes to reduce outdoor water usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Most urban outdoor water use in California is residential,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> illustrating a reason for outreach to households as well as businesses.
</p><p>One fundamental conservation goal is universal metering. The prevalence of residential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_meter" title="Water meter">water metering</a> varies significantly worldwide. Recent studies have estimated that water supplies are metered in less than 30% of UK households,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and about 61% of urban Canadian homes (as of 2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although individual water meters have often been considered impractical in homes with private wells or in multifamily buildings, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> estimates that metering alone can reduce consumption by 20 to 40 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-Conserve_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-Conserve-17">[17]</a></sup> In addition to raising consumer awareness of their water use, metering is also an important way to identify and localize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_leakage" title="Water leakage">water leakage</a>.  Water metering would benefit society, in the long run, it is proven that water metering increases the efficiency of the entire water system, as well as help unnecessary expenses for individuals for years to come.  One would be unable to waste water unless they are willing to pay the extra charges, this way the water department would be able to monitor water usage by the public, domestic and manufacturing services.
</p><p>Some researchers have suggested that water conservation efforts should be primarily directed at farmers, in light of the fact that crop irrigation accounts for 70% of the world's fresh water use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  The agricultural sector of most countries is important both economically and politically, and water subsidies are common.  Conservation advocates have urged removal of all subsidies to force <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmers" title="Farmers">farmers</a> to grow more water-efficient crops and adopt less wasteful irrigation techniques.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>New technology poses a few new options for consumers, features such as full flush and half flush when using a toilet are trying to make a difference in water consumption and waste.  Also available are modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shower_head" title="Shower head">shower heads</a> that help reduce wasting water: Old shower heads are said to use 5-10 gallons per minute, while new fixtures available are said to use 2.5 gallons per minute and offer equal water coverage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_applications">Household applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Household applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Home Water Works website contains useful information on household water conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Contrary to the popular view that the most effective way to save water is to curtail water-using behavior (e.g., by taking shorter showers),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> experts suggest the most efficient way is replacing toilets and retrofitting washers; as demonstrated by two <a href="/wiki/Residential_water_use_in_the_U.S._and_Canada" title="Residential water use in the U.S. and Canada">household end use logging</a> studies in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-REUW2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-REUW2-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water-saving technology for the home includes:
</p>
<ol><li>Low-flow shower heads sometimes called energy-efficient shower heads as they also use less energy</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_flush_toilet" title="Low flush toilet">Low-flush toilets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Composting_toilet" title="Composting toilet">composting toilets</a>. These have a dramatic impact in the developed world, as conventional Western <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilets</a> use large volumes of water</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dual_flush_toilet" title="Dual flush toilet">Dual flush toilets</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Caroma" title="Caroma">Caroma</a> includes two buttons or handles to flush different levels of water. Dual flush toilets use up to 67% less water than conventional toilets</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Faucet_aerator" title="Faucet aerator">Faucet aerators</a>, which break water flow into fine droplets to maintain "wetting effectiveness" while using less water. An additional benefit is that they reduce splashing while washing hands and dishes</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_water_flushing" title="Raw water flushing">Raw water flushing</a> where toilets use sea water or non-purified water</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">Wastewater reuse</a> or recycling systems, allowing:
<ul><li>Reuse of <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">graywater</a> for flushing toilets or watering gardens</li>
<li>Recycling of wastewater through <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">purification</a> at a water treatment plant. <i>See also <a href="/wiki/Wastewater#Reuse" title="Wastewater">Wastewater - Reuse</a></i></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a></li>
<li>High-efficiency <a href="/wiki/Washing_machine" title="Washing machine">clothes washers</a></li>
<li>Weather-based irrigation controllers</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Garden_hose" title="Garden hose">Garden hose</a> <a href="/wiki/Nozzle" title="Nozzle">nozzles</a> that shut off the water when it is not being used, instead of letting a hose run.</li>
<li>Low flow taps in wash basins</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">Swimming pool</a> covers that reduce evaporation and can warm pool water to reduce water, energy and chemical costs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Automatic_faucet" title="Automatic faucet">Automatic faucet</a> is a water conservation faucet that eliminates water waste at the faucet. It automates the use of faucets without the use of hands.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_applications">Commercial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many water-saving devices (such as low-flush toilets) that are useful in homes can also be useful for business water saving. Other water-saving technology for businesses includes:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterless_urinal" title="Waterless urinal">Waterless urinals</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">X-ray</a> film processor re-circulation systems</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_applications">Agricultural applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agricultural applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:218px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="640" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/216px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/324px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg/432px-PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg 2x" width="216"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PivotIrrigationOnCotton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overhead irrigation, <a href="/wiki/Center_pivot_irrigation" title="Center pivot irrigation">center pivot</a> design</div></div></div>
<p>For crop irrigation, optimal <a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">water efficiency</a> means minimizing losses due to <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> or subsurface drainage while maximizing production.  An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporation_pan" title="Evaporation pan">evaporation pan</a> in combination with specific crop correction factors can be used to determine how much water is needed to satisfy plant requirements.  <a href="/wiki/Surface_irrigation" title="Surface irrigation">Flood irrigation</a>, the oldest and most common type, is often very uneven in distribution, as parts of a field may receive excess water in order to deliver sufficient quantities to other parts. <a href="/wiki/Irrigation#Overhead_(sprinkler)_irrigation" title="Irrigation">Overhead irrigation</a>, using center-pivot or lateral-moving sprinklers, has the potential for a much more equal and controlled distribution pattern. <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">Drip irrigation</a> is the most expensive and least-used type, but offers the ability to deliver water to plant roots with minimal losses. However, drip irrigation is increasingly affordable, especially for the home gardener and in light of rising water rates. Using drip irrigation methods can save up to 30,000 gallons of water per year when replacing irrigation systems that spray in all directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> There are also cheap effective methods similar to drip irrigation such as the use of soaking hoses that can even be submerged in the growing medium to eliminate evaporation.
</p><p>As changing irrigation systems can be a costly undertaking, conservation efforts often concentrate on maximizing the efficiency of the existing system.  This may include chiseling compacted soils, creating furrow dikes to prevent runoff, and using soil moisture and rainfall sensors to optimize irrigation schedules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-Conserve_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-Conserve-17">[17]</a></sup> Usually large gains in efficiency are possible through measurement and more effective management of the existing irrigation system. The 2011 UNEP Green Economy Report notes that "[i]mproved <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a> from the use of green manures, mulching, and recycling of crop residues and animal manure increases the water holding capacity of soils and their ability to absorb water during torrential rains",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which is a way to optimize the use of rainfall and irrigation during dry periods in the season.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Reuse">Water Reuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water Reuse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Water shortage has become an increasingly difficult problem to manage. More than 40% of the world's population live in a region where the demand for water exceeds its supply. The imbalance between supply and demand, along with persisting issues such as climate change and exponential <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a>, has made water reuse a necessary method for conserving water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> There are a variety of methods used in the <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">treatment of waste water</a> to ensure that it is safe to use for irrigation of food crops and/or drinking water.
</p><p>Seawater desalination requires more energy than the desalination of fresh water. Despite this, many seawater desalination plants have been built in response to water shortages around the world. This makes it necessary to evaluate the impacts of seawater desalination and to find ways to improve desalination technology. Current research involves the use of experiments to determine the most effective and least energy intensive methods of desalination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sand_filter" title="Sand filter">Sand filtration</a> is another method used to treat water. Recent studies show that sand filtration needs further improvements, but it is approaching optimization with its effectiveness at removing pathogens from water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Sand filtration is very effective at removing protozoa and bacteria, but struggles with removing viruses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Large-scale sand filtration facilities also require large surface areas to accommodate them.
</p><p>The removal of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> from recycled water is of high priority because wastewater always contains pathogens capable of infecting humans. The levels of pathogenic viruses have to be reduced to a certain level in order for recycled water to not pose a threat to human populations. Further research is necessary to determine more accurate methods of assessing the level of pathogenic viruses in treated wastewater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wasting_of_water">Wasting of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_conservation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wasting of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1632" data-file-width="1224" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg/240px-Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg/360px-Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg/480px-Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leaking_garden_hose_bib.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leaking garden hose bib</div></div></div>
<p>Wasting of water (also called "water waste" in the U.S.) is the flip side of water conservation and, in household applications, it means causing or permitting discharge of water without any practical purpose. Inefficient water use is also considered wasteful. By <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a> estimate, household leaks in the U.S. can waste approximately 900 billion gallons (3.4 billion cubic meters) of water annually nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Generally, water management agencies are reluctant or unwilling to give a concrete definition to the somewhat fuzzy concept of <b>water waste</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> However, definition of water waste is often given in local drought emergency ordinances. One example refers to any acts or omissions, whether willful or negligent, that are “causing or permitting water to leak, discharge, flow or run to waste into any gutter, sanitary sewer, watercourse or public or private storm drain, or to any adjacent property, from any tap, hose, faucet, pipe, sprinkler, pond, pool, waterway, fountain or nozzle.”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  In this example, the city code also clarifies that “in the case of washing, “discharge,” “flow” or “run to waste” means that water in excess of that necessary to wash, wet or clean the dirty or dusty object, such as an automobile, sidewalk, or parking area, flows to waste. Water utilities (and other media sources) often provide listings of wasteful water-use practices and prohibitions of wasteful uses. Examples include utilities in San Antonio, Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Las Vegas, Nevada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> California Water Service company in California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and City of San Diego, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The City of Palo Alto in California enforces permanent water use restrictions on wasteful practices such as leaks, runoff, irrigating during and immediately after rainfall, and use of potable water when non-potable water is available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Similar restrictions are in effect in the State of Victoria, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Temporary water use bans (also known as "hosepipe bans") are used in England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Strictly speaking, water that is discharged into the sewer, or directly to the environment is not wasted or lost. It remains within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrologic_cycle" title="Hydrologic cycle">hydrologic cycle</a> and returns to the land surface and surface water bodies as precipitation. However, in many cases, the source of the water is at a significant distance from the return point and may be in a different catchment. The separation between extraction point and return point can represent significant environmental degradation in the watercourse and riparian strip. What is "wasted" is the community's supply of water that was captured, stored, transported and treated to drinking quality standards. Efficient use of water saves the expense of water supply provision and leaves more fresh water in lakes, rivers and aquifers for other users and also for supporting ecosystems. A concept that is closely related to water wasting is "water-use efficiency." Water use is considered <a href="/wiki/Inefficiency" title="Inefficiency">inefficient</a> if the same purpose of its use can be accomplished with less water. Technical <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">efficiency</a> derives from engineering practice where it is typically used to describe the ratio of output to input and is useful in comparing various products and processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  For example, one showerhead would be considered more efficient than another if it could accomplish the same purpose (i.e., of showering) by using less water or other inputs (e.g., lower water pressure). However, the technical efficiency concept is not useful in making decisions of investing money (or resources) in water conservation measures unless the inputs and outputs are measured in value terms. This expression of efficiency is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Economic_efficiency" title="Economic efficiency">economic efficiency</a> and is incorporated into the concept of water conservation.
</p>
