<p><b>Climate change adaptation</b> is a response to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> (also known as "climate change" or "anthropogenic climate change"), that seeks to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability" title="Vulnerability">vulnerability</a> of social and biological systems to relatively sudden change and thus offset the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Even if emissions are stabilized relatively soon, global warming and its effects should last many years, and adaptation would be necessary to the resulting changes in climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Adaptation is especially important in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> since those countries are predicted to bear the brunt of the effects of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> That is, the capacity and potential for humans to adapt (called <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a>) is unevenly distributed across different regions and populations, and developing countries generally have less capacity to adapt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, the degree of adaptation correlates to the situational focus on environmental issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Therefore, adaptation requires the situational assessment of sensitivity and vulnerability to environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adaptive capacity is closely linked to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_development" title="Social development">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The economic costs of adaptation to climate change are likely to cost billions of dollars annually for the next several decades, though the amount of money needed is unknown. Donor countries promised an annual $100 billion by 2020 through the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a> for developing countries to adapt to climate change. However, while the fund was set up during <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">COP16 in Cancún</a>, concrete pledges by developed countries have not been forthcoming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Adaptation_Finance_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-International_Adaptation_Finance-9">[9]</a></sup> The adaptation challenge grows with the magnitude and the rate of climate change.
</p><p>Another response to climate change is known as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It advocates to reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> (GHG) emissions or enhance the removal of these gases from <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">the atmosphere</a> (through <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Even the most effective reductions in emissions, however, would not prevent further climate change impacts, making the need for adaptation unavoidable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> A study has concluded, with very high confidence, that in the absence of mitigation efforts, the effects of climate change would reach such a magnitude as to make adaptation impossible for some natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup> Others are concerned that climate adaptation programs might interfere with the existing development programs and thus lead to <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequences</a> for vulnerable groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> For human systems, the economic and <a href="/wiki/Social_cost" title="Social cost">social costs</a> of unmitigated climate change would be very high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_global_warming">Effects of global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects of global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a></div>
<p>The projected effects for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> are numerous and varied. The main effect is an increasing global average temperature. The average surface temperature could increase by 3 to 10 degrees Fahrenheit (approximately 1.67 to 5.56 degrees Celsius) by the end of the century if carbon emissions aren't reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This causes a variety of secondary effects, namely, changes in patterns of precipitation, rising sea levels, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">altered patterns of agriculture</a>, increased <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events, the expansion of the range of tropical diseases, and the opening of new marine trade routes.
</p><p>Potential effects include <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> of <span class="nowrap">110 to 770 mm</span> <span class="nowrap">(0.36 to 2.5 feet)</span> between 1990 and 2100, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">repercussions to agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">possible slowing of the thermohaline circulation</a>, reductions in the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>, increased intensity and frequency of extreme weather events, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">lowering</a> of ocean <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and the spread of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_disease" title="Tropical disease">tropical diseases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a>.
</p><p>A summary of probable effects and recent understanding can be found in the report made for the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">IPCC Third Assessment Report</a> by Working Group II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The 2007 contribution of Working Group II detailing the impacts of global warming for the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> has been summarized for policymakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adaptation is handicapped by uncertainty over the effects of global warming on specific locations such as the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a> or phenomena such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_monsoon" title="Indian monsoon">Indian monsoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD38_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CD38-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complementary_to_mitigation">Complementary to mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Complementary to mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> Working Group II,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-wg2-19">[19]</a></sup>
the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
the United Nations Disaster Risk Reduction Office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
and other science policy experts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> agree that while mitigating the emission of greenhouse gases is important, adaptation to the effects of global warming will still be necessary.
Some, like the UK <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Institution of Mechanical Engineers">Institution of Mechanical Engineers</a>, worry that mitigation efforts will largely fail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
The IPCC group points out that the world's ability to mitigate global warming is an economic and political challenge.  Given that greenhouse gas levels are already elevated, the lag of decades between emissions and some impacts, and the significant economic and political challenges of success, the IPCC group points out that it is uncertain how much climate change <i>will</i> be mitigated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg2_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-wg2-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">Developing countries</a> are the least able to adapt to climate change.  Doing so depends on such factors as wealth, technology, education, infrastructure, access to resources, management capabilities, acceptance of the existence of climate change and the consequent need for action, and sociopolitical will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>After assessing the literature on <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and climate change scientists concluded with high confidence that up to the year 2050, an effort to cap GHG emissions at 550 ppm would benefit developing countries significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This was judged to be especially the case when combined with enhanced adaptation. By 2100, however, it was still judged likely that there would be significant climate change impacts. This was judged to be the case even with aggressive mitigation and significantly enhanced adaptive capacity.
</p><p>The IPCC group also pointed out that climate change adaptation measures can reinforce and be reinforced by efforts to promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> and reduce poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg2_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-wg2-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_and_international_funding">Costs and international funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Costs and international funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-very_long" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>may be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_size" title="Wikipedia:Article size">too long</a> to read and navigate comfortably</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please consider <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Splitting" title="Wikipedia:Splitting">splitting</a> content into sub-articles, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Summary_style" title="Wikipedia:Summary style">condensing</a> it, or adding or removing <a href="/wiki/Help:Section#Subsections" title="Help:Section">subheadings</a>.</span> <small><i>(August 2016)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_of_adaptation_vs._mitigation">Cost of adaptation vs. mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cost of adaptation vs. mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Trade_offs" title="Economics of global warming">Economics of global warming § Trade offs</a></div>
<p>Adaptation and mitigation can be viewed as two competing policy responses, with tradeoffs between the two. The other tradeoff is with climate change impacts. In practice, however, the actual tradeoffs are debatable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This is because the people who bear emission reduction costs or benefits are often different from those who pay or benefit from adaptation measures.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">Economists</a>, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a>, have attempted to calculate an "optimal" balance of the costs and benefits between climate change impacts, adaptation, and mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> There are difficulties in doing this calculation, for example, future climate change damages are uncertain, as are the future costs of adaptation.
</p><p>Also, deciding what "optimal" is depends on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_judgement" title="Value judgement">value judgements</a> made by the economist doing the study (Azar, 1998).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> For example, how to value impacts occurring in different regions and different times, and "non-market" impacts, e.g., damages to ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Economics cannot provide definitive answers to these questions over valuation, and some valuations may be viewed as being controversial (Banuri <i>et al.</i>, 1996, pp. 87, 99).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-banuri-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some reviews indicate that policymakers are uncomfortable with using the results of this type of economic analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> This is due to the uncertainties surrounding cost estimates for climate change damages, adaptation, and mitigation. Another type of analysis is based on a <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a>-based approach to the problem. It has been argued that adaptation could play an important role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_policy" title="Climate policy">climate policy</a>, but not in an explicit trade-off against mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_estimates_and_international_aid">Cost estimates and international aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cost estimates and international aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many scientists, policy makers, and the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> have agreed that disadvantaged nations need to do more to adapt to climate change, especially highly populated regions of developing countries with low <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> estimated that an additional USD $86 billion per year would be needed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2016)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup> in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> estimates in 2007, costs of adaptation to climate change would cost $49–171 billion per year globally by 2030,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> of which a significant share of the additional investment and financial flows, USD $28–67 billion would be needed in 2030 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-Annex_I_Parties" title="Non-Annex I Parties">non-Annex I Parties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  This represents a doubling of current <a href="/wiki/Official_development_assistance" title="Official development assistance">official development assistance</a> (ODA).
</p><p>This estimate has been critiqued by some scientists who argues that the UNFCCC estimate underestimates the cost of adaptation to climate change by a factor of 2 or 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  Moreover, sectors such as tourism, mining, energy, and retail were not included in the UNFCCC estimate.
</p><p>The more recent World Bank Study, "Economics of Adaptation to Climate Change", found that the costs of adaptation would be in the range of $75–100 billion per year between 2010 and 2050; with higher estimates under the wetter global scenario than the drier scenario, assuming that warming will be about 2 degrees by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The benefits of strong, early action on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> considerably outweigh the costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Accord" title="Copenhagen Accord">Copenhagen Accord</a> was agreed on in order to create a commitment by developed countries to provide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODI-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>new and additional resources...approaching USD $30 billion for the period 2010- 2012 with balanced allocation between adaptation and mitigation... [and] in the context of meaningful mitigation actions and transparency on implementation, developed countries commit to a goal of mobilizing jointly USD$100 billion a year by 2020 to address the needs of developing countries.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Accord" title="Copenhagen Accord">Copenhagen Accord</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>However, a key point of contention between states at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCC" title="UNFCC">UNFCC</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Climate_Summit" title="Copenhagen Climate Summit">Copenhagen Climate Summit</a> was who was to foot the bill and if aid is to be given, how is it to affect other levels of <a href="/wiki/Development_aid" title="Development aid">development aid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODI-40">[40]</a></sup> The concept of <b>additionality</b> has thus arisen and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> has asked its member states to come up with definitions of what they understand additionality to mean, the four main definitions are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODI-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Climate finance classified as aid, but additional to (over and above) the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals#Controversy_over_funding_of_0.7%_of_GNI" title="Millennium Development Goals">0.7% ODA target</a>;</li>
<li>Increase on previous year's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_Development_Assistance" title="Official Development Assistance">Official Development Assistance</a> (ODA) spent on climate change mitigation;</li>
<li>Rising ODA levels that include climate change finance but where it is limited to a specified percentage; and</li>
<li>Increase in climate finance not connected to ODA.</li></ol>
<p>The main point being that there is a conflict between the OECD states budget deficit cuts, the need to help developing countries adapt to develop sustainably and the need to ensure that funding does not come from cutting aid to other important <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODI_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-ODI-40">[40]</a></sup> This is important, because international development through the sustainable (or millenium) development goals may be essential for a long-term solution to climate change <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webster2018_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Webster2018-41">[41]</a></sup>.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_aid_mechanisms">International aid mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International aid mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the aggregate of current climate change adaptation programs will not raise enough money to fund adaptation to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
There are, however, several programs and proposals to finance adaptation to climate change in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) runs a program called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Environmental_Facility" title="Global Environmental Facility">Global Environmental Facility</a>, which provides some funding for adaptation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_developed_countries" title="Least developed countries">least developed countries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Small_Island_Developing_States" title="Small Island Developing States">small island states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCCC_Adaptation_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCCC_Adaptation-43">[43]</a></sup> Under the GEF umbrella, the GEF Trust Fund, the <a class="external text" href="http://www.thegef.org/gef/LDCF" rel="nofollow">Least Developed Countries Fund (LDCF)</a>, and the <a class="external text" href="http://unfccc.int/cooperation_and_support/financial_mechanism/special_climate_change_fund/items/3657.php" rel="nofollow">Special Climate Change Fund (SCCF)</a> operate to carry out the climate change adaptation financing goals of the GEF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCCC_Adaptation_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCCC_Adaptation-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another UNFCCC mechanism is <a href="/wiki/The_Adaptation_Fund" title="The Adaptation Fund">The Adaptation Fund</a>, as a result of negotiations during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP15" title="COP15">COP15</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COP16" title="COP16">COP16</a>, which provides funds for projects that prove to have additional benefits for adaptation to climate change. The <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> (CDM), set up as part of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> to the Framework Convention, is the main source of income for the UNFCCC Adaptation Fund. This fund was established in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The CDM is subject to a 2% levy, which could raise between $300 million and $600 million over the 2008-12 period. The actual amount raised will depend on the carbon price. As of  August 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, The Adaptation Fund has not yet disbursed any funding;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (August 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> a call for proposals was issued in April 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  UNFCCC funding for Least Developed Countries includes development of a <a href="/wiki/National_Adaptation_Programme_of_Action" title="National Adaptation Programme of Action">National Adaptation Programme of Action</a>, which prioritizes requests to fund specific adaptation projects.
</p><p>There are several other climate change adaptation finance proposals, most of which employ <a href="/wiki/Official_development_assistance" title="Official development assistance">official development assistance</a> or ODA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Adaptation_Finance_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-International_Adaptation_Finance-9">[9]</a></sup> These proposals range from a World Bank program, to proposals involving auctioning of carbon allowances, to a global carbon or transportation tax, to compensation-based funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Adaptation_Finance_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-International_Adaptation_Finance-9">[9]</a></sup>
Other proposals suggest using market-based mechanisms, rather than ODA, such as the Higher Ground Foundation's vulnerability reduction credit (VRC™)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> or a program similar to the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> to raise private money for climate change adaptation.
The <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Accord" title="Copenhagen Accord">Copenhagen Accord</a>, the most recent global climate change agreement, commits developed countries to goal of sending $100 billion per year to developing countries in assistance for climate change mitigation and adaptation through 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  This agreement, while not binding, would dwarf current amounts dedicated to adaptation in developing countries.  This climate change fund is called the <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a> from the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integration_with_development_aid">Integration with development aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Integration with development aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> prioritize economic development over addressing the issue of climate change, as they are more concerned about pre-existing problems such as poverty, malnutrition, food insecurity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> availability of drinking water, indebtedness, illiteracy, unemployment, local resource conflicts, and lower technological development. It has even been argued that international development through the sustainable development goals, is essential for a long-term solution to climate change <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webster2018_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Webster2018-41">[41]</a></sup>. On the other hand, climate change threatens to exacerbate or stall progress on fixing some of these pre-existing problems. Advocates have thus proposed integrating climate change adaptation into poverty reduction programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Considerations_and_general_recommendations">Considerations and general recommendations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Considerations and general recommendations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_for_effective_policy">Principles for effective policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Principles for effective policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adaptive <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">policy</a> can occur at the global, national, or local scale, with outcomes dependent on the political will in that area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-52">[52]</a></sup> Scheraga and Grambsch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> identify 9 fundamental principles to be considered when designing adaptation policy.
</p>
<ol><li>The effects of climate change vary by region.</li>
<li>The effects of climate change may vary across demographic groups.</li>
<li>Climate change poses both risks and opportunities.</li>
<li>The effects of climate change must be considered in the context of multiple stressors and factors, which may be as important to the design of adaptive responses as the sensitivity of the change.</li>
<li>Adaptation comes at a cost.</li>
<li>Adaptive responses vary in effectiveness, as demonstrated by current efforts to cope with climate variability.</li>
<li>The systemic nature of climate impacts complicates the development of adaptation policy.</li>
<li>Maladaptation can result in negative effects that are as serious as the climate-induced effects that are being avoided.</li>
<li>Many opportunities for adaptation make sense whether or not the effects of climate change are realized.</li></ol>
<p>Scheraga and Grambsch make it clear that climate change policy is impeded by the high level of variance surrounding climate change impacts as well as the diverse nature of the problems they face.
</p><p>Adaptation can mitigate the adverse impacts of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, but it will incur costs and will not prevent all damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>  The IPCC points out that many adverse effects of climate change are not changes in the average conditions, but changes in the variation or the extremes of conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wg2_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-wg2-19">[19]</a></sup>  For example, the average sea level in a port might not be as important as the height of water during a storm surge (which causes flooding); the average rainfall in an area might not be as important as how frequent and severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> and extreme precipitation events become.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Additionally, effective adaptive policy can be difficult to implement because policymakers are rewarded more for enacting short-term change, rather than long-term planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Since the impacts of climate change are generally not seen in the short-term, this means that policymakers have less incentive to act upon those potential outcomes. Furthermore, these problems (both the causes and effects of climate change) are occurring on a global scale, which has caused the United Nations to lead global policy efforts such as the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>, in addition to creating a body of research through the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a>, in order to create a global framework for adapting to and combatting climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  However, the vast majority of climate change adaptation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_climate_change" title="Mitigation of climate change">mitigation</a> policies are being implemented on a more local scale due to the fact that different regions must adapt differently to climate change and because national and global policies are often more challenging to enact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria_for_assessing_responses">Criteria for assessing responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criteria for assessing responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>James Titus, project manager for sea level rise at the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>, identifies the following criteria that policy makers should use in assessing responses to global warming:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Economic <a href="/wiki/Efficiency" title="Efficiency">Efficiency</a>: Will the initiative yield benefits substantially greater than if the resources were applied elsewhere?</li>
<li>Flexibility: Is the strategy reasonable for the entire range of possible changes in temperatures, precipitation, and sea level?</li>
<li>Urgency: Would the strategy be successful if implementation were delayed ten or twenty years?</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_cost" title="Low cost">Low Cost</a>: Does the strategy require minimal resources?</li>
<li>Equity: Does the strategy unfairly benefit some at the expense of other regions, generations, or economic classes?</li>
<li>Institutional feasibility: Is the strategy acceptable to the public? Can it be implemented with existing institutions under existing laws?</li>
<li>Unique or Critical Resources: Would the strategy decrease the risk of losing unique environmental or cultural resources?</li>
<li>Health and Safety: Would the proposed strategy increase or decrease the risk of disease or injury?</li>
<li>Consistency: Does the policy support other national state, community, or private goals?</li>
<li>Private v. Public Sector: Does the strategy minimize governmental interference with decisions best made by the private sector?</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differing_time_scales">Differing time scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Differing time scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adaptation can either occur in anticipation of change (anticipatory adaptation), or be a response to those changes (reactive adaptation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uea.ac.uk_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-uea.ac.uk-60">[60]</a></sup>  Most adaptation being implemented at present is responding to current climate trends and variability, for example increased use of <a href="/wiki/Snowmaking" title="Snowmaking">artificial snow-making</a> in the European Alps. Some adaptation measures, however, are anticipating future climate change, such as the construction of the Confederation Bridge in Canada at a higher elevation to take into account the effect of future sea-level rise on ship clearance under the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc.ch_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc.ch-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Much adaptation takes place in relation to short-term climate variability, however this may cause <a href="/wiki/Maladaptation" title="Maladaptation">maladaptation</a> to longer-term climatic trends. For example, the expansion of irrigation in Egypt into the Western Sinai desert due to a period of higher river flows is a maladaptation when viewed in relation to the longer term projections of drying in the region<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iied.org_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-iied.org-62">[62]</a></sup>). Adaptations at one scale can also create externalities at another by reducing the adaptive capacity of other actors. This is often the case when broad assessments of the costs and benefits of adaptation are examined at smaller scales and it is possible to see that whilst the adaptation may benefit some actors, it has a negative effect on others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uea.ac.uk_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-uea.ac.uk-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_coping_strategies">Traditional coping strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Traditional coping strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People have always adapted to climatic changes and some community coping strategies already exist, for example changing sowing times or adopting new water-saving techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iied.org_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-iied.org-62">[62]</a></sup> Traditional knowledge and coping strategies must be maintained and strengthened, otherwise adaptive capacity may be weakened as local knowledge of the environment is lost. Strengthening these local techniques and building upon them also makes it more likely that adaptation strategies will be adopted, as it creates more community ownership and involvement in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc.ch_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc.ch-61">[61]</a></sup>  In many cases however this will not be enough to adapt to new conditions which are outside the range of those previously experienced, and new techniques will be needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geog.psu.edu_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-geog.psu.edu-63">[63]</a></sup> The incremental adaptations which were being implemented are now insufficient as the vulnerabilities and risks of climate change have increased, this causes a need for transformational adaptations which are much larger and costlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Current development efforts are increasingly focusing on community-based climate change adaptation, seeking to enhance local knowledge, participation and ownership of adaptation strategies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_adaptation">Methods of adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Methods of adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_adaptation_efforts">Local adaptation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Local adaptation efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cities, states, and provinces often have considerable responsibility in land use planning, public health, and disaster management.  Some have begun to take steps to adapt to threats intensified by climate change, such as flooding, bushfires, heatwaves, and rising sea levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Projects include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Installing protective and/ or resilient technologies and materials in properties that are prone to flooding<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></li>
<li>Changing to heat tolerant tree varieties (<a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainwater_storage" title="Rainwater storage">Rainwater storage</a> to deal with more frequent flooding rainfall – Changing to water <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">permeable pavements</a>, adding water-buffering vegetation, adding underground storage tanks, subsidizing household <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_barrel" title="Rain barrel">rain barrels</a> (Chicago)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>Reducing paved areas to deal with rainwater and heat (Chicago, Seoul)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dotearth_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-dotearth-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>Adding <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a> to deal with rainwater and heat (Chicago)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>Adding air conditioning in public schools (Chicago)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_69-3"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>Requiring waterfront properties to have higher foundations (Chula Vista, California)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Raising pumps at wastewater treatment plants (New York City)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Surveying local vulnerabilities, raising public awareness, and making climate change-specific planning tools like future flood maps (Seattle, Chicago, Norfolk, many others)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li>Incentivizing lighter-colored roofs to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_island_effect" title="Heat island effect">heat island effect</a> (Chula Vista, California)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_71-3"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Installing devices to prevent seawater from backflowing into storm drains (San Francisco)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_71-4"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Installing better flood defenses, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_wall" title="Sea wall">sea walls</a> and increased pumping capacity (Miami Beach)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_miami_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr_miami-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li>Buying out homeowners in flood-prone areas (New Jersey)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li>Raising street level to prevent flooding (Miami Beach)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_miami_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr_miami-77">[77]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Dealing with more frequent drenching rains may required increasing the capacity of <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> systems, and separating stormwater from <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_(waste)" title="Blackwater (waste)">blackwater</a>, so that overflows in peak periods do not contaminate rivers.  One example is the <a href="/wiki/SMART_Tunnel" title="SMART Tunnel">SMART Tunnel</a> in Kuala Lumpur.
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/English_Nature" title="English Nature">English Nature</a>, gardeners can help mitigate the effects of climate change by providing habitats for the most threatened species, and/or saving water by changing gardens to use plants which require less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>New York City produced a comprehensive report for its Rebuilding and Resiliency initiative after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>.  Its efforts include not only making buildings less prone flooding, but taking steps to reduce the future recurrence of specific problems encountered during and after the storm: weeks-long fuel shortages even in unaffected areas due to legal and transportation problems, flooded health care facilities, insurance premium increases, damage to electricity and steam generation in addition to distribution networks, and flooding of subway and roadway tunnels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancing_adaptive_capacity">Enhancing adaptive capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Enhancing adaptive capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adaptation can be defined as adjustments of a system to reduce <a href="/wiki/Vulnerability" title="Vulnerability">vulnerability</a> and to increase the resilience of a system to change,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc.ch_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc.ch-61">[61]</a></sup> also known as <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_capacity" title="Adaptive capacity">adaptive capacity</a>.  Those societies that can respond to change quickly and successfully have a high adaptive capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geog.psu.edu_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-geog.psu.edu-63">[63]</a></sup>  High adaptive capacity does not necessarily translate into successful adaptation. For example, the adaptive capacity in Western Europe is high, and the risks of warmer winters increasing the range of livestock diseases was well documented, but many parts of Europe were still badly affected by outbreaks of the <a href="/wiki/Bluetongue_disease" title="Bluetongue disease">Bluetongue virus</a> in livestock in 2007.
</p><p>Adaptive capacity is the ability of a system (human, natural or managed) to adjust to climate change (including climate variability and extremes) to moderate potential damages, to take advantage of opportunities, or to cope with consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Unmitigated climate change (i.e., future climate change without efforts to limit greenhouse gas emissions) would, in the long term, be likely to exceed the capacity of natural, managed and human systems to adapt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been found that enhanced adaptive capacity would reduce vulnerability to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Activities that enhance adaptive capacity are essentially equivalent to activities that promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>. These activities include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Improving access to resources</li>
<li>Reducing <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a></li>
<li>Lowering inequities of resources and wealth among groups</li>
<li>Improving <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and information</li>
<li>Improving <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a></li>
<li>Improving <a href="/wiki/Institutional_economics" title="Institutional economics">institutional</a> capacity and efficiency</li>
<li>Promoting <a class="external text" href="http://www.unesco.org/most/bpikpub.htm" rel="nofollow">local indigenous practices</a>, knowledge, and experiences</li></ul>
<p>Others have suggested that certain forms of gender inequity should be addressed at the same time;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> for example women may have participation in decision-making, or be constrained by lower levels of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc.ch_61-3"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc.ch-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a> found that development interventions to increase adaptive capacity have tended not to result in increased <a href="/wiki/Agency_(sociology)" title="Agency (sociology)">agency</a> for local people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> They argue that this should play a more prominent part in future intervention planning because agency is a central factor in all other aspects of adaptive capacity. Asset holdings and the ability to convert these resources through institutional and market processes are central to agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_production">Agricultural production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Agricultural production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A significant effect of global climate change is the altering of global rainfall patterns, with certain effects on agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cep-jennings_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-cep-jennings-88">[88]</a></sup> Rainfed agriculture constitutes 80% of global agriculture. Many of the 852 million poor people in the world live in parts of Asia and Africa that depend on rainfall to cultivate food crops. Climate change will modify <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" title="Soil moisture">soil moisture</a> storage. Extended drought can cause the failure of small and marginal farms with resultant economic, political and social disruption, more so than this currently occurs.
</p><p>Agriculture of any kind is strongly influenced by the availability of water.  Changes in total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasonal_precipitation" title="Seasonal precipitation">seasonal precipitation</a> or in its pattern of variability are both important. The occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Moisture_stress" title="Moisture stress">moisture stress</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering" title="Flowering">flowering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grain-filling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grain-filling (page does not exist)">grain-filling</a> is harmful to most crops and particularly so to <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soybeans" title="Soybeans">soybeans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>. Increased <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> from the soil and accelerated <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> in the plants themselves will cause moisture stress.
</p><p>Adaptive ideas include:
</p>
<ul><li>Taking advantage of global transportation systems to delivering surplus food to where it is needed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cep-jennings_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-cep-jennings-88">[88]</a></sup> (though this does not help subsistence farmers unless aid is given).</li>
<li>Developing crop varieties with greater <a href="/wiki/Drought_tolerance" title="Drought tolerance">drought tolerance</a>.</li>
<li>Rainwater storage. For example, according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">International Water Management Institute</a>, using small planting basins to 'harvest' water in Zimbabwe has been shown to boost maize yields, whether rainfall is abundant or scarce. And in Niger, they have led to three or fourfold increases in millet yields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></li>
<li>Falling back from crops to wild edible fruits, roots and leaves.  Promoting the growth of forests can provide these backup food supplies, and also provide watershed conservation, carbon sequestration, and aesthetic value.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="More_spending_on_irrigation">More spending on irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: More spending on irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demand_for_water" title="Demand for water">demand for water</a> for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> is projected to rise in a warmer climate, bringing increased competition between agriculture—already the largest consumer of water resources in <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid regions</a>—and urban as well as industrial users. Falling <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water tables</a> and the resulting increase in the energy needed to pump water will make the practice of irrigation more expensive, particularly when with drier conditions more water will be required per <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acre</a>. Other strategies will be needed to make the most efficient use of water resources. For example, the <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">International Water Management Institute</a> has suggested five strategies that could help Asia feed its growing population in light of climate change. These are:
</p>
<ul><li>Modernising existing irrigation schemes to suit modern methods of farming</li>
<li>Supporting farmers' efforts to find their own water supplies, by tapping into groundwater in a sustainable way</li>
<li>Looking beyond conventional "Participatory Irrigation Management" schemes, by engaging the private sector</li>
<li>Expanding capacity and knowledge</li>
<li>Investing outside the irrigation sector<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_control">Weather control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Weather control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Russian and American scientists have in the past tried to control the weather, for example by <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">seeding clouds</a> with chemicals to try to produce rain when and where it is needed. A new method being developed involves replicating the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect, where cities are slightly hotter than the countryside because they are darker and absorb more heat. This creates 28% more rain 20–40 miles <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward#mediaviewer/File:Upwind_downwind_example.png" title="Windward and leeward">downwind</a> from cities compared to upwind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> On the timescale of several decades, new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_control" title="Weather control">weather control</a> techniques may become feasible which would allow control of extreme weather such as hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>The World Meteorological Organization (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMO" title="WMO">WMO</a>) through its Commission for Atmospheric Sciences (CAS) opined in 2007: "Purposeful augmentation of precipitation, reduction of hail damage, dispersion of fog and other types of cloud and storm modifications by cloud seeding are developing technologies which are still striving to achieve a sound scientific foundation and which have to be adapted to enormously varied natural conditions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damming_glacial_lakes">Damming glacial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Damming glacial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake_outburst_flood" title="Glacial lake outburst flood">Glacial lake outburst floods</a> may become a bigger concern due to the retreat of glaciers, leaving behind numerous lakes that are impounded by often weak <a href="/wiki/Terminal_moraine" title="Terminal moraine">terminal moraine</a> dams. In the past, the sudden failure of these dams has resulted in localized property damage, injury and deaths. <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">Glacial lakes</a> in danger of bursting can have their <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> replaced with concrete dams (which may also provide hydroelectric power).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geoengineering">Geoengineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Geoengineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Climate engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management</a></div>
<p>IPCC (2007) concluded that <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a> options, such as ocean fertilization to remove CO<sub>2</sub> from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, remained largely unproven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> It was judged that reliable cost estimates for geoengineering had not been published.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> (2009) published the findings of a study into geoengineering. The authors of the study defined geoengineering as a "deliberate large-scale intervention in the Earth's climate system, in order to moderate global warming".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> According to the study, the safest and most predictable method of moderating climate change is early action to reduce GHG emissions.
</p><p>Scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Ken_Caldeira" title="Ken Caldeira">Ken Caldeira</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Crutzen" title="Paul Crutzen">Paul Crutzen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> suggest techniques such as:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management</a> may be seen as an adaptation to global warming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2010)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>  Techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Space_sunshade" title="Space sunshade">space sunshade</a>, creating <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfur_aerosols" title="Stratospheric sulfur aerosols">stratospheric sulfur aerosols</a> and painting roofing and paving materials white all fall into this category.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_geoengineering" title="Hydrological geoengineering">Hydrological geoengineering</a> - typically seeking to preserve <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> or adjust <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> by using methods such as diverting rivers to keep warm water away from <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a>, or tethering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icebergs" title="Icebergs">icebergs</a> to prevent them drifting into warmer waters and melting.  Though this is an adaptation technique, if it prevents <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a> it would also be classified as mitigation.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Migration frequently requires would-be migrants to have access to social and financial capital, such as support networks in the chosen destination, and the funds or physical resources to be able to move. It is frequently the last adaptive response households will take when confronted with environmental factors that threaten their livelihoods, and mostly resorted to when other mechanisms to cope have proven unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Migration_and_Climate_Change_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Migration_and_Climate_Change-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rhetoric of migration being related to climate change is complex and disputed. However, It is widely accepted that the results of migration events are multi-causal, with the environment being just a factor amongst many. Outside of policy, human rights organizations, expert demographers and environmental  climate scientists dominate this debate. Many discussions are based on projections and less with current migration data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>  While many migration events can be attributed to sudden environmental change, most migration events are a result of long term environmental changes and do not cause sudden migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> Some scholars attribute these events to sudden environmental changes, like natural disasters. Some choose to label it "climate change", which reflects a more long term onset of change, and the human impact element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is helpful to provide an intersectional approach to this discussion and understand that focusing on climate change as the issue frames the debate in terms of projections, causing the research to be speculative. Migration as tool for climate change adaptation is projected to be a more pressing issue in the decade to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> It is often framed in terms of human rights issues and national security. Migration events are often seen as a failure of the governments or policy making bodies that could not contain or effectively manage environmental changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> For example, extreme drought events in the Caribbean proliferate movement of peoples because of the lack of water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This is often seen as a failure on the local governments to provide structural and independent resources. These adaptation failures that have been the topic of concern for many scholars researching this area. The UN High Commissioner for Refugees has been viewed as one of the highest authorities to help those displaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance">Insurance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Insurance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Insurance spreads the financial impact of flooding and other extreme weather events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>  Although it can be preferable to take a proactive approach to eliminate the cause of the risk, reactive post-harm compensation can be used as a last resort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Access to reinsurance may be a form of increasing the resiliency of cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>  Where there are failures in the private insurance market, the public sector can subsidize premiums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> A study identified key equity issues for policy considerations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>transferring risk to the public purse does not reduce overall risk</li>
<li>governments can spread the cost of losses across time rather than space</li>
<li>governments can force home-owners in low risk areas to cross-subsidize the insurance premiums of those in high risk areas</li>
<li>cross-subsidization is increasingly difficult for private sector insurers operating in a competitive market</li>
<li>governments can tax people to pay for tomorrow's disaster</li></ul>
<p>Government-subsidized insurance, such as the U.S. National Flood Insurance Program, is criticized for providing a <a href="/wiki/Perverse_incentive" title="Perverse incentive">perverse incentive</a> to develop properties in hazardous areas, thereby increasing overall risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>  It is also suggested that insurance can undermine other efforts to increase adaptation, for instance through property level protection and resilience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> This behavioral effect may be countered with appropriate land-use policies that limit new construction where current or future climate risks are perceived and/or encourage the adoption of resilient building codes to mitigate potential damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_measures_by_region">Adaptation measures by region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Adaptation measures by region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment</a>, launched the <a href="/wiki/Global_Centre_of_Excellence_on_Climate_Adaptation" title="Global Centre of Excellence on Climate Adaptation">Global Centre of Excellence on Climate Adaptation</a> in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous countries, including <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming_in_Australia" title="Adaptation to global warming in Australia">Australia</a>, have held inquiries into and have planned or started adaptation measures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> has also issued a document titled "2009 California Climate Adaptation Strategy Discussion Draft" that summarizes the best known science on climate change impacts in seven specific sectors and provides recommendations on how to manage against those threats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Within the state of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> four counties (<a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami-Dade" title="Miami-Dade">Miami-Dade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monroe_County,_Florida" title="Monroe County, Florida">Monroe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County,_Florida" title="Palm Beach County, Florida">Palm Beach</a>) have created the Southeast Florida Regional Climate Change Compact in order to coordinate adaptation and mitigation strategies to cope with the impact of climate change on the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> 
Poorer communities have gotten help with climate adaptation in places like Bangladesh as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> The Commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> has issued grants to coastal cities and towns for adaptation activities such as fortification against flooding and preventing coastal erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> State is requiring climate change be taken into account in certain infrastructure permitting, zoning, and open space programs; and is mapping sea level rise along its coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>  After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>, New York and New Jersey accelerated voluntary government buy-back of homes in flood-prone areas. New York City announced in 2013 it planned to spend between $10 and $20 billion on local flood protection, reduction of the heat island effect with reflective and green roofs, flood-hardening of hospitals and public housing, resiliency in food supply, and beach enhancement; rezoned to allow private property owners to move critical features to upper stories; and required electrical utilities to harden infrastructure against flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>  Study of a large <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor_Storm-Surge_Barrier" title="New York Harbor Storm-Surge Barrier">storm barrier</a> spanning the entire harbor was previously proposed by the Governor of New York, but was dismissed in the City's plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, the German Federal Cabinet adopted the 'German Strategy for Adaptation to Climate Change'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> that sets out a framework for adaptation in Germany. Priorities are to collaborate with the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Federal States</a> of Germany in assessing the risks of climate change, identifying action areas and defining appropriate goals and measures. In 2011, the Federal Cabinet adopted the 'Adaptation Action Plan'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> that is accompanied by <a class="external text" href="http://climate-adapt.eea.europa.eu/countries-regions/countries/germany" rel="nofollow">other items</a> such as research programs, adaptation assessments and systematic observations.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Mesoamerica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Mesoamerica today, climate change is one of the main threats to rural central american farmers, as the region is plagued with frequent droughts, cyclones and the El Niño- Southern-Oscillation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-131">[131]</a></sup> Although there is a wide variety of adaption strategies, these can vary dramatically from country to country. Many of the adjustments that have been made are primarily agricultural or related to water supply. Some of these adaptive strategies include restoration of degraded lands, rearrangement of land uses across territories, livelihood diversification, changes to sowing dates or water harvest, and even migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_131-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-131">[131]</a></sup> The lack of available resources in Mesoamerica continues to pose as a barrier to more substantial adaptations, so the changes made today are much more incremental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_131-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nepal">Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation_in_Nepal" title="Climate change adaptation in Nepal">Climate change adaptation in Nepal</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_adaptation">Opposition to adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Opposition to adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>, writing in 1992 in <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_in_the_Balance" title="Earth in the Balance">Earth in the Balance</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoreEB239,240_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-GoreEB239,240-132">[132]</a></sup> adaptation represented a "kind of laziness, an arrogant faith in our ability to react in time to save our skins".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureTaboo_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatureTaboo-133">[133]</a></sup> Climate commentator David Roberts has written that "(b)oth mitigation and adaption are necessary at this point. But for every day mitigation is delayed, the need for adaptation grows," which is problematic because "adaptation is more expensive and requires bigger government than mitigation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_adaptation_denial">Climate adaptation denial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Climate adaptation denial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a <a href="/wiki/Report" title="Report">report</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenPeace_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenPeace-135">[135]</a></sup> released by Greenpeace USA in September 2013, climate change denial and the campaigns designed to block adaptation measures grew mainly out of the 1990s negotiations slated to develop a global agreement.  During these talks, a number of lobby groups were established with an objective of developing doubt within policymakers and the media through the use of publications in the guise of true science.  This tactic, similar to those of large tobacco companies, was utilized by the lobby groups in the hopes of delaying action and blurring the lines between the valid scientific efforts to challenge climate change findings and those designed to merely undermine the credibility of the scientific community.  This strategy feeds into the "uncertainty argument" and develops an impression of debate through references to the uncertainty of scientific findings that exist in any research model.  Additional tactics that the lobbyist groups have used include releasing non-stories manufactured from stolen emails and communications plans to develop more media coverage of the uncertainty argument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict-sensitive_adaptation">Conflict-sensitive adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_adaptation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Conflict-sensitive adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A book by the Berliner Wissenschafts-Verlag on 'conflict-sensitive adaptation' sheds light on unintended damaging effects of climate adaptation measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> For example, when disadvantaged groups are left out of the planning process, adaptation methods such as agricultural or water programmes may increase vulnerabilities. The book draws on findings from Africa and outlines how conflict-sensitive adaptation activities should look that are cognizant of the conflict-effects adaptation may have. The authors provide a "Memorandum for Action on Adaptation for Peace and Stability" that outlines principles to support processes for adaptation and peace such as the establishment of peace and conflict assessments for adaptation programmes, mainstreaming climate change adaptation in conflict-prone contexts, applying conflict sensitive approaches or provisions to ensure participatory processes to design and implement adaptation measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p>
