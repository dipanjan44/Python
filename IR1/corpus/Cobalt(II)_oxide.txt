<p><b>Cobalt(II) oxide</b> or <b>cobalt monoxide</b> is an inorganic compound that appears as olive-green to red crystals, or as a greyish or black powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is used extensively in the <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> industry as an additive to create blue colored glazes and enamels as well as in the chemical industry for producing cobalt(II) salts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_properties">Structure and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CoO crystals adopt the <a href="/wiki/Periclase" title="Periclase">periclase</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_salt" title="Rock salt">rock salt</a>) structure with a lattice constant of 4.2615 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetic</a> below 16 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II,III)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II,III) oxide">Cobalt(II,III) oxide</a> decomposes to cobalt(II) oxide at 950 °C:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> → 6 CoO + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Though commercially available, cobalt(II) oxide may be prepared in the laboratory by electrolyzing a solution of cobalt(II) chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>CoCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → CoO + H<sub>2</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub>
</p><p>It may also be prepared by precipitating the hydroxide, followed by thermal dehydration:
</p>
<dl><dd>CoX + 2 KOH → Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> + K<sub>2</sub>X</dd>
<dd>Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> → CoO + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As can be expected, cobalt(II) oxide reacts with mineral acids to form the corresponding cobalt salts:
</p>
<dl><dd>CoO + 2 HX → CoX<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cobalt(II) oxide has for centuries used as a coloring agent on <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a> fired pottery. The additive provides a deep shade of blue named <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt blue</a>. The band gap (CoO) is around 2.4 eV.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
It also is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue_glass" title="Cobalt blue glass">cobalt blue glass</a>.
</p>
