<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Physical impacts of climate change</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>This article is about the <b>physical impacts of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a></b>. For some of these physical impacts, their effect on social and economic systems are also described.
</p><p>This article refers to reports produced by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a>. In their usage, "climate change" refers to a change in the state of the climate that can be identified by changes in the mean and/or variability of its properties, and that persists for extended periods, typically decades or longer (IPCC, 2007d:30).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4syn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4syn-1">[1]</a></sup> The climate change referred to may be due to natural causes <a href="/wiki/And/or" title="And/or">and/or</a> the result of human activity.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">Instrumental temperature record</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Instrumental_Temperature_Record_(NASA).svg"><img alt="The NASA instrumental temperature record shows a long-term trend of global warming between 1880 and 2009" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg/220px-Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg/330px-Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg/440px-Instrumental_Temperature_Record_%28NASA%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Instrumental_Temperature_Record_(NASA).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global mean surface temperature difference from the average for 1880–2009.</div></div></div>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup> Global surface temperatures have increased about 0.74 °C (plus or minus 0.18 °C) since the late-19th century, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_trend" title="Linear trend">linear trend</a> for the past 50 years of 0.13 °C (plus or minus 0.03 °C) per decade is nearly twice that for the past 100 years. The warming has not been globally uniform. Some areas have, in fact, cooled slightly over the last century. The recent warmth has been greatest over <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> between 40 and 70°N. Lastly, seven of the eight warmest years on record have occurred since 2001 and the 10 warmest years have all occurred since 1995.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consistency_of_evidence_for_warming">Consistency of evidence for warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consistency of evidence for warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_(Kennedy_et_al.,_2010).png"><img alt="Refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1825" data-file-width="1589" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png/220px-11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png/330px-11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png/440px-11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_%28Kennedy_et_al.%2C_2010%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:11_key_indicators_of_global_warming_from_State_of_the_Climate_2009_(Kennedy_et_al.,_2010).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>11 key indicators of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kennedy_evidence_for_global_warming_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kennedy_evidence_for_global_warming-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_evidence_for_warming_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_evidence_for_warming-4">[4]</a></sup> Thousands of land and ocean temperature measurements are recorded each day around the globe. This includes measurements from climate reference stations, <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoys</a> and autonomous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glider_aircraft" title="Glider aircraft">gliders</a> in the oceans. These surface measurements are also supplemented with <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">satellite measurements</a>. These measurements are processed, examined for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_error" title="Random error">random</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systematic_error" title="Systematic error">systematic errors</a>, and then finally combined to produce a <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">time series</a> of global average temperature change. A number of agencies around the world have produced datasets of global-scale changes in surface temperature using different techniques to process the data and remove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measurement_error" title="Measurement error">measurement errors</a> that could lead to false interpretations of temperature trends (see <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">Instrumental temperature record</a>). The warming trend that is apparent in all of the independent methods of calculating global temperature change is also confirmed by other independent observations, such as the melting of mountain <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">glaciers</a> on every continent, reductions in the extent of <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover, earlier blooming of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> in spring, a shorter ice season on <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_content" title="Heat content">heat content</a>, reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">rising</a> sea levels. Some of these indicators are further discussed in this article.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_average_temperature">Global average temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global average temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Global average temperature is one of the most-cited indicators of global climate change, and shows an increase of approximately 1.4 °F since the early 20th Century. The global surface temperature is based on air temperature data over land and sea-surface temperatures observed from ships, buoys and satellites. There is a clear long-term global warming trend, while each individual year does not always show a temperature increase relative to the previous year, and some years show greater changes than others. These year-to-year <a href="/wiki/Climate_oscillation" title="Climate oscillation">fluctuations</a> in temperature are due to natural processes, such as the effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">El Niños</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niñas</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">eruption</a> of large volcanoes. Notably, the 20 warmest years have all occurred since 1981, and the 10 warmest have all occurred in the past 12 years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diurnal_temperature">Diurnal temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diurnal temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup> There has been a general, but not global, tendency toward reduced <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal temperature range</a> (DTR: the difference between daily high or maximum and daily low or minimum temperatures) over about 70% of the global land mass since the middle of the 20th century. However, for the period 1979–2005 the DTR shows no trend since the trend in both maximum and minimum temperatures for the same period are virtually identical; both showing a strong warming signal. A variety of factors likely contribute to this change in DTR, particularly on a regional and local basis, including changes in <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud cover</a>, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> effects.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_indicators_of_warming">Indirect indicators of warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indirect indicators of warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup> Indirect indicators of warming such as <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">borehole</a> temperatures, snow cover, and glacier recession data, are in substantial agreement with the more direct indicators of recent warmth. Evidence such as changes in glacial mass balance (the amount of snow and ice contained in a glacier) is useful since it not only provides qualitative support for existing <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> data, but glaciers often exist in places too remote to support meteorological stations. The records of glacial advance and retreat often extend back further than weather station records, and glaciers are usually at much higher <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> than weather stations, allowing scientists more insight into temperature changes higher in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_weather">Effects on weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effects on weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Increasing temperature is likely to lead to increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1a_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1a-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but the effects on storms are less clear. Extratropical storms partly depend on the <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">temperature gradient</a>, which is predicted to weaken in the northern hemisphere as the polar region warms more than the rest of the hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It is possible that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cells" title="Polar cells">Polar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrel_cell" title="Ferrel cell">Ferrel</a> cells in one or both hemispheres will weaken and eventually disappear, which would cause the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> to cover the whole planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langford,_Bill_and_Lewis,_Greg_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langford,_Bill_and_Lewis,_Greg-9">[9]</a></sup> This would greatly decrease the temperature gradient between the arctic and the tropics, and cause the earth to flip to a hothouse state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langford,_Bill_and_Lewis,_Greg_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langford,_Bill_and_Lewis,_Greg-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Historically (i.e., over the 20th century), <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropical</a> land regions have been mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">semi-arid</a>, while most <a href="/wiki/Subarctic_climate" title="Subarctic climate">subpolar</a> regions have had an excess of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> over <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. Future <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is expected to be accompanied by a reduction in rainfall in the subtropics and an increase in precipitation in subpolar latitudes and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_region" title="Equatorial region">equatorial regions</a>. In other words, regions which are dry at present will generally become even drier, while regions that are currently wet will generally become even wetter. This projection does not apply to every locale, and in some cases can be modified by local conditions. Drying is projected to be strongest near the poleward margins of the subtropics (for example, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Australia" title="Southern Australia">southern Australia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_basin" title="Mediterranean basin">Mediterranean</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">south-western U.S.</a>), a pattern that can be described as a poleward expansion of these semi-arid zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfdl_rainfall_projections_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-gfdl_rainfall_projections-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>This large-scale pattern of change is a robust feature present in nearly all of the simulations conducted by the world's climate modeling groups for the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">4th Assessment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC), and is also evident in observed 20th century precipitation trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfdl_rainfall_projections_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-gfdl_rainfall_projections-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Changes in regional climate are expected to include greater warming over land, with most warming at high northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>, and least warming over the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and parts of the North Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_SYR_2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_SYR_2007-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Future changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are expected to follow existing trends, with reduced precipitation over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> land areas, and increased precipitation at subpolar latitudes and some <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>
A 2015 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Climate_Change" title="Nature Climate Change">Nature Climate Change</a></i>, states: </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>About 18% of the moderate daily precipitation extremes over land are attributable to the observed temperature increase since pre-industrial times, which in turn primarily results from human influence. For 2 °C of warming the fraction of precipitation extremes attributable to human influence rises to about 40%. Likewise, today about 75% of the moderate daily hot extremes over land are attributable to warming. It is the most rare and extreme events for which the largest fraction is anthropogenic, and that contribution increases nonlinearly with further warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT42715_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT42715-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureCC42715_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatureCC42715-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_events">Extreme events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Extreme events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_humans#Social_effects_of_extreme_weather" title="Effects of climate change on humans">Effects of climate change on humans § Social effects of extreme weather</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire">Fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fire is a major agent for conversion of biomass and <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a> to CO<sub>2</sub> (Denman <i>et al</i>., 2007:527).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> There is a large potential for future alteration in the terrestrial carbon balance through altered fire regimes. With high confidence, Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007:789) <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Scenarios" title="Economics of global warming">projected</a> that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-schneider-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>An increase in global mean temperature of about 0 to 2 °C by 2100 relative to the 1990–2000 period would result in increased fire frequency and intensity in many areas.</li>
<li>An increase in the region of 2 °C or above would lead to increased frequency and intensity of fires.</li></ul>
<p>Sensitivity to fires in areas that were already vulnerable has been steadily increasing. In high altitude temperate areas, increased temperature is causing snow pack to melt sooner and in greater quantities. The number of days that of higher stream flow caused by snowmelt in the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio</a> rivers has been increasing in recent years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>. The substantial amount of snow that remains atop mountains year around is also disappearing. This leads to the surrounding densely forested areas becoming more dry and staying dry for longer periods of time. In the 1970s, the length of a fire season, which is the period of the year fires are most likely to occur, was about five months. Today, the period is usually seven months, extending into the springtime <a href="/wiki/Mud_season" title="Mud season">mud season</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>. In addition, many areas are experiencing higher than normal droughts. Between 2011 and 2014, California experienced the driest period in its recorded history<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and more than 100 million trees died in the drought, creating areas of dead, dry wood<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. The decrease in rainfall is also going to increase the risk of wildfire by allowing the fire access to drier fuels. Dry foliage is more susceptible to a wildfire trigger. Wildfire specialists use foliar moisture content to determine how susceptible an area is to a wildfire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 2015 was the most destructive year on record for wildfires, with a total of 10,125,149 total acres destroyed by fires. 2017 was the second worst year on record with 10,026,086 acres destroyed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>. The <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fire" title="Thomas Fire">Thomas Fire</a> occurred in 2017 and was the largest fire in California's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The increasing frequency of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a> as a result of climate change will also lead to an increase in the amount of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> in the atmosphere. This will, in turn, increase the temperature and the frequency of hot days, which will further increase fire danger. It was forecasted that double levels of CO<sub>2,</sub> would bring a greater risk of wildfires to Australia, especially the Australian outback. All of the eight sites tested projected an increase in fire danger as a result of CO<sub>2</sub> level increase and all but one projected a longer fire season. The largest population center said to be affected is <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>, a city deep in the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">Outback</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather">Extreme weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extreme weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes_and_climate_change" title="Hurricanes and climate change">Hurricanes and climate change</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic_hurricane_records" title="List of Atlantic hurricane records">List of Atlantic hurricane records</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="689" data-file-width="2062" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/220px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/330px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/440px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frequency of occurrence (vertical axis) of local June–July–August temperature anomalies (relative to 1951–1980 mean) for Northern Hemisphere land in units of local <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> (horizontal axis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-27">[27]</a></sup>  According to Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-27">[27]</a></sup> the distribution of anomalies has shifted to the right as a consequence of global warming, meaning that unusually hot summers have become more common. This is analogous to the rolling of a die: cool summers now cover only half of one side of a six-sided die, white covers one side, red covers four sides, and an extremely hot (red-brown) anomaly covers half of one side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-27">[27]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>IPCC (2007a:8) projected that in the future, over most land areas, the frequency of warm spells or <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> would very likely increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1-28">[28]</a></sup> Other likely changes are listed below:
</p>
<ul><li>Increased areas will be affected by <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_extreme_events_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_extreme_events-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>There will be increased intense <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> activity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_extreme_events_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_extreme_events-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>There will be increased incidences of extreme high sea level (excluding <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_extreme_events_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_extreme_events-29">[29]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Storm strength leading to extreme weather is increasing, such as the power dissipation index of hurricane intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a> writes that hurricane power dissipation is highly correlated with temperature, reflecting <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, a further study by Emanuel using current model output concluded that the increase in power dissipation in recent decades cannot be completely attributed to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Hurricane modeling has produced similar results, finding that hurricanes, simulated under warmer, high-CO<sub>2</sub> conditions, are more intense, however, hurricane frequency will be reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Worldwide, the proportion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> reaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">categories 4 or 5</a> – with wind speeds above 56 metres per second – has risen from 20% in the 1970s to 35% in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Precipitation hitting the US from hurricanes has increased by 7% over the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The extent to which this is due to global warming as opposed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a> is unclear. Some studies have found that the increase in <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> may be offset by an increase in <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, leading to little or no change in hurricane activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Hoyos <i>et al.</i> (2006) have linked the increasing trend in number of category 4 and 5 hurricanes for the period 1970–2004 directly to the trend in sea surface temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyos2006_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyos2006-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Knutson" title="Thomas Knutson">Thomas Knutson</a> and Robert E. Tuleya of <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> stated in 2004 that warming induced by <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> may lead to increasing occurrence of highly destructive category-5 storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2008, Knutson <i>et al.</i> found that Atlantic hurricane and tropical storm frequencies could reduce under future greenhouse-gas-induced warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knutson2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knutson2008-41">[41]</a></sup> Vecchi and Soden find that <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, the increase of which acts to inhibit <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, also changes in model-projections of global warming. There are projected increases of <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> in the tropical Atlantic and East Pacific associated with the deceleration of the <a href="/wiki/Walker_circulation" title="Walker circulation">Walker circulation</a>, as well as decreases of wind shear in the western and central Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The study does not make claims about the net effect on Atlantic and East Pacific hurricanes of the warming and moistening atmospheres, and the model-projected increases in Atlantic wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> explains that "though there is evidence both for and against the existence of a detectable anthropogenic signal in the tropical cyclone climate record to date, no firm conclusion can be made on this point."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO-IWTC_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-IWTC-44">[44]</a></sup> They also clarified that "no individual tropical cyclone can be directly attributed to climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO-IWTC_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-IWTC-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>A substantially higher risk of extreme weather does not necessarily mean a noticeably greater risk of slightly-above-average weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> However, the evidence is clear that severe weather and moderate rainfall are also increasing. Increases in temperature are expected to produce more intense convection over land and a higher frequency of the most severe storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmer_Drought_Severity_Index" title="Palmer Drought Severity Index">Palmer Drought Severity Index</a>, a 2010 study by the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> projects increasingly dry conditions across much of the globe in the next 30 years, possibly reaching a scale in some regions by the end of the century that has rarely, if ever, been observed in modern times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coumou <i>et al.</i> (2013)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> estimated that global warming had increased the probability of local record-breaking monthly temperatures worldwide by a factor of 5. This was compared to a baseline climate in which no global warming had occurred. Using a medium <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">global warming scenario</a>, they project that by 2040, the number of monthly heat records globally could be more than 12 times greater than that of a scenario with no long-term warming.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_evaporation">Increased evaporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Increased evaporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="775" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png/200px-BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png/300px-BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png/400px-BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BAMS_climate_assess_boulder_water_vapor_2002_-_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Increasing water vapor at Boulder, Colorado.</div></div></div>
<p>Over the course of the 20th century, evaporation rates have reduced worldwide;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> this is thought by many to be explained by <a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">global dimming</a>. As the climate grows warmer and the causes of global dimming are reduced, <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> will increase due to warmer oceans. Because the world is a closed system this will cause heavier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>, with more <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. This erosion, in turn, can in vulnerable tropical areas (especially in Africa) lead to <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>. On the other hand, in other areas, increased rainfall lead to growth of forests in dry desert areas.
</p><p>Scientists have found evidence that increased evaporation could result in more extreme <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> as global warming progresses. The IPCC Third Annual Report says: "...global average water vapor concentration and precipitation are projected to increase during the 21st century. By the second half of the 21st century, it is likely that precipitation will have increased over northern mid- to high latitudes and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> in winter. At low latitudes there are both regional increases and decreases over land areas. Larger year-to-year variations in precipitation are very likely over most areas where an increase in mean precipitation is projected."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1a_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1a-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_freshwater_flow">Increased freshwater flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Increased freshwater flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Research based on satellite observations, published in October 2010, shows an increase in the flow of freshwater into the world's oceans, partly from melting ice and partly from increased precipitation driven by an increase in global ocean evaporation. The increase in global freshwater flow, based on data from 1994 to 2006, was about 18%. Much of the increase is in areas which already experience high rainfall. One effect, as perhaps experienced in the <a href="/wiki/2010_Pakistan_floods" title="2010 Pakistan floods">2010 Pakistan floods</a>, is to overwhelm flood control infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_climate_change">Regional climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regional climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">Regional effects of global warming</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_effects">General effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: General effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a literature assessment, Hegerl <i>et al.</i> (2007) assessed evidence for <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">attributing</a> observed climate change. They concluded that since the middle of the 20th century, it was likely that human influences had significantly contributed to surface temperature increases in every continent except Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> reported <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=top-10-places-already-affected-by-climate-change" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> on December 23, 2008, that the 10 places most affected by climate change were <a href="/wiki/Darfur" title="Darfur">Darfur</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_nation" title="Island nation">island nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_hemisphere">Northern hemisphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Northern hemisphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_dipole" title="Arctic dipole">Arctic dipole</a></div>
<p>In the northern hemisphere, the southern part of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> region (home to 4,000,000 people) has experienced a temperature rise of 1 °C to 3 °C (1.8 °F to 5.4 °F) over the last 50 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Canada, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and Russia are experiencing initial melting of <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>. This may disrupt ecosystems and by increasing bacterial activity in the soil lead to these areas becoming carbon sources instead of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> A study (published in <i>Science</i>) of changes to eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>'s permafrost suggests that it is gradually disappearing in the southern regions, leading to the loss of nearly 11% of Siberia's nearly 11,000 lakes since 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> At the same time, western Siberia is at the initial stage where melting permafrost is creating new lakes, which will eventually start disappearing as in the east. Furthermore, permafrost melting will eventually cause methane release from melting permafrost peat bogs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_regions">Polar regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Polar regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">Arctic shrinkage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_amplification" title="Polar amplification">Polar amplification</a></div>
<p>Anisimov <i>et al</i>. (2007) assessed the literature on impacts of climate change in Polar regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Model projections showed that Arctic terrestrial ecosystems and the active layer (the top layer of soil or rock in permafrost that is subjected to seasonal freezing and thawing) would be a small sink for carbon (i.e., net uptake of carbon) over this century (p. 662). These projections were viewed as being uncertain. It was judged that increased emissions of carbon from thawing of permafrost could occur. This would lead to an amplification of warming.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noctilucent_cloud" title="Noctilucent cloud">Noctilucent cloud</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere,_mid_to_upper_troposphere,_lower_troposphere,_and_surface,_1957-2005_(NOAA).gif"><img alt="Refer to caption and image description" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="976" data-file-width="598" height="359" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif/220px-Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif/330px-Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif/440px-Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere%2C_mid_to_upper_troposphere%2C_lower_troposphere%2C_and_surface%2C_1957-2005_%28NOAA%29.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Temperature_trends_in_the_lower_stratosphere,_mid_to_upper_troposphere,_lower_troposphere,_and_surface,_1957-2005_(NOAA).gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Temperature trends in the lower <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, mid to upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, lower troposphere, and surface, 1957-2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>An enhanced <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> is expected to cause cooling in higher parts of the atmosphere. Cooling of the lower <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> (about 49,000-79,500 ft.) since 1979 is shown by both satellite <a href="/wiki/Microwave_sounding_unit" title="Microwave sounding unit">Microwave sounding unit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> data, but is larger in the radiosonde data likely due to uncorrected errors in the radiosonde data (see figure opposite). <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A contraction of the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> has been observed as a possible result in part due to increased carbon dioxide concentrations, the strongest cooling and contraction occurring in that layer during <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">solar minimum</a>. The most recent contraction in 2008–2009 was the largest such since at least 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-fringe-theories" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Unbalanced scales.svg" data-file-height="354" data-file-width="400" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/45px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/68px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/90px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>may present <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:FRINGE" title="Wikipedia:FRINGE">fringe theories</a>, without giving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:UNDUE" title="Wikipedia:UNDUE">appropriate weight</a> to the mainstream view</b>, and explaining the responses to the fringe theories.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit">help improve it</a> or discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Physical_impacts_of_climate_change" title="Talk:Physical impacts of climate change">talk page</a>.</span> <small><i>(September 2012)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback" title="Water vapor feedback">water vapor feedback</a></div>
<p>Recent evidence suggests that warming of the tropical oceans since a "<a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping point</a>" in 2000 may have acted as a <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>, reducing the observed warming during the 2000s (decade). As warming and evaporation above the Pacific Ocean, temperatures in the lower stratosphere near the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> declined due to both greenhouse gases and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone-depleting_substance" title="Ozone-depleting substance">ozone-depleting substances</a>, reducing <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> levels and removing its warming effect, with vapor concentrations below 2.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ppmv" title="Ppmv">ppmv</a> as measured by the HALOE instrument on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Atmosphere_Research_Satellite" title="Upper Atmosphere Research Satellite">Upper Atmosphere Research Satellite</a>, in the lower stratosphere of the tropics between 5°N - 5°S first being observed since 2001, although a reversal in this pattern is also likely. The water vapor in the stratosphere arrives through tall <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>, while 15% of this vapor is delivered by <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, and through chemical breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> into water vapor and carbon dioxide, both of which are greenhouse gases. The vapor is frozen out of the stratosphere as more of the water is subjected to temperatures that freeze it out of the stratosphere. Water vapor concentrations in the lower stratosphere have declined by 10% (0.4 ppmv) since 2000, reducing warming during the decade by 25%. A rapid cooling of 4 °C to 6 °C also occurred in the lower stratosphere in the mid-1990s, while the rate of ocean warming increased. During the 1990s, increased stratospheric water vapor led to a 30% increase in warming. After 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> of the tropical Western Pacific, where a warm pool of water exists and where temperatures are heavily influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">ENSO</a>, between 10°N - 10°S and 139° - 171° longitude became anti-correlated with temperatures at the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> in the same latitudes between 171° - 200° longitude, both measured since the early 1980s; although the correlation had been previously positive, since 2000 the SST anomalies increased while tropopause temperatures decreased. A sharp increase in average SSTs within the Western Pacific warm pool by more than 0.25 °C in 2000, which has since stabilized, occurred as the "cold point" temperature of the study area at the tropopause experienced a significant reduction. This resulted in less water vapor from tropical thunderstorms entering the stratosphere. However, prior to 2000, increases in average Western Pacific SSTs had resulted in increases in tropopause cold point temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysical_systems">Geophysical systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Geophysical systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeochemical_cycles">Biogeochemical cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Biogeochemical cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a></div>
<p>Climate change can have an effect on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> in an interactive "feedback" process . A feedback exists where an initial process triggers changes in a second process that in turn influences the initial process. A <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> intensifies the original process, and a negative feedback reduces it (IPCC, 2007d:78).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4syn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4syn-1">[1]</a></sup> Models suggest that the interaction of the climate system and the carbon cycle is one where the feedback effect is positive (Schneider <i>et al</i>., 2007:792).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schneider_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-schneider-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using the A2 SRES emissions scenario, Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007:789) found that this effect led to additional warming by 2100, relative to the 1990–2000 period, of 0.1 to 1.5 °C. This estimate was made with high confidence. The climate projections made in the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report of 1.1 to 6.4 °C account for this feedback effect. On the other hand, with medium confidence, Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007) commented that additional releases of GHGs were possible from permafrost, peat lands, wetlands, and large stores of marine hydrates at high latitudes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_hydrates">Gas hydrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gas hydrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">Clathrate gun hypothesis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">Gas hydrates</a> are ice-like deposits containing a mixture of water and gas, the most common gas of which is methane (Maslin, 2004:1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Gas hydrates are stable under high pressures and at relatively low temperatures and are found underneath the oceans and permafrost regions. Future warming at intermediate depths in the world's oceans, as predicted by climate models, will tend to destabilize gas hydrates resulting in the release of large quantities of methane. On the other hand, projected rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea level rise</a> in the coming centuries associated with global warming will tend to stabilize marine gas hydrate deposits.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_cycle">Carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Models have been used to assess the effect that climate change will have on the carbon cycle (Meehl <i>et al</i>., 2007:789-790).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-meehl-65">[65]</a></sup> In the Coupled Climate-Carbon Cycle Model Intercomparison Project, eleven climate models were used. Observed emissions were used in the models and future emission projections were based on the IPCC SRES A2 emissions scenario.
</p><p>Unanimous agreement was found among the models that future climate change will reduce the efficiency of the land and ocean carbon cycle to absorb human-induced CO<sub>2</sub>. As a result, a larger fraction of human-induced CO<sub>2</sub> will stay airborne if climate change controls the carbon cycle. By the end of the 21st century, this additional CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere varied between 20 and 220 ppm for the two extreme models, with most models lying between 50 and 100 ppm. This additional CO<sub>2</sub> led to a projected increase in warming of between 0.1 and 1.5 °C.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryosphere">Cryosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Cryosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">Deglaciation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> average annual snow cover has declined in recent decades. This pattern is consistent with warmer global temperatures. Some of the largest declines have been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_ice">Sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="1440" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/220px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/330px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/97/SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg/440px-SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SeaIce-Sept162012_HR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Record low Arctic sea ice extent for September 2012</div></div></div>
<p>As the climate warms, snow cover and sea ice extent decrease. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_climate_warming_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_climate_warming-2">[2]</a></sup> Large-scale measurements of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea-ice</a> have only been possible since the satellite era, but through looking at a number of different satellite estimates, it has been determined that September <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> has decreased between 1973 and 2007 at a rate of about -10% +/- 0.3% per decade. Sea ice extent for September for 2012 was by far the lowest on record at 3.29 million square kilometers, eclipsing the previous record low sea ice extent of 2007 by 18%.  The age of the sea ice is also an important feature of the state of the sea ice cover, and for the month of March 2012, older ice (4 years and older) has decreased from 26% of the ice cover in 1988 to 7% in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>  Sea ice in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> has shown very little trend over the same period, or even a slight increase since 1979. Though extending the Antarctic sea-ice record back in time is more difficult due to the lack of direct observations in this part of the world.
</p><p>In a literature assessment, Meehl <i>et al</i>. (2007:750) found that model projections for the 21st century showed a reduction of sea ice in both the Arctic and Antarctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meehl_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-meehl-65">[65]</a></sup> The range of model responses was large. Projected reductions were accelerated in the Arctic. Using the high-emission A2 SRES scenario, some models projected that summer sea ice cover in the Arctic would disappear entirely by the latter part of the 21st century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacier_retreat_and_disappearance">Glacier retreat and disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Glacier retreat and disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Retreat of glaciers since 1850</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="650" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/220px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/330px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png/440px-Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacier_Mass_Balance_Map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of the change in thickness of mountain glaciers since 1970. Thinning in orange and red, thickening in blue.</div></div></div>
<p>Warming temperatures lead to the melting of glaciers and ice sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_faq_glaciers_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_faq_glaciers-68">[68]</a></sup> IPCC (2007a:5) found that, on average, mountain glaciers and snow cover had decreased in both the northern and southern hemispheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1-28">[28]</a></sup> This widespread decrease in glaciers and ice caps has contributed to observed sea level rise.
</p><p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_faq_glaciers_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_faq_glaciers-68">[68]</a></sup> As stated above, the total volume of glaciers on Earth is declining sharply. Glaciers have been retreating worldwide for at least the last century; the rate of retreat has increased in the past decade. Only a few glaciers are actually advancing (in locations that were well below freezing, and where increased precipitation has outpaced melting). The progressive disappearance of glaciers has implications not only for a rising global sea level, but also for <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supplies</a> in certain regions of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.
</p><p>With very high or high confidence, IPCC (2007d:11) made a number of projections related to future changes in glaciers:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4syn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ar4syn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Mountainous areas in Europe will face glacier retreat</li>
<li>In Latin America, changes in precipitation patterns and the disappearance of glaciers will significantly affect water availability for human consumption, agriculture, and energy production</li>
<li>In Polar regions, there will be reductions in glacier extent and the thickness of glaciers.</li></ul>
<p>In historic times, glaciers grew during a cool period from about 1550 to 1850 known as the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>. Subsequently, until about 1940, glaciers around the world retreated as the climate warmed. <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Glacier retreat</a> declined and reversed in many cases from 1950 to 1980 as a slight global cooling occurred. Since 1980, glacier retreat has become increasingly rapid and ubiquitous, and has threatened the existence of many of the glaciers of the world. This process has increased markedly since 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Excluding the <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> of the Arctic and Antarctic, the total surface area of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> worldwide has decreased by 50% since the end of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munichre_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-munichre-70">[70]</a></sup> Currently glacier retreat rates and mass balance losses have been increasing in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Cascades" title="North Cascades">North Cascades</a>.
</p><p>The loss of glaciers not only directly causes landslides, flash floods and <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">glacial lake</a> overflow,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> but also increases annual variation in water flows in rivers. Glacier runoff declines in the summer as glaciers decrease in size, this decline is already observable in several regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Glaciers retain water on mountains in high precipitation years, since the snow cover accumulating on glaciers protects the ice from melting. In warmer and drier years, glaciers offset the lower precipitation amounts with a higher meltwater input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-munichre_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-munichre-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some world regions, such as the French Alps, already show signs of an increase in landslide frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of particular importance are the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himalaya" title="Himalaya">Himalayan</a> glacial melts that comprise the principal dry-season water source of many of the major rivers of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asian</a> mainland. Increased melting would cause greater flow for several decades, after which "some areas of the most populated regions on Earth are likely to 'run out of water'" as source glaciers are depleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> contains the world's third-largest store of ice. Temperatures there are rising four times faster than in the rest of China, and glacial retreat is at a high speed compared to elsewhere in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a Reuters report, the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan</a> glaciers that are the sources of Asia's biggest rivers—<a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmaputra" title="Brahmaputra">Brahmaputra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salween" title="Salween">Salween</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow</a>—could diminish as temperatures rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Approximately 2.4 billion people live in the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> of the Himalayan rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> India, China, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> could experience floods followed by <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> in coming decades. The Indus, Ganges and Brahmaputra river basins support 700 million people in Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In India alone, the Ganges provides water for drinking and farming for more than 500 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> It has to be acknowledged, however, that increased seasonal runoff of Himalayan glaciers led to increased agricultural production in northern India throughout the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Research studies suggest that climate change will have a marked affect on meltwater in the Indus Basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>The recession of mountain glaciers, notably in Western North America, Franz-Josef Land, Asia, the Alps, the Pyrenees, Indonesia and Africa, and tropical and sub-tropical regions of South America, has been used to provide qualitative support to the rise in global temperatures since the late 19th century. Many glaciers are being lost to melting further raising concerns about future local water resources in these glaciated areas. In Western North America the 47 North Cascade glaciers observed all are retreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier,_Greenland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="868" data-file-width="540" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg/200px-Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg/300px-Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg/400px-Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier%2C_Greenland.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Retreat_of_the_Helheim_Glacier,_Greenland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Retreat of the Helheim Glacier, Greenland</div></div></div>
<p>Despite their proximity and importance to <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">human populations</a>, the mountain and valley glaciers of temperate latitudes amount to a small fraction of glacial ice on the earth. About 99% is in the great ice sheets of polar and subpolar Antarctica and Greenland. These continuous continental-scale ice sheets, 3 kilometres (1.9 mi) or more in thickness, cap the polar and subpolar land masses. Like rivers flowing from an enormous lake, numerous outlet glaciers transport ice from the margins of the ice sheet to the ocean.
</p><p>Glacier retreat has been observed in these outlet glaciers, resulting in an increase of the ice flow rate. In <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> the period since the year 2000 has brought retreat to several very large glaciers that had long been stable. Three glaciers that have been researched, <a href="/wiki/Helheim_Glacier" title="Helheim Glacier">Helheim</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakobshavn_Isbr%C3%A6" title="Jakobshavn Isbræ">Jakobshavn Isbræ</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kangerdlugssuaq_Glacier" title="Kangerdlugssuaq Glacier">Kangerdlugssuaq Glaciers</a>, jointly drain more than 16% of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenland_Ice_Sheet" title="Greenland Ice Sheet">Greenland Ice Sheet</a>. Satellite images and aerial photographs from the 1950s and 1970s show that the front of the glacier had remained in the same place for decades. But in 2001 it began retreating rapidly, retreating 7.2 km (4.5 mi) between 2001 and 2005. It has also accelerated from 20 m (66 ft)/day to 32 m (105 ft)/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Jakobshavn Isbræ in western Greenland had been moving at speeds of over 24 m (79 ft)/day with a stable terminus since at least 1950. The glacier's ice tongue began to break apart in 2000, leading to almost complete disintegration in 2003, while the retreat rate increased to over 30 m (98 ft)/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The role of the oceans in global warming is a complex one. The oceans serve as a sink for carbon dioxide, taking up much that would otherwise remain in the atmosphere, but increased levels of CO<sub>2</sub> have led to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>. Furthermore, as the temperature of the oceans increases, they become less able to absorb excess CO<sub>2</sub>. Global warming is projected to have a number of effects on the oceans. Ongoing effects include rising sea levels due to thermal expansion and melting of glaciers and ice sheets, and warming of the ocean surface, leading to increased temperature stratification. Other possible effects include large-scale changes in ocean circulation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level_rise">Sea level rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sea level rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">Current sea level rise</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Amundsen_Sea" title="Amundsen Sea">Amundsen Sea</a></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:204px;max-width:204px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holocene_Sea_Level.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="512" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Holocene_Sea_Level.png/200px-Holocene_Sea_Level.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Holocene_Sea_Level.png/300px-Holocene_Sea_Level.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Holocene_Sea_Level.png/400px-Holocene_Sea_Level.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Sea level rise during the Holocene.</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="373" data-file-width="537" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/200px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/300px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/400px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Sea level has been rising 0.2 cm/year, based on measurements of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> from 23 long <a href="/wiki/Tide_gauge" title="Tide gauge">tide gauge</a> records in geologically stable environments.</div></div></div></div>
<p>IPCC (2007a:5) reported that since 1961, global average sea level had risen at an average rate of 1.8 [1.3 to 2.3] mm/yr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1-28">[28]</a></sup> Between 1993 and 2003, the rate increased above the previous period to 3.1 [2.4 to 3.8] mm/yr. IPCC (2007a) were uncertain whether the increase in rate from 1993 to 2003 was due to natural variations in sea level over the time period, or whether it reflected an increase in the underlying long-term trend.
</p><p>IPCC (2007a:13, 14) projected sea level rise to the end of the 21st century using the <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a> emission <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming#Scenarios" title="Economics of global warming">scenarios</a>. Across the six SRES marker scenarios, sea level was projected to rise by 18 to 59 cm (7.1 to 23.2 inches). This projection was for the time period 2090–2099, with the increase in level relative to average sea levels over the 1980–1999 period. Due to a lack of scientific understanding, this sea level rise estimate does not include all of the possible contributions of ice sheets.
</p><p>With increasing average global temperature, the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in the oceans expands in volume, and additional water enters them which had previously been locked up on land in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciers" title="Glaciers">glaciers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sheets" title="Ice sheets">ice sheets</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheets</a> are major ice masses, and at least the former of which may suffer irreversible decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> For most glaciers worldwide, an average volume loss of 60% until 2050 is predicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneeberger2993_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schneeberger2993-88">[88]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the estimated total ice melting rate over Greenland is 239 ± 23 cubic kilometres (57.3 ± 5.5 cu mi) per year, mostly from East Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chen2006_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chen2006-89">[89]</a></sup> The Antarctic ice sheet, however, is expected to grow during the 21st century because of increased precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1ch5_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1ch5-90">[90]</a></sup> Under the IPCC Special Report on Emission Scenario (SRES) A1B, by the mid-2090s global sea level will reach 0.22 to 0.44 m (8.7 to 17.3 in) above 1990 levels, and is currently rising at about 4 mm (0.16 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1ch5_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1ch5-90">[90]</a></sup> Since 1900, the sea level has risen at an average of 1.7 mm (0.067 in) per year;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1ch5_90-2"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1ch5-90">[90]</a></sup> since 1993, <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> altimetry from <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">TOPEX/Poseidon</a> indicates a rate of about 3 mm (0.12 in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1ch5_90-3"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1ch5-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sea level has risen more than 120 metres (390 ft) since the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a> about 20,000 years ago. The bulk of that occurred before 7000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleming1998_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fleming1998-91">[91]</a></sup> Global temperature declined after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocene_Climatic_Optimum" title="Holocene Climatic Optimum">Holocene Climatic Optimum</a>, causing a sea level lowering of 0.7 ± 0.1 m (27.6 ± 3.9 in) between 4000 and 2500 years before present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodwin1998_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodwin1998-92">[92]</a></sup> From 3000 years ago to the start of the 19th century, sea level was almost constant, with only minor fluctuations. However, the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a> may have caused some sea level rise; evidence has been found in the Pacific Ocean for a rise to perhaps 0.9 m (2 ft 11 in) above present level in 700 BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a paper published in 2007, the climatologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a> <i>et al.</i> claimed that ice at the poles does not melt in a gradual and linear fashion, but that another according to the geological record, the <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> can suddenly destabilize when a certain threshold is exceeded. In this paper Hansen <i>et al.</i> state:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Our concern that BAU GHG scenarios would cause large sealevel rise this century (Hansen 2005) differs from estimates of IPCC (2001, 2007), which foresees little or no contribution to twentyfirst century sealevel rise from Greenland and Antarctica. However, the IPCC analyses and projections do not well account for the nonlinear physics of wet ice sheet disintegration, ice streams and eroding ice shelves, nor are they consistent with the palaeoclimate evidence we have presented for the absence of discernible lag between ice sheet forcing and sealevel rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Sea level rise due to the collapse of an ice sheet would be distributed nonuniformly across the globe. The loss of mass in the region around the ice sheet would decrease the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential</a> there, reducing the amount of local sea level rise or even causing local sea level fall. The loss of the localized mass would also change the <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moment of inertia</a> of the Earth, as flow in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">Earth's mantle</a> will require  10–15 thousand years to make up the mass deficit. This change in the moment of inertia results in <a href="/wiki/True_polar_wander" title="True polar wander">true polar wander</a>, in which the Earth's rotational axis remains fixed with respect to the sun, but the rigid sphere of the Earth rotates with respect to it. This changes the location of the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">equatorial bulge</a> of the Earth and further affects the <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a>, or global potential field. A 2009 study of the effects of collapse of the <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_Ice_Sheet" title="West Antarctic Ice Sheet">West Antarctic Ice Sheet</a> shows the result of both of these effects. Instead of a global 5-meter sea level rise, western Antarctica would experience approximately 25 centimeters of sea level fall, while the United States, parts of Canada, and the Indian Ocean, would experience up to 6.5 meters of sea level rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>A paper published in 2008 by a group of researchers at the University of Wisconsin led by Anders Carlson used the deglaciation of North America at 9000 years before present as an analogue to predict sea level rise of 1.3 meters in the next century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> which is also much higher than the IPCC projections. However, models of glacial flow in the smaller present-day ice sheets show that a probable maximum value for sea level rise in the next century is 80 centimeters, based on limitations on how quickly ice can flow below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_line_altitude" title="Equilibrium line altitude">equilibrium line altitude</a> and to the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_rise_and_ocean_heat_content">Temperature rise and ocean heat content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Temperature rise and ocean heat content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evidence_of_global_warming_-_time_series_of_seasonal_(red_dots)_and_annual_average_(black_line)_of_global_upper_ocean_heat_content_for_the_0-700m_layer_between_1955-2008.gif"><img alt="Refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="800" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Evidence_of_global_warming_-_time_series_of_seasonal_%28red_dots%29_and_annual_average_%28black_line%29_of_global_upper_ocean_heat_content_for_the_0-700m_layer_between_1955-2008.gif/220px-thumbnail.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Evidence_of_global_warming_-_time_series_of_seasonal_%28red_dots%29_and_annual_average_%28black_line%29_of_global_upper_ocean_heat_content_for_the_0-700m_layer_between_1955-2008.gif/330px-thumbnail.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Evidence_of_global_warming_-_time_series_of_seasonal_%28red_dots%29_and_annual_average_%28black_line%29_of_global_upper_ocean_heat_content_for_the_0-700m_layer_between_1955-2008.gif/440px-thumbnail.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evidence_of_global_warming_-_time_series_of_seasonal_(red_dots)_and_annual_average_(black_line)_of_global_upper_ocean_heat_content_for_the_0-700m_layer_between_1955-2008.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">Time series</a> of seasonal (red dots) and annual average (black line) global upper ocean heat content for the 0-700m layer between 1955 and 2008. The graph shows that ocean heat content has increased over this time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content-99">[99]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>From 1961 to 2003, the global ocean temperature has risen by 0.10 °C from the surface to a depth of 700 m. There is variability both year-to-year and over longer time scales, with global ocean heat content observations showing high rates of warming for 1991 to 2003, but some cooling from 2003 to 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg1ch5_90-4"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg1ch5-90">[90]</a></sup> Nevertheless, there is a strong trend during the period of reliable measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content-99">[99]</a></sup> Increasing heat content in the ocean is also consistent with sea level rise, which is occurring mostly as a result of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> of the ocean water as it warms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content_99-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_faq_ocean_heat_content-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>The temperature of the Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> rose by 0.17 °C (0.31 °F) between the 1950s and the 1980s, nearly twice the rate for the world's oceans as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> As well as having effects on ecosystems (e.g. by melting sea ice, affecting algae that grow on its underside), warming reduces the ocean's ability to absorb CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidification">Acidification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Acidification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>
<p>Ocean acidification is an effect of rising concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> in the atmosphere, and is not a direct consequence of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. The oceans soak up much of the CO<sub>2</sub> produced by living organisms, either as dissolved gas, or in the skeletons of tiny marine creatures that fall to the bottom to become chalk or limestone. Oceans currently absorb about one tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> per person per year. It is estimated that the oceans have absorbed around half of all CO<sub>2</sub> generated by human activities since 1800 (118 ± 19 petagrams of carbon from 1800 to 1994).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sabine2004_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sabine2004-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>In water, CO<sub>2</sub> becomes a weak <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, and the increase in the greenhouse gas since the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> has already lowered the average <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> (the laboratory measure of acidity) of seawater by 0.1 units, to 8.2. Predicted emissions could lower the pH by a further 0.5 by 2100, to a level probably not seen for hundreds of millennia and, critically, at a rate of change probably 100 times greater than at any time over this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are concerns that increasing acidification could have a particularly detrimental effect on <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> (16% of the world's coral reefs have died from bleaching caused by warm water in 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walther2002_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walther2002-105">[105]</a></sup> which coincidentally was, at the time, the warmest year ever recorded) and other marine organisms with <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> shells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2009 an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> by scientists at <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Fisheries_and_Oceans" title="Department of Fisheries and Oceans">Department of Fisheries and Oceans</a> reported they had found very low levels of the building blocks for the calcium chloride that forms <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> shells in the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Sea" title="Beaufort Sea">Beaufort Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canwest2009-11-20_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canwest2009-11-20-107">[107]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Fiona_McLaughlin" title="Fiona McLaughlin">Fiona McLaughlin</a>, one of the DFO authors, asserted that the increasing acidification of the Arctic Ocean was close to the point it would start dissolving the walls of existing plankton: <i>"[the] Arctic ecosystem may be risk. In actual fact, they'll dissolve the shells."</i>
Because cold water absorbs CO<sub>2</sub> more readily than warmer water the acidification is more severe in the polar regions.  McLaughlin predicted the acidified water would travel to the North Atlantic within the next ten years.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a></div>
<p>There is some speculation that global warming could, via a shutdown or slowdown of the thermohaline circulation, trigger localized cooling in the North Atlantic and lead to cooling, or lesser warming, in that region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenton2008_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenton2008-108">[108]</a></sup> This would affect in particular areas like <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> that are warmed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a>.
</p><p>The chances of this near-term collapse of the circulation are unclear; there is some evidence for the short-term stability of the Gulf Stream and possible weakening of the North Atlantic drift.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the degree of weakening, and whether it will be sufficient to shut down the circulation, is under debate. As yet, no cooling has been found in northern Europe or nearby seas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Lenton et al. found that "simulations clearly pass a THC tipping point this century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenton2008_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lenton2008-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p><p>IPCC (2007b:17) concluded that a slowing of the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> would very likely occur this century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4wg2_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4wg2-109">[109]</a></sup> Due to global warming, temperatures across the Atlantic and Europe were still projected to increase.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_depletion">Oxygen depletion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Oxygen depletion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The amount of oxygen dissolved in the oceans may decline, with adverse consequences for ocean life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfur_aerosols">Sulfur aerosols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Sulfur aerosols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(March 2010)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfur_aerosols" title="Stratospheric sulfur aerosols">stratospheric sulfur aerosols</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a></div>
<p>Sulfur aerosols, especially <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfur_aerosols" title="Stratospheric sulfur aerosols">stratospheric sulfur aerosols</a> have a significant effect on climate. One source of such <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> release gases such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">DMS</a> which eventually becomes <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidised</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> in the atmosphere. Disruption to the oceans as a result of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> or disruptions to the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> may result in disruption of the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>, thus reducing its cooling effect on the planet through the creation of <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfur_aerosols" title="Stratospheric sulfur aerosols">stratospheric sulfur aerosols</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Volcanoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">Post-glacial rebound</a></div>
<p>The retreat of glaciers and ice caps can cause increased <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>. Reduction in ice cover reduces the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confining_pressure" title="Confining pressure">confining pressure</a> exerted on the volcano, increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deviatoric_stress" title="Deviatoric stress">deviatoric stresses</a> and potentially causing the volcano to erupt. This reduction of pressure can also cause <a href="/wiki/Magma#Partial_melting" title="Magma">decompression melting</a> of material in the mantle, resulting in the generation of more magma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Researchers in Iceland have shown that the rate of volcanic rock production there following deglaciation (10,000 to 4500 years <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_present" title="Before present">before present</a>) was 20–30 times greater than that observed after 2900 years before present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> While the original study addresses the first reason for increased volcanism (reduced confining pressure), scientists have more recently shown that these lavas have unusually high <a href="/wiki/Trace_element" title="Trace element">trace element</a> concentrations, indicative of increased melting in the mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> This work in Iceland has been corroborated by a study in California, in which scientists found a strong correlation between volcanism and periods of global deglaciation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> The effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">current sea level rise</a> could include increased <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crustal</a> stress at the base of coastal volcanoes from a rise in the volcano's <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> (and the associated <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_intrusion" title="Saltwater intrusion">saltwater intrusion</a>), while the mass from extra water could activate dormant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic_fault" title="Seismic fault">seismic faults</a> around volcanoes. In addition, the wide-scale displacement of water from melting in places such as <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctica" title="West Antarctica">West Antarctica</a> is likely to slightly alter the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_period" title="Rotational period">rotational period</a> and may shift its <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> on the scale of hundreds of metres, inducing further crustal stress changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Current melting of ice is predicted to increase the size and frequency of volcanic eruptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_volc_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-rs_volc-118">[118]</a></sup> In particular, lateral collapse events at <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcanoes</a> are likely to increase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_volc_118-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs_volc-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> and there are potential positive feedbacks between the removal of ice and magmatism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_volc_118-2"><a href="#cite_note-rs_volc-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">Earthquakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_impacts_of_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_model" title="Numerical model">numerical modeling</a> study has demonstrated that <a href="/wiki/Seismicity" title="Seismicity">seismicity</a> increases during unloading, such as that due to the removal of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p>
