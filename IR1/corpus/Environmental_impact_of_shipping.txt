<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of shipping</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ship_pumping_ballast_water.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="179" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a4/Ship_pumping_ballast_water.jpg" width="179"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ship_pumping_ballast_water.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cargo ship discharging ballast water into the sea.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of shipping</b> includes <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_shipping#Sound_pollution" title="Environmental impact of shipping">acoustic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_pollution" title="Oil pollution">oil pollution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Maritime_Organization" title="International Maritime Organization">International Maritime Organization</a> (IMO) estimates that <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> emissions from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">shipping</a> were equal to 2.2% of the global human-made emissions in 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and expects them to rise 50 to 250 percent by 2050 if no action is taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The First Intersessional Meeting of the IMO Working Group on Greenhouse Gas Emissions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> from Ships took place in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway on 23–27 June 2008. It was tasked with developing the technical basis for the reduction mechanisms that may form part of a future IMO regime to control greenhouse gas emissions from international shipping, and a draft of the actual reduction mechanisms themselves, for further consideration by IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballast_water">Ballast water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ballast water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ballast_water_discharge_and_the_environment" title="Ballast water discharge and the environment">Ballast water discharge and the environment</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballast_water" title="Ballast water">Ballast water</a> discharges by ships can have a negative impact on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_environment" title="Marine environment">marine environment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cruise ships, large tankers, and bulk cargo carriers use a huge amount of ballast water, which is often taken on in the coastal waters in one region after ships discharge <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> or unload cargo, and discharged at the next port of call, wherever more cargo is loaded. Ballast water discharge typically contains a variety of biological materials, including <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>. These materials often include non-native, nuisance, invasive, exotic species that can cause extensive ecological and economic damage to aquatic ecosystems along with serious human health problems.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound_pollution">Sound pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sound pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Noise pollution caused by shipping and other human enterprises has increased in <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_history" title="Contemporary history">recent history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The noise produced by ships can travel long distances, and marine species who may rely on sound for their orientation, communication, and feeding, can be harmed by this sound pollution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonn_Convention" title="Bonn Convention">Convention on the Conservation of Migratory Species</a> has identified ocean noise as a potential threat to marine life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
The disruption of whales' ability to communicate with one another is an extreme threat and is affecting their ability to survive. According to Discovery Channel's article on Sonic Sea Journeys Deep Into the Ocean,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> over the last century, extremely loud noise from commercial ships, oil and gas exploration, naval sonar exercises and other sources has transformed the ocean's delicate acoustic habitat, challenging the ability of whales and other marine life to prosper and ultimately to survive. Whales are starting to react to this in ways that are life-threatening. Kenneth C. Balcomb, a whale researcher and a former U.S Navy officer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> states that the day March 15, 2000, is the day of infamy. As Discovery says,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> where him and his crew discovered whales swimming dangerously close to the shore. They're supposed to be in deep water.  So I pushed it back out to sea, says Balcomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Although sonar helps to protect us, it is destroying marine life. According to IFAW Animal Rescue Program Director Katie Moore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> "There's different ways that sounds can affect animals. There's that underlying ambient noise level that's rising, and rising, and rising that interferes with communication and their movement patterns. And then there's the more acute kind of traumatic impact of sound, that's causing physical damage or a really strong behavioral response. It's fight or flight".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_collisions">Wildlife collisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wildlife collisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">Marine mammals</a>, such as whales and manatees, risk being struck by ships, causing injury and death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, if a ship is traveling at a speed of only 15 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a>, there is a 79 percent chance of a collision being lethal to a whale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>One notable example of the impact of ship collisions is the endangered <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_right_whale" title="North Atlantic right whale">North Atlantic right whale</a>, of which 400 or less remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TaylorWalker2017_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-TaylorWalker2017-16">[16]</a></sup> The greatest danger to the North Atlantic right whale is injury sustained from ship strikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-15">[15]</a></sup> Between 1970 and 1999, 35.5 percent of recorded deaths were attributed to collisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> During 1999 to 2003, incidents of mortality and serious injury attributed to ship strikes averaged one per year. In 2004 to 2006, that number increased to 2.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Deaths from collisions has become an extinction threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-19">[19]</a></sup> The United States' <a href="/wiki/National_Marine_Fisheries_Service" title="National Marine Fisheries Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</a> (NMFS) and <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) introduced <a href="/wiki/Vessel_speed_restrictions_to_reduce_ship_collisions_with_North_Atlantic_right_whales" title="Vessel speed restrictions to reduce ship collisions with North Atlantic right whales">vessel speed restrictions to reduce ship collisions with North Atlantic right whales</a> in 2008, which expired in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, in 2017 an unprecedented mortality event occurred, resulting in the deaths of 17 North Atlantic Right Whales caused primarily from ship-strikes and entanglement in fishing gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TaylorWalker2017_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-TaylorWalker2017-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_pollution">Atmospheric pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atmospheric pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">Exhaust gases</a> from ships are considered to be a significant source of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, both for conventional pollutants and greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a perception that cargo transport by ship is low in air pollutants, because for equal weight and distance it is the most efficient transport method, according to shipping researcher Alice Bows-Larkin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bows_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bows-21">[21]</a></sup> This is particularly true in comparison to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">air freight</a>; however, because sea shipment accounts for far more annual tonnage and the distances are often large, shipping's emissions are globally substantial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bows_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-bows-21">[21]</a></sup> A difficulty is that the year-on-year increasing amount shipping overwhelms gains in efficiency, such as from <a href="/wiki/Slow_steaming" title="Slow steaming">slow-steaming</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Kite_applications#Cargo" title="Kite applications">use of kites</a>. The growth in tonne-kilometers of sea shipment has averaged 4 percent yearly since the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyndall_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyndall-23">[23]</a></sup> And it has grown by a factor of 5 since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bows_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-bows-21">[21]</a></sup> There are now over 100,000 transport ships at sea, of which about 6,000 are large container ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bows_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-bows-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventional_pollutants">Conventional pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conventional pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a> from cruise ships is generated by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a> that burn high <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> content <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, also known as bunker oil, producing <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate</a>, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and hydrocarbons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">Diesel exhaust</a> has been classified by EPA as a likely human <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>. EPA recognizes that these emissions from marine diesel engines contribute to <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> and carbon monoxide nonattainment (i.e., failure to meet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> standards), as well as adverse health effects associated with ambient concentrations of particulate matter and visibility, <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid deposition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrification" title="Nitrification">nitrification</a> of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68FR9751_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-68FR9751-24">[24]</a></sup> EPA estimates that large marine diesel engines accounted for about 1.6 percent of mobile source nitrogen oxide emissions and 2.8 percent of mobile source particulate emissions in the United States in 2000. Contributions of marine diesel engines can be higher on a port-specific basis. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-low_sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low sulfur diesel">Ultra-low sulfur diesel</a> (ULSD) is a standard for defining <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a> with substantially lowered <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> contents.  As of 2006, almost all of the petroleum-based diesel fuel available in Europe and North America is of a ULSD type.
</p><p>In 2016 the IMO has made new sulfur regulations which must be implemented by larger ships by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of total global air emissions, shipping accounts for 18 to 30 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a> and 9 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulphur oxides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrooten1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schrooten1-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vidal2009_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-vidal2009-27">[27]</a></sup> Sulfur in the air creates <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> which damages crops and buildings. When inhaled, sulfur is known to cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory" title="Respiratory">respiratory</a> problems and even increases the risk of a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrabin_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrabin-28">[28]</a></sup> According to Irene Blooming, a spokeswoman for the European environmental coalition <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seas_at_Risk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seas at Risk (page does not exist)">Seas at Risk</a>, the fuel used in oil tankers and container ships is high in sulfur and cheaper to buy compared to the fuel used for domestic land use. "A ship lets out around 50 times more sulfur than a lorry per metric tonne of cargo carried."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrabin_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrabin-28">[28]</a></sup> Cities in the U.S. like <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston" title="Galveston">Galveston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> see some of the heaviest shipping traffic in the nation and have left local officials desperately trying to clean up the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-29">[29]</a></sup> Increasing trade between the U.S. and China is helping to increase the number of vessels navigating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> and exacerbating many of the environmental problems. To maintain the level of growth China is experiencing, large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> are being shipped to China by the boat load. The number of voyages are expected to continue increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_pollutants">Greenhouse gas pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>3.5 to 4 percent of all <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change emissions</a> are caused by shipping, primarily carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vidal2009_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-vidal2009-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>As one way to reduce the impact of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions from shipping, vetting agency RightShip developed an online "Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Emissions Rating" as a systematic way for the industry to compare a ship's CO<sub>2</sub> emissions with peer vessels of a similar size and type. Based on the International Maritime Organisation's (IMO) Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) that applies to ships built from 2013, RightShip's GHG Rating can also be applied to vessels built prior to 2013, allowing for effective vessel comparison across the world's fleet. The GHG Rating utilises an A to G scale, where A represents the most efficient ships. It measures the theoretical amount of carbon dioxide emitted per tonne nautical mile travelled, based on the design characteristics of the ship at time of build such as cargo carrying capacity, engine power and fuel consumption. Higher rated ships can deliver significantly lower CO<sub>2</sub> emissions across the voyage length, which means they also use less fuel and are cheaper to run.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress_for_improvement">Stress for improvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stress for improvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One source of environmental stresses on maritime vessels recently has come from states and localities, as they assess the contribution of commercial marine vessels to regional air quality problems when ships are docked at port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrooten2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schrooten2-31">[31]</a></sup>
For instance, large marine diesel engines are believed to contribute 7 percent of mobile source nitrogen oxide emissions in <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">Baton Rouge</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a>. Ships can also have a significant impact in areas without large commercial ports: they contribute about 37 percent of total area nitrogen oxide emissions in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a> area, and that percentage is expected to increase to 61 percent by 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68FR9751_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-68FR9751-24">[24]</a></sup> Again, there is little cruise-industry specific data on this issue. They comprise only a small fraction of the world shipping fleet, but cruise ship emissions may exert significant impacts on a local scale in specific coastal areas that are visited repeatedly. Shipboard incinerators also burn large volumes of garbage, plastics, and other <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>, producing ash that must be disposed of. Incinerators may release toxic emissions as well.
</p><p>In 2005, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MARPOL" title="MARPOL">MARPOL</a> Annex VI came into force to combat this problem. As such cruise ships now employ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCTV" title="CCTV">CCTV</a> monitoring on the smokestacks as well as recorded measuring via opacity meter while some are also using clean burning gas turbines for electrical loads and propulsion in sensitive areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_spills">Oil spills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oil spills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most commonly associated with ship pollution are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_spills" title="Oil spills">oil spills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> While less frequent than the pollution that occurs from daily operations, oil spills have devastating effects. While being toxic to marine life, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs), the components in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, are very difficult to clean up, and last for years in the <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> and marine environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panetta_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Panetta-32">[32]</a></sup> Marine species constantly exposed to PAHs can exhibit developmental problems, susceptibility to disease, and abnormal reproductive cycles. One of the more widely known spills was the <a href="/wiki/Exxon_Valdez" title="Exxon Valdez">Exxon Valdez</a> incident in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. The ship ran aground and dumped a massive amount of oil into the ocean in March 1989. Despite efforts of scientists, managers and volunteers, over 400,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seabirds" title="Seabirds">seabirds</a>, about 1,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_otters" title="Sea otters">sea otters</a>, and immense numbers of fish were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panetta_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Panetta-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_regulation">International regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the major international efforts in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaties" title="Treaties">treaties</a> are the Marine Pollution Treaty, Honolulu, which deals with regulating marine pollution from ships, and the UN Convention on Law of the Sea, which deals with marine species and pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> While plenty of local and international regulations have been introduced throughout <a href="/wiki/Maritime_history" title="Maritime history">maritime history</a>, much of the current regulations are considered inadequate. "In general, the treaties tend to emphasize the technical features of safety and pollution control measures without going to the root causes of sub-standard shipping, the absence of incentives for compliance and the lack of enforceability of measures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The most common problems encountered with international shipping arise from paperwork errors and customs brokers not having the proper information about your items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Cruise ships, for example, are exempt from regulation under the US discharge permit system (NPDES, under the Clean Water Act) that requires compliance with technology-based standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panetta_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Panetta-32">[32]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, many ports lack proper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">waste disposal</a> facilities, and many ships dump their waste at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Moreover, due to the complexities of shipping trade and the difficulties involved in regulating this business, a comprehensive and generally acceptable regulatory framework on corporate responsibility for reducing GHG emissions is unlikely to be achieved soon. In fact, emissions are continuing to increase. Under these circumstances, it is necessary for the states, the shipping industry and global organizations to explore and discuss market based mechanisms for vessel-sourced GHG emissions reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sewage">Sewage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sewage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Smelly_whale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Smelly_whale.jpg/220px-Smelly_whale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Smelly_whale.jpg/330px-Smelly_whale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Smelly_whale.jpg/440px-Smelly_whale.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Smelly_whale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carcass of a whale on a shore in Iceland.</div></div></div>
<p>The cruise line industry dumps 255,000 US gallons (970 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a> and 30,000 US gallons (110 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_(waste)" title="Blackwater (waste)">blackwater</a> into the sea every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> Blackwater is sewage, <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> from <a href="/wiki/Toilet" title="Toilet">toilets</a> and medical facilities, which can contain harmful <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, pathogens, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestinal_parasite" title="Intestinal parasite">intestinal parasites</a>, and harmful nutrients. Discharges of untreated or inadequately treated sewage can cause bacterial and viral <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">contamination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> beds, producing risks to public health. Nutrients in sewage, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, promote excessive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algal_blooms" title="Algal blooms">algal blooms</a>, which consumes <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the water and can lead to <a href="/wiki/Fish_kill" title="Fish kill">fish kills</a> and destruction of other aquatic life. A large cruise ship (3,000 passengers and crew) generates an estimated 55,000 to 110,000 liters per day of blackwater waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cruisecontrol_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-cruisecontrol-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the environmental impact of shipping, and sewage in particular <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">marpol</a> annex IV was brought into force September 2003 strictly limiting untreated waste discharge. Modern cruise ships are most commonly installed with a <a href="/wiki/Membrane_bioreactor" title="Membrane bioreactor">membrane bioreactor</a> type treatment plant for all blackwater and greywater, such as (<a class="external free" href="http://www.gertsen-olufsen.com/Ship-Offshore/Products/G-O_Brands/G-O_Bioreactor.aspx" rel="nofollow">http://www.gertsen-olufsen.com/Ship-Offshore/Products/G-O_Brands/G-O_Bioreactor.aspx</a>), Zenon or <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110715193114/http://www.rochem.com/htm/Rochem_Ultrafiltration.htm" rel="nofollow">Rochem</a> which produce near drinkable quality effluent to be re-used in the machinery spaces as technical water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning">Cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greywater is wastewater from the <a href="/wiki/Sink" title="Sink">sinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shower" title="Shower">showers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galley_(kitchen)" title="Galley (kitchen)">galleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laundry" title="Laundry">laundry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cleanliness" title="Cleanliness">cleaning</a> activities aboard a ship. It can contain a variety of pollutant substances, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal_coliforms" title="Fecal coliforms">fecal coliforms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> and grease, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, nutrients, <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">medical</a> and dental waste. Sampling done by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a> and the state of Alaska found that untreated greywater from cruise ships can contain pollutants at variable strengths and that it can contain levels of fecal coliform bacteria several times greater than is typically found in untreated domestic wastewater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Greywater has potential to cause adverse environmental effects because of concentrations of nutrients and other <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_oxygen_demand" title="Biochemical oxygen demand">oxygen-demanding</a> materials, in particular. Greywater is typically the largest source of liquid waste generated by cruise ships (90 to 95 percent of the total). Estimates of greywater range from 110 to 320 liters per day per person, or 330,000 to 960,000 liters per day for a 3,000-person cruise ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_waste">Solid waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solid waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid waste generated on a ship includes <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, cardboard, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> cans, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> It can be either non-hazardous or hazardous in nature. Solid waste that enters the ocean may become <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a>, and can then pose a threat to marine organisms, humans, coastal communities, and industries that utilize marine waters. Cruise ships typically manage solid waste by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Source_reduction" title="Source reduction">source reduction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_minimization" title="Waste minimization">waste minimization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>. However, as much as 75 percent of solid waste is <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incinerated</a> on board, and the <a href="/wiki/Incinerator_bottom_ash" title="Incinerator bottom ash">ash</a> typically is discharged at sea, although some is landed ashore for disposal or recycling. <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">Marine mammals</a>, fish, <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, and birds can be injured or killed from entanglement with plastics and other solid waste that may be released or disposed off of cruise ships. On average, each cruise ship passenger generates at least two pounds of non-hazardous solid waste per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> With large cruise ships carrying several thousand passengers, the amount of waste generated in a day can be massive. For a large cruise ship, about 8 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of solid waste are generated during a one-week cruise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It has been estimated that 24 percent of the solid waste generated by vessels worldwide (by weight) comes from cruise ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Most cruise ship garbage is treated on board (incinerated, pulped, or ground up) for discharge overboard. When garbage must be off-loaded (for example, because glass and aluminium cannot be incinerated), cruise ships can put a strain on port reception facilities, which are rarely adequate to the task of serving a large passenger vessel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bilge_water">Bilge water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_shipping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bilge water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On a ship, oil often leaks from <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machinery</a> spaces or from engine maintenance activities and mixes with water in the <a href="/wiki/Bilge" title="Bilge">bilge</a>, the lowest part of the <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a> of the ship, but there is a filter to clean bilge water before being discharged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2019_Environmental_Effects_of_Marine_Transportation-1">[1]</a></sup> Oil, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, and <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a> from the biological breakdown of petroleum products can harm fish and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> and pose threats to human health if ingested. Oil in even minute concentrations can kill fish or have various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-lethal_damage" title="Sub-lethal damage">sub-lethal</a> chronic effects. Bilge water also may contain solid wastes and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollutants</a> containing high levels of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_oxygen_demand" title="Biological oxygen demand">oxygen-demanding</a> material, oil and other chemicals. A typically large cruise ship will generate an average of 8 metric tons of oily bilge water for each 24 hours of operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> To maintain ship stability and eliminate potentially hazardous conditions from oil <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapors</a> in these areas, the bilge spaces need to be flushed and periodically pumped dry. However, before a bilge can be cleared out and the water discharged, the oil that has been accumulated needs to be extracted from the bilge water, after which the extracted oil can be reused, incinerated, and/or offloaded in port. If a separator, which is normally used to extract the oil, is faulty or is deliberately bypassed, untreated oily bilge water could be discharged directly into the ocean, where it can damage marine life. A number of cruise lines have been charged with <a href="/wiki/Environmental_crime" title="Environmental crime">environmental violations</a> related to this issue in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
