<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Aqueous solution</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Aqueous" redirects here. For the fluid in the eye, see <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_humour" title="Aqueous humour">Aqueous humour</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Na%2BH2O.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Na%2BH2O.svg/220px-Na%2BH2O.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Na%2BH2O.svg/330px-Na%2BH2O.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Na%2BH2O.svg/440px-Na%2BH2O.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Na%2BH2O.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first <a href="/wiki/Solvation_shell" title="Solvation shell">solvation shell</a> of a sodium ion dissolved in water.</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>aqueous solution</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. It is mostly shown in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equation" title="Chemical equation">chemical equations</a> by appending (aq) to the relevant <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a>. For example, a solution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_salt" title="Table salt">table salt</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> (NaCl), in water would be represented as Na<sup>+</sup>(aq) + Cl<sup>−</sup>(aq). The word <i>aqueous</i> (comes from <i>aqua</i>) means pertaining to, related to, similar to, or <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolved</a> in, water. As water is an excellent solvent and is also naturally abundant, it is a ubiquitous  solvent in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. Aqueous solution is water with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 7.0 where the hydrogen ions (H<sup>+</sup>) and hydroxide ions (OH<sup>-</sup>) are in <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction#Arrhenius_theory" title="Acid–base reaction">Arrhenius</a> balance (10<sup>-7</sup>).
</p><p>A <i>non-aqueous solution</i> is a solution in which the solvent is a liquid, but is not water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Substances that are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a></i> ('water-fearing') often do not dissolve well in water, whereas those that are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a></i> ('water-friendly') do. An example of a hydrophilic substance is sodium chloride. <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">Acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> are aqueous solutions, as part of their <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction#Arrhenius_Definition" title="Acid–base reaction">Arrhenius definitions</a>.
</p><p>The ability of a substance to dissolve in water is determined by whether the substance can match or exceed the strong <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force#Dipole-dipole_interactions" title="Intermolecular force">attractive forces</a> that water molecules generate between themselves. If the substance lacks the ability to dissolve in water the molecules form a <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a>.
</p><p>Reactions in aqueous solutions are usually metathesis reactions. Metathesis reactions are another term for double-displacement; that is, when a cation displaces to form an ionic bond with the other anion. The cation bonded with the latter anion will dissociate and bond with the other anion.
</p><p>Aqueous solutions that conduct <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> efficiently contain strong <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>, while ones that conduct poorly are considered to have weak electrolytes. Those strong electrolytes are substances that are completely <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionized</a> in water, whereas the weak electrolytes exhibit only a small degree of ionization in water.
</p><p>Nonelectrolytes are substances that dissolve in water yet maintain their molecular integrity (do not dissociate into ions). Examples include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methylsulfonylmethane" title="Methylsulfonylmethane">methylsulfonylmethane</a> (MSM).
</p><p>When writing the equations of aqueous reactions, it is essential to determine the precipitate. To determine the precipitate, one must consult a chart of solubility. Soluble compounds are aqueous, while insoluble compounds are the precipitate.  There may not always be a precipitate.
</p><p>When performing calculations regarding the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">reacting</a> of one or more aqueous solutions, in general one must know the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molarity" title="Molarity">molarity</a>, of the aqueous solutions. Solution concentration is given in terms of the form of the solute prior to it dissolving.
</p><p>Aqueous solutions may contain, especially in alcaline zone or subjected to radiolysis, hydrated atomic hydrogen an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated_electron" title="Hydrated electron">hydrated electron</a>.
</p>
