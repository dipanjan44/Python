<p><b>Uranium trioxide (UO<sub>3</sub>)</b>, also called <b><a href="/wiki/Uranyl" title="Uranyl">uranyl</a> oxide</b>, <b>uranium(VI) oxide</b>, and <b>uranic oxide</b>, is the hexavalent <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. The solid may be obtained by heating <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">uranyl nitrate</a> to 400 °C. Its most commonly encountered <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorph</a>, γ-UO<sub>3</sub>, is a yellow-orange powder.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_use">Production and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three methods to generate uranium trioxide. As noted below, two are used industrially in the reprocessing of nuclear fuel and uranium enrichment.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranium-trioxide-formation.png" title="Methods of forming uranium trioxide"><img alt="Methods of forming uranium trioxide" data-file-height="502" data-file-width="1088" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Uranium-trioxide-formation.png/500px-Uranium-trioxide-formation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Uranium-trioxide-formation.png/750px-Uranium-trioxide-formation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Uranium-trioxide-formation.png/1000px-Uranium-trioxide-formation.png 2x" width="500"/></a>
</p>
<ol><li>U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> can be oxidized at 500 °C with oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Note that above 750 °C even in 5 atm O<sub>2</sub> UO<sub>3</sub> decomposes into U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheeler_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wheeler-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">Uranyl nitrate</a>, UO<sub>2</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O can be heated to yield UO<sub>3</sub>. This occurs during the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">reprocessing of nuclear fuel</a>. Fuel rods are dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">HNO<sub>3</sub></a> to separate <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">uranyl nitrate</a> from <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> and the fission products (the <a href="/wiki/PUREX" title="PUREX">PUREX</a> method). The pure uranyl nitrate is converted to solid UO<sub>3</sub> by heating at 400 °C. After reduction with hydrogen (with other inert gas present) to <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a>, the uranium can be used in new <a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">MOX fuel</a> rods.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_diuranate" title="Ammonium diuranate">Ammonium diuranate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_diuranate" title="Sodium diuranate">sodium diuranate</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>U<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O) may be decomposed. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_diuranate" title="Sodium diuranate">Sodium diuranate</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Yellowcake" title="Yellowcake">yellowcake</a>, is converted to uranium trioxide in the <a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enrichment of uranium</a>. <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">Uranium dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium_tetrafluoride" title="Uranium tetrafluoride">uranium tetrafluoride</a> are intermediates in the process which ends in <a href="/wiki/Uranium_hexafluoride" title="Uranium hexafluoride">uranium hexafluoride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Uranium trioxide is shipped between processing facilities in the form of a gel, most often from <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a> to conversion plants. When used for conversion, all uranium oxides are often called <a href="/wiki/Reprocessed_uranium" title="Reprocessed uranium">reprocessed uranium</a>(RepU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameco_Corporation" title="Cameco Corporation">Cameco Corporation</a>, which operates at the world's largest uranium refinery at <a href="/wiki/Blind_River,_Ontario" title="Blind River, Ontario">Blind River, Ontario</a>, produces high-purity uranium trioxide.
</p><p>It has been reported that the corrosion of uranium in a silica rich aqueous solution forms <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a>, uranium trioxide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Coffinite" title="Coffinite">coffinite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In pure water, <a href="/wiki/Schoepite" title="Schoepite">schoepite</a> (UO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>8</sub>O<sub>2</sub>(OH)<sub>12</sub>·12(H<sub>2</sub>O) is formed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> in the first week and then after four months <a href="/wiki/Studtite" title="Studtite">studtite</a> (UO<sub>2</sub>)O<sub>2</sub>·4(H<sub>2</sub>O) was produced. This alteration of uranium oxide also leads to the formation of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Metastudtite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Metastudtite (page does not exist)">metastudtite</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> a more stable uranyl peroxide, often found in the surface of spent nuclear fuel exposed to water. Reports on the corrosion of uranium metal have been published by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety_hazards">Health and safety hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health and safety hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like all hexavalent uranium compounds, UO<sub>3</sub> is hazardous by inhalation, ingestion, and through skin contact. It is a poisonous, slightly radioactive substance, which may cause shortness of breath, coughing, acute arterial lesions, and changes in the chromosomes of <a href="/wiki/White_blood_cell" title="White blood cell">white blood cells</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonads" title="Gonads">gonads</a> leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">congenital malformations</a> if inhaled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morrow_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-morrow-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, once ingested, uranium is mainly toxic for the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a> and may severely affect their function.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_state_structure">Solid state structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solid state structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The only well characterized binary trioxide of any <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> is UO<sub>3</sub>, of which several <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> are known. Solid UO<sub>3</sub> loses O<sub>2</sub> on heating to give green-colored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triuranium_octaoxide" title="Triuranium octaoxide">U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub></a>: reports of the decomposition temperature in air vary from 200–650 °C. Heating at 700 °C under H<sub>2</sub> gives dark brown <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> (UO<sub>2</sub>), which is used in <a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">MOX</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> rods.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_forms">Molecular forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Molecular forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While uranium trioxide is encountered as a polymeric solid under ambient conditions, some work has been done on the molecular form in the gas phase, in matrix isolations studies, and computationally.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_phase">Gas phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gas phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At elevated temperatures gaseous UO<sub>3</sub> is in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> with solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triuranium_octaoxide" title="Triuranium octaoxide">U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub></a> and molecular <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>2 U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>(s) + O<sub>2</sub>(g) ⇌ 6 UO<sub>3</sub>(g)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>With increasing temperature the equilibrium is shifted to the right. This system has been studied at temperatures between 900 °C and 2500 °C. The vapor pressure of monomeric UO<sub>3</sub> in equilibrium with air and solid U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> at ambient pressure, about 10<sup>−5</sup> mbar (1 mPa) at 980 °C, rising to 0.1 mbar (10 Pa) at 1400 °C, 0.34 mbar (34 Pa) at 2100 °C, 1.9 mbar (193 Pa) at 2300 °C, and 8.1 mbar (809 Pa) at 2500 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Matrix_isolation">Matrix isolation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Matrix isolation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Infrared spectroscopy of molecular UO<sub>3</sub> isolated in an argon matrix indicates a T-shaped structure (<a href="/wiki/Point_group" title="Point group">point group</a> <i>C<sub>2v</sub></i>) for the molecule. This is in contrast to the commonly encountered <i>D<sub>3h</sub></i> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a> exhibited by most trioxides. From the force constants the authors deduct the U-O bond lengths to be between 1.76 and 1.79 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> (176 to 179 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E-12_m" title="1 E-12 m">pm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_study">Computational study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Computational study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1100" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png/200px-Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png/300px-Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png/400px-Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranium-trioxide-Pyykko-3D-balls-B.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The calculated geometry of molecular uranium trioxide has C<sub>2v</sub> symmetry.</div></div></div>
<p>Calculations predict that the point group of molecular UO<sub>3</sub> is <i>C<sub>2v</sub></i>, with an axial bond length of 1.75 Å, an equatorial bond length of 1.83 Å and an angle of 161° between the axial oxygens. The more symmetrical <i>D<sub>3h</sub></i> species is a saddle point, 49 kJ/mol above the <i>C<sub>2v</sub></i> minimum. The authors invoke a second-order <a href="/wiki/Jahn%E2%80%93Teller_effect" title="Jahn–Teller effect">Jahn–Teller effect</a> as explanation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cubic_form_of_uranium_trioxide">Cubic form of uranium trioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cubic form of uranium trioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The crystal structure of a uranium trioxide phase of composition UO<sub>2·82</sub> has been determined by X-ray powder diffraction techniques using a Guinier-type focusing camera. The unit cell is cubic with a = 4·138 ± 0·005 kX. A uranium atom is located at (000) and oxygens at (View the MathML source), (View the MathML source), and (View the MathML source) with some anion vacancies. The compound is isostructural with ReO<sub>3</sub>. The U-O bond distance of 2·073 Å agrees with that predicted by Zachariasen for a bond strength S = 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">Reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Uranium trioxide reacts at 400 °C with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freon-12" title="Freon-12">freon-12</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium_tetrafluoride" title="Uranium tetrafluoride">uranium tetrafluoride</a>. The freon-12 can be replaced with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freon-11" title="Freon-11">freon-11</a> which forms <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> instead of carbon dioxide. This is a case of a hard perhalogenated <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">freon</a> which is normally considered to be inert being converted chemically at a moderate temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 CF<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub> + UO<sub>3</sub> → UF<sub>4</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + COCl<sub>2</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>4 CFCl<sub>3</sub> + UO<sub>3</sub> → UF<sub>4</sub> + 3 COCl<sub>2</sub> + CCl<sub>4</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Uranium trioxide can be dissolved in a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Tributyl_phosphate" title="Tributyl phosphate">tributyl phosphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thenoyltrifluoroacetone" title="Thenoyltrifluoroacetone">thenoyltrifluoroacetone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a>, ultrasound was employed during the dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical_modification">Electrochemical modification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Electrochemical modification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The reversible insertion of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> cations into the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">lattice</a> of uranium trioxide by <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_voltammetry" title="Cyclic voltammetry">cyclic voltammetry</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> electrode modified with microscopic particles of the uranium oxide has been investigated. This experiment has also been done for U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>. This is an example of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> of a solid modified <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a>, the experiment which used for uranium trioxide is related to a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_paste_electrode" title="Carbon paste electrode">carbon paste electrode</a> experiment. It is also possible to reduce uranium trioxide with <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> metal to form sodium uranium oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been the case that it is possible to insert <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> into the uranium trioxide lattice by electrochemical means, this is similar to the way that some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rechargeable" title="Rechargeable">rechargeable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_ion_batteries" title="Lithium ion batteries">lithium ion batteries</a> work. In these rechargeable cells one of the electrodes is a metal oxide which contains a metal such as <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> which can be reduced, to maintain the electroneutrality for each electron which is added to the electrode material a lithium ion enters the lattice of this oxide electrode.
</p>
<h3><span id="Amphoterism_and_reactivity_to_form_related_uranium.28VI.29_anions_and_cations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphoterism_and_reactivity_to_form_related_uranium(VI)_anions_and_cations">Amphoterism and reactivity to form related uranium(VI) anions and cations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Amphoterism and reactivity to form related uranium(VI) anions and cations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uranium oxide is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric_(chemistry)" title="Amphoteric (chemistry)">amphoteric</a> and reacts as <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>, depending on the conditions.
</p>
<dl><dt>As an acid</dt>
<dd></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>UO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → <span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Dissolving uranium oxide in a strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> like <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> forms the doubly negatively charged <a href="/wiki/Uranate" title="Uranate">uranate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>). Uranates tend to concatenate, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diuranate" title="Diuranate">diuranate</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">U<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span>, or other poly-uranates.
Important diuranates include <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_diuranate" title="Ammonium diuranate">ammonium diuranate</a> ((NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>U<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Sodium_diuranate" title="Sodium diuranate">sodium diuranate</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>U<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>) and
<a href="/wiki/Magnesium_diuranate" title="Magnesium diuranate">magnesium diuranate</a> (MgU<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>), which forms part of some <a href="/wiki/Yellowcake" title="Yellowcake">yellowcakes</a>. It is worth noting that uranates of the form M<sub>2</sub>UO<sub>4</sub> do <i>not</i> contain <span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> ions, but rather flattened UO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, containing a uranyl group and bridging oxygens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>As a base</dt>
<dd></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>UO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → <span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Dissolving uranium oxide in a strong acid like <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> forms the double positive charged <a href="/wiki/Uranyl" title="Uranyl">uranyl</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">uranyl nitrate</a> formed (UO<sub>2</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O) is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>; for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tributylphosphate" title="Tributylphosphate">tributylphosphate</a>. This solubility is used to separate uranium from other elements in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">nuclear reprocessing</a>, which begins with the dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> rods in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">uranyl nitrate</a> is then converted to uranium trioxide by heating.
</p><p>From <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> one obtains <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_nitrate" title="Uranyl nitrate">uranyl nitrate</a>, <i>trans</i>-UO<sub>2</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, consisting of eight-coordinated uranium with two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bidentate" title="Bidentate">bidentate</a> nitrato ligands and two water ligands as well as the familiar O=U=O core.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uranium_oxides_in_ceramics">Uranium oxides in ceramics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Uranium oxides in ceramics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UO<sub>3</sub>-based ceramics become green or black when fired in a reducing atmosphere and yellow to orange when fired with oxygen. Orange-coloured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiestaware" title="Fiestaware">Fiestaware</a> is a well-known example of a product with a uranium-based glaze. UO<sub>3</sub>-has also been used in formulations of <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium_glass" title="Uranium glass">uranium glass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>.
</p><p>Prior to 1960, UO<sub>3</sub> was used as an agent of crystallization in crystalline coloured glazes. It is possible to determine with a <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a> if a glaze or glass was made from UO<sub>3</sub>.
</p>
