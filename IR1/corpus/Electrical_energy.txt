<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Electrical energy</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Electrical energy</b> is an energy newly derived from  <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential_energy" title="Electric potential energy">electric potential energy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>. When loosely used to describe energy absorbed or maybe  delivered by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_circuit" title="Electrical circuit">electrical circuit</a> (for example, one provided by an <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> utility) "electrical energy" talks about energy which has been converted <i>from</i> electric potential energy. This energy is supplied by the combination of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electric potential</a> that is delivered by the circuit. At the point that this electric potential energy has been converted to another type of energy, it ceases to be electric potential energy. 
Thus, all electrical energy is potential energy before it is delivered to the end-use. Once converted from potential energy, electrical energy can always be called another type of energy (heat, light, motion, etc.).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_generation">Electricity generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrical_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Electricity generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">Electricity generation</a></div>
<p><b>Electricity generation</b> is the process of generating electrical energy from other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forms_of_energy" title="Forms of energy">forms of energy</a>.
</p><p>The fundamental principle of electricity generation was discovered during the 1820s and early 1830s by the British scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>.  His basic method is still used today: electricity is generated by the movement of a loop of wire, or <a href="/wiki/Homopolar_generator" title="Homopolar generator">disc of copper</a> between the poles of a <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faraday_House_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faraday_House-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">elechttps://en.wikipedia.org/w/tric utilities</a>, it is thhttps://en.wikipedia.org/w/e first step in the delivery of electricity to consumers. The other processes, electricity <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">distribution</a>, and electrical power storage and recovery using <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage</a> methods <a class="external free" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/are">https://en.wikipedia.org/w/are</a> normally carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_industry" title="Electric power industry">electric power industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEC-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Electricity is most often generated at a <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a> by electromechanical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">generators</a>, primarily driven by <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a> fueled by chemical <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> but also by other means such as the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of flowing water and wind. There are many other technologies that can be and are used to generate electricity such as solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a>.
</p>
