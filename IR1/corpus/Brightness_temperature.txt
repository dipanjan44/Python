<p><b>Brightness temperature</b> or <b>radiance temperature</b> is the temperature a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">thermal equilibrium</a> with its surroundings would have to be to duplicate the observed <a href="/wiki/Intensity_(heat_transfer)" title="Intensity (heat transfer)">intensity</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grey_body" title="Grey body">grey body</a> object at a frequency <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \nu }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>ν<!-- ν --></mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \nu }</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="\nu " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.232ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
This concept is used in <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>. 
</p><p>The brightness temperature of a surface is typically determined by an optical measurement, for example using a <a href="/wiki/Pyrometer" title="Pyrometer">pyrometer</a>, with the intention of determining the real temperature. As detailed below, the real temperature of a surface can in some cases be calculated by dividing the brightness temperature by the <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> of the surface. Since the emissivity is a value between 0 and 1, the real temperature will be greater than or equal to the brightness temperature. At high frequencies (short wavelengths) and low temperatures, the conversion must proceed through <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck's law">Planck's law</a>.
</p><p>The brightness temperature is not a temperature as ordinarily understood. It characterizes radiation, and depending on the mechanism of radiation can differ considerably from the physical temperature of a radiating body (though it is theoretically possible to construct a device which will heat up by a source of radiation with some brightness temperature to the actual temperature equal to brightness temperature).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RybickiLightman_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RybickiLightman-2">[2]</a></sup> Nonthermal sources can have very high brightness temperatures. In <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a> the brightness temperature can reach 10<sup>26</sup> K. For the radiation of a typical <a href="/wiki/Helium%E2%80%93neon_laser" title="Helium–neon laser">helium–neon laser</a> with a power of 60 mW and a coherence length of 20 cm, focused in a spot with a diameter of 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a>, the brightness temperature will be nearly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010140000000000000♠</span>14<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> K</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
