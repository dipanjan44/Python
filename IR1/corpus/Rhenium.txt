<p><b>Rhenium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Re</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 75. It is a silvery-gray, heavy, third-row <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Group_7_element" title="Group 7 element">group 7</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. With an estimated average concentration of 1 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">part per billion</a> (ppb), rhenium is one of the rarest elements in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>. Rhenium has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_elements_by_melting_point" title="List of elements by melting point">third-highest</a> <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> and second-highest boiling point of any element at 5903 K. Rhenium resembles <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a> chemically and is mainly obtained as a <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> of the extraction and refinement of <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> ores. Rhenium shows in its compounds a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> ranging from −1 to +7.
</p><p>Discovered in 1908, rhenium was the second-last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_element" title="Stable element">stable element</a> to be discovered. It was named after the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> in Europe.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Nickel</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a> of rhenium are used in the combustion chambers, turbine blades, and exhaust nozzles of <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>. These alloys contain up to 6% rhenium, making jet engine construction the largest single use for the element. The second-most important use is as a <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a>: rhenium is an excellent catalyst for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> and isomerization, and is used for example in <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_reforming" title="Catalytic reforming">catalytic reforming</a> of naphtha for use in gasoline (Rheniforming process). Because of the low availability relative to demand, rhenium is expensive, with price reaching an all-time high in 2008/2009 US$10,600 per <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> (US$4,800 per pound). Due to increases in rhenium recycling and a drop in demand for Rhenium in catalysts, the price of rhenium has dropped to US$2,844 per <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> (US$1,290 per pound) as of July 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Nipponium" redirects here. For element 113, see <a href="/wiki/Nihonium" title="Nihonium">nihonium</a>.</div>
<p>Rhenium (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Rhenus</i> meaning: "<a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> was the second last-discovered of the elements that have a stable isotope (other new elements discovered in nature since then, such as <a href="/wiki/Francium" title="Francium">francium</a>, are radioactive).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-6">[6]</a></sup> The existence of a yet-undiscovered element at this position in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> had been first predicted by <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a>. Other calculated information was obtained by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> in 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1908, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Masataka_Ogawa" title="Masataka Ogawa">Masataka Ogawa</a> announced that he had discovered the 43rd element and named it <i>nipponium</i> (Np) after <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (<i>Nippon</i> in Japanese). However, recent analysis indicated the presence of rhenium (element 75), not <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">element 43</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> although this reinterpretation has been questioned by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Scerri" title="Eric Scerri">Eric Scerri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The symbol Np was later used for the element <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>, and the name "nihonium", also <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Japan#Nihon_and_Nippon" title="Names of Japan">named after Japan</a>, along with symbol Nh, was later used for <a href="/wiki/Nihonium" title="Nihonium">element 113</a>. Element 113 was also discovered by a team of Japanese scientists and was named in respectful homage to Ogawa's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rhenium is generally considered to have been discovered by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Noddack" title="Walter Noddack">Walter Noddack</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ida_Tacke" title="Ida Tacke">Ida Noddack</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Otto_Berg_(scientist)" title="Otto Berg (scientist)">Otto Berg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. In 1925 they reported that they had detected the element in platinum ore and in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a>. They also found rhenium in <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekamangane_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ekamangane-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1928 they were able to extract 1 g of the element by processing 660 kg of molybdenite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1g_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-1g-12">[12]</a></sup> It was estimated in 1968 that 75% of the rhenium metal in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> was used for research and the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refractory_metal" title="Refractory metal">refractory metal</a> alloys. It took several years from that point before the superalloys became widely used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rhenium is a silvery-white metal with one of the highest <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> of all elements, exceeded by only <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>. It also has one of the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_points" title="Boiling points">boiling points</a> of all elements. It is also one of the densest, exceeded only by <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a>. Rhenium has a hexagonal close-packed crystal structure, with lattice parameters <i>a</i> = 276.1 pm and <i>c</i> = 445.6 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Its usual commercial form is a powder, but this element can be consolidated by pressing and <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a> in a vacuum or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atmosphere. This procedure yields a compact solid having a density above 90% of the density of the metal. When <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealed</a> this metal is very ductile and can be bent, coiled, or rolled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-16">[16]</a></sup> Rhenium-molybdenum <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> are <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductive</a> at 10 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>; tungsten-rhenium alloys are also superconductive<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> around 4–8 K, depending on the alloy. Rhenium metal superconducts at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000169700000000000♠</span>1.697<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.006 K</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In bulk form and at room temperature and atmospheric pressure, the element resists alkalis, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, dilute (but not concentrated) <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_rhenium" title="Isotopes of rhenium">Isotopes of rhenium</a></div>
<p>Rhenium has one <a href="/wiki/Stable_nuclide" title="Stable nuclide">stable</a> isotope, rhenium-185, which nevertheless occurs in minority abundance, a situation found only in two other elements (<a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a>). Naturally occurring rhenium is only 37.4% <sup>185</sup>Re, and 62.6% <sup>187</sup>Re, which is <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">unstable</a> but has a very long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> (≈10<sup>10</sup> years). This lifetime can be greatly affected by the charge state of rhenium atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bosch1996_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bosch1996-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> of <sup>187</sup>Re is used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhenium-osmium_dating" title="Rhenium-osmium dating">rhenium-osmium dating</a> of ores. The available energy for this beta decay (2.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KeV" title="KeV">keV</a>) is one of the lowest known among all <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a>. The isotope rhenium-186m is notable as being one of the longest lived metastable isotopes with a half-life of around 200,000 years. There are twenty-five other recognized radioactive isotopes of rhenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Rhenium_compounds" title="Category:Rhenium compounds">Category:Rhenium compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Rhenium compounds are known for all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation_states" title="Oxidation states">oxidation states</a> between −3 and +7 except −2. The oxidation states +7, +6, +4, and +2 are the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-23">[23]</a></sup>  Rhenium is most available commercially as salts of <a href="/wiki/Perrhenate" title="Perrhenate">perrhenate</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Sodium_perrhenate" title="Sodium perrhenate">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_perrhenate" title="Ammonium perrhenate">ammonium perrhenates</a>.  These are white, water-soluble compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brauer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides_and_oxyhalides">Halides and oxyhalides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Halides and oxyhalides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most common rhenium chlorides are ReCl<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium_pentachloride" title="Rhenium pentachloride">ReCl<sub>5</sub></a>, ReCl<sub>4</sub>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhenium_trichloride" title="Rhenium trichloride">ReCl<sub>3</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup> The structures of these compounds often feature extensive Re-Re bonding, which is characteristic of this metal in oxidation states lower than VII.  Salts of [Re<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>8</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> feature a <a href="/wiki/Quadruple_bond" title="Quadruple bond">quadruple</a> metal-metal bond.  Although the highest rhenium chloride features Re(VI), fluorine gives the d<sup>0</sup> Re(VII) derivative <a href="/wiki/Rhenium_heptafluoride" title="Rhenium heptafluoride">rhenium heptafluoride</a>.  Bromides and iodides of rhenium are also well known.
</p><p>Like tungsten and molybdenum, with which it shares chemical similarities, rhenium forms a variety of <a href="/wiki/Oxohalide" title="Oxohalide">oxyhalides</a>.  The oxychlorides are most common, and include ReOCl<sub>4</sub>, ReOCl<sub>3</sub>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_and_sulfides">Oxides and sulfides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oxides and sulfides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="786" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png/110px-Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png/165px-Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png/220px-Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Perrhenic-acid-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Perrhenic acid (H<sub>4</sub>Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>9</sub>) adopts an unconventional structure.</div></div></div>
<p>The most common oxide is the volatile colourless <a href="/wiki/Rhenium(VII)_oxide" title="Rhenium(VII) oxide">Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ReO3" title="ReO3">Rhenium trioxide</a> ReO<sub>3</sub> adopts a <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">perovskite</a>-like structure.  Other oxides include Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium(IV)_oxide" title="Rhenium(IV) oxide">ReO<sub>2</sub></a>, and Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a> are <a href="/wiki/Rhenium_disulfide" title="Rhenium disulfide">ReS<sub>2</sub></a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dirhenium_heptasulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dirhenium heptasulfide (page does not exist)">Re<sub>2</sub>S<sub>7</sub></a>. Perrhenate salts can be converted to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrathioperrhenate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tetrathioperrhenate (page does not exist)">tetrathioperrhenate</a> by the action of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_hydrosulfide" title="Ammonium hydrosulfide">ammonium hydrosulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_compounds">Other compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rhenium_diboride" title="Rhenium diboride">Rhenium diboride</a> (ReB<sub>2</sub>) is a hard compound having the hardness similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">silicon carbide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_diboride" title="Titanium diboride">titanium diboride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_diboride" title="Zirconium diboride">zirconium diboride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Organorhenium_compounds">Organorhenium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organorhenium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organorhenium_chemistry" title="Organorhenium chemistry">Organorhenium chemistry</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dirhenium_decacarbonyl" title="Dirhenium decacarbonyl">Dirhenium decacarbonyl</a> is the most common entry to organorhenium chemistry. Its reduction with sodium <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgam</a> gives Na[Re(CO)<sub>5</sub>] with rhenium in the formal oxidation state −1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Dirhenium decacarbonyl can be oxidised with <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bromopentacarbonylrhenium(I)" title="Bromopentacarbonylrhenium(I)">bromopentacarbonylrhenium(I)</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Re<sub>2</sub>(CO)<sub>10</sub> + Br<sub>2</sub> → 2 Re(CO)<sub>5</sub>Br</dd></dl>
<p>Reduction of this pentacarbonyl with <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> gives <a href="/wiki/Pentacarbonylhydridorhenium" title="Pentacarbonylhydridorhenium">pentacarbonylhydridorhenium</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urb_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Urb-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Re(CO)<sub>5</sub>Br  +  Zn +  HOAc   →   Re(CO)<sub>5</sub>H + ZnBr(OAc)</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methylrhenium_trioxide" title="Methylrhenium trioxide">Methylrhenium trioxide</a> ("MTO"), CH<sub>3</sub>ReO<sub>3</sub> is a volatile, colourless solid has been used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in some laboratory experiments. It can be prepared by many routes, a typical method is the reaction of Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Tetramethyltin" title="Tetramethyltin">tetramethyltin</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>  +  (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>Sn  →  CH<sub>3</sub>ReO<sub>3</sub>  +  (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>SnOReO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Analogous alkyl and aryl derivatives are known.  MTO catalyses for the oxidations with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>.  Terminal <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a> yield the corresponding acid or ester, internal alkynes yield diketones, and <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> give epoxides. MTO also catalyses the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diazoalkane" title="Diazoalkane">diazoalkanes</a> into an alkene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonahydridorhenate">Nonahydridorhenate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nonahydridorhenate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1060" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png/110px-Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png/165px-Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png/220px-Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nonahydridorhenate-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structure of <span class="chemf nowrap">ReH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span></span>.</div></div></div>
<p>A distinctive derivative of rhenium is <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nonahydridorhenate" title="Potassium nonahydridorhenate">nonahydridorhenate</a>, originally thought to be the <i>rhenide</i> anion, Re<sup>−</sup>, but actually containing the <span class="chemf nowrap">ReH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span></span> anion in which the oxidation state of rhenium is +7.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molybdenit_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1260" data-file-width="1704" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Molybdenit_1.jpg/220px-Molybdenit_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Molybdenit_1.jpg/330px-Molybdenit_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Molybdenit_1.jpg/440px-Molybdenit_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Molybdenit_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molybdenite</div></div></div>
<p>Rhenium is one of the rarest elements in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> with an average concentration of 1 ppb;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup> other sources quote the number of 0.5 ppb making it the 77th most abundant element in Earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley2001p358_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley2001p358-32">[32]</a></sup> Rhenium is probably not found free in nature (its possible natural occurrence is uncertain), but occurs in amounts up to 0.2%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup> in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite</a> (which is primarily <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disulfide" title="Molybdenum disulfide">molybdenum disulfide</a>), the major commercial source, although single molybdenite samples with up to 1.88% have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rousch_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rousch-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> has the world's largest rhenium reserves, part of the copper ore deposits, and was the leading producer as of 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It was only recently that the first rhenium <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> was found and described (in 1994), a rhenium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfide_mineral" title="Sulfide mineral">sulfide mineral</a> (ReS<sub>2</sub>) condensing from a <a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">fumarole</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kudriavy" title="Kudriavy">Kudriavy</a> volcano, <a href="/wiki/Iturup" title="Iturup">Iturup</a> island, in the <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Islands" title="Kuril Islands">Kuril Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Kudryavy discharges up to 20–60 kg rhenium per year mostly in the form of rhenium disulfide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Named <a href="/wiki/Rheniite" title="Rheniite">rheniite</a>, this rare mineral commands high prices among collectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="595" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg/220px-Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg/330px-Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg/440px-Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ammonium_perrhenate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ammonium perrhenate</div></div></div>
<p>Commercial rhenium is extracted from molybdenum roaster-flue gas obtained from copper-sulfide ores. Some molybdenum ores contain 0.001% to 0.2% rhenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rousch_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rousch-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhenium(VII)_oxide" title="Rhenium(VII) oxide">Rhenium(VII) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perrhenic_acid" title="Perrhenic acid">perrhenic acid</a> readily dissolve in water; they are leached from flue dusts and gasses and extracted by precipitating with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Perrhenate" title="Perrhenate">perrhenate</a> salts, and purified by <a href="/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry)" title="Recrystallization (chemistry)">recrystallization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-25">[25]</a></sup> Total world production is between 40 and 50 tons/year; the main producers are in Chile, the United States, Peru, and Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2012_summary_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_2012_summary-39">[39]</a></sup> Recycling of used Pt-Re catalyst and special alloys allow the recovery of another 10 tons per year. Prices for the metal rose rapidly in early 2008, from $1000–$2000 per <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a> in 2003–2006 to over $10,000 in February 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minormetals_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-minormetals-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The metal form is prepared by reducing <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_perrhenate" title="Ammonium perrhenate">ammonium perrhenate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> at high temperatures:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brauer_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NH<sub>4</sub>ReO<sub>4</sub> + 7 H<sub>2</sub> → 2 Re + 8 H<sub>2</sub>O + 2 NH<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="501" data-file-width="750" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg/220px-Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg/330px-Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg/440px-Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney F100">The Pratt &amp; Whitney F-100 engine</a> uses rhenium-containing second-generation superalloys</div></div></div>
<p>Rhenium is added to high-temperature superalloys that are used to make <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a> parts, using 70% of the worldwide rhenium production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naumov_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naumov-42">[42]</a></sup> Another major application is in platinum–rhenium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>, which are primarily used in making <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>-free, high-octane <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2009_yearbook_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_2009_yearbook-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nickel-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a> have improved <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep strength</a> with the addition of rhenium. The alloys normally contain 3% or 6% of rhenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Second-generation alloys contain 3%; these alloys were used in the engines for the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney F100">F-15 and F-16</a>, whereas the newer single-crystal third-generation alloys contain 6% of rhenium; they are used in the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F119" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney F119">F-22</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F135" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney F135">F-35</a> engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2009_yearbook_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_2009_yearbook-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Rhenium is also used in the superalloys, such as CMSX-4 (2nd gen) and CMSX-10 (3rd gen) that are used in industrial <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> engines like the GE 7FA. Rhenium can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a> to become microstructurally unstable, forming undesirable TCP (topologically close packed) <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a>. In 4th- and 5th-generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> is used to avoid this effect. Among others the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a> are EPM-102 (with 3% Ru) and TMS-162 (with 6% Ru),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> as well as TMS-138<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and TMS-174.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CFM56_P1220759.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/CFM56_P1220759.jpg/220px-CFM56_P1220759.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/CFM56_P1220759.jpg/330px-CFM56_P1220759.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/CFM56_P1220759.jpg/440px-CFM56_P1220759.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CFM56_P1220759.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CFM International CFM56 jet engine still with blades made with 3% rhenium</div></div></div>
<p>For 2006, the consumption is given as 28% for <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>, 28% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce plc</a> and 12% <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a>, all for superalloys, whereas the use for catalysts only accounts for 14% and the remaining applications use 18%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naumov_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Naumov-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2006, 77% of the rhenium consumption in the United States was in alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2009_yearbook_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_2009_yearbook-43">[43]</a></sup> The rising demand for military jet engines and the constant supply made it necessary to develop superalloys with a lower rhenium content. For example, the newer <a href="/wiki/CFM_International_CFM56" title="CFM International CFM56">CFM International CFM56</a>  high-pressure turbine (HPT) blades will use Rene N515 with a rhenium content of 1.5% instead of Rene N5 with 3%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rhenium improves the properties of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>. Tungsten-rhenium alloys are more ductile at low temperature, allowing them to be more easily machined.  The high-temperature stability is also improved. The effect increases with the rhenium concentration, and therefore tungsten alloys are produced with up to 27% of Re, which is the solubility limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Tungsten-rhenium wire was originally created in efforts to develop a wire that was more ductile after recrystallization. This allows the wire to meet specific performance objectives, including superior vibration resistance, improved ductility, and higher resistivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> One application for the tungsten-rhenium alloys is <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> sources. The high melting point of both elements, together with their high atomic mass, makes them stable against the prolonged electron impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Rhenium tungsten alloys are also applied as <a href="/wiki/Thermocouple" title="Thermocouple">thermocouples</a> to measure temperatures up to 2200 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The high temperature stability, low vapor pressure, good <a href="/wiki/Wear" title="Wear">wear resistance</a> and ability to withstand arc corrosion of rhenium are useful in self-cleaning <a href="/wiki/Switch#Contacts" title="Switch">electrical contacts</a>. In particular, the discharge occurring during the switching oxidizes the contacts. However, rhenium oxide Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> has poor stability (sublimes at ~360 °C) and therefore is removed during the discharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naumov_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Naumov-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rhenium has a high melting point and a low vapor pressure similar to <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> and tungsten. Therefore, rhenium filaments exhibit a higher stability if the filament is operated not in vacuum, but in oxygen-containing atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Those filaments are widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometer" title="Mass spectrometer">mass spectrometers</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_gauge" title="Ion gauge">ion gauges</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and in photoflash lamps in <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysts">Catalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Catalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhenium in the form of rhenium-platinum alloy is used as catalyst for <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_reforming" title="Catalytic reforming">catalytic reforming</a>, which is a chemical process to convert petroleum refinery <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_naphtha" title="Petroleum naphtha">naphthas</a> with low <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane ratings</a> into high-octane liquid products. Worldwide, 30% of catalysts used for this process contain rhenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Olefin_metathesis" title="Olefin metathesis">olefin metathesis</a> is the other reaction for which rhenium is used as catalyst. Normally Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> is used for this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Rhenium catalysts are very resistant to <a href="/wiki/Catalyst_poisoning" title="Catalyst poisoning">chemical poisoning</a> from nitrogen, sulfur and phosphorus, and so are used in certain kinds of hydrogenation reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The isotopes <sup>188</sup>Re and <sup>186</sup>Re are radioactive and are used for treatment of <a href="/wiki/Liver_cancer" title="Liver cancer">liver cancer</a>. They both have similar penetration depth in tissue (5 mm for <sup>186</sup>Re and 11 mm for <sup>188</sup>Re), but <sup>186</sup>Re has advantage of longer lifetime (90 hours vs. 17 hours).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dilw_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dilw-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p><sup>188</sup>Re is also being used experimentally in a novel treatment of pancreatic cancer where it is delivered by means of the bacterium <i>Listeria monocytogenes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Related by <a href="/wiki/Periodic_trends" title="Periodic trends">periodic trends</a>, rhenium has a similar chemistry to that of <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>; work done to label rhenium onto target compounds can often be translated to technetium. This is useful for radiopharmacy, where it is difficult to work with technetium – especially the 99m isotope used in medicine – due to its expense and short half-life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dilw_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dilw-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Very little is known about the toxicity of rhenium and its compounds because they are used in very small amounts. Soluble salts, such as the rhenium halides or perrhenates, could be hazardous due to elements other than rhenium or due to rhenium itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-67">[67]</a></sup> Only a few compounds of rhenium have been tested for their acute toxicity; two examples are potassium perrhenate and rhenium trichloride, which were injected as a solution into rats. The perrhenate had an <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub>50</sub></a> value of 2800 mg/kg after seven days (this is very low toxicity, similar to that of table salt) and the rhenium trichloride showed LD<sub>50</sub> of 280 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
