<p><b>Gallium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ga</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 31. It is in <a href="/wiki/Boron_group" title="Boron group">group 13</a> of the periodic table, and thus has similarities to the other metals of the group, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>. Gallium does not occur as a free element in nature, but as gallium(III) compounds in trace amounts in <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ores and in  <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Elemental gallium is a soft, silvery blue metal at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, a brittle solid at low temperatures, and a liquid at temperatures greater than 29.76 °C (85.57 °F) (above <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>, but below the normal human body temperature of 37.5 °C (99.5 °F), hence, the metal will melt in a person's hands).
</p><p>The melting point of gallium is used as a temperature reference point. Gallium alloys are used in thermometers as a non-toxic and <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> alternative to mercury, and can withstand higher temperatures than mercury. The alloy <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">galinstan</a> (70% gallium, 21.5% <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>, and 10% <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>) has an even lower melting point of −19 °C (−2 °F), well below the freezing point of water.
</p><p>Since its discovery in 1875, gallium has been used to make <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> with low melting points. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in semiconductor substrates.
</p><p>Gallium is predominantly used in <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">Gallium arsenide</a>, the primary <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> of gallium in electronics, is used in <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> circuits, high-speed switching circuits, and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> circuits. Semiconducting <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_nitride" title="Indium gallium nitride">indium gallium nitride</a> produce blue and violet <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diode_laser" title="Diode laser">diode lasers</a>. Gallium is also used in the production of artificial <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium_gallium_garnet" title="Gadolinium gallium garnet">gadolinium gallium garnet</a> for jewelry.
</p><p>Gallium has no known natural role in biology. Gallium(III) behaves in a similar manner to <a href="/wiki/Ferric#Ferric_iron_and_life" title="Ferric">ferric</a> salts in biological systems and has been used in some medical applications, including pharmaceuticals and <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmacology" title="Radiopharmacology">radiopharmaceuticals</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2018" data-file-width="2470" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG/220px-Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG/330px-Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG/440px-Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallium_kristallisiert.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystallization of gallium from the melt</div></div></div>
<p>Elemental gallium is not found in nature, but it is easily obtained by <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>. Very pure gallium metal has a silvery color and its solid metal fractures <a href="/wiki/Conchoidal_fracture" title="Conchoidal fracture">conchoidally</a> like <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. Gallium liquid expands by 3.1% when it solidifies; therefore, it should not be stored in glass or metal containers because the container may rupture when the gallium changes state. Gallium shares the higher-density liquid state with a short list of other materials that includes <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal_embrittlement" title="Liquid metal embrittlement">attacks</a> most other metals by <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusing</a> into the metal <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">lattice</a>. For example, it diffuses into the <a href="/wiki/Grain_boundary" title="Grain boundary">grain boundaries</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> making them very brittle. Gallium easily alloys with many metals, and is used in small quantities in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium-gallium_alloy" title="Plutonium-gallium alloy">plutonium-gallium alloy</a> in the plutonium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium_pit" title="Plutonium pit">cores</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear bombs</a> to stabilize the plutonium crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of gallium, at 302.9146 K (29.7646 °C, 85.5763 °F), is just above room temperature, and is approximately the same as the average summer daytime temperatures in Earth's mid-latitudes. This melting point (mp) is one of the formal temperature reference points in the <a href="/wiki/International_Temperature_Scale_of_1990" title="International Temperature Scale of 1990">International Temperature Scale of 1990</a> (ITS-90) established by the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (BIPM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of gallium, 302.9166 K (29.7666 °C, 85.5799 °F), is used by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) in preference to the melting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The melting point of gallium allows it to melt in the human hand, and then refreeze if removed. The liquid metal has a strong tendency to <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercool</a> below its <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a>: Ga nanoparticles can be kept in the liquid state below 90 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Seed_crystal" title="Seed crystal">Seeding</a> with a crystal helps to initiate freezing. Gallium is one of the four non-radioactive metals (with <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>) that are known to be liquid at, or near, normal room temperature. Of the four, gallium is the only one that is neither highly reactive (rubidium and caesium) nor highly toxic (mercury) and can therefore be used in metal-in-glass high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a>. It is also notable for having one of the largest liquid ranges for a metal, and for having (unlike mercury) a low <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> at high temperatures. Gallium's boiling point, 2673 K, is more than eight times higher than its melting point on the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_scale" title="Absolute scale">absolute scale</a>, the greatest ratio between melting point and boiling point of any element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood224_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood224-15">[15]</a></sup> Unlike mercury, liquid gallium metal <a href="/wiki/Wetting" title="Wetting">wets</a> glass and skin, along with most other materials (with the exceptions of quartz, graphite, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teflon" title="Teflon">Teflon</a>), making it mechanically more difficult to handle even though it is substantially less toxic and requires far fewer precautions. Gallium painted onto glass is a brilliant mirror.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood221_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood221-16">[16]</a></sup> For this reason as well as the metal contamination and freezing-expansion problems, samples of gallium metal are usually supplied in polyethylene packets within other containers.
</p>

<p>Gallium does not <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystallize</a> in any of the simple <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structures</a>. The stable phase under normal conditions is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> with 8 atoms in the conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_cell" title="Unit cell">unit cell</a>. Within a unit cell, each atom has only one nearest neighbor (at a distance of 244 <a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">pm</a>). The remaining six unit cell neighbors are spaced 27, 30 and 39 pm farther away, and they are grouped in pairs with the same distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  Many stable and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastability_in_molecules" title="Metastability in molecules">metastable</a> phases are found as function of temperature and pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The bonding between the two nearest neighbors is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent</a>; hence Ga<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimers</a> are seen as the fundamental building blocks of the crystal. This explains the low melting point relative to the neighbor elements, aluminium and indium. This structure is strikingly similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> and forms because of interactions between the single 4p electrons of gallium atoms, further away from the nucleus than the 4s electrons and the [Ar]3d<sup>10</sup> core. This phenomenon recurs with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> with its "pseudo-noble-gas" [Xe]4f<sup>14</sup>5d<sup>10</sup>6s<sup>2</sup> electron configuration, which is liquid at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood223_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood223-20">[20]</a></sup> The 3d<sup>10</sup> electrons do not shield the outer electrons very well from the nucleus and hence the first ionisation energy of gallium is greater than that of aluminium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The physical properties of gallium are highly anisotropic, i.e. have different values along the three major crystallographical axes <i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, and <i>c</i> (see table), producing a significant difference between the linear (α) and volume <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> coefficients. The properties of gallium are strongly temperature-dependent, particularly near the melting point. For example, the coefficient of thermal expansion increases by several hundred percent upon melting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anis_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-anis-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_gallium" title="Isotopes of gallium">Isotopes of gallium</a></div>
<p>Gallium has 31 known isotopes, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> from 56 to 86. Only two isotopes are stable and occur naturally, gallium-69 and gallium-71. Gallium-69 is more abundant: it makes up about 60.1% of natural gallium, while gallium-71 makes up the remaining 39.9%. All the other isotopes are radioactive. The two longest-lived and only commercially important ones are gallium-67 (half-life 3.261 days) and gallium-68 (half-life 67.7 min). Isotopes lighter than gallium-69 usually decay through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_plus_decay" title="Beta plus decay">beta plus decay</a> (positron emission) or <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, although the lightest few (with mass numbers 56 through 59) decay through prompt <a href="/wiki/Proton_emission" title="Proton emission">proton emission</a>. Isotopes heavier than gallium-71 decay through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_minus_decay" title="Beta minus decay">beta minus decay</a> (electron emission), possibly with delayed <a href="/wiki/Neutron_emission" title="Neutron emission">neutron emission</a>, to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, while gallium-70 can decay through both beta minus decay and electron capture. Gallium-67 is unique among the light isotopes in having only electron capture as a decay mode, as its decay energy is not sufficient to allow positron emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Gallium_compounds" title="Category:Gallium compounds">Category:Gallium compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Gallium is found primarily in the +3 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>. The +1 oxidation state is also found in some compounds, although it is less common than it is for gallium's heavier congeners indium and thallium. For example, the very stable GaCl<sub>2</sub> contains both gallium(I) and gallium(III) and can be formulated as Ga<sup>I</sup>Ga<sup>III</sup>Cl<sub>4</sub>; in contrast, the monochloride is unstable above 0 °C, <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionating</a> into elemental gallium and gallium(III) chloride. Compounds containing Ga–Ga bonds are true gallium(II) compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Gallium(II)_sulfide" title="Gallium(II) sulfide">GaS</a> (which can be formulated as Ga<sub>2</sub><sup>4+</sup>(S<sup>2−</sup>)<sub>2</sub>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxan" title="Dioxan">dioxan</a> complex Ga<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>4</sub>(C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>8</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood240_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood240-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>

style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> with <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S</span> leads to the formation of <i>thiogallates</i> containing the <span class="chemf nowrap">[Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> anion. Strong acids decompose these salts, releasing <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S</span> in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104–105</span></sup> The mercury salt, <span class="chemf nowrap">HgGa<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>, can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium also forms sulfides in lower oxidation states, such as <a href="/wiki/Gallium(II)_sulfide" title="Gallium(II) sulfide">gallium(II) sulfide</a> and the green <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium(I)_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gallium(I) sulfide (page does not exist)">gallium(I) sulfide</a>, the latter of which is produced from the former by heating to 1000 °C under a stream of nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>94</span></sup>
</p><p>The other binary chalcogenides, <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Se<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Te<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, have the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zincblende_(crystal_structure)" title="Zincblende (crystal structure)">zincblende</a> structure. They are all semiconductors but are easily <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysed</a> and have limited utility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrides_and_pnictides">Nitrides and pnictides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nitrides and pnictides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:328px;max-width:328px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:162px;max-width:162px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crystal-GaN.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="331" data-file-width="389" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Crystal-GaN.jpg/160px-Crystal-GaN.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Crystal-GaN.jpg/240px-Crystal-GaN.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Crystal-GaN.jpg/320px-Crystal-GaN.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:162px;max-width:162px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="293" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg/160px-Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg/240px-Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6f/Gallium_arsenide_crystal.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Gallium nitride (<i>left</i>) and gallium arsenide (<i>right</i>) crystals</div></div></div>
<p>Gallium reacts with ammonia at 1050 °C to form <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a>, GaN. Gallium also forms binary compounds with <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>: <a href="/wiki/Gallium_phosphide" title="Gallium phosphide">gallium phosphide</a> (GaP), <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> (GaAs), and <a href="/wiki/Gallium_antimonide" title="Gallium antimonide">gallium antimonide</a> (GaSb). These compounds have the same structure as <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">ZnS</a>, and have important <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconducting</a> properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1034</span></sup> GaP, GaAs, and GaSb can be synthesized by the direct reaction of gallium with elemental phosphorus, arsenic, or antimony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-5"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>99</span></sup> They exhibit higher electrical conductivity than GaN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-6"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>101</span></sup> GaP can also be synthesized by reacting <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> with phosphorus at low temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium forms ternary <a href="/wiki/Nitride" title="Nitride">nitrides</a>; for example:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-7"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>99</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">Li<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>Ga</span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Li<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>GaN<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Similar compounds with phosphorus and arsenic are possible: <span class="chemf nowrap">Li<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>GaP<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">Li<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>GaAs<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. These compounds are easily hydrolyzed by dilute <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emeleus_sharpe_28-8"><a href="#cite_note-emeleus_sharpe-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>101</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Gallium_halides" title="Gallium halides">Gallium halides</a></div>
<p>Gallium(III) oxide reacts with <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">fluorinating agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">HF</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine"><span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a> to form <a href="/wiki/Gallium(III)_fluoride" title="Gallium(III) fluoride">gallium(III) fluoride</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">GaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. It is an ionic compound strongly insoluble in water. However, it dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>, in which it forms an <a href="/wiki/Adduct" title="Adduct">adduct</a> with water, <span class="chemf nowrap">GaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>·3H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span>. Attempting to dehydrate this adduct forms <span class="chemf nowrap">GaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>OH·<i>n</i>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span>. The adduct reacts with ammonia to form <span class="chemf nowrap">GaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>·3NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which can then be heated to form anhydrous <span class="chemf nowrap">GaF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-downs-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>128–129</span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gallium_trichloride" title="Gallium trichloride">Gallium trichloride</a> is formed by the reaction of gallium metal with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagleson_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-eagleson-25">[25]</a></sup> Unlike the trifluoride, gallium(III) chloride exists as dimeric molecules, <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>, with a melting point of 78 °C. Eqivalent compounds are formed with bromine and iodine, <a href="/wiki/Gallium(III)_bromide" title="Gallium(III) bromide"><span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Br<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span></a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallium(III)_iodide" title="Gallium(III) iodide"><span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>I<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-downs-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>133</span></sup>
</p><p>Like the other group 13 trihalides, gallium(III) halides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acids</a>, reacting as halide acceptors with alkali metal halides to form salts containing <span class="chemf nowrap">GaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> anions, where X is a halogen. They also react with <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">alkyl halides</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a> and <span class="chemf nowrap">GaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_24-7"><a href="#cite_note-downs-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>136–137</span></sup>
</p><p>When heated to a high temperature, gallium(III) halides react with elemental gallium to form the respective gallium(I) halides. For example, <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> reacts with Ga to form <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl</span>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ga + <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> ⇌ 3 GaCl (g)</dd></dl>
<p>At lower temperatures, the equilibrium shifts toward the left and GaCl disproportionates back to elemental gallium and <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. GaCl can also be produced by reacting Ga with HCl at 950 °C; the product can be condensed as a red solid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1036</span></sup>
</p><p>Gallium(I) compounds can be stabilized by forming adducts with Lewis acids. For example:
</p>

<p>The so-called "gallium(II) halides", <span class="chemf nowrap">GaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, are actually adducts of gallium(I) halides with the respective gallium(III) halides, having the structure <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>[GaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>. For example:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagleson_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-eagleson-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1036</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arora_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-arora-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>GaCl + <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>[GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrides">Hydrides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hydrides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, gallium also forms a <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydride</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">GaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, known as <i><a href="/wiki/Gallane" title="Gallane">gallane</a></i>, which may be produced by reacting lithium gallanate (<span class="chemf nowrap">LiGaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallium(III)_chloride" title="Gallium(III) chloride">gallium(III) chloride</a> at −30 °C:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1031</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3 <span class="chemf nowrap">LiGaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">GaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → 3 LiCl + 4 <span class="chemf nowrap">GaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>In the presence of <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a> as solvent, <span class="chemf nowrap">GaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> polymerizes to <span class="chemf nowrap">(GaH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i></sub></span></span>. If no solvent is used, the dimer <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> (<i><a href="/wiki/Digallane" title="Digallane">digallane</a></i>) is formed as a gas. Its structure is similar to <a href="/wiki/Diborane" title="Diborane">diborane</a>, having two hydrogen atoms bridging the two gallium centers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1031</span></sup> unlike α-<a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydride" title="Aluminium hydride"><span class="chemf nowrap">AlH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></a> in which aluminium has a coordination number of 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg_holleman_23-7"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg_holleman-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1008</span></sup>
</p><p>Gallane is unstable above −10 °C, decomposing to elemental gallium and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sykes_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sykes-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organogallium_compounds">Organogallium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organogallium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organogallium_chemistry" title="Organogallium chemistry">Organogallium chemistry</a></div>
<p>Organogallium compounds are of similar reactivity to organoindium compounds. less reactive than organoaluminium compounds, but more reactive than organothallium compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood262_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood262-34">[34]</a></sup> Alkylgalliums are monomeric. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acidity</a> decreases in the order Al &gt; Ga &gt; In and as a result organogallium compounds do not form bridged dimers as organoaluminum compounds do. Organogallium compounds are also less reactive than organoaluminum compounds. They do form stable peroxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> These alkylgalliums are liquids at room temperature, having low melting points, and are quite mobile and flammable. Triphenylgallium is monomeric in solution, but its crystals form chain structures due to weak intermolecluar Ga···C interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood262_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood262-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium trichloride is a common starting reagent for the formation of organogallium compounds, such as in <a href="/wiki/Carbometalation" title="Carbometalation">carbogallation</a> reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Gallium trichloride reacts with <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> cyclopentadienide in <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> to form the trigonal planar gallium cyclopentadienyl complex GaCp<sub>3</sub>. Gallium(I) forms complexes with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arene" title="Arene">arene</a> ligands such as <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylbenzene" title="Hexamethylbenzene">hexamethylbenzene</a>. Because this ligand is quite bulky, the structure of the [Ga(η<sup>6</sup>-C<sub>6</sub>Me<sub>6</sub>)]<sup>+</sup> is that of a half-<a href="/wiki/Sandwich_compound" title="Sandwich compound">sandwich</a>. Less bulky ligands such as <a href="/wiki/Mesitylene" title="Mesitylene">mesitylene</a> allow two ligands to be attached to the central gallium atom in a bent sandwich structure. <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">Benzene</a> is even less bulky and allows the formation of dimers: an example is [Ga(η<sup>6</sup>-C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub>)<sub>2</sub>] [GaCl<sub>4</sub>]·3C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood262_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood262-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1871, the existence of gallium was first predicted by Russian chemist <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a>, who named it "<a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">eka-aluminium</a>" from its position in his <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. He also predicted several properties of eka-aluminium that correspond closely to the real properties of gallium, such as its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>, oxide character and bonding in chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>

<p>Mendeleev further predicted that eka-aluminium would be discovered by means of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscope</a>, and that metallic eka-aluminium would dissolve slowly in both acids and alkalis and would not react with air. He also predicted that M<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> would dissolve in acids to give MX<sub>3</sub> salts, that eka-aluminium salts would form basic salts, that eka-aluminium sulfate should form <a href="/wiki/Alum" title="Alum">alums</a>, and that anhydrous MCl<sub>3</sub> should have a greater volatility than ZnCl<sub>2</sub>: all of these predictions turned out to be true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood217_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood217-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium was discovered using <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> by French chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Emile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul Emile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Emile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a> in 1875 from its characteristic spectrum (two <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">violet</a> lines) in a sample of <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bois_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bois-39">[39]</a></sup>   Later that year, Lecoq obtained the free metal by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> in <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> solution. He named the element "gallia", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Gallia</i> meaning <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, after his native land of France. It was later claimed that, in one of those multilingual <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">puns</a> so beloved by men of science in the 19th century, he had also named gallium after himself: "Le coq" is French for "the <a href="/wiki/Rooster" title="Rooster">rooster</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for "rooster" is "<i>gallus</i>".  In an 1877 article, Lecoq denied this conjecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-40">[40]</a></sup> Originally, de Boisbaudran determined the density of gallium as 4.7 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, the only property that failed to match Mendeleev's predictions; Mendeleev then wrote to him and suggested that he should remeasure the density, and de Boisbaudran then obtained the correct value of 5.9 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, that Mendeleev had predicted almost exactly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood217_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood217-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>From its discovery in 1875 until the era of semiconductors, the primary uses of gallium were high-temperature thermometrics and metal alloys with unusual properties of stability or ease of melting (some such being liquid at room temperature). The development of <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Direct_and_indirect_band_gaps" title="Direct and indirect band gaps">direct band gap semiconductor</a> in the 1960s ushered in the most important stage in the applications of gallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood221_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood221-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gallium does not exist as a free element in the Earth's crust, and the few high-content minerals, such as gallite (CuGaS<sub>2</sub>), are too rare to serve as a primary source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-41">[41]</a></sup> The abundance <a href="/wiki/Earth#Chemical_composition" title="Earth">in the Earth's crust</a> is approximately 16.9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burton-42">[42]</a></sup> This is comparable to the crustal abundances of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>. Yet unlike these elements, gallium does not form its own ore deposits with concentrations of &gt; 0.1 wt.% in ore. Rather it occurs at trace concentrations similar to the crustal value in zinc ores,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and at somewhat higher values (~ 50 ppm) in aluminium ores, from both of which it is extracted as a by-product. This lack of independent deposits is due to gallium's geochemical behaviour, showing no strong enrichment in the processes relevant to the formation of most ore deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a>) estimates that more than 1 million tons of gallium is contained in known reserves of bauxite and zinc ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2008_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2008-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2006_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2006-45">[45]</a></sup> Some coal <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">flue</a> <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dusts</a> contain small quantities of gallium, typically less than 1% by weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> However, these amounts are not extractable without mining of the host materials (see below). Thus, the availability of gallium is fundamentally determined by the rate at which bauxite, zinc ores (and coal) are extracted.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_availability">Production and availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Production and availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg/220px-6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg/330px-6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg/440px-6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:6N_Gallium_sealed_in_vacuum_ampoule.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>99.9999% (6N) gallium sealed in vacuum ampoule</div></div></div>
<p>Gallium is produced exclusively as a <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> during the processing of the ores of other metals. Its main source material is <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>, the chief ore of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, but minor amounts are also extracted from sulfidic zinc ores (<a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a> being the main host mineral). In the past, certain coals were an important source.
</p><p>During the processing of bauxite to <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">alumina</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a>, gallium accumulates in the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> liquor. From this it can be extracted by a variety of methods. The most recent is the use of <a href="/wiki/Ion-exchange_resin" title="Ion-exchange resin">ion-exchange resin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Achievable extraction efficiencies critically depend on the original concentration in the feed bauxite. At a typical feed concentration of 50 ppm, about 15% of the contained gallium is extractable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> The remainder reports to the <a href="/wiki/Red_mud" title="Red mud">red mud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">aluminium hydroxide</a> streams. Gallium is removed from the ion-exchange resin in solution. Electrolysis then gives gallium metal. For <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> use, it is further purified with <a href="/wiki/Zone_melting" title="Zone melting">zone melting</a> or single-crystal extraction from a melt (<a href="/wiki/Czochralski_process" title="Czochralski process">Czochralski process</a>). Purities of 99.9999% are routinely achieved and commercially available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1324" data-file-width="2012" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg/220px-Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg/330px-Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg/440px-Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bauxite_Jamaica_1984.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bauxite mine in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> (1984).</div></div></div>
<p>Its by-product status means that gallium production is constrained by the amount of bauxite, sulfidic zinc ores (and coal) extracted per year. Therefore, its availability needs to be discussed in terms of supply potential. The supply potential of a by-product is defined as that amount which is economically extractable from its host materials <i>per year</i> under current market conditions (i.e. technology and price).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Reserves and resources are not relevant for by-products, since they <i>cannot</i> be extracted independently from the main-products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Recent estimates put the supply potential of gallium at a minimum of 2,100 t/yr from bauxite, 85 t/yr from sulfidic zinc ores, and potentially 590 t/yr from coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> These figures are significantly greater than current production (375 t in 2016).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Thus, major future increases in the by-product production of gallium will be possible without significant increases in production costs or price. The average gallium price in 2015 was $US317/kg, down from $US688/kg in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Semiconductor applications dominate the commercial demand for gallium, accounting for 98% of the total.  The next major application is for <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium_gallium_garnet" title="Gadolinium gallium garnet">gadolinium gallium garnets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductors">Semiconductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Semiconductors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg/220px-Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg/330px-Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg/440px-Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue_LED_and_Reflection.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gallium-based blue LEDs</div></div></div>
<p>Extremely high-purity (&gt;99.9999%) gallium is commercially available to serve the semiconductor industry. <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">Gallium arsenide</a> (GaAs) and <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> (GaN) used in electronic components represented about 98% of the gallium consumption in the United States in 2007. About 66% of semiconductor gallium is used in the U.S. in integrated circuits (mostly gallium arsenide), such as the manufacture of ultra-high-speed logic chips and <a href="/wiki/MESFET" title="MESFET">MESFETs</a> for low-noise microwave preamplifiers in cell phones. About 20% of this gallium is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optoelectronic" title="Optoelectronic">optoelectronics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2008_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2008-44">[44]</a></sup> Worldwide, gallium arsenide makes up 95% of the annual global gallium consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium arsenide is used in a variety of optoelectronic infrared devices. <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_gallium_arsenide" title="Aluminium gallium arsenide">Aluminium gallium arsenide</a> (AlGaAs) is used in high-power infrared laser diodes. The semiconductors gallium nitride and <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_nitride" title="Indium gallium nitride">indium gallium nitride</a> are used in blue and violet optoelectronic devices, mostly <a href="/wiki/Laser_diode" title="Laser diode">laser diodes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a>. For example, gallium nitride 405 nm diode lasers are used as a violet light source for higher-density <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Disc</a> compact data disc drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multijunction_photovoltaic_cell" title="Multijunction photovoltaic cell">Multijunction photovoltaic cells</a>, developed for <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> power applications, are made by <a href="/wiki/Molecular-beam_epitaxy" title="Molecular-beam epitaxy">molecular-beam epitaxy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapour-phase_epitaxy" title="Metalorganic vapour-phase epitaxy">metalorganic vapour-phase epitaxy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin films</a> of gallium arsenide, <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_phosphide" title="Indium gallium phosphide">indium gallium phosphide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_arsenide" title="Indium gallium arsenide">indium gallium arsenide</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rovers</a> and several satellites use triple-junction gallium arsenide on germanium cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Gallium is also a component in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> compounds (such as copper indium gallium selenium sulfide Cu(In,Ga)(Se,S)<sub>2</sub>) used in solar panels as a cost-efficient alternative to <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">crystalline silicon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galinstan_and_other_alloys">Galinstan and other alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Galinstan and other alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galinstan_on_glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="600" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Galinstan_on_glass.jpg/220px-Galinstan_on_glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Galinstan_on_glass.jpg/330px-Galinstan_on_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Galinstan_on_glass.jpg/440px-Galinstan_on_glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galinstan_on_glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Galinstan from a broken thermometer, easily wetting a piece of glass.</div></div></div>
<p>Gallium readily <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> with most metals, and is used as an ingredient in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-melting_alloy" title="Low-melting alloy">low-melting alloys</a>. The nearly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> alloy of gallium, <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> is a room temperature liquid used in medical thermometers. This alloy, with the trade-name <i><a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">Galinstan</a></i> (with the "-stan" referring to the tin, <i>stannum</i> in Latin), has a low freezing point of −19 °C (−2.2 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> It has been suggested that this family of alloys could also be used to cool computer chips in place of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Gallium alloys have been evaluated as substitutes for mercury <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_amalgam" title="Dental amalgam">dental amalgams</a>, but these materials have yet to see wide acceptance.
</p><p>Because gallium <a href="/wiki/Wetting" title="Wetting">wets</a> glass or <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>, gallium can be used to create brilliant <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>. When the wetting action of gallium-alloys is not desired (as in <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">Galinstan</a> glass thermometers), the glass must be protected with a transparent layer of <a href="/wiki/Gallium(III)_oxide" title="Gallium(III) oxide">gallium(III) oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium_pit" title="Plutonium pit">nuclear weapon pits</a> is stabilized in the <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_plutonium" title="Allotropes of plutonium">δ phase</a> and made machinable by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium-gallium_alloy" title="Plutonium-gallium alloy">alloying with gallium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomedical_applications">Biomedical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biomedical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although gallium has no natural function in biology, gallium ions interact with processes in the body in a manner similar to <a href="/wiki/Ferric#Ferric_iron_and_life" title="Ferric">iron(III)</a>. Because these processes include <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>, a marker for many disease states, several gallium salts are used (or are in development) as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceuticals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmacology" title="Radiopharmacology">radiopharmaceuticals</a> in medicine. Interest in the anticancer properties of gallium emerged when it was discovered that <sup>67</sup>Ga(III) citrate injected in tumor-bearing animals localized to sites of tumor. Clinical trials have shown gallium nitrate to have antineoplastic activity against non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma and urothelial cancers. A new generation of gallium-ligand complexes such as tris(8-quinolinolato)gallium(III) (KP46)
and gallium maltolate has emerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitrate" title="Gallium nitrate">Gallium nitrate</a> (brand name Ganite) has been used as an intravenous pharmaceutical to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypercalcemia" title="Hypercalcemia">hypercalcemia</a> associated with tumor <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasis</a> to bones. Gallium is thought to interfere with <a href="/wiki/Osteoclast" title="Osteoclast">osteoclast</a> function, and the therapy may be effective when other treatments have failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gallium_maltolate" title="Gallium maltolate">Gallium maltolate</a>, an oral, highly absorbable form of gallium(III) ion, is an anti-proliferative to pathologically proliferating cells, particularly cancer cells and some bacteria that accept it in place of ferric iron (Fe<sup>3+</sup>). Researchers are conducting clinical and preclinical trials on this compound as a potential treatment for a number of cancers, infectious diseases, and inflammatory diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>When gallium ions are mistakenly taken up in place of iron(III) by bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas" title="Pseudomonas">Pseudomonas</a></i>, the ions interfere with respiration, and the bacteria die. This happens because iron is redox-active, allowing the transfer of electrons during respiration, while gallium is redox-inactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>A complex <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a>-<a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> Ga(III) compound MR045 is selectively toxic to parasites resistant to <a href="/wiki/Chloroquine" title="Chloroquine">chloroquine</a>, a common drug against <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>. Both the Ga(III) complex and chloroquine act by inhibiting crystallization of <a href="/wiki/Hemozoin" title="Hemozoin">hemozoin</a>, a disposal product formed from the digestion of blood by the parasites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiogallium_salts">Radiogallium salts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Radiogallium salts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallium-67" title="Gallium-67">Gallium-67</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> such as gallium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrate" title="Citrate">citrate</a> and gallium <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> are used as <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceutical</a> agents in the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> imaging known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallium_67_scan" title="Gallium 67 scan">gallium scan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radioactive isotope</a> <sup>67</sup>Ga is used, and the compound or salt of gallium is unimportant. The body handles Ga<sup>3+</sup> in many ways as though it were Fe<sup>3+</sup>, and the ion is bound (and concentrates) in areas of inflammation, such as infection, and in areas of rapid cell division. This allows such sites to be imaged by nuclear scan techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordberg_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nordberg-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallium-68" title="Gallium-68">Gallium-68</a>, a positron emitter with a half-life of 68 min, is now used as a diagnostic radionuclide in PET-CT when linked to pharmaceutical preparations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DOTATOC" title="DOTATOC">DOTATOC</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Somatostatin" title="Somatostatin">somatostatin</a> analogue used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine_tumors" title="Neuroendocrine tumors">neuroendocrine tumors</a> investigation, and <a href="/wiki/DOTA-TATE" title="DOTA-TATE">DOTA-TATE</a>, a newer one, used for neuroendocrine <a href="/wiki/Metastasis" title="Metastasis">metastasis</a> and lung neuroendocrine cancer, such as certain types of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Microcytoma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Microcytoma (page does not exist)">microcytoma</a></i>. Gallium-68's preparation as a pharmaceutical is chemical, and the radionuclide is extracted by <a href="/wiki/Elution" title="Elution">elution</a> from germanium-68, a <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">synthetic radioisotope</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Gallium-68_generator" title="Gallium-68 generator">gallium-68 generators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gallium is used for <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> detection. Possibly the largest amount of pure gallium ever collected in a single spot is the Gallium-Germanium Neutrino Telescope used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAGE_(Soviet-American_Gallium_Experiment)" title="SAGE (Soviet-American Gallium Experiment)">SAGE</a> experiment at the Baksan Neutrino Observatory in Russia. This detector contains 55–57 tonnes (~9 cubic metres) of liquid gallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Another experiment was the <a href="/wiki/GALLEX" title="GALLEX">GALLEX</a> neutrino detector operated in the early 1990s in an Italian mountain tunnel. The detector contained 12.2 tons of watered gallium-71. Solar neutrinos caused a few atoms of <sup>71</sup>Ga to become radioactive <sup>71</sup><a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">Ge</a>, which were detected. This experiment showed that the solar neutrino flux is 40% less than theory predicted. This deficit was not explained until better solar neutrino detectors and theories were constructed (see <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Neutrino_Observatory" title="Sudbury Neutrino Observatory">SNO</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gallium is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal_ion_source" title="Liquid metal ion source">liquid metal ion source</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Focused_ion_beam" title="Focused ion beam">focused ion beam</a>. For example, a focused gallium-ion beam was used to create the world's smallest book, <i><a href="/wiki/Teeny_Ted_from_Turnip_Town" title="Teeny Ted from Turnip Town">Teeny Ted from Turnip Town</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pr_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-pr-74">[74]</a></sup> Another use of gallium is as an additive in glide wax for skis, and other low-friction surface materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>A well-known <a href="/wiki/Practical_joke" title="Practical joke">practical joke</a> among chemists is to fashion gallium spoons and use them to serve tea to unsuspecting guests, since gallium has a similar appearance to its lighter homolog aluminium. The spoons then melt in the hot tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sam_Kean2010_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sam_Kean2010-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metallic gallium is not toxic. However, exposure to gallium halide complexes can result in acute toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The Ga<sup>3+</sup> ion of soluble gallium salts tends to form the insoluble hydroxide when injected in large doses; precipitation of this hydroxide resulted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal" title="Renal">renal</a> toxicity in animals. In lower doses, soluble gallium is tolerated well and does not accumulate as a poison, instead being excreted mostly through urine. Excretion of gallium occurs in two phases: the first phase has a <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> of 1 hour, while the second has a biological half-life of 25 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordberg_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordberg-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
