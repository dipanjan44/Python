<p><b>Scandium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Sc</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 21. A silvery-white metallic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-block" title="D-block">d-block element</a>, it has historically been classified as a <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth element</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> together with <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>. It was discovered in 1879 by spectral analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.
</p><p>Scandium is present in most of the deposits of rare-earth and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> compounds, but it is extracted from these ores in only a few mines worldwide. Because of the low availability and the difficulties in the preparation of metallic scandium, which was first done in 1937, applications for scandium were not developed until the 1970s. The positive effects of scandium on <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">aluminium alloys</a> were discovered in the 1970s, and its use in such alloys remains its only major application. The global trade of scandium oxide is about 10 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> per year.
</p><p>The properties of scandium compounds are intermediate between those of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Diagonal_relationship" title="Diagonal relationship">diagonal relationship</a> exists between the behavior of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and scandium, just as there is between <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> and aluminium. In the chemical compounds of the elements in group 3, the predominant <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> is +3.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_characteristics">Chemical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scandium is a soft metal with a silvery appearance. It develops a slightly yellowish or pinkish cast when <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidized</a> by air. It is susceptible to weathering and dissolves slowly in most dilute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acids" title="Acids">acids</a>. It does not react with a 1:1 mixture of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> (HNO<sub>3</sub>) and 48% <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> (HF), possibly due to the formation of an impermeable <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passive layer</a>. Scandium turnings ignite in air with a brilliant yellow flame to form <a href="/wiki/Scandium_oxide" title="Scandium oxide">scandium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_scandium" title="Isotopes of scandium">Isotopes of scandium</a></div>
<p>In nature, scandium is found exclusively as the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> <sup>45</sup>Sc, which has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_spin" title="Nuclear spin">nuclear spin</a> of 7/2; this is its only stable isotope. Thirteen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been characterized with the most stable being <sup>46</sup>Sc, which has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 83.8 days; <sup>47</sup>Sc, 3.35 days; the <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> emitter <a href="/wiki/Scandium-44" title="Scandium-44"><sup>44</sup>Sc</a>, 4 h; and <sup>48</sup>Sc, 43.7 hours. All of the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives less than 4 hours, and the majority of these have half-lives less than 2 minutes. This element also has five <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a>, with the most stable being <sup>44m</sup>Sc (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> = 58.6 h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The isotopes of scandium range from <sup>36</sup>Sc to <sup>60</sup>Sc. The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> at masses lower than the only stable isotope, <sup>45</sup>Sc, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, and the primary mode at masses above it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> at atomic weights below <sup>45</sup>Sc are <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> isotopes and the primary products from higher atomic weights are <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>, scandium is not rare. Estimates vary from 18 to 25 ppm, which is comparable to the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> (20–30 ppm). Scandium is only the 50th most common element on Earth (35th most abundant in the crust), but it is the 23rd most common element in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rubber_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rubber-8">[8]</a></sup> However, scandium is distributed sparsely and occurs in trace amounts in many <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Rare minerals from Scandinavia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thort_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thort-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mada_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mada-11">[11]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Thortveitite" title="Thortveitite">thortveitite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> are the only known concentrated sources of this element. Thortveitite can contain up to 45% of scandium in the form of <a href="/wiki/Scandium_oxide" title="Scandium oxide">scandium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thort_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thort-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stable form of scandium is created in <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovas</a> via the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The world production of scandium is in the order of 15 tonnes per year, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Scandium_oxide" title="Scandium oxide">scandium oxide</a>. The demand is about 50% higher, and both the production and demand keep increasing. In 2003, only three mines produced scandium: the uranium and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> mines in <a href="/wiki/Zhovti_Vody" title="Zhovti Vody">Zhovti Vody</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, the rare-earth mines in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayan_Obo" title="Bayan Obo">Bayan Obo</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and the apatite mines in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kola_peninsula" title="Kola peninsula">Kola peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>; since then many other countries have built scandium-producing facilities, including 5 tonnes/year (7.5 tonnes/year Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) by <a href="/wiki/Nickel_Asia_Corporation" title="Nickel Asia Corporation">Nickel Asia Corporation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Metal_Mining" title="Sumitomo Metal Mining">Sumitomo Metal Mining</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMMPressRelease_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMMPressRelease-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMMAbstract_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMMAbstract-14">[14]</a></sup> In each case scandium is a byproduct from the extraction of other elements and is sold as scandium oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deschamps_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deschamps-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2015_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2015-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>To produce metallic scandium, the oxide is converted to <a href="/wiki/Scandium_fluoride" title="Scandium fluoride">scandium fluoride</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> with metallic <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iveland" title="Iveland">Iveland</a>-<a href="/wiki/Evje" title="Evje">Evje</a> region in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> have the only deposits of minerals with high scandium content, <a href="/wiki/Thortveitite" title="Thortveitite">thortveitite</a> (Sc,Y)<sub>2</sub>(Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Kolbeckite" title="Kolbeckite">kolbeckite</a> ScPO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, but these are not being exploited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2015_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2015-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The absence of reliable, secure, stable, long-term production has limited the commercial applications of scandium. Despite this low level of use, scandium offers significant benefits. Particularly promising is the strengthening of aluminium alloys with as little as 0.5% scandium. Scandium-stabilized zirconia enjoys a growing market demand for use as a high-efficiency <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> in <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_fuel_cell" title="Solid oxide fuel cell">solid oxide fuel cells</a>. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of its rarity, scandium is among the most expensive elements. Price for pure scandium fluctuates between 4,000 and 20,000 US dollars per kilogram. Meanwhile, the limited market generates a variety of prices at any given time. In 2010, at the peak of the rare-earths shortage, the price of scandium rose to over 15,000 US dollars per kilogram, and the widely commercially used scandium oxide (Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) was selling above 7 000 US dollars per kilogram. Since then the limited demand coupled with steady production keeps the price at its 20-year average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides_and_pseudohalides">Halides and pseudohalides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Halides and pseudohalides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> ScX<sub>3</sub>, where X= <a href="/wiki/Scandium_chloride" title="Scandium chloride">Cl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandium_bromide" title="Scandium bromide">Br</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Scandium_triiodide" title="Scandium triiodide">I</a>, are very soluble in water, but <a href="/wiki/Scandium_fluoride" title="Scandium fluoride">ScF<sub>3</sub></a> is insoluble. In all four halides, the scandium is 6-coordinated. The halides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acids" title="Lewis acids">Lewis acids</a>; for example, <a href="/wiki/Scandium_fluoride" title="Scandium fluoride">ScF<sub>3</sub></a> dissolves in a solution containing excess fluoride ion to form [ScF<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup>. The coordination number 6 is typical for Sc(III). In the larger Y<sup>3+</sup> and La<sup>3+</sup> ions, <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination numbers</a> of 8 and 9 are common. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium_triflate" title="Scandium triflate">Scandium triflate</a> is sometimes used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> catalyst in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_derivatives">Organic derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organic derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organoscandium_chemistry" title="Organoscandium chemistry">Organoscandium chemistry</a></div>
<p>Scandium forms a series of organometallic compounds with <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">cyclopentadienyl</a> ligands (Cp), similar to the behavior of the lanthanides. One example is the chlorine-bridged dimer, [ScCp<sub>2</sub>Cl]<sub>2</sub> and related derivatives of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl" title="Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl">pentamethylcyclopentadienyl</a> ligands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncommon_oxidation_states">Uncommon oxidation states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uncommon oxidation states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Compounds that feature scandium in the oxidation state other than +3 are rare but well characterized. The blue-black compound CsScCl<sub>3</sub> is one of the simplest. This material adopts a sheet-like structure that exhibits extensive bonding between the scandium(II) centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Scandium_hydride" title="Scandium hydride">Scandium hydride</a> is not well understood, although it appears not to be a <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">saline hydride</a> of Sc(II).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGuire_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-McGuire-2">[2]</a></sup> As is observed for most elements, a diatomic scandium hydride has been observed spectroscopically at high temperatures in the gas phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-3">[3]</a></sup> Scandium borides and carbides are <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric</a>, as is typical for neighboring elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lower oxidation states (+2, +1, 0) have also been observed in organoscandium compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a>, who is referred to as the father of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>, predicted the existence of an element <i><a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements#Ekaboron_and_scandium" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">ekaboron</a></i>, with an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> between 40 and 48 in 1869. <a href="/wiki/Lars_Fredrik_Nilson" title="Lars Fredrik Nilson">Lars Fredrik Nilson</a> and his team <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">detected this element</a> in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> in 1879. Nilson prepared 2 grams of <a href="/wiki/Scandium_oxide" title="Scandium oxide">scandium oxide</a> of high purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nilsonfr_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nilsonfr-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nilsonde_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nilsonde-29">[29]</a></sup> He named the element scandium, from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Scandia</i> meaning "Scandinavia". Nilson was apparently unaware of Mendeleev's prediction, but <a href="/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve" title="Per Teodor Cleve">Per Teodor Cleve</a> recognized the correspondence and notified Mendeleev.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Metallic scandium was produced for the first time in 1937 by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> mixture of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scandium_chloride" title="Scandium chloride">scandium chlorides</a>, at 700–800 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The first pound of 99% pure scandium metal was produced in 1960. Production of aluminium alloys began in 1971, following a US patent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Aluminium-scandium alloys were also developed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zark_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zark-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Laser crystals of gadolinium-scandium-gallium garnet (GSGG) were used in strategic defense applications developed for the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a> (SDI) in the 1980s and 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_Giants_near_Galactic_center">Red Giants near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">Galactic center</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Red Giants near Galactic center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early 2018, evidence was gathered from <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a> data of significant scandium, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> abundances in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Giant" title="Red Giant">Red Giant</a> suns in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_Star_Cluster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nuclear Star Cluster (page does not exist)">Nuclear Star Cluster</a> (NSC) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">Galactic center</a>. Further research showed that this was an illusion caused by the relatively low temperature (below 3,500 K) of these stars masking the abundance signals, and that this phenomenon was observable in other Red Giants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-””_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-””-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mig-29_on_landing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Mig-29_on_landing.jpg/220px-Mig-29_on_landing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Mig-29_on_landing.jpg/330px-Mig-29_on_landing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Mig-29_on_landing.jpg/440px-Mig-29_on_landing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mig-29_on_landing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-29" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-29">MiG-29</a> are made from Al-Sc alloy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmad2003_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmad2003-37">[37]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The addition of scandium to aluminium limits the grain growth in the heat zone of welded aluminium components. This has two beneficial effects: the precipitated Al<sub>3</sub>Sc forms smaller crystals than in other <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">aluminium alloys</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmad2003_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmad2003-37">[37]</a></sup> and the volume of precipitate-free zones at the grain boundaries of age-hardening aluminium alloys is reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmad2003_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmad2003-37">[37]</a></sup> Both of these effects increase the usefulness of the alloy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=" (May 2013)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_alloy" title="Titanium alloy">titanium alloys</a>, which are similar in lightness and strength, are cheaper and much more widely used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarz2004_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarz2004-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alloy Al<sub>20</sub>Li<sub>20</sub>Mg<sub>10</sub>Sc<sub>20</sub>Ti<sub>30</sub> is as strong as titanium, light as aluminium, and hard as ceramic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main application of scandium by weight is in aluminium-scandium alloys for minor aerospace industry components. These alloys contain between 0.1% and 0.5% of scandium. They were used in the Russian military aircraft, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-21" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-21">MiG-21</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan_MiG-29" title="Mikoyan MiG-29">MiG-29</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmad2003_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmad2003-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some items of sports equipment, which rely on high-performance materials, have been made with scandium-aluminium alloys, including <a href="/wiki/Baseball_bat" title="Baseball bat">baseball bats</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bat_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bat-40">[40]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_frame" title="Bicycle frame">bicycle frames</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_bicycle_parts" title="List of bicycle parts">components</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacrosse_sticks" title="Lacrosse sticks">Lacrosse sticks</a> are also made with scandium. The American firearm manufacturing company <a href="/wiki/Smith_%26_Wesson" title="Smith &amp; Wesson">Smith &amp; Wesson</a> produces semi-automatic pistols and revolvers with frames of scandium alloy and cylinders of titanium or carbon steel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James2004_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-James2004-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweeney2004_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeney2004-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dentists use erbium-chromium-doped yttrium-scandium-gallium garnet (Er,Cr:YSGG) lasers for cavity preparation and in endodontics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first scandium-based metal-halide lamps were patented by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> and initially made in North America, although they are now produced in all major industrialized countries. Approximately 20 kg of scandium (as Sc<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub>) is used annually in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for high-intensity discharge lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-45">[45]</a></sup> One type of <a href="/wiki/Metal-halide_lamp" title="Metal-halide lamp">metal-halide lamp</a>, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Mercury-vapor_lamp" title="Mercury-vapor lamp">mercury-vapor lamp</a>, is made from <a href="/wiki/Scandium_triiodide" title="Scandium triiodide">scandium triiodide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a>. This lamp is a white-light source with high <a href="/wiki/Color_rendering_index" title="Color rendering index">color rendering index</a> that sufficiently resembles sunlight to allow good color-reproduction with <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">TV</a> cameras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> About 80 kg of scandium is used in metal-halide lamps/light bulbs globally per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="80kg? That's got to be wrong - too small a number to track (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotope" title="Radioactive isotope">radioactive isotope</a> <sup>46</sup>Sc is used in <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a> as a tracing agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-45">[45]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium_triflate" title="Scandium triflate">Scandium triflate</a> is a catalytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety">Health and safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Health and safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elemental scandium is considered non-toxic, though extensive animal testing of scandium compounds has not been done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) levels for <a href="/wiki/Scandium_chloride" title="Scandium chloride">scandium chloride</a> for rats have been determined as 4 mg/kg for <a href="/wiki/Intraperitoneal_injection" title="Intraperitoneal injection">intraperitoneal</a> and 755 mg/kg for oral administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In the light of these results, compounds of scandium should be handled as compounds of moderate toxicity.
</p>
