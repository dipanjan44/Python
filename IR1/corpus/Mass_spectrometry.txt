<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mass spectrometry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1977" data-file-width="2768" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg/220px-LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg/330px-LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg/440px-LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LTQ_OrbiTrap_XL-Thermo_Scientific_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orbitrap mass spectrometer</div></div></div>
<p><b>Mass spectrometry</b> (<b>MS</b>) is an analytical technique that ionizes <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> and sorts the <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> based on their <a href="/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio" title="Mass-to-charge ratio">mass-to-charge ratio</a>. In simpler terms, a <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrum" title="Mass spectrum">mass spectrum</a> measures the masses within a sample. Mass spectrometry is used in many different fields and is applied to pure samples as well as complex mixtures.
</p><p>A mass spectrum is a plot of the ion signal as a function of the mass-to-charge ratio. These spectra are used to determine the elemental or <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">isotopic signature</a> of a sample, the masses of particles and of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, and to elucidate the chemical structures of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>.
</p><p>In a typical MS procedure, a sample, which may be solid, liquid, or gas, is ionized, for example by bombarding it with electrons. This may cause some of the sample's molecules to break into charged fragments. These ions are then separated according to their mass-to-charge ratio, typically by accelerating them and subjecting them to an electric or magnetic field: ions of the same mass-to-charge ratio will undergo the same amount of deflection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-9660813-2-3_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-9660813-2-3-1">[1]</a></sup> The ions are detected by a mechanism capable of detecting charged particles, such as an <a href="/wiki/Electron_multiplier" title="Electron multiplier">electron multiplier</a>. Results are displayed as spectra of the relative abundance of detected ions as a function of the mass-to-charge ratio. The atoms or molecules in the sample can be identified by correlating known masses (e.g. an entire molecule) to the identified masses or through a characteristic fragmentation pattern.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_mass_spectrometry" title="History of mass spectrometry">History of mass spectrometry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Early_Mass_Spectrometer_(replica).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="610" data-file-width="1926" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg/220px-Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg/330px-Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg/440px-Early_Mass_Spectrometer_%28replica%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Early_Mass_Spectrometer_(replica).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Replica of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.J._Thomson" title="J.J. Thomson">J.J. Thomson</a>'s third mass spectrometer</div></div></div>
<p>In 1886, <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Goldstein" title="Eugen Goldstein">Eugen Goldstein</a> observed rays in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_discharge" title="Gas discharge">gas discharges</a> under low pressure that traveled away from the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> and through channels in a perforated <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>, opposite to the direction of negatively charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode_rays" title="Cathode rays">cathode rays</a> (which travel from cathode to anode). Goldstein called these positively charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anode_rays" title="Anode rays">anode rays</a> "Kanalstrahlen"; the standard translation of this term into English is "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_rays" title="Canal rays">canal rays</a>". <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wien" title="Wilhelm Wien">Wilhelm Wien</a> found that strong electric or magnetic fields deflected the canal rays and, in 1899, constructed a device with perpendicular electric and magnetic fields that separated the positive rays according to their charge-to-mass ratio (<i>Q/m</i>). Wien found that the charge-to-mass ratio depended on the nature of the gas in the discharge tube. English scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.J._Thomson" title="J.J. Thomson">J.J. Thomson</a> later improved on the work of Wien by reducing the pressure to create the mass spectrograph.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="709" data-file-width="480" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg/220px-Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg/330px-Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg/440px-Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alpha_calutron_tank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Calutron mass spectrometers were used in the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> for uranium enrichment.</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>spectrograph</i> had become part of the <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">international scientific vocabulary</a> by 1884.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Early <i>spectrometry</i> devices that measured the mass-to-charge ratio of ions were called <i><a href="/wiki/Spectrograph" title="Spectrograph">mass spectrographs</a></i> which consisted of instruments that recorded a <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> of mass values on a <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a804629h_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-a804629h-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A <i>mass spectroscope</i> is similar to a <i>mass spectrograph</i> except that the beam of ions is directed onto a <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a> screen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A mass spectroscope configuration was  used in early instruments when it was desired that the effects of adjustments be quickly observed. Once the instrument was properly adjusted, a photographic plate was inserted and exposed. The term mass spectroscope continued to be used even though the direct illumination of a phosphor screen was replaced by indirect measurements with an <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siri_1947_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siri_1947-7">[7]</a></sup> The use of the term <i>mass spectroscopy</i> is now discouraged due to the possibility of confusion with light <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-9660813-2-3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-9660813-2-3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price_1991_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Price_1991-8">[8]</a></sup> Mass spectrometry is often abbreviated as <i>mass-spec</i> or simply as <i>MS</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-9660813-2-3_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-9660813-2-3-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern techniques of mass spectrometry were devised by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Jeffrey_Dempster" title="Arthur Jeffrey Dempster">Arthur Jeffrey Dempster</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F.W._Aston" title="F.W. Aston">F.W. Aston</a> in 1918 and 1919 respectively.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sector_instrument" title="Sector instrument">Sector mass spectrometers</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Calutron" title="Calutron">calutrons</a> were developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_O._Lawrence" title="Ernest O. Lawrence">Ernest O. Lawrence</a> and used for separating the <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_uranium" title="Isotopes of uranium">isotopes of uranium</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSTI_ID:_20699216_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSTI_ID:_20699216-9">[9]</a></sup> Calutron mass spectrometers were used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_enrichment" title="Uranium enrichment">uranium enrichment</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge,_Tennessee" title="Oak Ridge, Tennessee">Oak Ridge, Tennessee</a> <a href="/wiki/Y-12_National_Security_Complex" title="Y-12 National Security Complex">Y-12 plant</a> established during World War II.
</p><p>In 1989, half of the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> was awarded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hans_Dehmelt" title="Hans Dehmelt">Hans Dehmelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Paul" title="Wolfgang Paul">Wolfgang Paul</a> for the development of the ion trap technique in the 1950s and 1960s.
</p><p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> was awarded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Bennett_Fenn" title="John Bennett Fenn">John Bennett Fenn</a> for the development of <a href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization" title="Electrospray ionization">electrospray ionization</a> (ESI) and <a href="/wiki/Koichi_Tanaka" title="Koichi Tanaka">Koichi Tanaka</a> for the development of <a href="/wiki/Soft_laser_desorption" title="Soft laser desorption">soft laser desorption</a> (SLD) and their application to the ionization of biological macromolecules, especially proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts_of_a_mass_spectrometer">Parts of a mass spectrometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Parts of a mass spectrometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="493" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg/220px-Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg/330px-Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg/440px-Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mass_Spectrometer_Schematic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematics of a simple mass spectrometer with sector type mass analyzer. This one is for the measurement of carbon dioxide <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> ratios (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_ratio_mass_spectrometry" title="Isotope ratio mass spectrometry">IRMS</a>) as in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a> <a href="/wiki/Urea_breath_test" title="Urea breath test">urea breath test</a></div></div></div>
<p>A mass spectrometer consists of three components: an ion source, a mass analyzer, and a detector. The <i><a href="/wiki/Ion_source" title="Ion source">ionizer</a></i> converts a portion of the sample into ions.  There is a wide variety of ionization techniques, depending on the phase (solid, liquid, gas) of the sample and the efficiency of various ionization mechanisms for the unknown species. An extraction system removes ions from the sample, which are then targeted through the mass analyzer and into the <i>detector</i>.  The differences in masses of the fragments allows the mass analyzer to sort the ions by their mass-to-charge ratio. The detector measures the value of an indicator quantity and thus provides data for calculating the abundances of each ion present. Some detectors also give spatial information, e.g., a multichannel plate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_example">Theoretical example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following example describes the operation of a spectrometer mass analyzer, which is of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sector_instrument" title="Sector instrument">sector</a> type. (Other analyzer types are treated below.) Consider a sample of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> (table salt). In the ion source, the sample is <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vaporized</a> (turned into <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>) and ionized (transformed into electrically charged particles) into <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (Na<sup>+</sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> (Cl<sup>−</sup>) ions. Sodium atoms and ions are <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic</a>, with a mass of about 23 u. Chloride atoms and ions come in two isotopes with masses of approximately 35 u (at a natural abundance of about 75 percent) and approximately 37 u (at a natural abundance of about 25 percent). The analyzer part of the spectrometer contains <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> fields, which exert forces on ions traveling through these fields. The speed of a charged particle may be increased or decreased while passing through the electric field, and its direction may be altered by the magnetic field. The magnitude of the deflection of the moving ion's trajectory depends on its mass-to-charge ratio. Lighter ions get deflected by the magnetic force more than heavier ions (based on <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's second law of motion</a>, <i>F</i> = <i>ma</i>). The streams of sorted ions pass from the analyzer to the detector, which records the relative abundance of each ion type. This information is used to determine the chemical element composition of the original sample (i.e. that both sodium and chlorine are present in the sample) and the isotopic composition of its constituents (the ratio of <sup>35</sup>Cl to <sup>37</sup>Cl).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creating_ions">Creating ions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Creating ions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1395" data-file-width="2100" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg/220px-CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg/330px-CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg/440px-CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CARIBU_at_ATLAS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_ionization" title="Surface ionization">Surface ionization</a> source at the <a href="/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory" title="Argonne National Laboratory">Argonne National Laboratory</a> linear accelerator</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ion_source" title="Ion source">ion source</a> is the part of the mass spectrometer that ionizes the material under analysis (the analyte). The ions are then transported by <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric fields</a> to the mass analyzer.
</p><p>Techniques for ionization have been key to determining what types of samples can be analyzed by mass spectrometry.
<a href="/wiki/Electron_ionization" title="Electron ionization">Electron ionization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_ionization" title="Chemical ionization">chemical ionization</a> are used for <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapors</a>. In chemical ionization sources, the analyte is ionized by chemical ion-molecule reactions during collisions in the source. Two techniques often used with <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> biological samples include <a href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization" title="Electrospray ionization">electrospray ionization</a> (invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Bennett_Fenn" title="John Bennett Fenn">John Fenn</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization</a> (MALDI, initially developed as a similar technique "Soft Laser Desorption (SLD)" by K. Tanaka<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> for which a Nobel Prize was awarded and as MALDI by M. Karas and F. Hillenkamp<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_ionization_and_soft_ionization">Hard ionization and soft ionization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hard ionization and soft ionization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1914" data-file-width="2000" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg/220px-Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg/330px-Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg/440px-Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fenn_ESI_Instrument.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Quadrupole mass spectrometer and electrospray ion source used for Fenn's early work</div></div></div>
<p>In mass spectrometry, ionization refers to the production of gas phase ions suitable for resolution in the mass analyser or mass filter. Ionization occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Ion_source" title="Ion source">ion source</a>. There are several <a href="/wiki/Ion_source" title="Ion source">ion sources</a> available; each has advantages and disadvantages for particular applications. For example, <a href="/wiki/Electron_ionization" title="Electron ionization">electron ionization</a> (EI) gives a high degree of fragmentation, yielding highly detailed mass spectra which when skilfully analysed can provide important information for structural elucidation/characterisation and facilitate identification of unknown compounds by comparison to mass spectral libraries obtained under identical operating conditions. However, EI is not suitable for coupling to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High performance liquid chromatography">HPLC</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry">LC-MS</a>, since at atmospheric pressure, the filaments used to generate electrons burn out rapidly. Thus EI is coupled predominantly with <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography" title="Gas chromatography">GC</a>, i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GC-MS" title="GC-MS">GC-MS</a>, where the entire system is under high vacuum.
</p><p>Hard ionization techniques are processes which impart high quantities of residual energy in the subject molecule invoking large degrees of fragmentation (i.e. the systematic rupturing of bonds acts to remove the excess energy, restoring stability to the resulting ion). Resultant ions tend to have <i>m/z</i> lower than the molecular mass (other than in the case of proton transfer and not including isotope peaks). The most common example of hard ionization is electron ionization (EI).
</p><p>Soft ionization refers to the processes which impart little residual energy onto the subject molecule and as such result in little fragmentation. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Fast_atom_bombardment" title="Fast atom bombardment">fast atom bombardment</a> (FAB), <a href="/wiki/Chemical_ionization" title="Chemical ionization">chemical ionization</a> (CI), <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric-pressure_chemical_ionization" title="Atmospheric-pressure chemical ionization">atmospheric-pressure chemical ionization</a> (APCI), <a href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization" title="Electrospray ionization">electrospray ionization</a> (ESI), and <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization</a> (MALDI).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inductively_coupled_plasma">Inductively coupled plasma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inductively coupled plasma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2976" data-file-width="3968" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG/220px-ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG/330px-ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG/440px-ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ICPMS_Thermo_torch_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inductively coupled plasma ion source</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICP-MS" title="ICP-MS">Inductively coupled plasma</a> (ICP) sources are used primarily for cation analysis of a wide array of sample types. In this source, a plasma that is electrically neutral overall, but that has had a substantial fraction of its atoms ionized by high temperature, is used to atomize introduced sample molecules and to further strip the outer electrons from those atoms. The plasma is usually generated from argon gas, since the first ionization energy of argon atoms is higher than the first of any other elements except He, O, F and Ne, but lower than the second ionization energy of all except the most electropositive metals. The heating is achieved by a radio-frequency current passed through a coil surrounding the plasma.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoionization_mass_spectrometry">Photoionization mass spectrometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Photoionization mass spectrometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Photoionization" title="Photoionization">Photoionization</a> can be used in experiments which seek to use mass spectrometry as a means of resolving chemical kinetics mechanisms and isomeric product branching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In such instances a high energy photon, either X-ray or uv, is used to dissociate stable gaseous molecules in a carrier gas of He or Ar. In instances where a <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">synchrotron</a> light source is utilzed, a tuneable photon energy can be utilized to acquire a photoionization efficiency curve which can be used in conjunction with the charge ratio m/z to fingerprint molecular and ionic species.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_ionization_techniques">Other ionization techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other ionization techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Others include <a href="/wiki/Glow_discharge" title="Glow discharge">glow discharge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_desorption" title="Field desorption">field desorption</a> (FD), <a href="/wiki/Fast_atom_bombardment" title="Fast atom bombardment">fast atom bombardment</a> (FAB), <a href="/wiki/Thermospray" title="Thermospray">thermospray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desorption/ionization_on_silicon" title="Desorption/ionization on silicon">desorption/ionization on silicon</a> (DIOS), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DART_ion_source" title="DART ion source">Direct Analysis in Real Time</a> (DART), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure_chemical_ionization" title="Atmospheric pressure chemical ionization">atmospheric pressure chemical ionization</a> (APCI), <a href="/wiki/Secondary_ion_mass_spectrometry" title="Secondary ion mass spectrometry">secondary ion mass spectrometry</a> (SIMS), <a href="/wiki/Spark_ionization" title="Spark ionization">spark ionization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_ionization" title="Thermal ionization">thermal ionization</a> (TIMS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

</math></span><img alt="(m/Q)\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{E}+ \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}." aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a9168ed4f7208ae94e0518561ed71166491b48a" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:22.645ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span></dd></dl>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equation</a> is the classic equation of motion for <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a>. Together with the particle's initial conditions, it completely determines the particle's motion in space and time in terms of <i>m/Q</i>. Thus mass spectrometers could be thought of as "mass-to-charge spectrometers". When presenting data, it is common to use the (officially) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless" title="Dimensionless">dimensionless</a> <i>m/z</i>, where z is the number of <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary charges</a> (<i>e</i>) on the ion (z=Q/e). This quantity, although it is informally called the mass-to-charge ratio, more accurately speaking represents the ratio of the mass number and the charge number, <i>z</i>.
</p><p>There are many types of mass analyzers, using either static or dynamic fields, and magnetic or electric fields, but all operate according to the above differential equation. Each analyzer type has its strengths and weaknesses. Many mass spectrometers use two or more mass analyzers for <a href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometry" title="Tandem mass spectrometry">tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS)</a>. In addition to the more common mass analyzers listed below, there are others designed for special situations.
</p><p>There are several important analyser characteristics. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_resolving_power" title="Mass resolving power">mass resolving power</a> is the measure of the ability to distinguish two peaks of slightly different <i>m/z</i>. The mass accuracy is the ratio of the <i>m/z</i> measurement error to the true m/z. Mass accuracy is usually measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Milli_mass_unit" title="Milli mass unit">milli mass units</a>. The mass range is the range of <i>m/z</i> amenable to analysis by a given analyzer. The linear dynamic range is the range over which ion signal is linear with analyte concentration. Speed refers to the time frame of the experiment and ultimately is used to determine the number of spectra per unit time that can be generated.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sector_instruments">Sector instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sector instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FAB_MS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1264" data-file-width="2256" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FAB_MS.jpg/220px-FAB_MS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FAB_MS.jpg/330px-FAB_MS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FAB_MS.jpg/440px-FAB_MS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FAB_MS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ThermoQuest AvantGarde sector mass spectrometer</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sector_mass_spectrometer" title="Sector mass spectrometer">Sector mass spectrometer</a></div>
<p>A sector field mass analyzer uses a static electric and/or magnetic field to affect the path and/or <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charged</a> particles in some way.
As shown above, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sector_instrument" title="Sector instrument">sector instruments</a> bend the trajectories of the ions as they pass through the mass analyzer, according to their mass-to-charge ratios, deflecting the more charged and faster-moving, lighter ions more. The analyzer can be used to select a narrow range of <i>m/z</i> or to scan through a range of <i>m/z</i> to catalog the ions present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time-of-flight">Time-of-flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Time-of-flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Time-of-flight_mass_spectrometry" title="Time-of-flight mass spectrometry">time-of-flight mass spectrometry</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-of-flight" title="Time-of-flight">time-of-flight</a> (TOF) analyzer uses an <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> to accelerate the ions through the same <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">potential</a>, and then measures the time they take to reach the detector. If the particles all have the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_charge" title="Electrical charge">charge</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energies</a> tends to be identical, and their <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocities</a>, in this case, will depend only on their <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">masses</a>. Ions with a lower mass will reach the detector first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> However, in reality, even particles with the same m/z can arrive at different times at the detector, because they have different initial velocities. The initial velocity is not dependent on the mass of the ion what becomes a problem for the TOF-MS. The difference in initial velocity turns into difference in the final velocity. In this way, ions with the same m/z are going to arrive at different times in the detector. For fixing this problem, time-lag focusing/<a href="/wiki/Delayed_extraction" title="Delayed extraction">delayed extraction</a> has been coupled with TOF-MS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadrupole_mass_filter">Quadrupole mass filter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Quadrupole mass filter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_mass_analyzer" title="Quadrupole mass analyzer">Quadrupole mass analyzer</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_mass_analyzer" title="Quadrupole mass analyzer">Quadrupole mass analyzers</a> use oscillating electrical fields to selectively stabilize or destabilize the paths of ions passing through a <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> (RF) <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">quadrupole</a> field created between 4 parallel rods. Only the ions in a certain range of mass/charge ratio are passed through the system at any time, but changes to the potentials on the rods allow a wide range of m/z values to be swept rapidly, either continuously or in a succession of discrete hops. A quadrupole mass analyzer acts as a mass-selective filter and is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap" title="Quadrupole ion trap">quadrupole ion trap</a>, particularly the linear quadrupole ion trap except that it is designed to pass the untrapped ions rather than collect the trapped ones, and is for that reason referred to as a transmission quadrupole.
A magnetically enhanced quadrupole mass analyzer includes the addition of a magnetic field, either applied axially or transversely. This novel type of instrument leads to an additional performance enhancement in terms of resolution and/or sensitivity depending upon the magnitude and orientation of the applied magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> 
A common variation of the transmission quadrupole is the triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. The “triple quad” has three consecutive quadrupole stages, the first acting as a mass filter to transmit a particular incoming ion to the second quadrupole, a collision chamber, wherein that ion can be broken into fragments. The third quadrupole also acts as a mass filter, to transmit a particular fragment ion to the detector. If a quadrupole is made to rapidly and repetitively cycle through a range of mass filter settings, full spectra can be reported.  Likewise, a triple quad can be made to perform various scan types characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometry" title="Tandem mass spectrometry">tandem mass spectrometry</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ion_traps">Ion traps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ion traps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-dimensional_quadrupole_ion_trap">Three-dimensional quadrupole ion trap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Three-dimensional quadrupole ion trap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap" title="Quadrupole ion trap">quadrupole ion trap</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap" title="Quadrupole ion trap">quadrupole ion trap</a> works on the same physical principles as the quadrupole mass analyzer, but the ions are trapped and sequentially ejected. Ions are trapped in a mainly quadrupole RF field, in a space defined by a ring electrode (usually connected to the main RF potential) between two endcap electrodes (typically connected to DC or auxiliary AC potentials). The sample is ionized either internally (e.g. with an electron or laser beam), or externally, in which case the ions are often introduced through an aperture in an endcap electrode.
</p><p>There are many mass/charge separation and isolation methods but the most commonly used is the mass instability mode in which the RF potential is ramped so that the orbit of ions with a mass <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> &gt; <i>b</i></span> are stable while ions with mass <i>b</i> become unstable and are ejected on the <i>z</i>-axis onto a detector. There are also non-destructive analysis methods.
</p><p>Ions may also be ejected by the resonance excitation method, whereby a supplemental oscillatory excitation voltage is applied to the endcap electrodes, and the trapping voltage amplitude and/or excitation voltage frequency is varied to bring ions into a resonance condition in order of their mass/charge ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cylindrical_ion_trap">Cylindrical ion trap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cylindrical ion trap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylindrical_ion_trap_mass_spectrometer" title="Cylindrical ion trap mass spectrometer">cylindrical ion trap mass spectrometer</a> (CIT) is a derivative of the quadrupole ion trap where the electrodes are formed from flat rings rather than hyperbolic shaped electrodes. The architecture lends itself well to miniaturization because as the size of a trap is reduced, the shape of the electric field near the center of the trap, the region where the ions are trapped, forms a shape similar to that of a hyperbolic trap.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_quadrupole_ion_trap">Linear quadrupole ion trap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Linear quadrupole ion trap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_quadrupole_ion_trap" title="Linear quadrupole ion trap">linear quadrupole ion trap</a> is similar to a quadrupole ion trap, but it traps ions in a two dimensional quadrupole field, instead of a three-dimensional quadrupole field as in a 3D quadrupole ion trap. Thermo Fisher's LTQ ("linear trap quadrupole") is an example of the linear ion trap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>A toroidal ion trap can be visualized as a linear quadrupole curved around and connected at the ends or as a cross section of a 3D ion trap rotated on edge to form the toroid, donut shaped trap. The trap can store large volumes of ions by distributing them throughout the ring-like trap structure. This toroidal shaped trap is a configuration that allows the increased miniaturization of an ion trap mass analyzer. Additionally all ions are stored in the same trapping field and ejected together simplifying detection that can be complicated with array configurations due to variations in detector alignment and machining of the arrays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>As with the toroidal trap, linear traps and 3D quadrupole ion traps are the most commonly miniaturized mass analyzers due to their high sensitivity, tolerance for mTorr pressure, and capabilities for single analyzer tandem mass spectrometry (e.g. product ion scans).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbitrap">Orbitrap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Orbitrap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1495" data-file-width="2196" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG/220px-Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG/330px-Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG/440px-Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orbitrap_mass_analyzer_-_partial_cross-section.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orbitrap mass analyzer</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Orbitrap" title="Orbitrap">Orbitrap</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Orbitrap" title="Orbitrap">Orbitrap</a> instruments are similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_ion_cyclotron_resonance" title="Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance">Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance</a> mass spectrometers (see text below). Ions are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatically</a> trapped in an orbit around a central, spindle shaped electrode. The electrode confines the ions so that they both orbit around the central electrode and oscillate back and forth along the central electrode's long axis. This oscillation generates an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Image_current&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Image current (page does not exist)">image current</a> in the detector plates which is recorded by the instrument. The frequencies of these image currents depend on the mass-to-charge ratios of the ions. Mass spectra are obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transformation" title="Fourier transformation">Fourier transformation</a> of the recorded image currents.
</p><p>Orbitraps have a high mass accuracy, high sensitivity and a good dynamic range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourier_transform_ion_cyclotron_resonance">Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IonSpec_FT-ICR_(Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance)_Mass_spectrometer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg/220px-IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg/330px-IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg/440px-IonSpec_FT-ICR_%28Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance%29_Mass_spectrometer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IonSpec_FT-ICR_(Fourier_transform_Ion_cyclotron_resonance)_Mass_spectrometer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance mass spectrometer</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_ion_cyclotron_resonance" title="Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance">Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_mass_spectrometry" title="Fourier transform mass spectrometry">Fourier transform mass spectrometry</a> (FTMS), or more precisely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_ion_cyclotron_resonance" title="Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance">Fourier transform ion cyclotron resonance</a> MS, measures mass by detecting the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Image_current&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Image current (page does not exist)">image current</a> produced by ions <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotroning</a> in the presence of a magnetic field. Instead of measuring the deflection of ions with a detector such as an <a href="/wiki/Electron_multiplier" title="Electron multiplier">electron multiplier</a>, the ions are injected into a <a href="/wiki/Penning_trap" title="Penning trap">Penning trap</a> (a static electric/magnetic <a href="/wiki/Ion_trap" title="Ion trap">ion trap</a>) where they effectively form part of a circuit. Detectors at fixed positions in space measure the electrical signal of ions which pass near them over time, producing a periodic signal. Since the frequency of an ion's cycling is determined by its mass-to-charge ratio, this can be <a href="/wiki/Deconvolution" title="Deconvolution">deconvoluted</a> by performing a <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">Fourier transform</a> on the signal. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FTMS" title="FTMS">FTMS</a> has the advantage of high sensitivity (since each ion is "counted" more than once) and much higher <a href="/wiki/Resolution_(mass_spectrometry)" title="Resolution (mass spectrometry)">resolution</a> and thus precision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ion_cyclotron_resonance" title="Ion cyclotron resonance">Ion cyclotron resonance</a> (ICR) is an older mass analysis technique similar to FTMS except that ions are detected with a traditional detector. Ions trapped in a <a href="/wiki/Penning_trap" title="Penning trap">Penning trap</a> are excited by an RF electric field until they impact the wall of the trap, where the detector is located. Ions of different mass are resolved according to impact time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detectors">Detectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Detectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cont_dynode_detector.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="668" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Cont_dynode_detector.jpg/220px-Cont_dynode_detector.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Cont_dynode_detector.jpg/330px-Cont_dynode_detector.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Cont_dynode_detector.jpg/440px-Cont_dynode_detector.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cont_dynode_detector.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A continuous dynode particle multiplier detector</div></div></div>
<p>The final element of the mass spectrometer is the detector. The detector records either the charge induced or the current produced when an ion passes by or hits a surface. In a scanning instrument, the signal produced in the detector during the course of the scan versus where the instrument is in the scan (at what <i>m/Q</i>) will produce a <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrum" title="Mass spectrum">mass spectrum</a>, a record of ions as a function of <i>m/Q</i>.
</p><p>Typically, some type of <a href="/wiki/Electron_multiplier" title="Electron multiplier">electron multiplier</a> is used, though other detectors including <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cup" title="Faraday cup">Faraday cups</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion-to-photon_detector" title="Ion-to-photon detector">ion-to-photon detectors</a> are also used. Because the number of ions leaving the mass analyzer at a particular instant is typically quite small, considerable amplification is often necessary to get a signal. <a href="/wiki/Microchannel_plate_detector" title="Microchannel plate detector">Microchannel plate detectors</a> are commonly used in modern commercial instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FTMS" title="FTMS">FTMS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbitrap" title="Orbitrap">Orbitraps</a>, the detector consists of a pair of metal surfaces within the mass analyzer/ion trap region which the ions only pass near as they oscillate. No direct current is produced, only a weak AC image current is produced in a circuit between the electrodes. Other inductive detectors have also been used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tandem_mass_spectrometry">Tandem mass spectrometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Tandem mass spectrometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TandemMS.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="217" data-file-width="943" height="51" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TandemMS.svg/220px-TandemMS.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TandemMS.svg/330px-TandemMS.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/TandemMS.svg/440px-TandemMS.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TandemMS.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tandem mass spectrometry for biological molecules using ESI or <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">MALDI</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometer" title="Tandem mass spectrometer">tandem mass spectrometer</a> is one capable of multiple rounds of mass spectrometry, usually separated by some form of molecule fragmentation. For example, one mass analyzer can isolate one <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> from many entering a mass spectrometer. A second mass analyzer then stabilizes the peptide ions while they collide with a gas, causing them to fragment by <a href="/wiki/Collision-induced_dissociation" title="Collision-induced dissociation">collision-induced dissociation</a> (CID). A third mass analyzer then sorts the fragments produced from the peptides. Tandem MS can also be done in a single mass analyzer over time, as in a <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap" title="Quadrupole ion trap">quadrupole ion trap</a>. There are various methods for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(chemistry)" title="Fragmentation (chemistry)">fragmenting</a> molecules for tandem MS, including <a href="/wiki/Collision-induced_dissociation" title="Collision-induced dissociation">collision-induced dissociation</a> (CID), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_capture_dissociation" title="Electron capture dissociation">electron capture dissociation</a> (ECD), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_transfer_dissociation" title="Electron transfer dissociation">electron transfer dissociation</a> (ETD), <a href="/wiki/Infrared_multiphoton_dissociation" title="Infrared multiphoton dissociation">infrared multiphoton dissociation</a> (IRMPD), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_infrared_radiative_dissociation" title="Blackbody infrared radiative dissociation">blackbody infrared radiative dissociation</a> (BIRD), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron-detachment_dissociation" title="Electron-detachment dissociation">electron-detachment dissociation</a> (EDD) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface-induced_dissociation" title="Surface-induced dissociation">surface-induced dissociation</a> (SID). An important application using tandem mass spectrometry is in <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry#Protein_identification" title="Mass spectrometry">protein identification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tandem mass spectrometry enables a variety of experimental sequences. Many commercial mass spectrometers are designed to expedite the execution of such routine sequences as <a href="/wiki/Selected_reaction_monitoring" title="Selected reaction monitoring">selected reaction monitoring (SRM)</a> and precursor ion scanning. In SRM, the first analyzer allows only a single mass through and the second analyzer monitors for multiple user-defined fragment ions. SRM is most often used with scanning instruments where the second mass analysis event is <a href="/wiki/Duty_cycle" title="Duty cycle">duty cycle</a> limited. These experiments are used to increase specificity of detection of known molecules, notably in pharmacokinetic studies. Precursor ion scanning refers to monitoring for a specific loss from the precursor ion. The first and second mass analyzers scan across the spectrum as partitioned by a user-defined <i>m/z</i> value. This experiment is used to detect specific motifs within unknown molecules.
</p><p>Another type of tandem mass spectrometry used for <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> is <a href="/wiki/Accelerator_mass_spectrometry" title="Accelerator mass spectrometry">accelerator mass spectrometry</a> (AMS), which uses very high voltages, usually in the mega-volt range, to accelerate negative ions into a type of tandem mass spectrometer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_mass_spectrometer_configurations_and_techniques">Common mass spectrometer configurations and techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Common mass spectrometer configurations and techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When a specific combination of source, analyzer, and detector becomes conventional in practice, a compound <a href="/wiki/List_of_mass_spectrometry_acronyms" title="List of mass spectrometry acronyms">acronym</a> may arise to designate it succinctly. One example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MALDI-TOF" title="MALDI-TOF">MALDI-TOF</a>, which refers to a combination of a <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization</a> source with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-of-flight" title="Time-of-flight">time-of-flight</a> mass analyzer. Other examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICP-MS" title="ICP-MS">inductively coupled plasma-mass spectrometry (ICP-MS)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accelerator_mass_spectrometry" title="Accelerator mass spectrometry">accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_ionization" title="Thermal ionization">thermal ionization-mass spectrometry (TIMS)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spark_ionization" title="Spark ionization">spark source mass spectrometry (SSMS)</a>.
</p><p>Certain applications of mass spectrometry have developed monikers that although strictly speaking would seem to refer to a broad application, in practice have come instead to connote a specific or a limited number of instrument configurations. An example of this is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_ratio_mass_spectrometry" title="Isotope ratio mass spectrometry">isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS)</a>, which refers in practice to the use of a limited number of sector based mass analyzers; this name is used to refer to both the application and the instrument used for the application.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_techniques_combined_with_mass_spectrometry">Separation techniques combined with mass spectrometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Separation techniques combined with mass spectrometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An important enhancement to the mass resolving and mass determining capabilities of mass spectrometry is using it in tandem with <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatographic</a> and other separation techniques.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_chromatography">Gas chromatography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Gas chromatography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry">Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GCMS_open.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="2016" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/GCMS_open.jpg/220px-GCMS_open.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/GCMS_open.jpg/330px-GCMS_open.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/GCMS_open.jpg/440px-GCMS_open.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GCMS_open.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A gas chromatograph (right) directly coupled to a mass spectrometer (left)</div></div></div>
<p>A common combination is <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC/MS or GC-MS). In this technique, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_chromatograph" title="Gas chromatograph">gas chromatograph</a> is used to separate different compounds. This stream of separated compounds is fed online into the <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> source, a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metallic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_filament" title="Electrical filament">filament</a> to which <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> is applied. This filament emits electrons which ionize the compounds. The ions can then further fragment, yielding predictable patterns. Intact ions and fragments pass into the mass spectrometer's analyzer and are eventually detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_chromatography">Liquid chromatography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Liquid chromatography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry">Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg/220px-IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg/330px-IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg/440px-IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IMA_Conservation_Science.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art" title="Indianapolis Museum of Art">Indianapolis Museum of Art</a> conservation scientist performing <a href="/wiki/Liquid_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry">liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry</a></div></div></div>
<p>Similar to gas chromatography MS (GC-MS), liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC/MS or LC-MS) separates compounds chromatographically before they are introduced to the ion source and mass spectrometer. It differs from GC-MS in that the mobile phase is liquid, usually a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and organic <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, instead of gas. Most commonly, an <a href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization" title="Electrospray ionization">electrospray ionization</a> source is used in LC-MS. Other popular and commercially available LC-MS ion sources are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure_chemical_ionization" title="Atmospheric pressure chemical ionization">atmospheric pressure chemical ionization</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure_photoionization" title="Atmospheric pressure photoionization">atmospheric pressure photoionization</a>. There are also some newly developed ionization techniques like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_spray" title="Laser spray">laser spray</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Capillary_electrophoresis.E2.80.93mass_spectrometry"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Capillary_electrophoresis–mass_spectrometry">Capillary electrophoresis–mass spectrometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Capillary electrophoresis–mass spectrometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Capillary_electrophoresis%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Capillary electrophoresis–mass spectrometry">Capillary electrophoresis–mass spectrometry</a></div>
<p>Capillary electrophoresis–mass spectrometry (CE-MS) is a technique that combines the liquid separation process of <a href="/wiki/Capillary_electrophoresis" title="Capillary electrophoresis">capillary electrophoresis</a> with mass spectrometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid2774189_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2774189-33">[33]</a></sup> CE-MS is typically coupled to electrospray ionization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18790125_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18790125-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ion_mobility">Ion mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Ion mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ion-mobility_spectrometry%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry" title="Ion-mobility spectrometry–mass spectrometry">Ion-mobility spectrometry–mass spectrometry</a></div>
<p>Ion mobility spectrometry-mass spectrometry (IMS/MS or IMMS) is a technique where ions are first separated by drift time through some neutral gas under an applied electrical potential gradient before being introduced into a mass spectrometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GFVerbeck06012002_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-GFVerbeck06012002-35">[35]</a></sup> Drift time is a measure of the radius relative to the charge of the ion. The <a href="/wiki/Duty_cycle" title="Duty cycle">duty cycle</a> of IMS (the time over which the experiment takes place) is longer than most mass spectrometric techniques, such that the mass spectrometer can sample along the course of the IMS separation. This produces data about the IMS separation and the mass-to-charge ratio of the ions in a manner similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_chromatography-mass_spectrometry" title="Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry">LC-MS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The duty cycle of IMS is short relative to liquid chromatography or gas chromatography separations and can thus be coupled to such techniques, producing triple modalities such as LC/IMS/MS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_and_analysis">Data and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Data and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="361" data-file-width="400" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif/220px-ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif/330px-ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7b/ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ObwiedniaPeptydu.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mass spectrum of a peptide showing the isotopic distribution</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_representations">Data representations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Data representations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry_data_format" title="Mass spectrometry data format">Mass spectrometry data format</a></div>
<p>Mass spectrometry produces various types of data. The most common data representation is the <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrum" title="Mass spectrum">mass spectrum</a>.
</p><p>Certain types of mass spectrometry data are best represented as a <a href="/wiki/Mass_chromatogram" title="Mass chromatogram">mass chromatogram</a>. Types of chromatograms include <a href="/wiki/Selected_ion_monitoring" title="Selected ion monitoring">selected ion monitoring</a> (SIM), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_ion_current" title="Total ion current">total ion current</a> (TIC), and <a href="/wiki/Selected_reaction_monitoring" title="Selected reaction monitoring">selected reaction monitoring</a> (SRM), among many others.
</p><p>Other types of mass spectrometry data are well represented as a three-dimensional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contour_map" title="Contour map">contour map</a>. In this form, the mass-to-charge, <i>m/z</i> is on the <i>x</i>-axis, intensity the <i>y</i>-axis, and an additional experimental parameter, such as time, is recorded on the <i>z</i>-axis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_analysis">Data analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Data analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mass spectrometry data analysis is specific to the type of experiment producing the data. General subdivisions of data are fundamental to understanding any data.
</p><p>Many mass spectrometers work in either <i>negative ion mode</i> or <i>positive ion mode</i>. It is very important to know whether the observed ions are negatively or positively charged. This is often important in determining the neutral mass but it also indicates something about the nature of the molecules.
</p><p>Different types of ion source result in different arrays of fragments produced from the original molecules. An electron ionization source produces many fragments and mostly single-charged (1-) radicals (odd number of electrons), whereas an electrospray source usually produces non-radical quasimolecular ions that are frequently multiply charged. Tandem mass spectrometry purposely produces fragment ions post-source and can drastically change the sort of data achieved by an experiment.
</p><p>Knowledge of the origin of a sample can provide insight into the component molecules of the sample and their fragmentations. A sample from a synthesis/manufacturing process will probably contain impurities chemically related to the target component. A crudely prepared biological sample will probably contain a certain amount of salt, which may form <a href="/wiki/Adduct" title="Adduct">adducts</a> with the analyte molecules in certain analyses.
</p><p>Results can also depend heavily on sample preparation and how it was run/introduced. An important example is the issue of which matrix is used for MALDI spotting, since much of the energetics of the desorption/ionization event is controlled by the matrix rather than the laser power. Sometimes samples are spiked with sodium or another ion-carrying species to produce adducts rather than a protonated species.
</p><p>Mass spectrometry can measure molar mass, molecular structure, and sample purity. Each of these questions requires a different experimental procedure; therefore, adequate definition of the experimental goal is a prerequisite for collecting the proper data and successfully interpreting it.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation_of_mass_spectra">Interpretation of mass spectra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Interpretation of mass spectra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toluene_ei_ms.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Toluene_ei_ms.PNG/220px-Toluene_ei_ms.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Toluene_ei_ms.PNG/330px-Toluene_ei_ms.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Toluene_ei_ms.PNG/440px-Toluene_ei_ms.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Toluene_ei_ms.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">Toluene</a> electron ionization mass spectrum</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrum_analysis" title="Mass spectrum analysis">Mass spectrum analysis</a></div>
<p>Since the precise <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">structure</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptide_sequence" title="Peptide sequence">peptide sequence</a> of a molecule is deciphered through the set of fragment masses, the interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrum" title="Mass spectrum">mass spectra</a> requires combined use of various techniques. Usually the first strategy for identifying an unknown compound is to compare its experimental mass spectrum against a library of mass spectra. If no matches result from the search, then manual interpretation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry_software" title="Mass spectrometry software">software assisted interpretation of mass spectra</a> must be performed. Computer simulation of <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> and fragmentation processes occurring in mass spectrometer is the primary tool for assigning structure or peptide sequence to a molecule. An <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> structural information is fragmented <i><a href="/wiki/In_silico" title="In silico">in silico</a></i> and the resulting pattern is compared with observed spectrum. Such simulation is often supported by a fragmentation library<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> that contains published patterns of known decomposition reactions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry_software" title="Mass spectrometry software">Software</a> taking advantage of this idea has been developed for both small molecules and <a href="/wiki/Peptide_mass_fingerprinting" title="Peptide mass fingerprinting">proteins</a>.
</p><p>Analysis of mass spectra can also be  spectra with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accurate_mass" title="Accurate mass">accurate mass</a>. A mass-to-charge ratio value (<i>m/z</i>) with only integer precision can represent an immense number of theoretically possible ion structures; however, more precise mass figures significantly reduce the number of candidate <a href="/wiki/Molecule#Molecular_formula" title="Molecule">molecular formulas</a>.  A computer algorithm called formula generator calculates all molecular formulas that theoretically fit a given <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accurate_mass" title="Accurate mass">mass</a> with specified tolerance.
</p><p>A recent technique for structure elucidation in mass spectrometry, called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Precursor_ion_fingerprinting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Precursor ion fingerprinting (page does not exist)">precursor ion fingerprinting</a>, identifies individual pieces of structural information by conducting a search of the <a href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometry" title="Tandem mass spectrometry">tandem spectra</a> of the molecule under investigation against a library of the <a href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometry#Tandem_MS_experiments" title="Tandem mass spectrometry">product-ion spectra</a> of structurally characterized precursor ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg/220px-WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg/330px-WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg/440px-WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WB-57_PALMS_Mount.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Particle Analysis by Laser Mass Spectrometry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosol_mass_spectrometer" title="Aerosol mass spectrometer">aerosol mass spectrometer</a> aboard a <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Martin_B-57_Canberra" title="Martin B-57 Canberra">WB-57</a> high-altitude research aircraft</div></div></div>
<p>Mass spectrometry has both <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_research" title="Qualitative research">qualitative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_analysis_(chemistry)" title="Quantitative analysis (chemistry)">quantitative</a> uses. These include identifying unknown compounds, determining the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> composition of elements in a molecule, and determining the <a href="/wiki/Structure" title="Structure">structure</a> of a compound by observing its fragmentation. Other uses include quantifying the amount of a compound in a sample or studying the fundamentals of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase_ion_chemistry" title="Gas phase ion chemistry">gas phase ion chemistry</a> (the chemistry of ions and neutrals in a vacuum). MS is now commonly used in analytical laboratories that study physical, chemical, or biological properties of a great variety of compounds.
</p><p>As an analytical technique it possesses distinct advantages such as: Increased sensitivity over most other analytical techniques because the analyzer, as a mass-charge filter, reduces background interference, Excellent specificity from characteristic fragmentation patterns to identify unknowns or confirm the presence of suspected compounds, Information about molecular weight, Information about the isotopic abundance of elements, Temporally resolved chemical data.
</p><p>A few of the disadvantages of the method is that it often fails to distinguish between optical and geometrical isomers and the positions of substituent in o-, m- and p- positions in an aromatic ring. Also, its scope is limited in identifying hydrocarbons that produce similar fragmented ions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotope_ratio_MS:_isotope_dating_and_tracing">Isotope ratio MS: isotope dating and tracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Isotope ratio MS: isotope dating and tracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1677" data-file-width="2607" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg/220px-Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg/330px-Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg/440px-Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mass-spectrometer_awi_hg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mass spectrometer to determine the <sup>16</sup>O/<sup>18</sup>O and <sup>12</sup>C/<sup>13</sup>C isotope ratio on biogenous carbonate</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotope-ratio_mass_spectrometry" title="Isotope-ratio mass spectrometry">Isotope-ratio mass spectrometry</a></div>
<p>Mass spectrometry is also used to determine the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> composition of elements within a sample. Differences in mass among isotopes of an element are very small, and the less abundant isotopes of an element are typically very rare, so a very sensitive instrument is required. These instruments, sometimes referred to as isotope ratio mass spectrometers (IR-MS), usually use a single magnet to bend a beam of ionized particles towards a series of <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cup" title="Faraday cup">Faraday cups</a> which convert particle impacts to <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. A fast on-line analysis of <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> content of water can be done using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowing_afterglow_mass_spectrometry" title="Flowing afterglow mass spectrometry">flowing afterglow mass spectrometry</a>, FA-MS. Probably the most sensitive and accurate mass spectrometer for this purpose is the <a href="/wiki/Accelerator_mass_spectrometry" title="Accelerator mass spectrometry">accelerator mass spectrometer</a> (AMS). This is because it provides ultimate sensitivity, capable of measuring individual atoms and measuring nuclides with a dynamic range of ~10<sup>15</sup> relative to the major stable isotope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maher_S,_Jjunju_FPM,_Taylor_S_2015_113–135_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maher_S,_Jjunju_FPM,_Taylor_S_2015_113–135-41">[41]</a></sup> Isotope ratios are important markers of a variety of processes. Some isotope ratios are used to determine the age of materials for example as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dating" title="Carbon dating">carbon dating</a>. Labeling with stable isotopes is also used for protein quantification. (see <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry#Protein_characterization" title="Mass spectrometry">protein characterization</a> below)
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane-Inlet_Mass_Spectrometry:_Measuring_gasses_in_solution">Membrane-Inlet Mass Spectrometry: Measuring gasses in solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Membrane-Inlet Mass Spectrometry: Measuring gasses in solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membrane_inlet_mass_spectrometry" title="Membrane inlet mass spectrometry">Membrane-inlet Mass Spectrometry</a>, combines the Isotope ratio MS with a reaction chamber/cell separated by a gas-permeable membrane. This method allows the study of gasses as they evolve in solution. This method has been extensively used for the study of the production of oxygen by <a href="/wiki/Photosystem_II" title="Photosystem II">Photosystem II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trace_gas_analysis">Trace gas analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Trace gas analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several techniques use ions created in a dedicated ion source injected into a flow tube or a drift tube: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIFT-MS_selected_ion_flow_tube_mass_spectrometry" title="SIFT-MS selected ion flow tube mass spectrometry">selected ion flow tube</a> (SIFT-MS), and <a href="/wiki/Proton-transfer-reaction_mass_spectrometry" title="Proton-transfer-reaction mass spectrometry">proton transfer reaction</a> (PTR-MS), are variants of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_ionization" title="Chemical ionization">chemical ionization</a> dedicated for trace gas analysis of air, breath or liquid headspace using well defined reaction time allowing calculations of analyte concentrations from the known reaction kinetics without the need for internal standard or calibration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atom_probe">Atom probe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Atom probe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Atom_probe" title="Atom probe">atom probe</a> is an instrument that combines <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-of-flight" title="Time-of-flight">time-of-flight</a> mass spectrometry and field-evaporation microscopy to map the location of individual atoms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacokinetics">Pharmacokinetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Pharmacokinetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pharmacokinetics" title="Pharmacokinetics">Pharmacokinetics</a></div>
<p>Pharmacokinetics is often studied using mass spectrometry because of the complex nature of the matrix (often blood or urine) and the need for high sensitivity to observe low dose and long time point data. The most common instrumentation used in this application is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_chromatography-mass_spectrometry" title="Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry">LC-MS</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_mass_analyzer" title="Quadrupole mass analyzer">triple quadrupole mass spectrometer</a>. Tandem mass spectrometry is usually employed for added specificity. Standard curves and internal standards are used for quantitation of usually a single pharmaceutical in the samples. The samples represent different time points as a pharmaceutical is administered and then metabolized or cleared from the body. Blank or t=0 samples taken before administration are important in determining background and ensuring data integrity with such complex sample matrices. Much attention is paid to the linearity of the standard curve; however it is not uncommon to use <a href="/wiki/Curve_fitting" title="Curve fitting">curve fitting</a> with more complex functions such as quadratics since the response of most mass spectrometers is less than linear across large concentration ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is currently considerable interest in the use of very high sensitivity mass spectrometry for <a href="/wiki/Microdosing" title="Microdosing">microdosing</a> studies, which are seen as a promising alternative to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_experimentation" title="Animal experimentation">animal experimentation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protein_characterization">Protein characterization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Protein characterization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Protein_mass_spectrometry" title="Protein mass spectrometry">Protein mass spectrometry</a></div>
<p>Mass spectrometry is an important method for the characterization and <a href="/wiki/Protein_sequencing" title="Protein sequencing">sequencing</a> of proteins. The two primary methods for ionization of whole proteins are <a href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization" title="Electrospray ionization">electrospray ionization</a> (ESI) and <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization</a> (MALDI). In keeping with the performance and mass range of available mass spectrometers, two approaches are used for characterizing proteins. In the first, intact proteins are ionized by either of the two techniques described above, and then introduced to a mass analyzer. This approach is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Top-down_proteomics" title="Top-down proteomics">top-down</a>" strategy of protein analysis.  The top-down approach however is largely limited to low-throughput single-protein studies. In the second, proteins are enzymatically digested into smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptides" title="Peptides">peptides</a> using <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">proteases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a>, either in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> or <a href="/wiki/In-gel_digestion" title="In-gel digestion">in gel</a> after <a href="/wiki/Electrophoresis" title="Electrophoresis">electrophoretic</a> separation. Other proteolytic agents are also used. The collection of peptide products are then introduced to the mass analyzer. When the characteristic pattern of peptides is used for the identification of the protein the method is called <a href="/wiki/Peptide_mass_fingerprinting" title="Peptide mass fingerprinting">peptide mass fingerprinting</a> (PMF), if the identification is performed using the sequence data determined in <a href="/wiki/Tandem_mass_spectrometry" title="Tandem mass spectrometry">tandem MS</a> analysis it is called <a href="/wiki/De_novo_peptide_sequencing" title="De novo peptide sequencing">de novo peptide sequencing</a>. These procedures of protein analysis are also referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/Bottom-up_proteomics" title="Bottom-up proteomics">bottom-up</a>" approach.  A third approach however is beginning to be used, this intermediate "middle-down" approach involves analyzing proteolytic peptide larger than the typical tryptic peptide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycan_analysis">Glycan analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Glycan analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mass spectrometry (MS), with its low sample requirement and high sensitivity, has been predominantly used in <a href="/wiki/Glycobiology" title="Glycobiology">glycobiology</a> for characterization and elucidation of <a href="/wiki/Glycan" title="Glycan">glycan structures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Mass spectrometry provides a complementary method to <a href="/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High-performance liquid chromatography">HPLC</a> for the analysis of glycans. Intact glycans may be detected directly as singly charged ions by <a href="/wiki/Matrix-assisted_laser_desorption/ionization" title="Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization">matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization mass spectrometry</a> (MALDI-MS) or, following permethylation or peracetylation, by <a href="/wiki/Fast_atom_bombardment" title="Fast atom bombardment">fast atom bombardment mass spectrometry</a> (FAB-MS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrospray_ionization_mass_spectrometry" title="Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry">Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry</a> (ESI-MS) also gives good signals for the smaller glycans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Various <a href="/wiki/Glycobiology#Software_for_MS_Data_Interpretation" title="Glycobiology">free and commercial software</a> are now available which interpret MS data and aid in Glycan structure characterization.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_exploration">Space exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Space exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="512" height="440" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg/220px-Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg/330px-Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg/440px-Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rosy_Red_to_TEGA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NASA's <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft)" title="Phoenix (spacecraft)">Phoenix Mars Lander</a> analyzing a soil sample from the "Rosy Red" trench with the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_and_Evolved_Gas_Analyzer" title="Thermal and Evolved Gas Analyzer">TEGA</a> mass spectrometer</div></div></div>
<p>As a standard method for analysis, mass spectrometers have reached other planets and moons. Two were taken to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking program</a>. In early 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> mission delivered a specialized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GC-MS" title="GC-MS">GC-MS</a> instrument aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huygens_probe" title="Huygens probe">Huygens probe</a> through the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, the largest moon of the planet <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>. This instrument analyzed atmospheric samples along its descent trajectory and was able to vaporize and analyze samples of Titan's frozen, hydrocarbon covered surface once the probe had landed. These measurements compare the abundance of isotope(s) of each particle comparatively to earth's natural abundance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Also on board the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> spacecraft was an ion and neutral mass spectrometer which had been taking measurements of Titan's atmospheric composition as well as the composition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">Enceladus</a>' plumes. A <a href="/wiki/Thermal_and_Evolved_Gas_Analyzer" title="Thermal and Evolved Gas Analyzer">Thermal and Evolved Gas Analyzer</a> mass spectrometer was carried by the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft)" title="Phoenix (spacecraft)">Mars Phoenix Lander</a> launched in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mass spectrometers are also widely used in space missions to measure the composition of plasmas. For example, the Cassini spacecraft carried the Cassini Plasma Spectrometer (CAPS),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> which measured the mass of ions in Saturn's <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Respired_gas_monitor">Respired gas monitor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Respired gas monitor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mass spectrometers were used in hospitals for respiratory gas analysis beginning around 1975 through the end of the century. Some are probably still in use but none are currently being manufactured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Found mostly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operating_room" title="Operating room">operating room</a>, they were a part of a complex system, in which respired gas samples from patients undergoing <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a> were drawn into the instrument through a valve mechanism designed to sequentially connect up to 32 rooms to the mass spectrometer. A computer directed all operations of the system. The data collected from the mass spectrometer was delivered to the individual rooms for the anesthesiologist to use.
</p><p>The uniqueness of this magnetic sector mass spectrometer may have been the fact that a plane of detectors, each purposely positioned to collect all of the ion species expected to be in the samples, allowed the instrument to simultaneously report all of the gases respired by the patient. Although the mass range was limited to slightly over 120 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a>, fragmentation of some of the heavier molecules negated the need for a higher detection limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparative_mass_spectrometry">Preparative mass spectrometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_spectrometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Preparative mass spectrometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The primary function of mass spectrometry is as a tool for chemical analyses based on detection and quantification of ions according to their mass-to-charge ratio. However, mass spectrometry also shows promise for material synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maher_S,_Jjunju_FPM,_Taylor_S_2015_113–135_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maher_S,_Jjunju_FPM,_Taylor_S_2015_113–135-41">[41]</a></sup> Ion soft landing is characterized by deposition of intact species on surfaces at low kinetic energies which precludes the fragmentation of the incident species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The soft landing technique was first reported in 1977 for the reaction of low energy sulfur containing ions on a lead surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
