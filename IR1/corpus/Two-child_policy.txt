<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Two-child policy</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>two-child policy</b> is a government-imposed limit of two children allowed per family or the payment of government subsidies only to the first two children. It has previously been used in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. In <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British Hong Kong</a> in the 1970s, citizens were also highly encouraged to have two children as a limit (although it was not mandated by law), and it was used as part of the region's family planning strategies. Since 2016, it has been implemented in China, replacing the country's previous <a href="/wiki/One-child_policy" title="One-child policy">one-child policy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="China_.28Mainland.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="China_(Mainland)">China (Mainland)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: China (Mainland)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 1970s, Chinese citizens were encouraged to have only one child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The ongoing <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Revolution" title="Cultural Revolution">Cultural Revolution</a> and the strain it placed on the nation were large factors. During this time, the birth rate dropped from nearly 6 children per woman to just under 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (The colloquial term "births per woman" is usually formalized as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Fertility_Rate" title="Total Fertility Rate">Total Fertility Rate</a></i> (TFR), a technical term in demographic analysis meaning the average number of children that would be born to a woman over her lifetime if she were to experience the exact current age-specific fertility rates through her lifetime.)
</p><p>As China's youngest generation (born under the <a href="/wiki/One-child_policy" title="One-child policy">one-child policy</a>, which first became a requirement for most couples in 1979) came of age for formation of the next generation, a single child would be left with having to provide support for his or her two parents and four grandparents. In response to this issue, by 2009 all provinces allowed couples to have two children if both parents were only children themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After a policy change of the Chinese government in late 2013, most Chinese provinces further relaxed the policy in 2014 by allowing families to have two children if one of the parents is an only child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ft_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ft-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Han Chinese living in rural areas were often permitted to have two children, as exceptions existed if the first child was a daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yardley_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-yardley-9">[9]</a></sup> Because of cases such as these, as well as urban couples who simply paid a fine (or "social maintenance fee") to have more children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_rate" title="Fertility rate">fertility rate</a> of mainland China is, in fact, closer to two children per family than to one child per family (1.8). In addition, since 2012, Han Chinese in southern <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> were allowed to have two children. This, along with incentives and restrictions against higher Muslim <a href="/wiki/Uyghurs" title="Uyghurs">Uyghur</a> fertility, was seen as attempt to counter the threat of <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang_conflict" title="Xinjiang conflict">Uyghur separatism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 29, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency" title="Xinhua News Agency">Xinhua</a> reported the change in the existing law to a two-child policy citing a statement from the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup> The new policy allowing Chinese couples to have two children was proposed in order to help address the aging issue in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On December 27, 2015, the new law was passed in the session of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Congress_Standing_Committee" title="National People's Congress Standing Committee">National People's Congress Standing Committee</a>, which governs country's laws, effective from January 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, about two years after the new policy reform, China is facing new ramifications from the two-child policy. Since the revision of the one-child policy, 90 million women have become eligible to have a second child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-17">[17]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, the new two-child policy may have negative implications on gender roles, with new expectations from females to bear more children and to abandon their careers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the reform, China saw a short-lived boost in fertility rate for 2016. Chinese women gave birth to 17.2 million babies in 2016, but declined by 3.5% in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-18">[18]</a></sup> Husbands still have greater marital power, thus increasing fertility pressure on their female counterpart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-17">[17]</a></sup> The dynamic of relationships (amount of "power" held by each parent), and the amount of resources each parent has contributes to the struggle for dominance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-19">[19]</a></sup> Resources would be items such as income, and health insurance. Dominance would be described as who has the final say in pregnancy, who has to resign in their career for maternal/parental leave. However, females have shown interest in a second child if the first child did not possess the desired sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:12-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chinese couples have also polled and stated that they would rather invest in one child opposed to two children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-:02-17">[17]</a></sup> To add, another concern for couples would be the high costs of raising another child; China's childcare system needs to be furthered developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The change in cultural norms appears to be having negative consequences and leads to fear of a large aging population with smaller younger generations; thus the lack of workforce to drive the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:22-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2018, it was reported that the Chinese authorities were in the process of ending their population control policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong<span id="Hong_Kong"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Hong_Kong" title="Demographics of Hong Kong">Demographics of Hong Kong</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British Hong Kong</a>, the Eugenics League was founded in 1936, which became <a href="/wiki/The_Family_Planning_Association_of_Hong_Kong" title="The Family Planning Association of Hong Kong">The Family Planning Association of Hong Kong</a> in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famplan.org.hk_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-famplan.org.hk-22">[22]</a></sup> The organization provides family planning advice, <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> services to the general public of Hong Kong. In the 1970s due to the rapidly rising population, it launched the "Two is Enough" campaign,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-famplan.org.hk_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-famplan.org.hk-22">[22]</a></sup> which reduced the general birth rate through educational means. The organization<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ippf.org_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ippf.org-23">[23]</a></sup> founded the <a href="/wiki/International_Planned_Parenthood_Federation" title="International Planned Parenthood Federation">International Planned Parenthood Federation</a>, with its counterparts in seven other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ippf.org_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ippf.org-23">[23]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">total fertility rate</a> in Hong Kong is currently 1.04 children per woman, one of the lowest in the world.
Although the "Two is Enough" campaign found widespread approval, it does not reflect current government policy in supporting families. Tax allowances of 100,000 HK$ per child can be claimed for up to 9 children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Furthermore, parents who have fallen into hardship can apply for special assistance from the state. This is a means-tested financial benefit, which is not limited to a particular number of children either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iranian authorities encouraged families in Iran not to have more than two children when conducting <a href="/wiki/Family_planning_in_Iran" title="Family planning in Iran">family planning in Iran</a> from early 1990s to late 2006. Iran's government "declared that Islam favored families with only two children", as one historian put it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> When the family planning program was initiated, Iran's Health Ministry launched a nationwide campaign and introduced contraceptives - pills, condoms, IUDs, implants, tubal ligations, and vasectomies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciolino_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciolino-27">[27]</a></sup>
Starting in 2006, the government's population control policy changed when Ahmadinejad called for reversal of Iran's existing policy of "two children is enough"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and later in 2012 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayatollah_Khamenei" title="Ayatollah Khamenei">Ayatollah Khamenei</a> also stated that Iran's contraceptive policy made sense 20 years ago, "but its continuation in later years was wrong ... Scientific and experts studies show that we will face population aging and reduction (in population) if the birth-control policy continues."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_control_in_Singapore" title="Population control in Singapore">Population control in Singapore</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, the two-child policy until the 1980s was called "Stop at Two".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> Optimum Population Trust (now <a href="/wiki/Population_Matters" title="Population Matters">Population Matters</a>) advocated what the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> described as a "'two-child' policy" to combat population increases and climate change in the United Kingdom.  The article stated: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>"According to the report, published by the Optimum Population Trust, Britain's high birth rate is a major factor in the current level of climate change, which can only be combated if families voluntarily limit the number of children they have."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In October 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>'s proposed policy of only paying child benefit for the first two children of unemployed parents has been described as a 'two-child policy',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and has been fronted by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Work_and_Pensions" title="Secretary of State for Work and Pensions">Secretary of State for Work and Pensions</a> and former leader of the Conservative Party <a href="/wiki/Iain_Duncan_Smith" title="Iain Duncan Smith">Iain Duncan Smith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2015, <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> denied any such plans to cut child benefits or tax credits. However, three months later, <a href="/wiki/George_Osborne" title="George Osborne">George Osborne</a>, the then Finance Minister, announced that child tax credits would be limited to the first two children only. This was to come into force from the 2017/2018 financial year and apply to children born after that date only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two-child policy took effect on 5 April 2017. One particular aspect of the new rules, termed the "rape clause", has caused controversy. Although the policy excludes all but the first two children from all available benefits, an exemption can be applied for if the conception of the third and any further children occurred as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a> of the claimant. A woman wishing to claim this exemption must fill in an eight-page form: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The form requires women wanting to be eligible for the exemption to sign a declaration saying they were raped or otherwise coerced into sex – and giving the child’s name. They must sign a declaration reading: 'I believe the non-consensual conception exemption applies to my child.' They must also sign another declaration that says: 'I confirm that I am not living with the other parent of this child.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>That the woman must thereby identify the child in question (which is thought to be assigned a tax code created for this exemption<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>), that first or second children conceived by rape do not count towards this exemption and that women who are still living with their abusers are not eligible are just some of the aspects of the rape clause that caused widespread condemnation. Ruth Graham, writing for <a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate</a>, summarises the issues surrounding this new policy thus:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The policy and the exemption have received harsh criticism from a wide variety of sources since they were announced in 2015. One member of parliament called the exemption implementation 'inhumane and barbaric.' Feminists have pointed out the cuts disproportionately affect women. A coalition of the U.K.’s largest Christian denominations and Jewish groups pointed out that the policy discriminates against people whose religion compels them to have larger families. A United Nations committee on children’s rights asked the British government to explain the policy last year, because of concerns about women having to somehow prove they were raped."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam">Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> has had a population policy for over 50 years.  It was launched by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vietnamese_government" title="Vietnamese government">Vietnamese government</a> in the early 1960s in <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> and continues in a modified form today, throughout all of Vietnam (not just the north).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup> The policy emphasizes the official family-size goal to be <i>một hoặc hai con,</i> which means "one or two children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_Council_Daniel_Goodkind_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population_Council_Daniel_Goodkind-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Vietnam had an estimated population of 92.5 million people, which represented 1.28% of the total world population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population-39">[39]</a></sup> Currently, the <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">total fertility rate</a> of Vietnam is 1.8 (births per woman),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wold_Bank_TFR_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wold_Bank_TFR-40">[40]</a></sup> which is below the <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">replacement-level fertility</a> of 2.1, the rate "at which a population exactly replaces itself from one generation to the next" according to the World Resources Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Resources_Institute_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Resources_Institute-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1954 to 1975, Vietnam was split into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_and_South_Vietnam" title="North and South Vietnam">North and South Vietnam</a> along the 17th parallel with separate governments and policies in each region. <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> and had a communist government, whereas <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a> became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Republic of Vietnam">Republic of Vietnam</a> and was more aligned with the United States and other Western nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnam_Book_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnam_Book-42">[42]</a></sup> In 1963, North Vietnam began a policy advocating a two-child norm due to the sharp population increase of the largely poor and rural population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_Council_Daniel_Goodkind_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Population_Council_Daniel_Goodkind-38">[38]</a></sup> Vietnam's family planning policy was developed before those of other countries, such as China and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the government used a system of information, education, communication (IEC) campaign and publicly accessible contraceptives to curb the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reproductive_Rights_Overview_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reproductive_Rights_Overview-43">[43]</a></sup> After the reunification of North and South Vietnam in 1975 under the Communist Party, there was a governmental effort to extend the policies of the North to the rest of Vietnam, which extended into the next decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fertility_Family_Planning_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fertility_Family_Planning-44">[44]</a></sup> Though the government of the Republic of Vietnam adopted family planning in general as the official state policy, inadequate medical facilities prevented the policy from being effectively implemented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_Viet_Nam_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_Viet_Nam-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1982, the Vietnam government practiced various family planning measures, including the allowance of use of abortion and the creation of the National Committee for Population and Family Planning. After 1983, each family was required to limit the number of children to two. In 1985, the government increased incentives, such as contraceptives and abortion acceptors, and disincentives, such as penalties for violations in family planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_Viet_Nam_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-UN_Viet_Nam-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, the Party implemented the Renovation (<i><a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BB%95i_M%E1%BB%9Bi" title="Đổi Mới">Đổi Mới</a></i>) Policy, which completely reversed the Communist Party economy to implement capitalistic market ideals. The aims of the Renovation Policy were to end Vietnam's economic isolation, increase competitiveness, and raise living standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_Doi_Moi_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_Doi_Moi-46">[46]</a></sup> In an attempt to effectively develop the population socioeconomically and increase the standard of living within the population, the Vietnam government emphasized the need to contain birth rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_Asian_Values_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_Asian_Values-47">[47]</a></sup> In 1988, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Vietnam" title="Government of Vietnam">Council of Ministers</a> issued an in-depth family planning policy, adding additional restrictions beyond the previous restriction of keeping the maximum number of children per household to two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policy_1989_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Policy_1989-48">[48]</a></sup> The detailed one-or-two-child policy of Vietnam was established nine years after China's <a href="/wiki/One-child_policy" title="One-child policy">one-child policy</a> was implemented, and elements of China's policy are reflected in Vietnam's, such as the emphasis on marrying later,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup> postponing childbearing age (22-years of age or older for women and 24-years of age or older for men),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policy_1989_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Policy_1989-48">[48]</a></sup> and spacing out birth of children (3–5 years apart).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policy_1989_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Policy_1989-48">[48]</a></sup> The state was required to supply free birth control devices (such as <a href="/wiki/Intrauterine_device" title="Intrauterine device">intrauterine loops</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condoms" title="Condoms">condoms</a>, and birth control pills) and to provide facilities for individuals who are eligible for abortions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policy_1989_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-Policy_1989-48">[48]</a></sup> Furthermore, if families did not comply with the two-child policy, they were required to pay high fees and were unable to move into urban centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policy_1989_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-Policy_1989-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, the Vietnamese government issued the first formalization for the unified Vietnam of the one-to-two child policy as a mandatory national policy. The policy combined advertisements and education to promote a smaller family "so people may enjoy a plentiful and happy life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_1993_Resolution_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_1993_Resolution-49">[49]</a></sup> The Vietnamese government explicitly linked the family planning policy with "historical and cultural traditions, value structures and development objectives," encouraging a collectivist mindset in which individuals honor the needs of the nation above their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Human_Rights_Asian_Values_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Human_Rights_Asian_Values-47">[47]</a></sup> The goal of the policy was to reduce the Vietnamese fertility rate to the replacement level of 2.1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Resources_Institute_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-World_Resources_Institute-41">[41]</a></sup> by 2015, so that the country could have a stable population during mid-21st century. In 1997, the goal was accelerated to reach the replacement level by 2005, and the government subsequently integrated an increased use of abortion as a means to curb population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Policy_Springer_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Policy_Springer-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, the Standing Parliamentary Committee of the National Assembly issued the highest legislative document on population titled the Population Ordinance, which restructured the official family planning policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup> According to the ordinance, couples "shall have the right to decide on the time to have babies, the number of children and the duration between child births."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_2003_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population_2003-51">[51]</a></sup> However, shortly after, the government implemented the National Strategy on Population 2001-2010, which again called for decreasing the fertility rate to the replacement level by 2005. This caused controversy as individuals protested the conflicting messages purported by the government in regards to their reproductive rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup> To address this confusion, the government issued Resolution 47 in 2005 which stated that "to sustain high economic growth, Viet Nam needs to pursue a population control policy until it has become an industrialized country." However at this time, the population had already reached the goal of having a total fertility rate below the replacement level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-5"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, the Population Ordinance was amended to again restrict the number of children to be one or two children, although individuals were allowed to decide the timing and spacing of their births.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_Ordinance_2008_Amend_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Population_Ordinance_2008_Amend-52">[52]</a></sup> The government is currently drafting a new Law on Population to replace the Population Ordinance in 2015. However, there is disagreement between policy makers and academics on what should be included in the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Population_Law_2015_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Population_Law_2015-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The organizational structure of the two-child policy was housed under different governmental units since its conception in the 1960s. As the policy evolved from "Initiation in the 1960s–1970s; Maturity in the 1980s–1990s; and Legalization in the 2000s–2010s",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-6"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup> the administration of the population policy also changed. From 1961-1983, the population program fell under the Population and Birth Control Unit. From 1984–2002, it was under the control of National Committee for Population and Family Planning. From 2003–2006, it was in the jurisdiction of the Vietnam Commission for Population, Family and Children. Since 2007, the population program has been under the General Office for Population and Family Planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-7"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the policy was advocated on the national level, the central government did not utilize specific fines or incentives, instead delegating implementation responsibilities to local governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reproductive_Rights_Overview_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reproductive_Rights_Overview-43">[43]</a></sup> Each family was required to have at most two children, and local governments were responsible to decide the details of enforcement. Depending on the specific location, district governments charged fines ranging from 60 to 800 kilograms of paddy rice, equivalent to the worth of a month to a year's wages, for each additional child, and additionally, women who agreed to be sterilized were given bonuses of 120 to 400 kilograms of rice. Individuals who did not use contraceptives sometimes had their names announced over the intercom system of the village to shame them into using them, whereas individuals who did could be selected to win the Labor Medal for "good realization of the population – family planning program".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reproductive_Rights_Overview_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reproductive_Rights_Overview-43">[43]</a></sup> The government and large companies also regularly denied people who violated the policy of their salaries, promotions, and sometimes even their jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reproductive_Rights_Overview_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reproductive_Rights_Overview-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, the effective population policy is the revised 2009 Population Ordinance which states that "each couple and individual has the right and responsibility to participate in the campaigns on population and family planning, reproductive health care: (i) decide time and birth spacing; (ii) have one or two children, exceptional cases to be determined by the Government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Population_Ordinance_2008_Amend_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Population_Ordinance_2008_Amend-52">[52]</a></sup> Thus, individuals have control over the timing and spacing of the births of their children but are still restricted in the number of children they are allowed to have. Furthermore, later that year, Chief Executive Trương Tấn San stressed the need for continued diligence in population control and stated that the population of Vietnam should be 100 million people by 2020, and suggested that a new comprehensive Law on Population be introduced to the government by 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_Population_37-8"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_Population-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_this_policy">Effects of this policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Effects of this policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_of_the_birthrate">Reduction of the birthrate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reduction of the birthrate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The total fertility rate in Vietnam dropped from 5.6 in 1979 to 3.2 by 1993, suggesting the two-child policy was successful in containing the population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Falling_Fertility_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Falling_Fertility-54">[54]</a></sup> According to one demographic model, the <a href="/wiki/Fertility#Bongaarts'_model_of_components_of_fertility" title="Fertility">Bongaarts' model of components of fertility</a>, high rates of contraceptive use and of induced abortion are plausible explanations for the decreased fertility rate. Furthermore, because of this policy, the population has fundamentally changed their ideas of the family. In 1988, the Inter-Censal Demographic and Health Survey found that parents wanted an average of 3.3 children, and in 1994, they found that the ideal number of children fell to 2.8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Falling_Fertility_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Falling_Fertility-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the reported findings differ depending on the <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a> model utilized and on the particular research study cited. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Commission_for_Asia_and_the_Pacific" title="United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific">United Nations Economic and Social Council for Asia and the Pacific</a> found that the average number in household was 3.1 in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Changing_Policy_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Changing_Policy-55">[55]</a></sup> In another study conducted by the America-based non-profit, non-governmental organization <a href="/wiki/Population_Reference_Bureau" title="Population Reference Bureau">Population Reference Bureau</a>, the number found was lower at 2.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRB_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRB-56">[56]</a></sup> Another study, published in the Worldwide State of the Family in 1995 by Tran Xuan Nhi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnam_Families_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vietnam_Families-57">[57]</a></sup> found a contrasting finding that the <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">total fertility rate</a> only dropped slightly and the size of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_families" title="Nuclear families">nuclear families</a> experienced only a slight change, dipping from 4.8 to 4.7 from 1989 to 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Changing_Policy_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Changing_Policy-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex-based_differences">Sex-based differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sex-based differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There is evidence that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Son_preference" title="Son preference">son preference</a> exists in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Preference_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Preference-58">[58]</a></sup> Traditionally, men oversee and are responsible for household enterprises, managing agriculture, ancestral worship, and carrying on the family name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection-59">[59]</a></sup> However, although the desire for a son is seen in the Vietnamese family's fertility practices, the desire for more than one son is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection-59">[59]</a></sup> Families with two daughters are twice as likely to have a third child than families with at least one son, presumably with the hopes that this one will be a boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection-59">[59]</a></sup> Furthermore, women who do not have any sons are around 15% less likely to use contraceptives than families who have at least one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Preference_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Preference-58">[58]</a></sup> There were also increased rates of "contraceptive failure" amongst couples who had a son, as families secretly removed an IUD to bypass the policy in hopes of having a son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_Sex_Disparity_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-No_Sex_Disparity-60">[60]</a></sup> This is consistent with findings from other East Asian countries in which son preference corresponds with a demand for fewer children so that families will have at least one son to maintain the ancestral line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Preference_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Preference-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the evidence for son preference, there is no clear evidence that Vietnam's sex ratio at birth is increasing, as seen in other East Asian countries, notably China, though evidence is conflicting depending on the source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_Sex_Disparity_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-No_Sex_Disparity-60">[60]</a></sup> In fact, according to the Vietnamese census data for 1989 and 1999, the sex ratios of males to females at birth are actually decreasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_Sex_Disparity_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-No_Sex_Disparity-60">[60]</a></sup> On the other hand, some sources state that the impact of son preference varies by region of Vietnam. In the north, there is a strong relationship between sex bias in fertility decisions and number of male births, while in the south, this relationship is nonexistent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-Son_Pref_Sex_Selection-59">[59]</a></sup> However, mothers who pursue certain occupations, such as government cadres and farmers, are more likely to want a particular sex of child and have higher sex-ratio differences at birth. This reflects the pressure for government employees to adhere to the two-child limit, and the perceived necessity of males for manual labor in the farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No_Sex_Disparity_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-No_Sex_Disparity-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Inadequate_contraceptives">Inadequate contraceptives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Inadequate contraceptives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although the policy states that "the state will supply, free of charge, birth control devices... to eligible persons who are cadres, manual workers, civil servants or members of the armed forces... and poor persons who register to practice family planning... The widespread sale of birth control devices will be permitted to facilitate their use by everybody that needs them,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPRS_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-JPRS-61">[61]</a></sup> the only modern contraceptive readily available in Vietnam is the IUD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contraceptive_Need_Modern_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contraceptive_Need_Modern-62">[62]</a></sup> However, many women choose not to use it due to the side effects, such as increased bleeding, back and abdominal pains, headache, and general weakness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_IUD_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_IUD-63">[63]</a></sup> Thus, contraceptive use is low among women under the age of 25, and experts have speculated that "contraceptive use among young women might increase if temporary, easy-to-use methods, such as the pill and the condom, were more accessible. For the government to achieve its two-child policy, the survey committee recommends increased promotion of the commercial availability of the condom and the pill, and strengthening of the government family planning program."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contraceptive_Need_Modern_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Contraceptive_Need_Modern-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_abortion">Increased abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Two-child_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Increased abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Abortion rates in Vietnam are unusually high by international standards, with a total abortion rate of at least 2.5 abortions per woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Goodkind_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Goodkind-64">[64]</a></sup> Generally, the abortion rate for young age groups is higher than older age groups due to a limited awareness of contraceptive methods and availability. Individuals of lower educational levels also have higher abortion rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Overview_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Overview-65">[65]</a></sup> Vietnam also has some of the world's most liberal abortion laws, though the Vietnamese government is aiming to reduce the number of unwanted pregnancies and abortion-related difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Book_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Book-66">[66]</a></sup> Although sex-selective abortions were banned by the government in 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Book_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Book-66">[66]</a></sup> there is evidence that suggests that son preference is associated with a higher likelihood of repeat abortions, as women with no sons were significantly less likely to have a repeat abortion compared to women with one son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repeat_Abortion_Factors_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Repeat_Abortion_Factors-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are multiple factors influencing Vietnam's high abortion rates. First, because women do not have access to contraceptive methods besides IUDs, whilst condoms remain expensive relative to average income, as a result many do not use effective birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Goodkind_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Goodkind-64">[64]</a></sup> Women who have undergone multiple abortions used short-term methods of contraceptives, such as condoms and contraceptive pills, which are less effective than long-acting contraceptives to which many do not have access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repeat_Abortion_Factors_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Repeat_Abortion_Factors-67">[67]</a></sup> Secondly, due to the higher costs of raising a child in some geographic areas of Vietnam, abortions have become more acceptable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Goodkind_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Goodkind-64">[64]</a></sup> Furthermore, the era of modernization and development of free-market reforms since the 1980s has led to a rise in premarital and unwanted pregnancy, and subsequently increased abortion services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Goodkind_64-3"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Goodkind-64">[64]</a></sup> Additionally, the Vietnamese government has insufficient alternatives to abortion within the family planning purposes and a lack of post-abortion contraceptive dialogues for families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abortion_Book_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-Abortion_Book-66">[66]</a></sup> Thus, experts have suggested providing more diverse, long-acting contraceptive alternatives and increasing counseling for families that have experienced an abortion as methods to decrease abortion in Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repeat_Abortion_Factors_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-Repeat_Abortion_Factors-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
