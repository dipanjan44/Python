<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide removal</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="50" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Merge-arrow.svg/50px-Merge-arrow.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Merge-arrow.svg/75px-Merge-arrow.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Merge-arrow.svg/100px-Merge-arrow.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">It has been suggested that this article be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Merging" title="Wikipedia:Merging">merged</a> into <i><a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">Carbon sequestration</a></i>. (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Carbon_sequestration#Merge_carbon_dioxide_removal_into_this?" title="Talk:Carbon sequestration">Discuss</a>)<small><i> Proposed since April 2018.</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Carbon dioxide removal</b> (CDR) refers to a number of technologies, the objective of which is the large-scale removal of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">from the atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Negative_emissions:_Part_1_–_research_landscape_and_synthesis_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Negative_emissions:_Part_1_–_research_landscape_and_synthesis-2">[2]</a></sup>  Among such technologies are <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">ocean fertilization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">enhanced weathering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">direct air capture</a> when combined with storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup> CDR is a different approach than removing CO<sub>2</sub> from the stack emissions of large <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> point sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>. The latter reduces emission to the atmosphere but cannot reduce the amount of carbon dioxide already in the atmosphere. As CDR removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, it creates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission" title="Negative carbon dioxide emission">negative emissions</a>, offsetting emissions from small and dispersed point sources such as domestic heating systems, airplanes and vehicle exhausts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vergragt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vergragt-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azar_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Azar-4">[4]</a></sup>  It is regarded by some as a form of <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-1">[1]</a></sup> while other commentators describe it as a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> or extreme mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCCSI_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCCSI-5">[5]</a></sup> Whether CDR would satisfy common definitions of "climate engineering" or "geoengineering" usually depends upon the scale on which it would be undertaken.
</p><p>The likely need for CDR has been publicly expressed by a range of individuals and organizations involved with climate change issues, including  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> chief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rajendra_Pachauri" title="Rajendra Pachauri">Rajendra Pachauri</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> executive secretary <a href="/wiki/Christiana_Figueres" title="Christiana Figueres">Christiana Figueres</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Watch_Institute" title="World Watch Institute">World Watch Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Institutions with major programs focusing on CDR include the Lenfest Center for Sustainable Energy at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Institute" title="Earth Institute">Earth Institute, Columbia University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the Climate Decision Making Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDMC_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDMC-10">[10]</a></sup> an international collaboration operated out of Carnegie-Mellon University's Department of Engineering and Public Policy.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> effectiveness of air capture is limited by societal investment, land use, availability of geologic reservoirs, and leakage. The reservoirs are estimated to be sufficient to for storing at least 545 GtC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Storing 771 GtC would cause a 186 ppm atmospheric reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenton2009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenton2009-12">[12]</a></sup> In order to return the atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> content to 350 ppm we need atmospheric reduction of 50 ppm plus an additional 2 ppm per year of current emissions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide removal is different from reducing emissions, as the former produces an outlet of carbon dioxide from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, whereas the latter decreases the inlet of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide to the atmosphere</a>. Both have the same net effect, but for achieving carbon dioxide concentration levels below present levels,  carbon dioxide removal is critical. Also for meeting higher concentration levels, carbon dioxide removal is  increasingly considered to be crucial as it provides the only possibility to fill the gap between needed reductions to meet <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigation targets</a> and <a href="/wiki/File:Global_Warming_Observed_CO2_Emissions_from_fossil_fuel_burning_vs_IPCC_scenarios.jpg" title="File:Global Warming Observed CO2 Emissions from fossil fuel burning vs IPCC scenarios.jpg">global emission trends</a>.
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> Environmental Outlook to 2050</i> released at the <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>, the authors commented on the need for negative emissions, stating "Achieving lower concentration targets (450 ppm) depends significantly on the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>".
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">carbon dioxide sink</a> such as a concentrated group of plants or any other primary producer that binds <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into biomass, such as within forests and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelp_bed" title="Kelp bed">kelp beds</a>, is not carbon negative, as sinks are not permanent. A carbon dioxide sink of this type moves <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, in the form of carbon dioxide, from the atmosphere or hydrosphere to the biosphere. This process could be undone, for example by <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a> or <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>.
</p><p>Carbon dioxide sinks that store carbon dioxide in the Earth's crust by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_sequestration_of_CO2" title="Geologic sequestration of CO2">injecting it into the subsurface</a>, or in the form of insoluble <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> salts (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage#Mineral_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">mineral sequestration</a>), are considered carbon negative. This is because they are removing carbon from the atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestering</a> it indefinitely and presumably for a considerable duration (thousands to millions of years). However, Carbon Capture technology remains, at best, theoretical and is yet to reach more than 33% efficiency. Furthermore, this process could be rapidly undone, for example by <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_.26_storage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_&amp;_storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture &amp; storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bio-energy with carbon capture &amp; storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage, or BECCS, uses biomass to extract carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, and carbon capture and storage technologies to concentrate and permanently store it in deep geological formations.
</p><p>BECCS is currently (as of October 2012) the only CDR technology deployed at full industrial scale, with 550 000 tonnes CO<sub>2</sub>/year in total capacity operating, divided between three different facilities (as of January 2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Status_of_BECCS_Projects_2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Status_of_BECCS_Projects_2010-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECN_Global_Technology_Roadmap_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECN_Global_Technology_Roadmap-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>, the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyndall_Centre_for_Climate_Change_Research" title="Tyndall Centre for Climate Change Research">Tyndall Centre for Climate Change Research</a>, the <a class="external text" href="http://www.walker-institute.ac.uk/" rel="nofollow">Walker Institute for Climate System Research</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grantham_Institute_for_Climate_Change" title="Grantham Institute for Climate Change">Grantham Institute for Climate Change</a> issued a joint report on carbon dioxide removal technologies as part of the <i>AVOID: Avoiding dangerous climate change</i> research program, stating that "Overall, of the technologies studied in this report, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a> has the greatest maturity and there are no major practical barriers to its introduction into today’s energy system. The presence of a primary product will support early deployment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVOID_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVOID-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a>, "Achieving lower concentration targets (450 ppm) depends significantly on the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OECD_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochar">Biochar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biochar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a> is created by the <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, and is under investigation as a method of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a> is a charcoal that is used for agricultural purposes which also aids in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>, the capture or hold of carbon.  It is created using a process called pyrolysis, which is basically the act of high temperature heating biomass in an environment with low oxygen levels.  What remains is a material known as char, similar to charcoal but is made through a sustainable process, thus the use of biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  Biomass is organic matter produced by living organisms or recently living organisms, most commonly plants or plant based material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The offset of GHG emission, if biochar were to be implemented, would be a maximum of 12%.  This equates to about 106 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents.  On a medium conservative level, it would be 23% less than that, at  82 metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A study done by the UK Biochar Research Center has stated that, on a conservative level, biochar can store 1 gigaton of carbon per year.  With greater effort in marketing and acceptance of biochar, the benefit could be the storage of 5–9 gigatons per year of carbon in biochar soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_weathering">Enhanced weathering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Enhanced weathering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">Enhanced weathering</a></div>
<p>Enhanced weathering refers to chemical approach to remove carbon dioxide involving land or ocean-based techniques. Examples of land based enhanced weathering techniques are in-situ carbonation of silicates. <a href="/wiki/Ultramafic_rock" title="Ultramafic rock">Ultramafic rock</a>, for example, has the potential to store from hundreds to thousands of years worth of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions according to estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Ocean-based techniques involve alkalinity enhancement, such as, grinding, dispersing and dissolving olivine, limestone, silicates, or calcium hydroxide to address ocean acidification and CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration. Enhanced weathering is considered as one of the least expensive of geoengineering options. One example of a research project on the feasibility of enhanced weathering is the <a href="/wiki/CarbFix" title="CarbFix">CarbFix</a> project in Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Direct_air_capture_.28DAC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_air_capture_(DAC)">Direct air capture (DAC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Direct air capture (DAC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide can be removed from ambient air through chemical processes, sequestered, and stored. Traditional modes of carbon capture such as precombustion and postcombustion CO<sub>2</sub> capture from large point sources can help slow the rate of increase of the atmospheric
CO<sub>2</sub> concentration, but only the direct removal of CO<sub>2</sub> from the air, or “direct air capture” (DAC), can actually reduce the global atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration if combined with long-term storage of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>A few engineering proposals have been made for removing CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere, but work in this area is still in its infancy.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewDAC2016_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewDAC2016-29">[29]</a></sup>
Among the main technologies proposed, three of them stand out: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Causticization&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Causticization (page does not exist)">Causticization</a> with alkali and alkali-earth hydroxides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
and organic−inorganic hybrid sorbents consisting of amines supported in porous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbents" title="Adsorbents">adsorbents</a>. A 2016 article reviews the research in these various areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewDAC2016_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewDAC2016-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>One proposed method is by so-called <i>artificial trees</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This concept, proposed by climate scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wallace_S._Broecker" title="Wallace S. Broecker">Wallace S. Broecker</a> and science writer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kunzig" title="Robert Kunzig">Robert Kunzig</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> imagines huge numbers of artificial trees around the world to remove ambient CO<sub>2</sub>. The technology is now being pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Lackner" title="Klaus Lackner">Klaus Lackner</a>, a researcher at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Institute" title="Earth Institute">Earth Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> whose artificial tree technology can suck up to 1,000 times more CO<sub>2</sub> from the air than real trees can,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> at a rate of about one ton of carbon per day if the artificial tree is approximately the size of an actual tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> would be captured in a filter and then removed from the filter and stored.
</p><p>The chemistry used is a variant of that described <a href="#Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry">below</a>, as it is based on <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. However, in a more recent design proposed by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Lackner" title="Klaus Lackner">Klaus Lackner</a>, the process can be carried out at only 40 °C by using a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_exchange_resin" title="Ion exchange resin">ion exchange resin</a>, which takes advantage of changes in <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> to prompt the release of captured CO<sub>2</sub>, instead of using a kiln. This reduces the energy required to operate the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another substance which can be used are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">Metal-organic frameworks</a> (or MOF's).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A special MOF has been made specifically for locking CO<sub>2</sub> by Joeri Denayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a> covered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> the work of <a href="/wiki/David_Keith_(scientist)" title="David Keith (scientist)">David Keith</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Calgary" title="University of Calgary">University of Calgary</a>, who built a tower, 4 feet wide and 20 feet tall (1.2×6.1 meters), with a fan at the bottom that sucks air in, which comes out again at the top. In the process, about half the CO<sub>2</sub> is removed from the air.
</p><p>This device uses the chemical process described in detail <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal#Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry" title="Carbon dioxide removal">below</a>. The system demonstrated on the Discovery Channel was a 1/90,000th scale test system of the capture section; the reagents are regenerated in a separate facility. The main costs of a full plant will be the cost to build it, and the energy input to regenerate the chemicals and produce a pure stream of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>To put this into perspective, people in the U.S. emit about 20 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per person annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In other words, each person in the U.S. would require a tower like the one featured by the Discovery Channel to remove this amount of CO<sub>2</sub> from the air, requiring an annual 2 megawatt-hours of electricity to operate it. By comparison, a refrigerator consumes about 1.2 megawatt-hours annually (2001 figures). A modern, A++ rated refrigerator uses about 140 Kwh annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> But, by combining many small systems such as this into one large system, the construction costs and energy use can be reduced.
</p><p>It has been proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Solar_updraft_tower" title="Solar updraft tower">Solar updraft tower</a> to generate electricity from thermal air currents also be used at the same time for amine gravity scrubbing of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Some heat would be required to regenerate the amine.
</p><p>A similar CO<sub>2</sub> scrubber has also been built by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Engineering" title="Carbon Engineering">Carbon Engineering</a>. Besides simply focusing on capturing the CO<sub>2</sub>, the company also puts emphasis on reuse of the CO<sub>2</sub>, for example in the production of fuels, which would thus be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_emissions" title="Zero emissions">carbon-neutral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Direct air capture has been proposed as a way of generating carbon-neutral organic chemicals, by harvesting the atmospheric compounds and then using them in the production and synthesis of polymers and fuels.
</p><p>The Swiss-based Climeworks built the first industrial scale direct air capturing plant in <a href="/wiki/Hinwil" title="Hinwil">Hinwil</a>, in the canton of Zurich, Switzerland. It started up in May 2017,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-48">[48]</a></sup> and could scrub up to 900 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub> per year using heat from a local waste incineration plant. The CO2 was then pumped into local <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a>s, where it was used to help grow vegetables like tomatoes and cucumbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climeworks and <a href="/wiki/Orkuveita_Reykjav%C3%ADkur" title="Orkuveita Reykjavíkur">Reykjavik Energy</a> also started up a small test direct air capturing plant in <a href="/wiki/Hellishei%C3%B0i_Power_Station" title="Hellisheiði Power Station">Hellisheidi</a>, Iceland in October 2017. Using waste heat from a local geothermal power plant, the test plant captured up to 50 tons of CO<sub>2</sub> per year which was bound with water and injected over 700 metres underground into basaltic bedrock.  The CO<sub>2</sub> reacted with the basalt to form solid carbonate minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_fertilization">Ocean fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ocean fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">Ocean fertilization</a></div>
<p>Ocean fertilization or ocean nourishment is a type of climate engineering based on the purposeful introduction of nutrients to the upper ocean to increase marine food production and to remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. A number of techniques, including fertilization by iron, urea and phosphorus have been proposed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wooden_building_construction">Wooden building construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wooden building construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In their "roadmap for rapid decarbonization", Rockström et al. (2017) proposed in the journal <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a> that "[a]fter 2030, all building construction must be carbon-neutral or carbon-negative. The construction industry must either use emissions-free concrete and steel or replace those materials with zero- or negative-emissions substances such as wood, stone, and carbon fiber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Especially wooden building constructions are a promising way to store emissions, as wood mainly contains carbon, grows faster due to the higher CO2 concentration and is established as a building material for several centuries. Projects like <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W350&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="W350 (page does not exist)">W350</a> in Tokyo demonstrate that even skyscrapers could potentially be built from wood and, thus, store large amounts of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_CO2_scrubbing_chemistry">Example CO<sub>2</sub> scrubbing chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Example CO2 scrubbing chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">Carbon dioxide scrubber</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calcium_oxide">Calcium oxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Calcium oxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">Calcium oxide</a> (quicklime) will absorb CO<sub>2</sub> from <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric air</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> at 400 °C (forming <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>) and release it at 1,000 °C. This process, proposed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A._Steinfeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A. Steinfeld (page does not exist)">A. Steinfeld</a>, can be performed using <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> from thermal <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist1_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist1-54">[54]</a></sup>
Quicklime is made by heating limestone to release the CO<sub>2</sub> within it.
Quicklime is mixed with sand for brick building as mortar, where it hardens by  absorption of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sodium_hydroxide">Sodium hydroxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sodium hydroxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zeman and Lackner outlined a specific method of air capture using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber#Potassium_hydroxide" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">sodium hydroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Carbon Engineering, a Calgary, Alberta firm founded in 2009 and partially funded by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, is developing a process to capture carbon dioxide using a solution of potassium hydroxide mixed with some water at their <a href="/wiki/Pilot_plant" title="Pilot plant">pilot plant</a>. They hope to create and sell synthetic fuels at a cost of $100 a ton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT01513_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT01513-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Direct_air_capture_.28DAC.29_process_example_using_NaOH"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_air_capture_(DAC)_process_example_using_NaOH">Direct air capture (DAC) process example using NaOH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Direct air capture (DAC) process example using NaOH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon dioxide scrubber</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="986" data-file-width="1996" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png/600px-Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png/900px-Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png/1200px-Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Direct_Air_Capture_Process_Flow_Diagram_using_Caustic_Soda.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main steps in a DCA process that uses NaOH (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caustic_soda" title="Caustic soda">caustic soda</a>) as the absorber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Among the technologies studied for direct air capture (DAC), the use of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber#Sodium_Hydroxide" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">aqueous hydroxide sorbents</a> is one of the most promising approaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReviewDAC2016_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReviewDAC2016-29">[29]</a></sup>
In this process, CO<sub>2</sub> from the air is chemically dissolved into NaOH(aq) solution as Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>; the Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> is then reacted with solid Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>, which regenerates the solvent and produces CaCO<sub>3</sub> crystals; lastly, heat is applied to the CaCO<sub>3</sub> crystals to produce pure CO<sub>2</sub> gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Air is pumped through the CO<sub>2</sub> absorber as the first step of this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-58">[58]</a></sup>  CO<sub>2</sub> absorber for DAC are designed either as a <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">counter-current</a> <a href="/wiki/Spray_tower" title="Spray tower">spray tower</a> or as a counter-current <a href="/wiki/Falling-film_column" title="Falling-film column">thin-falling-film contractor</a> to maximize the contact area between the air and the solvent and thus maximize the absorption driving force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-58">[58]</a></sup>  The solvent is regenerated in the causticization unit by reacting the Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> with Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>, which also transfers the captured CO<sub>2</sub> to the form of CaCO<sub>3</sub> solid crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  A mechanical filter is then used to separate the CaCO<sub>3</sub> crystals from the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  Since the crystals come out wet from the filter, they are dried in a steam dryer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  Then the dry crystals are heated in a furnace to produce CaO and pure CO<sub>2</sub> gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  The CaO is then hydrated to regenerate the Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> used for the causticization reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  The pure CO<sub>2</sub> stream is then compressed and ready to be transported for geologic sequestration, <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">EOR</a>, or other commercial applications.
</p><p>1 M NaOH (aq) is a typical solvent concentration because this concentrations is limited by the causticization reaction that regenerates the solvent it is not too far from the practical maximum of 2 M NaOH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>  The furnace/kiln can be powered renewably or by burning fuel on-site with pure oxygen produces in an on-site air separation unit.
</p><p>NaOH is economically competitive with other absorbents (e.g. Amines) used for DAC processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-10"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup>
DAC processes are energy intensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-11"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-58">[58]</a></sup>
Calcination (at the furnace) is the most energy intensive step of this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_57-12"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_issues">Economic issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economic issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A crucial issue for CDR methods is their cost, which differs substantially among the different technologies: some of these are not sufficiently developed to perform cost assessments. In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> estimated the costs for direct air capture to be $600/tonne with optimistic assumptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> A 2018 study found this estimate lowered to between $94 and $232 per tonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeithHolmes2018_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-KeithHolmes2018-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The IEA Greenhouse Gas R&amp;D Programme and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ecofys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ecofys (page does not exist)">Ecofys</a> provides an estimate that 3.5 billion tonnes could be removed annually from the atmosphere with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a> (Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage) at carbon prices as low as €50 per tonne,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> while a report from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biorecro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biorecro (page does not exist)">Biorecro</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage_(timeline)#Global_Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Institute" title="Carbon capture and storage (timeline)">Global Carbon Capture and Storage Institute</a> estimates costs "below €100" per tonne for large scale BECCS deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCCSI_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-GCCSI-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Risks.2C_problems_and_criticisms"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks,_problems_and_criticisms">Risks, problems and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Risks, problems and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CDR is slow to act, and requires a long-term political and engineering program to effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> CDR is even slower to take effect on <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidified oceans</a>. In a <i>Business as usual</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathways" title="Representative Concentration Pathways">concentration pathway</a>, the deep ocean will remain acidified for centuries, and as a consequence many marine species are in danger of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
