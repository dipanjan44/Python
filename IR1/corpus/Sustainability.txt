<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sustainability</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">process of maintaining change in a balanced fashion</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg"><img alt="A view of the Earth from space." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8000" data-file-width="8000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg/220px-North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg/330px-North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg/440px-North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_America_from_low_orbiting_satellite_Suomi_NPP.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Achieving sustainability will enable the Earth to continue supporting human life.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3782" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg/220px-Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg/330px-Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg/440px-Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inside_the_Batad_rice_terraces.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Batad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_terrace" title="Rice terrace">rice terraces</a> in the Philippines, a UNESCO World Heritage site.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Sustainability</b> is the process of maintaining change in a <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">balanced</a> fashion, in which the exploitation of resources, the direction of investments, the orientation of technological development and institutional change are all in harmony and enhance both current and future potential to meet human needs and aspirations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> For many in the field, sustainability is defined through the following interconnected domains or pillars: environment, economic and social.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Sub-domains of sustainable development have been considered also: cultural, technological and political.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> may be the <a href="/wiki/Organizing_principle" title="Organizing principle">organizing principle</a> for sustainability for some, for others, the two terms are paradoxical (i.e. development is inherently unsustainable).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Sustainable development is the development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> Brundtland Report for the World Commission on Environment and Development (1987) introduced the term of sustainable development.
</p><p>Sustainability can also be defined as a <a href="/wiki/Socio-ecological_system" title="Socio-ecological system">socio-ecological</a> process characterized by the pursuit of a common ideal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr._Joel_Diemer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dr._Joel_Diemer-6">[6]</a></sup> 
An ideal is by definition unattainable in a given time and space. However, by persistently and dynamically approaching it, the process results in a sustainable system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr._Joel_Diemer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dr._Joel_Diemer-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Healthy ecosystems and <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environments</a> are necessary to the survival of humans and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisms" title="Organisms">organisms</a>. Ways of reducing negative human impact are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmentally-friendly" title="Environmentally-friendly">environmentally-friendly</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_resources_management" title="Environmental resources management">environmental resources management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>. Information is gained from <a href="/wiki/Green_computing" title="Green computing">green computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation biology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a> studies the fields of academic research that aim to address human economies and natural ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Moving towards sustainability is also a social challenge that entails <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international</a> and national <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, local and individual <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumerism</a>. Ways of living more sustainably can take many forms from reorganizing living conditions (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecovillages" title="Ecovillages">ecovillages</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-municipalities" title="Eco-municipalities">eco-municipalities</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_cities" title="Sustainable cities">sustainable cities</a>), reappraising economic sectors (<a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>), or work practices (<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a>), using science to develop new technologies (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_technologies" title="Green technologies">green technologies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and sustainable <a href="/wiki/Generation_IV_reactor" title="Generation IV reactor">fission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">fusion power</a>), or designing systems in a flexible and reversible manner,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_2012_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang_2012-9">[9]</a></sup> and adjusting individual <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyles</a> that conserve natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>"The term 'sustainability' should be viewed as humanity's target goal of human-ecosystem equilibrium (homeostasis), while 'sustainable development' refers to the holistic approach and temporal processes that lead us to the end point of sustainability." (305)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite the increased popularity of the use of the term "sustainability", the possibility that human societies will achieve environmental sustainability has been, and continues to be, questioned—in light of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, population growth and societies' pursuit of unlimited <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a></div>
<p>The name sustainability is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>sustinere</i> (<i>tenere</i>, to hold; <i>sub</i>, under). <i>Sustain</i> can mean "maintain", "support", or "endure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Since the 1980s <i>sustainability</i> has been used more in the sense of human sustainability on planet Earth and this has resulted in the most widely quoted definition of sustainability as a part of the concept <i><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a></i>, that of the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">Brundtland Commission</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> on March 20, 1987: "sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_dimensions_of_sustainability">Three dimensions of sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Three dimensions of sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<td style="border: 0px;"><a href="/wiki/Venn_diagram" title="Venn diagram">Venn diagram</a> of sustainable development:<br/> at the confluence of three constituent parts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams2006-19">[19]</a></sup>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/2005_World_Summit" title="2005 World Summit">2005 World Summit on Social Development</a> identified sustainable development goals, such as economic development, social development and environmental protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This view has been expressed as an illustration using three overlapping ellipses indicating that the three pillars of sustainability are not mutually exclusive and can be mutually reinforcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In fact, the three pillars are interdependent, and in the long run none can exist without the others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The three pillars have served as a common ground for numerous <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_standards_and_certification" title="Sustainability standards and certification">sustainability standards and certification</a> systems in recent years, in particular in the food industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Standards which today explicitly refer to the triple bottom line include <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Alliance" title="Rainforest Alliance">Rainforest Alliance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairtrade" title="Fairtrade">Fairtrade</a> and <a href="/wiki/UTZ_Certified" title="UTZ Certified">UTZ Certified</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some sustainability experts and practitioners have illustrated four pillars of sustainability, or a quadruple bottom line. One such pillar is future generations, which emphasizes the long-term thinking associated with sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  There is also an opinion that considers resource use and financial sustainability as two additional pillars of sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sustainable development consists of balancing local and global efforts to meet basic human needs without destroying or degrading the natural environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The question then becomes how to represent the relationship between those needs and the environment.
</p><p>A study from 2005 pointed out that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> is as important as  sustainable development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Ecological economist <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> asked, "what use is a sawmill without a forest?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daly_&amp;_Cobb_1989_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daly_&amp;_Cobb_1989-32">[32]</a></sup> From this perspective, the economy is a subsystem of human society, which is itself a subsystem of the biosphere, and a gain in one sector is a loss from another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This perspective led to the nested circles figure of 'economics' inside 'society' inside the 'environment'.
</p><p>The simple definition that sustainability is something that improves "the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of human life</a> while living within the carrying capacity of supporting eco-systems",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caring_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-caring-34">[34]</a></sup> though vague, conveys the idea of sustainability having quantifiable limits. But sustainability is also a call to action, a task in progress or "journey" and therefore a political process, so some definitions set out common goals and values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Earth_Charter" title="Earth Charter">Earth Charter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthCharter_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-EarthCharter-36">[36]</a></sup> speaks of "a sustainable global society founded on respect for nature, universal human rights, economic justice, and a culture of peace". This suggested a more complex figure of sustainability, which included the importance of the domain of 'politics'.
</p><p>More than that, sustainability implies responsible and proactive decision-making and innovation that minimizes negative impact and maintains balance between ecological resilience, economic prosperity, political justice and cultural vibrancy to ensure a desirable planet for all species now and in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Specific types of sustainability include, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Understanding sustainable development is important but without clear targets an unfocused term like "liberty" or "justice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It has also been described as a "dialogue of values that challenge the sociology of development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Circles_of_sustainability_and_the_fourth_dimension_of_sustainability">Circles of sustainability and the fourth dimension of sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Circles of sustainability and the fourth dimension of sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sao_Paulo_Profile,_Level_1,_2012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1156" data-file-width="1074" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg/220px-Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg/330px-Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg/440px-Sao_Paulo_Profile%2C_Level_1%2C_2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sao_Paulo_Profile,_Level_1,_2012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Urban sustainability analysis of the greater urban area of the city of São Paulo using the ‘Circles of Sustainability' method of the UN and Metropolis Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Millennium_Declaration" title="United Nations Millennium Declaration">United Nations Millennium Declaration</a> identified principles and treaties on sustainable development, including economic development, <a href="/wiki/Social_sustainability" title="Social sustainability">social development</a> and environmental protection it continued using three domains: economics, environment and social sustainability. More recently, using a systematic domain model that responds to the debates over the last decade, the <a href="/wiki/Circles_of_Sustainability" title="Circles of Sustainability">Circles of Sustainability</a> approach distinguished four domains of economic, ecological, political and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_sustainability" title="Cultural sustainability">cultural sustainability</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> this in accord with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unesco" title="Unesco">Unesco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a>, and in particular the <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21_for_culture" title="Agenda 21 for culture">Agenda 21 for culture</a> which specifies <b>culture</b> as the <b>fourth</b> domain of sustainable development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agenda21culture.net_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-agenda21culture.net-43">[43]</a></sup> The model is now being used by organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Global_Compact" title="United Nations Global Compact">United Nations</a> Cities Programme<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and Metropolis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In the case of Metropolis, this approach does not mean adding a fourth domain of culture to the dominant <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">triple bottom line</a> figure of the economy, environment and the social. Rather, it involves treating all four domains—economy, ecology, politics and culture—as social (including economics) and distinguishing between ecology (as the intersection of the human and natural worlds) and environment as that which goes far beyond what we as humans can ever know.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seven_modalities">Seven modalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Seven modalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another model suggests humans attempt to achieve all of their needs and aspirations via seven modalities: economy, community, occupational groups, government, environment, culture, and physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> From the global to the individual human scale, each of the seven modalities can be viewed across seven hierarchical levels. Human sustainability can be achieved by attaining sustainability in all levels of the seven modalities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaping_the_future">Shaping the future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shaping the future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Integral elements of sustainability are research and innovation activities. A telling example is the <a href="/wiki/European_environmental_research_and_innovation_policy" title="European environmental research and innovation policy">European environmental research and innovation policy</a>. It aims at defining and implementing a transformative agenda to greening the economy and the society as a whole so to make them sustainable. Research and innovation in Europe are financially supported by the programme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_2020" title="Horizon 2020">Horizon 2020</a>, which is also open to participation worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
Encouraging good farming practices ensures farmers fully benefit from the environment and at the same time conserving it for future generations. Additionally, instigating innovative and sustainable travel and transportation solutions must play a vital role in this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resiliency">Resiliency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Resiliency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/resilience" title="wikt:resilience">Resiliency</a> in ecology is the capacity of an ecosystem to absorb disturbance and still retain its basic structure and viability. Resilience-thinking evolved from the need to manage interactions between human-constructed systems and natural ecosystems in a sustainable way despite the fact that to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policymaker" title="Policymaker">policymakers</a> a definition remains elusive. Resilience-thinking addresses how much planetary ecological systems can withstand assault from human disturbances and still deliver the service's current and future generations need from them.  It is also concerned with commitment from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geopolitical" title="Geopolitical">geopolitical</a> policymakers to promote and manage essential planetary ecological resources in order to promote resilience and achieve sustainability of these essential resources for benefit of future generations of life?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The resiliency of an ecosystem, and thereby, its sustainability, can be reasonably measured at <a href="/wiki/Juncture" title="Juncture">junctures</a> or events where the combination of <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">naturally</a> occurring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_(design)" title="Regenerative (design)">regenerative</a> forces (<a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, water, soil, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, vegetation, and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>) interact with the energy released into the ecosystem from disturbances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>A practical view of sustainability is <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed systems</a> that maintain processes of <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> indefinitely by replacing resources used by actions of people with resources of equal or greater value by those same people without degrading or endangering natural biotic systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In this way, sustainability can be concretely measured in human projects if there is a transparent accounting of the resources put back into the ecosystem to replace those displaced. In nature, the accounting occurs naturally through a process of <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a> as an ecosystem returns to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/viability" title="wikt:viability">viability</a> from an external disturbance. The adaptation is a multi-stage process that begins with the disturbance event (earthquake, volcanic eruption, hurricane, tornado, flood, or thunderstorm), followed by <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">absorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utilization" title="Utilization">utilization</a>, or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rejection" title="wikt:rejection">deflection</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> or energies that the external forces created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In analysing systems such as urban and national parks, dams, farms and gardens, theme parks, open-pit mines, water catchments, one way to look at the relationship between sustainability and resiliency is to view the former with a long-term vision and resiliency as the capacity of human engineers to respond to immediate environmental events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_sustainability" title="History of sustainability">History of sustainability</a></div>
<p>The history of sustainability traces human-dominated <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> systems from the earliest <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a> to the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> This history is characterized by the increased regional success of a particular <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>, followed by crises that were either resolved, producing sustainability, or not, leading to decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In early human history, the use of fire and desire for specific foods may have altered the natural composition of plant and animal communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Between 8,000 and 10,000 years ago, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrarian_community" title="Agrarian community">agrarian communities</a> emerged which depended largely on their <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> and the creation of a "structure of permanence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> of the 18th to 19th centuries tapped into the vast growth potential of the energy in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">Coal</a> was used to power ever more efficient engines and later to generate electricity. Modern <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> systems and advances in medicine protected large populations from disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In the mid-20th century, a gathering <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> pointed out that there were environmental costs associated with the many material benefits that were now being enjoyed. In the late 20th century, environmental problems became global in scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPR_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-LPR-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The 1973 and 1979 <a href="/wiki/Energy_crisis" title="Energy crisis">energy crises</a> demonstrated the extent to which the global community had become dependent on non-renewable energy resources.
</p><p>In the 21st century, there is increasing global awareness of the threat posed by the human <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>, produced largely by forest clearing and the burning of fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_and_concepts">Principles and concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Principles and concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The philosophical and analytic framework of sustainability draws on and connects with many different disciplines and fields; in recent years an area that has come to be called <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_science" title="Sustainability science">sustainability science</a> has emerged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_and_context">Scale and context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Scale and context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sustainability is studied and managed over many scales (levels or frames of reference) of time and space and in many contexts of environmental, social and economic organization. The focus ranges from the total <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> (sustainability) of planet Earth to the sustainability of economic sectors, ecosystems, countries, municipalities, neighbourhoods, home gardens, individual lives, individual goods and services<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, occupations, lifestyles, behaviour patterns and so on. In short, it can entail the full compass of biological and human activity or any part of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> As Daniel Botkin, author and environmentalist, has stated: "We see a landscape that is always in flux, changing over many scales of time and space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sheer size and complexity of the planetary ecosystem has proved problematic for the design of practical measures to reach global sustainability. To shed light on the big picture, explorer and sustainability campaigner <a href="/wiki/Jason_Lewis_(adventurer)" title="Jason Lewis (adventurer)">Jason Lewis</a> has drawn parallels to other, more tangible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_systems" title="Closed systems">closed systems</a>. For example, he likens human existence on Earth — isolated as the planet is in space, whereby people cannot be evacuated to relieve population pressure and resources cannot be imported to prevent accelerated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depletion_of_resources" title="Depletion of resources">depletion of resources</a> — to life at sea on a small boat isolated by water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In both cases, he argues, exercising the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a> is a key factor in survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption">Consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A major driver of human impact on Earth systems is the destruction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biophysical" title="Biophysical">biophysical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resources" title="Resources">resources</a>, and especially, the Earth's ecosystems. The environmental impact of a community or of humankind as a whole depends both on population and impact per person, which in turn depends in complex ways on what resources are being used, whether or not those resources are renewable, and the scale of the human activity relative to the carrying capacity of the ecosystems involved. Careful resource management can be applied at many scales, from economic sectors like agriculture, manufacturing and industry, to work organizations, the consumption patterns of households and individuals and to the resource demands of individual goods and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brower_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brower-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the initial attempts to express human impact mathematically was developed in the 1970s and is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_PAT" title="I PAT">I PAT</a> formula. This formulation attempts to explain human consumption in terms of three components: <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> numbers, levels of consumption (which it terms "affluence", although the usage is different), and impact per unit of resource use (which is termed "technology", because this impact depends on the <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> used). The equation is expressed:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>I = P × A × T</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>Where: I = Environmental impact, P = Population, A = Affluence, T = Technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich&amp;Holden_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich&amp;Holden-74">[74]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circularity">Circularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Circularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years, concepts based on (re-)cycling resources are increasingly gaining importance. The most prominent among these concepts might be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_Economy" title="Circular Economy">Circular Economy</a>, with its comprehensive support by the Chinese and the European Union. There is also a broad range of similar concepts or schools of thought, including cradle-to-cradle laws of ecology, looped and performance economy, regenerative design, industrial ecology, biomimicry, and the blue economy. These concepts seem intuitively to be more sustainable than the current linear economic system. The reduction of resource inputs into and waste and emission leakage out of the system reduces resource depletion and environmental pollution. However, these simple assumptions are not sufficient to deal with the involved systemic complexity and disregards potential trade-offs. For example, the social dimension of sustainability seems to be only marginally addressed in many publications on the Circular Economy, and there are cases that require different or additional strategies, like purchasing new, more energy efficient equipment. A review of a team of researchers from Cambridge and TU Delft identified eight different relationship types between sustainability and the circular economy, namely a (1) conditional relation, a (2) strong conditional relation, a (3) necessary but not sufficient conditional relation, a (4) beneficial relationship a (structured and unstructured) (5) subset relation, a (6) degree relation, a (7) cost-benefit/trade-off relation, and a (8) selective relation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_measurement" title="Sustainability measurement">Sustainability measurement</a></div>
<p>Sustainability measurement is the quantitative basis for the informed management of sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The metrics used for the measurement of sustainability (involving the sustainability of environmental, social and economic domains, both individually and in various combinations) are evolving: they include <a href="/wiki/Ecological_indicator" title="Ecological indicator">indicators</a>, benchmarks, audits, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_standards_and_certification" title="Sustainability standards and certification">sustainability standards and certification</a> systems like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairtrade" title="Fairtrade">Fairtrade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_certification" title="Organic certification">Organic</a>, indexes and accounting, as well as assessment, appraisal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and other reporting systems. They are applied over a wide range of spatial and temporal scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the best known and most widely used sustainability measures include corporate <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_reporting" title="Sustainability reporting">sustainability reporting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">Triple Bottom Line accounting</a>, World Sustainability Society, <a href="/wiki/Circles_of_Sustainability" title="Circles of Sustainability">Circles of Sustainability</a>, and estimates of the quality of sustainability governance for individual countries using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Sustainability_Index" title="Environmental Sustainability Index">Environmental Sustainability Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Performance_Index" title="Environmental Performance Index">Environmental Performance Index</a>.
</p><p>Companies such as Lieef (www.Lieef.com) have started reporting ESG metrics on behalf of companies, and investment funds, in an effort to increase transparency through patenting pending technology that measures emissions from a gross to net basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_population_planning" title="Human population planning">Human population planning</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg"><img alt="Graph showing human population growth from 10,000 BC – 2000 AD, illustrating current exponential growth" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="547" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/300px-Population_curve.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/450px-Population_curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Population_curve.svg/600px-Population_curve.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Population_curve.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graph showing human population growth from 10,000 BC – 2000 AD, illustrating current exponential growth</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WorldPopGrowth.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="754" data-file-width="979" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/WorldPopGrowth.png/300px-WorldPopGrowth.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/WorldPopGrowth.png/450px-WorldPopGrowth.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/WorldPopGrowth.png/600px-WorldPopGrowth.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WorldPopGrowth.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World population growth rate, 1950–2050, as estimated in 2011 by the U.S. Census Bureau, International Data Base</div></div></div>
<p>According to the most recent (July 2015) revision of the official United Nations World Population Prospects, the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> is projected to reach 8.5 billion by 2030, up from the current 7.3 billion (July 2015), to exceed 9 billion people by 2050, and to reach 11.2 billion by the year 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Most of the increase will be in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> whose population is projected to rise from 5.6 billion in 2009 to 7.9 billion in 2050. This increase will be distributed among the population aged 15–59 (1.2 billion) and 60 or over (1.1 billion) because the number of children under age 15 in developing countries is predicted to decrease. In contrast, the population of the more <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed regions</a> is expected to undergo only slight increase from 1.23 billion to 1.28 billion, and this would have declined to 1.15 billion but for a projected net migration from developing to developed countries, which is expected to average 2.4 million persons annually from 2009 to 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Long-term estimates in 2004 of global population suggest a peak at around 2070 of nine to ten billion people, and then a slow decrease to 8.4 billion by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Emerging economies like those of China and India aspire to the living standards of the Western world as does the non-industrialized world in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> It is the combination of population increase in the developing world and unsustainable consumption levels in the developed world that poses a stark challenge to sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2006_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2006-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carrying_capacity">Carrying capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Carrying capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">Carrying capacity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg"><img alt="Graph comparing the Ecological Footprint of different nations with their Human Development Index" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="522" data-file-width="850" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/300px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/450px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/600px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Ecological footprint for different nations compared to their Human Development Index (HDI)</center></div></div></div>
<p>At the global scale, scientific data now indicates that humans are living beyond the <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of planet Earth and that this cannot continue indefinitely. This scientific evidence comes from many sources but is presented in detail in the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">planetary boundaries</a> framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> An early detailed examination of global limits was published in the 1972 book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limits_to_Growth" title="Limits to Growth">Limits to Growth</a></i>, which has prompted follow-up commentary and analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> A 2012 review in <i>Nature</i> by 22 international researchers expressed concerns that the Earth may be "approaching a state shift" in its biosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a> measures human consumption in terms of the biologically productive land needed to provide the resources, and absorb the wastes of the average global citizen. In 2008 it required 2.7 <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> per person, 30% more than the natural biological capacity of 2.1 global hectares (assuming no provision for other organisms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPR_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-LPR-62">[62]</a></sup> The resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_deficit" title="Ecological deficit">ecological deficit</a> must be met from unsustainable <i>extra</i> sources and these are obtained in three ways: embedded in the goods and services of world trade; taken from the past (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>); or borrowed from the future as unsustainable resource usage (e.g. by <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">over exploiting</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests#Forest_management_and_forest_loss" title="Forests">forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries</a>).
</p><p>The figure (right) examines sustainability at the scale of individual countries by contrasting their Ecological Footprint with their UN <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (a measure of standard of living). The graph shows what is necessary for countries to maintain an acceptable standard of living for their citizens while, at the same time, maintaining sustainable resource use. The general trend is for higher standards of living to become less sustainable. As always, <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> has a marked influence on levels of consumption and the efficiency of resource use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich&amp;Holden_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich&amp;Holden-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> The sustainability goal is to raise the global standard of living without increasing the use of resources beyond globally sustainable levels; that is, to not exceed "one planet" consumption. Information generated by reports at the national, regional and city scales confirm the global trend towards societies that are becoming less sustainable over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_American" title="Romanian American">Romanian American</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen</a>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_considered_father_or_mother_of_a_scientific_field" title="List of people considered father or mother of a scientific field">progenitor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift#Kuhnian_paradigm_shifts" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm founder</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>, has argued that the carrying capacity of Earth — that is, Earth's capacity to sustain human populations and consumption levels — is bound to decrease sometime in the future as Earth's finite stock of mineral resources is presently being extracted and put to use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>303</span></sup> Leading ecological economist and <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy#Herman_Daly's_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy" title="Steady-state economy">steady-state theorist</a> <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a>, a student of Georgescu-Roegen, has <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Daly's_concession" title="Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen">propounded the same argument</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>369–371</span></sup>
</p><p>At the enterprise scale, carrying capacity now also plays a critical role in making it possible to measure and report the sustainability performance of individual organizations.  This is most clearly demonstrated through use of <a href="/wiki/Context-Based_Sustainability" title="Context-Based Sustainability">Context-Based Sustainability</a> (CBS) tools, methods and metrics, including the MultiCapital Scorecard, which have been in development since 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>  Contrary to many other mainstream approaches to measuring the sustainability performance of organizations – which tend to be more incrementalist in form – CBS is explicitly tied to social, environmental and economic limits and thresholds in the world.  Thus, rather than simply measure and report changes in relative terms from one period to another, CBS makes it possible to compare the impacts of organizations to organization-specific norms, standards or thresholds for what they (the impacts) would have to be in order to be empirically sustainable (i.e., which if generalized to a larger population would not fail to maintain the sufficiency of vital resources for human or non-human well-being).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_human_impact_on_biodiversity">Global human impact on biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Global human impact on biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a></div>
<p>At a fundamental level, <a href="/wiki/Energy_flow_(ecology)" title="Energy flow (ecology)">energy flow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycling</a> set an upper limit on the number and mass of organisms in any ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Human impacts on the Earth are demonstrated in a general way through detrimental changes in the global biogeochemical cycles of chemicals that are critical to life, most notably those of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</i> is an international synthesis by over 1000 of the world's leading biological scientists that analyzes the state of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and provides summaries and guidelines for decision-makers. It concludes that human activity is having a significant and escalating impact on the biodiversity of world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>, reducing both their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a>. The report refers to natural systems as humanity's "life-support system", providing essential "<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>". The assessment measures 24 ecosystem services concluding that only four have shown improvement over the last 50 years, 15 are in serious decline, and five are in a precarious condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_development_goals">Sustainable development goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sustainable development goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_development_goals" title="Sustainable development goals">Sustainable development goals</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> (SDGs) are the current harmonized set of seventeen future international development targets.
</p><p>The Official Agenda for Sustainable Development adopted on 25 September 2015 has 92 paragraphs, with the main paragraph (51) outlining the 17 Sustainable Development Goals and its associated 169 targets.  This included the following seventeen goals:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><b>Poverty</b> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_poverty_in_all_its_forms_everywhere" title="End poverty in all its forms everywhere">End poverty in all its forms everywhere</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Food</b> – <a href="/wiki/Hunger#Global_initiatives_to_end_hunger" title="Hunger">End hunger</a>, achieve <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> and improved nutrition and promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Health</b> – Ensure healthy lives and promote <a href="/wiki/Well-being" title="Well-being">well-being</a> for all at all ages<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Education</b> – Ensure <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_(education)" title="Inclusion (education)">inclusive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Educational_equity" title="Educational equity">equitable</a> quality education and promote <a href="/wiki/Lifelong_learning" title="Lifelong learning">lifelong learning</a> opportunities for all<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Women</b> – Achieve <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Empowerment" title="Empowerment">empower</a> all women and girls<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Water</b> – Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Energy</b> – Ensure access to affordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Economy</b> – Promote sustained, inclusive and sustainable economic growth, full and productive employment and <a href="/wiki/Decent_work" title="Decent work">decent work</a> for all<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Infrastructure</b> – Build resilient <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, promote inclusive and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_industries" title="Sustainable industries">sustainable industrialization</a> and foster <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Inequality</b> – Reduce <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">inequality</a> within and among countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Habitation</b> – Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Consumption</b> – Ensure sustainable consumption and production patterns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Climate</b> – Take urgent action to combat <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and its impacts, ensuring that both mitigation and adaptation strategies are in placed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Marine-ecosystems</b> – Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a> for sustainable development<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Ecosystems</b> – Protect, restore and promote sustainable use of terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, sustainably manage forests, combat <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, and halt and reverse <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a> and halt <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Institutions</b> – Promote peaceful and inclusive societies for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_fair_trial" title="Right to fair trial">access to justice</a> for all and build effective, accountable and inclusive institutions at all levels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Sustainability</b> – Strengthen the means of implementation and revitalize the global partnership for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>As of August 2015, there were 169 proposed targets for these goals and 304 proposed indicators to show compliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a> (SDGs) replace the eight <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Development_Goals" title="Millennium Development Goals">Millennium Development Goals</a> (MDGs), which expired at the end of 2015. The MDGs were established in 2000 following the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Summit" title="Millennium Summit">Millennium Summit</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>. Adopted by the 189 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_member_states" title="United Nations member states">United Nations member states</a> at the time and more than twenty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_organizations" title="International organizations">international organizations</a>, these goals were advanced to help achieve the following <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> standards by 2015.
</p>
<ol><li>To eradicate <a href="/wiki/Extreme_poverty" title="Extreme poverty">extreme poverty and hunger</a></li>
<li>To achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_primary_education" title="Universal primary education">universal primary education</a></li>
<li>To promote <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> and empower women</li>
<li>To reduce <a href="/wiki/Child_mortality" title="Child mortality">child mortality</a></li>
<li>To improve <a href="/wiki/Maternal_health" title="Maternal health">maternal health</a></li>
<li>To combat <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, and other diseases</li>
<li>To ensure environmental sustainability (one of the targets in this goal focuses on increasing sustainable access to safe <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">drinking water</a> and basic <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a>)</li>
<li>To develop a global partnership for development</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_development">Sustainable development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sustainable development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the data that member countries represented to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> was the only country in the world in 2006 that met the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a>'s definition of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, with an <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of less than 1.8 hectares per capita, 1.5, and a <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> of over 0.8, 0.855.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_dimension">Environmental dimension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental dimension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Healthy ecosystems provide vital goods and services to humans and other organisms. There are two major ways of reducing negative human impact and enhancing <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> and the first of these is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_management" title="Environmental management">environmental management</a>. This direct approach is based largely on information gained from <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">conservation biology</a>.
However, this is management at the end of a long series of indirect causal factors that are initiated by human <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a>, so a second approach is through demand management of human resource use.
</p><p>Management of human consumption of resources is an indirect approach based largely on information gained from <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. Herman Daly has suggested three broad criteria for ecological sustainability: renewable resources should provide a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_yield" title="Sustainable yield">sustainable yield</a> (the rate of harvest should not exceed the rate of regeneration); for non-renewable resources there should be equivalent development of renewable substitutes; waste generation should not exceed the assimilative capacity of the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_management">Environmental management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Environmental management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_and_environmental_management" title="Sustainability and environmental management">Sustainability and environmental management</a></div>
<p>At the global scale and in the broadest sense environmental management involves the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> systems, land and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, but following the sustainability principle of scale it can be equally applied to any ecosystem from a tropical rainforest to a home garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At a March 2009 meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Climate_Council" title="Copenhagen Climate Council">Copenhagen Climate Council</a>, 2,500 climate experts from 80 countries issued a keynote statement that there is now "no excuse" for failing to act on global warming and that without strong carbon reduction "abrupt or irreversible" shifts in climate may occur that "will be very difficult for contemporary societies to cope with".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Management of the global atmosphere now involves assessment of all aspects of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> to identify opportunities to address human-induced <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and this has become a major focus of scientific research because of the potential catastrophic effects on biodiversity and human communities (see <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> below).
</p><p>Other human impacts on the atmosphere include the <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> in cities, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollutants" title="Pollutants">pollutants</a> including toxic chemicals like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur oxides</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">airborne particulate matter</a> that produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_smog" title="Photochemical smog">photochemical smog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> that degrade the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Anthropogenic</a> particulates such as sulfate <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> in the atmosphere reduce the direct <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> and reflectance (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface. Known as <a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">global dimming</a>, the decrease is estimated to have been about 4% between 1960 and 1990 although the trend has subsequently reversed. Global dimming may have disturbed the global <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a> by reducing evaporation and rainfall in some areas. It also creates a cooling effect and this may have partially masked the effect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Freshwater_and_oceans">Freshwater and oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Freshwater and oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bleached_coral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="467" data-file-width="600" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Bleached_coral.jpg/220px-Bleached_coral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Bleached_coral.jpg/330px-Bleached_coral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Bleached_coral.jpg/440px-Bleached_coral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bleached_coral.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Changes in environmental conditions lead to <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> and harm to biodiversity of fragile marine ecosystems.</div></div></div>
<p>Water covers 71% of the Earth's surface. Of this, 97.5% is the salty water of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> and only 2.5% freshwater, most of which is locked up in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheet</a>. The remaining freshwater is found in glaciers, lakes, rivers, wetlands, the soil, aquifers and atmosphere. Due to the water cycle, fresh water supply is continually replenished by precipitation, however there is still a limited amount necessitating management of this resource. Awareness of the global importance of preserving water for <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> has only recently emerged as, during the 20th century, more than half the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a> have been lost along with their valuable environmental services. Increasing <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> pollutes clean water supplies and much of the world still does not have access to clean, safe water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlas_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas-128">[128]</a></sup> Greater emphasis is now being placed on the improved management of blue (harvestable) and green (soil water available for plant use) water, and this applies at all scales of water management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-water_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-water-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a> circulation patterns have a strong influence on <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and, in turn, the food supply of both humans and other organisms. Scientists have warned of the possibility, under the influence of climate change, of a sudden alteration in circulation patterns of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> that could drastically alter the climate in some regions of the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> Ten per cent of the world's population—about 600 million people—live in low-lying areas vulnerable to sea level rise.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rice_Field.jpg"><img alt="A farmer working in a rice paddy." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="279" data-file-width="315" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Rice_Field.jpg/220px-Rice_Field.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Rice_Field.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rice_Field.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A rice paddy in Bangladesh. Rice, wheat, corn and potatoes make up more than half the world's food supply.</div></div></div>
<p>Loss of biodiversity stems largely from the habitat loss and fragmentation produced by the human appropriation of land for development, forestry and agriculture as <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> is progressively converted to man-made capital. Land use change is fundamental to the operations of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> because alterations in the relative proportions of land dedicated to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanisation" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> have a marked effect on the global water, carbon and nitrogen biogeochemical cycles and this can impact negatively on both natural and human systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> At the local human scale, major sustainability benefits accrue from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_gardening" title="Sustainable gardening">sustainable parks and gardens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_cities" title="Green cities">green cities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a> about 47% of the world's forests have been lost to human use. Present-day forests occupy about a quarter of the world's ice-free land with about half of these occurring in the tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> In temperate and boreal regions forest area is gradually increasing (with the exception of Siberia), but <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in the tropics is of major concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">Food</a> is essential to life. Feeding more than seven billion human bodies takes a heavy toll on the Earth's resources. This begins with the appropriation of about 38% of the Earth's land surface<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup> and about 20% of its net primary productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> Added to this are the resource-hungry activities of industrial agribusiness—everything from the crop need for irrigation water, synthetic <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> to the resource costs of food packaging, transport (now a major part of global trade) and retail. Environmental problems associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" title="Industrial agriculture">industrial agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusiness</a> are now being addressed through such movements as sustainable agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> and more sustainable business practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_of_human_consumption">Management of human consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Management of human consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">Consumption (economics)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Helix_of_sustainability.png"><img alt="Diagram showing ways that the manufacturing process can reduce the use of energy" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1280" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/Helix_of_sustainability.png/220px-Helix_of_sustainability.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/Helix_of_sustainability.png/330px-Helix_of_sustainability.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/13/Helix_of_sustainability.png/440px-Helix_of_sustainability.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Helix_of_sustainability.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Helix of sustainability—the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> of manufacturing</div></div></div>
<p>The underlying driver of direct human impacts on the environment is human consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> This impact is reduced by not only consuming less but by also making the full cycle of production, use and disposal more sustainable. Consumption of goods and services can be analysed and managed at all scales through the chain of consumption, starting with the effects of individual lifestyle choices and spending patterns, through to the resource demands of specific goods and services, the impacts of economic sectors, through national economies to the global economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> Analysis of consumption patterns relates resource use to the environmental, social and economic impacts at the scale or context under investigation. The ideas of embodied resource use (the total resources needed to produce a product or service), <a href="/wiki/Resource_intensity" title="Resource intensity">resource intensity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Resource_productivity" title="Resource productivity">resource productivity</a> are important tools for understanding the impacts of consumption. Key resource categories relating to human needs are <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, materials and water.
</p><p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/International_Resource_Panel" title="International Resource Panel">International Resource Panel</a>, hosted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP), published the first global scientific assessment on the impacts of consumption and production<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> and identified priority actions for developed and developing countries. The study found that the most critical impacts are related to <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> health, human health and <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>. From a production perspective, it found that fossil-fuel combustion processes, agriculture and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> have the most important impacts. Meanwhile, from a final <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> perspective, it found that household consumption related to mobility, shelter, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> and energy-using products cause the majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">life-cycle</a> impacts of consumption.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">Sustainable energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">Renewable energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">Efficient energy use</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg"><img alt="Diagram showing the flow of CO2 in an ecosystem" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="563" data-file-width="750" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg/220px-Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg/330px-Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg/440px-Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Genomics_GTL_Program_Payoffs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flow of CO<sub>2</sub> in an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Sun's energy, stored by plants (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_producer" title="Primary producer">primary producers</a>) during <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, passes through the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> to other organisms to ultimately power all living processes. Since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> the concentrated energy of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> stored in fossilized plants as <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> has been a major driver of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> which, in turn, has been the source of both economic and political power. In 2007 climate scientists of the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> concluded that there was at least a 90% probability that atmospheric increase in CO<sub>2</sub> was human-induced, mostly as a result of fossil fuel emissions but, to a lesser extent from changes in land use. Stabilizing the world's climate will require high-income countries to reduce their emissions by 60–90% over 2006 levels by 2050 which should hold CO<sub>2</sub> levels at 450–650 ppm from current levels of about 380 ppm. Above this level, temperatures could rise by more than 2 °C to produce "catastrophic" <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> Reduction of current CO<sub>2</sub> levels must be achieved against a background of global population increase and developing countries aspiring to energy-intensive high consumption Western lifestyles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reducing greenhouse emissions, is being tackled at all scales, ranging from tracking the passage of carbon through the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_commercialization" title="Renewable energy commercialization">commercialization of renewable energy</a>, developing less carbon-hungry technology and transport systems and attempts by individuals to lead <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a> lifestyles by monitoring the fossil fuel use embodied in all the goods and services they use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">Engineering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Emerging_technologies" title="Emerging technologies">emerging technologies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral_fuel" title="Carbon-neutral fuel">carbon-neutral fuel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves2011rev_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graves2011rev-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson2012_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2012-148">[148]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> and energy storage systems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">power to gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air_energy_storage" title="Compressed air energy storage">compressed air energy storage</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> are necessary to store power from transient <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources including emerging renewables such as <a href="/wiki/Airborne_wind_turbine" title="Airborne wind turbine">airborne wind turbines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">Water resources</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_security" title="Water security">Water security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> are inextricably linked. In the decade 1951–60 human water withdrawals were four times greater than the previous decade. This rapid increase resulted from scientific and technological developments impacting through the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>—especially the increase in irrigated land, growth in industrial and power sectors, and intensive <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dam</a> construction on all continents. This altered the water cycle of <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, affected their <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> and had a significant impact on the global water cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shik_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shik-156">[156]</a></sup> Currently towards 35% of human water use is unsustainable, drawing on diminishing aquifers and reducing the flows of major rivers: this percentage is likely to increase if <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> impacts become more severe, <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">populations</a> increase, aquifers become progressively depleted and supplies become polluted and unsanitary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> From 1961 to 2001 water demand doubled—agricultural use increased by 75%, industrial use by more than 200%, and domestic use more than 400%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup> In the 1990s it was estimated that humans were using 40–50% of the globally available freshwater in the approximate proportion of 70% for agriculture, 22% for <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, and 8% for domestic purposes with total use progressively increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shik_156-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shik-156">[156]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">Water efficiency</a> is being improved on a global scale by increased <a href="/wiki/Demand_management" title="Demand management">demand management</a>, improved infrastructure, improved water <a href="/wiki/Resource_productivity" title="Resource productivity">productivity</a> of agriculture, minimising the water intensity (embodied water) of goods and services, addressing shortages in the non-industrialized world, concentrating food production in areas of high productivity, and planning for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, such as through flexible system design. A promising direction towards sustainable development is to design systems that are flexible and reversible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tandfonline.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline.com-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_2012_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang_2012-9">[9]</a></sup> At the local level, people are becoming more self-sufficient by harvesting rainwater and reducing use of mains water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-water_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-water-129">[129]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Feijoada_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="268" data-file-width="342" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Feijoada_01.jpg/220px-Feijoada_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Feijoada_01.jpg/330px-Feijoada_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Feijoada_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Feijoada_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Feijoada — A typical black bean food dish from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">Food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">Food security</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Sustainable_food_system" title="Category:Sustainable food system">Category:Sustainable food system</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Public_Health_Association" title="American Public Health Association">American Public Health Association</a> (APHA) defines a "sustainable food system"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> as "one that provides healthy food to meet current food needs while maintaining healthy ecosystems that can also provide food for generations to come with minimal negative impact to the environment. A sustainable food system also encourages local production and distribution infrastructures and makes nutritious food available, accessible, and affordable to all. Further, it is humane and just, protecting farmers and other workers, consumers, and communities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> Concerns about the environmental impacts of agribusiness and the stark contrast between the <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> problems of the Western world and the poverty and food insecurity of the developing world have generated a strong movement towards healthy, sustainable eating as a major component of overall <a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumerism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup> The environmental effects of different dietary patterns depend on many factors, including the proportion of animal and plant foods consumed and the method of food production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> has published a <i>Global Strategy on Diet, Physical Activity and Health</i> report which was endorsed by the May 2004 <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Assembly" title="World Health Assembly">World Health Assembly</a>. It recommends the Mediterranean diet which is associated with health and <a href="/wiki/Longevity" title="Longevity">longevity</a> and is low in <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>, rich in <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, low in added sugar and limited salt, and low in <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated fatty</a> acids; the traditional source of <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> in the Mediterranean is <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>, rich in <a href="/wiki/Monounsaturated_fat" title="Monounsaturated fat">monounsaturated fat</a>. The healthy rice-based Japanese diet is also high in <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and low in fat. Both diets are low in meat and <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated fats</a> and high in <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> and other vegetables; they are associated with a low incidence of ailments and low environmental impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the global level the environmental impact of agribusiness is being addressed through <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a>. At the local level there are various movements working towards local food production, more productive use of urban wastelands and domestic gardens including <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_horticulture" title="Urban horticulture">urban horticulture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_food" title="Local food">local food</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slow_food" title="Slow food">slow food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_gardening" title="Sustainable gardening">sustainable gardening</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_gardening" title="Organic gardening">organic gardening</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_seafood" title="Sustainable seafood">Sustainable seafood</a> is seafood from either fished or farmed sources that can maintain or increase production in the future without jeopardizing the ecosystems from which it was acquired. The sustainable seafood movement has gained momentum as more people become aware about both <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> and environmentally destructive fishing methods.
</p>
<h4><span id="Materials.2C_toxic_substances.2C_waste"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials,_toxic_substances,_waste">Materials, toxic substances, waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Materials, toxic substances, waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1568" data-file-width="2352" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/280px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/420px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg/560px-Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Electric_wire_reel_reused_in_a_furniture_ecodesign.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An electric wire reel reused as a center table in a <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> <a href="/wiki/Interior_design" title="Interior design">decoration</a> <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a>. The reuse of materials is a sustainable practice that is rapidly growing among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Designers" title="Designers">designers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>As global population and affluence has increased, so has the use of various materials increased in volume, diversity and distance transported. Included here are raw materials, minerals, synthetic chemicals (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous_substances" title="Hazardous substances">hazardous substances</a>), manufactured products, food, living organisms and waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VITAL_171-0"><a href="#cite_note-VITAL-171">[171]</a></sup> By 2050, humanity could consume an estimated 140 billion tons of minerals, ores, fossil fuels and biomass per year (three times its current amount) unless the economic growth rate is decoupled from the rate of natural <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a>. Developed countries' citizens consume an average of 16 tons of those four key resources per capita, ranging up to 40 or more tons per person in some developed countries with resource consumption levels far beyond what is likely sustainable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sustainable use of materials has targeted the idea of <a href="/wiki/Dematerialization_(economics)" title="Dematerialization (economics)">dematerialization</a>, converting the linear path of materials (extraction, use, disposal in landfill) to a <a href="/wiki/Material_flow_accounting" title="Material flow accounting">circular material flow</a> that reuses materials as much as possible, much like the cycling and reuse of waste in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup> This approach is supported by <a href="/wiki/Product_stewardship" title="Product stewardship">product stewardship</a> and the increasing use of <a href="/wiki/Material_flow_analysis" title="Material flow analysis">material flow analysis</a> at all levels, especially individual countries and the global economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup> The use of sustainable biomaterials that come from renewable sources and that can be recycled is preferred to the use on non-renewables from a life cycle standpoint.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg"><img alt="Diagram showing ways of dealing with waste with the most important ones towards the top" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="378" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg/220px-Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg/330px-Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg/440px-Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waste_hierarchy_rect-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>The <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">waste hierarchy</a></center></div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_chemical" title="Synthetic chemical">Synthetic chemical</a> production has escalated following the stimulus it received during the second World War. Chemical production includes everything from herbicides, pesticides and fertilizers to domestic chemicals and hazardous substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup> Apart from the build-up of greenhouse gas emissions in the atmosphere, chemicals of particular concern include: <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" title="Nuclear waste">nuclear waste</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutants" title="Persistent organic pollutants">persistent organic pollutants</a> and all harmful chemicals capable of <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a>. Although most synthetic chemicals are harmless there needs to be rigorous testing of new chemicals, in all countries, for adverse environmental and health effects. International legislation has been established to deal with the global distribution and management of <a href="/wiki/Dangerous_goods" title="Dangerous goods">dangerous goods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup> The effects of some chemical agents needed long-term measurements and a lot of legal battles to realize their danger to human health. The classification of the toxic carcinogenic agents is handle by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IARC_Group_1_carcinogen" title="IARC Group 1 carcinogen">International Agency for Research on Cancer</a>.
</p><p>Every economic activity produces material that can be classified as waste. To reduce waste, industry, business and government are now mimicking nature by turning the waste produced by <a href="/wiki/Industrial_metabolism" title="Industrial metabolism">industrial metabolism</a> into resource. Dematerialization is being encouraged through the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">industrial ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecodesign" title="Ecodesign">ecodesign</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecolabelling" title="Ecolabelling">ecolabelling</a>. In addition to the well-established "reduce, reuse and recycle", shoppers are using their purchasing power for <a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumerism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brower_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brower-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The European Union is expected to table by the end of 2015 an ambitious Circular Economy package which is expected to include concrete legislative proposals on waste management, ecodesign and limits on land fills.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_dimension">Economic dimension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Economic dimension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">Environmental economics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">Green economy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg"><img alt="Reproduction of painting The Great Fish Market, painted by Jan Brueghel the Elder" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1851" data-file-width="2859" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg/220px-Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg/330px-Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg/440px-Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder-Great_Fish_market.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Great Fish Market</i>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Brueghel_the_Elder" title="Jan Brueghel the Elder">Jan Brueghel the Elder</a></div></div></div>
<p>On one account, sustainability "concerns the specification of a set of actions to be taken by present persons that will not diminish the prospects of future persons to enjoy levels of consumption, wealth, utility, or welfare comparable to those enjoyed by present persons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bromley_179-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bromley-179">[179]</a></sup> Sustainability interfaces with economics through the social and ecological consequences of economic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daly_&amp;_Cobb_1989_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Daly_&amp;_Cobb_1989-32">[32]</a></sup> Sustainability economics represents: "... a broad interpretation of ecological economics where environmental and ecological variables and issues are basic but part of a multidimensional perspective. Social, cultural, health-related and monetary/financial aspects have to be integrated into the analysis."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> However, the concept of sustainability is much broader than the concepts of sustained yield of welfare, resources, or profit margins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup> At present, the average per capita consumption of people in the developing world is sustainable but population numbers are increasing and individuals are aspiring to high-consumption Western lifestyles. The developed world population is only increasing slightly but consumption levels are unsustainable. The challenge for sustainability is to curb and manage Western consumption while raising the standard of living of the developing world without increasing its resource use and environmental impact. This must be done by using strategies and technology that break the link between, on the one hand, economic growth and on the other, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_damage" title="Environmental damage">environmental damage</a> and resource depletion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent UNEP report proposes a <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a> defined as one that "improves human well-being and social equity, while significantly reducing environmental risks and ecological scarcities": it "does not favor one political perspective over another but works to minimize excessive depletion of <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>". The report makes three key findings: "that greening not only generates increases in wealth, in particular a gain in ecological commons or natural capital, but also (over a period of six years) produces a higher rate of GDP growth"; that there is "an inextricable link between poverty eradication and better maintenance and conservation of the ecological commons, arising from the benefit flows from natural capital that are received directly by the poor"; "in the transition to a green economy, new jobs are created, which in time exceed the losses in "brown economy" jobs. However, there is a period of job losses in transition, which requires investment in re-skilling and re-educating the workforce".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several key areas have been targeted for economic analysis and reform: the environmental effects of unconstrained economic growth; the consequences of nature being treated as an economic <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externality</a>; and the possibility of an economics that takes greater account of the social and environmental consequences of market behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decoupling_environmental_degradation_and_economic_growth">Decoupling environmental degradation and economic growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Decoupling environmental degradation and economic growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Historically there has been a close correlation between <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>: as communities grow, so the environment declines. This trend is clearly demonstrated on graphs of human population numbers, economic growth, and environmental indicators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup> Unsustainable economic growth has been starkly compared to the malignant growth of a cancer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> because it eats away at the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> which are its life-support system. There is concern that, unless resource use is checked, modern global civilization will follow the path of ancient civilizations that collapsed through <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of their resource base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-collapse_187-0"><a href="#cite_note-collapse-187">[187]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup> While conventional economics is concerned largely with economic growth and the efficient allocation of resources, ecological economics has the explicit goal of sustainable scale (rather than continual growth), <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_distribution" title="Sustainable distribution">fair distribution</a> and efficient allocation, in that order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costanza_190-0"><a href="#cite_note-Costanza-190">[190]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Business_Council_for_Sustainable_Development" title="World Business Council for Sustainable Development">World Business Council for Sustainable Development</a> states that "business cannot succeed in societies that fail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">environmental</a> fields, the term <a href="/wiki/Eco-economic_decoupling" title="Eco-economic decoupling">decoupling</a> is becoming increasingly used in the context of economic production and environmental quality. When used in this way, it refers to the ability of an economy to grow without incurring corresponding increases in environmental pressure. Ecological economics includes the study of societal metabolism, the throughput of resources that enter and exit the economic system in relation to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_quality" title="Environmental quality">environmental quality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Costanza_190-1"><a href="#cite_note-Costanza-190">[190]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup> An economy that is able to sustain GDP growth without having a negative impact on the environment is said to be decoupled. Exactly how, if, or to what extent this can be achieved is a subject of much debate. In 2011 the <a href="/wiki/International_Resource_Panel" title="International Resource Panel">International Resource Panel</a>, hosted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP), warned that by 2050 the human race could be devouring 140 billion tons of minerals, ores, fossil fuels and biomass per year—three times its current rate of consumption—unless nations can make serious attempts at decoupling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> The report noted that citizens of developed countries consume an average of 16 tons of those four key resources per capita per annum (ranging up to 40 or more tons per person in some developed countries). By comparison, the average person in India today consumes four tons per year. Sustainability studies analyse ways to reduce <a href="/wiki/Resource_intensity" title="Resource intensity">resource intensity</a> (the amount of resource (e.g. water, energy, or materials) needed for the production, consumption and disposal of a unit of good or service) whether this be achieved from improved economic management, product design, or new technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are conflicting views whether improvements in technological efficiency and innovation will enable a complete decoupling of economic growth from environmental degradation. On the one hand, it has been claimed repeatedly by efficiency experts that resource use intensity (i.e., energy and materials use per unit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a>) could in principle be reduced by at least four or five-fold, thereby allowing for continued economic growth without increasing resource depletion and associated pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup> On the other hand, an extensive historical analysis of technological efficiency improvements has conclusively shown that improvements in the efficiency of the use of energy and materials were almost always outpaced by economic growth, in large part because of the <a href="/wiki/Rebound_effect_(conservation)" title="Rebound effect (conservation)">rebound effect (conservation)</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jevons_Paradox" title="Jevons Paradox">Jevons Paradox</a> resulting in a net increase in resource use and associated pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup> Furthermore, there are inherent thermodynamic (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>) and practical limits to all efficiency improvements. For example, there are certain minimum unavoidable material requirements for growing food, and there are limits to making automobiles, houses, furniture, and other products lighter and thinner without the risk of losing their necessary functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup> Since it is both theoretically and practically impossible to increase resource use efficiencies indefinitely, it is equally impossible to have continued and infinite economic growth without a concomitant increase in resource depletion and environmental pollution, i.e., economic growth and resource depletion can be decoupled to some degree over the short run but not the long run. Consequently, long-term sustainability requires the transition to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_state_economy" title="Steady state economy">steady state economy</a> in which total GDP remains more or less constant, as has been advocated for decades by <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> and others in the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a> community.
</p><p>A different proposed solution to partially decouple economic growth from environmental degradation is the <i>restore</i> approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-restore_200-0"><a href="#cite_note-restore-200">[200]</a></sup> This approach views "restore" as a fourth component to the common reduce, reuse, recycle motto. Participants in such efforts are encouraged to voluntarily donate towards nature conservation a small fraction of the financial savings they experience through a more frugal use of resources. These financial savings would normally lead to rebound effects, but a theoretical analysis suggests that donating even a small fraction of the experienced savings can potentially more than eliminate rebound effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-restore_200-1"><a href="#cite_note-restore-200">[200]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_as_an_economic_externality">Nature as an economic externality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Nature as an economic externality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg"><img alt="Deforastation of native rain forest in Rio de Janeiro City for extraction of clay for civil construction" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1696" data-file-width="2544" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg/300px-Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg/450px-Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg/600px-Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hillside_deforestation_in_Rio_de_Janeiro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> of native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a> in Rio de Janeiro City for extraction of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> for <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> (2009 picture)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a></div>
<p>The economic importance of nature is indicated by the use of the expression <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> to highlight the market relevance of an increasingly scarce natural world that can no longer be regarded as both unlimited and free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tragedy_201-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tragedy-201">[201]</a></sup> In general, as a <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> or service becomes more scarce the <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a> increases and this acts as a restraint that encourages frugality, technical innovation and alternative products. However, this only applies when the product or service falls within the market system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-202"><a href="#cite_note-202">[202]</a></sup> As ecosystem services are generally treated as economic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Externalities" title="Externalities">externalities</a> they are unpriced and therefore overused and degraded, a situation sometimes referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_Commons" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tragedy_201-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tragedy-201">[201]</a></sup>
</p><p>One approach to this dilemma has been the attempt to "internalize" these "externalities" by using market strategies like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecotaxes" title="Ecotaxes">ecotaxes</a> and incentives, tradeable permits for carbon, and the encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Payment_for_ecosystem_services" title="Payment for ecosystem services">payment for ecosystem services</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_currencies" title="Community currencies">Community currencies</a> associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_Exchange_Trading_Systems" title="Local Exchange Trading Systems">Local Exchange Trading Systems</a> (LETS), a <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">gift economy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Banking" title="Time Banking">Time Banking</a> have also been promoted as a way of supporting local economies and the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-204"><a href="#cite_note-204">[204]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_economics" title="Green economics">Green economics</a> is another market-based attempt to address issues of equity and the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-205"><a href="#cite_note-205">[205]</a></sup> The global recession and a range of associated government policies are likely to bring the biggest annual fall in the world's carbon dioxide emissions in 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_opportunity">Economic opportunity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Economic opportunity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treating the environment as an externality may generate short-term profit at the expense of sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[207]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_business" title="Sustainable business">Sustainable business</a> practices, on the other hand, integrate ecological concerns with social and economic ones (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">triple bottom line</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-208"><a href="#cite_note-208">[208]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-209"><a href="#cite_note-209">[209]</a></sup> Growth that depletes ecosystem services is sometimes termed "<a href="/wiki/Uneconomic_growth" title="Uneconomic growth">uneconomic growth</a>" as it leads to a decline in <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-210"><a href="#cite_note-210">[210]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-211"><a href="#cite_note-211">[211]</a></sup> Minimizing such growth can provide opportunities for local businesses. For example, industrial waste can be treated as an "economic resource in the wrong place". The benefits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_reduction" title="Waste reduction">waste reduction</a> include savings from disposal costs, fewer environmental penalties, and reduced liability insurance. This may lead to increased market share due to an improved public image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-212"><a href="#cite_note-212">[212]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-213"><a href="#cite_note-213">[213]</a></sup> Energy efficiency can also increase profits by reducing costs.
</p><p>The idea of sustainability as a business opportunity has led to the formation of organizations such as the Sustainability Consortium of the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Organizational_Learning" title="Society for Organizational Learning">Society for Organizational Learning</a>, the Sustainable Business Institute, and the World Council for Sustainable Development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-214"><a href="#cite_note-214">[214]</a></sup> The expansion of sustainable business opportunities can contribute to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_creation" title="Job creation">job creation</a> through the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green-collar" title="Green-collar">green-collar</a> workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-215"><a href="#cite_note-215">[215]</a></sup>  Research focusing on progressive corporate leaders who have integrated sustainability into commercial strategy has yielded a leadership competency model for sustainability,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-216"><a href="#cite_note-216">[216]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-217"><a href="#cite_note-217">[217]</a></sup> and led to emergence of the concept of "embedded sustainability"—defined by its authors Chris Laszlo and <a href="/wiki/Nadya_Zhexembayeva" title="Nadya Zhexembayeva">Nadya Zhexembayeva</a> as "incorporation of environmental, health, and social value into the core business with no trade-off in price or quality—in other words, with no social or green premium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-218"><a href="#cite_note-218">[218]</a></sup> Laszlo and Zhexembayeva's research showed that embedded sustainability offers at least seven distinct opportunities for business value creation: a) better risk-management, b) increased efficiency through reduced waste and resource use, c) better product differentiation, d) new market entrances, e) enhanced brand and reputation, f) greater opportunity to influence industry standards, and g) greater opportunity for radical innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-219"><a href="#cite_note-219">[219]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nadya_Zhexembayeva" title="Nadya Zhexembayeva">Nadya Zhexembayeva</a>'s 2014 research further suggested that innovation driven by resource depletion can result in fundamental advantages for company products and services, as well as the company strategy as a whole, when right principles of innovation are applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-220"><a href="#cite_note-220">[220]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosocialist_approach">Ecosocialist approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Ecosocialist approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Eco-socialism" title="Eco-socialism">Eco-socialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a></div>
<p>One school of thought, often labeled <a href="/wiki/Eco-socialism" title="Eco-socialism">ecosocialism</a> or ecological Marxism, asserts that the <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist economic system</a> is fundamentally incompatible with the ecological and social requirements of sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-221"><a href="#cite_note-221">[221]</a></sup> This theory rests on the premises that:
</p>
<ol><li>Capitalism's sole economic purpose is "unlimited <a href="/wiki/Capital_accumulation" title="Capital accumulation">capital accumulation</a>" in the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">capitalist class</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-222"><a href="#cite_note-222">[222]</a></sup></li>
<li>The urge to accumulate (the <a href="/wiki/Profit_motive" title="Profit motive">profit motive</a>) drives capitalists to continually reinvest and expand production, creating indefinite and unsustainable economic growth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-223"><a href="#cite_note-223">[223]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-224"><a href="#cite_note-224">[224]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Capital tends to degrade the conditions of its own production" (the ecosystems and resources on which any economy depends)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kovel_2007,_p._38_225-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kovel_2007,_p._38-225">[225]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Thus, according to this analysis:
</p>
<ol><li>Giving economic priority to the fulfillment of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human needs</a> while staying within ecological limits, as <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> demands, is in conflict with the structural workings of capitalism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-226"><a href="#cite_note-226">[226]</a></sup></li>
<li>A steady-state capitalist economy is impossible;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-227"><a href="#cite_note-227">[227]</a></sup> further, a steady-state capitalist economy is socially undesirable due to the inevitable outcome of massive <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment and underemployment</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-228"><a href="#cite_note-228">[228]</a></sup></li>
<li>Capitalism will, unless overcome by <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolution</a>, run up against the physical limits of the biosphere and <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">self-destruct</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-229"><a href="#cite_note-229">[229]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>By this logic, market-based solutions to ecological crises (<a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_economics" title="Environmental economics">environmental economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a>) are rejected as technical tweaks that do not confront capitalism's structural failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-230"><a href="#cite_note-230">[230]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-231"><a href="#cite_note-231">[231]</a></sup> "Low-risk" technology/science-based solutions such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>, and increases in <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a> are seen as necessary but insufficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-232"><a href="#cite_note-232">[232]</a></sup> "High-risk" technological solutions such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">climate engineering</a> are entirely rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-233"><a href="#cite_note-233">[233]</a></sup> Attempts made by businesses to "<a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwash</a>" their practices are regarded as false advertising, and it is pointed out that implementation of renewable technology (such as <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>'s proposition to supply their electricity with solar power) has <a href="/wiki/Jevons_paradox" title="Jevons paradox">the effect opposite of reductions in resource consumption</a>, viz. further economic growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-234"><a href="#cite_note-234">[234]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_business" title="Sustainable business">Sustainable business</a> models and the <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">triple bottom line</a> are viewed as morally praiseworthy but ignorant to the tendency in capitalism for the <a href="/wiki/Distribution_of_wealth" title="Distribution of wealth">distribution of wealth</a> to become increasingly unequal and socially unstable/unsustainable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kovel_2007,_p._38_225-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kovel_2007,_p._38-225">[225]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-235"><a href="#cite_note-235">[235]</a></sup> Ecosocialists claim that the general unwillingness of capitalists to tolerate—and capitalist governments to implement—constraints on maximum profit (such as ecotaxes or preservation and conservation measures) renders <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental reforms</a> incapable of facilitating large-scale change: "History teaches us that although capitalism has at times responded to environmental movements ... at a certain point, at which the system's underlying accumulation drive is affected, its resistance to environmental demands stiffens."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-236"><a href="#cite_note-236">[236]</a></sup> They also note that, up until the event of total <a href="/wiki/Ecological_collapse" title="Ecological collapse">ecological collapse</a>, destruction caused by natural disasters generally causes an increase in economic growth and accumulation; thus, capitalists have no foreseeable motivation to reduce the probability of disasters (i.e. convert to sustainable/ecological production).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-237"><a href="#cite_note-237">[237]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecosocialists advocate for the <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutionary</a> succession of capitalism by ecosocialism—an <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarian</a> economic/political/social structure designed to harmonize human society with non-human ecology and to fulfill <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human needs</a>—as the only sufficient solution to the present-day ecological crisis, and hence the only path towards sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-238"><a href="#cite_note-238">[238]</a></sup> Sustainability is viewed not as a domain exclusive to scientists, environmental activists, and business leaders but as a holistic project that must involve the whole of humanity redefining its place in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a>: "What every environmentalist needs to know ... is that capitalism is not the solution but the problem, and that if humanity is going to survive this crisis, it will do so because it has exercised its capacity for human freedom, through social struggle, in order to create a whole new world—in coevolution with the planet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-239"><a href="#cite_note-239">[239]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_dimension">Social dimension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Social dimension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Social_sustainability" title="Social sustainability">Social sustainability</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy" title="Environmental policy">Environmental policy</a></div>
<p>Sustainability issues are generally expressed in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> and environmental terms, as well as in ethical terms of <a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a>, but implementing change is a social challenge that entails, among other things, <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international</a> and national <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> and transport, local and individual <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethical_consumerism" title="Ethical consumerism">ethical consumerism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-240"><a href="#cite_note-240">[240]</a></sup> "The relationship between human rights and human development, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_capitalism" title="Corporate capitalism">corporate power</a> and environmental justice, global poverty and citizen action, suggest that responsible global citizenship is an inescapable element of what may at first glance seem to be simply matters of personal consumer and moral choice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blewitt2008,96_241-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blewitt2008,96-241">[241]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Peace.2C_security.2C_social_justice"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Peace,_security,_social_justice">Peace, security, social justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Peace, security, social justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">Peace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">Social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">Environmental justice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_ethics" title="Environmental ethics">Environmental ethics</a></div>
<p>Social disruptions like <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">corruption</a> divert resources from areas of greatest human need, damage the capacity of societies to plan for the future, and generally threaten human well-being and the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blewitt2008,96_241-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blewitt2008,96-241">[241]</a></sup> Broad-based strategies for more sustainable social systems include: improved education and the political empowerment of women, especially in developing countries; greater regard for social justice, notably equity between rich and poor both within and between countries; and intergenerational equity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2006_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2006-85">[85]</a></sup> Depletion of natural resources including fresh water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-242"><a href="#cite_note-242">[242]</a></sup> increases the likelihood of "resource wars".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-243"><a href="#cite_note-243">[243]</a></sup> This aspect of sustainability has been referred to as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_security" title="Environmental security">environmental security</a> and creates a clear need for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_agreements" title="List of environmental agreements">global environmental agreements</a> to manage resources such as aquifers and rivers which span political boundaries, and to protect shared global systems including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-244"><a href="#cite_note-244">[244]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty">Poverty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Poverty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">Poverty</a></div>
<p>A major hurdle to achieve sustainability is the alleviation of poverty. It has been widely acknowledged that poverty is one source of environmental degradation. Such acknowledgment has been made by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Commission report Our Common Future</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-245"><a href="#cite_note-245">[245]</a></sup> and the Millennium Development Goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-246"><a href="#cite_note-246">[246]</a></sup> There is a growing realization in national governments and multilateral institutions that it is impossible to separate economic development issues from environment issues: according to the Brundtland report, "poverty is a major cause and effect of global environmental problems. It is therefore futile to attempt to deal with environmental problems without a broader perspective that encompasses the factors underlying world poverty and international inequality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-247"><a href="#cite_note-247">[247]</a></sup> Individuals living in poverty tend to rely heavily on their local ecosystem as a source for basic needs (such as nutrition and medicine) and general well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-248"><a href="#cite_note-248">[248]</a></sup> As population growth continues to increase, increasing pressure is being placed on the local ecosystem to provide these basic essentials. According to the UN Population Fund, high fertility and poverty have been strongly correlated, and the world's poorest countries also have the highest fertility and population growth rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-249"><a href="#cite_note-249">[249]</a></sup> The word sustainability is also used widely by western country development agencies and international charities to focus their poverty alleviation efforts in ways that can be sustained by the local populace and its environment. For example, teaching <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a> to the poor by boiling their water with <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, would not generally be considered a sustainable strategy, whereas using <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">PET</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_disinfection" title="Solar water disinfection">solar water disinfection</a> would be. Also, sustainable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_practices" title="Best practices">best practices</a> can involve the <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> of materials, such as the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics_recycling" title="Plastics recycling">recycled</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics" title="Plastics">plastics</a> for lumber where deforestation has devastated a country's timber base. Another example of sustainable practices in poverty alleviation is the use of exported recycled materials from developed to developing countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Bridges_to_Prosperity" title="Bridges to Prosperity">Bridges to Prosperity</a>'s use of wire rope from shipping container <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gantry_cranes" title="Gantry cranes">gantry cranes</a> to act as the structural wire rope for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Footbridges" title="Footbridges">footbridges</a> that cross rivers in poor rural areas in Asia and Africa.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_relationship_to_nature">Human relationship to nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Human relationship to nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a>, the idea that humans must dominate nature is common in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_hierarchy" title="Social hierarchy">hierarchical</a> societies. Bookchin contends that <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> relationships, if unchecked, have the capacity to reduce the planet to a mere resource to be exploited. Nature is thus treated as a <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a>: "The plundering of the human spirit by the market place is paralleled by the plundering of the earth by capital."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-250"><a href="#cite_note-250">[250]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_ecology_(theory)" title="Social ecology (theory)">Social ecology</a>, founded by Bookchin, is based on the conviction that nearly all of humanity's present ecological problems originate in, indeed are mere symptoms of, dysfunctional social arrangements. Whereas most authors proceed as if our ecological problems implementing recommendations which stem from physical, biological, economic etc., studies, Bookchin's claim is that these problems can only be resolved by understanding the underlying social processes and intervening in those processes by applying the concepts and methods of the social sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-251"><a href="#cite_note-251">[251]</a></sup>
</p><p>A pure capitalist approach has also been criticized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stern_Review_on_the_Economics_of_Climate_Change" title="Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change">Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> in this excerpt:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>the greatest example of <a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">market failure</a> we have ever seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-252"><a href="#cite_note-252">[252]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-253"><a href="#cite_note-253">[253]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In regards of the United States of America, The Government and the Economy has had a long lasting impact on the environment, but in a problematic way. Policy issues regarding the environment has shown that the country regards the protection of the environment as a "second hand issue." One causation from this is a certain dilemma called "collective action problem" or collective action dilemmas." These occur when individuals, firms, or governments would be better off if they cooperated in the pursuit of a common goal, but, for one reason or another, one or more of those involved choose a less optimal course of action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_254-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-254">[254]</a></sup> Matthew Potoski and Aseem Prakash have made a model establishing 4 cells that are explaining each benefit for the government or the economic process. For the government, one cost might be the loss of public confidence and trust, while a firm might lose market share and profitability <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_254-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-254">[254]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology</a> is a movement founded by <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Naess</a> that establishes principles for the well-being of all life on Earth and the richness and diversity of life forms. The movement advocates, among other things, a substantial decrease in human population and consumption along with the reduction of human interference with the nonhuman world. To achieve this, deep ecologists advocate policies for basic economic, technological, and ideological structures that will improve the <i><a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a></i> rather than the <i><a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a></i>. Those who subscribe to these principles are obliged to make the necessary change happen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-255"><a href="#cite_note-255">[255]</a></sup> The concept of a billion-year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainocene" title="Sustainocene">Sustainocene</a> has been developed to initiate policy consideration of an earth where human structures power and fuel the needs of that species (for example through <a href="/wiki/Artificial_photosynthesis" title="Artificial photosynthesis">artificial photosynthesis</a>) allowing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rights_of_Nature" title="Rights of Nature">Rights of Nature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-256"><a href="#cite_note-256">[256]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_settlements">Human settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Human settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style=";"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><center><b><a href="/wiki/The_Natural_Step#System_conditions_of_sustainability" title="The Natural Step">Sustainability principles</a></b><br/></center>
<p>1. Reduce dependence upon fossil fuels,<br/>
underground metals, and minerals<br/>
2. Reduce dependence upon synthetic chemicals<br/>
and other unnatural substances<br/>
3. Reduce encroachment upon nature<br/>
</p>
4. Meet human needs fairly &amp; efficiently<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_257-0"><a href="#cite_note-James-257">[257]</a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>One approach to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">sustainable living</a>, exemplified by small-scale urban <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_Towns" title="Transition Towns">transition towns</a> and rural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecovillages" title="Ecovillages">ecovillages</a>, seeks to create self-reliant communities based on principles of <a href="/wiki/Simple_living" title="Simple living">simple living</a>, which maximize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" title="Self-sufficiency">self-sufficiency</a> particularly in food production. These principles, on a broader scale, underpin the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">bioregional</a> economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-258"><a href="#cite_note-258">[258]</a></sup> These approaches often utilize commons based knowledge sharing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source_appropriate_technology" title="Open source appropriate technology">open source appropriate technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-259"><a href="#cite_note-259">[259]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other approaches, loosely based around <a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>, are successfully reducing environmental impacts by altering the built environment to create and preserve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable_cities" title="Sustainable cities">sustainable cities</a> which support <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">sustainable transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zero_carbon_housing" title="Zero carbon housing">zero emission housing</a>. Residents in compact urban neighborhoods drive fewer miles, and have significantly lower environmental impacts across a range of measures, compared with those living in <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">sprawling</a> suburbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-260"><a href="#cite_note-260">[260]</a></sup> Compact urban neighbourhoods would also promote a great people climate, where by increasing the accessibility to bike, walk or take public transport within neighbourhoods would increase the amount of interaction between people. With more diversification between people, this increases people's happiness and leads to a better standard of living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-261"><a href="#cite_note-261">[261]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">sustainable architecture</a> the recent movement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Classical_Architecture" title="New Classical Architecture">New Classical Architecture</a> promotes a sustainable approach towards construction, that appreciates and develops <a href="/wiki/Smart_growth" title="Smart growth">smart growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_architecture" title="Vernacular architecture">architectural tradition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_architecture" title="Classical architecture">classical design</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-262"><a href="#cite_note-262">[262]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-263"><a href="#cite_note-263">[263]</a></sup> This in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">modernist</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">globally uniform</a> architecture, as well as opposing solitary <a href="/wiki/Housing_estate" title="Housing estate">housing estates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">suburban sprawl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-264"><a href="#cite_note-264">[264]</a></sup> Both trends started in the 1980s. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Circular_flow_land_use_management" title="Circular flow land use management">Circular flow land use management</a> has also been introduced in Europe to promote sustainable land use patterns that strive for compact cities and a reduction of greenfield land take by urban sprawl.
</p><p>Large scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_movements" title="Social movements">social movements</a> can influence both community choices and the built environment. <a href="/wiki/Eco-municipality" title="Eco-municipality">Eco-municipalities</a> may be one such movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-265"><a href="#cite_note-265">[265]</a></sup> Eco-municipalities take a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systems" title="Systems">systems</a> approach, based on sustainability principles. The eco-municipality movement is participatory, involving community members in a bottom-up approach. In Sweden, more than 70 cities and towns—25 per cent of all municipalities in the country—have adopted a common set of <a href="/wiki/The_Natural_Step#System_conditions_of_sustainability" title="The Natural Step">"Sustainability Principles"</a> and implemented these systematically throughout their municipal operations. There are now twelve eco-municipalities in the United States and the <a href="/wiki/American_Planning_Association" title="American Planning Association">American Planning Association</a> has adopted sustainability objectives based on the same principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_257-1"><a href="#cite_note-James-257">[257]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a wealth of advice available to individuals wishing to reduce their personal and social impact on the environment through small, inexpensive and easily achievable steps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-266"><a href="#cite_note-266">[266]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-267"><a href="#cite_note-267">[267]</a></sup> But the transition required to reduce global human consumption to within sustainable limits involves much larger changes, at all levels and contexts of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-268"><a href="#cite_note-268">[268]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> has recognised the central role of education, and have declared a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Decade_of_Education_for_Sustainable_Development" title="United Nations Decade of Education for Sustainable Development">decade of education for sustainable development</a>, 2005–2014, which aims to "challenge us all to adopt new behaviours and practices to secure our future".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-269"><a href="#cite_note-269">[269]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldwide_Fund_for_Nature" title="Worldwide Fund for Nature">Worldwide Fund for Nature</a> proposes a strategy for sustainability that goes beyond education to tackle underlying individualistic and materialistic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)#Cultural_values" title="Value (personal and cultural)">societal values</a> head-on and strengthen people's connections with the natural world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-270"><a href="#cite_note-270">[270]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_and_labor_rights">Human and labor rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Human and labor rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Application of <a href="/wiki/Social_sustainability" title="Social sustainability">social sustainability</a> requires stakeholders to look at human and labor rights, prevention of human trafficking, and other human rights risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-271"><a href="#cite_note-271">[271]</a></sup> These issues should be considered in production and procurement of various worldwide commodities. The international community has identified many industries whose practices have been known to violate social sustainability, and many of these industries have organizations in place that aid in verifying the social sustainability of products and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-272"><a href="#cite_note-272">[272]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Equator_Principles" title="Equator Principles">Equator Principles</a> (financial industry), <a href="/wiki/Fair_Wear_Foundation" title="Fair Wear Foundation">Fair Wear Foundation</a> (garments), and Electronics Industry Citizenship Coalition are examples of such organizations and initiatives. Resources are also available for verifying the life-cycle of products and the producer or vendor level, such as <a href="/wiki/Green_Seal" title="Green Seal">Green Seal</a> for cleaning products, <a href="/wiki/NSF_International" title="NSF International">NSF</a>-140 for carpet production, and even labeling of <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">Organic food</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-273"><a href="#cite_note-273">[273]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_dimension">Cultural dimension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Cultural dimension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cultural_sustainability" title="Cultural sustainability">Cultural sustainability</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21_for_culture" title="Agenda 21 for culture">Agenda 21 for culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Cities_and_Local_Governments" title="United Cities and Local Governments">United Cities and Local Governments</a></div>
<p>The cultural dimension of sustainability is known as <b><a href="/wiki/Cultural_sustainability" title="Cultural sustainability">cultural sustainability</a></b>. Important in the advancement of this notion have been the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unesco" title="Unesco">Unesco</a>, and in particular their <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21_for_culture" title="Agenda 21 for culture">Agenda 21 for culture</a> (now also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culture_21" title="Culture 21">Culture 21</a>), a program for <a href="/wiki/Cultural_governance" title="Cultural governance">cultural governance</a> developed in 2002–2004 and coordinated by <a href="/wiki/United_Cities_and_Local_Governments" title="United Cities and Local Governments">United Cities and Local Governments UCLG</a>, created in 2004.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sustainability is central to underpinning feelings of authenticity in tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-274"><a href="#cite_note-274">[274]</a></sup> Experiences can be enhanced when substituting the contrived for the genuine, and at the same time inspire a potentially deleterious appetite for follow-up visits to the real thing: objectively authentic sites untouched by repair or rejuvenation. Feelings of authenticity at a tourist site are thus implicitly linked to sustainable tourism; as the maximisation of existential "felt" authenticity at sites of limited historical provenance increases the likelihood of return visits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-275"><a href="#cite_note-275">[275]</a></sup>
</p>
