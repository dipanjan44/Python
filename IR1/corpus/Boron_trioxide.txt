<p><b>Boron trioxide</b> (or <b>diboron trioxide</b>) is one of the <a href="/wiki/Boron_oxide" title="Boron oxide">oxides of boron</a>. It is a white, glassy solid with the formula B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. It is almost always found as the vitreous (amorphous) form; however, it can be crystallized after extensive <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a> (that is, under prolonged heat).
</p><p>Glassy boron oxide (g-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is thought to be composed of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boroxol_rings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boroxol rings (page does not exist)">boroxol rings</a> which are six-membered rings composed of alternating 3-coordinate boron and 2-coordinate oxygen. Because of the difficulty of building disordered models at the correct density with a large number of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boroxol_rings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boroxol rings (page does not exist)">boroxol rings</a>, this view was initially controversial, but such models have recently been constructed and exhibit properties in excellent agreement with experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is now recognized, from experimental and theoretical studies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> that the fraction of boron atoms belonging to boroxol rings in glassy B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is somewhere between 0.73 and 0.83, with 0.75 (​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span>) corresponding to a 1:1 ratio between ring and non-ring units. 
</p><p>The crystalline form (α-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) (see structure in the infobox<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-a1-1">[1]</a></sup>) is exclusively composed of BO<sub>3</sub> triangles. This trigonal, <a href="/wiki/Trapezohedron" title="Trapezohedron">quartz-like</a> network undergoes a <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>-like transformation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> β-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at several gigapascals (9.5 GPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boron_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boron trioxide is produced by treating <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">borax</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> in a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fusion_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fusion furnace (page does not exist)">fusion furnace</a>. At temperatures above 750 °C, the molten boron oxide layer separates out from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a>. It is then decanted, cooled and obtained in 96–97% purity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another method is heating <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> above ~300 °C. Boric acid will initially decompose into steam, (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>(g)</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Metaboric_acid" title="Metaboric acid">metaboric acid</a> (HBO<sub>2</sub>) at around 170 °C, and further heating above 300 °C will produce more steam and boron trioxide. The reactions are:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>3</sub>BO<sub>3</sub> → HBO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 HBO<sub>2</sub> → B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Boric acid goes to anhydrous microcrystalline B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> in a heated fluidized bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Carefully controlled heating rate avoids gumming as water evolves.  Molten boron oxide attacks silicates.  Internally graphitized tubes via acetylene thermal decomposition are passivated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crystallization of molten α-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at ambient pressure is strongly kinetically disfavored (compare liquid and crystal densities).  Threshold conditions for crystallization of the amorphous solid are 10 kbar and ~200 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Its proposed crystal structure in enantiomorphic space groups P3<sub>1</sub>(#144); P3<sub>2</sub>(#145)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (e.g., γ-glycine) has been revised to enantiomorphic space groups P3<sub>1</sub>21(#152); P3<sub>2</sub>21(#154)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>(e.g., α-quartz).
</p><p>Boron oxide will also form when <a href="/wiki/Diborane" title="Diborane">diborane</a> (B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>) reacts with oxygen in the air or trace amounts of moisture:
</p>
<dl><dd>2B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>(g) + 3O<sub>2</sub>(g) → 2B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>(s) + 6H<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>(g) + 3H<sub>2</sub>O(g) → B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>(s) + 6H<sub>2</sub>(g)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boron_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ceramic_flux" title="Ceramic flux">Fluxing agent</a> for <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a></li>

<li>It is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a></li>
