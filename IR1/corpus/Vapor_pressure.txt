<p><b>Vapor pressure</b> or <b>equilibrium vapor pressure</b> is defined as the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> exerted by a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> with its <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensed</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a> (solid or liquid) at a given temperature in a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system#Closed_system" title="Thermodynamic system">closed system</a>. The equilibrium vapor pressure is an indication of a liquid's <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> rate. It relates to the tendency of particles to escape from the liquid (or a solid). A substance with a high vapor pressure at normal temperatures is often referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a></i>. The pressure exhibited by vapor present above a liquid surface is known as vapor pressure. As the temperature of a liquid increases, the kinetic energy of its molecules also increases. As the kinetic energy of the molecules increases, the number of molecules transitioning into a vapor also increases, thereby increasing the vapor pressure.
</p><p>The vapor pressure of any substance increases non-linearly with temperature according to the <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron relation">Clausius–Clapeyron relation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> of a liquid (also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_boiling_point" title="Normal boiling point">normal boiling point</a>) is the temperature at which the vapor pressure equals the ambient atmospheric pressure.  With any incremental increase in that temperature, the vapor pressure becomes sufficient to overcome <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> and lift the liquid to form vapor bubbles inside the bulk of the substance. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_bubble" title="Liquid bubble">Bubble</a> formation deeper in the liquid requires a higher temperature due to the higher fluid pressure, because fluid pressure increases above the atmospheric pressure as the depth increases. More important at shallow depths is the higher temperature required to start bubble formation. The surface tension of the bubble wall leads to an overpressure in the very small, initial bubbles. Thus, thermometer calibration should not rely on the temperature in boiling water.
</p><p>The vapor pressure that a single component in a mixture contributes to the total pressure in the system is called <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a>. For example, air at sea level, and saturated with water vapor at 20 °C, has partial pressures of about 2.3 kPa of water, 78 kPa of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 21 kPa of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and 0.9 kPa of <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, totaling 102.2 kPa, making the basis for <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmospheric pressure</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_and_units">Measurement and units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurement and units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vapor pressure is measured in the standard units of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. The <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI) recognizes pressure as a <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">derived unit</a> with the dimension of force per area and designates the <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a> (Pa) as its standard unit. One pascal is one <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_meter" title="Square meter">square meter</a> (N·m<sup>−2</sup> or kg·m<sup>−1</sup>·s<sup>−2</sup>).
</p><p>Experimental measurement of vapor pressure is a simple procedure for common pressures between 1 and 200 kPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most accurate results are obtained near the boiling point of substances and large errors result for measurements smaller than <span style="white-space:nowrap">1<span style="margin-left:0.25em">kPa</span></span>. Procedures often consist of purifying the test substance, isolating it in a container, evacuating any foreign gas, then measuring the equilibrium pressure of the gaseous phase of the substance in the container at different temperatures. Better accuracy is achieved when care is taken to ensure that the entire substance and its vapor are at the prescribed temperature.  This is often done, as with the use of an <a href="/wiki/Isoteniscope" title="Isoteniscope">isoteniscope</a>, by submerging the containment area in a liquid bath.
</p><p>Very low vapor pressures of solids can be measured using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knudsen_effusion_cell" title="Knudsen effusion cell">Knudsen effusion cell</a> method.
</p><p>In a medical context, vapor pressure is sometimes expressed in other units, specifically <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">millimeters of mercury (mmHg)</a>. This is important for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_anesthetics" title="Volatile anesthetics">volatile anesthetics</a>, most of which are liquids at body temperature, but with a relatively high vapor pressure. Anesthetics with a higher vapor pressure at body temperature will be excreted more quickly, as they are exhaled from the lungs.
</p>

<p>Sublimations and vaporizations of the same substance have separate sets of Antoine coefficients, as do components in mixtures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frostburg_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-frostburg-2">[2]</a></sup> Each parameter set for a specific compound is only applicable over a specified temperature range. Generally, temperature ranges are chosen to maintain the equation's accuracy of a few up to 8–10 percent.  For many volatile substances, several different sets of parameters are available and used for different temperature ranges. The Antoine equation has poor accuracy with any single parameter set when used from a compound's melting point to its critical temperature.  Accuracy is also usually poor when vapor pressure is under 10 Torr because of the limitations of the apparatus used to establish the Antoine parameter values.
</p><p>The Wagner Equation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> gives "one of the best"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> fits to experimental data but is quite complex.  It expresses reduced vapor pressure as a function of reduced temperature.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_boiling_point_of_liquids">Relation to boiling point of liquids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to boiling point of liquids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">Boiling point</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_pressure_chart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="512" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/220px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/330px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/440px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_pressure_chart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A log-lin vapor pressure chart for various liquids</div></div></div>
<p>As a general trend, vapor pressures of liquids at ambient temperatures increase with decreasing boiling points. This is illustrated in the vapor pressure chart (see right) that shows graphs of the <b>vapor pressures versus temperatures</b> for a variety of liquids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At the normal boiling point of a liquid, the vapor pressure is equal to the standard atmospheric pressure defined as 1 atmosphere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (760 Torr or 101.325 kPa or 14.69595 PSI).
</p><p>For example, at any given temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a> has the highest vapor pressure of any of the liquids in the chart. It also has the lowest normal boiling point (−24.2 °C), which is where the vapor pressure curve of methyl chloride (the blue line) intersects the horizontal pressure line of one atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>) of absolute vapor pressure.
</p><p>Although the relation between vapor pressure and temperature is non-linear, the chart uses a logarithmic vertical axis to produce slightly curved lines, so one chart can graph many liquids. A nearly straight line is obtained when the logarithm of the vapor pressure is plotted against 1/(T+230)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> where T is the temperature in degrees Celsius. The vapor pressure of a liquid at its boiling point equals the pressure of its surrounding environment.
</p>

</p><p>Systems that have vapor pressures higher than indicated by the above formula are said to have positive deviations.  Such a deviation suggests weaker intermolecular attraction than in the pure components, so that the molecules can be thought of as being "held in" the liquid phase less strongly than in the pure liquid.  An example is the <a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotrope</a> of approximately 95% ethanol and water.  Because the azeotrope's vapor pressure is higher than predicted by Raoult's law, it boils at a temperature below that of either pure component.
</p><p>There are also systems with negative deviations that have vapor pressures that are lower than expected.  Such a deviation is evidence for stronger intermolecular attraction between the constituents of the mixture than exists in the pure components.  Thus, the molecules are "held in" the liquid more strongly when a second molecule is present.  An example is a mixture of trichloromethane (chloroform) and 2-propanone (acetone), which boils above the boiling point of either pure component.
</p><p>The negative and positive deviations can be used to determine <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_activity" title="Thermodynamic activity">thermodynamic activity</a> coefficients of the components of mixtures.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solids">Solids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="472" data-file-width="534" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png/300px-Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png/450px-Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_Pressure_Curve_of_Liquid_and_Solid_Benzene.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vapor pressure of liquid and solid benzene</div></div></div>
<p>Equilibrium vapor pressure can be defined as the pressure reached when a condensed phase is in equilibrium with its own vapor. In the case of an equilibrium solid, such as a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>, this can be defined as the pressure when the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(physics)" title="Sublimation (physics)">sublimation</a> of a solid matches the rate of deposition of its vapor phase. For most solids this pressure is very low, but some notable exceptions are <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> (the vapor pressure of dry ice is 5.73 MPa (831 psi, 56.5 atm) at 20 °C, which causes most sealed containers to rupture), and ice.  All solid materials have a vapor pressure. However, due to their often extremely low values, measurement can be rather difficult. Typical techniques include the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermogravimetry" title="Thermogravimetry">thermogravimetry</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_transpiration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gas transpiration (page does not exist)">gas transpiration</a>.
</p><p>There are a number of methods for calculating the sublimation pressure (i.e., the vapor pressure) of a solid. <p>This method assumes that the heat of fusion is temperature-independent, ignores additional transition temperatures between different solid phases, and it gives a fair estimation for temperatures not too far from the melting point. It also shows that the sublimation pressure is lower than the extrapolated liquid vapor pressure (Δ<sub>fus</sub><i>H</i> &gt; 0) and the difference grows with increased distance from the melting point.
</p>

<h2><span id="D.C3.BChring.27s_rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dühring's_rule">Dühring's rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dühring's rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BChring%27s_rule" title="Dühring's rule">Dühring's rule</a></div>
<p>Dühring's rule states that a linear relationship exists between the temperatures at which two solutions exert the same vapor pressure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_vapor_pressure_from_molecular_structure">Estimating vapor pressure from molecular structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Estimating vapor pressure from molecular structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several empirical methods exist to estimate liquid vapor pressure from molecular structure for organic molecules. Some examples are SIMPOL,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the method of Moller et al.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moller_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moller-9">[9]</a></sup> and EVAPORATION.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_in_meteorology">Meaning in meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Meaning in meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, the term <i>vapor pressure</i> is used to mean the partial pressure of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in the atmosphere, even if it is not in equilibrium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the <i>equilibrium vapor pressure</i> is specified otherwise. Meteorologists also use the term <i>saturation vapor pressure</i> to refer to the equilibrium vapor pressure of water or <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> above a flat surface, to distinguish it from equilibrium vapor pressure, which takes into account the shape and size of water droplets and particulates in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
