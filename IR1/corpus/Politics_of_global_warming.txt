<p>The complex <b>politics of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></b> results from numerous cofactors arising from the global economy's interdependence on carbon dioxide (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>) emitting <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> energy sources and because CO<sub>2</sub> is directly implicated in global warming<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20151129_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151129-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151128_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151128-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20150525_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20150525-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151128-jg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151128-jg-4">[4]</a></sup>—making global warming a non-traditional environmental challenge.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<ol><li><b>Implications to all aspects of a nation-state's economy</b>: The vast majority of the world economy relies on energy sources or manufacturing techniques that release <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> at almost every stage of production, transportation, storage, delivery &amp; disposal while a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">consensus of the world's scientists</a> attribute <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to the release of CO<sub>2</sub> and other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.  This intimate linkage between global warming and economic vitality implicates almost every aspect of a nation-state's economy;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Dartmouth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Dartmouth-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Perceived lack of adequate advanced energy technologies</b>: Fossil fuel abundance and low prices continue to put pressure on the development of adequate advanced energy technologies that can realistically <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_phase-out" title="Fossil fuel phase-out">replace the role of fossil fuels</a>—as of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_global_warming&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, over 91% of the world's energy is derived from fossil fuels and non-carbon-neutral technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mary_S._Booth_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mary_S._Booth-6">[6]</a></sup> Developing countries do not have cost effective access to <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_developing_countries" title="Renewable energy in developing countries">the advanced energy technologies that they need for development</a> (most advanced technologies has been developed by and exist in the developed world).  Without adequate and cost effective post-hydrocarbon energy sources, it is unlikely the countries of the developed or developing world would accept policies that would materially affect their economic vitality or economic development prospects;</li>
<li><b>Industrialization of the developing world</b>: As developing nations industrialize their energy needs increase and since conventional energy sources produce CO<sub>2</sub>, the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions of developing countries are beginning to rise at a time when the scientific community, global governance institutions and advocacy groups are telling the world that CO<sub>2</sub> emissions should be decreasing.  Without access to cost effective and abundant energy sources many developing countries see climate change as a hindrance to their unfettered economic development;</li>
<li><b>Metric selection (transparency) and perceived responsibility / ability to respond</b>: Among the countries of the world, disagreements exist over which greenhouse gas emission metrics should be used like total emissions per year, per capita emissions per year, CO2 emissions only, deforestation emissions, livestock emissions or even total historical emissions. Historically, the release of CO<sub>2</sub> has not been historically even among all nation-states and nation-states have challenges with determining who should restrict emissions and at what point of their industrial development they should be subject to such commitments;</li>
<li><b>Vulnerable developing countries and developed country legacy emissions</b>: Some developing nations blame the developed world for having created the global warming crisis because it was the developed countries that emitted most of the CO<sub>2</sub> over the twentieth century and vulnerable countries perceive that it should be the developed countries that should pay to address the challenge;</li>
<li><b>Consensus-driven global governance models</b>: The global governance institutions that evolved during the 20th century are all consensus driven deliberative forums where agreement is difficult to achieve and even when agreement is achieved it is almost impossible to enforce;</li>
<li><b>Well organized and funded special-interest lobbying bodies</b>: Special interest lobbying by well organized groups distort and amplify aspects of the challenge (environmental lobbying, <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels_lobby" title="Fossil fuels lobby">energy industry lobbying</a>, other special interest lobbying);</li>
<li><b>Politicization of climate science</b>: Although there is a consensus on the science of global warming and its likely effects—some special interests groups <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">work to suppress the consensus</a> while others work to amplify the alarm of global warming.  All parties that engage in such acts add to the politicization of the science of global warming.  The result is a clouding of the reality of the global warming problem.</li></ol>
<p>The focus areas for global warming politics are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">Adaptation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">Mitigation</a>, Finance, Technology and Losses which are well quantified and studied but the urgency of the global warming challenge combined with the implication to almost every facet of a nation-state's economic interests places significant burdens on the established largely-voluntary global institutions that have developed over the last century; institutions that have been unable to effectively reshape themselves and move fast enough to deal with this unique challenge.  Rapidly developing countries who see traditional energy sources as a means to fuel their development, well funded aggressive environmental lobbying groups and an established fossil fuel energy paradigm boasting a mature and sophisticated political lobbying infrastructure all combine to make global warming politics extremely polarized.  Distrust between developed and developing countries at most international conferences that seek to address the topic add to the challenges.  Further adding to the complexity is the advent of the Internet and the development of media technologies like blogs and other mechanisms for disseminating information that enable the exponential growth in production and dissemination of competing points of view which make it nearly impossible for the development and dissemination of an objective view into the enormity of the subject matter and its politics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nontraditional_environmental_challenge">Nontraditional environmental challenge</span></h2>
<p>Traditional environmental challenges generally involve behavior by a small group of industries who create products or services for a limited set of consumers in a manner that causes some form of damage to the environment which is clear.  As an example, a gold mine might release a dangerous chemical byproduct into a waterway that kills the fish in the waterway: a clear environmental damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arsenic_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-arsenic-7">[7]</a></sup>  By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> is a naturally occurring colorless odorless trace gas that is essential to the biosphere.  Carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) is produced by all animals and utilized by plants and algae to build their body structures.  Plant structures buried for tens of millions of years sequester carbon to form coal, oil and gas which modern industrial societies find essential to economic vitality. Over 80% of the worlds energy is derived from CO<sub>2</sub> emitting fossil fuels and over 91% of the world's energy is derived from non <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral_fuel" title="Carbon-neutral fuel">carbon-neutral</a> energy sources. Scientists attribute the increases of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere to industrial emissions and scientists have linked CO<sub>2</sub> to global warming.  However, the scientific consensus is difficult for the average individual layperson to readily see and grasp. This essential nature to the world's economies combined with the complexity of the science and the interests of countless interested parties make climate change a non-traditional environmental challenge.
</p>
<h3><span id="Carbon_dioxide_and_a_nation-state.27s_economy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_and_a_nation-state's_economy">Carbon dioxide and a nation-state's economy</span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:404px;max-width:404px"><div style="clear:both;font-weight:bold;text-align:;background-color:">Energy Consumption Linked to CO2 Emissions</div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:402px;max-width:402px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World-energy-consumption-by-fuel-projections-1990---2035-USDOE-IEA-2011.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="400" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2b/World-energy-consumption-by-fuel-projections-1990---2035-USDOE-IEA-2011.png" width="400"/></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:402px;max-width:402px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World-co2-emissions-by-fuel-1990---2035-USDOE-IEA-2011.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="400" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/World-co2-emissions-by-fuel-1990---2035-USDOE-IEA-2011.png" width="400"/></a></div></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:right;background-color:transparent">source: USDOE EIA IEO 2011</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">World energy consumption</a></div>
<p>The vast majority of developed countries rely on CO<sub>2</sub> emitting energy sources for large components of their economic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Fossil fuel energy generally dominates the following areas of an OECD economy:
</p>
<ul><li>agriculture (fertilizers, irrigation, plowing, planting, harvesting, pesticides)</li>
<li>transportation &amp; distribution (automobiles, shipping, trains, airplanes)</li>
<li>storage (refrigeration, warehousing)</li>
<li>national defense (armies, tanks, military aircraft, manufacture of munitions)</li></ul>
<p>In addition, CO<sub>2</sub> emitting fossil fuels many times dominate the utilities aspect of an economy that provide electricity for:
</p>
<ul><li>lighting</li>
<li>heating &amp; cooling</li>
<li>refrigeration</li>
<li>production of products</li>
<li>computing and telecommunications</li></ul>
<p>Also, activities like cement production, deforestation, brick production, livestock raising, refrigeration and other industrial activity contributes greenhouse gases that together are believed to account for 1/3 of global warming.
</p><p>Because CO<sub>2</sub> emitting fossil fuels are intrinsically connected to a developed nation-state's economy, the taxation of fossil fuels or policies that decrease the availability of cost-effective fossil fuels is a significant political matter for fear that those taxes might precipitate a decrease in economic vitality.  The replacement of cost-effective fossil fuels with more expensive renewable energy sources are seen by many as a hidden tax that would achieve the same result of depressing economic vitality and lead to impoverishment.  Beyond the economic vitality of a single nation, some are concerned that taxation would depress economic activity in a manner that could affect the geopolitical order by providing incentives to one set of countries over another.
</p><p>In developing countries the challenges are slightly different.  Developing countries see CO<sub>2</sub> emitting fossil fuels as a cost effective and proven energy source to fuel their growing economies.  Sometimes renewable energy technologies are not readily available to developing countries because of cost or due to export restrictions from developed countries who own those technologies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perceived_lack_of_adequate_advanced_energy_technologies">Perceived lack of adequate advanced energy technologies</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1268" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png/400px-Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png/600px-Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png/800px-Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Total_World_Energy_Consumption_by_Source_2010.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 2010, renewable energy accounted for 16.7% of total <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">energy consumption</a> but <a href="/wiki/Biomass_heating_system" title="Biomass heating system">Biomass energy</a> which releases CO<sub>2</sub> and is not carbon-neutral accounted for 11.4%, leaving only 4.9% for carbon-neutral renewables (<a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> 3.3% and 1.56% for all others).</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_Oil" title="Peak Oil">Peak Oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_Fuels" title="Fossil Fuels">Fossil Fuels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Energy" title="Solar Energy">Solar Energy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_Energy" title="Wind Energy">Wind Energy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy" title="Renewable Energy">Renewable Energy</a></div>
<p>Carbon dioxide emitting fossil fuels continue to be abundant and their prices are consequently low accounting in 2010 for over 80% of the world's energy needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mary_S._Booth_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mary_S._Booth-6">[6]</a></sup> Advanced recovery technologies like horizontal drilling, offshore oil production, and oil sand recovery technologies continue to push back the threshold of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_Oil" title="Peak Oil">Peak Oil</a> and with it the high prices seen necessary to foster the development of viable alternative energy technologies that can replace fossil fuels in a post-hydrocarbon economy. Renewables in 2010 accounted for 16.7% of the world's energy, however <a href="/wiki/Biomass_heating_system" title="Biomass heating system">Biomass energy</a> accounted for 11.4% meaning that non-carbon dioxide producing renewables accounted for only 4.9% of the world's energy use with the vast majority of that renewable energy coming from hydroelectric production at 3.34% further leaving 1.56% of renewable energy derived from newer advanced technologies like ethanol, biodiesel, wind, solar, ocean power and geothermal (see graph to right).
</p><p>The biomass-is-carbon-neutral proposal put forward in the early 1990s has been superseded by more recent science that recognizes that mature, intact forests sequester carbon more effectively than cut-over areas. When a tree's carbon is released into the atmosphere in a single pulse, it contributes to climate change much more than woodland timber rotting slowly over decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mary_S._Booth_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mary_S._Booth-6">[6]</a></sup> Current studies indicate that "even after 50 years the forest has not recovered to its initial carbon storage" and "the optimal strategy is likely to be protection of the standing forest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cc-fffce_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cc-fffce-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>After adjustment, carbon neutral renewables account for 4.9% of the world's energy needs in 2010 with solar accounting for .23% and wind for 0.51% of total world energy need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
Recent optimistic projections from the EIA and the IEA show that renewables will account for one sixth of global energy production in the next few decades (which includes <a href="/wiki/Biomass_heating_system" title="Biomass heating system">Biomass energy</a>), substantially below what is needed to significantly curtail CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.
</p><p>Without help developing countries usually do not have access to the advanced energy technologies like wind and solar that they require for development forcing them to rely on hydrocarbon energy sources like fossil fuels and biomass. Without adequate and cost effective post-hydrocarbon energy sources, it is very unlikely the countries in developed or developing world would accept policies that would materially affect their economic vitality or economic development prospects.  To date, developing countries have resisted adopting verifiable CO<sub>2</sub> targets for fear of impacts to their economies and the United States, Russia, Canada, Japan, New Zealand, Belarus and Ukraine have either not ratified the Kyoto Protocol, withdrawn from the Kyoto Protocol or have chosen to not accept a second commitment period leaving the Kyoto Protocol extension covering only 15% of global CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.  A strong contributor to these decisions is that the existing technologies are not yet adequate to replace the role of fossil hydrocarbon fuels.
</p><p>Arguments have been made that <a href="/wiki/Energy_subsidies" title="Energy subsidies">fostering renewable energy through subsidies</a> and other adoption-mechanisms are the path towards increasing the percentage of carbon-neutral renewable technologies that are used. According to <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">IEA</a> (2011) energy subsidies artificially lower the price of energy paid by consumers, raise the price received by producers or lower the cost of production. "Fossil fuels subsidies costs generally outweigh the benefits. Subsidies to renewables and low-carbon energy technologies can bring long-term economic and environmental benefits".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA-FS2011_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEA-FS2011-11">[11]</a></sup> In November 2011, an IEA report entitled <i>Deploying Renewables 2011</i> said "subsidies in green energy technologies that were not yet competitive are justified in order to give an incentive to investing into technologies with clear environmental and energy security benefits". The IEA's report disagreed with claims that renewable energy technologies are only viable through costly subsidies and not able to produce energy reliably to meet demand. "A portfolio of renewable energy technologies is becoming cost-competitive in an increasingly broad range of circumstances, in some cases providing investment opportunities without the need for specific economic support," the IEA said, and added that "cost reductions in critical technologies, such as wind and solar, are set to continue."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henning_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-henning-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_consumption_subsidies" title="Fossil-fuel consumption subsidies">Fossil-fuel consumption subsidies</a> were $409 billion in 2010, oil products ca half of it. Renewable-energy subsidies were $66 billion in 2010 and will reach according to IEA $250 billion by 2035. Renewable energy is subsidized in order to compete in the market, increase their volume and develop the technology so that the subsidies become unnecessary with the development. Eliminating fossil-fuel subsidies could bring economic and environmental benefits. Phasing out fossil-fuel subsidies by 2020 would cut primary energy demand 5%. Since the start of 2010, <i>at least 15 countries have taken steps to phase out fossil-fuel subsidies. </i> According to IEA onshore wind may become competitive around 2020 in the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA-FS2011_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEA-FS2011-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrialization_of_the_developing_world">Industrialization of the developing world</span></h3>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:404px;max-width:404px"><div style="clear:both;font-weight:bold;text-align:;background-color:">Economic development linked to energy consumption</div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:402px;max-width:402px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OECD-non-OECD-GDP-1990-2035-DOEEIA-IEO-2011.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="400" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/OECD-non-OECD-GDP-1990-2035-DOEEIA-IEO-2011.png" width="400"/></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:402px;max-width:402px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World-energy-consumption--projections-USDOE-IEA-2011.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="400" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a3/World-energy-consumption--projections-USDOE-IEA-2011.png" width="400"/></a></div></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:right;background-color:transparent">source: USDOE EIA IEO 2011</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">Developing world</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newly_industrialized_country" title="Newly industrialized country">Newly industrialized country</a></div>
<p>The developing world sees economic and industrial development as a natural right and the evidence shows that the developing world is industrializing.  The developing world is leveraging the use of CO<sub>2</sub> emitting fossil fuels as one of the primary energy sources to fuel their development.  At the same time the scientific consensus on climate change and the existing global governance bodies like the United Nations are urging all countries to decrease their CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.  Developing countries logically resist this lobbying to decrease their use of fossil fuels without significant concessions like:
</p>
<ul><li>advanced energy technologies</li>
<li>advanced adaptation technologies</li>
<li>monies to adapt to climate change.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Metric_selection_and_perceived_responsibility_.2F_ability_to_respond"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_selection_and_perceived_responsibility_/_ability_to_respond">Metric selection and perceived  responsibility / ability to respond</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_economy_of_climate_change" title="Political economy of climate change">Political economy of climate change</a></div>
<p>There are significant disagreements over which metrics to use when tracking global warming and there are also disagreements over which countries should be subject to emissions restrictions.
</p><p>While the biosphere is indifferent to whether the greenhouse gases are produced by one country or by a multitude, the countries of the world do express an interest in such matters.  As such disagreements arise on whether per capita emissions should be used or whether total emissions should be used as a metric for each individual country.  Countries also disagree over whether a developing country should share the same commitment as a developed country that has been emitting CO<sub>2</sub> and other greenhouse gases for close to a century.
</p><p>Some developing countries expressly state that they require assistance if they are to develop, which is seen as a right, in a fashion that does not contribute CO<sub>2</sub> or other greenhouse gases to the atmosphere.  Many times, these needs materialize as profound differences in global conferences by countries on the subject and the debates quickly turn to pecuniary matters.
</p><p>Most developing countries are unwilling to accept limits on their CO<sub>2</sub> and other greenhouse gas emissions while most developed countries place very modest limits on their willingness to assist developing countries.  In addition, most developed countries would rather not participate in greenhouse gas reduction treaties if those would lead to decreased economic activity, transfers of wealth to developing countries, or significant shifts in the geopolitical balance of power of the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulnerable_developing_countries_and_developed_country_legacy_emissions">Vulnerable developing countries and developed country legacy emissions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Political_economy_of_climate_change" title="Political economy of climate change">Political economy of climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_Vulnerable_Forum" title="Climate Vulnerable Forum">Climate Vulnerable Forum</a></div>
<p>Some developing countries fall under the category of vulnerable to climate change.  These countries involve small, sometimes isolated, island nations, low lying nations, nations who rely on drinking water from shrinking glaciers etc.  These vulnerable countries see themselves as the victims of climate change and some have organized themselves under groups like the <a href="/wiki/Climate_Vulnerable_Forum" title="Climate Vulnerable Forum">Climate Vulnerable Forum</a>.  These countries seek mitigation monies from the developed and the industrializing countries to help them adapt to the impending catastrophes that they see climate change will bring upon them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> For these countries climate change is seen as an existential threat and the politics of these countries is to seek reparation and adaptation monies from the developed world and some see it as their right.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming_politics_focus_areas">Global warming politics focus areas</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">Adaptation to global warming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">Mitigation of global warming</a></div>
<p>Government politics regarding climate change and many official reports on the subject usually revolve around addressing one of the following topic areas:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Adaptation</b>: social and other changes that must be undertaken to successfully <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adapt to climate change</a>.  Adaptation might encompass, but is not limited to, changes in agriculture and urban planning.</li>
<li><b>Finance</b>: how countries will finance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation</a> to and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> of climate change, whether from public or private sources or from wealth/technology transfers from developed countries to developing countries and the management mechanisms for those monies.</li>
<li><b>Mitigation</b>: steps and actions that the countries of the world can take to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigate</a>  the effects of climate change.</li>
<li><b>Technology</b>: the technologies that are needed lower carbon emissions through increasing energy efficiency or replacement or CO<sub>2</sub> emitting technologies and technologies needed to adapt or mitigate climate change.  Also encompasses ways that developed countries can support developing countries in adopting new technologies or increasing efficiency.</li>
<li><b>Loss and damage</b>: first articulated at the 2012 conference and in part based on the agreement that was signed at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cancun" title="Cancun">Cancun</a>. It introduces the principle that countries vulnerable to the effects of climate change may be financially compensated in future by countries that fail to curb their carbon emissions.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus-driven_global_political_institutions">Consensus-driven global political institutions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a></div>
<p>The primary mechanism for the world to tackle global warming is through a process established under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) treaty. The current state of global warming politics is that there is frustration over a perceived lack of progress with the establish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> overall process which has progressed over eighteen years but which has been unable to curb global <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.  Todd Stern—the US Climate Change envoy—has expressed the challenges with the UNFCCC process as follows, "Climate change is not a conventional environmental issue ... It implicates virtually every aspect of a state's economy, so it makes countries nervous about growth and development. This is an economic issue every bit as it is an environmental one."  He went on to explain that, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change is a multilateral body concerned with climate change and can be an inefficient system for enacting international policy. Because the framework system includes over 190 countries and because negotiations are governed by consensus, small groups of countries can often block progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Dartmouth_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Dartmouth-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The eighteenth conference of the parties held in <a href="/wiki/Doha" title="Doha">Doha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, <a href="/wiki/2012_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2012 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2012 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>, yielded minor to modest results.  At the <a href="/wiki/2012_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2012 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2012 Doha climate change talks</a>, Parties to the Kyoto Protocol agreed to an extension of the Kyoto Protocol to 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ritter_2012_doha_talks_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ritter_2012_doha_talks-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrabin_2012_doha_talks_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-harrabin_2012_doha_talks-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_2012_doha_talks_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvey_2012_doha_talks-16">[16]</a></sup> Participants in the extension to the Kyoto Protocol have taken on targets for the period 2013–2020, and include Australia, the European Union, and a number of other developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfccc_2012_kyoto_extension_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-unfccc_2012_kyoto_extension-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, which withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> in 2011, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>—which never ratified the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>—have been joined by <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> who have stated that they would not sign up to a second <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> commitment period or extension due lack of commitments from the developing world which today include the world's largest CO<sub>2</sub> emitters.  Japan and New Zealand also added that their country's CO2 emissions are minor when compared to the emissions of China, The United States, and the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These defections place significant pressures on the UNFCCC process which to date has not been able to curtail CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, whose latest Kyoto Protocol extension only accounts for 15% of greenhouse gas emissions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ritter_2012_doha_talks_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ritter_2012_doha_talks-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrabin_2012_doha_talks_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-harrabin_2012_doha_talks-15">[15]</a></sup> and whose process is seen by some as slow, cumbersome, expensive and an inefficient use of taxpayer money: in the UK alone the climate change department has taken over 3,000 flights over the course of two years at a cost to the taxpayer of over ₤1,500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfccc_2012_kyoto_extension_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-unfccc_2012_kyoto_extension-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The outcome of the Doha talks has received a mixed response,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ritter_2012_doha_talks_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-ritter_2012_doha_talks-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrabin_2012_doha_talks_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-harrabin_2012_doha_talks-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_2012_doha_talks_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-harvey_2012_doha_talks-16">[16]</a></sup> with small island states critical of the overall package.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrabin_2012_doha_talks_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-harrabin_2012_doha_talks-15">[15]</a></sup> Other results of the conference include a timetable for a global agreement to be adopted by 2015 which includes all countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfccc_2012_doha_press_release_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-unfccc_2012_doha_press_release-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result, some have argued that perhaps the consensus driven model could be replaced with a majority vote model.  However, that model would likely drive disagreement at the country-level-ratification by countries who disagreed with any global treaties that might passed through a majority vote at such restructured institutions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_emissions_reductions">Voluntary emissions reductions</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_and_Clean_Air_Coalition_to_Reduce_Short-Lived_Climate_Pollutants" title="Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants">Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants</a></div>
<p>The perceived slow process of efforts for countries to agree to a comprehensive global level binding agreements has led some countries to seek independent/voluntary steps and focus on alternative high-value voluntary activities like the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Climate_and_Clean_Air_Coalition_to_Reduce_Short-Lived_Climate_Pollutants" title="Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants">Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> which seeks to regulate short-lived pollutants such as methane, black carbon and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) which together are believed to account for up to 1/3 of current global warming but whose regulation is not as fraught with wide economic impacts and opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Climate_and_Clean_Air_Coalition_to_Reduce_Short-Lived_Climate_Pollutants" title="Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants">Climate and Clean Air Coalition to Reduce Short-Lived Climate Pollutants</a> (CCAC) was launched on February 16, 2012 to regulate short-lived climate pollutants (SLCPs) that together contribute up to 1/3 of global warming. The coalition's creation is seen as a necessary and pragmatic step given the slow pace of global climate change agreements under the UNFCCC.[1]
</p><p>As part of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2010 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2010 Cancún agreements</a>, 76 developed and developing countries have made voluntary pledges to control their emissions of greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_2011_cancun_agreement_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-king_2011_cancun_agreement-22">[22]</a></sup>  These voluntary steps are seen by some as a new model where countries pledge to voluntarily take action against global warming outside of international treaties or obligations to other parties.  This voluntary mechanism, while promising, does not address many of the challenges seen by the developing world in their efforts to mitigate global warming, adapt to global warming, and increasingly to deal with losses and damages that they directly attribute to global warming that they blame on the developed world's historical emissions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_interests_and_lobbying_by_non-country_interested_parties">Special interests and lobbying by non-country interested parties</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3480" data-file-width="4640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg/220px-BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg/330px-BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg/440px-BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BoiseDSAClimateChangeMarchers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global warming has attracted the attention of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_left" title="Far left">far left</a> groups, as here with the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Socialists_of_America" title="Democratic Socialists of America">Democratic Socialists of America</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>There are numerous special interest groups, PACs, organizations, corporations who have public and private positions on the multifaceted topic of global warming.  The following is a partial list of the types of special interest parties that have demonstrated an interest in the politics of global warming:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Financial Institutions</b>: Financial institutions generally support restrictive policies regarding global warming, particularly the implementation of carbon trading schemes and the creation of market mechanisms that associate a price with carbon.  These new markets would require trading infrastructures which banking institutions are well positioned to provide.  Financial institutions would also be positioned well to invest, trade and develop various financial instruments that they could profit from through speculative positions on carbon prices and the use of brokerage and other financial functions like insurance and derivative instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Environmental groups</b>: Environmental groups generally take ideological positions on global warming and favor strict restrictions on CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.  Environmental groups, as activists, engage in raising awareness and attracting investment into the advocacy movement to further raise awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Fossil fuel companies</b>: Traditional fossil fuel corporations could benefit or lose from stricter global warming regulations.  A reduction in the use of fossil fuels could negatively impact fossil fuel corporations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michaels_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michaels-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoggan_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoggan-26">[26]</a></sup>  However, the fact that fossil fuel companies are a large source of energy, are also the primary source of CO<sub>2</sub>, and are engaged in energy trading might mean that their participation in trading schemes and other such mechanisms might give them a unique advantage and makes it unclear whether traditional fossil fuel companies would all and always be against stricter global warming policies.  As an example, Enron, a traditional gas pipeline company with a large trading desk heavily lobbied the government for the EPA to regulate CO2: they thought that they would dominate the energy industry if they could be at the center of energy trading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Alternative energy companies</b>: alternative energy companies like wind and solar generally support stricter global warming policies.  They would expect their share of the energy market to expand as fossil fuels are made more expensive through trading schemes or taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Nuclear energy companies</b>: nuclear energy companies could see a renaissance in a world where fossil fuels are taxed directly or through a carbon trading mechanism.  For this reason, it is likely that nuclear energy companies would likely support stricter global warming policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Traditional retailers and marketers</b>: traditional retailers, marketers, and the general corporations respond  by adopting policies that resonate with their customers.  If "being green" helps a general corporation, then they could undertake modest programs to please and better align with their customers.  However, since the general corporation does not make a profit from their particular position, it is unlikely that they would strongly lobby either for or against a stricter global warming policy position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Governments</b>: On the Australian Sunday morning political discussion show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bolt_Report" title="The Bolt Report">The Bolt Report</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Richard Lindzen</a> said in a 2011 interview that governments might use global warming as a rationale for additional taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The various interested parties sometimes align with one another to reinforce their message.  Sometimes industries will fund specialty nonprofit organizations  to raise awareness and lobby on their behest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>  The combinations and tactics that the various interested parties use are nuanced and sometimes unlimited in the variety of their approaches to promote their positions onto the general public.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_of_climate_science_and_actual_policy">Interaction of climate science and actual policy</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">Global warming controversy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politicization_of_science" title="Politicization of science">Politicization of science</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_policy" title="Knowledge policy">Knowledge policy</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a>, there is a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">strong consensus that global surface temperatures have increased</a> in recent decades and that the trend is caused primarily by human-induced emissions of greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OreskesBeyondTheIvoryTower_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-OreskesBeyondTheIvoryTower-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericasClimateChoices-2010-SciPanel_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmericasClimateChoices-2010-SciPanel-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNAS-2008-ClimateChoices_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNAS-2008-ClimateChoices-36">[36]</a></sup> With regard to the global warming controversy, the scientific mainstream puts neither doubt on the existence of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> nor on its causes and <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects</a>.
</p><p>The politicization of science in the sense of a manipulation of science for political gains is a part of the political process. It is part of the controversies about <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design" title="Intelligent design">intelligent design</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nejm_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-nejm-38">[38]</a></sup> (compare the <a href="/wiki/Wedge_strategy" title="Wedge strategy">Wedge strategy</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Merchants_of_Doubt" title="Merchants of Doubt">Merchants of Doubt</a>, scientists that are under suspicion to willingly obscure findings. e.g. about issues like tobacco smoke, ozone depletion, global warming or acid rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merchants_doubt_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-merchants_doubt-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, e.g. in case of the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Ozone depletion</a>, global regulation based on the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> has been successful, in a climate of high uncertainty and against strong resistance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-RG-41">[41]</a></sup> while in case of Climate Change, the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cass_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-cass-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>While the IPCC process tries to find and orchestrate the findings of global (climate) change research to shape a worldwide consensus on the matter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> it has been itself been object of a strong politicization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLT_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLT-44">[44]</a></sup> Anthropogenic climate change evolved from a mere science issue to a top global policy topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLT_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-CLT-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC process faces currently a paradox lockstep<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLT_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-CLT-44">[44]</a></sup> where having built a broad science consensus does not hinder governments to follow different, if not opposing goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-par_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-par-45">[45]</a></sup> In case of the ozone depletion challenge, there was global regulation already being installed before a scientific consensus was established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RG_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-RG-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>A linear model of policy-making, based on a  <i>more knowledge we have, the better the political response will be</i> does therefore not apply. <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_policy" title="Knowledge policy">Knowledge policy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLT_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-CLT-44">[44]</a></sup> successfully managing knowledge and uncertainties as base of political decision making requires a better understanding of the relation between science, public (lack of) understanding and policy instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cass_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-cass-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-par_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-par-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Oppenheimer" title="Michael Oppenheimer">Michael Oppenheimer</a> confirms limitations of the IPCC consensus approach and asks for concurring, smaller assessments of special problems instead of large scale attempts as in the previous IPCC assessment reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MO_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-MO-47">[47]</a></sup> He claims that governments require a broader exploration of uncertainties in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MO_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-MO-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Historically, the politics of climate change dates back to several conferences in the late 1960s and the early 1970s under NATO and President Richard Nixon.  1979 saw the world's first <a href="/wiki/World_Climate_Conference" title="World Climate Conference">World Climate Conference</a>.  1985 was the year that the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer</a> was created and two years later in 1987 saw the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> under the Vienna convention.  This model of using a Framework conference followed by Protocols under the Framework was seen as a promising governing structure that could be used as a path towards a functional governance approach that could be used to tackle broad global multi-nation/state challenges like global warming.
</p><p>One year later in 1988 the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> was created by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> to assess the risk of human-induced climate change. <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> 1988 strongly supported IPCC and 1990 was instrumental to found the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a> in Exeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1991 the book <a href="/wiki/The_First_Global_Revolution" title="The First Global Revolution">The First Global Revolution</a> was published by the <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> report which sought to connect environment, water availability, food production, energy production, materials, population growth and other elements into a blueprint for the twenty-first century: political thinking was evolving to look at the world in terms of an integrated global system not just in terms of weather and climate but in terms of energy needs, food, population, etc.
</p><p>1992 was the year that the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a>) was agreed at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> and the framework entered into force 21 March 1994.  The conference established a yearly meeting, a conference of the parties or COP meeting to be held to continue work on Protocols which would be enforceable treaties
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="540" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/220px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/330px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/440px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a> by country.</div></div></div>
<p>1995 saw the creation of the phrase "preventing dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system" (also called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">avoiding dangerous climate change</a></i>) first appeared in a policy document of a governmental organization, the IPCC's Second Assessment Report: Climate Change 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and in 1996 the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> adopt a goal of limiting temperature rises to a maximum 2 °C rise in average global temperature.
</p><p>In 1997 the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> was created under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a>) in a very similar structure as the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> was under the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer</a> which would have yearly meetings of the members or CMP meetings.  However, in the same year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Senate" title="US Senate">US Senate</a> passed <a href="/wiki/Byrd%E2%80%93Hagel_Resolution" title="Byrd–Hagel Resolution">Byrd–Hagel Resolution</a> rejecting Kyoto without more commitments from developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatCent.org_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatCent.org-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1992 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> treaty, eighteen COP sessions and eight CMP sessions have been held under the existing structure.  In that time, global CO2 emissions have risen significantly and developing countries have grown significantly with <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> replacing the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> as the largest emitter of greenhouse gases.  To some, the UNFCCC has made significant progress in helping the world become aware of the perils of global warming and has moved the world forward in the addressing of the challenge. To others, the UNFCCC process has been a failure due to its inability to control the rise of greenhouse gas emissions.
</p><p>A number of proposals for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Climate_Regime" title="Global Climate Regime">Global Climate Regime</a> are currently discussed, as the <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference#Durban_Platform" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">Durban Platform for Enhanced Action</a> calls for a comprehensive new agreement in 2015 that includes both <a href="/wiki/List_of_parties_to_the_United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="List of parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex-I and Non-Annex-I parties</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selective_historical_timeline_of_significant_climate_change_political_events">Selective historical timeline of significant climate change political events</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:179px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DanielPatrickMoynihan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="177" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/DanielPatrickMoynihan.jpg" width="177"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DanielPatrickMoynihan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patrick_Moynihan" title="Daniel Patrick Moynihan">Daniel Patrick Moynihan</a>, pioneer of the political treatment of the greenhouse effect</div></div></div>
<ul><li>1969, on Initiative of US President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>, <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> tried to establish a third civil column and planned to establish itself as a hub of research and initiatives in the civil region, especially on environmental topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patrick_Moynihan" title="Daniel Patrick Moynihan">Daniel Patrick Moynihan</a>, Nixons NATO delegate for the topic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> named <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> as suitable international challenges to be dealt by NATO. NATO had suitable expertise in the field, experience with international research coordination and a direct access to governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> After an enthusiastic start on authority level, the German government reacted skeptically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> The initiative was seen as an American attempt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> to regain international terrain after the lost Vietnam War. The topics and the internal coordination and preparation effort however gained momentum in civil conferences and institutions in Germany and beyond during the Brandt government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>1972 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup> leading role of Nobel Prize winner <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olof_Palme" title="Olof Palme">Olof Palme</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Germany saw enhanced international research cooperation on the greenhouse topic as necessary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_52-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>1978 <a href="/wiki/Brandt_Report" title="Brandt Report">Brandt Report</a>, the greenhouse effect dealt with in the energy section<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willy_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willy-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>1979: First <a href="/wiki/World_Climate_Conference" title="World Climate Conference">World Climate Conference</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>1987: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brundtland_Report" title="Brundtland Report">Brundtland Report</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willy_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Willy-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>1987: <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> on restricting <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>-damaging <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFCs</a> demonstrates the possibility of coordinated international action on global environmental issues.</li>
<li>1988: <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> set up to coordinate scientific research, by two United Nations organizations, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) to assess the "risk of human-induced climate change".</li>
<li>1992: <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> was formed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">"prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system"</a></li>
<li>1996: <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> adopts target of a maximum 2 °C rise in average global temperature</li>
<li>25 June 1997: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Senate" title="US Senate">US Senate</a> passes <a href="/wiki/Byrd%E2%80%93Hagel_Resolution" title="Byrd–Hagel Resolution">Byrd–Hagel Resolution</a> rejecting Kyoto without more commitments from developing countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatCent.org_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-NatCent.org-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>1997: <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> agreed</li>
<li>2001: <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> withdraws from the Kyoto negotiations</li>
<li>16 February 2005: Kyoto Protocol comes into force (not including the US or Australia)</li>
<li>2005: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Union_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emissions Trading Scheme">European Union Emissions Trading Scheme</a> is launched, the first such scheme</li>
<li>July 2005: <a href="/wiki/31st_G8_summit" title="31st G8 summit">31st G8 summit</a> has climate change on the agenda, but makes relatively little concrete progress</li>
<li>November/December 2005: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="United Nations Climate Change Conference">United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>; the first meeting of the Parties of the Kyoto Protocol, alongside the 11th Conference of the Parties (COP11), to plan further measures for 2008–2012 and beyond.</li>
<li>30 October 2006: The <a href="/wiki/Stern_Review" title="Stern Review">Stern Review</a> is published. It is the first comprehensive contribution to the global warming debate by an economist and its conclusions lead to the promise of urgent action by the UK government to further curb Europe's CO<sub>2</sub> emissions and engage other countries to do so. It discusses the consequences of climate change, mitigation measures to prevent it, possible adaptation measures to deal with its consequences, and prospects for international cooperation.</li>
<li>26 June 2009: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_House_of_Representatives" title="US House of Representatives">US House of Representatives</a> passes the <a href="/wiki/American_Clean_Energy_and_Security_Act" title="American Clean Energy and Security Act">American Clean Energy and Security Act</a>, the "first time either house of Congress had approved a bill meant to curb the heat-trapping gases scientists have linked to climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>12 December 2015: World leaders meet in Paris, France for the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">21st Conference of the Parties of the UNFCCC</a>.  One hundred eighty seven countries eventually signed on to the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>.  As of September 2016, 187 UNFCCC members have signed the treaty, 60 of which have ratified it. The agreement will only enter into force provided that 55 countries that produce at least 55% of the world's greenhouse gas emissions ratify, accept, approve or accede to the agreement; although the minimum number of ratifications has been reached, the ratifying states do not produce the requisite percentage of greenhouse gases for the agreement to enter into force.</li>
<li>1 June 2017: <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">withdraws the United States from the Paris Agreement</a></li></ul>
