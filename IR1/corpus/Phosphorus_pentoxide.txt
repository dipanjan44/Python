<p><b>Phosphorus pentoxide</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with molecular formula <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">P</a><sub>4</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>10</sub> (with its common name derived from its <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a>, P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>).  This white crystalline solid is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydride" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>. It is a powerful <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydrating agent</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phosphorus pentoxide crystallizes in at least four forms or <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>. The most familiar one, a metastable form,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-1">[1]</a></sup> shown in the figure, comprises molecules of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>. Weak <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a> hold these molecules together in a hexagonal lattice (However, in spite of the high symmetry of the molecules, the crystal packing is not a close packing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>). The structure of the P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> cage is reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Adamantane" title="Adamantane">adamantane</a> with <i>T</i><sub>d</sub> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetry_point_group" title="Symmetry point group">symmetry point group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is closely related to the corresponding anhydride of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorous_acid" title="Phosphorous acid">phosphorous acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trioxide" title="Phosphorus trioxide">P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub></a>. The latter lacks terminal oxo groups. Its density is 2.30 g/cm<sup>3</sup>. It boils at 423 °C under atmospheric pressure; if heated more rapidly it can sublimate. This form can be made by condensing the vapor of phosphorus pentoxide rapidly, the result is an extremely hygroscopic solid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The other polymorphs are polymeric, but in each case the phosphorus atoms are bound by a tetrahedron of oxygen atoms, one of which forms a terminal P=O bond involving the donation of the terminal oxygen p-orbital electrons to the antibonding phosphorus-oxygen single bonds. The macromolecular form can be made by heating the compound in a sealed tube for several hours, and maintaining the melt at a high temperature before cooling the melt to the solid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-4">[4]</a></sup> The metastable orthorhombic, "O"-form (density 2.72 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> 562 °C), adopts a layered structure consisting of interconnected P<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> rings, not unlike the structure adopted by certain poly<a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>. The stable form is a higher density phase, also orthorhombic, the so-called O' form. It consists of a 3-dimensional framework, density 3.5 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The remaining polymorph is a <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> or amorphous form; it can be made by fusing any of the others.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> is prepared by burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraphosphorus" title="Tetraphosphorus">tetraphosphorus</a> with sufficient supply of oxygen:
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub> + 5 O<sub>2</sub> → P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub></dd></dl>
<p>For most of the 20th century, phosphorus pentoxide was used to provide a supply of concentrated pure <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>. In the thermal process, the phosphorus pentoxide obtained by burning white phosphorus was dissolved in dilute <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> to produce concentrated acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Improvements in filter technology is leading to the "wet phosphoric acid process" taking over from the thermal process, obviating the need to produce <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_phosphorus#White_phosphorus" title="Allotropes of phosphorus">white phosphorus</a> as a starting material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The dehydration of phosphoric acid to give phosphorus pentoxide is not possible as on heating metaphosphoric acid will boil without losing all its water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phosphorus pentoxide is a potent <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydrating</a> agent as indicated by the exothermic nature of its hydrolysis:  
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O → 4 H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>   (–177 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>)</dd></dl>
<p>However, its utility for drying is limited somewhat by its tendency to form a protective viscous coating that inhibits further dehydration by unspent material. A granular form of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> is used in <a href="/wiki/Desiccator" title="Desiccator">desiccators</a>.
</p><p>Consistent with its strong desiccating power, P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> is used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> for dehydration. The most important application is for the conversion of primary <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitriles" title="Nitriles">nitriles</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> + RC(O)NH<sub>2</sub> → P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub> + RCN</dd></dl>
<p>The indicated coproduct P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub> is an idealized formula for undefined products resulting from the hydration of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>.
</p><p>Alternatively, when combined with a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>, the result is the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydride" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> + RCO<sub>2</sub>H → P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub> + [RC(O)]<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The "Onodera reagent", a solution of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> in <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">DMSO</a>, is employed for the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This reaction is reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Swern_oxidation" title="Swern oxidation">Swern oxidation</a>.
</p><p>The desiccating power of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub> is strong enough to convert many mineral acids to their anhydrides. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">HNO<sub>3</sub></a> is converted to <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_pentoxide" title="Dinitrogen pentoxide">N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a>;  <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub></a> is converted to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">SO<sub>3</sub></a>;  <a href="/wiki/Perchloric_acid" title="Perchloric acid">HClO<sub>4</sub></a> is converted to <a href="/wiki/Dichlorine_heptoxide" title="Dichlorine heptoxide">Cl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a>;  <a href="/wiki/Triflic_acid" title="Triflic acid">CF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>H</a> is converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triflic_anhydride" title="Triflic anhydride">(CF<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phosphorus pentoxide is not flammable. Just like <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a>, it reacts vigorously with water and water-containing substances like wood or cotton, liberates much heat and may even cause fire. It is corrosive to metal and is very irritating – it may cause severe burns to the eye, skin, <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membrane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory tract</a> even at concentrations as low as 1 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
