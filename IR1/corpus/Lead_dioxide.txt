<p><b>Lead(IV) oxide</b>, commonly called <b>lead dioxide</b> or <b>plumbic oxide</b> or <b>anhydrous plumbic acid</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (sometimes wrongly called <b>lead peroxide</b>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) is a chemical compound with the formula PbO<sub>2</sub>.  It is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> where <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> is in an <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of +4; bond type is predominantly covalent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>. It is an odorless dark-brown crystalline powder which is nearly insoluble in water. It exists in two crystalline forms. The alpha phase has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> symmetry; it was first synthesized in 1941 and was identified in nature as a rare mineral <a href="/wiki/Scrutinyite" title="Scrutinyite">scrutinyite</a> in 1988. The more common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> beta phase was first identified as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Plattnerite" title="Plattnerite">plattnerite</a> around 1845 and later produced synthetically. Lead dioxide is a strong oxidizing agent which is used in the manufacture of matches, pyrotechnics, dyes and other chemicals. It also has several important applications in <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, in particular in the positive plates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_acid_battery" title="Lead acid battery">lead acid batteries</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ScrutinyiteStructure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="867" data-file-width="765" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/ScrutinyiteStructure.png/120px-ScrutinyiteStructure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/ScrutinyiteStructure.png/180px-ScrutinyiteStructure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/ScrutinyiteStructure.png/240px-ScrutinyiteStructure.png 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ScrutinyiteStructure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of α-PbO<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PlattneriteStructure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="864" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PlattneriteStructure.png/120px-PlattneriteStructure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PlattneriteStructure.png/180px-PlattneriteStructure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PlattneriteStructure.png/240px-PlattneriteStructure.png 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PlattneriteStructure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of β-PbO<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<p>Lead dioxide is an odorless dark-brown crystalline powder which is nearly insoluble in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-en-3">[3]</a></sup> It has two major polymorphs, alpha and beta, which occur naturally as rare minerals <a href="/wiki/Scrutinyite" title="Scrutinyite">scrutinyite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plattnerite" title="Plattnerite">plattnerite</a>, respectively. Whereas the beta form had been identified in 1845,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pl_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pl-4">[4]</a></sup> α-PbO<sub>2</sub> was first identified in 1946 and found as a naturally occurring mineral 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-j1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alpha form has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> symmetry, <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> Pbcn (No. 60), <a href="/wiki/Pearson_symbol" title="Pearson symbol">Pearson symbol</a> oP12, lattice constants <i>a</i> = 0.497 nm, <i>b</i> = 0.596 nm, <i>c</i> = 0.544 nm, <i>Z</i> = 4 (four formula units per unit cell).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-j1-5">[5]</a></sup> The lead atoms are six-coordinate. 
</p><p>The symmetry of the beta form is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> P4<sub>2</sub>/mnm (No. 136), <a href="/wiki/Pearson_symbol" title="Pearson symbol">Pearson symbol</a> tP6, lattice constants <i>a</i> = 0.491 nm, <i>c</i> = 0.3385 nm, <i>Z</i> = 2<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and related to the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structure and can be envisaged as containing columns of octahedra sharing opposite edges and joined to other chains by corners. This contrasts with the alpha form where the octahedra are linked by adjacent edges to give zigzag chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-j1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-j1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lead dioxide decomposes upon heating in air as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO<sub>2</sub> → Pb<sub>12</sub>O<sub>19</sub> → Pb<sub>12</sub>O<sub>17</sub> → Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> → PbO</dd></dl>
<p>The stoichiometry of the end product can be controlled by changing the temperature – for example, in the above reaction, the first step occurs at 290 °C, second at 350 °C, third at 375 °C and fourth at 600 °C. In addition, Pb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> can be obtained by decomposing PbO<sub>2</sub> at 580–620 °C under an oxygen pressure of 1,400 bar (140 MPa). Therefore, thermal decomposition of lead dioxide is a common industrial way of producing various lead oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-g-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lead dioxide is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> compound with prevalent acidic properties. It dissolves in strong bases to form the hydroxy<a href="/wiki/Plumbate" title="Plumbate">plumbate</a> ion, [Pb(OH)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-en-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaOH + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O   →  Na<sub>2</sub>[Pb(OH)<sub>6</sub>]</dd></dl>
<p>It also reacts with basic oxides in the melt, yielding orthoplumbates M<sub>4</sub>[PbO<sub>4</sub>].
</p><p>Because of the instability of its Pb<sup>4+</sup> cation, lead dioxide reacts with hot acids, converting to the more stable Pb<sup>2+</sup> state and liberating oxygen:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-g-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 PbO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 2 PbSO<sub>4</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O  +  O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>2 PbO<sub>2</sub> + 4 HNO<sub>3</sub>  →  2 Pb(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>PbO<sub>2</sub> + 4 HCl →  PbCl<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>However these reactions are slow
</p><p>Lead dioxide is well known for being a good <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>, with an example reaction listed below:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 MnSO<sub>4</sub> + 5 PbO<sub>2</sub> + 6 HNO<sub>3</sub> → 2 HMnO<sub>4</sub> + 2 PbSO<sub>4</sub> + 3 Pb(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>2 Cr(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 10 KOH + 3 PbO<sub>2</sub> → 2 K<sub>2</sub>CrO<sub>4</sub> + 3<b>K</b> <sub>2</sub>PbO<sub>2</sub> + 8 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical">Electrochemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electrochemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the formula of lead dioxide is nominally given as PbO<sub>2</sub>, the actual oxygen to lead ratio varies between 1.90 and 1.98 depending on the preparation method. Deficiency of oxygen (or excess of lead) results in the characteristic metallic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">conductivity</a> of lead dioxide, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> as low as 10<sup>−4</sup> Ω·cm and which is exploited in various electrochemical applications. Like metals, lead dioxide has a characteristic <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">electrode potential</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> it can be polarized both <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anodically</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathodically</a>. Lead dioxide electrodes have a dual action, that is both the lead and oxygen ions take part in the electrochemical reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_processes">Chemical processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lead dioxide is produced commercially by several methods, which include oxidation of Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in alkaline slurry in a chlorine atmosphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-g-7">[7]</a></sup> reaction of <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate" title="Lead(II) acetate">lead(II) acetate</a> with "chloride of lime" (a mixture approximating calcium <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorite" title="Hypochlorite">hypochlorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOWI_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOWI-10">[10]</a></sup> or reacting Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> with dilute <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-en_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-en-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 4 HNO<sub>3</sub> → PbO<sub>2</sub> + 2 Pb(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Treating PbCl<sub>2</sub> with a <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> solution yields PbO<sub>2</sub>. By this way lead(II) is oxidized to lead(IV) and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas rises from the hypochlorite solution. By the decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">NaOCl</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a>, stoichiometric amounts of PbO<sub>2</sub> react with NaOH to form the hexahydroxoplumbate(IV) ion [Pb(OH)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>, soluble in water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolysis">Electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An alternative synthesis method is <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemical</a>: lead dioxide forms on pure lead, in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, when polarized anodically at electrode potential about +1.5 V at room temperature. This procedure is used for large-scale industrial production of PbO<sub>2</sub> anodes. Lead and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> electrodes are immersed in sulfuric acid flowing at a rate of 5–10 L/min. The electrodeposition is carried out <a href="/wiki/Galvanostat" title="Galvanostat">galvanostatically</a>, by applying a current of about 100 A/m<sup>2</sup> for about 30 minutes. The drawback of the lead electrode is its softness, especially compared to the hard and brittle PbO<sub>2</sub> which has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 5.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mindat_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mindat-12">[12]</a></sup> This mismatch in mechanical properties results in peeling of the coating. Therefore, an alternative method is to use harder substrates, such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> and deposit PbO<sub>2</sub> onto them from <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_nitrate" title="Lead(II) nitrate">lead(II) nitrate</a> in static or flowing nitric acid. The substrate is usually <a href="/wiki/Abrasive_blasting" title="Abrasive blasting">sand-blasted</a> before the deposition to remove surface oxide and contamination and to increase the surface roughness and adhesion of the coating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-el_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-el-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lead dioxide is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Match" title="Match">matches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic" title="Pyrotechnic">pyrotechnics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a> and the curing of <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>. It is also used in the construction of high-voltage <a href="/wiki/Lightning_arrester" title="Lightning arrester">lightning arresters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-g-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lead dioxide is used as an <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> material in electrochemistry. Beta-PbO<sub>2</sub> is more attractive for this purpose than the alpha form because it has relatively low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a>, good <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance even in low-<a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> medium, and a high <a href="/wiki/Overvoltage" title="Overvoltage">overvoltage</a> for the evolution of oxygen in sulfuric-acid- and nitric-acid-based electrolytes. Lead dioxide can also withstand <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> evolution in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>. Lead dioxide anodes are inexpensive and were once used instead of conventional <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> electrodes for regenerating <a href="/wiki/Potassium_dichromate" title="Potassium dichromate">potassium dichromate</a>. They were also applied as oxygen anodes for <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> in sulfate baths. In organic synthesis, lead dioxide anodes were applied for the production of <a href="/wiki/Glyoxylic_acid" title="Glyoxylic acid">glyoxylic acid</a> from <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a> in a sulfuric acid electrolyte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-el_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-el-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most important use of lead dioxide is as the cathode of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_acid_battery" title="Lead acid battery">lead acid batteries</a>. Its utility arises from the anomalous metallic conductivity of PbO<sub>2</sub>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_acid" title="Lead acid">lead acid</a> battery stores and releases energy by shifting the equilibrium (a comproportionation) between metallic lead, lead dioxide, and lead(II) salts in <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Being a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidant" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a>, lead dioxide is a poison when ingested. The associated symptoms include abdominal pain and spasms, nausea, vomiting and headache. Acute poisoning can lead to muscle weakness, metallic taste, loss of appetite, insomnia, dizziness, with shock, coma and death in extreme cases. The poisoning also results in high lead levels in blood and urine. Contact with skin or eyes results in local irritation and pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
