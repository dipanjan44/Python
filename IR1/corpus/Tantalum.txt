<p><b>Tantalum</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ta</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 73. Previously known as <i>tantalium</i>, its name comes from <i><a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a></i>, a villain from Greek mythology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Tantalum is a rare, hard, blue-gray, <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">lustrous</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> that is highly corrosion-resistant. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Refractory_metals" title="Refractory metals">refractory metals</a> group, which are widely used as minor components in alloys. The chemical inertness of tantalum makes it a valuable substance for laboratory equipment and a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>. Its main use today is in <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_capacitor" title="Tantalum capacitor">tantalum capacitors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> equipment such as <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, <a href="/wiki/DVD_player" title="DVD player">DVD players</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_game_systems" title="Video game systems">video game systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">computers</a>.
Tantalum, always together with the chemically similar <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> groups <a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">coltan</a> (a mix of columbite and tantalite, though not recognised as a separate mineral species).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mindat.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mindat.org-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tantalum was discovered in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> in 1802 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anders_Ekeberg" title="Anders Ekeberg">Anders Ekeberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> One year earlier, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hatchett" title="Charles Hatchett">Charles Hatchett</a> had discovered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbium" title="Columbium">columbium</a> (now niobium),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and in 1809 the English chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a> compared its oxide, <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a> with a density of 5.918 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, to that of tantalum, <a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a> with a density of 7.935 g/cm<sup>3</sup>. He concluded that the two oxides, despite their difference in measured density, were identical and kept the name tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolla_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolla-10">[10]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> confirmed these results, it was thought that columbium and tantalum were the same element. This conclusion was disputed in 1846 by the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rose" title="Heinrich Rose">Heinrich Rose</a>, who argued that there were two additional elements in the tantalite sample, and he named them after the children of <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>: niobium (from <a href="/wiki/Niobe" title="Niobe">Niobe</a>, the goddess of tears), and pelopium (from <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelop_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelop-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The supposed element "pelopium" was later identified as a mixture of tantalum and niobium, and it was found that the niobium was identical to the columbium already discovered in 1801 by Hatchett.
</p><p>The differences between tantalum and niobium were demonstrated unequivocally in 1864 by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wilhelm_Blomstrand" title="Christian Wilhelm Blomstrand">Christian Wilhelm Blomstrand</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilmen_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ilmen-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henri_Etienne_Sainte-Claire_Deville" title="Henri Etienne Sainte-Claire Deville">Henri Etienne Sainte-Claire Deville</a>, as well as by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_J._Troost" title="Louis J. Troost">Louis J. Troost</a>, who determined the empirical formulas of some of their compounds in 1865.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ilmen_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ilmen-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> Further confirmation came from the Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in 1866, who proved that there were only two elements. These discoveries did not stop scientists from publishing articles about the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Ilmenium" title="Ilmenium">ilmenium</a></i> until 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> De Marignac was the first to produce the metallic form of tantalum in 1864, when he <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> tantalum chloride by heating it in an atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nauti_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-nauti-17">[17]</a></sup> Early investigators had only been able to produce impure tantalum, and the first relatively pure ductile metal was produced by <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Bolton" title="Werner von Bolton">Werner von Bolton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charlottenburg" title="Charlottenburg">Charlottenburg</a> in 1903. Wires made with metallic tantalum were used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulb</a> filaments until <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> replaced it in widespread use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The name tantalum was derived from the name of the mythological <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>, the father of <a href="/wiki/Niobe" title="Niobe">Niobe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. In the story, he had been punished after death by being condemned to stand knee-deep in water with perfect fruit growing above his head, both of which eternally <i>tantalized</i> him. (If he bent to drink the water, it drained below the level he could reach, and if he reached for the fruit, the branches moved out of his grasp.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Anders Ekeberg wrote "This metal I call <i>tantalum</i> ... partly in allusion to its incapacity, when immersed in acid, to absorb any and be saturated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>For decades, the commercial technology for separating tantalum from niobium involved the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_heptafluorotantalate" title="Potassium heptafluorotantalate">potassium heptafluorotantalate</a> away from potassium oxypentafluoroniobate monohydrate, a process that was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a> in 1866. This method has been supplanted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvent_extraction" title="Solvent extraction">solvent extraction</a> from fluoride-containing solutions of tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum is dark (blue-gray),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> dense, ductile, very hard, easily fabricated, and highly conductive of heat and electricity. The metal is renowned for its resistance to <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>; in fact, at temperatures below 150 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> tantalum is almost completely immune to attack by the normally aggressive <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>. It can be dissolved with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> or acidic solutions containing the <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> ion and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a>, as well as with a solution of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>. Tantalum's high melting point of 3017 °C (boiling point 5458 °C) is exceeded among the elements only by <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> for metals, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.
</p><p>Tantalum exists in two crystalline phases, alpha and beta. The alpha phase is relatively <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a> and soft; it has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> <i>Im3m</i>, lattice constant <i>a</i> = 0.33058 nm), <a href="/wiki/Knoop_hardness_test" title="Knoop hardness test">Knoop hardness</a> 200–400 HN and electrical resistivity 15–60 µΩ⋅cm. The beta phase is hard and brittle; its crystal symmetry is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> (space group <i>P42/mnm</i>, <i>a</i> = 1.0194 nm, <i>c</i> = 0.5313 nm), Knoop hardness is 1000–1300 HN and electrical resistivity is relatively high at 170–210 µΩ⋅cm. The beta phase is metastable and converts to the alpha phase upon heating to 750–775 °C. Bulk tantalum is almost entirely alpha phase, and the beta phase usually exists as thin films obtained by magnetron
<a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">sputtering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemical deposition</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Eutectic_system" title="Eutectic system">eutectic</a> molten salt solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_tantalum" title="Isotopes of tantalum">Isotopes of tantalum</a></div>
<p>Natural tantalum consists of two <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: <sup>180m</sup>Ta (0.012%) and <sup>181</sup>Ta (99.988%). <sup>181</sup>Ta is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotope</a>. <sup>180m</sup>Ta (<i>m</i> denotes a metastable state) is predicted to decay in three ways: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomeric_transition" title="Isomeric transition">isomeric transition</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> of <sup>180</sup>Ta, <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> to <sup>180</sup><a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">W</a>, or electron capture to <sup>180</sup><a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">Hf</a>. However, radioactivity of this <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> has never been observed, and only a lower limit on its <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 2.0 × 10<sup>16</sup> years has been set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The ground state of <sup>180</sup>Ta has a half-life of only 8 hours. <sup>180m</sup>Ta is the only naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomer</a> (excluding radiogenic and cosmogenic short-living nuclides). It is also the rarest isotope in the Universe, taking into account the elemental abundance of tantalum and isotopic abundance of <sup>180m</sup>Ta in the natural mixture of isotopes (and again excluding radiogenic and cosmogenic short-living nuclides).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tantalum has been examined theoretically as a "<a href="/wiki/Salted_bomb" title="Salted bomb">salting</a>" material for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> is the better-known hypothetical salting material). An external shell of <sup>181</sup>Ta would be irradiated by the intensive high-energy neutron flux from a hypothetical exploding nuclear weapon. This would transmute the tantalum into the radioactive isotope <sup>182</sup>Ta, which has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 114.4 days and produces <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> with approximately 1.12 million electron-volts (MeV) of energy apiece, which would significantly increase the radioactivity of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear fallout</a> from the explosion for several months. Such "salted" weapons have never been built or tested, as far as is publicly known, and certainly never used as weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tantalum can be used as a target material for accelerated proton beams for the production of various short-lived isotopes including <sup>8</sup>Li, <sup>80</sup>Rb, and <sup>160</sup>Yb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">Chemical compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tantalum forms compounds in oxidation states -III to V. Most commonly encountered are oxides of Ta(V), which includes all minerals.  The chemical properties of Ta and Nb are very similar.
</p>
<h3><span id="Oxides.2C_nitrides.2C_carbides.2C_sulfides"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides,_nitrides,_carbides,_sulfides">Oxides, nitrides, carbides, sulfides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oxides, nitrides, carbides, sulfides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tantalum_pentoxide" title="Tantalum pentoxide">Tantalum pentoxide</a> (Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) is the most important compound from the perspective of applications. Oxides of tantalum in lower oxidation states are numerous, including many <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Defect_structures&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Defect structures (page does not exist)">defect structures</a>, are lightly studied or poorly characterized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tantalates, compounds containing [TaO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>3-</sup> or [TaO<sub>3</sub>]<sup>−</sup> are numerous. <a href="/wiki/Lithium_tantalate" title="Lithium tantalate">Lithium tantalate</a> (LiTaO<sub>3</sub>) adopts a  perovskite structure. <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">Lanthanum</a> tantalate (LaTaO<sub>4</sub>) contains isolated <span class="chemf nowrap">TaO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> tetrahedra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>As in the cases of other <a href="/wiki/Refractory_metals" title="Refractory metals">refractory metals</a>, the hardest known compounds of tantalum are nitrides and carbides. <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_carbide" title="Tantalum carbide">Tantalum carbide</a>, TaC, like the more commonly used <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a>, is a hard ceramic that is used in cutting tools. Tantalum(III) nitride is used as a thin film insulator in some microelectronic fabrication processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The best studied chalcogenide is TaS<sub>2</sub>, a layered <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>, as seen for other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metal_dichalcogenide" title="Transition metal dichalcogenide">transition metal dichalcogenides</a>.   A tantalum-tellurium alloy forms <a href="/wiki/Quasicrystal" title="Quasicrystal">quasicrystals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum halides span the oxidation states of +5, +4, and +3.  Tantalum pentafluoride (TaF<sub>5</sub>) is a white solid with a melting point of 97.0 °C. The anion [TaF<sub>7</sub>]<sup>2-</sup> is used for its separation from niobium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-30">[30]</a></sup>  The chloride <span class="chemf nowrap">TaCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>, which exists as a dimer, is the main reagent in synthesis of new Ta compounds. It hydrolyzes readily to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxychloride" title="Oxychloride">oxychloride</a>. The lower halides <span class="chemf nowrap">TaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">TaX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, feature Ta-Ta bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aguly_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aguly-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organotantalum_compounds">Organotantalum compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organotantalum compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Organotantalum_chemistry" title="Organotantalum chemistry">Organotantalum compounds</a> include <a href="/wiki/Pentamethyltantalum" title="Pentamethyltantalum">pentamethyltantalum</a>, mixed alkyltantalum chlorides, alkyltantalum hydrides, alkylidene complexes as well as cyclopentadienyl derivatives of the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrock_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schrock-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>  Diverse salts and substituted derivatives are known for the hexacarbonyl [Ta(CO)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>−</sup> and related <a href="/wiki/Isocyanide" title="Isocyanide">isocyanides</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tantalite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="700" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tantalite.jpg/220px-Tantalite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tantalite.jpg/330px-Tantalite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tantalite.jpg/440px-Tantalite.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tantalite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Tantalite</b>, <a href="/wiki/Pilbara" title="Pilbara">Pilbara district</a>, Australia</div></div></div>
<p>Tantalum is estimated to make up about 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-34">[34]</a></sup> or 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aguly_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aguly-31">[31]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">Earth's crust by weight</a>. There are many species of tantalum minerals, only some of which are so far being used by industry as raw materials: <a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a> (a series consisting of tantalite-(Fe), tantalite-(Mn) and tantalite-(Mg)) <a href="/wiki/Microlite" title="Microlite">microlite</a> (now a group name), <a href="/wiki/Wodginite" title="Wodginite">wodginite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> (actually euxenite-(Y)), and <a href="/wiki/Polycrase" title="Polycrase">polycrase</a> (actually polycrase-(Y)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mindat.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mindat.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Tantalite (<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Mn</a>)Ta<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>6</sub> is the most important mineral for tantalum extraction. Tantalite has the same mineral structure as <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Mn</a>) (Ta, <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">Nb</a>)<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>6</sub>; when there is more tantalum than niobium it is called tantalite and when there is more niobium than tantalum is it called columbite (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niobite" title="Niobite">niobite</a>). The high density of tantalite and other tantalum containing minerals makes the use of <a href="/wiki/Gravity_separation" title="Gravity separation">gravitational separation</a> the best method. Other minerals include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarskite" title="Samarskite">samarskite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fergusonite" title="Fergusonite">fergusonite</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg"><img alt="Grey and white world map with China, Australia, Brazil and Kongo colored blue representing less than 10% of the tantalum world production each and Rwanda colored in green representing 60% of tantalum world production" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg/310px-World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg/465px-World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg/620px-World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Tantalum_Production_2015.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tantalum producers in 2015 with Rwanda being the main producer</div></div></div>
<p>The primary mining of tantalum is in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, where the largest producer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Advanced_Metals" title="Global Advanced Metals">Global Advanced Metals</a>, formerly known as <a href="/wiki/Talison_Minerals" title="Talison Minerals">Talison Minerals</a>, operates two mines in Western Australia, <a href="/wiki/Greenbushes,_Western_Australia" title="Greenbushes, Western Australia">Greenbushes</a> in the Southwest and <a href="/wiki/Wodgina_mine" title="Wodgina mine">Wodgina</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pilbara" title="Pilbara">Pilbara</a> region. The Wodgina mine was reopened in January 2011 after mining at the site was suspended in late-2008 due to the global financial crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Less than a year after it reopened, Global Advanced Metals announced that due to again "... softening tantalum demand ...", and other factors, tantalum mining operations were to cease at the end of February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wodgina-tant-closed_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wodgina-tant-closed-36">[36]</a></sup> Wodgina produces a primary tantalum concentrate which is further upgraded at the Greenbushes operation before being sold to customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talison_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talison-37">[37]</a></sup> Whereas the large-scale producers of niobium are in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the ore there also yields a small percentage of tantalum. Some other countries such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a> mine ores with a higher percentage of tantalum, and they produce a significant percentage of the world's output of it. Tantalum is also produced in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> as a by-product of the <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> mining there. During gravitational separation of the ores from placer deposits, not only is <a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a> (SnO<sub>2</sub>) found, but a small percentage of tantalite also included. The slag from the tin smelters then contains economically useful amounts of tantalum, which is leached from the slag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2006_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2006-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg"><img alt="Grey and white world map with Canada, Brazil and Mozambique colored blue representing less than 20% of the tantalum world production each and Australia colored in green representing 60% of tantalum world production" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg/310px-World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg/465px-World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg/620px-World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Tantalum_Production_2006.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tantalum producers in 2006 with Australia being the main producer</div></div></div>
<p>World tantalum mine production has undergone an important geographic shift since the start of the 21st century when production was predominantly from Australia and Brazil. Beginning in 2007 and through 2014, the major sources of tantalum production from mines dramatically shifted to the DRC, Rwanda, and some other African countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Future sources of supply of tantalum, in order of estimated size, are being explored in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mining_Journal_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mining_Journal-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is estimated that there are less than 50 years left of tantalum resources, based on extraction at current rates, demonstrating the need for increased <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_as_a_conflict_resource">Status as a conflict resource</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Status as a conflict resource">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coltan_mining_and_ethics" title="Coltan mining and ethics">Coltan mining and ethics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coltan#Ethics_of_mining_in_the_Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="Coltan">Coltan § Ethics of mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo</a></div>
<p>Tantalum is considered a <a href="/wiki/Conflict_resource" title="Conflict resource">conflict resource</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">Coltan</a>, the industrial name for a <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a>–<a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a> mineral from which niobium and tantalum are extracted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>, which is why tantalum is being linked to <a href="/wiki/Second_Congo_War" title="Second Congo War">warfare in the Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>). According to an October 23, 2003 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> report,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> the smuggling and exportation of coltan has helped fuel the war in the Congo, a crisis that has resulted in approximately 5.4 million deaths since 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> – making it the world’s deadliest documented conflict since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Ethical questions have been raised about responsible corporate behavior, human rights, and endangering wildlife, due to the exploitation of resources such as coltan in the armed conflict regions of the Congo Basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> However, although important for the local economy in Congo, the contribution of coltan mining in Congo to the world supply of tantalum is usually small. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> reports in its yearbook that this region produced a little less than 1% of the world's tantalum output in 2002–2006, peaking at 10% in 2000 and 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2006_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2006-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stated aim of the <i>Solutions for Hope Tantalum Project</i> is to "source conflict-free tantalum from the Democratic Republic of Congo"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_fabrication">Production and fabrication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Production and fabrication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tantalum_world_production.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="460" data-file-width="750" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tantalum_world_production.svg/220px-Tantalum_world_production.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tantalum_world_production.svg/330px-Tantalum_world_production.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Tantalum_world_production.svg/440px-Tantalum_world_production.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tantalum_world_production.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Time trend of tantalum production until 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Several steps are involved in the extraction of tantalum from tantalite. First, the mineral is <a href="/wiki/Crusher" title="Crusher">crushed</a> and concentrated by <a href="/wiki/Gravity_separation" title="Gravity separation">gravity separation</a>. This is generally carried out near the <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mine</a> site.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refining">Refining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Refining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The refining of tantalum from its ores is one of the more demanding separation processes in industrial metallurgy.  The chief problem is that tantalum ores contain significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, which has chemical properties almost identical to those of Ta. A large number of procedures have been developed to address this challenge.
</p><p>In modern times, the separation is achieved by <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">hydrometallurgy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chang_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chang-52">[52]</a></sup> Extraction begins with <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(metallurgy)" title="Leaching (metallurgy)">leaching</a> the ore with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> together with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>. This step allows the tantalum and niobium to be separated from the various non-metallic impurities in the rock.  Although Ta occurs as various minerals, it is conveniently represented as the pentoxide, since most oxides of tantalum(V) behave similarly under these conditions.  A simplified equation for its extraction is thus:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 14 HF   →  2 H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Completely analogous reactions occur for the niobium component, but the hexafluoride is typically predominant under the conditions of the extraction.
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 12 HF   →   2 H[NbF<sub>6</sub>] + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>These equations are simplified: it is suspected that bisulfate (HSO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>) and chloride compete as ligands for the Nb(V) and Ta(V) ions, when sulfuric and hydrochloric acids are used, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chang_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chang-52">[52]</a></sup>  The tantalum and niobium fluoride complexes are then removed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aqueous</a> solution by <a href="/wiki/Liquid%E2%80%93liquid_extraction" title="Liquid–liquid extraction">liquid-liquid extraction</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvents" title="Organic solvents">organic solvents</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octanol" title="Octanol">octanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isobutyl_ketone" title="Methyl isobutyl ketone">methyl isobutyl ketone</a>. This simple procedure allows the removal of most metal-containing impurities (e.g. iron, manganese, titanium, zirconium), which remain in the aqueous phase in the form of their <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluorides</a> and other complexes.
</p><p>Separation of the tantalum <i>from</i> niobium is then achieved by lowering the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_strength" title="Ionic strength">ionic strength</a> of the acid mixture, which causes the niobium to dissolve in the aqueous phase.  It is proposed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyfluoride" title="Oxyfluoride">oxyfluoride</a> H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] is formed under these conditions.  Subsequent to removal of the niobium, the solution of purified H<sub>2</sub>TaF<sub>7</sub>] is neutralised with aqueous <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to precipitate hydrated tantalum oxide as a solid, which can be <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcined</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_pentoxide" title="Tantalum pentoxide">tantalum pentoxide</a> (Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Instead of hydrolysis, the H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] can be treated with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_fluoride" title="Potassium fluoride">potassium fluoride</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Potassium_heptafluorotantalate" title="Potassium heptafluorotantalate">potassium heptafluorotantalate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>]  + 2 KF   →   K<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] + 2 HF</dd></dl>
<p>Unlike H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>], the potassium salt is readily crystallized and handled as a solid.
</p><p>K<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] can be converted to metallic tantalum by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduction-oxidation" title="Reduction-oxidation">reduction</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, at approximately 800 °C in <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt" title="Molten salt">molten salt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>K<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>]  +  5 Na   →   Ta + 5 NaF + 2 KF</dd></dl>
<p>In an older method, called the <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Marignac</a> process, the mixture of H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] and H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] was converted to a <i>mixture</i> of  K<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] and K<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>], which was then be separated by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a>, exploiting their different water solubilities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolysis">Electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/FFC_Cambridge_process" title="FFC Cambridge process">FFC Cambridge process</a></div>
<p>Electrolysis using a modified version of the <a href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall–Héroult process">Hall–Héroult process</a>. Instead of requiring the input oxide and output metal to be in liquid form, tantalum electrolysis operates on non-liquid powdered oxides. The initial discovery came in 1997 when Cambridge University researchers immersed small samples of certain oxides in baths of molten salt and reduced the oxide with electric current. The cathode uses powdered metal oxide. The anode is made of carbon. The molten salt at 1,000 °C (1,830 °F) is the electrolyte. The first refinery has enough capacity to supply 3–4% of annual global demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabrication_and_metalworking">Fabrication and metalworking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fabrication and metalworking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> of tantalum must be done in an inert atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> in order to shield it from contamination with atmospheric gases. Tantalum is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solderable" title="Solderable">solderable</a>. Grinding tantalum is difficult, especially so for <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealed</a> tantalum. In the annealed condition, tantalum is extremely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> and can be readily formed as metal sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tantal-Perle-Wiki-07-02-25-P1040364b.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="313" data-file-width="236" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Tantal-Perle-Wiki-07-02-25-P1040364b.jpg/170px-Tantal-Perle-Wiki-07-02-25-P1040364b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fe/Tantal-Perle-Wiki-07-02-25-P1040364b.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tantal-Perle-Wiki-07-02-25-P1040364b.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tantalum electrolytic capacitor</div></div></div>
<p>The major use for tantalum, as the metal powder, is in the production of electronic components, mainly <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a> and some high-power <a href="/wiki/Resistor" title="Resistor">resistors</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_capacitor" title="Tantalum capacitor">Tantalum electrolytic capacitors</a> exploit the tendency of tantalum to form a protective <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> surface layer, using tantalum powder, pressed into a pellet shape, as one "plate" of the capacitor, the oxide as the <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a>, and an electrolytic solution or conductive solid as the other "plate". Because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_static_permittivity" title="Relative static permittivity">dielectric layer</a> can be very thin (thinner than the similar layer in, for instance, an aluminium electrolytic capacitor), a high <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a> can be achieved in a small volume. Because of the size and weight advantages, tantalum capacitors are attractive for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portable_telephone" title="Portable telephone">portable telephones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_electronics" title="Automotive electronics">automotive electronics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cameras" title="Cameras">cameras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCR08_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCR08-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum is also used to produce a variety of <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> that have high melting points, strength, and ductility. Alloyed with other metals, it is also used in making carbide tools for metalworking equipment and in the production of <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> for jet engine components, chemical process equipment, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>, missile parts, heat exchangers, tanks, and vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCR08_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCR08-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Because of its ductility, tantalum can be drawn into fine wires or filaments, which are used for evaporating metals such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>. Since it resists attack by body fluids and is nonirritating, tantalum is widely used in making surgical instruments and implants. For example, porous tantalum coatings are used in the construction of orthopedic implants due to tantalum's ability to form a direct bond to hard tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tantalum is inert against most acids except <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and hot <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, and hot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> solutions also cause tantalum to corrode. This property makes it a useful metal for chemical reaction vessels and pipes for corrosive liquids. Heat exchanging coils for the steam heating of hydrochloric acid are made from tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balke_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Balke-61">[61]</a></sup> Tantalum was extensively used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">ultra high frequency</a> <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">electron tubes</a> for radio transmitters. Tantalum is capable of capturing oxygen and nitrogen by forming nitrides and oxides and therefore helped to sustain the high vacuum needed for the tubes when used for internal parts such as grids and plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICE-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Balke_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Balke-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tantalio.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="604" data-file-width="1219" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Tantalio.png/320px-Tantalio.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Tantalio.png/480px-Tantalio.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Tantalio.png/640px-Tantalio.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tantalio.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bi-metallic_coin" title="Bi-metallic coin">Bimetallic</a> coins minted by the Bank of <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> with silver ring and tantalum center.</div></div></div>
<p>The high melting point and oxidation resistance lead to the use of the metal in the production of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_furnace" title="Vacuum furnace">vacuum furnace</a> parts. Tantalum is extremely inert and is therefore formed into a variety of corrosion resistant parts, such as <a href="/wiki/Thermowell" title="Thermowell">thermowells</a>, valve bodies, and tantalum fasteners. Due to its high density, <a href="/wiki/Shaped_charge" title="Shaped charge">shaped charge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explosively_formed_penetrator" title="Explosively formed penetrator">explosively formed penetrator</a> liners have been constructed from tantalum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Tantalum greatly increases the armor penetration capabilities of a shaped charge due to its high density and high melting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>  It is also occasionally used in precious <a href="/wiki/Watch" title="Watch">watches</a> e.g. from <a href="/wiki/Audemars_Piguet" title="Audemars Piguet">Audemars Piguet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F.P._Journe" title="F.P. Journe">F.P. Journe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hublot" title="Hublot">Hublot</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montblanc_(pens)" title="Montblanc (pens)">Montblanc</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega_Watches" title="Omega Watches">Omega</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panerai" title="Panerai">Panerai</a>. Tantalum is also highly bioinert and is used as an orthopedic implant material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerald_L._Burke_1940_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerald_L._Burke_1940-65">[65]</a></sup> The high stiffness of tantalum makes it necessary to use it as highly porous foam or scaffold with lower stiffness for hip replacement implants to avoid <a href="/wiki/Stress_shielding" title="Stress shielding">stress shielding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Because tantalum is a non-ferrous, non-magnetic metal, these implants are considered to be acceptable for patients undergoing MRI procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PaganiasTsakotos2012_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-PaganiasTsakotos2012-67">[67]</a></sup> The oxide is used to make special high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> for <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">camera</a> lenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tantalum receives far less attention in the environmental field than it does in other geosciences. Upper Crust Concentrations (UCC) and the Nb/Ta ratio in the upper crust and in minerals are available because these measurements are useful as a geochemical tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The latest values for UCC and the Nb/Ta(w/w) ratio in the upper crust stand at 0.92 ppm and 12.7 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Little data is available on tantalum concentrations in the different environmental compartments, especially in natural waters where reliable estimates of ‘dissolved’ tantalum concentrations in seawater and freshwaters have not even been produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-71">[71]</a></sup> Some values on dissolved concentrations in oceans have been published, but they are contradictory. Values in freshwaters fare little better, but, in all cases, they are probably below 1 ng L<sup>−1,</sup>since ‘dissolved’ concentrations in natural waters are well below most current analytical capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Analysis requires pre-concentration procedures that, for the moment, do not give consistent results. And in any case, tantalum appears to be present in natural waters mostly as particulate matter rather than dissolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Values for concentrations in soils, bed sediments and atmospheric aerosols are easier to come by.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-71">[71]</a></sup> Values in soils are close to 1 ppm and thus to UCC values. This indicates detrital origin. For atmospheric aerosols the values available are scattered and limited. When tantalum enrichment is observed, it is probably due to loss of more water-soluble elements in aerosols in the clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pollution linked to human use of the element has not been detected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Tantalum appears to be a very conservative element in biogeochemical terms, but its cycling and reactivity are still not fully understood.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compounds containing tantalum are rarely encountered in the laboratory. The metal is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocompatible" title="Biocompatible">biocompatible</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerald_L._Burke_1940_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gerald_L._Burke_1940-65">[65]</a></sup> and is used for body <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">implants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a>, therefore attention may be focused on other elements or the physical nature of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to tantalum in the workplace by breathing it in, skin contact, or eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for tantalum exposure in the workplace as 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday and a short-term limit of 10 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. At levels of 2500 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, tantalum is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
