<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Birth control</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Birth control</b>, also known as <b>contraception</b> and <b>fertility control</b>, is a method or device used to prevent <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Birth control has been used since ancient times, but effective and safe methods of birth control only became available in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup> Planning, making available, and using birth control is called <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED2012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO-health-topic_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-health-topic-4">[4]</a></sup> Some cultures limit or discourage access to birth control because they consider it to be morally, religiously, or politically undesirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most effective methods of birth control are <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(medicine)" title="Sterilization (medicine)">sterilization</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Vasectomy" title="Vasectomy">vasectomy</a> in males and <a href="/wiki/Tubal_ligation" title="Tubal ligation">tubal ligation</a> in females, <a href="/wiki/Intrauterine_device" title="Intrauterine device">intrauterine devices</a> (IUDs), and <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_implant" title="Contraceptive implant">implantable birth control</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup> This is followed by a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormonal_contraceptive" title="Hormonal contraceptive">hormone-based methods</a> including <a href="/wiki/Combined_oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Combined oral contraceptive pill">oral pills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_patch" title="Contraceptive patch">patches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_ring" title="Vaginal ring">vaginal rings</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injectable_contraceptives" title="Injectable contraceptives">injections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup> Less effective methods include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_contraception" title="Barrier contraception">physical barriers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condoms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(contraceptive)" title="Diaphragm (contraceptive)">diaphragms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_sponge" title="Contraceptive sponge">birth control sponges</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_awareness_method" title="Fertility awareness method">fertility awareness methods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup> The least effective methods are <a href="/wiki/Spermicide" title="Spermicide">spermicides</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Withdrawal_method" title="Withdrawal method">withdrawal by the male before ejaculation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup> Sterilization, while highly effective, is not usually reversible; all other methods are reversible, most immediately upon stopping them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Safe_sex" title="Safe sex">Safe sex</a> practices, such as with the use of male or <a href="/wiki/Female_condom" title="Female condom">female condoms</a>, can also help prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infections" title="Sexually transmitted infections">sexually transmitted infections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22423463_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22423463-6">[6]</a></sup> Other methods of birth control do not protect against sexually transmitted diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22341164_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22341164-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_contraceptive" title="Emergency contraceptive">Emergency birth control</a> can prevent pregnancy if taken within the 72 to 120 hours after unprotected sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gizzo2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gizzo2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some argue <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abstinence" title="Sexual abstinence">not having sex</a> as a form of birth control, but <a href="/wiki/Abstinence-only_sex_education" title="Abstinence-only sex education">abstinence-only sex education</a> may increase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancies" title="Teenage pregnancies">teenage pregnancies</a> if offered without birth control education, due to non-compliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12065267_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12065267-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18923389_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18923389-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancy" title="Teenage pregnancy">teenagers</a>, pregnancies are at greater risk of poor outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22764559_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22764559-12">[12]</a></sup> Comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a> and access to birth control decreases the rate of unwanted pregnancies in this age group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22764559_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22764559-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22764557_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22764557-13">[13]</a></sup> While all forms of birth control can generally be used by young people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p260_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p260-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-acting_reversible_birth_control" title="Long-acting reversible birth control">long-acting reversible birth control</a> such as implants, IUDs, or vaginal rings are more successful in reducing rates of teenage pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22764557_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22764557-13">[13]</a></sup> After the delivery of a child, a woman who is not exclusively breastfeeding may become pregnant again after as few as four to six weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p260_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p260-14">[14]</a></sup> Some methods of birth control can be started immediately following the birth, while others require a delay of up to six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p260_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p260-14">[14]</a></sup> In women who are breastfeeding, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progestogen_only_contraception" title="Progestogen only contraception">progestin-only methods</a> are preferred over <a href="/wiki/Combined_oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Combined oral contraceptive pill">combined oral birth control pills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p260_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p260-14">[14]</a></sup> In women who have reached <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a>, it is recommended that birth control be continued for one year after the last period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p260_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p260-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>About 222 million women who want to avoid pregnancy in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> are not using a modern birth control method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guttmacher2012_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guttmacher2012-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784540_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784540-16">[16]</a></sup> Birth control use in developing countries has decreased the number of <a href="/wiki/Maternal_death" title="Maternal death">deaths during or around the time of pregnancy</a> by 40% (about 270,000 deaths prevented in 2008) and could prevent 70% if the full demand for birth control were met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784531_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784531-18">[18]</a></sup> By lengthening the time between pregnancies, birth control can improve adult women's delivery outcomes and the survival of their children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup> In the developing world women's earnings, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assets" title="Assets">assets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Body_mass_index" title="Body mass index">weight</a>, and their children's schooling and health all improve with greater access to birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784535_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784535-19">[19]</a></sup> Birth control increases <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> because of fewer dependent children, more women participating in the <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">workforce</a>, and less use of scarce resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784535_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784535-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784542_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784542-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Birth control methods include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_methods" title="Barrier methods">barrier methods</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormonal_birth_control" title="Hormonal birth control">hormonal birth control</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrauterine_devices" title="Intrauterine devices">intrauterine devices</a> (IUDs), <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(medicine)" title="Sterilization (medicine)">sterilization</a>, and behavioral methods. They are used before or during sex while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_contraceptive" title="Emergency contraceptive">emergency contraceptives</a> are effective for up to five days after sex. Effectiveness is generally expressed as the percentage of women who become pregnant using a given method during the first year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and sometimes as a lifetime failure rate among methods with high effectiveness, such as <a href="/wiki/Tubal_ligation" title="Tubal ligation">tubal ligation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dew2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dew2012-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most effective methods are those that are long acting and do not require ongoing health care visits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> Surgical sterilization, implantable hormones, and intrauterine devices all have first-year failure rates of less than 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> Hormonal contraceptive pills, patches or vaginal rings, and the <a href="/wiki/Lactational_amenorrhea" title="Lactational amenorrhea">lactational amenorrhea method</a> (LAM), if adhered to strictly, can also have first-year (or for LAM, first-6-month) failure rates of less than 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> With typical use, first-year failure rates are considerably high, at 9%, due to inconsistent use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> Other methods such as condoms, diaphragms, and spermicides have higher first-year failure rates even with perfect usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Pediatrics" title="American Academy of Pediatrics">American Academy of Pediatrics</a> recommends <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_acting_reversible_birth_control" title="Long acting reversible birth control">long acting reversible birth control</a> as first line for young individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>While all methods of birth control have some potential adverse effects, the risk is less than that of <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> After stopping or removing many methods of birth control, including oral contraceptives, IUDs, implants and injections, the rate of pregnancy during the subsequent year is the same as for those who used no birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>For individuals with specific health problems, certain forms of birth control may require further investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2009_10_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2009_10-30">[30]</a></sup> For women who are otherwise healthy, many methods of birth control should not require a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_exam" title="Medical exam">medical exam</a>—including birth control pills, injectable or implantable birth control, and condoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> For example, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_exam" title="Pelvic exam">pelvic exam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breast_exam" title="Breast exam">breast exam</a>, or blood test before starting birth control pills does not appear to affect outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p10_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p10-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) published a detailed list of medical eligibility criteria for each type of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2009_10_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2009_10-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hormonal">Hormonal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hormonal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hormonal_contraception" title="Hormonal contraception">Hormonal contraception</a> is available in a number of different forms, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oral_contraceptive" title="Oral contraceptive">oral pills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_implant" title="Contraceptive implant">implants</a> under the skin, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injectable_contraceptives" title="Injectable contraceptives">injections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_patch" title="Contraceptive patch">patches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormonal_IUD" title="Hormonal IUD">IUDs</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_ring" title="Vaginal ring">vaginal ring</a>. They are currently available only for women, although hormonal contraceptives for men have been and are being clinically tested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There are two types of oral birth control pills, the <a href="/wiki/Combined_oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Combined oral contraceptive pill">combined oral contraceptive pills</a> (which contain both <a href="/wiki/Estrogen_(medication)" title="Estrogen (medication)">estrogen</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Progestin" title="Progestin">progestin</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Progestogen-only_pill" title="Progestogen-only pill">progestogen-only pills</a> (sometimes called minipills).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ammer_2009a_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ammer_2009a-36">[36]</a></sup>  If either is taken during pregnancy, they do not increase the risk of <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a> nor cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birth_defects" title="Birth defects">birth defects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p10_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p10-33">[33]</a></sup> Both types of birth control pills prevent <a href="/wiki/Human_fertilization" title="Human fertilization">fertilization</a> mainly by inhibiting <a href="/wiki/Ovulation" title="Ovulation">ovulation</a> and thickening cervical mucus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_2011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson_2011-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2011_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2011-38">[38]</a></sup> They may also change the lining of the uterus and thus decrease implantation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2011_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2011-38">[38]</a></sup> Their effectiveness depends on the user's adherence to taking the pills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p10_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p10-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Combined hormonal contraceptives are associated with a slightly increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Venous_thrombosis" title="Venous thrombosis">venous</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_thrombosis" title="Arterial thrombosis">arterial blood clots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review2011_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Review2011-39">[39]</a></sup> Venous clots, on average, increase from 2.8 to 9.8 per 10,000 women years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> which is still less than that associated with pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review2011_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Review2011-39">[39]</a></sup> Due to this risk, they are not recommended in women over 35 years of age who continue to smoke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Due to the increased risk, they are included in decision tools such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DASH_score" title="DASH score">DASH score</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PERC_rule" title="PERC rule">PERC rule</a> used to predict the risk of blood clots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effect on sexual desire is varied, with increase or decrease in some but with no effect in most.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Combined oral contraceptives reduce the risk of <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endometrial_cancer" title="Endometrial cancer">endometrial cancer</a> and do not change the risk of <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shulman2011_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shulman2011-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> They often reduce menstrual bleeding and <a href="/wiki/Dysmenorrhea" title="Dysmenorrhea">painful menstruation cramps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011p10_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011p10-33">[33]</a></sup> The lower doses of estrogen released from the vaginal ring may reduce the risk of breast tenderness, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, and headache associated with higher dose estrogen products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shulman2011_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shulman2011-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Progestin-only pills, injections and intrauterine devices are not associated with an increased risk of blood clots and may be used by women with a history of blood clots in their veins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review2011_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Review2011-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In those with a history of arterial blood clots, non-hormonal birth control or a progestin-only method other than the injectable version should be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review2011_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Review2011-39">[39]</a></sup> Progestin-only pills may improve menstrual symptoms and can be used by breastfeeding women as they do not affect <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">milk production</a>. Irregular bleeding may occur with progestin-only methods, with some users reporting <a href="/wiki/Amenorrhea" title="Amenorrhea">no periods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21961819_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21961819-47">[47]</a></sup> The progestins <a href="/wiki/Drospirenone" title="Drospirenone">drospirenone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desogestrel" title="Desogestrel">desogestrel</a> minimize the <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgenic</a> side effects but increase the risks of blood clots and are thus not first line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The perfect use first-year failure rate of <a href="/wiki/Medroxyprogesterone_acetate" title="Medroxyprogesterone acetate">injectable progestin</a> is 0.2%; the typical use first failure rate is 6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barrier">Barrier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Barrier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_contraceptive" title="Barrier contraceptive">Barrier contraceptives</a> are devices that attempt to prevent <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> by physically preventing <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a> from entering the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neinstein2008_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neinstein2008-49">[49]</a></sup> They include male <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Female_condom" title="Female condom">female condoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cap" title="Cervical cap">cervical caps</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(contraceptive)" title="Diaphragm (contraceptive)">diaphragms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive_sponge" title="Contraceptive sponge">contraceptive sponges</a> with <a href="/wiki/Spermicide" title="Spermicide">spermicide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neinstein2008_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neinstein2008-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Globally, condoms are the most common method of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_condoms" title="Male condoms">Male condoms</a> are put on a man's erect <a href="/wiki/Human_penis" title="Human penis">penis</a> and physically block ejaculated sperm from entering the body of a sexual partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamil2012_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamil2012-51">[51]</a></sup> Modern condoms are most often made from <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a>, but some are made from other materials such as <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>, or lamb's intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamil2012_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hamil2012-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Female_condom" title="Female condom">Female condoms</a> are also available, most often made of <a href="/wiki/Nitrile_rubber" title="Nitrile rubber">nitrile</a>, latex or polyurethane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Male condoms have the advantage of being inexpensive, easy to use, and have few adverse effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Making condoms available to teenagers does not appear to affect the age of onset of sexual activity or its frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In Japan, about 80% of couples who are using birth control use condoms, while in Germany this number is about 25%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebe2010_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebe2010-55">[55]</a></sup> and in the United States it is 18%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Male condoms and the diaphragm with spermicide have typical use first-year failure rates of 18% and 12%, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> With perfect use condoms are more effective with a 2% first-year failure rate versus a 6% first-year rate with the diaphragm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> Condoms have the additional benefit of helping to prevent the spread of some sexually transmitted infections such as <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>, however, condoms made from animal intestine do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO_FP2011_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-WHO_FP2011-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Contraceptive sponges combine a barrier with a spermicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> Like diaphragms, they are inserted vaginally before intercourse and must be placed over the <a href="/wiki/Cervix" title="Cervix">cervix</a> to be effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> Typical failure rates during the first year depend on whether or not a woman has previously given birth, being 24% in those who have and 12% in those who have not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> The sponge can be inserted up to 24 hours before intercourse and must be left in place for at least six hours afterward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> Allergic reactions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and more severe adverse effects such as <a href="/wiki/Toxic_shock_syndrome" title="Toxic shock syndrome">toxic shock syndrome</a> have been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intrauterine_devices">Intrauterine devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intrauterine devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1019" data-file-width="2188" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg/220px-T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg/330px-T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg/440px-T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:T%C3%AAte_de_st%C3%A9rilet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Copper T shaped IUD with removal strings</div></div></div>
<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Intrauterine_device" title="Intrauterine device">intrauterine devices</a> (IUD) are small devices, often 'T'-shaped, containing either copper or <a href="/wiki/Levonorgestrel" title="Levonorgestrel">levonorgestrel</a>, which are inserted into the uterus. They are one form of <a href="/wiki/Long-acting_reversible_contraception" title="Long-acting reversible contraception">long-acting reversible contraception</a> which are the most effective types of reversible birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Failure rates with the <a href="/wiki/Copper_IUDs" title="Copper IUDs">copper IUD</a> is about 0.8% while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levonorgestrel_intrauterine_system" title="Levonorgestrel intrauterine system">levonorgestrel IUD</a> has a failure rates of 0.2% in the first year of use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010b_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010b-61">[61]</a></sup> Among types of birth control, they, along with birth control implants, result in the greatest satisfaction among users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comm2012_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comm2012-62">[62]</a></sup> As of 2007, IUDs are the most widely used form of reversible contraception, with more than 180 million users worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darney2010_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Darney2010-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Evidence supports effectiveness and safety in adolescents<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comm2012_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Comm2012-62">[62]</a></sup> and those who have and have not previously had children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> IUDs do not affect <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a> and can be inserted immediately after delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabbe2012_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gabbe2012-65">[65]</a></sup> They may also be used immediately after an <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Once removed, even after long term use, fertility returns to normal immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>While copper IUDs may increase menstrual bleeding and result in more painful cramps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimes2007_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grimes2007-68">[68]</a></sup> hormonal IUDs may reduce menstrual bleeding or stop menstruation altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabbe2012_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gabbe2012-65">[65]</a></sup> Cramping can be treated with painkillers like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NSAIDs" title="NSAIDs">non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marnach2013_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marnach2013-69">[69]</a></sup> Other potential complications include expulsion (2–5%) and rarely perforation of the uterus (less than 0.7%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabbe2012_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gabbe2012-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marnach2013_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marnach2013-69">[69]</a></sup> A previous model of the intrauterine device (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalkon_shield" title="Dalkon shield">Dalkon shield</a>) was associated with an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_inflammatory_disease" title="Pelvic inflammatory disease">pelvic inflammatory disease</a>, however the risk is not affected with current models in those without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infections" title="Sexually transmitted infections">sexually transmitted infections</a> around the time of insertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guttmacher2007_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-guttmacher2007-70">[70]</a></sup> IUDs appear to decrease the risk of <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sterilization">Sterilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sterilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sterilisation_(medicine)" title="Sterilisation (medicine)">Surgical sterilization</a> is available in the form of <a href="/wiki/Tubal_ligation" title="Tubal ligation">tubal ligation</a> for women and <a href="/wiki/Vasectomy" title="Vasectomy">vasectomy</a> for men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup> There are no significant long term side effects, and tubal ligation decreases the risk of <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup> Short term complications are twenty times less likely from a vasectomy than a tubal ligation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> After a vasectomy, there may be swelling and pain of the scrotum which usually resolves in one or two weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> With tubal ligation, complications occur in 1 to 2 percent of procedures with serious complications usually due to the <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Neither method offers protection from sexually transmitted infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>This decision may cause regret in some men and women. Of women aged over 30 who have undergone tubal ligation, about 5% regret their decision, as compared with 20% of women aged under 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup> By contrast, less than 5% of men are likely to regret sterilization. Men who are more likely to regret sterilization are younger, have young or no children, or have an unstable marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In a survey of biological parents, 9% stated they would not have had children if they were able to do it over again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although sterilization is considered a permanent procedure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deff2011_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deff2011-77">[77]</a></sup> it is possible to attempt a <a href="/wiki/Tubal_reversal" title="Tubal reversal">tubal reversal</a> to reconnect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallopian_tubes" title="Fallopian tubes">fallopian tubes</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Vasectomy_reversal" title="Vasectomy reversal">vasectomy reversal</a> to reconnect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasa_deferentia" title="Vasa deferentia">vasa deferentia</a>. In women, the desire for a reversal is often associated with a change in spouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deff2011_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deff2011-77">[77]</a></sup> Pregnancy success rates after tubal reversal are between 31 and 88 percent, with complications including an increased risk of <a href="/wiki/Ectopic_pregnancy" title="Ectopic pregnancy">ectopic pregnancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deff2011_77-2"><a href="#cite_note-Deff2011-77">[77]</a></sup> The number of males who request reversal is between 2 and 6 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shri2010_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shri2010-78">[78]</a></sup> Rates of success in fathering another child after reversal are between 38 and 84 percent; with success being lower the longer the time period between the vasectomy and the reversal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shri2010_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shri2010-78">[78]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sperm_extraction" title="Sperm extraction">Sperm extraction</a> followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilization" title="In vitro fertilization">in vitro fertilization</a> may also be an option in men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioral">Behavioral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Behavioral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Behavioral methods involve <a href="/wiki/Calendar-based_contraceptive_methods" title="Calendar-based contraceptive methods">regulating the timing</a> or method of intercourse to prevent introduction of sperm into the female reproductive tract, either altogether or when an egg may be present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grim2004_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grim2004-80">[80]</a></sup> If used perfectly the first-year failure rate may be around 3.4%, however if used poorly first-year failure rates may approach 85%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fertility_awareness">Fertility awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fertility awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geburtenkontrollkette_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="a birth control chain calendar necklace" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1486" data-file-width="2318" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Geburtenkontrollkette_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geburtenkontrollkette_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/CycleBeads" title="CycleBeads">CycleBeads</a> tool, used for estimating fertility based on days since last menstruation</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_awareness_method" title="Fertility awareness method">Fertility awareness methods</a> involve determining the most fertile days of the <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">menstrual cycle</a> and avoiding unprotected intercourse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grim2004_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grim2004-80">[80]</a></sup> Techniques for determining fertility include monitoring <a href="/wiki/Basal_body_temperature" title="Basal body temperature">basal body temperature</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervical_secretions" title="Cervical secretions">cervical secretions</a>, or the day of the cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grim2004_80-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grim2004-80">[80]</a></sup> They have typical first-year failure rates of 24%; perfect use first-year failure rates depend on which method is used and range from 0.4% to 5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> The evidence on which these estimates are based, however, is poor as the majority of people in trials stop their use early.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grim2004_80-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grim2004-80">[80]</a></sup> Globally, they are used by about 3.6% of couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sivin2010_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sivin2010-82">[82]</a></sup> If based on both basal body temperature and another primary sign, the method is referred to as symptothermal. First-year failure rates of 20% overall and 0.4% for perfect use have been reported in clinical studies of the symptothermal method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennings_2011_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jennings_2011-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> A number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_tracking" title="Fertility tracking">fertility tracking</a> apps are available, as of 2016, but they are more commonly designed to assist those trying to get pregnant rather than prevent pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Withdrawal">Withdrawal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Withdrawal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Withdrawal_method" title="Withdrawal method">withdrawal method</a> (also known as coitus interruptus) is the practice of ending intercourse ("pulling out") before ejaculation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2009_100_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2009_100-85">[85]</a></sup> The main risk of the withdrawal method is that the man may not perform the maneuver correctly or in a timely manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2009_100_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2009_100-85">[85]</a></sup> First-year failure rates vary from 4% with perfect usage to 22% with typical usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trus2011_21-9"><a href="#cite_note-Trus2011-21">[21]</a></sup> It is not considered birth control by some medical professionals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is little data regarding the sperm content of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-ejaculatory_fluid" title="Pre-ejaculatory fluid">pre-ejaculatory fluid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2009_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2009-86">[86]</a></sup> While some tentative research did not find sperm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2009_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2009-86">[86]</a></sup> one trial found sperm present in 10 out of 27 volunteers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> The withdrawal method is used as birth control by about 3% of couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sivin2010_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sivin2010-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstinence">Abstinence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Abstinence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sexual_abstinence" title="Sexual abstinence">Sexual abstinence</a> may be used as a form of birth control, meaning either not engaging in any type of sexual activity, or specifically not engaging in vaginal intercourse, while engaging in other forms of non-vaginal sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PP2009_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-PP2009-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Complete sexual abstinence is 100% effective in preventing pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> However, among those who take a <a href="/wiki/Virginity_pledge" title="Virginity pledge">pledge to abstain</a> from <a href="/wiki/Premarital_sex" title="Premarital sex">premarital sex</a>, as many as 88% who engage in sex, do so prior to marriage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Den2005_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Den2005-92">[92]</a></sup> The choice to abstain from sex cannot protect against pregnancy as a result of <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, and public health efforts emphasizing abstinence to reduce unwanted pregnancy may have limited effectiveness, especially in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> and among <a href="/wiki/Disadvantaged" title="Disadvantaged">disadvantaged groups</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Deliberate <a href="/wiki/Non-penetrative_sex" title="Non-penetrative sex">non-penetrative sex</a> without vaginal sex or deliberate <a href="/wiki/Oral_sex" title="Oral sex">oral sex</a> without vaginal sex are also sometimes considered birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PP2009_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-PP2009-88">[88]</a></sup> While this generally avoids pregnancy, pregnancy can still occur with <a href="/wiki/Intercrural_sex" title="Intercrural sex">intercrural sex</a> and other forms of penis-near-vagina sex (genital rubbing, and the penis exiting from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anal_intercourse" title="Anal intercourse">anal intercourse</a>) where sperm can be deposited near the entrance to the vagina and can travel along the vagina's lubricating fluids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Abstinence-only_sex_education" title="Abstinence-only sex education">Abstinence-only sex education</a> does not reduce <a href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancy" title="Teenage pregnancy">teenage pregnancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22341164_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22341164-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Santelli2017_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Santelli2017-97">[97]</a></sup> Teen pregnancy rates and STI rates are generally the same or higher in states where students are given abstinence-only education, as compared with <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_sex_education" title="Comprehensive sex education">comprehensive sex education</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Santelli2017_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-Santelli2017-97">[97]</a></sup> Some authorities recommend that those using abstinence as a primary method have backup methods available (such as condoms or emergency contraceptive pills).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lactation">Lactation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lactation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lactational_amenorrhea_method" title="Lactational amenorrhea method">lactational amenorrhea method</a> involves the use of a woman's natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postpartum_infertility" title="Postpartum infertility">postpartum infertility</a> which occurs after delivery and may be extended by <a href="/wiki/Breastfeeding" title="Breastfeeding">breastfeeding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blackburn2007_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blackburn2007-99">[99]</a></sup> This usually requires the presence of no <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">periods</a>, exclusively breastfeeding the infant, and a child younger than six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blenning2005_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blenning2005-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> states that if breastfeeding is the infant's only source of nutrition, the failure rate is 2% in the six months following delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> Six uncontrolled studies of lactational amenorrhea method users found failure rates at 6 months postpartum between 0% and 7.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vander2008_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vander2008-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Updated version https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26457821 (February 2017)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Failure rates increase to 4–7% at one year and 13% at two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritz2012_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fritz2012-102">[102]</a></sup> Feeding formula, pumping instead of nursing, the use of a <a href="/wiki/Pacifier" title="Pacifier">pacifier</a>, and feeding solids all increase its failure rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> In those who are exclusively breastfeeding, about 10% begin having periods before three months and 20% before six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritz2012_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fritz2012-102">[102]</a></sup> In those who are not breastfeeding, fertility may return four weeks after delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fritz2012_102-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fritz2012-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency">Emergency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Emergency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emergency_contraceptive_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="emergency contraceptive pills" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="807" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Emergency_contraceptive_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emergency_contraceptive_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A split dose of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_contraceptive" title="Emergency contraceptive">emergency contraceptive</a> pills</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_contraceptive" title="Emergency contraceptive">Emergency contraceptive</a> methods are medications (sometimes misleadingly referred to as "morning-after pills")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> or devices used after unprotected sexual intercourse with the hope of preventing pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gizzo2012_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gizzo2012-8">[8]</a></sup> They work primarily by preventing ovulation or fertilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leu2010_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leu2010-105">[105]</a></sup> They are unlikely to affect implantation, but this has not been completely excluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leu2010_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leu2010-105">[105]</a></sup> A number of options exist, including <a href="/wiki/Yuzpe_regimen" title="Yuzpe regimen">high dose birth control pills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Levonorgestrel" title="Levonorgestrel">levonorgestrel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mifepristone" title="Mifepristone">mifepristone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulipristal" title="Ulipristal">ulipristal</a> and IUDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CochEmerg2017_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-CochEmerg2017-106">[106]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Levonorgestrel" title="Levonorgestrel">Levonorgestrel</a> pills, when used within 3 days, decrease the chance of pregnancy after a single episode of unprotected sex or condom failure by 70% (resulting in a pregnancy rate of 2.2%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gizzo2012_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gizzo2012-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulipristal" title="Ulipristal">Ulipristal</a>, when used within 5 days, decreases the chance of pregnancy by about 85% (pregnancy rate 1.4%) and is more effective than levonorgestrel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gizzo2012_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gizzo2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CochEmerg2017_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-CochEmerg2017-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mifepristone" title="Mifepristone">Mifepristone</a> is also more effective than levonorgestrel, while copper IUDs are the most effective method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CochEmerg2017_106-2"><a href="#cite_note-CochEmerg2017-106">[106]</a></sup> IUDs can be inserted up to five days after intercourse and prevent about 99% of pregnancies after an episode of unprotected sex (pregnancy rate of 0.1 to 0.2%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> This makes them the most effective form of emergency contraceptive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleland2012_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleland2012-109">[109]</a></sup> In those who are <a href="/wiki/Overweight" title="Overweight">overweight</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obese" title="Obese">obese</a>, levonorgestrel is less effective and an IUD or ulipristal is recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Providing emergency contraceptive pills to women in advance does not affect rates of sexually transmitted infections, condom use, pregnancy rates, or sexual risk-taking behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17894132_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17894132-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21923590_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21923590-112">[112]</a></sup> All methods have minimal side effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CochEmerg2017_106-3"><a href="#cite_note-CochEmerg2017-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_protection">Dual protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dual protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dual protection is the use of methods that prevent both <a href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infection" title="Sexually transmitted infection">sexually transmitted infections</a> and pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> This can be with condoms either alone or along with another birth control method or by the avoidance of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">penetrative sex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cates2002_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cates2002-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>If pregnancy is a high concern, using two methods at the same time is reasonable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cates2002_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cates2002-114">[114]</a></sup> For example, two forms of birth control are recommended in those taking the anti-<a href="/wiki/Acne" title="Acne">acne</a> drug <a href="/wiki/Isotretinoin" title="Isotretinoin">isotretinoin</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-epileptic_drugs" title="Anti-epileptic drugs">anti-epileptic drugs</a> like <a href="/wiki/Carbamazepine" title="Carbamazepine">carbamazepine</a>, due to the high risk of <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a> if taken during pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta2011_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta2011-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Maternal_health" title="Maternal health">Maternal health</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg"><img alt="maternal mortality rate map" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="1800" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/290px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/435px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/580px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maternal mortality rate as of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA2010_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIA2010-118">[118]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="345" data-file-width="675" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png/290px-Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png/435px-Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png/580px-Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scatterplot_of_contraceptive_use_and_TFR_by_region_2010.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Birth control use and <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">total fertility rate</a> by region.</div></div></div>
<p>Contraceptive use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> is estimated to have decreased the number of <a href="/wiki/Maternal_death" title="Maternal death">maternal deaths</a> by 40% (about 270,000 deaths prevented in 2008) and could prevent 70% of deaths if the full demand for birth control were met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784531_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784531-18">[18]</a></sup> These benefits are achieved by reducing the number of unplanned pregnancies that subsequently result in unsafe abortions and by preventing pregnancies in those at high risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Birth control also improves child survival in the developing world by lengthening the time between pregnancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup> In this population, outcomes are worse when a mother gets pregnant within eighteen months of a previous delivery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784533_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784533-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shol2010_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shol2010-119">[119]</a></sup> Delaying another pregnancy after a <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a> however does not appear to alter risk and women are advised to attempt pregnancy in this situation whenever they are ready.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shol2010_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shol2010-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Teenage_pregnancy" title="Teenage pregnancy">Teenage pregnancies</a>, especially among younger teens, are at greater risk of adverse outcomes including <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">early birth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low_birth_weight" title="Low birth weight">low birth weight</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">death of the infant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22764559_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22764559-12">[12]</a></sup> In the United States 82% of pregnancies in those between 15 and 19 are unplanned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marnach2013_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marnach2013-69">[69]</a></sup> Comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a> and access to birth control are effective in decreasing pregnancy rates in this age group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Finances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Family_economics" title="Family economics">Family economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_raising_a_child" title="Cost of raising a child">Cost of raising a child</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1357" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png/290px-Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png/435px-Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png/580px-Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fertility_rate_world_map_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of countries by fertility rate (2018)</div></div></div>
<p>In the developing world, birth control increases <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> due to there being fewer dependent children and thus more women participating in or increased contribution to the <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">workforce</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784535_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784535-19">[19]</a></sup> Women's earnings, assets, <a href="/wiki/Body_mass_index" title="Body mass index">body mass index</a>, and their children's schooling and body mass index all improve with greater access to birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784535_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784535-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">Family planning</a>, via the use of modern birth control, is one of the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-effective" title="Cost-effective">cost-effective</a> health interventions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsui2010_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tsui2010-121">[121]</a></sup> For every dollar spent, the United Nations estimates that two to six dollars are saved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22784540_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22784540-16">[16]</a></sup> These cost savings are related to preventing unplanned pregnancies and decreasing the spread of sexually transmitted illnesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsui2010_121-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tsui2010-121">[121]</a></sup> While all methods are beneficial financially, the use of copper IUDs resulted in the greatest savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tsui2010_121-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tsui2010-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>The total medical cost for a pregnancy, delivery and care of a newborn in the United States is on average $21,000 for a vaginal delivery and $31,000 for a <a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">caesarean delivery</a> as of 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2013_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2013-122">[122]</a></sup> In most other countries, the cost is less than half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2013_122-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2013-122">[122]</a></sup> For a child born in 2011, an average US family will spend $235,000 over 17 years to raise them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDAChildExpense_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDAChildExpense-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">Prevalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Prevalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Globally, as of 2009, approximately 60% of those who are married and able to have children use birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup> How frequently different methods are used varies widely between countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-1"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup> The most common method in the developed world is condoms and oral contraceptives, while in Africa it is oral contraceptives and in Latin America and Asia it is sterilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-2"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup> In the developing world overall, 35% of birth control is via female sterilization, 30% is via IUDs, 12% is via oral contraceptives, 11% is via condoms, and 4% is via male sterilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-3"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>While less used in the developed countries than the developing world, the number of women using IUDs as of 2007 was more than 180 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darney2010_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Darney2010-63">[63]</a></sup> Avoiding sex when fertile is used by about 3.6% of women of childbearing age, with usage as high as 20% in areas of South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dar2010p315_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dar2010p315-125">[125]</a></sup> As of 2005, 12% of couples are using a male form of birth control (either condoms or a vasectomy) with higher rates in the developed world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naz2009_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naz2009-126">[126]</a></sup> Usage of male forms of birth control has decreased between 1985 and 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-4"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup> Contraceptive use among women in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> has risen from about 5% in 1991 to about 30% in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2012, 57% of women of childbearing age want to avoid pregnancy (867 of 1,520 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dar2013_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dar2013-128">[128]</a></sup> About 222 million women however were not able to access birth control, 53 million of whom were in sub-Saharan Africa and 97 million of whom were in Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dar2013_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dar2013-128">[128]</a></sup> This results in 54 million unplanned pregnancies and nearly 80,000 maternal deaths a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darroch2013_124-5"><a href="#cite_note-Darroch2013-124">[124]</a></sup> Part of the reason that many women are without birth control is that many countries limit access due to religious or political reasons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-2">[2]</a></sup> while another contributor is <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rasch2011_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rasch2011-129">[129]</a></sup> Due to restrictive <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> laws in Sub-Saharan Africa, many women turn to unlicensed abortion providers for <a href="/wiki/Unintended_pregnancy" title="Unintended pregnancy">unintended pregnancy</a>, resulting in about 2–4% obtaining <a href="/wiki/Unsafe_abortion" title="Unsafe abortion">unsafe abortions</a> each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rasch2011_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rasch2011-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_birth_control" title="History of birth control">History of birth control</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silphium.jpg"><img alt="ancient coin depicting silphium" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="164" data-file-width="179" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Silphium.jpg" width="179"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silphium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ancient silver coin from <a href="/wiki/Cyrene,_Libya" title="Cyrene, Libya">Cyrene</a> depicting a stalk of <a href="/wiki/Silphium" title="Silphium">silphium</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Egyptian <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> from 1550 BC and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kahun_Papyrus" title="Kahun Papyrus">Kahun Papyrus</a> from 1850 BC have within them some of the earliest documented descriptions of birth control: the use of honey, <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacia</a> leaves and lint to be placed in the vagina to block sperm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History2010_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-History2010-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipsey2005_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lipsey2005-131">[131]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silphium" title="Silphium">Silphium</a>, a species of <a href="/wiki/Ferula" title="Ferula">giant fennel</a> native to north Africa, may have been used as birth control in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">ancient Near East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herbalcontraceptives_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herbalcontraceptives-132">[132]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> Due to its supposed desirability, by the first century AD, it had become so rare that it was worth more than its weight in silver and, by late antiquity, it was fully extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herbalcontraceptives_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-Herbalcontraceptives-132">[132]</a></sup> Most methods of birth control used in antiquity were probably ineffective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 384–322 BC) recommended applying <a href="/wiki/Cedar_oil" title="Cedar oil">cedar oil</a> to the womb before intercourse, a method which was probably only effective on occasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocratic</a> text <i>On the Nature of Women</i> recommended that a woman drink a copper <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> dissolved in water, which it claimed would prevent pregnancy for a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup> This method was not only ineffective, but also dangerous, as the later medical writer <a href="/wiki/Soranus_of_Ephesus" title="Soranus of Ephesus">Soranus of Ephesus</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 98–138 AD) pointed out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup> Soranus attempted to list reliable methods of birth control based on rational principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup> He rejected the use of superstition and amulets and instead prescribed mechanical methods such as vaginal plugs and pessaries using wool as a base covered in oils or other gummy substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-5"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup> Many of Soranus's methods were probably also ineffective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrick2001_134-6"><a href="#cite_note-Carrick2001-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p><p>In medieval Europe, any effort to halt pregnancy was deemed immoral by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History2010_130-1"><a href="#cite_note-History2010-130">[130]</a></sup> although it is believed that women of the time still used a number of birth control measures, such as <a href="/wiki/Coitus_interruptus" title="Coitus interruptus">coitus interruptus</a> and inserting lily root and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rue" title="Rue">rue</a> into the vagina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> Women in the Middle Ages were also encouraged to tie weasel testicles around their thighs during sex to prevent pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-136">[136]</a></sup> The oldest condoms discovered to date were recovered in the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Castle" title="Dudley Castle">Dudley Castle</a> in England, and are dated back to 1640.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_136-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-136">[136]</a></sup> They were made of animal gut, and were most likely used to prevent the spread of sexually transmitted diseases during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_136-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-136">[136]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casanova" title="Casanova">Casanova</a>, living in 18th century <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, described the use of a lambskin covering to prevent pregnancy; however, condoms only became widely available in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History2010_130-2"><a href="#cite_note-History2010-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_control_movement">Birth control movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Birth control movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VictorianPostcard.jpg"><img alt="a cartoon of a woman being chased by a stork with a baby" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="408" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/VictorianPostcard.jpg/290px-VictorianPostcard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/VictorianPostcard.jpg 1.5x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VictorianPostcard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"And the villain still pursues her", a satirical <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> postcard</div></div></div>
<p>The birth control movement developed during the 19th and early 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_League" title="Malthusian League">Malthusian League</a>, based on the ideas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a>, was established in 1877 in the United Kingdom to educate the public about the importance of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> and to advocate for getting rid of penalties for promoting birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> It was founded during the "Knowlton trial" of <a href="/wiki/Annie_Besant" title="Annie Besant">Annie Besant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bradlaugh" title="Charles Bradlaugh">Charles Bradlaugh</a>, who were prosecuted for publishing on various methods of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Sanger" title="Margaret Sanger">Margaret Sanger</a> and Otto Bobsein popularized the phrase "birth control" in 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilk2004_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilk2004-140">[140]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galvin_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galvin-141">[141]</a></sup> Sanger primarily advocated for birth control on the idea that it would prevent women from seeking unsafe abortions, but during her lifetime, she began to campaign for it on the grounds that it would reduce mental and physical defects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYU2017_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYU2017-143">[143]</a></sup> She was mainly active in the United States but had gained an international reputation by the 1930s. At the time, under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comstock_Law" title="Comstock Law">Comstock Law</a>, distribution of birth control information was illegal. She <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jumped_bail" title="Jumped bail">jumped bail</a> in 1914 after her arrest for distributing birth control information and left the United States for the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> In the U.K., Sanger, influenced by Havelock Ellis, further developed her arguments for birth control. She believed women needed to enjoy sex without fearing a pregnancy. During her time abroad, Sanger also saw a more flexible diaphragm in a Dutch clinic, which she thought was a better form of contraceptive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYU2017_143-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYU2017-143">[143]</a></sup> Once Sanger returned to the United States, she established a short-lived birth-control clinic with the help of her sister, Ethel Bryne, based in the Brownville section of <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, New York<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> in 1916. It was shut down after eleven days and resulted in her arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker2012_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker2012-146">[146]</a></sup> The publicity surrounding the arrest, trial, and appeal sparked birth control activism across the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> Besides her sister, Sanger was helped in the movement by her first husband, William Sanger, who distributed copies of “Family Limitation.” Sanger’s second husband, James Noah H. Slee, would also later become involved in the movement, acting as its main funder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYU2017_143-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYU2017-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first permanent birth-control clinic was established in Britain in 1921 by <a href="/wiki/Marie_Stopes" title="Marie Stopes">Marie Stopes</a> working with the Malthusian League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> The clinic, run by midwives and supported by visiting doctors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup> offered women's birth-control advice and taught them the use of a <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cap" title="Cervical cap">cervical cap</a>. Her clinic made contraception acceptable during the 1920s by presenting it in scientific terms. In 1921, Sanger founded the American Birth Control League, which later became the <a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood" title="Planned Parenthood">Planned Parenthood</a> Federation of America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-150">[150]</a></sup> In 1924 the Society for the Provision of Birth Control Clinics was founded to campaign for municipal clinics; this led to the opening of a second clinic in <a href="/wiki/Greengate,_Salford" title="Greengate, Salford">Greengate, Salford</a> in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> Throughout the 1920s, Stopes and other <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a> pioneers, including <a href="/wiki/Dora_Russell" title="Dora Russell">Dora Russell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stella_Browne" title="Stella Browne">Stella Browne</a>, played a major role in breaking down <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboos</a> about sex. In April 1930 the Birth Control Conference assembled 700 delegates and was successful in bringing birth control and abortion into the political sphere – three months later, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health" title="Ministry of Health">Ministry of Health</a>, in the United Kingdom, allowed local authorities to give birth-control advice in welfare centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Birth Control Association was founded in Britain in 1931, and became the <a href="/wiki/Family_Planning_Association" title="Family Planning Association">Family Planning Association</a> eight years later. The Association amalgamated several British birth control-focused groups into 'a central organisation' for administering and overseeing birth control in Britain. The group incorporated the Birth Control Investigation Committee, a collective of physicians and scientists that was founded to investigate scientific and medical aspects of contraception with 'neutrality and impartiality'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> Subsequently, the Association effected a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_science" title="Pure science">'pure'</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">'applied'</a> product and safety standards that manufacturers must meet to ensure their contraceptives could be prescribed as part of the Association's standard two-part-technique combining ‘a rubber appliance to protect the mouth of the womb’ with a ‘chemical preparation capable of destroying... sperm’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> Between 1931 and 1959, the Association founded and funded a series of tests to assess chemical efficacy and safety and rubber quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> These tests became the basis for the Association's Approved List of contraceptives, which was launched in 1937, and went on to become an annual publication that the expanding network of FPA clinics relied upon as a means to 'establish facts [about contraceptives] and to publish these facts as a basis on which a sound public and scientific opinion can be built'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1936 the U.S. court ruled in U.S. v. One Package that medically prescribing contraception to save a person's life or well-being was not illegal under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comstock_Law" title="Comstock Law">Comstock Law</a>; following this decision, the <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a> Committee on Contraception revoked its 1936 statement condemning birth control. A national survey in 1937 showed 71 percent of the adult population supported the use of contraception. By 1938 347 birth control clinics were running in the United States despite their advertisement still being illegal. <a href="/wiki/First_Lady" title="First Lady">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a> publicly supported birth control and family planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> In 1966, <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">President Lyndon B. Johnson</a> started endorsing public funding for family planning services, and the Federal Government began subsidizing birth control services for low-income families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">The Affordable Care Act</a>, passed into law on March 23, 2010 under President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, requires all plans in the Health Insurance Marketplace to cover contraceptive methods. These include barrier methods, hormonal methods, implanted devices, emergency contraceptives, and sterilization procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_methods">Modern methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Modern methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1909, Richard Richter developed the first intrauterine device made from silkworm gut, which was further developed and marketed in Germany by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Gr%C3%A4fenberg" title="Ernst Gräfenberg">Ernst Gräfenberg</a> in the late 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> In 1951, a chemist, named Carl Djerassi from Mexico City made the hormones in progesterone pills using Mexican yams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> Djerassi had chemically created the pill but was not equipped to distribute it to patients. Meanwhile, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gregory_Pincus" title="Gregory Pincus">Gregory Pincus</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Rock_(American_scientist)" title="John Rock (American scientist)">John Rock</a> with help from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood_Federation_of_America" title="Planned Parenthood Federation of America">Planned Parenthood Federation of America</a> developed the first birth control pills in the 1950s, such as <a href="/wiki/Mestranol/noretynodrel" title="Mestranol/noretynodrel">mestranol/noretynodrel</a>, which became publicly available in the 1960s through the Food and Drug Administration under the name <i>Enovid</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_150-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Medical_abortion" title="Medical abortion">Medical abortion</a> became an alternative to surgical abortion with the availability of <a href="/wiki/Prostaglandin_analogue" title="Prostaglandin analogue">prostaglandin analogs</a> in the 1970s and <a href="/wiki/Mifepristone" title="Mifepristone">mifepristone</a> in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_positions">Legal positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Legal positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_reproductive_rights_legislation" title="Timeline of reproductive rights legislation">Timeline of reproductive rights legislation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">Human rights</a> agreements require most governments to provide family planning and contraceptive information and services. These include the requirement to create a national plan for family planning services, remove laws that limit access to family planning, ensure that a wide variety of safe and effective birth control methods are available including emergency contraceptives, make sure there are appropriately trained healthcare providers and facilities at an affordable price, and create a process to review the programs implemented. If governments fail to do the above it may put them in breach of binding international treaty obligations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrintlaw_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrintlaw-164">[164]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the 1965 Supreme Court decision <i><a href="/wiki/Griswold_v._Connecticut" title="Griswold v. Connecticut">Griswold v. Connecticut</a></i> overturned a state law prohibiting dissemination of contraception information based on a constitutional right to privacy for marital relationships. In 1971, <i><a href="/wiki/Eisenstadt_v._Baird" title="Eisenstadt v. Baird">Eisenstadt v. Baird</a></i> extended this right to privacy to single people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, the United Nations launched the <i>Every Woman Every Child</i> movement to assess the progress toward meeting women's contraceptive needs. The initiative has set a goal of increasing the number of users of modern birth control by 120 million women in the world's 69 poorest countries by the year 2020. Additionally, they aim to eradicate discrimination against girls and young women who seek contraceptives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutt2012_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gutt2012-166">[166]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Congress_of_Obstetricians_and_Gynecologists" title="American Congress of Obstetricians and Gynecologists">American Congress of Obstetricians and Gynecologists</a> (ACOG) recommended in 2014 that oral birth control pills should be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over_the_counter_medications" title="Over the counter medications">over the counter medications</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since at least the 1870s, American religious, medical, legislative, and legal commentators have debated contraception laws. Ana Garner and Angela Michel have found that in these discussions men often attach reproductive rights to moral and political matters, as part of an ongoing attempt to regulate human bodies. In press coverage between 1873–2013 they found a divide between institutional ideology and real-life experiences of women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_and_birth_control" title="Religion and birth control">Religion and birth control</a></div><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Jewish_views_on_contraception" title="Jewish views on contraception">Jewish views on contraception</a></div>
<p>Religions vary widely in their views of the <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srikanthan2008_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-Srikanthan2008-169">[169]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> officially only accepts <a href="/wiki/Natural_family_planning" title="Natural family planning">natural family planning</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pope1_170-0"><a href="#cite_note-pope1-170">[170]</a></sup> although large numbers of Catholics in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a> accept and use modern methods of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup> Among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestants" title="Protestants">Protestants</a>, there is a wide range of views from supporting none, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Quiverfull" title="Quiverfull">Quiverfull movement</a>, to allowing all methods of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup> Views in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> range from the stricter <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox</a> sect, which prohibits all methods of birth control, to the more relaxed <a href="/wiki/Reform_Judaism" title="Reform Judaism">Reform</a> sect, which allows most.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jew_BC_175-0"><a href="#cite_note-jew_BC-175">[175]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindus</a> may use both natural and modern contraceptives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hindu_BC_176-0"><a href="#cite_note-hindu_BC-176">[176]</a></sup> A common <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> view is that preventing conception is acceptable, while intervening after conception has occurred is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buddhist_BC_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-buddhist_BC-177">[177]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, contraceptives are allowed if they do not threaten health, although their use is discouraged by some.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Contraception_Day">World Contraception Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: World Contraception Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>September 26 is World Contraception Day, devoted to raising awareness and improving education about sexual and reproductive health, with a vision of <i>a world where every pregnancy is wanted.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldContraceptionDay_179-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorldContraceptionDay-179">[179]</a></sup> It is supported by a group of governments and international NGOs, including the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Population_Affairs" title="Office of Population Affairs">Office of Population Affairs</a>, the Asian Pacific Council on Contraception, Centro Latinamericano Salud y Mujer, the European Society of Contraception and Reproductive Health, the <a href="/wiki/German_Foundation_for_World_Population" title="German Foundation for World Population">German Foundation for World Population</a>, the International Federation of Pediatric and Adolescent Gynecology, <a href="/wiki/International_Planned_Parenthood_Federation" title="International Planned Parenthood Federation">International Planned Parenthood Federation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marie_Stopes_International" title="Marie Stopes International">Marie Stopes International</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_Services_International" title="Population Services International">Population Services International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Population_Council" title="Population Council">Population Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">United States Agency for International Development</a> (USAID), and <a href="/wiki/Women_Deliver" title="Women Deliver">Women Deliver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldContraceptionDay_179-1"><a href="#cite_note-WorldContraceptionDay-179">[179]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Misconceptions">Misconceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Misconceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_misconceptions" title="Common misconceptions">common misconceptions</a> regarding sex and pregnancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Douche" title="Douche">Douching</a> after sexual intercourse is not an effective form of birth control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup> Additionally, it is associated with a number of health problems and thus is not recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup> Women can become pregnant the first time they have sexual intercourse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup> and in any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_position" title="Sexual position">sexual position</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup> It is possible, although not very likely, to become pregnant during menstruation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_directions">Research directions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Research directions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Females">Females</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Females">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improvements of existing birth control methods are needed, as around half of those who get pregnant unintentionally are using birth control at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Will2012_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-Will2012-27">[27]</a></sup> A number of alterations of existing contraceptive methods are being studied, including a better female condom, an improved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SILCS_diaphragm" title="SILCS diaphragm">diaphragm</a>, a patch containing only progestin, and a vaginal ring containing long-acting progesterone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen2011_186-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2011-186">[186]</a></sup> This vaginal ring appears to be effective for three or four months and is currently available in some areas of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen2011_186-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2011-186">[186]</a></sup> For women who rarely have sex, the taking of the hormonal birth control <a href="/wiki/Levonorgestrel" title="Levonorgestrel">levonorgestrel</a> around the time of sex looks promising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of methods to perform sterilization via the cervix are being studied. One involves putting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quinacrine" title="Quinacrine">quinacrine</a> in the uterus which causes scarring and infertility. While the procedure is inexpensive and does not require surgical skills, there are concerns regarding long-term side effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cast2010_188-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cast2010-188">[188]</a></sup> Another substance, <a href="/wiki/Polidocanol" title="Polidocanol">polidocanol</a>, which functions in the same manner is being looked at.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen2011_186-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2011-186">[186]</a></sup> A device called <a href="/wiki/Essure" title="Essure">Essure</a>, which expands when placed in the fallopian tubes and blocks them, was approved in the United States in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cast2010_188-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cast2010-188">[188]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Males">Males</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Males">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Male_contraceptive" title="Male contraceptive">Male contraceptive</a></div>
<p>Methods of male birth control include condoms, vasectomies and withdrawal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasier2010_189-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glasier2010-189">[189]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-190"><a href="#cite_note-190">[190]</a></sup> Between 25 and 75% of males who are sexually active would use hormonal birth control if it was available for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naz2009_126-1"><a href="#cite_note-Naz2009-126">[126]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasier2010_189-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glasier2010-189">[189]</a></sup> A number of hormonal and non-hormonal methods are in trials,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naz2009_126-2"><a href="#cite_note-Naz2009-126">[126]</a></sup> and there is some research looking at the possibility of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraceptive_vaccine" title="Contraceptive vaccine">contraceptive vaccines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup>
</p><p>A reversible surgical method under investigation is <a href="/wiki/Reversible_inhibition_of_sperm_under_guidance" title="Reversible inhibition of sperm under guidance">reversible inhibition of sperm under guidance</a> (RISUG) which consists of injecting a polymer gel, <a href="/wiki/Styrene_maleic_anhydride" title="Styrene maleic anhydride">styrene maleic anhydride</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a>, into the <a href="/wiki/Vas_deferens" title="Vas deferens">vas deferens</a>. An injection with sodium bicarbonate washes out the substance and restores fertility. Another is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravas_device" title="Intravas device">intravas device</a> which involves putting a <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">urethane</a> plug into the <a href="/wiki/Vas_deferens" title="Vas deferens">vas deferens</a> to block it. A combination of an <a href="/wiki/Androgen" title="Androgen">androgen</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Progestin" title="Progestin">progestin</a> seems promising, as do <a href="/wiki/Selective_androgen_receptor_modulator" title="Selective androgen receptor modulator">selective androgen receptor modulators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naz2009_126-3"><a href="#cite_note-Naz2009-126">[126]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a> and methods to heat the testicles have undergone preliminary studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_control&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neutering" title="Neutering">Neutering</a> or spaying, which involves removing some of the reproductive organs, is often carried out as a method of birth control in household pets. Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_shelters" title="Animal shelters">animal shelters</a> require these procedures as part of adoption agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-193"><a href="#cite_note-193">[193]</a></sup> In large animals the surgery is known as <a href="/wiki/Castration" title="Castration">castration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup>
</p><p>Birth control is also being considered as an alternative to hunting as a means of controlling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overpopulation_in_wild_animals" title="Overpopulation in wild animals">overpopulation in wild animals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boyle_2009_195-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boyle_2009-195">[195]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraceptive_vaccine" title="Contraceptive vaccine">Contraceptive vaccines</a> have been found to be effective in a number of different animal populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup> Kenyan goat herders fix a skirt, called an <a href="/wiki/Olor" title="Olor">olor</a>, to male goats to prevent them from impregnating female goats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup>
</p>
