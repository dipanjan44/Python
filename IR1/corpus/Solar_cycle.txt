The smoothed sunspot number has been rising again towards this second peak over the last five months and has now surpassed the level of the first peak (66.9 in February 2012). Many cycles are double peaked but this is the first in which the second peak in sunspot number was larger than the first. We are currently over five years into Cycle 24. The current predicted and observed size makes this the smallest sunspot cycle since Cycle 14 which had a maximum of 64.2 in February of 1906."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>solar cycle</b> or <b>solar magnetic activity cycle</b> is the nearly periodic 11-year change in the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s activity (including changes in the levels of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> and ejection of solar material) and appearance (changes in the number and size of <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">flares</a>, and other manifestations).
</p><p>They have been observed (by changes in the sun's appearance and by changes seen on Earth, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroras</a>) for centuries.
</p><p>The changes on the Sun cause effects in space, in the atmosphere, and on Earth's surface. While it is the dominant variable in solar activity, aperiodic fluctuations also occur.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solar cycles have an average duration of about 11 years. <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">Solar maximum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">solar minimum</a> refer respectively to periods of maximum and minimum sunspot counts. Cycles span from one minimum to the next.
</p>

German astronomer, discovered the solar cycle through extended observations of sunspots</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:190px;max-width:190px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RudolfWolf.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="366" data-file-width="357" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg/188px-RudolfWolf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg/282px-RudolfWolf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg 2x" width="188"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Rudolf Wolf (1816–1893), Swiss astronomer, carried out historical reconstruction of solar activity back to the seventeenth century</div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>The solar cycle was discovered in 1843 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Heinrich_Schwabe" title="Samuel Heinrich Schwabe">Samuel Heinrich Schwabe</a>, who after 17 years of observations noticed a periodic variation in the average number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwabe_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabe-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Wolf" title="Rudolf Wolf">Rudolf Wolf</a> compiled and studied these and other observations, reconstructing the cycle back to 1745, eventually pushing these reconstructions to the earliest observations of sunspots by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a> and contemporaries in the early seventeenth century.
</p><p>Following Wolf's numbering scheme, the 1755–1766 cycle is traditionally numbered "1". Wolf created a standard sunspot number index, the Wolf index, which continues to be used today.
</p><p>The period between 1645 and 1715, a time of few sunspots,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maunder_minimum" title="Maunder minimum">Maunder minimum</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Edward_Walter_Maunder" title="Edward Walter Maunder">Edward Walter Maunder</a>, who extensively researched this peculiar event, first noted by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Sp%C3%B6rer" title="Gustav Spörer">Gustav Spörer</a>.
</p><p>In the second half of the nineteenth century <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">Richard Carrington</a> and Spörer independently noted the phenomena of sunspots appearing at different latitudes at different parts of the cycle.
</p><p>The cycle's physical basis was elucidated by <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">George Ellery Hale</a> and collaborators, who in 1908 showed that sunspots were strongly magnetized (the first detection of magnetic fields beyond the Earth). In 1919 they showed that the magnetic polarity of sunspot pairs:
</p>
<ul><li>Is constant throughout a cycle;</li>
<li>Is opposite across the equator throughout a cycle;</li>
<li>Reverses itself from one cycle to the next.</li></ul>
<p>Hale's observations revealed that the complete magnetic cycle spans two solar cycles, or 22 years, before returning to its original state. However, because nearly all manifestations are insensitive to polarity, the "11-year solar cycle" remains the focus of research. Furthermore, the two halves of the 22-year cycle are typically not identical; the 11-year cycles usually alternate between higher and lower sums of <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">Wolf’s sunspot numbers</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Gnevyshev-Ohl_rule" title="Gnevyshev-Ohl rule">Gnevyshev-Ohl rule</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathaway_review_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hathaway_review-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1961 the father-and-son team of <a href="/wiki/Harold_D._Babcock" title="Harold D. Babcock">Harold</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace_Babcock" title="Horace Babcock">Horace Babcock</a> established that the solar cycle is a spatiotemporal magnetic process unfolding over the Sun as a whole. They observed that the solar surface is magnetized outside of sunspots, that this (weaker) magnetic field is to first order a <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a>, and that this dipole undergoes polarity reversals with the same period as the sunspot cycle. Horace's <a href="/wiki/Babcock_Model" title="Babcock Model">Babcock Model</a> described the Sun's oscillatory magnetic field as having a quasi-steady periodicity of 22 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwabe_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwabe-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oma.be_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oma.be-5">[5]</a></sup> It covered the oscillatory exchange of energy between <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">toroidal and poloidal</a> solar magnetic field ingredients.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_history">Cycle history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cycle history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunspots_11000_years.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1360" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Sunspots_11000_years.svg/300px-Sunspots_11000_years.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Sunspots_11000_years.svg/450px-Sunspots_11000_years.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Sunspots_11000_years.svg/600px-Sunspots_11000_years.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunspots_11000_years.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of solar activity over 11,400 years. Period of equally high activity over 8,000 years ago marked.</div></div></div>
<p>Sunspot numbers over the past 11,400 years have been reconstructed using <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">Carbon-14</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Dendroclimatology" title="Dendroclimatology">dendroclimatology</a>. The level of solar activity beginning in the 1940s is exceptional – the last period of similar magnitude occurred around 9,000 years ago (during the warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_(period)" title="Boreal (period)">Boreal period</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Usoskin07_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Usoskin07-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solanski2004_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solanski2004-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Sun was at a similarly high level of magnetic activity for only ~10% of the past 11,400 years. Almost all earlier high-activity periods were shorter than the present episode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solanski2004_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Solanski2004-7">[7]</a></sup> Fossil records suggest that the Solar cycle has been stable for at least the last 700 million years. For example, the cycle length during the early <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> is estimated to be 10.62 years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luthardt2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luthardt2017-9">[9]</a></sup> and similarly in the <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NeoP_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NeoP-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Until 2009 it was thought that 28 cycles had spanned the 309 years between 1699 and 2008, giving an average length of 11.04 years, but research then showed that the longest of these (1784–1799) seems actually to have been two cycles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> so that the average length is only around 10.7 years. Since observations began cycles as short as 9 years and as long as 14 years have been observed, and in the double cycle of 1784-99 one of the two component cycles had to be less than 8 years in length. Significant amplitude variations also occur.
</p><p>A list of historical "grand minima" of solar activity exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Usoskin07_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Usoskin07-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_cycles">Recent cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_25">Cycle 25</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cycle 25">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are many, often mutually contradictory predictions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADS_serach_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADS_serach-15">[15]</a></sup> based on different methods, for the solar cycle 25, ranging from very weak to moderate magnitude. At present, no definite prediction can be made.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_24">Cycle 24</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cycle 24">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_24" title="Solar cycle 24">current solar cycle</a> began on January 4, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> with minimal activity until early 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It is on track to have the lowest recorded sunspot activity since accurate records began in 1750. The cycle featured a "double-peaked" <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">solar maximum</a>. The first peak reached 99 in 2011 and the second in early 2014 at 101.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It appears likely that Cycle 24 will end in mid-2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_23">Cycle 23</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cycle 23">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This cycle lasted 11.6 years, beginning in May 1996 and ending in January 2008.  The maximum smoothed sunspot number (monthly number of sunspots averaged over a twelve-month period) observed during the solar cycle was 120.8 (March 2000), and the minimum was 1.7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIDC_Monthly_Smoothed_Sunspot_Number_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIDC_Monthly_Smoothed_Sunspot_Number-21">[21]</a></sup> A total of 805 days had no sunspots during this cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spotless_Days_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spotless_Days-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What's_Wrong_with_the_Sun?_(Nothing)_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-What's_Wrong_with_the_Sun?_(Nothing)-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solaemon's_Spotless_Days_Page_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solaemon's_Spotless_Days_Page-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomena">Phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_phenomena" title="Solar phenomena">Solar phenomena</a></div><p> Because the solar cycle reflects magnetic activity, various magnetically driven solar phenomena follow the solar cycle, including sunspots and coronal mass ejections.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunspots">Sunspots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sunspots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChroniclesofJohnofWorcester.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="516" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/ChroniclesofJohnofWorcester.jpg/350px-ChroniclesofJohnofWorcester.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/ChroniclesofJohnofWorcester.jpg 1.5x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChroniclesofJohnofWorcester.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A drawing of a sunspot in the Chronicles of <a href="/wiki/John_of_Worcester" title="John of Worcester">John of Worcester</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">Sunspot</a></div>
<p>The Sun's apparent surface, the photosphere, radiates more actively when there are more sunspots. Satellite monitoring of <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a> revealed a direct relationship between the Schwabe cycle and luminosity with a peak-to-peak amplitude of about 0.1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson91_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willson91-25">[25]</a></sup> Luminosity decreases by as much as 0.3% on a 10-day timescale when large groups of sunspots rotate across the Earth's view and increase by as much as 0.05% for up to 6 months due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faculae" title="Faculae">faculae</a> associated with large sunspot groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson81_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willson81-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The best information today comes from <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> (a cooperative project of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>), such as the MDI <a href="/wiki/Magnetogram" title="Magnetogram">magnetogram</a>, where the solar "surface" <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> can be seen.
</p><p>As each cycle begins, sunspots appear at mid-latitudes, and then move closer and closer to the equator until a solar minimum is reached. This pattern is best visualized in the form of the so-called butterfly diagram. Images of the Sun are divided into latitudinal strips, and the monthly-averaged fractional surface of sunspots is calculated. This is plotted vertically as a color-coded bar, and the process is repeated month after month to produce this time-series diagram.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot-bfly.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="1950" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sunspot-bfly.gif/600px-Sunspot-bfly.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sunspot-bfly.gif/900px-Sunspot-bfly.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Sunspot-bfly.gif/1200px-Sunspot-bfly.gif 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot-bfly.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sunspot butterfly diagram. This modern version is constructed (and regularly updated) by the solar group at NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.</div></div></div></div><p>While magnetic field changes are concentrated at sunspots, the entire sun undergoes analogous changes, albeit of smaller magnitude.</p><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Synoptic-solmag.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1300" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg/700px-Synoptic-solmag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg/1050px-Synoptic-solmag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg 2x" width="700"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Synoptic-solmag.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Time vs. solar latitude diagram of the radial component of the solar magnetic field, averaged over successive solar rotation. The "butterfly" signature of sunspots is clearly visible at low latitudes. Diagram constructed (and regularly updated) by the solar group at NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.</div></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronal_mass_ejection">Coronal mass ejection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Coronal mass ejection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">Coronal mass ejection</a></div><p>The solar magnetic field structures the corona, giving it its characteristic shape visible at times of solar eclipses. Complex coronal magnetic field structures evolve in response to fluid motions at the solar surface, and emergence of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">magnetic flux</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Solar_dynamo" title="Solar dynamo">dynamo</a> action in the solar interior. For reasons not yet understood in detail, sometimes these structures lose stability, leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">coronal mass ejections</a> into interplanetary space, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">flares</a>, caused by sudden localized release of magnetic energy driving emission of ultraviolet and X-ray radiation as well as energetic particles. These eruptive phenomena can have a significant impact on Earth's upper atmosphere and space environment, and are the primary drivers of what is now called <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a>.
</p><p>The occurrence frequency of coronal mass ejections and flares is strongly modulated by the cycle. Flares of any given size are some 50 times more frequent at solar maximum than at minimum. Large coronal mass ejections occur on average a few times a day at solar maximum, down to one every few days at solar minimum. The size of these events themselves does not depend sensitively on the phase of the solar cycle. A case in point are the three large X-class flares that occurred in December 2006, very near solar minimum; an X9.0 flare on Dec 5 stands as one of the brightest on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patterns">Patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1453" data-file-width="3175" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/320px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/480px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg/640px-SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SpaceEnvironmentOverview_From_19830101.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An overview of three solar cycles shows the relationship between the sunspot cycle, galactic cosmic rays, and the state of our near-space environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Waldmeier effect names the observation that cycles with larger maximum amplitudes tend to take less time to reach their maxima than cycles with smaller amplitudes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> maximum amplitudes are negatively correlated to the lengths of earlier cycles, aiding prediction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solar maxima and minima also exhibit fluctuations at time scales greater than solar cycles. Increasing and decreasing trends can continue for periods of a century or more.
</p><p>The 87 year (70–100 year) <b>Gleissberg</b> cycle, named after Wolfgang Gleißberg, is thought to be an amplitude modulation of the Schwabe Cycle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathaway_review_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-hathaway_review-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun05_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braun05-32">[32]</a></sup> The Gleisberg cycle implied that the next solar cycle have a maximum smoothed sunspot number of about 145±30 in 2010 (instead 2010 was just after the cycle's solar minimum) and that the following cycle have a maximum of about 70±30 in 2023.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hathaway2005_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hathaway2005-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Associated centennial variations in magnetic fields in the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">Corona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">Heliosphere</a> have been detected using <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">Carbon-14</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-10" title="Beryllium-10">beryllium-10</a> cosmogenic isotopes stored in terrestrial reservoirs such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and by using historic observations of <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">Geomagnetic storm</a> activity, which bridge the time gap between the end of the usable cosmogenic isotope data and the start of modern satellite data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>
These variations have been successfully reproduced using models that employ magnetic flux continuity equations and observed sunspot numbers to quantify the emergence of magnetic flux from the top of the solar atmosphere and into the <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">Heliosphere</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> showing that sunspot observations, geomagnetic activity and cosmogenic isotopes offer a convergent understanding of solar activity variations.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png" title="File:Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit Cycles.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="815" data-file-width="1202" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png/300px-Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png/450px-Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png/600px-Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon-14-10kyr-Hallstadtzeit_Cycles.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>2,300 year Hallstatt solar variation cycles.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothesized_cycles">Hypothesized cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hypothesized cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Periodicity of solar activity with periods longer than the sunspot cycle has been proposed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathaway_review_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-hathaway_review-4">[4]</a></sup> including:
</p><p>The 210 year Suess cycle (a.k.a. "de Vries cycle", named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hans_Eduard_Suess" title="Hans Eduard Suess">Hans Eduard Suess</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hessel_De_Vries&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hessel De Vries (page does not exist)">Hessel De Vries</a> respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun05_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Braun05-32">[32]</a></sup> This cycle is recorded from radiocarbon studies, although "little evidence of the Suess Cycle" appears in the 400-year sunspot record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hathaway_review_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-hathaway_review-4">[4]</a></sup>)
</p><p>The Hallstatt cycle (named after a cool and wet <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">period in Europe when glaciers advanced</a>) is hypothesized to extend for approximately 2,400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>An as yet unnamed cycle may extend over 6,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> cycles of 105, 131, 232, 385, 504, 805 and 2,241 years have been observed, possibly matching cycles derived from other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Damon and Sonett<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> proposed carbon 14-based medium- and short-term variations of periods 208 and 88 years; as well as suggesting a 2300-year radiocarbon period that modulates the 208-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZgeos462climsolar_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-AZgeos462climsolar-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Upper Permian</a> 240 million years ago, mineral layers created in the Castile Formation show cycles of 2,500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_magnetic_field">Solar magnetic field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Solar magnetic field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sun's <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> structures its atmosphere and outer layers all the way through the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. Its spatiotemporal variations lead to various measurable solar phenomena. Other solar phenomena are closely related to the cycle, which serves as the energy source and dynamical engine for the former.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar-cycle-data.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="700" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png/350px-Solar-cycle-data.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png/525px-Solar-cycle-data.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar-cycle-data.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activity cycles 21, 22 and 23 seen in sunspot number index, TSI, 10.7cm radio flux, and flare index. Temporal variations of all quantities are tightly locked in phase, but the degree of correlation in amplitudes is variable to some degree.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_magnetism">Surface magnetism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Surface magnetism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">Sunspots</a> eventually decay, releasing magnetic flux in the photosphere. This flux is dispersed and churned by turbulent convection and solar large-scale flows. These transport mechanisms lead to the accumulation of magnetized decay products at high solar latitudes, eventually reversing the polarity of the polar fields (notice how the blue and yellow fields reverse in the Hathaway/NASA/MSFC graph above).
</p><p>The dipolar component of the solar magnetic field reverses polarity around the time of solar maximum and reaches peak strength at the solar minimum.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space">Space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>CMEs (<a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejections</a>) produce a radiation flux of high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>, sometimes known as solar cosmic rays.  These can cause radiation damage to electronics and <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a>.  Solar proton events also can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-event_upset" title="Single-event upset">single-event upset</a> (SEU) events on electronics; at the same, the reduced flux of galactic cosmic radiation during solar maximum decreases the high-energy component of particle flux.
</p><p>CME radiation is dangerous to <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> on a space mission who are outside the shielding produced by the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>. Future mission designs (<i>e.g.</i>, for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">Mars Mission</a>) therefore incorporate a radiation-shielded "storm shelter" for astronauts to retreat to during such an event.
</p><p>Gleißberg developed a CME forecasting method that relies on consecutive cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the positive side, the increased irradiance during solar maximum expands the envelope of the Earth's atmosphere, causing low-orbiting <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> to re-enter more quickly.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Galactic_cosmic_ray_flux">Galactic cosmic ray flux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Galactic cosmic ray flux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The outward expansion of solar ejecta into interplanetary space provides overdensities of plasma that are efficient at scattering high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> entering the solar system from elsewhere in the galaxy. The frequency of solar eruptive events is modulated by the cycle, changing the degree of cosmic ray scattering in the outer solar system accordingly. As a consequence, the cosmic ray flux in the inner solar system is anticorrelated with the overall level of solar activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> This anticorrelation is clearly detected in cosmic ray flux measurements at the Earth's surface.
</p><p>Some high-energy cosmic rays entering Earth's atmosphere collide hard enough with molecular atmospheric constituents to cause occasionally nuclear <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation" title="Cosmic ray spallation">spallation reactions</a>. Fission products include radionuclides such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14"><sup>14</sup>C</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-10" title="Beryllium-10"><sup>10</sup>Be</a> that settle on the Earth's surface. Their concentration can be measured in ice cores, allowing a reconstruction of solar activity levels into the distant past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Such reconstructions indicate that the overall level of solar activity since the middle of the twentieth century stands amongst the highest of the past 10,000 years, and that epochs of suppressed activity, of varying durations have occurred repeatedly over that time span.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric">Atmospheric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Atmospheric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_irradiance">Solar irradiance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Solar irradiance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">Solar irradiance</a></div><p>The total solar irradiance (TSI) is the amount of solar radiative energy incident on the Earth's upper atmosphere. TSI variations were undetectable until satellite observations began in late 1978. A series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiometers" title="Radiometers">radiometers</a> were launched on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a> from the 1970s to the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACRIM_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACRIM-49">[49]</a></sup> TSI measurements varied from 1360 to 1370 W/m<sup>2</sup> across ten satellites. One of the satellites, the <a href="/wiki/ACRIMSAT" title="ACRIMSAT">ACRIMSAT</a> was launched by the ACRIM group. The controversial 1989–1991 "ACRIM gap" between non-overlapping ACRIM satellites was interpolated by the ACRIM group into a composite showing +0.037%/decade rise. Another series based on the ACRIM data is produced by the PMOD group and shows a −0.008%/decade downward trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> This 0.045%/decade difference impacts climate models.
</p><p>Solar irradiance varies systematically over the cycle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> both in total irradiance and in its relative components (UV vs visible and other frequencies). The <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">solar luminosity</a> is an estimated 0.07 percent brighter during the mid-cycle solar maximum than the terminal solar minimum. <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">Photospheric</a> magnetism appears to be the primary cause (96%) of 1996–2013 TSI variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The ratio of ultraviolet to visible light varies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InvertedForcingpaper_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-InvertedForcingpaper-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>TSI varies in phase with the solar magnetic activity cycle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> with an amplitude of about 0.1% around an average value of about 1361.5 W/m<sup>2</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> (the "<a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>"). Variations about the average of up to −0.3% are caused by large sunspot groups and of +0.05% by large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faculae" title="Faculae">faculae</a> and the bright network on a 7-10-day timescale<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> (see TSI variation graphics).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACRIM-graphic_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACRIM-graphic-57">[57]</a></sup> Satellite-era TSI variations show small but detectable trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>TSI is higher at solar maximum, even though sunspots are darker (cooler) than the average photosphere. This is caused by magnetized structures other than sunspots during solar maxima, such as faculae and active elements of the "bright" network, that are brighter (hotter) than the average photosphere. They collectively overcompensate for the irradiance deficit associated with the cooler, but less numerous sunspots. The primary driver of TSI changes on solar rotational and sunspot cycle timescales is the varying photospheric coverage of these radiatively active solar magnetic structures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Energy changes in UV irradiance involved in production and loss of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> have atmospheric effects. The 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HPa" title="HPa">HPa</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric pressure</a> level changed height in phase with solar activity during solar cycles 20–23. UV irradiance increase caused higher ozone production, leading to stratospheric heating and to poleward displacements in the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">tropospheric</a> wind systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Short-wavelength_radiation">Short-wavelength radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Short-wavelength radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2790" data-file-width="3563" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg/300px-The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg/450px-The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg/600px-The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Solar_Cycle_XRay_hi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A solar cycle: a montage of ten years' worth of <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">Yohkoh</a> SXT images, demonstrating the variation in solar activity during a sunspot cycle, from after August 30, 1991, to September 6, 2001. Credit: the Yohkoh mission of <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Space_and_Astronautical_Science" title="Institute of Space and Astronautical Science">ISAS</a> (Japan) and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> (US).</div></div></div>
<p>With a temperature of 5870 K, the <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> emits a proportion of radiation in the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a> (EUV) and above. However, hotter upper layers of the Sun's atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>) emit more short-wavelength radiation. Since the upper atmosphere is not homogeneous and contains significant magnetic structure, the solar ultraviolet (UV), <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">EUV</a> and X-ray flux varies markedly over the cycle.
</p><p>The photo montage to the left illustrates this variation for soft <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a>, as observed by the Japanese satellite <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">Yohkoh</a> from after August 30, 1991, at the peak of cycle 22, to September 6, 2001, at the peak of cycle 23. Similar cycle-related variations are observed in the flux of solar UV or EUV radiation, as observed, for example, by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> or <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">TRACE</a> satellites.
</p><p>Even though it only accounts for a minuscule fraction of total solar radiation, the impact of solar UV, EUV and X-ray radiation on the Earth's upper atmosphere is profound. Solar UV flux is a major driver of <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric chemistry</a>, and increases in ionizing radiation significantly affect <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>-influenced temperature and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a>.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_radio_flux">Solar radio flux</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Solar radio flux">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Emission from the Sun at centimetric (radio) wavelength is due primarily to coronal plasma trapped in the magnetic fields overlying active regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The F10.7 index is a measure of the solar radio flux per unit frequency at a wavelength of 10.7 cm, near the peak of the observed solar radio emission. F10.7 is often expressed in SFU or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_irradiance#Solar_energy" title="Spectral irradiance">solar flux units</a> (1 SFU = 10<sup>−22</sup> W m<sup>−2</sup> Hz<sup>−1</sup>). It represents a measure of diffuse, nonradiative coronal plasma heating. It is an excellent indicator of overall solar activity levels and correlates well with solar UV emissions.
</p><p>Sunspot activity has a major effect on long distance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_communications" title="Radio communications">radio communications</a>, particularly on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> bands although medium wave and low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">VHF</a> frequencies are also affected. High levels of sunspot activity lead to improved signal propagation on higher frequency bands, although they also increase the levels of solar noise and ionospheric disturbances. These effects are caused by impact of the increased level of solar radiation on the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>.
</p><p>10.7 cm solar flux could interfere with point-to-point terrestrial communications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clouds">Clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The cosmic ray changes over the cycle potentially have significant atmospheric effects. Speculations about cosmic rays include:
</p>
<ul><li>Changes in ionization affect the aerosol abundance that serves as the condensation nucleus for cloud formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tinsley2004_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tinsley2004-63">[63]</a></sup> During solar minima more cosmic rays reach Earth, potentially creating ultra-small aerosol particles as precursors to <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">Cloud condensation nuclei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CERN_Clouds_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-CERN_Clouds-64">[64]</a></sup> Clouds formed from greater amounts of condensation nuclei are brighter, longer lived and likely to produce less precipitation.</li>
<li>A change in cosmic rays could cause an increase in certain types of clouds, affecting Earth's <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>It was proposed that, particularly at high <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>, cosmic ray variation may impact terrestrial low altitude cloud cover (unlike a lack of correlation with high altitude clouds), partially influenced by the solar-driven interplanetary magnetic field (as well as passage through the galactic arms over longer timeframes),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaviv2005_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-shaviv2005-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Svensmark2007_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Svensmark2007-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Svensmark1998_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Svensmark1998-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> but this hypothesis was not confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Later papers claimed that production of clouds via cosmic rays could not be explained by nucleation particles. Accelerator results failed to produce sufficient, and sufficiently large, particles to result in cloud formation;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> this includes observations after a major solar storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlykin,_A.,_et_al._137_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erlykin,_A.,_et_al._137-72">[72]</a></sup>  Observations after <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl</a> do not show any induced clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial">Terrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Terrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisms">Organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The impact of the solar cycle on living organisms has been investigated (see <a href="/wiki/Chronobiology" title="Chronobiology">chronobiology</a>). Some researchers claim to have found connections with human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>The amount of ultraviolet UVB light at 300 nm reaching the Earth varies by as much as 400% over the solar cycle due to variations in the protective <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. In the stratosphere, <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone-oxygen_cycle" title="Ozone-oxygen cycle">continuously regenerated</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation" title="Photodissociation">splitting</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a> molecules by ultraviolet light. During a solar minimum, the decrease in ultraviolet light received from the Sun leads to a decrease in the concentration of ozone, allowing increased UVB to reach the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_communication">Radio communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Radio communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">Skywave</a></div>
<p>Skywave modes of radio communication operate by bending (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refracting" title="Refracting">refracting</a>) radio waves (<a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>) through the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">Ionosphere</a>. During the "peaks" of the solar cycle, the ionosphere becomes increasingly ionized by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_photonics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solar photonics (page does not exist)">solar photons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. This affects the <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">propagation</a> of the radio wave in complex ways that can either facilitate or hinder communications. Forecasting of skywave modes is of considerable interest to commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications" title="Communications">communications</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_operators" title="Amateur radio operators">amateur radio operators</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcasters</a>. These users occupy frequencies within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Frequency" title="High Frequency">High Frequency</a> or 'HF' radio spectrum that are most affected by these solar and ionospheric variances. Changes in solar output affect the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_usable_frequency" title="Maximum usable frequency">maximum usable frequency</a>, a limit on the highest <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> usable for communications.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Both long-term and short-term variations in solar activity are theorized to affect global climate, but it has proven challenging to quantify the link between solar variation and climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haigh_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-haigh-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early research attempted to correlate weather with limited success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spencer_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-spencer-78">[78]</a></sup> followed by attempts to correlate solar activity with global temperature. The cycle also impacts regional climate. Measurements from the SORCE's Spectral Irradiance Monitor show that solar UV variability produces, for example, colder winters in the U.S. and northern Europe and warmer winters in Canada and southern Europe during solar minima.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SolarForcing_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-SolarForcing-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Three hypothetical mechanisms mediate solar variations' climate impacts:
</p>
<ul><li>Total solar irradiance ("<a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">Radiative forcing</a>").</li>
<li>Ultraviolet irradiance. The UV component varies by more than the total, so if UV were for some (as yet unknown) reason having a disproportionate effect, this might affect climate.</li>
<li>Solar wind-mediated galactic <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> changes, which may affect cloud cover.</li></ul>
<p>The sunspot cycle variation of 0.1% has small but detectable effects on the Earth’s climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Camp and Tung suggest that solar irradiance correlates with a variation of 0.18 K ±0.08 K (0.32 °F ±0.14 °F) in measured average global temperature between solar maximum and minimum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solar-climate_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-solar-climate-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other statistically significant effects have been reported. One study found a relationship with wheat prices<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>, and another found a correlation with the flow of water in the <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_River" title="Paraná River">Paraná River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Eleven-year cycles have been found in tree-ring thicknesses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luthardt2017_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luthardt2017-9">[9]</a></sup> and layers at the bottom of a lake<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NeoP_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NeoP-10">[10]</a></sup> hundreds of millions of years ago.
</p><p>The current scientific consensus, most specifically that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>, is that solar variations do play a marginal role in driving <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-haigh_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-haigh-77">[77]</a></sup> since the measured magnitude of recent solar variation is much smaller than the forcing due to greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grida_fig6-6_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-grida_fig6-6-86">[86]</a></sup> Also, solar activity in the 2010s was not higher than in the 1950s (see above), whereas <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> had risen markedly. Otherwise, the level of understanding of solar impacts on weather is low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_dynamo">Solar dynamo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Solar dynamo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_dynamo" title="Solar dynamo">Solar dynamo</a></div>
<p>The 11-year sunspot cycle is half of a 22-year <a href="/wiki/Babcock_Model" title="Babcock Model">Babcock</a>–Leighton solar dynamo cycle, which corresponds to an oscillatory exchange of energy between <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">toroidal and poloidal</a> solar magnetic fields. At <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">solar-cycle maximum</a>, the external poloidal dipolar magnetic field is near its dynamo-cycle minimum strength, but an internal <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">toroidal</a> quadrupolar field, generated through differential rotation within the <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">tachocline</a>, is near its maximum strength. At this point in the dynamo cycle, buoyant upwelling within the <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">Convection zone</a> forces emergence of the toroidal magnetic field through the photosphere, giving rise to pairs of sunspots, roughly aligned east–west with opposite magnetic polarities. The magnetic polarity of sunspot pairs alternates every solar cycle, a phenomenon known as the Hale cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarcycle_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-solarcycle-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the solar cycle’s declining phase, energy shifts from the internal toroidal magnetic field to the external poloidal field, and sunspots diminish in number. At solar minimum, the toroidal field is, correspondingly, at minimum strength, sunspots are relatively rare and the poloidal field is at maximum strength. During the next cycle, differential rotation converts magnetic energy back from the poloidal to the toroidal field, with a polarity that is opposite to the previous cycle. The process carries on continuously, and in an idealized, simplified scenario, each 11-year sunspot cycle corresponds to a change in the polarity of the Sun's large-scale magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">tachocline</a> has long been thought to be the key to generating the Sun's large-scale magnetic field, recent research has questioned this assumption.  Radio observations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_dwarfs" title="Brown dwarfs">brown dwarfs</a> have indicated that they also maintain large-scale magnetic fields and may display cycles of magnetic activity.  The Sun has a radiative core surrounded by a convective envelope, and at the boundary of these two is the <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">tachocline</a>.  However, brown dwarfs lack radiative cores and tachoclines.  Their structure consists of a solar-like convective envelope that exists from core to surface.  Since they lack a <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">tachocline</a> yet still display solar-like magnetic activity, it has been suggested that solar magnetic activity is only generated in the convective envelope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
