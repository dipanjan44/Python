<p><b>Mercury</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Hg</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 80. It is commonly known as <b>quicksilver</b> and was formerly named <b>hydrargyrum</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">hy-<span style="font-size:90%">DRAR</span>-jər-əm</i></a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy</a>, silvery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-block" title="D-block">d-block</a> element, mercury is the only metallic element that is liquid at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard conditions for temperature and pressure</a>; the only other element that is liquid under these conditions is <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>, though metals such as <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> melt just above <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>.
</p><p>Mercury occurs in deposits throughout the world mostly as <a href="/wiki/Cinnabar" title="Cinnabar">cinnabar</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_sulfide" title="Mercuric sulfide">mercuric sulfide</a>). The red pigment <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a> is obtained by grinding natural cinnabar or synthetic mercuric sulfide.
</p><p>Mercury is used in <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manometer" title="Manometer">manometers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Float_valve" title="Float valve">float valves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_switch" title="Mercury switch">mercury switches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_relay" title="Mercury relay">mercury relays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a> and other devices, though concerns about the element's toxicity have led to mercury thermometers and sphygmomanometers being largely phased out in clinical environments in favor of alternatives such as <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">galinstan</a>-filled glass thermometers and <a href="/wiki/Thermistor" title="Thermistor">thermistor</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Infrared_thermometer" title="Infrared thermometer">infrared</a>-based electronic instruments.  Likewise, mechanical pressure gauges and electronic strain gauge sensors have replaced mercury sphygmomanometers.
</p><p>Mercury remains in use in scientific research applications and in <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(dentistry)" title="Amalgam (dentistry)">amalgam</a> for <a href="/wiki/Dental_restoration" title="Dental restoration">dental restoration</a> in some locales. It is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting">fluorescent lighting</a>. Electricity passed through mercury vapor in a fluorescent lamp produces short-wave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>, which then causes the phosphor in the tube to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluoresce" title="Fluoresce">fluoresce</a>, making visible light.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">Mercury poisoning</a> can result from exposure to water-soluble forms of mercury (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_chloride" title="Mercuric chloride">mercuric chloride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>), by inhalation of mercury vapor, or by ingesting any form of mercury.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1836" data-file-width="2448" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg/220px-Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg/330px-Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg/440px-Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pound-coin-floating-in-mercury.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_pound_(British_decimal_coin)" title="One pound (British decimal coin)">pound coin</a> (density ~7.6 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) floats in mercury due to the combination of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoyant_force" title="Buoyant force">buoyant force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Mercury is a heavy, silvery-white liquid metal. Compared to other metals, it is a poor conductor of heat, but a fair conductor of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a> of −38.83 °C and a <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> of 356.73 °C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Senese1997_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Senese1997-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norrby_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norrby-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> both the lowest of any stable metal, although preliminary experiments on <a href="/wiki/Copernicium" title="Copernicium">copernicium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flerovium" title="Flerovium">flerovium</a> have indicated that they have even lower boiling points (copernicium being the element below mercury in the periodic table, following the trend of decreasing boiling points down group 12).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Upon freezing, the volume of mercury decreases by 3.59% and its density changes from 13.69 g/cm<sup>3</sup> when liquid to 14.184 g/cm<sup>3</sup> when solid.  The coefficient of volume expansion is 181.59 × 10<sup>−6</sup> at 0 °C, 181.71 × 10<sup>−6</sup> at 20 °C and 182.50 × 10<sup>−6</sup> at 100 °C (per °C). Solid mercury is malleable and ductile and can be cut with a knife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>A complete explanation of mercury's extreme volatility delves deep into the realm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>, but it can be summarized as follows: mercury has a unique <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configuration</a> where electrons fill up all the available 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 3d, 4s, 4p, 4d, 4f, 5s, 5p, 5d, and 6s <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell#Subshells" title="Electron shell">subshells</a>. Because this configuration strongly resists removal of an electron, mercury behaves similarly to <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a>, which form weak bonds and hence melt at low temperatures.
</p><p>The stability of the 6s shell is due to the presence of a filled 4f shell. An f shell poorly screens the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_charge" title="Nuclear charge">nuclear charge</a> that increases the attractive <a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb's law">Coulomb interaction</a> of the 6s shell and the nucleus (see <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a>). The absence of a filled inner f shell is the reason for the somewhat higher melting temperature of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, although both these metals still melt easily and, in addition, have unusually low boiling points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Senese1997_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Senese1997-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norrby_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norrby-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span></h3>
<p>Mercury does not react with most acids, such as dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_acid" title="Oxidizing acid">oxidizing acids</a> such as concentrated sulfuric acid and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a> dissolve it to give <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_sulfate" title="Mercuric sulfate">sulfate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_nitrate" title="Mercury(II) nitrate">nitrate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_chloride" title="Mercury(II) chloride">chloride</a>. Like silver, mercury reacts with atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. Mercury reacts with solid sulfur flakes, which are used in mercury spill kits to absorb mercury (spill kits also use <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a> and powdered zinc).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Amalgams">Amalgams</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="3000" height="73" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg/220px-Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg/330px-Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg/440px-Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_discharge_tube.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mercury-discharge spectral calibration lamp</div></div></div>
<p>Mercury dissolves many other metals such as gold and silver to form <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgams</a>. Iron is an exception, and iron flasks have traditionally been used to trade mercury. Several other first row transition metals with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> are reluctant to form amalgams. Other elements that do not readily form amalgams with mercury include <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sodium_amalgam" title="Sodium amalgam">Sodium amalgam</a> is a common reducing agent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, and is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_sodium" title="High-pressure sodium">high-pressure sodium</a> lamps.
</p><p>Mercury readily combines with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_amalgam" title="Aluminium amalgam">mercury-aluminium amalgam</a> when the two pure metals come into contact. Since the amalgam destroys the <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> layer which protects metallic aluminium from oxidizing in-depth (as in iron <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rusting</a>), even small amounts of mercury can seriously corrode aluminium. For this reason, mercury is not allowed aboard an aircraft under most circumstances because of the risk of it forming an amalgam with exposed aluminium parts in the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorrAl_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorrAl-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal_embrittlement#Mercury_embrittlement" title="Liquid metal embrittlement">Mercury embrittlement</a> is the most common type of liquid metal embrittlement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_mercury" title="Isotopes of mercury">Isotopes of mercury</a></div>
<p>There are seven stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of mercury with <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">202</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Hg</span> being the most abundant (29.86%). The longest-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> are <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">194</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Hg</span> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 444 years, and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">203</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Hg</span> with a half-life of 46.612 days. Most of the remaining radioisotopes have half-lives that are less than a day. <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">199</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Hg</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">201</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Hg</span> are the most often studied <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a>-active nuclei, having spins of ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> and ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>Hg is the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_symbol" title="Chemical symbol">chemical symbol</a> for mercury. It comes from <i>hydrargyrum</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinized</a> form of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word ὑδράργυρος (<i>hydrargyros</i>), which is a compound word meaning "water-silver" (from ὑδρ- <i>hydr-</i>, the root of ὕδωρ, "water," and ἄργυρος <i>argyros</i> "silver") – since it is liquid like water and shiny like silver. The element was named after the Roman god <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a>, known for his speed and mobility. It is associated with the planet <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>; the astrological symbol for the planet is also one of the <a href="/wiki/Alchemical_symbol" title="Alchemical symbol">alchemical symbols</a> for the metal; the Sanskrit word for alchemy is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rasav%C4%81tam" title="Rasavātam">Rasavātam</a></i> which means "the way of mercury".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Mercury is the only metal for which the alchemical planetary name became the common name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stillman_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stillman-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_symbol.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="50" data-file-width="50" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Mercury_symbol.svg/120px-Mercury_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Mercury_symbol.svg/180px-Mercury_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Mercury_symbol.svg/240px-Mercury_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_symbol.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The symbol for <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">the planet Mercury</a> (☿) has been used since ancient times to represent the element</div></div></div>
<p>Mercury was found in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptian</a> tombs that date from 1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, mercury use was thought to prolong life, heal fractures, and maintain generally good health, although it is now known that exposure to mercury vapor leads to serious adverse health effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The first emperor of China, <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qín Shǐ Huáng Dì</a>—allegedly buried in a <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_the_First_Qin_Emperor" title="Mausoleum of the First Qin Emperor">tomb</a> that contained rivers of flowing mercury on a model of the land he ruled, representative of the rivers of China—was killed by drinking a mercury and powdered <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a> mixture formulated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" title="Qin Dynasty">Qin</a> alchemists (causing <a href="/wiki/Liver_failure" title="Liver failure">liver failure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brain_death" title="Brain death">brain death</a>) who intended to give him eternal life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wright_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-wright-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Khumarawayh_ibn_Ahmad_ibn_Tulun" title="Khumarawayh ibn Ahmad ibn Tulun">Khumarawayh ibn Ahmad ibn Tulun</a>, the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tulunid" title="Tulunid">Tulunid</a> ruler of Egypt (r. 884–896), known for his extravagance and profligacy, reportedly built a basin filled with mercury, on which he would lie on top of air-filled cushions and be rocked to sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2014 "large quantities" of mercury were discovered in a chamber 60 feet below the 1800-year-old pyramid known as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temple_of_the_Feathered_Serpent" title="Temple of the Feathered Serpent">Temple of the Feathered Serpent</a>," "the third largest pyramid of <a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a>," Mexico along with "jade statues, jaguar remains, a box filled with carved shells and rubber balls."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yuhas_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yuhas-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greeks</a> used <a href="/wiki/Cinnabar" title="Cinnabar">cinnabar</a> (mercury sulfide) in ointments; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptians" title="Ancient Egyptians">ancient Egyptians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> used it in <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Lamanai" title="Lamanai">Lamanai</a>, once a major city of the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya civilization</a>, a pool of mercury was found under a marker in a <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_ballcourt" title="Mesoamerican ballcourt">Mesoamerican ballcourt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> By 500 BC mercury was used to make <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgams</a> (Medieval Latin <i>amalgama</i>, "alloy of mercury") with other metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">Alchemists</a> thought of mercury as the <a href="/wiki/Prima_materia" title="Prima materia">First Matter</a> from which all metals were formed. They believed that different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">metals</a> could be produced by varying the quality and quantity of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> contained within the mercury. The purest of these was gold, and mercury was called for in attempts at the <a href="/wiki/Chrysopoeia" title="Chrysopoeia">transmutation</a> of base (or impure) metals into gold, which was the goal of many alchemists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stillman_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stillman-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mines in <a href="/wiki/Almad%C3%A9n" title="Almadén">Almadén</a> (Spain), <a href="/wiki/Monte_Amiata" title="Monte Amiata">Monte Amiata</a> (Italy), and <a href="/wiki/Idrija" title="Idrija">Idrija</a> (now Slovenia) dominated mercury production from the opening of the mine in Almadén 2500 years ago, until new deposits were found at the end of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MercHz_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MercHz-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Mercury_minerals" title="Category:Mercury minerals">Category:Mercury minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Mercury_mines" title="Category:Mercury mines">Category:Mercury mines</a></div>
<p>Mercury is an extremely rare element in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, having an average crustal abundance by mass of only 0.08 parts per million (ppm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Because it does not blend <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemically</a> with those elements that constitute the majority of the crustal mass, mercury ores can be extraordinarily concentrated considering the element's abundance in ordinary rock. The richest mercury ores contain up to 2.5% mercury by mass, and even the leanest concentrated deposits are at least 0.1% mercury (12,000 times average crustal abundance). It is found either as a native metal (rare) or in <a href="/wiki/Cinnabar" title="Cinnabar">cinnabar</a>, metacinnabar, <a href="/wiki/Corderoite" title="Corderoite">corderoite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livingstonite" title="Livingstonite">livingstonite</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, with cinnabar (HgS) being the most common ore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacinnabar_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-metacinnabar-29">[29]</a></sup> Mercury ores <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">usually occur in very young <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenic belts</a> where rocks of high density are forced to the crust of Earth,</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> often in hot springs or other <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in 1558, with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Patio_process" title="Patio process">patio process</a> to extract silver from ore using mercury, mercury became an essential resource in the economy of Spain and its American colonies. Mercury was used to extract silver from the lucrative mines in <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. Initially, the Spanish Crown's mines in Almadén in Southern Spain supplied all the mercury for the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Mercury deposits were discovered in the New World, and more than 100,000 tons of mercury were mined from the region of <a href="/wiki/Huancavelica" title="Huancavelica">Huancavelica</a>, Peru, over the course of three centuries following the discovery of deposits there in 1563. The patio process and later <a href="/wiki/Pan_amalgamation" title="Pan amalgamation">pan amalgamation</a> process continued to create great demand for mercury to treat silver ores until the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury-27128.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="696" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Mercury-27128.jpg/220px-Mercury-27128.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Mercury-27128.jpg/330px-Mercury-27128.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Mercury-27128.jpg/440px-Mercury-27128.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury-27128.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Native mercury with <a href="/wiki/Cinnabar" title="Cinnabar">cinnabar</a>, Socrates mine, <a href="/wiki/Sonoma_County,_California" title="Sonoma County, California">Sonoma County, California</a>. Cinnabar sometimes alters to native mercury in the oxidized zone of mercury deposits.</div></div></div>
<p>Former mines in Italy, the United States and Mexico, which once produced a large proportion of the world supply, have now been completely mined out or, in the case of Slovenia (<a href="/wiki/Idrija" title="Idrija">Idrija</a>) and Spain (<a href="/wiki/Almad%C3%A9n" title="Almadén">Almadén</a>), shut down due to the fall of the price of mercury. <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDermitt,_Nevada-Oregon" title="McDermitt, Nevada-Oregon">McDermitt</a> Mine, the last mercury mine in the United States, closed in 1992. The price of mercury has been highly volatile over the years and in 2006 was $650 per 76-pound (34.46 kg) flask.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brooks_usgs_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-brooks_usgs-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mercury is extracted by heating cinnabar in a current of air and condensing the vapor. The equation for this extraction is
</p>
<dl><dd>HgS + O<sub>2</sub> → Hg + SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In 2005, China was the top producer of mercury with almost two-thirds global share followed by <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Several other countries are believed to have unrecorded production of mercury from copper <a href="/wiki/Electrowinning" title="Electrowinning">electrowinning</a> processes and by recovery from effluents.
</p><p>Because of the high toxicity of mercury, both the mining of cinnabar and refining for mercury are hazardous and historic causes of mercury poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In China, prison labor was used by a private mining company as recently as the 1950s to develop new cinnabar mines. Thousands of prisoners were used by the Luo Xi mining company to establish new tunnels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GREEN_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-GREEN-36">[36]</a></sup> Worker health in functioning mines is at high risk.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> directive calling for compact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_bulbs" title="Fluorescent bulbs">fluorescent bulbs</a> to be made mandatory by 2012 has encouraged China to re-open cinnabar mines to obtain the mercury required for CFL bulb manufacture. Environmental dangers have been a concern, particularly in the southern cities of <a href="/wiki/Foshan" title="Foshan">Foshan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a> province in the southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GREEN_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-GREEN-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Abandoned mercury mine processing sites often contain very hazardous waste piles of roasted cinnabar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcine" title="Calcine">calcines</a>. Water run-off from such sites is a recognized source of ecological damage. Former mercury mines may be suited for constructive re-use. For example, in 1976 <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_County,_California" title="Santa Clara County, California">Santa Clara County, California</a> purchased the historic <a href="/wiki/Almaden_Quicksilver_County_Park" title="Almaden Quicksilver County Park">Almaden Quicksilver Mine</a> and created a county park on the site, after conducting extensive safety and environmental analysis of the property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Mercury_compounds" title="Category:Mercury compounds">Category:Mercury compounds</a></div>
<p>Mercury exists in two oxidation states, I and II.  Despite claims otherwise,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Hg(III) and Hg(IV) compounds remain unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Compounds_of_mercury.28I.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds_of_mercury(I)">Compounds of mercury(I)</span></h3>
<p>Unlike its lighter neighbors, cadmium and zinc, mercury usually forms simple stable compounds with metal-metal bonds. Most mercury(I) compounds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a> and feature the dimeric cation, Hg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>. Stable derivatives include the chloride and nitrate. Treatment of Hg(I) compounds complexation with strong ligands such as sulfide, cyanide, etc. induces disproportionation to <span class="chemf nowrap">Hg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and elemental mercury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henderson_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-henderson-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_chloride" title="Mercury(I) chloride">Mercury(I) chloride</a>, a colorless solid also known as <a href="/wiki/Calomel" title="Calomel">calomel</a>, is really the compound with the formula Hg<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>, with the connectivity Cl-Hg-Hg-Cl. It is a standard in electrochemistry. It reacts with chlorine to give mercuric chloride, which resists further oxidation. <a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_hydride" title="Mercury(I) hydride">Mercury(I) hydride</a>, a colorless gas, has the formula HgH, containing no Hg-Hg bond.
</p><p>Indicative of its tendency to bond to itself, mercury forms <a href="/wiki/Mercury_polycations" title="Mercury polycations">mercury polycations</a>, which consist of linear chains of mercury centers, capped with a positive charge. One example is <span class="chemf nowrap">Hg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>(AsF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>)</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Compounds_of_mercury.28II.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds_of_mercury(II)">Compounds of mercury(II)</span></h3>
<p>Mercury(II) is the most common oxidation state and is the main one in nature as well. All four mercuric halides are known. They form tetrahedral complexes with other ligands but the halides adopt linear coordination geometry, somewhat like Ag<sup>+</sup> does. Best known is <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_chloride" title="Mercury(II) chloride">mercury(II) chloride</a>, an easily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimating</a> white solid. HgCl<sub>2</sub> forms <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a> that are typically tetrahedral, e.g. <span class="chemf nowrap">HgCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_oxide" title="Mercury(II) oxide">Mercury(II) oxide</a>, the main <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of mercury, arises when the metal is exposed to air for long periods at elevated temperatures. It reverts to the elements upon heating near 400 °C, as was demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> in an early synthesis of pure <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-11">[11]</a></sup> Hydroxides of mercury are poorly characterized, as they are for its neighbors gold and silver.
</p><p>Being a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HSAB" title="HSAB">soft metal</a>, mercury forms very stable derivatives with the heavier chalcogens. Preeminent is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_sulfide" title="Mercury(II) sulfide">mercury(II) sulfide</a>, HgS, which occurs in nature as the ore cinnabar and is the brilliant pigment <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermillion</a>. Like ZnS, HgS crystallizes in two <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">forms</a>, the reddish cubic form and the black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_blende" title="Zinc blende">zinc blende</a> form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup> The latter sometimes occurs naturally as metacinnabar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metacinnabar_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-metacinnabar-29">[29]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_selenide" title="Mercury(II) selenide">Mercury(II) selenide</a> (HgSe) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_telluride" title="Mercury(II) telluride">mercury(II) telluride</a> (HgTe) are also known, these as well as various derivatives, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_cadmium_telluride" title="Mercury cadmium telluride">mercury cadmium telluride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_zinc_telluride" title="Mercury zinc telluride">mercury zinc telluride</a> being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductors" title="Semiconductors">semiconductors</a> useful as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_detector" title="Infrared detector">infrared detector</a> materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mercury(II) salts form a variety of complex derivatives with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. These include Millon's base (Hg<sub>2</sub>N<sup>+</sup>), the one-dimensional polymer (salts of <span class="chemf nowrap">HgNH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">n</sub></span>), and "fusible white precipitate" or [Hg(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]Cl<sub>2</sub>. Known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nessler%27s_reagent" title="Nessler's reagent">Nessler's reagent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_tetraiodomercurate(II)" title="Potassium tetraiodomercurate(II)">potassium tetraiodomercurate(II)</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">HgI<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) is still occasionally used to test for ammonia owing to its tendency to form the deeply colored iodide salt of Millon's base.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_fulminate" title="Mercury(II) fulminate">Mercury fulminate</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Detonator" title="Detonator">detonator</a> widely used in <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organomercury_compounds">Organomercury compounds</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organomercury_compound" title="Organomercury compound">Organomercury compound</a></div>
<p>Organic mercury <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> are historically important but are of little industrial value in the western world. Mercury(II) salts are a rare example of simple metal complexes that react directly with aromatic rings. Organomercury compounds are always divalent and usually two-coordinate and linear geometry. Unlike <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organocadmium" title="Organocadmium">organocadmium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organozinc" title="Organozinc">organozinc</a> compounds, organomercury compounds do not react with water. They usually have the formula HgR<sub>2</sub>, which are often volatile, or HgRX, which are often solids, where R is <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> and X is usually halide or acetate. <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">Methylmercury</a>, a generic term for compounds with the formula CH<sub>3</sub>HgX, is a dangerous family of compounds that are often found in <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">polluted</a> water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-methylHg_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-methylHg-44">[44]</a></sup> They arise by a process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomethylation" title="Biomethylation">biomethylation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="608" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG/220px-Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG/330px-Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG/440px-Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maximum_thermometer_close_up_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The bulb of a <a href="/wiki/Mercury-in-glass_thermometer" title="Mercury-in-glass thermometer">mercury-in-glass thermometer</a></div></div></div>
<p>Mercury is used primarily for the manufacture of industrial chemicals or for electrical and electronic applications. It is used in some thermometers, especially ones which are used to measure high temperatures. A still increasing amount is used as gaseous mercury in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamps" title="Fluorescent lamps">fluorescent lamps</a>, while most of the other applications are slowly phased out due to health and safety regulations and is in some applications replaced with less toxic but considerably more expensive <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">Galinstan</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(dentistry)" title="Amalgam (dentistry)">Amalgam (dentistry)</a></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amalgam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="161" data-file-width="200" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/Amalgam.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amalgam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Amalgam filling</div></div></div>
<p>Mercury and its compounds have been used in medicine, although they are much less common today than they once were, now that the toxic effects of mercury and its compounds are more widely understood. The first edition of the Merck's Manual featured many mercuric compounds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> such as:
</p>

<li>Mercuro-iodo-hemol.</li>
<li>Mercury-ammonium chloride</li>
<li>Mercury Benzoate</li>
<li>Mercuric</li>
<li>Mercury Bichloride (Corrosive Mercuric Chloride, U.S.P.)</li>
<li>Mercury Chloride</li>
<li>Mild Mercury Cyanide</li>
<li>Mercury Succinimide</li>

<li>Mercury Iodide</li>
<li>Yellow Mercury Proto-iodide</li>
<li>Black (Hahnemann), Soluble Mercury Oxide</li>
<li>Red Mercury Oxide</li>
<li>Yellow Mercury Oxide</li>
<li>Mercury Salicylate</li>
<li>Mercury Succinimide</li>
<li>Mercury Imido-succinate</li>
<li>Mercury Sulphate</li>
<li>Basic Mercury Subsulphate; Turpeth Mineral</li>

</div>
<p>Mercury is an ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(dentistry)" title="Amalgam (dentistry)">dental amalgams</a>. <a href="/wiki/Thiomersal" title="Thiomersal">Thiomersal</a> (called <i>Thimerosal</i> in the United States) is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> used as a <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservative</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a>, though this use is in decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Thiomersal is metabolized to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethyl_mercury" title="Ethyl mercury">ethyl mercury</a>. Although it was <a href="/wiki/Thiomersal_controversy" title="Thiomersal controversy">widely speculated</a> that this mercury-based preservative could cause or trigger <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> in children, scientific studies showed no evidence supporting any such link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Nevertheless, thiomersal has been removed from, or reduced to trace amounts in all U.S. vaccines recommended for children 6 years of age and under, with the exception of inactivated influenza vaccine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another mercury compound, <a href="/wiki/Merbromin" title="Merbromin">merbromin</a> (Mercurochrome), is a topical antiseptic used for minor cuts and scrapes that is still in use in some countries.
</p><p>Mercury in the form of one of its common ores, cinnabar, is used in various traditional medicines, especially in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese medicine</a>. Review of its safety has found that cinnabar can lead to significant mercury intoxication when heated, consumed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overdose" title="Overdose">overdose</a>, or taken long term, and can have adverse effects at therapeutic doses, though effects from therapeutic doses are typically reversible. Although this form of mercury appears to be less toxic than other forms, its use in traditional Chinese medicine has not yet been justified, as the therapeutic basis for the use of cinnabar is not clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, the use of mercury in medicine has greatly declined in all respects, especially in developed countries. <a href="/wiki/Mercury-in-glass_thermometer" title="Mercury-in-glass thermometer">Thermometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometers</a> containing mercury were invented in the early 18th and late 19th centuries, respectively. In the early 21st century, their use is declining and has been banned in some countries, states and medical institutions. In 2002, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> passed legislation to phase out the sale of <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription" title="Medical prescription">non-prescription</a> mercury thermometers. In 2003, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> became the first states to ban mercury blood pressure devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Mercury compounds are found in some <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drugs</a>, including topical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiseptics" title="Antiseptics">antiseptics</a>, stimulant laxatives, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaper_rash" title="Diaper rash">diaper-rash</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ointment" title="Ointment">ointment</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eye_drops" title="Eye drops">eye drops</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nasal_spray" title="Nasal spray">nasal sprays</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a> has "inadequate data to establish general recognition of the safety and effectiveness" of the mercury ingredients in these products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Mercury is still used in some diuretics although substitutes now exist for most therapeutic uses.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_of_chlorine_and_caustic_soda">Production of chlorine and caustic soda</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Chlorine</a> is produced from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> (common salt, NaCl) using <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> to separate the metallic <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> from the chlorine gas. Usually the salt is dissolved in water to produce a brine. By-products of any such <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">chloralkali process</a> are hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (NaOH), which is commonly called caustic soda or <a href="/wiki/Lye" title="Lye">lye</a>. By far the largest use of mercury<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USEPA_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-USEPA-54">[54]</a></sup> in the late 20th century was in the mercury cell process (also called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castner-Kellner_process" title="Castner-Kellner process">Castner-Kellner process</a>) where metallic sodium is formed as an <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgam</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> made from mercury; this sodium is then reacted with water to produce sodium hydroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Many of the industrial mercury releases of the 20th century came from this process, although modern plants claimed to be safe in this regard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USEPA_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-USEPA-54">[54]</a></sup> After about 1985, all new chloralkali production facilities that were built in the United States used <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide#Methods_of_production" title="Sodium hydroxide">membrane cell or diaphragm cell technologies</a> to produce chlorine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_uses">Laboratory uses</span></h3>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Mercury-in-glass_thermometer" title="Mercury-in-glass thermometer">medical thermometers</a>, especially those for high temperatures, are filled with mercury; they are gradually disappearing. In the United States, non-prescription sale of mercury fever thermometers has been banned since 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mercury is also found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_mirror_telescopes" title="Liquid mirror telescopes">liquid mirror telescopes</a>.
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Meridian_circle#Structure" title="Meridian circle">transit telescopes</a> use a basin of mercury to form a flat and absolutely horizontal mirror, useful in determining an absolute vertical or perpendicular reference.  Concave horizontal parabolic mirrors may be formed by rotating liquid mercury on a disk, the parabolic form of the liquid thus formed reflecting and focusing incident light. Such telescopes are cheaper than conventional large mirror telescopes by up to a factor of 100, but the mirror cannot be tilted and always points straight up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Liquid mercury is a part of popular secondary reference <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a> (called the <a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_chloride" title="Mercury(I) chloride">calomel electrode</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> as an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_hydrogen_electrode" title="Standard hydrogen electrode">standard hydrogen electrode</a>. The calomel electrode is used to work out the <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">electrode potential</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_cell" title="Half cell">half cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Last, but not least, the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of mercury, −38.8344 °C, is a fixed point used as a temperature standard for the International Temperature Scale (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITS-90" title="ITS-90">ITS-90</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Polarography" title="Polarography">polarography</a> both the <a href="/wiki/Dropping_mercury_electrode" title="Dropping mercury electrode">dropping mercury electrode</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Hanging_mercury_drop_electrode" title="Hanging mercury drop electrode">hanging mercury drop electrode</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> use elemental mercury. This use allows a new uncontaminated electrode to be available for each measurement or each new experiment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span></h3>
<p>Gaseous mercury is used in <a href="/wiki/Mercury-vapor_lamp" title="Mercury-vapor lamp">mercury-vapor lamps</a> and some "<a href="/wiki/Neon_sign" title="Neon sign">neon sign</a>" type advertising signs and <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a>. Those low-pressure lamps emit very spectrally narrow lines, which are traditionally used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_spectroscopy" title="Optical spectroscopy">optical spectroscopy</a> for calibration of spectral position. Commercial calibration lamps are sold for this purpose; reflecting a fluorescent ceiling light into a spectrometer is a common calibration practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Gaseous mercury is also found in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_filled_tube" title="Gas filled tube">electron tubes</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Ignitron" title="Ignitron">ignitrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thyratron" title="Thyratron">thyratrons</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_arc_rectifier" title="Mercury arc rectifier">mercury arc rectifiers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> It is also used in specialist medical care lamps for skin tanning and disinfection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Gaseous mercury is added to <a href="/wiki/Cold_cathode" title="Cold cathode">cold cathode</a> <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>-filled lamps to increase the <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a>. An argon-filled lamp without mercury will have dull spots and will fail to light correctly. Lighting containing mercury can be <a href="/wiki/Neon_sign#Bombardment" title="Neon sign">bombarded</a>/oven pumped only once. When added to <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a> filled tubes the light produced will be inconsistent red/blue spots until the initial burning-in process is completed; eventually it will light a consistent dull off-blue color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmetics">Cosmetics</span></h4>
<p>Mercury, as <a href="/wiki/Thiomersal" title="Thiomersal">thiomersal</a>, is widely used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Mascara" title="Mascara">mascara</a>. In 2008, Minnesota became the first state in the United States to ban intentionally added mercury in cosmetics, giving it a tougher standard than the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study in geometric mean urine mercury concentration identified a previously unrecognized source of exposure (skin care products) to inorganic mercury among <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> residents. Population-based biomonitoring also showed that mercury concentration levels are higher in consumers of seafood and fish meals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firearms">Firearms</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_fulminate" title="Mercury(II) fulminate">Mercury(II) fulminate</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_explosive" title="Primary explosive">primary explosive</a> which is mainly used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primer_(firearm)#Internal_priming" title="Primer (firearm)">primer</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Cartridge_(firearms)" title="Cartridge (firearms)">cartridge</a> in firearms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_uses">Historic uses</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2039" data-file-width="3916" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg/220px-Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg/330px-Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg/440px-Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Switch_without_housing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <i>Single-Pole, Single-Throw</i> (<i>SPST</i>) mercury switch.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2415" data-file-width="1704" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg/170px-Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg/255px-Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg/340px-Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barometer_mercury_column_hg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mercury <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manometer" title="Manometer">manometer</a> to measure pressure</div></div></div>
<p>Many historic applications made use of the peculiar physical properties of mercury, especially as a dense liquid and a liquid metal:
</p>
<ul><li>Quantities of liquid mercury ranging from 90 to 600 grams (3.2 to 21.2 oz) have been recovered from elite <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a> tombs (100-700AD)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yuhas_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yuhas-22">[22]</a></sup> or ritual caches at six sites. This mercury may have been used in bowls as <a href="/wiki/Mirrors_in_Mesoamerican_culture" title="Mirrors in Mesoamerican culture">mirrors</a> for <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divinatory</a> purposes. Five of these date to the Classic Period of Maya civilization (c. 250–900) but one example predated this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_Spain" title="Islamic Spain">Islamic Spain</a>, it was used for filling decorative pools. Later, the American artist <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Calder" title="Alexander Calder">Alexander Calder</a> built a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_fountain" title="Mercury fountain">mercury fountain</a> for the Spanish Pavilion at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exposition_Internationale_des_Arts_et_Techniques_dans_la_Vie_Moderne_(1937)" title="Exposition Internationale des Arts et Techniques dans la Vie Moderne (1937)">1937 World Exhibition in Paris</a>. The fountain is now on display at the <a href="/wiki/Fundaci%C3%B3_Joan_Mir%C3%B3" title="Fundació Joan Miró">Fundació Joan Miró</a> in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mercury was used inside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wobbler_(fishing)" title="Wobbler (fishing)">wobbler</a> lures. Its heavy, liquid form made it useful since the lures made an attractive irregular movement when the mercury moved inside the plug. Such use was stopped due to environmental concerns, but illegal preparation of modern fishing plugs has occurred.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_lens" title="Fresnel lens">Fresnel lenses</a> of old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lighthouses" title="Lighthouses">lighthouses</a> used to float and rotate in a bath of mercury which acted like a bearing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mercury <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometers</a> (blood pressure meter), <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_pump" title="Diffusion pump">diffusion pumps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_coulometer" title="Mercury coulometer">coulometers</a>, and many other laboratory instruments. As an opaque liquid with a high density and a nearly linear thermal expansion, it is ideal for this role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>As an electrically conductive liquid, it was used in <a href="/wiki/Mercury_switch" title="Mercury switch">mercury switches</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Light_switch#Mercury_switch" title="Light switch">home mercury light switches</a> installed prior to 1970), tilt switches used in old fire detectors, and tilt switches in some home thermostats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>Owing to its acoustic properties, mercury was used as the propagation medium in <a href="/wiki/Delay_line_memory#Mercury_delay_lines" title="Delay line memory">delay line memory</a> devices used in early digital computers of the mid-20th century.</li>
<li>Experimental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_vapor_turbine" title="Mercury vapor turbine">mercury vapor turbines</a> were installed to increase the efficiency of fossil-fuel electrical power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The South Meadow power plant in Hartford, CT employed mercury as its <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a>, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_cycle_power_plant" title="Binary cycle power plant">binary</a> configuration with a secondary water circuit, for a number of years starting in the late 1920s in a drive to improve plant efficiency. Several other plants were built, including the Schiller Station in Portsmouth, NH, which went online in 1950. The idea did not catch on industry-wide due to the weight and toxicity of mercury, as well as the advent of <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> steam plants in later years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li>Similarly, liquid mercury was used as a <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> for some <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>; however, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> is proposed for reactors cooled with liquid metal, because the high density of mercury requires much more energy to circulate as coolant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mercury was a propellant for early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_engines" title="Ion engines">ion engines</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrically_powered_spacecraft_propulsion" title="Electrically powered spacecraft propulsion">electric space propulsion</a> systems. Advantages were mercury's high molecular weight, low ionization energy, low dual-ionization energy, high liquid density and liquid storability at <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>. Disadvantages were concerns regarding environmental impact associated with ground testing and concerns about eventual cooling and condensation of some of the propellant on the spacecraft in long-duration operations. The first spaceflight to use electric propulsion was a mercury-fueled ion thruster developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Glenn_Research_Center" title="NASA Glenn Research Center">NASA Lewis</a> and flown on the Space Electric Rocket Test "<a href="/wiki/SERT-1" title="SERT-1">SERT-1</a>" spacecraft launched by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> at its <a href="/wiki/Wallops_Flight_Facility" title="Wallops Flight Facility">Wallops Flight Facility</a> in 1964. The SERT-1 flight was followed up by the SERT-2 flight in 1970. Mercury and <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> were preferred propellants for ion engines until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_Research_Laboratory" title="Hughes Research Laboratory">Hughes Research Laboratory</a> performed studies finding <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a> gas to be a suitable replacement. Xenon is now the preferred propellant for ion engines as it has a high molecular weight, little or no reactivity due to its <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a> nature, and has a high liquid density under mild cryogenic storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Others applications made use of the chemical properties of mercury:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Mercury_battery" title="Mercury battery">mercury battery</a> is a non-rechargeable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical_battery" title="Electrochemical battery">electrochemical battery</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Primary_cell" title="Primary cell">primary cell</a>, that was common in the middle of the 20th century. It was used in a wide variety of applications and was available in various sizes, particularly button sizes. Its constant voltage output and long shelf life gave it a niche use for camera light meters and hearing aids. The mercury cell was effectively banned in most countries in the 1990s due to concerns about the mercury contaminating landfills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMERC_Mercury_Use_in_Batteries_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMERC_Mercury_Use_in_Batteries-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mercury was used for preserving wood, developing <a href="/wiki/Daguerreotype" title="Daguerreotype">daguerreotypes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silvering" title="Silvering">silvering</a> <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-fouling_paint" title="Anti-fouling paint">anti-fouling paints</a> (discontinued in 1990), <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a> (discontinued in 1995), handheld maze games, cleaning, and road leveling devices in cars. Mercury compounds have been used in <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptics</a>, laxatives, <a href="/wiki/Antidepressant" title="Antidepressant">antidepressants</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">antisyphilitics</a>.</li>
<li>It was allegedly used by <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">allied spies</a> to sabotage Luftwaffe planes: a mercury paste was applied to bare <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, causing the metal to rapidly <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrode</a>; this would cause structural failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">Chloralkali process</a>: The largest industrial use of mercury during the 20th century was in electrolysis for separating chlorine and sodium from brine; mercury being the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castner-Kellner_process" title="Castner-Kellner process">Castner-Kellner process</a>. The chlorine was used for bleaching paper (hence the location of many of these plants near paper mills) while the sodium was used to make sodium hydroxide for soaps and other cleaning products. This usage has largely been discontinued, replaced with other technologies that utilize membrane cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li>As <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> in some types of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mercury_battery" title="Mercury battery">mercury cells</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> production, handheld games, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a>.</li>
<li>Mercury was once used as a gun barrel bore cleaner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francis1849_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francis1849-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castles2005_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castles2005-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li>From the mid-18th to the mid-19th centuries, a process called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carroting" title="Carroting">carroting</a>" was used in the making of <a href="/wiki/Felt" title="Felt">felt</a> hats. Animal skins were rinsed in an orange solution (the term "carroting" arose from this color) of the mercury compound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_nitrate" title="Mercuric nitrate">mercuric nitrate</a>, Hg(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> This process separated the fur from the pelt and matted it together. This solution and the vapors it produced were highly toxic. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service" title="United States Public Health Service">United States Public Health Service</a> banned the use of mercury in the felt industry in December 1941. The psychological symptoms associated with mercury poisoning inspired the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Mad_as_a_hatter" title="Mad as a hatter">mad as a hatter</a>". <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter" title="Mad Hatter">Mad Hatter</a>" in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> was a play on words based on the older phrase, but the character himself does not exhibit symptoms of mercury poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></li>
<li>Gold and silver mining. Historically, mercury was used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_mining" title="Hydraulic mining">hydraulic gold mining</a> in order to help the gold to sink through the flowing water-gravel mixture. Thin gold particles may form mercury-gold amalgam and therefore increase the gold recovery rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup> Large-scale use of mercury stopped in the 1960s. However, mercury is still used in small scale, often clandestine, gold prospecting. It is estimated that 45,000 metric tons of mercury used in California for <a href="/wiki/Placer_mining" title="Placer mining">placer mining</a> have not been recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Mercury was also used in silver mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_medicinal_uses">Historic medicinal uses</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_chloride" title="Mercury(I) chloride">Mercury(I) chloride</a> (also known as calomel or mercurous chloride) has been used in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">traditional medicine</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Diuretic" title="Diuretic">diuretic</a>, topical <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfectant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxative</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_chloride" title="Mercury(II) chloride">Mercury(II) chloride</a> (also known as mercuric chloride or corrosive sublimate) was once used to treat <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a> (along with other mercury compounds), although it is so toxic that sometimes the symptoms of its toxicity were confused with those of the syphilis it was believed to treat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> It is also used as a disinfectant. <a href="/wiki/Blue_mass" title="Blue mass">Blue mass</a>, a pill or syrup in which mercury is the main ingredient, was prescribed throughout the 19th century for numerous conditions including constipation, depression, child-bearing and toothaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, mercury was administered to children yearly as a laxative and dewormer, and it was used in teething powders for infants. The mercury-containing organohalide <a href="/wiki/Merbromin" title="Merbromin">merbromin</a> (sometimes sold as Mercurochrome) is still widely used but has been banned in some countries such as the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Mercury and most of its compounds are extremely toxic and must be handled with care; in cases of spills involving mercury (such as from certain <a href="/wiki/Mercury-in-glass_thermometer" title="Mercury-in-glass thermometer">thermometers</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_light_bulb" title="Fluorescent light bulb">fluorescent light bulbs</a>), specific cleaning procedures are used to avoid exposure and contain the spill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Protocols call for physically merging smaller droplets on hard surfaces, combining them into a single larger pool for easier removal with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyedropper" title="Eyedropper">eyedropper</a>, or for gently pushing the spill into a disposable container. Vacuum cleaners and brooms cause greater dispersal of the mercury and should not be used. Afterwards, fine <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, or some other powder that readily forms an amalgam (alloy) with mercury at ordinary temperatures is sprinkled over the area before itself being collected and properly disposed of. Cleaning porous surfaces and clothing is not effective at removing all traces of mercury and it is therefore advised to discard these kinds of items should they be exposed to a mercury spill.
</p><p>Mercury can be absorbed through the skin and mucous membranes and mercury vapors can be inhaled, so containers of mercury are securely sealed to avoid spills and evaporation. Heating of mercury, or of compounds of mercury that may decompose when heated, should be carried out with adequate ventilation in order to minimize exposure to mercury vapor. The most toxic forms of mercury are its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethylmercury" title="Dimethylmercury">dimethylmercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>.  Mercury can cause both chronic and acute poisoning.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Releases_in_the_environment">Releases in the environment</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="744" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg/220px-Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg/330px-Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg/440px-Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_in_Ice_Core_Upper_Fremont_Glacier.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Amount of atmospheric mercury deposited at Wyoming's Upper Fremont Glacier over the last 270 years</div></div></div>
<p>Preindustrial deposition rates of mercury from the atmosphere may be about 4 ng /(1 L of ice deposit). Although that can be considered a natural level of exposure, regional or global sources have significant effects. Volcanic eruptions can increase the atmospheric source by 4–6 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, are responsible for approximately half of atmospheric mercury emissions. The human-generated half can be divided into the following estimated percentages:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pacyna_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pacyna-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>65% from stationary combustion, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_plant" title="Coal-fired power plant">coal-fired power plants</a> are the largest aggregate source (40% of U.S. mercury emissions in 1999). This includes power plants fueled with gas where the mercury has not been removed. Emissions from coal combustion are between one and two orders of magnitude higher than emissions from oil combustion, depending on the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pacyna_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pacyna-95">[95]</a></sup></li>
<li>11% from gold production. The three largest point sources for mercury emissions in the U.S. are the three largest gold mines. Hydrogeochemical release of mercury from gold-mine tailings has been accounted as a significant source of atmospheric mercury in eastern Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></li>
<li>6.8% from <a href="/wiki/Non-ferrous_metal" title="Non-ferrous metal">non-ferrous metal</a> production, typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smelter" title="Smelter">smelters</a>.</li>
<li>6.4% from <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> production.</li>
<li>3.0% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_disposal" title="Waste disposal">waste disposal</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_waste" title="Municipal waste">municipal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crematoria" title="Crematoria">crematoria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">sewage sludge</a> incineration.</li>
<li>3.0% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caustic_soda" title="Caustic soda">caustic soda</a> production.</li>
<li>1.4% from <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> production.</li>
<li>1.1% from mercury production, mainly for batteries.</li>
<li>2.0% from other sources.</li></ul>
<p>The above percentages are estimates of the global human-caused mercury emissions in 2000, excluding biomass burning, an important source in some regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pacyna_95-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pacyna-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent atmospheric mercury contamination in outdoor urban air was measured at 0.01–0.02 µg/m<sup>3</sup>. A 2001 study measured mercury levels in 12 indoor sites chosen to represent a cross-section of building types, locations and ages in the New York area. This study found mercury concentrations significantly elevated over outdoor concentrations, at a range of 0.0065 – 0.523 μg/m<sup>3</sup>. The average was 0.069 μg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mercury also enters into the environment through the improper disposal (e.g., land filling, incineration) of certain products. Products containing mercury include: auto parts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>, fluorescent bulbs, medical products, thermometers, and thermostats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> Due to health concerns (see below), <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">toxics use reduction</a> efforts are cutting back or eliminating mercury in such products. For example, the amount of mercury sold in thermostats in the United States decreased from 14.5 tons in 2004 to 3.9 tons in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most thermometers now use pigmented <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> instead of mercury, and <a href="/wiki/Galinstan" title="Galinstan">galinstan</a> alloy thermometers are also an option. Mercury thermometers are still occasionally used in the medical field because they are more accurate than alcohol thermometers, though both are commonly being replaced by electronic thermometers and less commonly by galinstan thermometers. Mercury thermometers are still widely used for certain scientific applications because of their greater accuracy and working range.
</p><p>Historically, one of the largest releases was from the Colex plant, a lithium-isotope separation plant at Oak Ridge, Tennessee. The plant operated in the 1950s and 1960s. Records are incomplete and unclear, but government commissions have estimated that some two million pounds of mercury are unaccounted for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>A serious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_disasters" title="Industrial disasters">industrial disaster</a> was the dumping of mercury compounds into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minamata" title="Minamata">Minamata</a> Bay, Japan. It is estimated that over 3,000 people suffered various deformities, severe mercury poisoning symptoms or death from what became known as <a href="/wiki/Minamata_disease" title="Minamata disease">Minamata disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_plant" title="Tobacco plant">tobacco plant</a> readily absorbs and accumulates <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> such as mercury from the surrounding soil into its leaves. These are subsequently inhaled during <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco smoking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> While mercury is a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalhoutSchulz2011_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-106">[106]</a></sup> studies have largely failed to discover a significant correlation between smoking and Hg uptake by humans compared to sources such as occupational exposure, fish consumption, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amalgam_tooth_fillings" title="Amalgam tooth fillings">amalgam tooth fillings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment_contamination">Sediment contamination</span></h3>
<p>Sediments within large urban-industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a> act as an important sink for point source and diffuse mercury pollution within catchments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneBeriro2015_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-VaneBeriro2015-108">[108]</a></sup> A 2015 study of foreshore sediments from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thames_estuary" title="Thames estuary">Thames estuary</a> measured total mercury at 0.01 to 12.07 mg/kg with mean of 2.10 mg/kg and median of 0.85 mg/kg (n=351).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneBeriro2015_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-VaneBeriro2015-108">[108]</a></sup> The highest mercury concentrations were shown to occur in and around the city of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in association with fine grain muds and high total organic carbon content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneBeriro2015_108-2"><a href="#cite_note-VaneBeriro2015-108">[108]</a></sup> The strong affinity of mercury for carbon rich sediments has also been observed in salt marsh sediments of the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">River Mersey</a> mean of 2 mg/kg up to 5 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneJones2009_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-VaneJones2009-109">[109]</a></sup> These concentrations are far higher than those shown in salt marsh river creek sediments of New Jersey and mangroves of Southern China which exhibit low mercury concentrations of about 0.2 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneHarrison2008_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-VaneHarrison2008-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VaneHarrison2009_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-VaneHarrison2009-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_exposure">Occupational exposure</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_(3986684199).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg/220px-EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg/330px-EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg/440px-EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_%283986684199%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EPA_workers_clean_up_residential_mercury_spill_(3986684199).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>EPA workers clean up residential mercury spill in 2004</div></div></div>
<p>Due to the health effects of mercury exposure, industrial and commercial uses are regulated in many countries. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">NIOSH</a> all treat mercury as an occupational hazard, and have established specific occupational exposure limits. Environmental releases and disposal of mercury are regulated in the U.S. primarily by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_and_symptoms_of_mercury_poisoning">Effects and symptoms of mercury poisoning</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">Mercury poisoning</a></div>
<p>Toxic effects include damage to the brain, kidneys and lungs. Mercury poisoning can result in several diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Acrodynia" title="Acrodynia">acrodynia</a> (pink disease), Hunter-Russell syndrome, and <a href="/wiki/Minamata_disease" title="Minamata disease">Minamata disease</a>.
</p><p>Symptoms typically include sensory impairment (vision, hearing, speech), disturbed sensation and a lack of coordination. The type and degree of symptoms exhibited depend upon the individual toxin, the dose, and the method and duration of exposure. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case%E2%80%93control_studies" title="Case–control studies">Case–control studies</a> have shown effects such as tremors, impaired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> skills, and sleep disturbance in workers with chronic exposure to mercury vapor even at low concentrations in the range 0.7–42 μg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngim_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngim-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liang_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-liang-113">[113]</a></sup> A study has shown that acute exposure (4–8 hours) to calculated elemental mercury levels of 1.1 to 44 mg/m<sup>3</sup> resulted in chest pain, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a>, cough, <a href="/wiki/Hemoptysis" title="Hemoptysis">hemoptysis</a>, impairment of pulmonary function, and evidence of interstitial <a href="/wiki/Pneumonitis" title="Pneumonitis">pneumonitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Acute exposure to mercury vapor has been shown to result in profound central nervous system effects, including psychotic reactions characterized by delirium, hallucinations, and suicidal tendency. Occupational exposure has resulted in broad-ranging functional disturbance, including <a href="/wiki/Erethism" title="Erethism">erethism</a>, irritability, excitability, excessive shyness, and insomnia. With continuing exposure, a fine tremor develops and may escalate to violent muscular spasms. Tremor initially involves the hands and later spreads to the eyelids, lips, and tongue. Long-term, low-level exposure has been associated with more subtle symptoms of erethism, including fatigue, irritability, loss of memory, vivid dreams and depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span></h4>
<p>Research on the treatment of mercury poisoning is limited. Currently available drugs for acute mercurial poisoning include chelators N-acetyl-D, L-<a href="/wiki/Penicillamine" title="Penicillamine">penicillamine</a> (NAP), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Anti-Lewisite" title="British Anti-Lewisite">British Anti-Lewisite</a> (BAL), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,3-dimercapto-1-propanesulfonic_acid" title="2,3-dimercapto-1-propanesulfonic acid">2,3-dimercapto-1-propanesulfonic acid</a> (DMPS), and <a href="/wiki/Dimercaptosuccinic_acid" title="Dimercaptosuccinic acid">dimercaptosuccinic acid</a> (DMSA). In one small study including 11 construction workers exposed to elemental mercury, patients were treated with DMSA and NAP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chelation_therapy" title="Chelation therapy">Chelation therapy</a> with both drugs resulted in the mobilization of a small fraction of the total estimated body mercury. DMSA was able to increase the excretion of mercury to a greater extent than NAP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish">Fish</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_in_fish" title="Mercury in fish">Mercury in fish</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">Fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> have a natural tendency to concentrate mercury in their bodies, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Methylmercury" title="Methylmercury">methylmercury</a>, a highly toxic organic compound of mercury. Species of fish that are high on the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">shark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swordfish" title="Swordfish">swordfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_mackerel" title="King mackerel">king mackerel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bluefin_tuna" title="Bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albacore_tuna" title="Albacore tuna">albacore tuna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tilefish" title="Tilefish">tilefish</a> contain higher concentrations of mercury than others. As mercury and methylmercury are fat soluble, they primarily accumulate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscera" title="Viscera">viscera</a>, although they are also found throughout the muscle tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> When this fish is consumed by a predator, the mercury level is accumulated. Since fish are less efficient at depurating than accumulating methylmercury, fish-tissue concentrations increase over time. Thus species that are high on the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> amass body burdens of mercury that can be ten times higher than the species they consume. This process is called <a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">biomagnification</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">Mercury poisoning</a> happened this way in <a href="/wiki/Minamata,_Kumamoto" title="Minamata, Kumamoto">Minamata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, now called <a href="/wiki/Minamata_disease" title="Minamata disease">Minamata disease</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span></h3>
<p>140 countries agreed in the <a href="/wiki/Minamata_Convention_on_Mercury" title="Minamata Convention on Mercury">Minamata Convention on Mercury</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) to prevent emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> The convention was signed on 10 October 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> is charged with regulating and managing mercury contamination. Several laws give the EPA this authority, including the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Resource_Conservation_and_Recovery_Act" title="Resource Conservation and Recovery Act">Resource Conservation and Recovery Act</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a>. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Containing_and_Rechargeable_Battery_Management_Act" title="Mercury-Containing and Rechargeable Battery Management Act">Mercury-Containing and Rechargeable Battery Management Act</a>, passed in 1996, phases out the use of mercury in batteries, and provides for the efficient and cost-effective disposal of many types of used batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_regs_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_regs-122">[122]</a></sup> North America contributed approximately 11% of the total global anthropogenic mercury emissions in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(1990)" title="Clean Air Act (1990)">Clean Air Act</a>, passed in 1990, put mercury on a list of toxic pollutants that need to be controlled to the greatest possible extent. Thus, industries that release high concentrations of mercury into the environment agreed to install maximum achievable control technologies (MACT). In March 2005, the EPA promulgated a regulation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> that added power plants to the list of sources that should be controlled and instituted a national <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">cap and trade</a> system. States were given until November 2006 to impose stricter controls, but after a legal challenge from several states, the regulations were struck down by a federal appeals court on 8 February 2008. The rule was deemed not sufficient to protect the health of persons living near coal-fired power plants, given the negative effects documented in the EPA Study Report to Congress of 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nj_vs_epa_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-nj_vs_epa-125">[125]</a></sup> However newer data published in 2015 showed that after introduction of the stricter controls mercury declined sharply, indicating that the Clean Air Act had its intended impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p>The EPA announced new rules for coal-fired power plants on 22 December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>  Cement kilns that burn hazardous waste are held to a looser standard than are standard <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incinerator" title="Incinerator">incinerators</a> in the United States, and as a result are a disproportionate source of mercury pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the directive on the Restriction of the Use of Certain Hazardous Substances in Electrical and Electronic Equipment (see <a href="/wiki/Restriction_of_Hazardous_Substances_Directive" title="Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive">RoHS</a>) bans mercury from certain electrical and electronic products, and limits the amount of mercury in other products to less than 1000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_regs_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-eu_regs-129">[129]</a></sup> There are restrictions for mercury concentration in packaging (the limit is 100 ppm for sum of mercury, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexavalent_chromium" title="Hexavalent chromium">hexavalent chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>) and batteries (the limit is 5 ppm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> In July 2007, the European Union also banned mercury in non-electrical measuring devices, such as thermometers and barometers. The ban applies to new devices only, and contains exemptions for the health care sector and a two-year grace period for manufacturers of barometers.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_reuters_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-eu_reuters-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> enacted a total ban on the use of mercury in the manufacturing and import/export of mercury products, effective 1 January 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norway_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-norway-132">[132]</a></sup> In 2002, several lakes in Norway were found to have a poor state of mercury pollution, with an excess of 1 µg/g of mercury in their sediment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-environment_norway_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-environment_norway-133">[133]</a></sup>
In 2008, Norway’s Minister of Environment Development Erik Solheim said: "Mercury is among the most dangerous environmental toxins. Satisfactory alternatives to Hg in products are
available, and it is therefore fitting to induce a ban."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda.gov_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span></h3>
<p>Products containing mercury were banned in Sweden in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span></h3>
<p>In 2008, Denmark also banned dental mercury amalgam,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda.gov_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-134">[134]</a></sup> except for molar masticating surface fillings in permanent (adult) teeth.
</p>
