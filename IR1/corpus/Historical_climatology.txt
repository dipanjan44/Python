<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Historical climatology</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Historical climatology</b> is the study of historical changes in <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and their effect on human history and development. This differs from <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a> which encompasses climate change over the entire <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">history of Earth</a>. The study seeks to define periods in human history where temperature or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> varied from what is observed in the present day. The primary sources include written records such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sagas" title="Sagas">sagas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">chronicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_history" title="Local history">local history</a> literature as well as pictorial representations such as <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">paintings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawings</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Rock_art" title="Rock art">rock art</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> record is equally important in establishing evidence of settlement, water and land usage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques_of_historical_climatology">Techniques of historical climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historical_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Techniques of historical climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In literate societies, historians may find written evidence of climatic variations over hundreds or thousands of years, such as <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenological</a> records of natural processes, for example <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">viticultural</a> records of grape harvest dates. In preliterate or non-literate societies, researchers must rely on other techniques to find evidence of historical climate differences.
</p><p>Past population levels and habitable ranges of humans or plants and animals may be used to find evidence of past differences in  climate for the region. <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Palynology</a>, the study of pollens, can show not only the range of plants and to reconstruct possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologies" title="Ecologies">ecology</a>, but to estimate the amount of precipitation in a given time period, based on the abundance of pollen in that layer of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> or ice.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_climatic_variations">Evidence of climatic variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historical_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evidence of climatic variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eruption of the <a href="/wiki/Toba_catastrophe_theory" title="Toba catastrophe theory">Toba supervolcano</a>, 70,000 to 75,000 years ago reduced the average global temperature by 5 degrees Celsius for several years and may have triggered an ice age. It has been postulated that this created a bottleneck in human evolution. A much smaller but similar effect occurred after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eruption_of_Krakatoa_in_1883" title="Eruption of Krakatoa in 1883">eruption of Krakatoa in 1883</a>, when global temperatures fell for about 5 years in a row.
</p><p>Before the retreat of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> (~9600 BC), <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> covered much of the northern <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea levels</a> were much lower than they are today. The start of our present <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> period appears to have helped spur the development of human <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_record">Human record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historical_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="398" data-file-width="341" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png/150px-Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png/225px-Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png/300px-Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sk%C3%A1lholt-Karte.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 16th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1lholt_Map" title="Skálholt Map">Skálholt Map</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_America" title="Norse America">Norse America</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grimspound_circle_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grimspound_circle_1.jpg/220px-Grimspound_circle_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grimspound_circle_1.jpg/330px-Grimspound_circle_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Grimspound_circle_1.jpg/440px-Grimspound_circle_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grimspound_circle_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of <a href="/wiki/Grimspound" title="Grimspound">Grimspound</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hut_circle" title="Hut circle">hut circles</a></div></div></div>
<p>Evidence of a warm climate in Europe, for example, comes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> studies of settlement and farming in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a> at <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> now beyond cultivation, such as <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exmoor" title="Exmoor">Exmoor</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_district" title="Lake district">Lake district</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. The climate appears to have deteriorated towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" title="Late Bronze Age">Late Bronze Age</a> however. Settlements and field boundaries have been found at high altitude in these areas, which are now wild and uninhabitable. <a href="/wiki/Grimspound" title="Grimspound">Grimspound</a> on Dartmoor is well preserved and shows the standing remains of an extensive settlement in a now inhospitable environment.
</p><p>Some parts of the present <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Saharan</a> desert may have been populated when the climate was cooler and wetter, judging by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cave_art" title="Cave art">cave art</a> and other signs of settlement in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Central_North_Africa" title="Prehistoric Central North Africa">Prehistoric Central North Africa</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a> was a time of warm weather between about AD 800–1300, during the European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> period.
Archaeological evidence supports studies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_sagas" title="Norse sagas">Norse sagas</a> which describe the settlement of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> in the 9th century AD of land now quite unsuitable for cultivation. For example, excavations at one settlement site have shown the presence of birch trees during the early Viking period. The same period records the discovery of an area called <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, probably in North America, which may also have been warmer than at present, judging by the alleged presence of grape vines. The interlude is known as the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Little_Ice_Age">Little Ice Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historical_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Little Ice Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Later examples include the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>, well documented by paintings, documents (such as diaries) and events such as the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames_frost_fairs" title="River Thames frost fairs">River Thames frost fairs</a> held on frozen lakes and rivers in the 17th and 18th centuries. The <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> was made more narrow and flowed faster after old <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a> was demolished in 1831, and the river was embanked in stages during the 19th century, both of which made the river less liable to freezing. Among the earliest references to the coming climate change is an entry in the <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> dated 1046: 
</p>
<ul><li>"And in this same year after the 2nd of February came the severe winter with frost and snow, and with all kinds of bad weather, so that there was no man alive who could remember so severe a winter as that, both through mortality of men and disease of cattle; both birds and fishes perished through the great cold and hunger."</li></ul>
<p>The <i>Chronicle</i> is the single most important historical source for the period in England between the departure of the Romans and the decades following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>. Much of the information given in the <i>Chronicle</i> is not recorded elsewhere.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="600" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg/250px-The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg/375px-The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg/500px-The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Frozen_Thames_1677.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frozen_Thames" title="Frozen Thames">Frozen Thames</a>, 1677</div></div></div>
<p>The Little Ice Age brought colder winters to parts of Europe and North America. In the mid-17th century, glaciers in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a> advanced, gradually engulfing farms and crushing entire villages. The <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and the canals and rivers of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> often froze over during the winter, and people skated and even held frost fairs on the ice. The first Thames frost fair was in 1607; the last in 1814, although changes to the bridges and the addition of an embankment affected the river flow and depth, diminishing the possibility of freezes. The freeze of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horn" title="Golden Horn">Golden Horn</a> and the southern section of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosphorus" title="Bosphorus">Bosphorus</a> took place in 1622. In 1658, a Swedish army marched across the <a href="/wiki/Great_Belt" title="Great Belt">Great Belt</a> to Denmark to invade <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> froze over, enabling sledge rides from Poland to Sweden, with seasonal inns built on the way. The winter of 1794/1795 was particularly harsh when the French invasion army under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pichegru" title="Pichegru">Pichegru</a> could march on the frozen rivers of the Netherlands, while the Dutch fleet was fixed in the ice in <a href="/wiki/Den_Helder" title="Den Helder">Den Helder</a> harbour. In the winter of 1780, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Harbour" title="New York Harbour">New York Harbour</a> froze, allowing people to walk from <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>. Sea ice surrounding <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> extended for miles in every direction, closing that island's harbours to shipping.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hvalsey_Church.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Hvalsey_Church.jpg/220px-Hvalsey_Church.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Hvalsey_Church.jpg/330px-Hvalsey_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Hvalsey_Church.jpg/440px-Hvalsey_Church.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hvalsey_Church.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The last written records of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_Greenlanders" title="Norse Greenlanders">Norse Greenlanders</a> are from a 1408 marriage in <a href="/wiki/Hvalsey_Church" title="Hvalsey Church">Hvalsey Church</a> — today the best-preserved of the Norse ruins</div></div></div>
<p>The severe winters affected human life in ways large and small. The population of Iceland fell by half, but this was perhaps also due to <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_fluorosis" title="Skeletal fluorosis">fluorosis</a> caused by the eruption of the volcano Laki in 1783.Iceland also suffered failures of cereal crops and people moved away from a grain-based diet. The Norse colonies in Greenland starved and vanished (by the 15th century) as crops failed and livestock could not be maintained through increasingly harsh winters, though <a href="/wiki/Jared_Diamond" title="Jared Diamond">Jared Diamond</a> noted that they had exceeded the agricultural <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> before then. In North America, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">American Indians</a> formed leagues in response to food shortages. In Southern Europe, in Portugal, snow storms were much more frequent while today they are rare. There are reports of heavy snowfalls in the winters of 1665, 1744 and 1886.
</p><p>In contrast to its uncertain beginning, there is a consensus that the Little Ice Age ended in the mid-19th century.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_anthropogenic_climate_change">Evidence of anthropogenic climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historical_climatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evidence of anthropogenic climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Through deforestation and agriculture, some scientists have proposed a human component in some historical climatic changes. Human-started fires have been implicated in the transformation of much of Australia from grassland to desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> If true, this would show that non-industrialized societies could have a role in influencing regional climate. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinization</a> of soils may have contributed to or caused other climatic changes throughout human history.
</p><p>For a discussion of recent human involvement in climatic changes, see <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change</a>.
</p>
