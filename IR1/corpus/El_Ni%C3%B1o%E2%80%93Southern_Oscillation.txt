<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Irregularly periodic variation in winds and sea surface temperatures over the tropical eastern Pacific Ocean</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soi.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="1170" height="68" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Soi.svg/220px-Soi.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Soi.svg/330px-Soi.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Soi.svg/440px-Soi.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soi.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southern Oscillation Index timeseries 1876–2012.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soi-map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="379" data-file-width="717" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/98/Soi-map.png/220px-Soi-map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/98/Soi-map.png/330px-Soi-map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/98/Soi-map.png/440px-Soi-map.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soi-map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southern Oscillation Index correlated with mean sea level pressure.</div></div></div>
<p><b>El Niño–Southern Oscillation</b> (<i>ENSO</i>) is an irregularly periodic variation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winds" title="Winds">winds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> over the tropical eastern Pacific Ocean, affecting climate of much of the tropics and subtropics. The warming phase of the sea temperature is known as <i><a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a></i> and the cooling phase as <i><a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a></i>. <i>Southern Oscillation</i> is the accompanying atmospheric component, coupled with the sea temperature change: <i>El Niño</i> is accompanied with high, and  <i>La Niña</i> with low air <a href="/wiki/Surface_pressure" title="Surface pressure">surface pressure</a> in the tropical western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_ENSO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPC_ENSO-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The two periods last several months each (typically occurring every few years) and their effects vary in intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two phases relate to the <a href="/wiki/Walker_circulation" title="Walker circulation">Walker circulation</a>, discovered by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Walker" title="Gilbert Walker">Gilbert Walker</a> during the early twentieth century. The Walker circulation is caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a> that results from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">high pressure system</a> over the eastern Pacific Ocean, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">low pressure system</a> over <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. When the Walker circulation weakens or reverses, an <i>El Niño</i> results, causing the ocean surface to be warmer than average, as upwelling of cold water occurs less or not at all.  An especially strong Walker circulation causes a <i>La Niña</i>, resulting in cooler ocean temperatures due to increased upwelling.
</p><p>Mechanisms that cause the oscillation remain under study. The extremes of this climate pattern's oscillations cause extreme weather (such as floods and droughts) in many regions of the world. Developing countries dependent upon agriculture and fishing, particularly those bordering the Pacific Ocean, are the most affected.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline">Outline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Outline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The El Niño–Southern Oscillation is a single climate phenomenon that periodically fluctuates between three phases: Neutral, La Niña or El Niño.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENSO:Nutshell_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENSO:Nutshell-4">[4]</a></sup> La Niña and El Niño are opposite phases that require certain changes to take place in both the ocean and the atmosphere, before an event is declared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENSO:Nutshell_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ENSO:Nutshell-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Normally the northward flowing <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a> brings relatively cold water from the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> northwards along South America's west coast to the tropics, where it is enhanced by up-welling taking place along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO-6">[6]</a></sup> Along the equator trade winds cause the ocean currents in the eastern Pacific to draw water from the deeper ocean towards the surface, helping to keep the surface cool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO-6">[6]</a></sup> Under the influence of the equatorial trade winds, this cold water flows westwards along the equator where it is slowly heated by the sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet-5">[5]</a></sup> As a direct result sea surface temperatures in the western Pacific are generally warmer, by about 8–10 °C (14–18 °F) than those in the Eastern Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet-5">[5]</a></sup> This warmer area of ocean is a source for convection and is associated with cloudiness and rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_Climate_Influences:ENSO-6">[6]</a></sup> During El Niño years the cold water weakens or disappears completely as the water in the Central and Eastern Pacific becomes as warm as the Western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_ENSO_Factsheet-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walker_circulation">Walker circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Walker circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Walker_circulation" title="Walker circulation">Walker circulation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaNina.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="624" data-file-width="696" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/LaNina.png/220px-LaNina.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/LaNina.png/330px-LaNina.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/LaNina.png/440px-LaNina.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaNina.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of the quasi-equilibrium and <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> phase of the Southern Oscillation. The Walker circulation is seen at the surface as easterly trade winds which move water and air warmed by the sun towards the west. The western side of the equatorial Pacific is characterized by warm, wet low pressure weather as the collected moisture is dumped in the form of typhoons and thunderstorms. The ocean is some 60 centimetres (24 in) higher in the western Pacific as the result of this motion. The water and air are returned to the east. Both are now much cooler, and the air is much drier. An El Niño episode is characterised by a breakdown of this water and air cycle, resulting in relatively warm water and moist air in the eastern Pacific.</div></div></div>
<p>The Walker circulation is caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a> that results from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">high pressure system</a> over the eastern Pacific Ocean, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">low pressure system</a> over <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.  The Walker circulations of the tropical Indian, Pacific, and Atlantic basins result in westerly surface winds in northern summer in the first basin and easterly winds in the second and third basins.  As a result, the temperature structure of the three oceans display dramatic asymmetries.  The equatorial Pacific and Atlantic both have cool surface temperatures in northern summer in the east, while cooler surface temperatures prevail only in the western Indian Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  These changes in surface temperature reflect changes in the depth of the thermocline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Changes in the Walker circulation with time occur in conjunction with changes in surface temperature.  Some of these changes are forced externally, such as the seasonal shift of the sun into the Northern Hemisphere in summer.  Other changes appear to be the result of coupled ocean-atmosphere feedback in which, for example,  easterly winds cause the sea surface temperature to fall in the east, enhancing the zonal heat contrast and hence intensifying easterly winds across the basin. These anomalous easterlies induce more equatorial <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> and raise the thermocline in the east, amplifying the initial cooling by the southerlies. This coupled ocean-atmosphere feedback was originally proposed by Bjerknes. From an oceanographic point of view, the equatorial cold tongue is caused by easterly winds. Were the Earth climate symmetric about the equator, cross-equatorial wind would vanish, and the cold tongue would be much weaker and have a very different zonal structure than is observed today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>During non-El Niño conditions, the Walker circulation is seen at the surface as easterly trade winds that move water and air warmed by the sun toward the west. This also creates ocean upwelling off the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> and brings nutrient-rich cold water to the surface, increasing fishing stocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennings_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jennings-10">[10]</a></sup>  The western side of the equatorial Pacific is characterized by warm, wet, low-pressure weather as the collected moisture is dumped in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>. The ocean is some 60 cm (24 in) higher in the western Pacific as the result of this motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_surface_temperature_oscillation">Sea surface temperature oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sea surface temperature oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NinoRegions.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="318" data-file-width="696" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/NinoRegions.png/220px-NinoRegions.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/NinoRegions.png/330px-NinoRegions.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/NinoRegions.png/440px-NinoRegions.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NinoRegions.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The various "Niño regions" where sea surface temperatures are monitored to determine the current ENSO phase (warm or cold)</div></div></div>
<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> in the Niño 3.4 region, which stretches from the 120th to 170th meridians west longitude astride the equator five degrees of latitude on either side, are monitored.  This region is approximately 3,000 kilometres (1,900 mi) to the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.  The most recent three-month average for the area is computed, and if the region is more than 0.5 °C (0.9 °F) above (or below) normal for that period, then an El Niño (or La Niña) is considered in progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  The United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> also uses a several month period to determine ENSO state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  When this warming or cooling occurs for only seven to nine months, it is classified as El Niño/La Niña "conditions"; when it occurs for more than that period, it is classified as El Niño/La Niña "episodes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutral_phase">Neutral phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neutral phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="645" data-file-width="432" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif/200px-Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif/300px-Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif/400px-Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Average equatorial Pacific temperatures</div></div></div>
<p>If the temperature variation from climatology is within 0.5 °C (0.9 °F), ENSO conditions are described as neutral.  Neutral conditions are the transition between warm and cold phases of ENSO.  Ocean temperatures (by definition), tropical precipitation, and wind patterns are near average conditions during this phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  Close to half of all years are within neutral periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  During the neutral ENSO phase, other climate anomalies/patterns such as the sign of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Pacific%E2%80%93North_American_teleconnection_pattern" title="Pacific–North American teleconnection pattern">Pacific–North American teleconnection pattern</a> exert more influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2548" data-file-width="2550" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/220px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/330px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/440px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 1997 El Niño observed by <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">TOPEX/Poseidon</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warm_phase">Warm phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Warm phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a></div>
<p>When the Walker circulation weakens or reverses and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_circulation" title="Hadley circulation">Hadley circulation</a> strengthens an El Niño results,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> causing the ocean surface to be warmer than average, as upwelling of cold water occurs less or not at all offshore northwestern South America.  El Niño (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>-/</a></span></span>, <small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[el ˈniɲo]</a></span>) is associated with a band of warmer than average ocean water temperatures that periodically develops off the Pacific coast of South America.  <i>El niño</i> is <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> for "the boy", and the capitalized term El Niño refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_Jesus" title="Child Jesus">Christ child</a>, Jesus, because periodic warming in the Pacific near <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> is usually noticed around <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DFG_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-DFG-22">[22]</a></sup>  It is a phase of 'El Niño–Southern Oscillation' (ENSO), which refers to variations in the temperature of the surface of the tropical eastern Pacific Ocean and in air <a href="/wiki/Surface_pressure" title="Surface pressure">surface pressure</a> in the tropical western Pacific. The warm oceanic phase, El Niño, accompanies high air surface pressure in the western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_ENSO_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPC_ENSO-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Mechanisms that cause the oscillation remain under study.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_phase">Cold phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cold phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a></div>
<p>An especially strong Walker circulation causes a La Niña, resulting in cooler ocean temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean due to increased upwelling. La Niña (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[la ˈniɲa]</a></span>) is a coupled ocean-atmosphere phenomenon that is the counterpart of El Niño as part of the broader El Niño Southern Oscillation <a href="/wiki/Climate_pattern" title="Climate pattern">climate pattern</a>. The name La Niña originates from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, meaning "the girl", analogous to El Niño meaning "the boy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> During a period of La Niña, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> across the equatorial eastern central Pacific will be lower than normal by 3–5 °C. In the United States, an <i>appearance</i> of La Niña happens for at least five months of La Niña conditions. However, each country and island nation has a different threshold for what constitutes a La Niña event, which is tailored to their specific interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2014_EU_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-December_2014_EU-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> for example, declares that a La Niña event has started when the average five month sea surface temperature deviation for the NINO.3 region, is over 0.5 °C (0.90 °F) cooler for 6 consecutive months or longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transitional_phases">Transitional phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transitional phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transitional phases at the onset or departure of El Niño or La Niña can also be important factors on global weather by affecting <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">teleconnections</a>. Significant episodes, known as Trans-Niño, are measured by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.cgd.ucar.edu/cas/catalog/climind/TNI_N34/" rel="nofollow">Trans-Niño index (TNI)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNI_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-TNI-27">[27]</a></sup> Examples of affected short-time climate in North America include precipitation in the Northwest US<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and intense tornado activity in the contiguous US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_Oscillation">Southern Oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Southern Oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="610" data-file-width="1000" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png/220px-Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png/330px-Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png/440px-Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fig4a_ENSOindices_Nino3.4only_large.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The regions where the air pressure are measured and compared to generate the Southern Oscillation Index.</div></div></div>
<p>The Southern Oscillation is the atmospheric component of El Niño. This component is an oscillation in surface air pressure between the tropical eastern and the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> waters. The strength of the Southern Oscillation is measured by the <b>Southern Oscillation Index</b> (<b>SOI</b>). The SOI is computed from fluctuations in the surface air pressure difference between <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> (in the Pacific) and <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin, Australia</a> (on the Indian Ocean).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aus_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aus-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>El Niño</b> episodes have negative SOI, meaning there is lower pressure over Tahiti and higher pressure in Darwin.</li>
<li><b>La Niña</b> episodes have positive SOI, meaning there is higher pressure in Tahiti and lower in Darwin.</li></ul>
<p>Low atmospheric pressure tends to occur over warm water and high pressure occurs over cold water, in part because of deep convection over the warm water. El Niño episodes are defined as sustained warming of the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean, thus resulting in a decrease in the strength of the Pacific <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a>, and a reduction in rainfall over eastern and northern Australia. La Niña episodes are defined as sustained cooling of the central and eastern tropical Pacific Ocean, thus resulting in an increase in the strength of the Pacific <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a>, and the opposite effects in Australia when compared to El Niño.
</p><p>Although the Southern Oscillation Index has a long station record going back to the 1800s, its reliability is limited due to the presence of both Darwin and Tahiti well south of the Equator, resulting in the surface air pressure at both locations being less directly related to ENSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EQSOI_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-EQSOI-31">[31]</a></sup> To overcome this question, a new index was created, being named <b>Equatorial Southern Oscillation Index</b> (<b>EQSOI</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EQSOI_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-EQSOI-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indexes_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indexes-32">[32]</a></sup> To generate this index data, two new regions, centered on the Equator, were delimited to create a new index: The western one is located over Indonesia and the eastern one is located over equatorial Pacific, close to the <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EQSOI_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-EQSOI-31">[31]</a></sup> However, data on EQSOI goes back only to 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EQSOI_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-EQSOI-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Madden.E2.80.93Julian_oscillation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Madden–Julian_oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Madden–Julian oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="700" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png/220px-MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png/330px-MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png/440px-MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MJO_5-day_running_mean_through_1_Oct_2006.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Hovm%C3%B6ller_diagram" title="Hovmöller diagram">Hovmöller diagram</a> of the 5-day running mean of <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">outgoing longwave radiation</a> showing the MJO. Time increases from top to bottom in the figure, so contours that are oriented from upper-left to lower-right represent movement from west to east.</div></div></div>
<p>The Madden–Julian oscillation, or (MJO), is the largest element of the intraseasonal (30- to 90-day) variability in the tropical atmosphere, and was discovered by Roland Madden and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Julian_(meteorologist)" title="Paul Julian (meteorologist)">Paul Julian</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR) in 1971. It is a large-scale coupling between atmospheric circulation and tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_convection" title="Deep convection">deep convection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agu1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-agu1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UEA1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-UEA1-34">[34]</a></sup> Rather than being a standing pattern like the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO), the MJO is a traveling pattern that propagates eastward at approximately 4 to 8 m/s (14 to 29 km/h; 9 to 18 mph), through the atmosphere above the warm parts of the Indian and Pacific oceans. This overall circulation pattern manifests itself in various ways, most clearly as anomalous <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>.  The wet phase of enhanced convection and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> is followed by a dry phase where <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity is suppressed.  Each cycle lasts approximately 30–60 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Because of this pattern, The MJO is also known as the <i>30- to 60-day oscillation</i>, <i>30- to 60-day wave</i>, or <i>intraseasonal oscillation</i>.
</p><p>There is strong year-to-year (interannual) variability in MJO activity, with long periods of strong activity followed by periods in which the oscillation is weak or absent. This interannual variability of the MJO is partly linked to the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO) cycle. In the Pacific, strong MJO activity is often observed 6 – 12 months prior to the onset of an El Niño episode, but is virtually absent during the maxima of some El Niño episodes, while MJO activity is typically greater during a La Niña episode.  Strong events in the Madden–Julian oscillation over a series of months in the western Pacific can speed the development of an El Niño or La Niña but usually do not in themselves lead to the onset of a warm or cold ENSO event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  However, observations suggest that the 1982–1983 El Niño developed rapidly during July 1982 in direct response to a <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_wave" title="Kelvin wave">Kelvin wave</a> triggered by an MJO event during late May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Further, changes in the structure of the MJO with the seasonal cycle and ENSO might facilitate more substantial impacts of the MJO on ENSO. For example, the surface westerly winds associated with active MJO convection are stronger during advancement toward El Niño and the surface easterly winds associated with the suppressed convective phase are stronger during advancement toward La Nina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_precipitation">On precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: On precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="539" data-file-width="486" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif/256px-La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif/384px-La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif 2x" width="256"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Nina_regional_impacts.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regional impacts of La Niña.</div></div></div>
<p>Developing countries dependent upon agriculture and fishing, particularly those bordering the Pacific Ocean, are the most affected by ENSO. The effects of El Niño in South America are direct and strong. An El Niño is associated with warm and very wet weather months in April–October along the coasts of northern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, causing major flooding whenever the event is strong or extreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> La Niña causes a drop in sea surface temperatures over Southeast Asia and heavy rains over <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>To the north across <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, La Niña events lead to drier than normal conditions, while El Niño events do not have a correlation towards dry or wet conditions. During El Niño events, increased precipitation is expected in California due to a more southerly, zonal, storm track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> During La Niña, increased precipitation is diverted into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> due to a more northerly storm track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> During La Niña events, the storm track shifts far enough northward to bring wetter than normal winter conditions (in the form of increased snowfall) to the Midwestern states, as well as hot and dry summers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> During the El Niño portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">ENSO</a>, increased precipitation falls along the Gulf coast and Southeast due to a stronger than normal, and more southerly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_jet_stream#Upper-level_variety" title="Polar jet stream">polar jet stream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In the late winter and spring during El Niño events, drier than average conditions can be expected in Hawaii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On Guam during El Niño years, dry season precipitation averages below normal. However, the threat of a tropical cyclone is over triple what is normal during El Niño years, so extreme shorter duration rainfall events are possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> On American Samoa during El Niño events, precipitation averages about 10 percent above normal, while La Niña events lead to precipitation amounts which average close to 10 percent below normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> ENSO is linked to rainfall over Puerto Rico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  During an El Niño, snowfall is greater than average across the southern Rockies and Sierra Nevada mountain range, and is well-below normal across the Upper Midwest and Great Lakes states.  During a La Niña, snowfall is above normal across the Pacific Northwest and western Great Lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_Tehuantepecers">On Tehuantepecers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: On Tehuantepecers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tehuantepecer" title="Tehuantepecer">Tehuantepecer</a></div>
<p>The synoptic condition for the Tehuantepecer, a violent mountain-gap wind in between the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_system" title="High-pressure system">high-pressure system</a> forming in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_de_Oaxaca" title="Sierra Madre de Oaxaca">Sierra Madre</a> of Mexico in the wake of an advancing cold front, which causes winds to accelerate through the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Tehuantepec" title="Isthmus of Tehuantepec">Isthmus of Tehuantepec</a>. Tehuantepecers primarily occur during the cold season months for the region in the wake of cold fronts, between October and February, with a summer maximum in July caused by the westward extension of the Azores-Bermuda high pressure system. Wind magnitude is greater during El Niño years than during La Niña years, due to the more frequent cold frontal incursions during El Niño winters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  Tehuantepec winds reach 20 knots (40 km/h) to 45 knots (80 km/h), and on rare occasions 100 knots (190 km/h). The wind’s direction is from the north to north-northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gloss_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-gloss-51">[51]</a></sup>  It leads to a localized acceleration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a> in the region, and can enhance <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity when it interacts with the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The effects can last from a few hours to six days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWL1971_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWL1971-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_global_warming">On global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: On global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1033" data-file-width="1422" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/220px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/330px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png/440px-Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enso-global-temp-anomalies.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> graph of Global Annual Temperature Anomalies 1950–2012, showing ENSO</div></div></div>
<p>El Niño events cause short-term (approximately 1 year in length) spikes in global average surface temperature while La Niña events cause short term cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Therefore, the relative frequency of El Niño compared to La Niña events can affect global temperature trends on decadal timescales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Over the last several decades, the number of El Niño events increased, and the number of La Niña events decreased,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trenberth1996_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trenberth1996-56">[56]</a></sup> although observation of ENSO for much longer is needed to detect robust changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The studies of historical data show the recent El Niño variation is most likely linked to global warming. For example, one of the most recent results, even after subtracting the positive influence of decadal variation, is shown to be possibly present in the ENSO trend,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fedorov2000_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fedorov2000-58">[58]</a></sup> the amplitude of the ENSO variability in the observed data still increases, by as much as 60% in the last 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang2008_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang2008-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Future trends in ENSO are uncertain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins10_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collins10-60">[60]</a></sup> as different models make different predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merryfield2006_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merryfield2006-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guilyardi2009_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guilyardi2009-62">[62]</a></sup> It may be that the observed phenomenon of more frequent and stronger El Niño events occurs only in the initial phase of the global warming, and then (e.g., after the lower layers of the ocean get warmer, as well), El Niño will become weaker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meehl2006_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meehl2006-63">[63]</a></sup> It may also be that the stabilizing and destabilizing forces influencing the phenomenon will eventually compensate for each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip2006_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philip2006-64">[64]</a></sup> More research is needed to provide a better answer to that question. The ENSO is considered to be a potential <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping element</a> in Earth's climate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and, under the global warming, can enhance or alternate regional climate extreme events through a strengthened teleconnection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> For example, an increase in the frequency and magnitude of El Niño events have triggered warmer than usual temperatures over the Indian Ocean, by modulating the Walker circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> This has resulted in a rapid warming of the Indian Ocean, and consequently a weakening of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">Asian Monsoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_coral_bleaching">On coral bleaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: On coral bleaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a></div>
<p>Following the El Nino event in 1997 – 1998, the Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory attributes the first large-scale <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> event to the warming waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_hurricanes">On hurricanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: On hurricanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes_and_climate_change" title="Hurricanes and climate change">Hurricanes and climate change</a></div>
<p>Based on modeled and observed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a> (ACE), El Niño years usually result in less active hurricane seasons in the Atlantic Ocean, but instead favor a shift of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> activity in the Pacific Ocean, compared to La Niña years favoring above average hurricane development in the Atlantic and less so in the Pacific basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity">Diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The traditional ENSO (El Niño Southern Oscillation), also called Eastern Pacific (EP) ENSO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yu2009_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yu2009-71">[71]</a></sup> involves temperature anomalies in the eastern Pacific. However, in the 1990s and 2000s, nontraditional ENSO conditions were observed, in which the usual place of the temperature anomaly (Niño 1 and 2) is not affected, but an anomaly arises in the central Pacific (Niño 3.4).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larkin2007_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin2007-72">[72]</a></sup> The phenomenon is called Central Pacific (CP) ENSO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yu2009_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yu2009-71">[71]</a></sup> "dateline" ENSO (because the anomaly arises near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_dateline" title="International dateline">dateline</a>), or ENSO "Modoki" (Modoki is <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> for "similar, but different").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-74">[74]</a></sup> There are flavors of ENSO additional to EP and CP types and some scientists argue that ENSO exists as a continuum often with hybrid types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effects of the CP ENSO are different from those of the traditional EP ENSO. The <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o#El_Niño_diversity" title="El Niño">El Niño Modoki</a> leads to more hurricanes more frequently making landfall in the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kim2009_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kim2009-76">[76]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a#La_Niña_diversity" title="La Niña">La Niña Modoki</a> leads to a rainfall increase over <a href="/wiki/North_West_Australia" title="North West Australia">northwestern Australia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Murray%E2%80%93Darling_basin" title="Murray–Darling basin">Murray–Darling basin</a>, rather than over the east as in a conventional La Niña.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNModoki_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNModoki-77">[77]</a></sup> Also, La Niña Modoki increases the frequency of cyclonic storms over <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>, but decreases the occurrence of severe storms in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LNM_Indian_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-LNM_Indian-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>The recent discovery of ENSO Modoki has some scientists believing it to be linked to global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yeh2009_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yeh2009-79">[79]</a></sup> However, comprehensive satellite data go back only to 1979. More research must be done to find the correlation and study past El Niño episodes.
More generally, there is no scientific consensus on how/if <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> might affect ENSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collins10_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collins10-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also a scientific debate on the very existence of this "new" ENSO. Indeed, a number of studies dispute the reality of this statistical distinction or its increasing occurrence, or both, either arguing the reliable record is too short to detect such a distinction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> finding no distinction or trend using other statistical approaches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> or that other types should be distinguished, such as standard and extreme ENSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Following the asymmetric nature of the warm and cold phases of ENSO, some studies could not identify such distinctions for La Niña, both in observations and in the climate models,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> but some sources indicate that there is a variation on La Niña with cooler waters on central Pacific and average or warmer water temperatures on both eastern and western Pacific, also showing eastern Pacific Ocean currents going to the opposite direction compared to the currents in traditional La Niñas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Climate_networks_and_El_Ni.C3.B1o"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_networks_and_El_Niño">Climate networks and El Niño</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate networks and El Niño">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years it was realized that network tools can be useful to identify and better understand large climate events such as El-Niño or monsoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Moreover, some indications have been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Climate_as_complex_networks" title="Climate as complex networks">climate networks</a> can be used for forecasting  El-Niño with accuracy 3/4 about one year in advance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> and even forecasting the magnitude. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Also, a climate network has been applied to study the global impacts of El Niño and La Niña. The climate network enables the identification of the regions that are most drastically affected by specific El Niño/La Niña events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoclimate_records">Paleoclimate records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Paleoclimate records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different modes of ENSO-like events have been registered in paleoclimatic archives, showing different triggering methods, feedbacks and environmental responses to the geological, atmospheric and oceanographic characteristics of the time. These paleorecords can be used to provide a qualitative basis for conservation practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>

<td><a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>
</td>
<td>240 Kya / Indian and Pacific oceans   / <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">Coccolithophore</a> in 9 deep sea cores
</td>
<td>9 deep cores in the equatorial Indian and Pacific show variations in primary productivity, related to glacial-interglacial variability and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)" title="Precession (astronomy)">precessional</a> periods (23 ky) related to changes in the <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>. There is also indication that the equatorial areas can be early responders to insolation forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>
</td>
<td>2.8 Mya / Spain / Lacustrine laminated sediments core
</td>
<td>The basin core shows light and dark layers, related to summer/autumn transition where more/less productivity is expected. The core shows thicker or thinner layers, with periodicities of 12, 6-7 and 2-3 years, related to ENSO, North Atlantic Oscillation (<a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">NAO</a>) and Quasi-biennial Oscillation (QBO), and possibly also insolation variability (<a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a>
</td>
<td>5.3 Mya / Equatorial Pacific  / <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">Foraminifera</a> in deep sea cores
</td>
<td>Deep sea cores at <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Drilling_Program" title="Ocean Drilling Program">ODP</a> site 847 and 806 show that the Pliocene warm period presented permanent El Niño-like conditions, possibly related to changes in the mean state of extratropical regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td><a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a>
</td>
<td>5.92-5.32 Mya / Italy / <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">Evaporite</a> varve thickness
</td>
<td>The <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varve</a> close to the Mediterranean shows 2-7 year variability, closely related to ENSO periodicity. Model simulations show that there is more correlation with ENSO than NAO, and that there is a strong teleconnection with the Mediterranean due to lower gradients of temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
