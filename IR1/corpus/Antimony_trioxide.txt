<p><b>Antimony(III) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.  It is the most important commercial compound of <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>.  It is found in nature as the minerals <a href="/wiki/Valentinite" title="Valentinite">valentinite</a> and senarmontite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Like most polymeric <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a>, Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> dissolves in aqueous solutions with <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_properties">Production and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Global production of antimony(III) oxide  in 2012 was 130,000 tonnes, an increase from 112,600 tonnes in 2002.  China produces the largest share followed by US/Mexico, Europe, Japan  and South Africa  and other countries (2%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2010, antimony(III) oxide was produced at four sites in EU27. It is produced via two routes, re-volatilizing of crude antimony(III) oxide and by oxidation of antimony metal.
Oxidation of antimony metal dominates in Europe. Several processes for the production of crude antimony(III) oxide or metallic antimony from virgin material. The choice of process depends on the composition of the ore and other factors.  Typical steps include mining, crushing and grinding of ore, sometimes followed by <a href="/wiki/Froth_flotation" title="Froth flotation">froth flotation</a> and separation of the metal using pyrometallurgical processes (smelting or roasting) or in a few cases (e.g. when the ore is rich in precious metals) by hydrometallurgical processes. These steps do not take place in the EU but closer to the mining location.
</p>
<h3><span id="Re-volatilizing_of_crude_antimony.28III.29_oxide"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-volatilizing_of_crude_antimony(III)_oxide">Re-volatilizing of crude antimony(III) oxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Re-volatilizing of crude antimony(III) oxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Step 1) Crude stibnite is oxidized to crude antimony(III) oxide using furnaces operating at approximately 500 to 1,000 °C. The reaction is the following:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>  +  9 O<sub>2</sub>  →   2 Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  +  6 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Step 2) The crude antimony(III) oxide is purified by sublimation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation_of_antimony_metal">Oxidation of antimony metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxidation of antimony metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antimony metal is oxidized to antimony(III) oxide in furnaces. The reaction is exothermic. Antimony(III) oxide is formed through sublimation and recovered in bag filters. The size of the formed particles is controlled by process conditions in furnace and gas flow.  The reaction can be schematically described by:<br/>
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Sb  +  3 O<sub>2</sub>   →   2 Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antimony(III) oxide is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric_oxide" title="Amphoteric oxide">amphoteric oxide</a>, it dissolves in aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> solution to give the meta-antimonite NaSbO<sub>2</sub>, which can be isolated as the trihydrate. Antimony(III) oxide also dissolves in concentrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_acids" title="Mineral acids">mineral acids</a> to give the corresponding salts, which hydrolyzes upon dilution with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-5">[5]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, the trioxide is oxidized to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimony(V)_oxide" title="Antimony(V) oxide">antimony(V) oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>When heated with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, the oxide is reduced to <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> metal. With other reducing agents such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_borohydride" title="Sodium borohydride">sodium borohydride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">lithium aluminium hydride</a>, the unstable and very toxic gas <a href="/wiki/Stibine" title="Stibine">stibine</a> is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> When heated with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bitartrate" title="Potassium bitartrate">potassium bitartrate</a>, a complex salt potassium antimony tartrate, KSb(OH)<sub>2</sub>•C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure of Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> depends on the temperature of the sample.  Dimeric Sb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is the high temperature  (1560 °C) gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-8">[8]</a></sup>  Sb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> molecules are bicyclic cages, similar to the related oxide of phosphorus(III), <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trioxide" title="Phosphorus trioxide">phosphorus trioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-9">[9]</a></sup>  The cage structure is retained in a solid that crystallizes in a cubic habit.  The Sb-O distance is 197.7 pm and the O-Sb-O angle of 95.6°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  This form exists in nature as the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senarmontite" title="Senarmontite">senarmontite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-9">[9]</a></sup> Above 606 °C, the more stable form is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a>, consisting of pairs of -Sb-O-Sb-O- chains that are linked by oxide bridges between the Sb centers.  This form exists in nature as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Valentinite" title="Valentinite">valentinite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The annual consumption of antimony(III) oxide in the United States and Europe is approximately 10,000 and 25,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>, respectively. The main application is as <a href="/wiki/Flame_retardant" title="Flame retardant">flame retardant</a> synergist in combination with halogenated materials. The combination of the halides and the antimony is key to the flame-retardant action for polymers, helping to form less flammable chars. Such flame retardants are found in electrical apparatuses, textiles, leather, and coatings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other applications:
</p>
<ul><li>Antimony(III) oxide is an <a href="/wiki/Opacifier" title="Opacifier">opacifying agent</a> for <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a>.</li>
<li>Some specialty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigments" title="Pigments">pigments</a> contain antimony.</li>
<li>Antimony(III) oxide is a useful <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a> in the production of <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> (PET plastic) and the <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanization</a> of rubber.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antimony(III) oxide has suspected carcinogenic potential for humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-11">[11]</a></sup>  Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threshold_Limit_Value" title="Threshold Limit Value">TLV</a> is 0.5 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, as for most antimony compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><br/>
No other human health hazards were identified for antimony(III) oxide, and no risks to human health and the environment were identified from the production and use of antimony trioxide in daily life.
</p>
