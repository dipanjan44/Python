<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amino acid</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the class of chemicals. For the structures and properties of the standard proteinogenic amino acids, see <a href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Proteinogenic amino acid">Proteinogenic amino acid</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AminoAcidball.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="702" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/AminoAcidball.svg/300px-AminoAcidball.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/AminoAcidball.svg/450px-AminoAcidball.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/AminoAcidball.svg/600px-AminoAcidball.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AminoAcidball.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The structure of an alpha amino acid in its un-ionized form</div></div></div>
<p><b>Amino acids</b> are <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> (-NH<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxyl</a> (-COOH) <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a>, along with a <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">side chain</a> (R group) specific to each amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The key <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> of an amino acid are <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (C), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H), <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O), and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N), although other elements are found in the side chains of certain amino acids. About 500 naturally occurring amino acids are known (though only 20 appear in the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>) and can be classified in many ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They can be classified according to the core structural functional groups' locations as <a href="/wiki/Alpha_and_beta_carbon" title="Alpha and beta carbon">alpha- <span style="white-space: nowrap">(α-)</span>, beta- <span style="white-space: nowrap">(β-)</span>, gamma- <span style="white-space: nowrap">(γ-)</span> or delta- <span style="white-space: nowrap">(δ-)</span></a> amino acids; other categories relate to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polarity</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> level, and side chain group type (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Open-chain_compound" title="Open-chain compound">acyclic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a>, containing <a href="/wiki/Hydroxy_group" title="Hydroxy group">hydroxyl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, etc.). In the form of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, amino acid <a href="/wiki/Residue_(chemistry)" title="Residue (chemistry)">residues</a> form the second-largest component (<a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is the largest) of human <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Beyond their role as residues in proteins, amino acids participate in a number of processes such as <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> transport and <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, amino acids having both the amine and the carboxylic acid groups attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-carbon" title="Alpha-carbon">first (alpha-) carbon</a> atom have particular importance. They are known as <b>2-, alpha-,</b> or <b>α-amino acids</b> (generic <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> H<sub>2</sub>NCHRCOOH in most cases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> where R is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a> known as a "<a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">side chain</a>");<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> often the term "amino acid" is used to refer specifically to these. They include the 22 <a href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Proteinogenic amino acid">proteinogenic</a> ("protein-building") amino acids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which combine into <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> chains ("polypeptides") to form the building-blocks of a vast array of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIGMS_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIGMS-11">[11]</a></sup> These are all <small>L</small>-<a href="/wiki/Stereoisomerism" title="Stereoisomerism">stereoisomers</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">left-handed</a>" <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a>), although a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-amino_acid" title="D-amino acid"><small>D</small>-amino acids</a> ("right-handed") occur in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_envelope" title="Bacterial envelope">bacterial envelopes</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Neuromodulation" title="Neuromodulation">neuromodulator</a> (<small>D</small>-<a href="/wiki/Serine" title="Serine">serine</a>), and in some <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Twenty of the proteinogenic amino acids are encoded directly by triplet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codon" title="Codon">codons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> and are known as "standard" amino acids. The other two ("non-standard" or "non-canonical") are <a href="/wiki/Selenocysteine" title="Selenocysteine">selenocysteine</a> (present in many <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> as well as most <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>, but not coded directly by <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Pyrrolysine" title="Pyrrolysine">pyrrolysine</a> (found only in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archea" title="Archea">archea</a> and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>). Pyrrolysine and selenocysteine are encoded via variant codons; for example, selenocysteine is encoded by <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codon</a> and <a href="/wiki/SECIS_element" title="SECIS element">SECIS element</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VoJw6fIISSkC_p.299_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-VoJw6fIISSkC_p.299-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-url_The_Genetic_Codes_NCBI_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-url_The_Genetic_Codes_NCBI-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/N-Formylmethionine" title="N-Formylmethionine"><i>N</i>-formylmethionine</a> (which is often the initial amino acid of proteins in bacteria, <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>) is generally considered as a form of <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a> rather than as a separate proteinogenic amino acid. Codon–<a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">tRNA</a> combinations not found in nature can also be used to <a href="/wiki/Expanded_genetic_code" title="Expanded genetic code">"expand" the genetic code</a> and form novel proteins known as <a href="/wiki/Alloprotein" title="Alloprotein">alloproteins</a> incorporating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Non-proteinogenic amino acid">non-proteinogenic amino acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16260173_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16260173-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19318213_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19318213-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-22046-1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-22046-1-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many important proteinogenic and non-proteinogenic amino acids have biological functions. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a>, glutamate (standard <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-amino-butyric_acid" title="Gamma-amino-butyric acid">gamma-amino-butyric acid</a> ("GABA", non-standard gamma-amino acid) are, respectively, the main <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter#Excitatory_and_inhibitory" title="Neurotransmitter">excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid12467378_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12467378-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyproline" title="Hydroxyproline">Hydroxyproline</a>, a major component of the <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a> <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a>, is synthesised from <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>. <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">Glycine</a> is a biosynthetic precursor to porphyrins used in <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carnitine" title="Carnitine">Carnitine</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid transport</a>.
</p><p>Nine proteinogenic amino acids are called "<a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential</a>" for humans because they cannot be produced from other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> by the human body and so must be taken in as food. Others may be <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid#Essentiality_vs._conditional_essentiality_in_humans" title="Essential amino acid">conditionally essential</a> for certain ages or medical conditions. Essential amino acids may also differ between <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of their biological significance, amino acids are important in nutrition and are commonly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutritional_supplement" title="Nutritional supplement">nutritional supplements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">feed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Food_technology" title="Food technology">food technology</a>. Industrial uses include the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">drugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable plastics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_catalysis" title="Asymmetric catalysis">chiral catalysts</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first few amino acids were discovered in the early 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1806, French chemists <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis-Nicolas_Vauquelin" title="Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin">Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Jean_Robiquet" title="Pierre Jean Robiquet">Pierre Jean Robiquet</a> isolated a compound in <a href="/wiki/Asparagus" title="Asparagus">asparagus</a> that was subsequently named <a href="/wiki/Asparagine" title="Asparagine">asparagine</a>, the first amino acid to be discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anfinsen_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anfinsen-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cystine" title="Cystine">Cystine</a> was discovered in 1810,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> although its monomer, <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a>, remained undiscovered until 1884.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anfinsen_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anfinsen-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">Glycine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">leucine</a> were discovered in 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The last of the 20 common amino acids to be discovered was <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a> in 1935 by <a href="/wiki/William_Cumming_Rose" title="William Cumming Rose">William Cumming Rose</a>, who also determined the <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acids</a> and established the minimum daily requirements of all amino acids for optimal growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The unity of the chemical category was recognized by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Adolphe_Wurtz" title="Charles Adolphe Wurtz">Wurtz</a> in 1865, but he gave no particular name to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Usage of the term "amino acid" in the English language is from 1898,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> while the German term, <i>Aminosäure</i>, was used earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Proteins were found to yield amino acids after enzymatic digestion or acid <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>. In 1902, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Emil_Fischer" title="Hermann Emil Fischer">Emil Fischer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Hofmeister" title="Franz Hofmeister">Franz Hofmeister</a> independently proposed that proteins are formed from many amino acids, whereby bonds are formed between the amino group of one amino acid with the carboxyl group of another, resulting in a linear structure that Fischer termed "<a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_structure">General structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amino_Acids.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="769" data-file-width="624" height="542" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Amino_Acids.svg/440px-Amino_Acids.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Amino_Acids.svg/660px-Amino_Acids.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Amino_Acids.svg/880px-Amino_Acids.svg.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amino_Acids.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 21 <a href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Proteinogenic amino acid">proteinogenic α-amino acids</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>, grouped according to their side chains' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pK<sub>a</sub></a> values and charges carried at <a href="/wiki/PH#Living_systems" title="PH">physiological pH (7.4)</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the structure shown at the top of the page, <b>R</b> represents a <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">side chain</a> specific to each amino acid. The <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom next to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl_group" title="Carboxyl group">carboxyl group</a> (which is therefore numbered 2 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_chain" title="Carbon chain">carbon chain</a> starting from that functional group) is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_carbon" title="Alpha carbon">α–carbon</a>.  Amino acids containing an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_group" title="Amino group">amino group</a> bonded directly to the alpha carbon are referred to as <i>alpha amino acids</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  These include amino acids such as <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a> which contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_amine" title="Secondary amine">secondary amines</a>, which used to be often referred to as "imino acids".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldbook_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldbook-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isomerism">Isomerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isomerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The alpha amino acids are the most common form found in nature, but only when occurring in the <small>L</small>-isomer. The alpha carbon is a <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> carbon atom, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> which has two indistinguishable hydrogen atoms on the alpha carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup>  Therefore, all alpha amino acids but <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> can exist in either of two <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomers</a>, called <small>L</small> or <small>D</small> amino acids, which are mirror images of each other (<i>see also <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">Chirality</a></i>). While <small>L</small>-amino acids represent all of the amino acids found in <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> during translation in the ribosome, <small>D</small>-amino acids are found in some proteins produced by enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posttranslational_modification" title="Posttranslational modification">posttranslational modifications</a> after <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translocation_(genetics)" title="Translocation (genetics)">translocation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>, as in exotic sea-dwelling organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Cone_snail" title="Cone snail">cone snails</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> They are also abundant components of the <a href="/wiki/Peptidoglycan" title="Peptidoglycan">peptidoglycan</a> <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a> of bacteria,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and <small>D</small>-serine may act as a <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> in the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <small>D</small>-amino acids are used in <a href="/wiki/Racemic_crystallography" title="Racemic crystallography">racemic crystallography</a> to create centrosymmetric crystals, which (depending on the protein) may allow for easier and more robust protein structure determination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <small>L</small> and <small>D</small> convention for amino acid configuration refers not to the optical activity of the amino acid itself but rather to the optical activity of the isomer of <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde" title="Glyceraldehyde">glyceraldehyde</a> from which that amino acid can, in theory, be synthesized (<small>D</small>-glyceraldehyde is dextrorotatory; <small>L</small>-glyceraldehyde is levorotatory).
In alternative fashion, the <a href="/wiki/Cahn%E2%80%93Ingold%E2%80%93Prelog_priority_rules" title="Cahn–Ingold–Prelog priority rules"><i>(S)</i> and <i>(R)</i> designators</a> are used to indicate the absolute <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry" title="Stereochemistry">stereochemistry</a>. Almost all of the amino acids in proteins are <i>(S)</i> at the α carbon, with cysteine being <i>(R)</i> and glycine non-<a href="/wiki/Chirality" title="Chirality">chiral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Cysteine has its side chain in the same geometric position as the other amino acids, but the <i>R/S</i> terminology is reversed because of the higher atomic number of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compared to the carboxyl oxygen gives the side chain a higher priority, whereas the atoms in most other side chains give them lower priority.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Side_chains">Side chains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Side chains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1047" data-file-width="1147" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg/220px-Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg/330px-Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg/440px-Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_fisher_structure_and_3d_ball.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">Lysine</a> with carbon atoms labeled by position</div></div></div>
<p>In amino acids that have a carbon chain attached to the α–carbon (such as <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>, shown to the right) the carbons are labeled in order as α, β, γ, δ, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In some amino acids, the amine group is attached to the β or γ-carbon, and these are therefore referred to as <i>beta</i> or <i>gamma amino acids</i>.
</p><p>Amino acids are usually classified by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">properties</a> of their side chain into four groups. The side chain can make an amino acid a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Weak_base" title="Weak base">weak base</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Hydrophile" title="Hydrophile">hydrophile</a> if the side chain is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_molecule" title="Polar molecule">polar</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobe" title="Hydrophobe">hydrophobe</a> if it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonpolar" title="Nonpolar">nonpolar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">chemical structures</a> of the 22 standard amino acids, along with their chemical properties, are described more fully in the article on these <a href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Proteinogenic amino acid">proteinogenic amino acids</a>.
</p><p>The phrase "<a href="/wiki/Branched-chain_amino_acid" title="Branched-chain amino acid">branched-chain amino acids</a>" or BCAA refers to the amino acids having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> side chains that are non-linear; these are <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">leucine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine">isoleucine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">valine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">Proline</a> is the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteinogenic" title="Proteinogenic">proteinogenic</a> amino acid whose side-group links to the α-amino group and, thus, is also the only proteinogenic amino acid containing a secondary amine at this position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup> In chemical terms, proline is, therefore, an <a href="/wiki/Imino_acid" title="Imino acid">imino acid</a>, since it lacks a <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">primary amino group</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> although it is still classed as an amino acid in the current biochemical nomenclature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and may also be called an "N-alkylated alpha-amino acid".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zwitterions">Zwitterions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zwitterions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="184" data-file-width="378" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg/220px-Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg/330px-Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg/440px-Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acid_zwitterions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An amino acid in its (1) un-ionized and (2) zwitterionic forms</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Zwitterion" title="Zwitterion">Zwitterion</a></div>
<p>The α-<a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> group of amino acids is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a>, meaning that it releases a <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">hydron</a> (such as a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>) at moderate pH values.  In other words, carboxylic acid groups (−CO<sub>2</sub>H) can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deprotonated" title="Deprotonated">deprotonated</a> to become negative <a href="/wiki/Carboxylate" title="Carboxylate">carboxylates</a> (−CO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> ).  The negatively charged carboxylate ion predominates at pH values greater than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pKa</a> of the carboxylic acid group (mean for the 20 common amino acids is about 2.2, see the table of amino acid structures above).  In a complementary fashion, the α-<a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> of amino acids is a <a href="/wiki/Weak_base" title="Weak base">weak base</a>, meaning that it accepts a proton at moderate pH values.  In other words, α-amino groups (NH<sub>2</sub>−) can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protonated" title="Protonated">protonated</a> to become positive α-ammonium groups (<sup>+</sup>NH<sub>3</sub>−).  The positively charged α-ammonium group predominates at pH values less than the pKa of the α-ammonium group (mean for the 20 common α-amino acids is about 9.4).
</p><p>Because all amino acids contain amine and carboxylic acid functional groups, they share <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphiprotic</a> properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup>  Below pH 2.2, the predominant form will have a neutral carboxylic acid group and a positive α-ammonium ion (net charge +1), and above pH 9.4, a negative carboxylate and neutral α-amino group (net charge −1).  But at pH between 2.2 and 9.4, an amino acid usually contains both a negative carboxylate and a positive α-ammonium group, as shown in structure (2) on the right, so has net zero charge.  This molecular state is known as a <a href="/wiki/Zwitterion" title="Zwitterion">zwitterion</a>, from the German <b>Zwitter</b> meaning <i>hermaphrodite</i> or <i>hybrid</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  The fully neutral form (structure (1) on the left) is a very minor species in aqueous solution throughout the pH range (less than 1 part in 10<sup>7</sup>). Amino acids exist as zwitterions also in the solid phase, and crystallize with salt-like properties unlike typical organic acids or amines.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isoelectric_point">Isoelectric point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Isoelectric point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amino_Acid_Titration_Curves_By_Side_Chain.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="480" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Amino_Acid_Titration_Curves_By_Side_Chain.png/360px-Amino_Acid_Titration_Curves_By_Side_Chain.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/Amino_Acid_Titration_Curves_By_Side_Chain.png 1.5x" width="360"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amino_Acid_Titration_Curves_By_Side_Chain.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Composite of <a href="/wiki/Titration_curve" title="Titration curve">titration curves</a> of twenty proteinogenic amino acids grouped by side chain category</div></div></div>
<p>The variation in titration curves when the amino acids can be grouped by category.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With the exception of tyrosine, using titration to distinguish among hydrophobic amino acids is problematic.
</p><p>At pH values between the two pKa values, the zwitterion predominates, but coexists in <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_equilibrium" title="Dynamic equilibrium">dynamic equilibrium</a> with small amounts of net negative and net positive ions. At the exact midpoint between the two pKa values, the trace amount of net negative and trace of net positive ions exactly balance, so that average net charge of all forms present is zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This pH is known as the <a href="/wiki/Isoelectric_point" title="Isoelectric point">isoelectric point</a> pI, so pI = ½(pKa<sub>1</sub> + pKa<sub>2</sub>). The individual amino acids all have slightly different pKa values, so have different isoelectric points. For amino acids with charged side chains, the pKa of the side chain is involved. Thus for Asp, Glu with negative side chains, pI = ½(pKa<sub>1</sub> + pKa<sub>R</sub>), where pKa<sub>R</sub> is the side chain pKa. Cysteine also has potentially negative side chain with pKa<sub>R</sub> = 8.14, so pI should be calculated as for Asp and Glu, even though the side chain is not significantly charged at neutral pH. For His, Lys, and Arg with positive side chains, pI = ½(pKa<sub>R</sub> + pKa<sub>2</sub>). Amino acids have zero mobility in electrophoresis at their isoelectric point, although this behaviour is more usually exploited for peptides and proteins than single amino acids. Zwitterions have minimum solubility at their isoelectric point and some amino acids (in particular, with non-polar side chains) can be isolated by precipitation from water by adjusting the pH to the required isoelectric point.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_functions_in_biochemistry">Occurrence and functions in biochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and functions in biochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proteinogenic_amino_acids">Proteinogenic amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Proteinogenic amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acids" title="Proteinogenic amino acids">Proteinogenic amino acids</a></div> <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Protein_primary_structure" title="Protein primary structure">Protein primary structure</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posttranslational_modification" title="Posttranslational modification">Posttranslational modification</a></div>
<p>Amino acids are the structural units (monomers) that make up proteins. They join together to form short <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> chains called <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a> or longer chains called either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptides" title="Polypeptides">polypeptides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. These polymers are linear and unbranched, with each amino acid within the chain attached to two neighboring amino acids. The process of making proteins encoded by DNA/RNA genetic material is called <i><a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a></i> and involves the step-by-step addition of amino acids to a growing protein chain by a <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">ribozyme</a> that is called a <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The order in which the amino acids are added is read through the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">mRNA</a> template, which is an <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> copy of one of the organism's <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>.
</p><p>Twenty-two amino acids are naturally incorporated into polypeptides and are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteinogenic" title="Proteinogenic">proteinogenic</a> or natural amino acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup> Of these, 20 are encoded by the universal <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a>. The remaining 2, <a href="/wiki/Selenocysteine" title="Selenocysteine">selenocysteine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyrrolysine" title="Pyrrolysine">pyrrolysine</a>, are incorporated into proteins by unique synthetic mechanisms. <a href="/wiki/Selenocysteine" title="Selenocysteine">Selenocysteine</a> is incorporated when the mRNA being translated includes a <a href="/wiki/SECIS_element" title="SECIS element">SECIS element</a>, which causes the UGA codon to encode selenocysteine instead of a <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pyrrolysine" title="Pyrrolysine">Pyrrolysine</a> is used by some <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> in enzymes that they use to produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. It is coded for with the codon UAG, which is normally a stop codon in other organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This UAG codon is followed by a <a href="/wiki/PYLIS_downstream_sequence" title="PYLIS downstream sequence">PYLIS downstream sequence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16164991_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16164991-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-proteinogenic_amino_acids">Non-proteinogenic amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-proteinogenic amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-proteinogenic_amino_acids" title="Non-proteinogenic amino acids">Non-proteinogenic amino acids</a></div>
<p>Aside from the 22 <a href="/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid" title="Proteinogenic amino acid">proteinogenic amino acids</a>, many <i>non-proteinogenic</i> amino acids are known. Those either are not found in proteins (for example <a href="/wiki/Carnitine" title="Carnitine">carnitine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-aminobutyric acid">GABA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Levothyroxine" title="Levothyroxine">levothyroxine</a>) or are not produced directly and in isolation by standard cellular machinery (for example, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyproline" title="Hydroxyproline">hydroxyproline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selenomethionine" title="Selenomethionine">selenomethionine</a>).
</p><p>Non-proteinogenic amino acids that are found in proteins are formed by <a href="/wiki/Post-translational_modification" title="Post-translational modification">post-translational modification</a>, which is modification after translation during protein synthesis. These modifications are often essential for the function or regulation of a protein.  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> allows for better binding of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_in_biology" title="Calcium in biology">calcium cations</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and  <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> contains hydroxyproline, generated by <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylation" title="Hydroxylation">hydroxylation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Another example is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Hypusine" title="Hypusine">hypusine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eukaryotic_initiation_factor" title="Eukaryotic initiation factor">translation initiation factor</a> <a href="/wiki/EIF5A" title="EIF5A">EIF5A</a>, through modification of a lysine residue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Such modifications can also determine the localization of the protein, e.g., the addition of long hydrophobic groups can cause a protein to bind to a <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipid</a> membrane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some non-proteinogenic amino acids are not found in proteins. Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-aminoisobutyric_acid" title="2-aminoisobutyric acid">2-aminoisobutyric acid</a> and the neurotransmitter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-aminobutyric acid">gamma-aminobutyric acid</a>. Non-proteinogenic amino acids often occur as intermediates in the <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a> for standard amino acids – for example, <a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">ornithine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citrulline" title="Citrulline">citrulline</a> occur in the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a>, part of amino acid <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a> (see below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> A rare exception to the dominance of α-amino acids in biology is the β-amino acid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_alanine" title="Beta alanine">beta alanine</a> (3-aminopropanoic acid), which is used in plants and microorganisms in the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Pantothenic_acid" title="Pantothenic acid">pantothenic acid</a> (vitamin B<sub>5</sub>), a component of <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_A" title="Coenzyme A">coenzyme A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D-amino_acid_natural_abundance"><small>D</small>-amino acid natural abundance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: D-amino acid natural abundance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><small>D</small>-isomers are uncommon in live organisms. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Gramicidin" title="Gramicidin">gramicidin</a> is a polypeptide made up from mixture of <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>-amino acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Other compounds containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-amino_acids" title="D-amino acids"><small>D</small>-amino acid</a> are <a href="/wiki/Tyrocidine" title="Tyrocidine">tyrocidine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valinomycin" title="Valinomycin">valinomycin</a>. These compounds disrupt bacterial cell walls, particularly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">Gram-positive</a> bacteria. Only 837 <small>D</small>-amino acids were found in Swiss-Prot database (187 million amino acids analysed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-standard_amino_acids">Non-standard amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Non-standard amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 20 amino acids that are encoded directly by the codons of the universal <a href="/wiki/Genetic_code" title="Genetic code">genetic code</a> are called <i>standard</i> or <i>canonical</i> amino acids. A modified form of methionine (<a href="/wiki/N-Formylmethionine" title="N-Formylmethionine"><i>N</i>-formylmethionine</a>) is often incorporated in place of methionine as the initial amino acid of proteins in bacteria, mitochondria and chloroplasts. Other amino acids are called <i>non-standard</i> or <i>non-canonical</i>. Most of the non-standard amino acids are also non-proteinogenic (i.e. they cannot be incorporated into proteins during translation), but two of them are proteinogenic, as they can be incorporated translationally into proteins by exploiting information not encoded in the universal genetic code.
</p><p>The two non-standard proteinogenic amino acids are <a href="/wiki/Selenocysteine" title="Selenocysteine">selenocysteine</a> (present in many non-eukaryotes as well as most eukaryotes, but not coded directly by DNA) and <a href="/wiki/Pyrrolysine" title="Pyrrolysine">pyrrolysine</a> (found only in some <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>). The incorporation of these non-standard amino acids is rare. For example, 25 human proteins include selenocysteine (Sec) in their primary structure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and the structurally characterized enzymes (selenoenzymes) employ Sec as the catalytic <a href="/wiki/Moiety_(chemistry)" title="Moiety (chemistry)">moiety</a> in their active sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Pyrrolysine and selenocysteine are encoded via variant codons. For example, selenocysteine is encoded by <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codon</a> and <a href="/wiki/SECIS_element" title="SECIS element">SECIS element</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VoJw6fIISSkC_p.299_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-VoJw6fIISSkC_p.299-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-url_The_Genetic_Codes_NCBI_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-url_The_Genetic_Codes_NCBI-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_human_nutrition">In human nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In human nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png"><img alt="Diagram showing the relative occurrence of different amino acids in blood serum as obtained from different diets" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="970" data-file-width="1648" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png/380px-Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png/570px-Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png/760px-Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png 2x" width="380"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acids_in_food_and_blood.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Share of amino acid in different human diets and the resulting mix of amino acids in human blood serum. Glutamate and glutamine are the most frequent in food at over 10%, while alanine, glutamine, and glycine are the most common in blood.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acids" title="Essential amino acids">Essential amino acids</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">Protein (nutrient)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid_synthesis" title="Amino acid synthesis">Amino acid synthesis</a></div>
<p>When taken up into the human body from the diet, the 20 standard amino acids either are used to synthesize proteins and other biomolecules or are oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a source of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The oxidation pathway starts with the removal of the amino group by a <a href="/wiki/Transaminase" title="Transaminase">transaminase</a>; the amino group is then fed into the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a>. The other product of transamidation is a <a href="/wiki/Keto_acid" title="Keto acid">keto acid</a> that enters the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glucogenic_amino_acid" title="Glucogenic amino acid">Glucogenic amino acids</a> can also be converted into glucose, through <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">gluconeogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Of the 20 standard amino acids, nine (<a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">His</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine">Ile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">Leu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">Lys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">Met</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenylalanine" title="Phenylalanine">Phe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">Thr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tryptophan" title="Tryptophan">Trp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">Val</a>) are called <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acids</a> because the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> cannot <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">synthesize</a> them from other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> at the level needed for normal growth, so they must be obtained from food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taurine" title="Taurine">taurine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyrosine" title="Tyrosine">tyrosine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> are considered semiessential amino-acids in children (though taurine is not technically an amino acid), because the metabolic pathways that synthesize these amino acids are not fully developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The amounts required also depend on the age and health of the individual, so it is hard to make general statements about the dietary requirement for some amino acids. Dietary exposure to the non-standard amino acid <a href="/wiki/Beta-Methylamino-L-alanine" title="Beta-Methylamino-L-alanine">BMAA</a> has been linked to human neurodegenerative diseases, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALS" title="ALS">ALS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holtcamp_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holtcamp-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox_and_Davis_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cox_and_Davis-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In humans, non-protein amino acids also have important roles as <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_intermediate" title="Metabolic intermediate">metabolic intermediates</a>, such as in the biosynthesis of the <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitter</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-amino-butyric_acid" title="Gamma-amino-butyric acid">gamma-amino-butyric acid</a> (GABA). Many amino acids are used to synthesize other molecules, for example:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tryptophan" title="Tryptophan">Tryptophan</a> is a precursor of the neurotransmitter <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tyrosine" title="Tyrosine">Tyrosine</a> (and its precursor phenylalanine) are precursors of the <a href="/wiki/Catecholamine" title="Catecholamine">catecholamine</a> <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a> <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">norepinephrine</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Trace_amine" title="Trace amine">trace amines</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phenylalanine" title="Phenylalanine">Phenylalanine</a> is a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Phenethylamine" title="Phenethylamine">phenethylamine</a> and tyrosine in humans.  In plants, it is a precursor of various <a href="/wiki/Phenylpropanoid" title="Phenylpropanoid">phenylpropanoids</a>, which are important in plant metabolism.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">Glycine</a> is a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Porphyrin" title="Porphyrin">porphyrins</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">Arginine</a> is a precursor of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">Ornithine</a> and <a href="/wiki/S-Adenosyl_methionine" title="S-Adenosyl methionine">S-adenosylmethionine</a> are precursors of <a href="/wiki/Polyamine" title="Polyamine">polyamines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate" title="Aspartate">Aspartate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a> are precursors of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_2002_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer_2002-84">[84]</a></sup> However, not all of the functions of other abundant non-standard amino acids are known.</li></ul>
<p>Some non-standard amino acids are used as <a href="/wiki/Plant_defense_against_herbivory" title="Plant defense against herbivory">defenses against herbivores</a> in plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hylin1969_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hylin1969-85">[85]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Canavanine" title="Canavanine">canavanine</a> is an analogue of <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> that is found in many <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1967_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1967-86">[86]</a></sup> and in particularly large amounts in <i><a href="/wiki/Canavalia_gladiata" title="Canavalia gladiata">Canavalia gladiata</a></i> (sword bean).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> This amino acid protects the plants from predators such as insects and can cause illness in people if some types of legumes are eaten without processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The non-protein amino acid <a href="/wiki/Mimosine" title="Mimosine">mimosine</a> is found in other species of legume, in particular <i><a href="/wiki/Leucaena_leucocephala" title="Leucaena leucocephala">Leucaena leucocephala</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> This compound is an analogue of <a href="/wiki/Tyrosine" title="Tyrosine">tyrosine</a> and can poison animals that graze on these plants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_industry">Uses in industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Uses in industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amino acids are used for a variety of applications in industry, but their main use is as additives to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_feed" title="Compound feed">animal feed</a>. This is necessary, since many of the bulk components of these feeds, such as <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>, either have low levels or lack some of the <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acids</a>: lysine, methionine, threonine, and tryptophan are most important in the production of these feeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leuchtenberger2005_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leuchtenberger2005-90">[90]</a></sup> In this industry, amino acids are also used to chelate metal cations in order to improve the absorption of minerals from supplements, which may be required to improve the health or production of these animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a> is also a major consumer of amino acids, in particular, <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a>, which is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavor_enhancer" title="Flavor enhancer">flavor enhancer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garattini_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garattini-92">[92]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Aspartame" title="Aspartame">aspartame</a> (aspartyl-phenylalanine-1-methyl ester) as a low-calorie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_sweetener" title="Artificial sweetener">artificial sweetener</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Similar technology to that used for animal nutrition is employed in the human nutrition industry to alleviate symptoms of mineral deficiencies, such as anemia, by improving mineral absorption and reducing negative side effects from inorganic mineral supplementation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>The chelating ability of amino acids has been used in fertilizers for agriculture to facilitate the delivery of minerals to plants in order to correct mineral deficiencies, such as iron chlorosis. These fertilizers are also used to prevent deficiencies from occurring and improving the overall health of the plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The remaining production of amino acids is used in the synthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">drugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leuchtenberger2005_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leuchtenberger2005-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, some amino acids derivatives are used in pharmaceutical industry. They include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5-HTP" title="5-HTP">5-HTP</a> (5-hydroxytryptophan) used for experimental treatment of depression,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/L-DOPA" title="L-DOPA"><small>L</small>-DOPA</a> (<small>L</small>-dihydroxyphenylalanine) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s" title="Parkinson's">Parkinson's</a> treatment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Eflornithine" title="Eflornithine">eflornithine</a> drug that inhibits <a href="/wiki/Ornithine_decarboxylase" title="Ornithine decarboxylase">ornithine decarboxylase</a> and used in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/African_trypanosomiasis" title="African trypanosomiasis">sleeping sickness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expanded_genetic_code">Expanded genetic code</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Expanded genetic code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Expanded_genetic_code" title="Expanded genetic code">Expanded genetic code</a></div>
<p>Since 2001, 40 non-natural amino acids have been added into protein by creating a unique codon (recoding) and a corresponding transfer-RNA:aminoacyl – tRNA-synthetase pair to encode it with diverse physicochemical and biological properties in order to be used as a tool to exploring <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">protein structure</a> and function or to create novel or enhanced proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16260173_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16260173-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19318213_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19318213-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nullomers">Nullomers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Nullomers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nullomers" title="Nullomers">Nullomers</a></div>
<p>Nullomers are codons that in theory code for an amino acid, however in nature there is a selective bias against using this codon in favor of another, for example bacteria prefer to use CGA instead of AGA to code for arginine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> This creates some sequences that do not appear in the genome.  This characteristic can be taken advantage of and used to create new selective cancer-fighting drugs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> and to prevent cross-contamination of DNA samples from crime-scene investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_building_blocks">Chemical building blocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Chemical building blocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_synthesis" title="Asymmetric synthesis">Asymmetric synthesis</a></div>
<p>Amino acids are important as low-cost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstocks</a>. These compounds are used in <a href="/wiki/Chiral_pool_synthesis" title="Chiral pool synthesis">chiral pool synthesis</a> as <a href="/wiki/Enantiomer" title="Enantiomer">enantiomerically pure</a> building-blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanessian1993_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanessian1993-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>Amino acids have been investigated as precursors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral_catalyst" title="Chiral catalyst">chiral catalysts</a>, e.g., for asymmetric <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> reactions, although no commercial applications exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blaser1992_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blaser1992-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradable_plastics">Biodegradable plastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Biodegradable plastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">Biodegradable plastic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biopolymer" title="Biopolymer">Biopolymer</a></div>
<p>Amino acids are under development as components of a range of biodegradable polymers. These materials have applications as <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> packaging and in medicine in <a href="/wiki/Drug_delivery" title="Drug delivery">drug delivery</a> and the construction of <a href="/wiki/Prosthesis" title="Prosthesis">prosthetic implants</a>. These polymers include polypeptides, <a href="/wiki/Polyamide" title="Polyamide">polyamides</a>, polyesters, polysulfides, and polyurethanes with amino acids either forming part of their main chains or bonded as side chains. These modifications alter the physical properties and reactivities of the polymers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanda1999_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanda1999-104">[104]</a></sup> An interesting example of such materials is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_poly(aspartate)" title="Sodium poly(aspartate)">polyaspartate</a>, a water-soluble biodegradable polymer that may have applications in disposable <a href="/wiki/Diaper" title="Diaper">diapers</a> and agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross2002_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gross2002-105">[105]</a></sup> Due to its solubility and ability to <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelate</a> metal ions, polyaspartate is also being used as a biodegradeable anti-<a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">scaling</a> agent and a <a href="/wiki/Corrosion_inhibitor" title="Corrosion inhibitor">corrosion inhibitor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thombre2005_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thombre2005-107">[107]</a></sup> In addition, the aromatic amino acid <a href="/wiki/Tyrosine" title="Tyrosine">tyrosine</a> is being developed as a possible replacement for toxic <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">bisphenol A</a> in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourke2003_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bourke2003-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As amino acids have both a primary <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> group and a primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl" title="Carboxyl">carboxyl</a> group, these chemicals can undergo most of the reactions associated with these functional groups. These include <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_addition" title="Nucleophilic addition">nucleophilic addition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide bond</a> formation, and <a href="/wiki/Alkylimino-de-oxo-bisubstitution" title="Alkylimino-de-oxo-bisubstitution">imine formation</a> for the amine group, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterification" title="Esterification">esterification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide bond</a> formation, and <a href="/wiki/Decarboxylation" title="Decarboxylation">decarboxylation</a> for the carboxylic acid group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> The combination of these functional groups allow amino acids to be effective polydentate ligands for metal-amino acid chelates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
The multiple side chains of amino acids can also undergo chemical reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> The types of these reactions are determined by the groups on these side chains and are, therefore, different between the various types of amino acid.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_synthesis">Chemical synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Chemical synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg"><img alt="For the steps in the reaction, see the text." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="104" data-file-width="469" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg/400px-Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg/600px-Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg/800px-Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis_scheme.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Strecker amino acid synthesis</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Chemical_synthesis_of_amino_acids" title="Category:Chemical synthesis of amino acids">Category:Chemical synthesis of amino acids</a></div>
<p>Several methods exist to synthesize amino acids. One of the oldest methods begins with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky_halogenation" title="Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky halogenation">bromination</a> at the α-carbon of a carboxylic acid. <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">Nucleophilic substitution</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> then converts the alkyl bromide to the amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> In alternative fashion, the <a href="/wiki/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis" title="Strecker amino acid synthesis">Strecker amino acid synthesis</a> involves the treatment of an aldehyde with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">potassium cyanide</a> and ammonia, this produces an α-amino nitrile as an intermediate. Hydrolysis of the nitrile in acid then yields an α-amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Using ammonia or ammonium salts in this reaction gives unsubstituted amino acids, whereas substituting primary and secondary amines will yield substituted amino acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Likewise, using <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, instead of aldehydes, gives α,α-disubstituted amino acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> The classical synthesis gives <a href="/wiki/Racemic_mixture" title="Racemic mixture">racemic mixtures</a> of α-amino acids as products, but several alternative procedures using asymmetric auxiliaries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> or asymmetric catalysts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> have been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the current time, the most-adopted method is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated" title="Automated">automated</a> synthesis on a solid support (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> beads), using <a href="/wiki/Protecting_group" title="Protecting group">protecting groups</a> (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fmoc_chloride" title="Fmoc chloride">Fmoc</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tert-butyloxycarbonyl_protecting_group" title="Tert-butyloxycarbonyl protecting group">t-Boc</a>) and activating groups (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide" title="Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide">DCC</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diisopropylcarbodiimide" title="Diisopropylcarbodiimide">DIC</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peptide_bond_formation">Peptide bond formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Peptide bond formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Peptide_synthesis" title="Peptide synthesis">Peptide synthesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">Peptide bond</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peptidformationball.svg"><img alt="Two amino acids are shown next to each other. One loses a hydrogen and oxygen from its carboxyl group (COOH) and the other loses a hydrogen from its amino group (NH2). This reaction produces a molecule of water (H2O) and two amino acids joined by a peptide bond (-CO-NH-). The two joined amino acids are called a dipeptide." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="990" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peptidformationball.svg/400px-Peptidformationball.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peptidformationball.svg/600px-Peptidformationball.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Peptidformationball.svg/800px-Peptidformationball.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peptidformationball.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The condensation of two amino acids to form a <i><a href="/wiki/Dipeptide" title="Dipeptide">dipeptide</a></i> through a <i><a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bond</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>As both the amine and carboxylic acid groups of amino acids can react to form amide bonds, one amino acid molecule can react with another and become joined through an amide linkage. This <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> of amino acids is what creates proteins. This <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation reaction</a> yields the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bond</a> and a molecule of water. In cells, this reaction does not occur directly; instead, the amino acid is first activated by attachment to a <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">transfer RNA</a> molecule through an <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> bond. This aminoacyl-tRNA is produced in an <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>-dependent reaction carried out by an <a href="/wiki/Aminoacyl_tRNA_synthetase" title="Aminoacyl tRNA synthetase">aminoacyl tRNA synthetase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> This aminoacyl-tRNA is then a substrate for the <a href="/wiki/Ribosome" title="Ribosome">ribosome</a>, which catalyzes the attack of the amino group of the elongating protein chain on the ester bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> As a result of this mechanism, all proteins made by ribosomes are synthesized starting at their N-terminus and moving toward their C-terminus.
</p><p>However, not all peptide bonds are formed in this way. In a few cases, peptides are synthesized by specific enzymes. For example, the tripeptide <a href="/wiki/Glutathione" title="Glutathione">glutathione</a> is an essential part of the defenses of cells against oxidative stress. This peptide is synthesized in two steps from free amino acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> In the first step, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-glutamylcysteine_synthetase" title="Gamma-glutamylcysteine synthetase">gamma-glutamylcysteine synthetase</a> condenses <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glutamic_acid" title="Glutamic acid">glutamic acid</a> through a peptide bond formed between the side chain carboxyl of the glutamate (the gamma carbon of this side chain) and the amino group of the cysteine. This dipeptide is then condensed with <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> by <a href="/wiki/Glutathione_synthetase" title="Glutathione synthetase">glutathione synthetase</a> to form glutathione.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>In chemistry, peptides are synthesized by a variety of reactions. One of the most-used in <a href="/wiki/Peptide_synthesis" title="Peptide synthesis">solid-phase peptide synthesis</a> uses the aromatic oxime derivatives of amino acids as activated units. These are added in sequence onto the growing peptide chain, which is attached to a solid resin support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> The ability to easily synthesize vast numbers of different peptides by varying the types and order of amino acids (using <a href="/wiki/Combinatorial_chemistry" title="Combinatorial chemistry">combinatorial chemistry</a>) has made peptide synthesis particularly important in creating libraries of peptides for use in drug discovery through <a href="/wiki/High-throughput_screening" title="High-throughput screening">high-throughput screening</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid_synthesis" title="Amino acid synthesis">Amino acid synthesis</a></div>
<p>In plants, nitrogen is first assimilated into organic compounds in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>, formed from alpha-ketoglutarate and ammonia in the mitochondrion. In order to form other amino acids, the plant uses <a href="/wiki/Transaminase" title="Transaminase">transaminases</a> to move the amino group to another alpha-keto carboxylic acid. For example, aspartate aminotransferase converts glutamate and oxaloacetate to alpha-ketoglutarate and aspartate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Other organisms use transaminases for amino acid synthesis, too.
</p><p>Nonstandard amino acids are usually formed through modifications to standard amino acids. For example, <a href="/wiki/Homocysteine" title="Homocysteine">homocysteine</a> is formed through the <a href="/wiki/Transsulfuration_pathway" title="Transsulfuration pathway">transsulfuration pathway</a> or by the demethylation of methionine via the intermediate metabolite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-adenosyl_methionine" title="S-adenosyl methionine">S-adenosyl methionine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brosnan_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brosnan-127">[127]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyproline" title="Hydroxyproline">hydroxyproline</a> is made by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posttranslational_modification" title="Posttranslational modification">posttranslational modification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microorganisms</a> and plants can synthesize many uncommon amino acids. For example, some microbes make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-aminoisobutyric_acid" title="2-aminoisobutyric acid">2-aminoisobutyric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lanthionine" title="Lanthionine">lanthionine</a>, which is a sulfide-bridged derivative of alanine. Both of these amino acids are found in peptidic <a href="/wiki/Lantibiotics" title="Lantibiotics">lantibiotics</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Alamethicin" title="Alamethicin">alamethicin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> However, in plants, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic_acid" title="1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid">1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid</a> is a small disubstituted cyclic amino acid that is a key intermediate in the production of the plant hormone <a href="/wiki/Ethylene#Ethylene_as_a_plant_hormone" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catabolism">Catabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Catabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="636" data-file-width="906" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png/300px-Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png/450px-Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png/600px-Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amino_acid_catabolism_revised.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Catabolism of proteinogenic amino acids. Amino acids can be classified according to the properties of their main products as either of the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> <br/>* <i>Glucogenic</i>, with the products having the ability to form <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">gluconeogenesis</a> <br/>* <i>Ketogenic</i>, with the products not having the ability to form glucose. These products may still be used for <a href="/wiki/Ketogenesis" title="Ketogenesis">ketogenesis</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_synthesis" title="Lipid synthesis">lipid synthesis</a>. <br/>* Amino acids catabolized into both glucogenic and ketogenic products.</div></div></div>
<p>Amino acids must first pass out of organelles and cells into blood circulation via <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid_transporter" title="Amino acid transporter">amino acid transporters</a>, since the amine and carboxylic acid groups are typically ionized. Degradation of an amino acid, occurring in the liver and kidneys, often involves <a href="/wiki/Deamination" title="Deamination">deamination</a> by moving its amino group to alpha-ketoglutarate, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a>. This process involves transaminases, often the same as those used in amination during synthesis. In many vertebrates, the amino group is then removed through the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a> and is excreted in the form of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>. However, amino acid degradation can produce <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a> or ammonia instead. For example, <a href="/wiki/Serine_dehydratase" title="Serine dehydratase">serine dehydratase</a> converts serine to pyruvate and ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_2002_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer_2002-84">[84]</a></sup> After removal of one or more amino groups, the remainder of the molecule can sometimes be used to synthesize new amino acids, or it can be used for energy by entering <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>, as detailed in image at right.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physicochemical_properties_of_amino_acids">Physicochemical properties of amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amino_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Physicochemical properties of amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 20 amino acids encoded directly by the genetic code can be divided into several groups based on their properties. Important factors are charge, <a href="/wiki/Hydrophile" title="Hydrophile">hydrophilicity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobe" title="Hydrophobe">hydrophobicity</a>, size, and functional groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Creighton_38-5"><a href="#cite_note-Creighton-38">[38]</a></sup> These properties are important for <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">protein structure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein%E2%80%93protein_interaction" title="Protein–protein interaction">protein–protein interactions</a>. The water-soluble proteins tend to have their hydrophobic residues (Leu, Ile, Val, Phe, and Trp) buried in the middle of the protein, whereas hydrophilic side chains are exposed to the aqueous solvent. (Note that in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, a residue refers to a specific <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric chain</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acid</a>.) The <a href="/wiki/Integral_membrane_protein" title="Integral membrane protein">integral membrane proteins</a> tend to have outer rings of exposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> amino acids that anchor them into the <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a>. In the case part-way between these two extremes, some <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_membrane_protein" title="Peripheral membrane protein">peripheral membrane proteins</a> have a patch of hydrophobic amino acids on their surface that locks onto the membrane. In similar fashion, proteins that have to bind to positively charged molecules have surfaces rich with negatively charged amino acids like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate" title="Aspartate">aspartate</a>, while proteins binding to negatively charged molecules have surfaces rich with positively charged chains like <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a>. There are different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobicity_scale" title="Hydrophobicity scale">hydrophobicity scales</a> of amino acid residues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some amino acids have special properties such as <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a>, that can form covalent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disulfide_bond" title="Disulfide bond">disulfide bonds</a> to other cysteine residues, <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a> that forms <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">a cycle</a> to the polypeptide backbone, and <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> that is more flexible than other amino acids.
</p><p>Many proteins undergo a range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posttranslational_modification" title="Posttranslational modification">posttranslational modifications</a>, when additional chemical groups are attached to the amino acids in proteins. Some modifications can produce hydrophobic <a href="/wiki/Lipoprotein" title="Lipoprotein">lipoproteins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> or hydrophilic <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> These type of modification allow the reversible targeting of a protein to a membrane. For example, the addition and removal of the fatty acid <a href="/wiki/Palmitic_acid" title="Palmitic acid">palmitic acid</a> to cysteine residues in some signaling proteins causes the proteins to attach and then detach from cell membranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In addition to the specific amino acid codes, placeholders are used in cases where <a href="/wiki/Protein_sequencing" title="Protein sequencing">chemical</a> or <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">crystallographic</a> analysis of a peptide or protein cannot conclusively determine the identity of a residue. They are also used to summarise <a href="/wiki/Conserved_sequence" title="Conserved sequence">conserved protein sequence</a> motifs. The use of single letters to indicate sets of similar residues is similar to the use of <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_notation" title="Nucleic acid notation">abbreviation codes for degenerate bases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>

<p><b>Unk</b> is sometimes used instead of <b>Xaa</b>, but is less standard.
</p><p>In addition, many <a href="/wiki/Non-proteinogenic_amino_acids" title="Non-proteinogenic amino acids">non-standard amino acids</a> have a specific code. For example, several peptide drugs, such as <a href="/wiki/Bortezomib" title="Bortezomib">Bortezomib</a> and <a href="/wiki/MG132" title="MG132">MG132</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Peptide_synthesis" title="Peptide synthesis">artificially synthesized</a> and retain their <a href="/wiki/Protecting_group" title="Protecting group">protecting groups</a>, which have specific codes. Bortezomib is <a href="/wiki/Pyrazinoic_acid" title="Pyrazinoic acid">Pyz</a>-Phe-boroLeu, and MG132 is <a href="/wiki/Carboxybenzyl" title="Carboxybenzyl">Z</a>-Leu-Leu-Leu-al. To aid in the analysis of protein structure, <a href="/wiki/Photo-reactive_amino_acid_analog" title="Photo-reactive amino acid analog">photo-reactive amino acid analogs</a> are available. These include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Photoleucine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Photoleucine (page does not exist)">photoleucine</a> (<b>pLeu</b>) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Photomethionine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Photomethionine (page does not exist)">photomethionine</a> (<b>pMet</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup>
</p>
