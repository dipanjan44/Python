<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lake Nyos</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Lake Nyos</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">crater lake</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Region,_Cameroon" title="Northwest Region, Cameroon">Northwest Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, located about 315 km (196 mi) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Yaound%C3%A9" title="Yaoundé">Yaoundé</a> the capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-1">[1]</a></sup> Nyos is a deep <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> high on the flank of an inactive <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oku_volcanic_plain" title="Oku volcanic plain">Oku volcanic plain</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_line" title="Cameroon line">Cameroon line</a> of volcanic activity. A <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_dam" title="Volcanic dam">volcanic dam</a> impounds the lake waters.
</p><p>A pocket of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> lies beneath the lake and leaks <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) into the water, changing it into <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>. Nyos is one of only three known <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">exploding lakes</a> to be saturated with carbon dioxide in this way, the others being <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a>, also in Cameroon, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a>.
</p><p>In 1986, possibly as the result of a landslide, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos_disaster" title="Lake Nyos disaster">Lake Nyos suddenly emitted a large cloud of CO<sub>2</sub></a>, which <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">suffocated</a> 1,746 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eos2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eos2016-2">[2]</a></sup> and 3,500 livestock in nearby towns and villages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithsonian_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-4">[4]</a></sup> Though not completely unprecedented, it was the first known large-scale asphyxiation caused by a natural event. To prevent a recurrence, a degassing tube that siphons water from the bottom layers to the top, allowing the carbon dioxide to leak in safe quantities, was installed in 2001. Two additional tubes were installed in 2011.
</p><p>Today, the lake also poses a threat because its natural wall is weakening. A geological tremor could cause this natural <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levée</a> to give way, allowing water to rush into downstream villages all the way into <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and allowing large amounts of carbon dioxide to escape.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat,_2014.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg/220px-Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg/330px-Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg/440px-Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat%2C_2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Nyos_from_Landsat,_2014.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lake and vicinity from <a href="/wiki/Landsat_8" title="Landsat 8">Landsat 8</a>, 2014</div></div></div>
<p>Lake Nyos lies within the <a href="/wiki/Oku_Volcanic_Field" title="Oku Volcanic Field">Oku Volcanic Field</a>, located near the northern boundary of the Cameroon Volcanic Line, a zone of volcanoes and other tectonic activity that extends southwest to the Mt. Cameroon <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>. The field consists of volcanic <a href="/wiki/Maar" title="Maar">maars</a> and basaltic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scoria_cone" title="Scoria cone">scoria cones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDSU_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDSU-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_geologic_history">Formation and geologic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and geologic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake Nyos is located south of the dirt road from <a href="/wiki/Wum" title="Wum">Wum</a>, about 30 km (19 mi) to the west, to <a href="/wiki/Nkamb%C3%A9" title="Nkambé">Nkambé</a> in the east. Villages along the road in the vicinity of the lake include <a href="/wiki/Chah_(Cameroon)" title="Chah (Cameroon)">Cha</a>, Nyos, Munji, Djingbe, and Subum. The lake is 50 km (31 mi) from the Nigerian border to the north, and lies on the northern slopes of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mbam_Massif&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mbam Massif (page does not exist)">Massif du Mbam</a>, drained by streams running north, then northwest, to the Katsina-Ala River in Nigeria which is part of the <a href="/wiki/Benue_River" title="Benue River">Benue River</a> basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lake Nyos fills a roughly circular <a href="/wiki/Maar" title="Maar">maar</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oku_Volcanic_Field" title="Oku Volcanic Field">Oku Volcanic Field</a>, an explosion crater caused when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_flow" title="Lava flow">lava flow</a> interacted violently with <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. The maar is believed to have formed in an eruption a maximum of 12,000 years ago, and is 1,800 m (5,900 ft) across and 208 m (682 ft) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The area has been volcanically active for millions of years—after <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> were split apart by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> about 110 million years ago, <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> also experienced rifting, although to a lesser degree. The rift is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mb%C3%A9r%C3%A9_Rift_Valley" title="Mbéré Rift Valley">Mbéré Rift Valley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crustal</a> extension has allowed <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> to reach the surface along a line extending through Cameroon. <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a> also lies on this fault line. Lake Nyos is surrounded by old lava flows and <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">pyroclastic</a> deposits.
</p><p>Although Nyos is situated within an extinct volcano, magma still exists beneath it. Approximately 80 kilometres (50 mi) directly below the lake resides a pool of <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a>, which releases carbon dioxide and other gases; the gases then travel upward through the earth. The fumes then dissolve in the natural springs encircling the lake, ultimately rising to the surface of the water and leaching into the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The lake waters are held in place by a natural <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dam</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rock</a>. At its narrowest point, the wall measures 40 metres (130 ft) high and 45 metres (148 ft) wide.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_saturation">Gas saturation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gas saturation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake Nyos is one of only three lakes in the world known to be saturated with carbon dioxide—the others are Lake Monoun, also in Cameroon, and Lake Kivu in the Democratic Republic of Congo. A <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">magma chamber</a> beneath the region is an abundant source of carbon dioxide, which seeps up through the lake bed, charging the waters of Lake Nyos with an estimated 90 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Lake Nyos is thermally <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratified</a>, with layers of warm, less dense water near the surface floating on the colder, denser water layers near the lake's bottom. Over long periods, carbon dioxide gas seeping into the cold water at the lake's bottom is dissolved in great amounts.
</p><p>Most of the time, the lake is stable and the CO<sub>2</sub> remains in solution in the lower layers. However, over time, the water becomes <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a>, and if an event such as an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> or <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslide</a> occurs, large amounts of CO<sub>2</sub> may suddenly come out of solution.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1986_disaster">1986 disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1986 disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos_disaster" title="Lake Nyos disaster">Lake Nyos disaster</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tml15-16_Nc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="1277" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/330px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/495px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Tml15-16_Nc.jpg/660px-Tml15-16_Nc.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tml15-16_Nc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lake Nyos as it appeared less than two weeks after the eruption; August 29, 1986.</div></div></div>
<p>Although a sudden <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> had occurred at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> in 1984, a similar threat from Lake Nyos was not anticipated. However, on August 21, 1986, a <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">limnic eruption</a> occurred at Lake Nyos, triggering the sudden release of about 100,000–300,000 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCIAM_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCIAM-10">[10]</a></sup> (some sources state as much as 1.6 million tonnes)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> of CO<sub>2</sub>. This gas cloud rose at nearly 100 kilometres per hour (62 mph) and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDSU_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SDSU-5">[5]</a></sup> spilled over the northern lip of the lake into a valley running roughly east-west from Cha to Subum. It then rushed down two valleys branching off to the north, displacing all of the air and suffocating 1,746 people within 25 kilometres (16 mi) of the lake, mostly rural villagers, as well as 3,500 livestock. The villages most affected were Cha, Nyos, and Subum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="254" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg/170px-Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">Cattle</a> suffocated by carbon dioxide from Lake Nyos</div></div></div>
<p>Scientists concluded from evidence that a 100 m (330 ft) fountain of water and foam formed at the surface of the lake. The huge amount of water rising suddenly caused much turbulence in the water, spawning a wave of at least 25 metres (82 ft) that would scour the shore of one side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ST_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ST-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not known what triggered the catastrophic outgassing. Most geologists suspect a landslide, but some believe that a small volcanic eruption may have occurred on the bed of the lake. A third possibility is that cool rainwater falling on one side of the lake triggered the overturn. Others still believe there was a small earthquake, but as witnesses did not report feeling any tremors on the morning of the disaster, this hypothesis is unlikely. Whatever the cause, the event resulted in the rapid mixing of the supersaturated deep water with the upper layers of the lake, where the reduced pressure allowed the stored CO<sub>2</sub> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effervescence_(chemistry)" title="Effervescence (chemistry)">effervesce</a> out of solution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>It is believed that about 1.2 cubic kilometres (0.29 cu mi) of gas was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neatorama_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-neatorama-14">[14]</a></sup> The normally blue waters of the lake turned a deep red after the outgassing, due to <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-rich water from the deep rising to the surface and being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidise" title="Oxidise">oxidised</a> by the air. The level of the lake dropped by about a metre and trees near the lake were knocked down.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degassing">Degassing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Degassing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale of the 1986 disaster led to much study on how a recurrence could be prevented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Estimates of the rate of carbon dioxide entering the lake suggested that outgassings could occur every 10–30 years, though a recent study shows<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that release of water from the lake, caused by erosion of the natural barrier that keeps in the lake's water, could in turn reduce pressure on the lake's carbon dioxide and cause a gas escape much sooner.
</p><p>Several researchers independently proposed the installation of degassing columns from rafts in the lake. The principle is simple: A pump lifts water from the bottom of the lake, heavily saturated with CO<sub>2</sub>, until the loss of pressure begins releasing the gas from the diphasic fluid, thus making the process self-powered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1992 at Monoun, and in 1995 at Nyos, a French team directed by Michel Halbwachs demonstrated the feasibility of this approach.  In 2001, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Foreign_Disaster_Assistance" title="Office of Foreign Disaster Assistance">Office of Foreign Disaster Assistance</a> funded a permanent installation at Nyos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, two additional pipes were installed by Michel Halbwachs and his French-Cameroonian team to assure the complete degassing of Lake Nyos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Degassingpump.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Degassingpump.jpg/260px-Degassingpump.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Degassingpump.jpg/390px-Degassingpump.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Degassingpump.jpg/520px-Degassingpump.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Degassingpump.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Degassing pump schematic</div></div></div>
<p>Following the Lake Nyos <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_(event)" title="Tragedy (event)">tragedy</a>, scientists investigated other African lakes to see if a similar phenomenon could happen elsewhere. Lake Kivu, 2,000 times larger than Lake Nyos, was also found to be supersaturated, and geologists found evidence for outgassing events around the lake about every thousand years. The eruption of nearby <a href="/wiki/Mount_Nyiragongo" title="Mount Nyiragongo">Mount Nyiragongo</a> in 2002 sent <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> flowing into the lake, raising fears that a gas eruption could be triggered, but it was not, as the flow of lava stopped well before it got near the bottom layers of the lake, where the gas is maintained in solution by the water pressure.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weakening_dam">Weakening dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Weakening dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2005, Isaac Njilah, a geologist at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Yaound%C3%A9" title="University of Yaoundé">University of Yaoundé</a>, suggested that the natural dam of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rock</a> that keeps in the lake's waters could collapse in the near future.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">Erosion</a> has worn the dam away, causing holes and pockets to develop in the dam's upper layer, and water already passes through the lower section. Meanwhile, landslides have reduced dam strength on the outside. Seismic activity caused by the lake's volcanic foundation could thus cause the lake wall to give way, resulting in up to 50 million m<sup>3</sup> (1.8 billion ft<sup>3</sup>) of water flooding downhill into areas of the Northwest Province and the Nigerian states of <a href="/wiki/Taraba_State" title="Taraba State">Taraba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benue_State" title="Benue State">Benue</a>.
</p><p>The Cameroonian government, speaking through Gregory Tanyi-Leke of the Institute of Mining and Geological Research, acknowledges the weakening wall, but denies that it presents any immediate threat. A <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> team led by Olaf Van Duin and Nisa Nurmohamed of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>' Ministry of Transport and Public Works inspected the dam over three days in September 2005, and confirmed that the natural lip had weakened. Van Duin believed that the dam would breach within the next 10 to 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>One possible means of averting such a catastrophe would be to strengthen the lake wall, though this would take much time and money. Engineers could also introduce a channel to allow excess water to drain; if the water level were lowered by about 20 m (66 ft), the pressure on the wall would be reduced significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_of_population">Return of population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Nyos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Return of population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite the risks from carbon dioxide and collapse of the lake's retaining wall, the area is being resettled. Settlers cite the wish to return to ancestral lands (although some are newcomers) and the great fertility of the land as reasons for their return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smithsonian-webformat_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-webformat-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
