<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of concrete</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>environmental impact of concrete</b>, its manufacture and applications, are complex. Some effects are harmful; others welcome. Many depend on circumstances.  A major component of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> is <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, which has its own <a href="/wiki/Cement#Environmental_and_social_impacts" title="Cement">environmental and social impacts</a> and contributes largely to those of concrete.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cement_industry" title="Cement industry">cement industry</a> is one of the primary producers of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, a potent <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wbcsd_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-wbcsd-1">[1]</a></sup> Concrete causes damage to the most fertile layer of the earth, the <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a>.
Concrete is used to create hard surfaces which contribute to <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> that may cause <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a>. Conversely, concrete is one of the most powerful tools for proper flood control, by means of <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">damming</a>, diversion, and deflection of flood waters, mud flows, and the like.
Light-colored concrete can reduce the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect, due to its higher <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave-2">[2]</a></sup>
Concrete dust released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_demolition" title="Building demolition">building demolition</a> and natural disasters can be a major source of dangerous <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>.
The presence of some substances in concrete, including useful and unwanted additives, can cause health concerns due to toxicity and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactivity</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 
Wet concrete is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> and should always be handled with proper protective equipment. Concrete <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> is increasing in response to improved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_awareness" title="Environmental awareness">environmental awareness</a>, legislation, and economic considerations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_emissions_and_climate_change">Carbon dioxide emissions and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide emissions and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concrete industry is one of two largest producers of carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), creating up to 5% of worldwide man-made emissions of this gas, of which 50% is from the chemical process and 40% from burning fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wbcsd_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-wbcsd-1">[1]</a></sup> The carbon dioxide CO<sub>2</sub> produced for the manufacture of structural concrete (using ~14% cement) is estimated at 410 kg/m<sup>3</sup> (~180 kg/tonne @ density of 2.3 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) (reduced to 290 kg/m<sup>3</sup> with 30% fly ash replacement of cement).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> emission from the concrete production is directly proportional to the cement content used in the concrete mix; 900 kg of CO<sub>2</sub> are emitted for the fabrication of every ton of cement, accounting for 88% of the emissions associated with the average concrete mix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup>
Cement manufacture contributes greenhouse gases both directly through the production of carbon dioxide when <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> is thermally decomposed, producing <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and also through the use of energy, particularly from the combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.
</p><p>One area of the concrete life cycle worth noting is the fact that concrete has a very low <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> relative to the quantity that is used. This is primarily the result of the fact that the materials used in concrete construction, such as aggregates, <a href="/wiki/Pozzolan" title="Pozzolan">pozzolans</a>, and water, are relatively plentiful and can often be drawn from local sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>  This means that transportation only accounts for 7% of the embodied energy of concrete, while the cement production accounts for 70%. With a total embodied energy of 1.69 GJ/tonne concrete is lower than any other building material besides wood. It is worth noting that this value is based on mix proportions for concrete of no more than 20% fly ash. It is estimated that one percent replacement of cement with fly ash represents a .7% reduction in <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">energy consumption</a>. With some proposed mixes containing as much as 80% <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a>, this would represent a considerable energy savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_improvements">Design improvements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design improvements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a growing interest in reducing carbon emissions related to concrete from both the academic and industrial sectors, especially with the possibility of future carbon tax implementation. Several approaches to reducing emissions have been suggested.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cement_Production_and_Use">Cement Production and Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cement Production and Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One reason why the carbon emissions are so high is because cement has to be heated to very high temperatures in order for <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> to form. A major culprit of this is <a href="/wiki/Alite" title="Alite">alite</a> (Ca<sub>3</sub>SiO<sub>5</sub>), a mineral in concrete that cures within hours of pouring and is therefore responsible for much of its initial strength. However, alite also has to be heated to 1,500 °C in the clinker-forming process. Some research suggests that alite can be replaced by a different mineral, such as <a href="/wiki/Belite" title="Belite">belite</a> (Ca<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>). Belite is also a mineral already used in concrete. It has a roasting temperature of 1,200 °C, which is significantly lower than that of alite. Furthermore, belite is actually stronger once concrete cures. However, belite takes on the order of days or months to set completely, which leaves concrete weak for an unacceptably long time. Current research is focusing on finding possible impurity additives, like magnesium, that might speed up the curing process. It is also worthwhile to consider that belite takes more energy to grind, which may make its full life impact similar to or even higher than alite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_cement_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green_cement-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another approach has been the partial replacement of conventional clinker with such alternatives as fly ash, <a href="/wiki/Bottom_ash" title="Bottom ash">bottom ash</a>, and slag, all of which are by-products of other industries that would otherwise end up in <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>. Fly ash and bottom ash come from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_station" title="Fossil fuel power station">thermoelectric power</a> plants, while slag is a waste from <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> in the ironworks industry. These materials are slowly gaining popularity as additives, especially since they can potentially increase strength, decrease density, and prolong durability of concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Additives_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Additives-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main obstacle to wider implementation of fly ash and slag may be largely due to the risk of construction with new technology that has not been exposed to long field testing. Until a carbon tax is implemented, companies are unwilling to take the chance with new concrete mix recipes even if this reduces carbon emissions. However, there are some examples of “green” concrete and its implementation. One instance is a concrete company called Ceratech that has started manufacturing concrete with 95% fly ash and 5% liquid additives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_cement_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Green_cement-8">[8]</a></sup>  Another is the <a href="/wiki/I-35W_Saint_Anthony_Falls_Bridge" title="I-35W Saint Anthony Falls Bridge">I-35W Saint Anthony Falls Bridge</a>, which was constructed with a novel mixture of concrete that included different compositions of Portland cement, fly ash, and slag depending on the portion of the bridge and its material properties requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St_Anthony_Bridge_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-St_Anthony_Bridge-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_Absorbing_Concrete">Emission Absorbing Concrete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Emission Absorbing Concrete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Italian company <a href="/wiki/Italcementi" title="Italcementi">Italcementi</a> designed a kind of cement that is supposed to fight air pollution. It should break down pollutants that come in contact with the concrete, thanks to the use of <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a> absorbing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>. Some environmental experts nevertheless remain skeptical and wonder if the special material can 'eat' enough pollutants to make it financially viable. <a href="/wiki/Jubilee_Church" title="Jubilee Church">Jubilee Church</a> in Rome is built from this kind of concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another proposed method of absorbing emissions is to absorb CO<sub>2</sub> in the curing process. Recent research has proposed the use of an admixture (a dicalcium silicate y phase) that absorbs CO<sub>2</sub> as the concrete cures. With the use of coal ash or another suitable substitute, this concrete could theoretically have a  CO<sub>2</sub> emissions below 0 kg/m<sup>3</sup>, compared to normal concrete at 400 kg/m<sup>3</sup>. The most effective method of production of this concrete would be using the exhaust gas of a power plant, where an isolated chamber could control temperature and humidity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  Even besides the use of advanced additives, carbonation naturally occurs within concrete, thus causing it to absorb CO<sub>2</sub> in a process that is effectively the reverse of cement production. While concerns about corrosion of reinforcement and alkalinity loss remain, this process cannot be discounted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Improvements">Other Improvements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other Improvements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many other improvements to concrete that do not deal directly with emissions. Recently, much research has gone into “smart” concretes: concretes that use electrical and mechanical signals to respond to changes in loading conditions. One variety uses carbon fiber reinforcement which provides an electrical response that can be used to measure strain . This allows for monitoring the structural integrity of the concrete without installing sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_construction" title="Road construction">road construction</a> and maintenance industry consumes tonnes of carbon intensive concrete every day to secure road-side and urban infrastructure. As populations grow this infrastructure is becoming increasingly vulnerable to impact from vehicles, creating an ever increasing cycle of damage and waste and ever increasing consumption of concrete for repairs (roadworks are now seen around our cities on almost a daily basis). A major development in the infrastructure industry involves the use of recycled petroleum waste to protect the concrete from damage and enable infrastructure to become dynamic, able to be easily maintained and updated without disturbance to the existing foundations. This simple innovation preserves the foundations for the entire lifespan of a development.
</p><p>Another area of concrete research involves the creation of certain <a href="/wiki/Lunarcrete" title="Lunarcrete">“waterless” concretes</a> for use in extraplanetary colonization. Most commonly, these concretes use sulfur to act as a non-reactive binder, allowing for construction of concrete structures in environments with no or very little water. These concretes are in many ways indistinguishable from normal hydraulic concrete: they have similar densities, can be used with currently existing metal reinforcement, and they actually gain strength faster than normal concrete<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This application has yet to be explored on Earth, but with concrete production representing as much as two-thirds of the total energy usage of some developing countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> any improvement is worth considering.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_Concrete">Carbon Concrete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon Concrete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another approach is to pump liquid carbon dioxide into the concrete before mixing. This can reduce the carbon emissions from concrete production when combined with a power plant or other industry that produces CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_runoff">Surface runoff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Surface runoff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">Surface runoff</a>, when water runs off <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">impervious surfaces</a>, such as non-porous concrete, can cause severe <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and flooding. <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">Urban runoff</a> tends to pick up gasoline, <a href="/wiki/Motor_oil" title="Motor oil">motor oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">trash</a> and other pollutants from sidewalks, roadways and parking lots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCE1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCE1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Without <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a>, the impervious cover in a typical urban area limits groundwater percolation and causes five times the amount of runoff generated by a typical woodland of the same size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA2-19">[19]</a></sup> A 2008 report by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a> identified urban runoff as a leading source of <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In an attempt to counteract the negative effects of impervious concrete, many new paving projects have begun to use <a href="/wiki/Pervious_concrete" title="Pervious concrete">pervious concrete</a>, which provides a level of automatic stormwater management. Pervious concrete is created by careful laying of concrete with specifically designed aggregate proportions, which allows for surface runoff to seep through and return to the groundwater. This both prevents flooding and contributes to groundwater replenishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup> If designed and layered properly, pervious concrete and other discreetly paved areas can also function as an automatic water filter by preventing certain harmful substances like oils and other chemicals from passing through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Unfortunately there are still downsides to large scale applications of pervious concrete: its reduced strength relative to conventional concrete limits use to low-load areas, and it must be laid properly to reduce susceptibility to freeze-thaw damage and sediment buildup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_heat">Urban heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Urban heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both concrete and <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> are the primary contributors to what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect.
</p><p>Using light-colored concrete has proven effective in reflecting up to 50% more light than asphalt and reducing ambient temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave-2">[2]</a></sup> A low <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> value, characteristic of black asphalt, absorbs a large percentage of solar heat and contributes to the warming of cities. By paving with light colored concrete, in addition to replacing asphalt with light-colored concrete, communities can lower their average temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldchange_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-worldchange-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many U.S. cities, pavement covers about 30–40% of the surface area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave-2">[2]</a></sup> This directly affects the temperature of the city and contributes to the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect. Paving with light-colored concrete would lower temperatures of paved areas and improve night-time visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolpave_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-coolpave-2">[2]</a></sup> The potential of energy saving within an area is also high. With lower temperatures, the demand for air conditioning theoretically decreases, saving energy. However, research into the interaction between reflective pavements and buildings has found that, unless the nearby buildings are fitted with reflective glass, solar radiation reflected off pavements can increase building temperatures, increasing air conditioning demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Example2006_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Example2006-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Atlanta has tried to mitigate the heat-island effect. City officials noted that when using heat-reflecting concrete, their average city temperature decreased by 6 °F (3.3 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Design Trust for Public Space found that by slightly raising the albedo value in New York City, beneficial effects such as energy savings could be achieved.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was concluded that this could be accomplished by the replacement of black asphalt with light-colored concrete.
</p><p>However, in winter this may be a disadvantage as ice will form more easily and remain longer on the light colored surfaces as they will be colder due to less energy absorbed from the reduced amount of sunlight in winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worldchange_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-worldchange-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete_dust">Concrete dust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Concrete dust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Building demolition and natural disasters such as earthquakes often release a large amount of concrete dust into the local atmosphere. Concrete dust was concluded to be the major source of dangerous air pollution following the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hanshin_earthquake" title="Great Hanshin earthquake">Great Hanshin earthquake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxic_and_radioactive_contamination">Toxic and radioactive contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Toxic and radioactive contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The presence of some substances in concrete, including useful and unwanted additives, can cause health concerns. Natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> elements (<a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">U</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Th</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">Rn</a>) can be present in various concentration in concrete dwellings, depending on the source of the raw materials used. For example, some stones naturally emit Radon, and Uranium was once common in mine refuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi:10.1016/j.jenvrad.2004.12.002_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-doi:10.1016/j.jenvrad.2004.12.002-27">[27]</a></sup> Toxic substances may also be unintentionally used as the result of contamination from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_accident" title="Nuclear accident">nuclear accident</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Dust from rubble or broken concrete upon demolition or crumbling may cause serious health concerns depending also on what had been incorporated in the concrete. However, embedding harmful materials in concrete is not always dangerous and may in fact be beneficial. In some cases, incorporating certain compounds such as metals in the hydration process of cement immobilizes them in a harmless state and prevents them from being released freely elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handling_precautions">Handling precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Handling precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information on safety issues associated with cement: <a href="/wiki/Cement#Safety_issues" title="Cement">Cement § Safety issues</a></div>
<p>Handling of wet concrete must always be done with proper protective equipment. Contact with wet concrete can cause skin <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">chemical burns</a> due to the <a href="/wiki/Corrosive_substance" title="Corrosive substance">caustic</a> nature of the mixture of cement and water. Indeed, the pH of fresh cement water is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> due to the presence of free <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxides</a> in solution (pH ~ 13.5). Eyes, hands and feet must be correctly protected to avoid any direct contact with wet concrete and washed without delay if necessary.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete_recycling">Concrete recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Concrete recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Concrete_recycling" title="Concrete recycling">Concrete recycling</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1947" data-file-width="2434" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg/220px-Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg/330px-Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg/440px-Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crushed_Concrete_Granular_Fill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Recycled crushed concrete being loaded into a semi-dump truck to be used as granular fill</div></div></div>
<p>Concrete recycling is an increasingly common method of disposing of concrete structures. Concrete debris was once routinely shipped to <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a> for disposal, but recycling is increasing due to improved environmental awareness, governmental laws and economic benefits.
</p><p>Concrete, which must be free of trash, wood, paper and other such materials, is collected from demolition sites and put through a <a href="/wiki/Crusher" title="Crusher">crushing machine</a>, often along with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">bricks</a> and rocks.
</p><p>Reinforced concrete contains <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a> and other metallic reinforcements, which are removed with <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnets</a> and recycled elsewhere. The remaining aggregate chunks are sorted by size. Larger chunks may go through the crusher again. Smaller pieces of concrete are used as gravel for new construction projects. <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_base" title="Aggregate base">Aggregate base</a> gravel is laid down as the lowest layer in a road, with fresh concrete or asphalt placed over it. Crushed recycled concrete can sometimes be used as the dry aggregate for brand new concrete if it is free of contaminants, though the use of recycled concrete limits strength and is not allowed in many jurisdictions. On 3 March 1983, a government-funded research team (the VIRL research.codep) approximated that almost 17% of worldwide landfill was by-products of concrete-based <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>.
</p>
