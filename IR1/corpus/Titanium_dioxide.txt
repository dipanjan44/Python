<p><b>Titanium dioxide</b>, also known as <b>titanium(IV) oxide</b> or <b>titania</b>, is the naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, chemical formula <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. When used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>, it is called <b>titanium white</b>, <b>Pigment White 6 (PW6)</b>, or <b><a href="/wiki/Colour_Index_International" title="Colour Index International">CI 77891</a></b>. Generally, it is sourced from <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatase" title="Anatase">anatase</a>. It has a wide range of applications, including <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunscreen" title="Sunscreen">sunscreen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">food coloring</a>. When used as a food coloring, it has <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E number</a> E171. World production in 2014 exceeded 9 million metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has been estimated that titanium dioxide is used in two-thirds of all pigments, and the oxide has been valued at $13.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Titanium dioxide occurs in nature as the well-known minerals <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatase" title="Anatase">anatase</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brookite" title="Brookite">brookite</a>, and additionally as two high pressure forms, a <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic_crystal_system" title="Monoclinic crystal system">monoclinic</a> <a href="/wiki/Baddeleyite" title="Baddeleyite">baddeleyite</a>-like form and an <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic_crystal_system" title="Orthorhombic crystal system">orthorhombic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lead_dioxide" title="Lead dioxide">α-PbO<sub>2</sub></a>-like form, both found recently at the <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6rdlinger_Ries" title="Nördlinger Ries">Ries crater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. One of these is known as <a href="/wiki/Akaogiite" title="Akaogiite">akaogiite</a> and should be considered as an extremely rare mineral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is mainly sourced from <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> ore. This is the most widespread form of titanium dioxide-bearing ore around the world. Rutile is the next most abundant and contains around 98% titanium dioxide in the ore. The metastable anatase and brookite phases convert irreversibly to the equilibrium rutile phase upon heating above temperatures in the range 600–800 °C (1,112–1,472 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Titanium dioxide has eight modifications – in addition to rutile, anatase, and brookite, three metastable phases can be produced synthetically (<a href="/wiki/Monoclinic_crystal_system" title="Monoclinic crystal system">monoclinic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal</a> and orthorombic), and five high-pressure forms (α-PbO<sub>2</sub>-like, baddeleyite-like, <a href="/wiki/Cotunnite" title="Cotunnite">cotunnite</a>-like, orthorhombic OI, and cubic phases) also exist:

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cotunnite" title="Cotunnite">cotunnite</a>-type phase was claimed by L. Dubrovinsky and co-authors to be the hardest known oxide with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vickers_hardness" title="Vickers hardness">Vickers hardness</a> of 38 GPa and the <a href="/wiki/Bulk_modulus" title="Bulk modulus">bulk modulus</a> of 431 GPa (i.e. close to diamond's value of 446 GPa) at atmospheric pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-du1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-du1-19">[19]</a></sup> However, later studies came to different conclusions with much lower values for both the hardness (7–20 GPa, which makes it softer than common oxides like corundum Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and rutile TiO<sub>2</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and bulk modulus (~300 GPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The oxides are commercially important ores of titanium. The metal can also be mined from other minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Leucoxene" title="Leucoxene">leucoxene</a> <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>, or one of the purest forms, rutile beach sand. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_sapphire_(jewel)" title="Star sapphire (jewel)">Star sapphires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">rubies</a> get their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asterism_(gemmology)" title="Asterism (gemmology)">asterism</a> from rutile impurities present in them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuildingBlocks451-3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuildingBlocks451-3-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Titanium dioxide (B) is found as a <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> in magmatic rocks and hydrothermal veins, as well as weathering rims on <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">perovskite</a>. TiO<sub>2</sub> also forms <a href="/wiki/Lamella_(materials)" title="Lamella (materials)">lamellae</a> in other minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spectral lines from titanium oxide are prominent in <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">class M</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, which are cool enough to allow molecules of this chemical to form.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg&amp;lang=en"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1322" data-file-width="1422" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg/220px-Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg/330px-Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg/440px-Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evolution_production_dioxyde_de_titane.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evolution of the global production of titanium dioxide according to process.</div></div></div>
<p>The production method depends on the feedstock. The most common mineral source is <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a>. The abundant <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">Rutile</a> mineral sand can also be purified with the <a href="/wiki/Chloride_process" title="Chloride process">chloride process</a> or other processes. Ilmenite converted into pigment grade titanium dioxide via either the sulfate process or the chloride process.  Sulfate and chloride process pigment tends to be used for lower and higher quality applications respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Sulfate process plants must utilise low-<a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> ilmenite, as vanadium is a penalty element. In the sulfate process Ilmenite is treated with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to extract <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfate" title="Iron(II) sulfate">iron(II) sulfate</a>.  The resulting synthetic rutile is further processed according to the specifications of the end user, i.e. pigment grade or otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Production_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Production-25">[25]</a></sup> In another method for the production of synthetic rutile from ilmenite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Becher_Process" title="Becher Process">Becher Process</a> first oxidizes the ilmenite as a means to separate the iron component.
</p><p>An alternative process, known as the <a href="/wiki/Chloride_process" title="Chloride process">Chloride process</a> converts ilmenite or other titanium sources to <a href="/wiki/Titanium_tetrachloride" title="Titanium tetrachloride">Titanium tetrachloride</a> via reaction with elemental <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, which is then purified by distillation, and reacted with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to regenerate chlorine and produce the Titanium dioxide. Titanium dioxide pigment can also be produced from higher titanium content feedstocks such as upgraded <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leucoxene" title="Leucoxene">leucoxene</a> via a chloride acid process.
</p><p>The five largest <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> pigment processors are Dupont, Cristal Global, Huntsman, Kronos and Tronox, Dupont having pioneered the chloride process in the 1960s and having converted to the use of the chloride process for all its applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayes_(2011)_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayes_(2011)-26">[26]</a></sup> Major paint and coating company end users for pigment grade titanium dioxide include Akzo Nobel, PPG Industries, Sherwin Williams, BASF, Kansai Paints and Valspar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayes_2011,_p._3_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hayes_2011,_p._3-27">[27]</a></sup> Global <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> pigment demand for 2010 was 5.3 Mt with annual growth expected to be about 3-4%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_methods">Specialized methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Specialized methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For specialty applications, TiO<sub>2</sub> films are prepared by various specialized chemistries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  Sol-gel routes involve the hydrolysis of titanium alkoxides, such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium_ethoxide" title="Titanium ethoxide">titanium ethoxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ti(OEt)<sub>4</sub>  + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O    →   TiO<sub>2</sub>  +  4 EtOH</dd></dl>
<p>This technology is suited for the preparation of films.  A related approach that also relies on molecular precursors involves <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a>.  In this application, the alkoxide is volatilized and then decomposed on contact with a hot surface:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ti(OEt)<sub>4</sub>   →   TiO<sub>2</sub>  +  2 Et<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most important application areas are paints and varnishes as well as paper and plastics, which account for about 80% of the world's titanium dioxide consumption. Other pigment applications such as printing inks, fibers, rubber, cosmetic products and food account for another 8%. The rest is used in other applications, for instance the production of technical pure titanium, glass and glass ceramics, electrical ceramics, catalysts, electric conductors and chemical intermediates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigment">Pigment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pigment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Titanium dioxide is the most widely used white pigment because of its brightness and very high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, in which it is surpassed only by a few other materials. (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_indices_of_refraction" title="List of indices of refraction">List of indices of refraction</a>.) Approximately 4.6 million tons of pigmentary TiO<sub>2</sub> are used annually worldwide, and this number is expected to increase as utilization continues to rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nano-scaled_titania_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nano-scaled_titania-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>TiO<sub>2</sub> is also an effective <a href="/wiki/Opacifier" title="Opacifier">opacifier</a> in powder form, where it is employed as a pigment to provide whiteness and <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a> to products such as <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">papers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">foods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicines</a> (i.e. pills and tablets) as well as most <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">toothpastes</a>. In paint, it is often referred to offhandedly as "brilliant white", "the perfect white", "the whitest white", or other similar terms. Opacity is improved by optimal sizing of the titanium dioxide particles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thin_films">Thin films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thin films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When deposited as a <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin film</a>, its refractive index and colour make it an excellent reflective optical coating for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_mirrors" title="Dielectric mirrors">dielectric mirrors</a>; it is also used in generating decorative thin films such as found in  "mystic fire topaz".
</p><p>Some grades of modified titanium based pigments as used in sparkly paints, plastics, finishes and cosmetics - these are man-made pigments whose particles have two or more layers of various oxides – often titanium dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> – in order to have glittering, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridescent" title="Iridescent">iridescent</a> and or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearlescent" title="Pearlescent">pearlescent</a> effects similar to crushed <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a> or <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a>-based products. In addition to these effects a limited colour change is possible in certain formulations depending on how and at which angle the finished product is illuminated and the thickness of the oxide layer in the pigment particle; one or more colours appear by reflection while the other tones appear due to interference of the transparent titanium dioxide layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In some products, the layer of titanium dioxide is grown in conjunction with iron oxide by calcination of titanium salts (sulfates, chlorates) around 800 °C<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> One example of a pearlescent pigment is Iriodin, based on mica coated with titanium dioxide or iron (III) oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The iridescent effect in these titanium oxide particles is unlike the opaque effect obtained with usual ground titanium oxide pigment obtained by mining, in which case only a certain diameter of the particle is considered and the effect is due only to scattering.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunscreen_and_UV_blocking_pigments">Sunscreen and UV blocking pigments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sunscreen and UV blocking pigments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skin_care" title="Skin care">skin care</a> products, titanium dioxide is used as a pigment, sunscreen and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thickener" title="Thickener">thickener</a>. As a sunscreen, it is notable in that combined with <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>, it is considered to be an effective sunscreen that is less harmful to <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> than sunscreens that include chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Oxybenzone" title="Oxybenzone">oxybenzone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Octyl_methoxycinnamate" title="Octyl methoxycinnamate">octinoxate</a>.
</p><p>Titanium dioxide is found in the majority of physical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunscreens" title="Sunscreens">sunscreens</a> because of its high refractive index, its strong UV light absorbing capabilities and its resistance to discolouration under <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light. This advantage enhances its stability and ability to protect the skin from ultraviolet light. Nano-scaled (particle size of 30–40 nm)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> titanium dioxide particles are primarily used in sunscreen lotion because they scatter visible light less than titanium dioxide pigments, while still providing UV protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nano-scaled_titania_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nano-scaled_titania-31">[31]</a></sup> Sunscreens designed for infants or people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensitive_skin_type" title="Sensitive skin type">sensitive skin</a> are often based on titanium dioxide and/or <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>, as these mineral UV blockers are believed to cause less skin irritation than other UV absorbing chemicals.
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is used extensively in plastics and other applications as a white pigment or an opacifier and for its UV resistant properties where the powder disperses light – unlike organic UV absorbers – and reduces UV damage, due mostly to the particle's high refractive index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain polymers used in the concrete or those used to impregnate concrete as a reinforcement are sometimes charged with titanium white pigment for UV shielding in the construction industry, but it only delays the oxidative photodegradation of the polymer in question, which is said to "chalk" as it flakes off due to lowered impact strength and may crumble after years of exposure in direct sunlight if UV stabilizers have not been included.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">ceramic glazes</a> titanium dioxide acts as an opacifier and seeds <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> formation.
</p><p>It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattoo</a> pigment and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Styptic_pencil" title="Styptic pencil">styptic pencils</a>. Titanium dioxide is produced in varying particle sizes, oil and water dispersible, and in certain grades for the cosmetic industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photocatalyst">Photocatalyst</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Photocatalyst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1632" data-file-width="2048" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg/220px-Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg/330px-Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg/440px-Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titanium_dioxide_nanofiber_spiral.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>TiO<sub>2</sub> fibers and spirals</div></div></div>
<p>Titanium dioxide, particularly in the anatase form, exhibits <a href="/wiki/Photocatalysis" title="Photocatalysis">photocatalytic activity</a> under ultraviolet (UV) irradiation. This photoactivity is reportedly most pronounced at the {001} planes of anatase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> although the {101} planes are thermodynamically more stable and thus more prominent in most synthesised and natural anatase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> as evident by the often observed tetragonal dipyramidal <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">growth habit</a>. Interfaces between rutile and anatase are further considered to improve photocatalytic activity by facilitating charge carrier separation and as a result, biphasic titanium dioxide is often considered to possess enhanced functionality as a photocatalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> It has been reported that titanium dioxide, when doped with nitrogen ions or doped with metal oxide like tungsten trioxide, exhibits excitation also under visible light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visible_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Visible-41">[41]</a></sup> The strong <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidative potential</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Electron_hole" title="Electron hole">positive holes</a> oxidizes <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> to create <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical" title="Hydroxyl radical">hydroxyl radicals</a>. It can also oxidize oxygen or organic materials directly. Hence, in addition to its use as a pigment, titanium dioxide can be added to paints, cements, windows, tiles, or other products for its sterilizing, deodorizing and anti-fouling properties and is used as a <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>. It is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dye-sensitized_solar_cells" title="Dye-sensitized solar cells">dye-sensitized solar cells</a>, which are a type of chemical solar cell (also known as a Graetzel cell).
</p><p>The photocatalytic properties of titanium dioxide were discovered by <a href="/wiki/Akira_Fujishima" title="Akira Fujishima">Akira Fujishima</a> in 1967<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujishima_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-fujishima-42">[42]</a></sup> and published in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The process on the surface of the titanium dioxide was called the <b>Honda-Fujishima effect</b> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%AC%E5%A4%9A-%E8%97%A4%E5%B6%8B%E5%8A%B9%E6%9E%9C" title="ja:本多-藤嶋効果">ja:本多-藤嶋効果</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujishima_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-fujishima-42">[42]</a></sup> Titanium dioxide, in <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanoparticle" title="Nanoparticle">nanoparticle</a> form has potential for use in energy production: as a photocatalyst, it can break water into hydrogen and oxygen. With the hydrogen collected, it could be used as a fuel. The efficiency of this process can be greatly improved by doping the oxide with carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Further efficiency and durability has been obtained by introducing disorder to the lattice structure of the surface layer of titanium dioxide nanocrystals, permitting infrared absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995 Fujishima and his group discovered the <a href="/wiki/Superhydrophilicity" title="Superhydrophilicity">superhydrophilicity</a> phenomenon for titanium dioxide coated glass exposed to sun light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujishima_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-fujishima-42">[42]</a></sup> This resulted in the development of <a href="/wiki/Self-cleaning_glass" title="Self-cleaning glass">self-cleaning glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-fog" title="Anti-fog">anti-fogging</a> coatings.
</p><p>TiO<sub>2</sub> incorporated into outdoor building materials, such as paving stones in <a href="/wiki/Noxer_block" title="Noxer block">noxer blocks</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  or paints, can substantially reduce concentrations of airborne pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> A cement that uses titanium dioxide as a photocatalytic component, produced by Italcementi Group, was included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a>'s Top 50 Inventions of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Attempts have been made to photocatalytically mineralize pollutants (to convert into CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O) in waste water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mineralize_Pollutants_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mineralize_Pollutants-49">[49]</a></sup> TiO<sub>2</sub> offers great potential as an industrial technology for detoxification or <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">remediation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> due to several factors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The process uses natural oxygen and sunlight and thus occurs under ambient conditions; it is wavelength selective and is accelerated by UV light.</li>
<li>The photocatalyst is inexpensive, readily available, non-toxic, chemically and mechanically stable, and has a high turnover.</li>
<li>The formation of photocyclized intermediate products, unlike direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photolysis" title="Photolysis">photolysis</a> techniques, is avoided.</li>
<li>Oxidation of the substrates to CO<sub>2</sub> is complete.</li>
<li>TiO<sub>2</sub> can be supported as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_films" title="Thin films">thin films</a> on suitable reactor substrates, which can be readily separated from treated water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>The photocatalytic destruction of organic matter is also exploited in photocatalytic antimicrobial coatings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> which are typically thin films applied to furniture in hospitals and other surfaces susceptible to be contaminated with bacteria, fungi and viruses.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroxyl_radical_formation">Hydroxyl radical formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hydroxyl radical formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although TiO<sub>2</sub> pigment does not absorb visible light, it does strongly absorb <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet (UV) radiation</a> (<i>hv</i>), leading to the formation of hydroxyl radicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-53">[53]</a></sup> This occurs when photo-induced valence bond holes (h<sup>+</sup><sub>vb</sub>) are trapped at the surface of TiO<sub>2</sub> leading to the formation of trapped holes (h<sup>+</sup><sub>tr</sub>) that cannot oxidize water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-:8-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>TiO<sub>2</sub> + <i>hv</i> → e<sup>−</sup> + h<sup>+</sup><sub>vb</sub></dd>
<dd>h<sup>+</sup><sub>vb</sub> → h<sup>+</sup><sub>tr</sub></dd>
<dd>O<sub>2</sub> + e<sup>−</sup> → O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup></dd>
<dd>O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> + O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup>+ 2<span class="Unicode"> </span><a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">H<sup>+</sup></a> → H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> + h<sup>+</sup><sub>vb</sub> → O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>O<sub>2</sub><sup>•−</sup> + h<sup>+</sup><sub>tr</sub> → O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd><span class="Unicode"> </span><a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH<sup>−</sup></a> + h<sup>+</sup><sub>vb</sub> → HO•</dd>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety">Health and safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Health and safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Titanium dioxide is incompatible with strong reducing agents and strong acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OHSINC_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-OHSINC-57">[57]</a></sup> Violent or incandescent reactions occur with molten metals that are very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a>, e.g. aluminium, calcium, magnesium, potassium, sodium, zinc and lithium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DANGPROP_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-DANGPROP-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many sunscreens use nanoparticle titanium dioxide (along with nanoparticle zinc oxide) which, despite reports of potential health risks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> is not actually absorbed through the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Other effects of titanium dioxide nanoparticles on human health are not well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Titanium dioxide dust, when inhaled, has been classified by the <a href="/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="International Agency for Research on Cancer">International Agency for Research on Cancer</a> (IARC) as an <a href="/wiki/List_of_IARC_Group_2B_carcinogens" title="List of IARC Group 2B carcinogens">IARC Group 2B carcinogen</a>, meaning it is <i>possibly carcinogenic to humans</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IARC_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-IARC-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The findings of the IARC are based on the discovery that high concentrations of pigment-grade (powdered) and ultrafine titanium dioxide dust caused respiratory tract cancer in rats exposed by inhalation and <a href="/wiki/Intratracheal_instillation" title="Intratracheal instillation">intratracheal instillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The series of biological events or steps that produce the rat lung cancers (e.g. particle deposition, impaired lung clearance, cell injury, fibrosis, mutations and ultimately cancer) have also been seen in people working in dusty environments. Therefore, the observations of cancer in animals were considered, by IARC, as relevant to people doing jobs with exposures to titanium dioxide dust. For example, titanium dioxide production workers may be exposed to high dust concentrations during packing, milling, site cleaning and maintenance, if there are insufficient dust control measures in place. However, the human studies conducted so far do not suggest an association between occupational exposure to titanium dioxide and an increased risk for cancer. The safety of the use of nano-particle sized titanium dioxide, which can penetrate the body and reach internal organs, has been criticized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Studies have also found that titanium dioxide nanoparticles cause inflammatory response and genetic damage in mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The mechanism by which <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> may cause cancer is unclear. Molecular research suggests that cell <a href="/wiki/Cytotoxicity" title="Cytotoxicity">cytotoxicity</a> due to <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> results from the interaction between <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>  nanoparticles and the <a href="/wiki/Lysosome" title="Lysosome">lysosomal</a> <a href="/wiki/Cellular_compartment" title="Cellular compartment">compartment</a>, independently of the known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoptotic" title="Apoptotic">apoptotic</a> signalling pathways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>The body of research regarding the carcinogenicity of different particle sizes of titanium dioxide has led the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> to recommend two separate exposure limits. NIOSH recommends that fine <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> particles be set at an exposure limit of 2.4 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, while <a href="/wiki/Ultrafine_particle" title="Ultrafine particle">ultrafine</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> be set at an exposure limit of 0.3 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, as time-weighted average concentrations up to 10 hours a day for a 40-hour work week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> These recommendations reflect the findings in the research literature that show smaller titanium dioxide particles are more likely to pose carcinogenic risk than the larger titanium dioxide particles.
</p><p>There is some evidence the rare disease <a href="/wiki/Yellow_nail_syndrome" title="Yellow nail syndrome">yellow nail syndrome</a> may be caused by titanium, either implanted for medical reasons or through eating various foods containing titanium dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMC3176400_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMC3176400-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dunkin%27_Donuts" title="Dunkin' Donuts">Dunkin' Donuts</a> in the United States is dropping titanium dioxide from its <a href="/wiki/Powdered_sugar" title="Powdered sugar">powdered sugar</a> <a href="/wiki/Doughnut" title="Doughnut">doughnuts</a> after public pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Due_and_undue_weight" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view"><span title="The material near this tag may be giving undue weight to a viewpoint or idea. (April 2015)">undue weight?</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Titanium_dioxide#undue" title="Talk:Titanium dioxide">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> However, Andrew Maynard, director of Risk Science Center at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>, downplayed the supposed danger from use of titanium dioxide in food. He says that the titanium dioxide used by Dunkin’ Brands and many other food producers is not a new material, and it is not a nanomaterial either. Nanoparticles are typically smaller than 100 nanometres in diameter, yet most of the particles in food grade titanium dioxide are much larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_waste_introduction">Environmental waste introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental waste introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Titanium dioxide (TiO₂) is mostly introduced into the environment as <a href="/wiki/Nanoparticle" title="Nanoparticle">nanoparticles</a> via wastewater treatment plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-75">[75]</a></sup> Cosmetic pigments including titanium dioxide enter the wastewater when the product is washed off into sinks after cosmetic use. Once in the sewage treatment plants, pigments separate into sewage sludge which can then be released into the soil when injected into the soil or distributed on its surface. 99% of these nanoparticles wind up on land rather than in aquatic environments due to their retention in sewage sludge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-75">[75]</a></sup> In the environment, titanium dioxide nanoparticles have low to negligible solubility and have been shown to be very stable once particle aggregates are formed in soil and water surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_75-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-75">[75]</a></sup> In the process of dissolution, water-soluble ions typically dissociate from the nanoparticle into solution when thermodynamically unstable. TiO<sub>2</sub> dissolution increases when there are higher levels of dissolved organic matter and clay in the soil. However, aggregation is promoted by pH at the isoelectric point of TiO<sub>2</sub> (pH = 5.8) which renders it neutral and solution ion concentrations above 4.5 mM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Titanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exterior of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket was painted with titanium dioxide; this later allowed astronomers to determine that <a href="/wiki/J002E3" title="J002E3">J002E3</a> was the <a href="/wiki/S-IVB" title="S-IVB">S-IVB</a> stage from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">Apollo 12</a> and not an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>.
</p>
