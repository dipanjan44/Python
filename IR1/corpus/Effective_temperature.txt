<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Effective temperature</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>effective temperature</b> of a body such as a star or planet is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> that would emit the same total amount of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archie2003_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archie2003-1">[1]</a></sup>  Effective temperature is often used as an estimate of a body's surface temperature when the body's <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> curve (as a function of <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>) is not known.
</p><p>When the star's or planet's net <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> in the relevant wavelength band is less than unity (less than that of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a>), the actual temperature of the body will be higher than the effective temperature.  The net emissivity may be low due to surface or atmospheric properties, including <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star">Star</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Star">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="3000" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png/250px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png/375px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png/500px-EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EffectiveTemperature_300dpi_e.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The effective temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> (5777 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>) is the temperature a black body of the same size must have to yield the same total emissive power.</div></div></div>
<p>The effective temperature of a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> is the temperature of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> with the same luminosity per <i>surface area</i> (<span class="texhtml"><span class="mathcal" style="font-family: 'Lucida Calligraphy', 'Monotype Corsiva', 'URW Chancery L', 'Apple Chancery', 'Tex Gyre Chorus', cursive, serif;">F</span><sub>Bol</sub></span>) as the star and is defined according to the <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">Stefan–Boltzmann law</a> <span class="texhtml"><span class="mathcal" style="font-family: 'Lucida Calligraphy', 'Monotype Corsiva', 'URW Chancery L', 'Apple Chancery', 'Tex Gyre Chorus', cursive, serif;">F</span><sub>Bol</sub> = <i>σT</i><sub>eff</sub><sup>4</sup></span>. Notice that the total (<a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">bolometric</a>) luminosity of a star is then <span class="texhtml"><i>L</i> = 4π<i>R</i><sup>2</sup><i>σT</i><sub>eff</sub><sup>4</sup></span>, where <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i></span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_radius" title="Stellar radius">stellar radius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The definition of the stellar radius is obviously not straightforward. More rigorously the effective temperature corresponds to the temperature at the radius that is defined by a certain value of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosseland_optical_depth" title="Rosseland optical depth">Rosseland optical depth</a> (usually 1) within the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere" title="Stellar atmosphere">stellar atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohm_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bohm-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The effective temperature and the bolometric luminosity are the two fundamental physical parameters needed to place a star on the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung–Russell diagram">Hertzsprung–Russell diagram</a>. Both effective temperature and bolometric luminosity depend on the chemical composition of a star.
</p><p>The effective temperature of our Sun is around 5780 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a> (K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ref__5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2004_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2004-6">[6]</a></sup>
Stars have a decreasing temperature gradient, going from their central core up to the atmosphere. The "core temperature" of the Sun—the temperature at the centre of the Sun where nuclear reactions take place—is estimated to be 15,000,000 K.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">color index</a> of a star indicates its temperature from the very cool—by stellar standards—red M stars that radiate heavily in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> to the very hot blue O stars that radiate largely in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>. The effective temperature of a star indicates the amount of heat that the star radiates per unit of surface area. From the warmest surfaces to the coolest is the sequence of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classifications</a> known as O, B, A, F, G, K, M.
</p><p>A red star could be a tiny <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarf</a>, a star of feeble energy production and a small surface or a bloated giant or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">supergiant</a> star such as <a href="/wiki/Antares" title="Antares">Antares</a> or <a href="/wiki/Betelgeuse" title="Betelgeuse">Betelgeuse</a>, either of which generates far greater energy but passes it through a surface so large that the star radiates little per unit of surface area. A star near the middle of the spectrum, such as the modest <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> or the giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capella_(star)" title="Capella (star)">Capella</a> radiates more energy per unit of surface area than the feeble red dwarf stars or the bloated supergiants, but much less than such a white or blue star as <a href="/wiki/Vega" title="Vega">Vega</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rigel" title="Rigel">Rigel</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planet">Planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_equilibrium_temperature" title="Planetary equilibrium temperature">Planetary equilibrium temperature</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blackbody_Temperature">Blackbody Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Blackbody Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The effective (blackbody) temperature of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> can be calculated by equating the power received by the planet with the power emitted by a blackbody of temperature <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span>.
</p><p>Take the case of a planet at a distance <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">D</span> from the star, of <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">L</span>.
</p><p>Assuming the star radiates isotropically and that the planet is a long way from the star, the power absorbed by the planet is given by treating the planet as a disc of radius <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span>, which intercepts some of the power which is spread over the surface of a sphere of radius <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">D</span> (the distance of the planet from the star). The calculation assumes the planet reflects some of the incoming radiation by incorporating a parameter called the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (a). </p><p>The effective temperature for <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> from this calculation is 88 K and <a href="/wiki/51_Pegasi_b" title="51 Pegasi b">51 Pegasi b</a> (Bellerophon) is 1,258 K.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  A better estimate of effective temperature for some planets, such as Jupiter, would need to include the <a href="/wiki/Internal_heating" title="Internal heating">internal heating</a> as a power input.  The actual temperature depends on <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> effects. The actual temperature from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_analysis" title="Spectroscopic analysis">spectroscopic analysis</a> for <a href="/wiki/HD_209458_b" title="HD 209458 b">HD 209458 b</a> (Osiris) is 1,130 K, but the effective temperature is 1,359 K.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The internal heating within Jupiter raises the effective temperature to about 152 K.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_temperature_of_a_planet">Surface temperature of a planet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface temperature of a planet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The surface temperature of a planet can be estimated by modifying the effective-temperature calculation to account for emissivity and temperature variation.
</p><p>The area of the planet that absorbs the power from the star is <span class="texhtml"><i>A</i><sub>abs</sub></span> which is some fraction of the total surface area <span class="texhtml"><i>A</i><sub>total</sub> = 4π<i>r</i><sup>2</sup></span>, where <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">r</span> is the radius of the planet. This area intercepts some of the power which is spread over the surface of a sphere of radius <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">D</span>. We also allow the planet to reflect some of the incoming radiation by incorporating a parameter <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span> called the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>. <p>Note the ratio of the two areas. Common assumptions for this ratio are <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Area_of_a_disk" title="Area of a disk">1</a></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><a href="/wiki/Sphere#Surface_area" title="Sphere">4</a></span></span> for a rapidly rotating body and <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> for a slowly rotating body, or a tidally locked body on the sunlit side. This ratio would be 1 for the <a href="/wiki/Subsolar_point" title="Subsolar point">subsolar point</a>, the point on the planet directly below the sun and gives the maximum temperature of the planet — a factor of <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">2</span></span> (1.414) greater than the effective temperature of a rapidly rotating planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also note here that this equation does not take into account any effects from internal heating of the planet, which can arise directly from sources such as <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> and also be produced from frictions resulting from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">tidal forces</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_Effective_Temperature">Earth Effective Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effective_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earth Effective Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_energy_budget" title="Earth's energy budget">Earth's energy budget</a></div>
<p>Let's look at the Earth. The Earth has an albedo of about 0.306.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The emissivity is dependent on the type of surface and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_models" title="Climate models">climate models</a> set the value of the Earth's emissivity to 1. However, a more realistic value is 0.96.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Earth is a fairly fast rotator so the area ratio can be estimated as <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">4</span></span>. The other variables are constant. This calculation gives us an effective temperature of the Earth of 252 K (−21 °C). The average temperature of the Earth is 288 K (15 °C). One reason for the difference between the two values is due to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>, which increases the average temperature of the Earth's surface.
</p>
