<p><b>Carbonyl sulfide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">linear formula</a> OCS.  Normally written as COS as a chemical formula that does not imply its structure,  it is a colourless flammable <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> with an unpleasant <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odor</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (December 2017)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup>.  It is a linear molecule consisting of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonded</a> to a sulfur atom.  Carbonyl sulfide can be considered to be intermediate between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a>, both of which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_isoelectronic" title="Valence isoelectronic">valence isoelectronic</a> with it.
</p><p>Carbonyl sulfide decomposes in the presence of humidity and bases to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsdb94_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsdb94-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>This compound is found to <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyze</a> the formation of <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a> from <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>.  This finding is an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment" title="Miller–Urey experiment">Miller–Urey experiment</a> and it is suggested that carbonyl sulfide played a significant role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonyl_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbonyl sulfide is the most abundant sulfur compound naturally present in the atmosphere, at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990500000000000000♠</span>0.5<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.05 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a></span>, because it is emitted from oceans, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sea_vent" title="Deep sea vent">deep sea vents</a>.  As such, it is a significant compound in the global <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>.  Measurements on the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a> and from air trapped in snow above glaciers (<a href="/wiki/Firn" title="Firn">firn</a> air) have provided a detailed picture of OCS concentrations from 1640 to the present day and allow an understanding of the relative importance of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> and non-anthropogenic sources of this gas to the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Some carbonyl sulfide that is transported into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> sulfate layer is oxidized to sulfuric acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Sulfuric acid forms particulate which affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Warming" title="Global Warming">energy balance</a> due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">light scattering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seinfeld_2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seinfeld_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> The long atmospheric lifetime of COS makes it the major source of stratospheric sulfate, though <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> from volcanic activity can be significant too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seinfeld_2006_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seinfeld_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> Carbonyl sulfide is also removed from the atmosphere by terrestrial vegetation by enzymes associated with the uptake of carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, and by hydrolysis in ocean waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Loss processes, such as these, limit the persistence (or lifetime) of a molecule of COS in the atmosphere to a few years.
</p><p>The largest man-made sources of carbonyl sulfide release include its primary use as a chemical intermediate and as a byproduct of  carbon disulfide production; however, it is also released from automobiles and their tire wear.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> coal-fired power plants, coking ovens, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> combustion, fish processing, combustion of refuse and plastics, petroleum manufacture, and manufacture of synthetic fibers, starch, and rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsdb94_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsdb94-2">[2]</a></sup>  The average total worldwide release of carbonyl sulfide to the atmosphere has been estimated at about 3 million tons/year, of which less than one third was related to human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsdb94_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-hsdb94-2">[2]</a></sup> It is also a significant sulfur-containing impurity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a>.
</p><p>Carbonyl sulfide is present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foodstuff" title="Foodstuff">foodstuffs</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a> and prepared <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a> family. Traces of COS are naturally present in <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> in the range of 0.05–0.1 mg·kg<sup>−1</sup>.
</p><p>Carbonyl sulfide has been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_molecules_in_interstellar_space" title="List of molecules in interstellar space">List of molecules in interstellar space</a>), in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_67P" title="Comet 67P">comet 67P</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">atmosphere of Venus</a>, where, because of the difficulty of producing COS inorganically, it is considered a possible indicator of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis2003b_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis2003b-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonyl_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbonyl sulfide is used as an intermediate in the production of thiocarbamate herbicides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup> Carbonyl sulfide is a potential alternative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumigant" title="Fumigant">fumigant</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_bromide" title="Methyl bromide">methyl bromide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">phosphine</a>. In some cases, however, residues on the grain result in flavours that are unacceptable to consumers, e.g. barley used for brewing. Carbonyl sulfide is readily converted to the <a href="/wiki/Gaseous_signaling_molecules" title="Gaseous signaling molecules">gaseous signaling molecule</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> enzymes in plants and mammals. Because of this chemistry, the release of carbonyl sulfide from small organic molecules has been identified as a strategy for delivering hydrogen sulfide in different biological contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonyl_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbonyl sulfide was first described in 1841,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> but was apparently mischaracterized as a mixture of carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Than" title="Carl von Than">Carl von Than</a> first characterized the substance in 1867. It forms when <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> reacts with molten sulfur.  This reaction reverses above <span style="white-space: nowrap">1<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">200</span></span> K (930 °C; <span style="white-space: nowrap">1<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">700</span></span> °F).  A laboratory synthesis entails the reaction <a href="/wiki/Potassium_thiocyanate" title="Potassium thiocyanate">potassium thiocyanate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. The resulting gas contains significant amounts of byproducts and requires purification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Hydrolysis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isothiocyanates" title="Isothiocyanates">isothiocyanates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> solution also affords COS.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonyl_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 1994, limited information existed on the acute toxicity of carbonyl sulfide in humans and in animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup>  High concentrations (&gt;1000 ppm) can cause sudden collapse, convulsions, and death from respiratory paralysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsdb94_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-hsdb94-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup>  Occasional fatalities have been reported, practically without local irritation or olfactory warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup>  In tests with rats, 50% animals died when exposed to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997140000000000000♠</span>1400 ppm</span> of COS for 90 minutes, or at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997300000000000000♠</span>3000 ppm</span> for 9 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup> Limited studies with laboratory animals also suggest that continued inhalation of low concentrations (~50 ppm for up to 12 weeks) does not affect the lungs or the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa94_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-epa94-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
