<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fisheries and climate change</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="541" data-file-width="900" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg/300px-Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg/450px-Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg/600px-Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bangladesh_Fishing_2006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fishing with a lift net in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. Coastal fishing communities in Bangladesh are vulnerable to flooding from sea-level rises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Rising <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">ocean temperatures</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are radically altering <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystems</a>. <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Climate change</a> is modifying fish distribution<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the productivity of marine and freshwater species. This has impacts on the sustainability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, on the livelihoods of the communities that depend on fisheries, and on the ability of the oceans to capture and store carbon (<a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological pump</a>). The effect of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> means that coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_community" title="Fishing community">fishing communities</a> are in the front line of climate change, while changing rainfall patterns and water use impact on inland (freshwater) fisheries and aquaculture. The full relationship between fisheries and climate change is difficult to explore due to the context of each fishery and the many pathways that climate change affects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_oceans">Role of oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role of oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg/220px-Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg/330px-Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg/440px-Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maldives_-_Kurumba_Island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Island with <a href="/wiki/Fringing_reef" title="Fringing reef">fringing reef</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reefs</a> are dying around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Oceans" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming § Oceans</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">Oceans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_ecosystems" title="Coastal ecosystems">coastal ecosystems</a> play an important role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_carbon_cycle" title="Global carbon cycle">global carbon cycle</a> and have removed about 25% of the carbon dioxide emitted by human activities between 2000 and 2007 and about half the anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> released since the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Rising ocean temperatures and ocean acidification means that the capacity of the ocean <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a> will gradually get weaker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> giving rise to global concerns expressed in the Monaco<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Manado<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
Declarations.  Healthy ocean ecosystems are essential for the mitigation of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Coral reefs provide habitat for millions of fish species and with no change it can provoke these reefs to die.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_fish_production">Impact on fish production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact on fish production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rising <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidity</a> makes it more difficult for marine organisms such as shrimps, oysters, or corals to form their shells – a process known as <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcification</a>. Many important animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>, that forms the base of the marine food chain have calcium shells. Thus the entire marine food web is being altered – there are ‘cracks in the food chain’.  As a result, the distribution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> productivity, and species composition of global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_fish_production" title="World fish production">fish production</a> is changing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> generating complex and inter-related impacts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> on oceans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">estuaries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coral_reefs" title="Coral reefs">coral reefs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mangroves" title="Mangroves">mangroves</a> and sea grass beds that provide habitats and nursery areas for fish. Changing rainfall patterns and <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> is impacting on river and lake fisheries and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> After the ice age about 200,000 years ago, the global air temperature has risen 3 degrees, leading to an increase in sea temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fish populations of <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bigeye_tuna" title="Bigeye tuna">bigeye tuna</a> are expected to be displaced further to the east due to the effects of climate change on ocean temperatures and currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This will shift the fishing grounds toward the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific islands</a> and away from its primary owner of <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>, disrupting western Pacific canneries, shifting tuna production elsewhere, and having an uncertain effect on food security.
</p><p>Species that are over-fished, such as the variants of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a>, are more susceptible to the effects of climate change. Over-fished populations have less size, genetic diversity, and age than other populations of fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This makes them more susceptible to environment related stress, including those resulting from climate change. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, which are stressed close to their upper limits, this could lead to consequences related to the population's average size and growth.
</p><p>Due to climate change, the distribution of zooplankton has changed. Cool water cope-pod assemblages have moved north because the waters get warmer, they have been replaced by warm water cope-pods assemblages however it has a lower biomass and certain small species. Atlantic cod require a diet of large cope-pods but because they have moved pole-wards morality rates are high and as a result the recruitment of this cod has plummeted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_fishing_communities">Impact on fishing communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on fishing communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1577" data-file-width="1589" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg/220px-Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg/330px-Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg/440px-Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fisherman_Seychelles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fisherman landing his catch, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a></div></div></div>
<p>Coastal and fishing populations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and countries dependent on fisheries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> are particularly vulnerable to climate change. Low-lying countries such as the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a> are particularly vulnerable and entire communities may become the first climate refugees. Fishing communities in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> are subject not only to sea-level rise, but also flooding and increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoons" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a>. Fishing communities along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_river" title="Mekong river">Mekong river</a> produce over 1 million tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basa_fish" title="Basa fish">basa fish</a> annually and livelihoods and fish production will suffer from <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_intrusion" title="Saltwater intrusion">saltwater intrusion</a> resulting from rising sea level and dams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>While climate change increases the effects of human activities, the inverse is also applicable. Human activities also increase the impact of climate change. Human activity has been linked to lake nutrition levels, which high levels are correlated to increasing vulnerability to climate change. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Annecy" title="Lake Annecy">Lake Annecy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Bourget" title="Lake Bourget">Lake Bourget</a> were subject to experiments related to their zooplankton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Lake Geneva and Lake Bourget had relatively high levels of nutrients and responded at a significant level towards factors related to climate change, such as weather variability. Lake Annecy had the lowest amount of nutrition levels and responded comparatively poorly.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">Fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> contribute significantly to <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> and livelihoods. Fish provides essential nutrition for 3 billion people and at least 50% of animal protein and minerals to 400 million people from the poorest countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This food security is threatened by climate change and the increasing world population. Climate change changes several parameters of the fishing population: availability, stability, access, and utilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The specific effects of climate change on these parameters will vary widely depending on the characteristics of the area, with some areas benefiting from the shift in trends and some areas being harmed based on the factors of exposure, sensitivity, and ability to respond to said changes.The lack of oxygen in warmer waters will possibly lead to the extinction of aquatic animals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Worldwide food security may not change significantly, however rural and poor populations would be disproportionately and negatively affected based on this criteria, as they lack the resources and manpower to rapidly change their infrastructure and adapt. Over 500 million people in developing countries depend, directly or indirectly, on fisheries and aquaculture for their livelihoods - aquaculture is the world’s fastest growing food production system, growing at 7% annually and fish products are among the most widely traded foods, with more than 37% (by volume) of world production traded internationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_and_mitigation">Adaptation and mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adaptation and mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The impacts of climate change can be addressed through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_climate_change" title="Adaptation to climate change">adaptation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigation</a>. The costs and benefits of adaptation are essentially local or national, while the costs of mitigation are essentially national whereas the benefits are global. Some activities generate both mitigation and adaptation benefits, for example, the restoration of <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forests can protect shorelines from erosion and provide breeding grounds for fish while also sequestering carbon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation">Adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several international agencies, including the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> have programs to help countries and communities adapt to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, for example by developing policies to improve the resilience<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> of natural resources, through assessments of risk and vulnerability, by increasing awareness<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> of climate change impacts and strengthening key institutions, such as for weather forecasting and early warning systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Development_Report" title="World Development Report">World Development Report</a> 2010 - Development and Climate Change, Chapter 3<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> shows that reducing overcapacity in <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleets</a> and rebuilding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_stocks" title="Fish stocks">fish stocks</a> can both improve resilience to climate change and increase economic returns from marine capture fisheries by US$50 billion per year, while also reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_emissions" title="GHG emissions">GHG emissions</a> by fishing fleets. Consequently, removal of subsidies on fuel for fishing can have a double benefit by reducing emissions and <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>.
</p><p>Investment in sustainable aquaculture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> can buffer water use in agriculture while producing food and diversifying economic activities. Algal <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> also show potential as <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> can produce 15-300 times more oil per acre than conventional crops, such as rapeseed, soybeans, or jatropha and marine algae do not require scarce freshwater. Programs such as the <a href="/wiki/Global_Environment_Facility" title="Global Environment Facility">GEF</a>-funded Coral Reef Targeted Research provide advice on building resilience and conserving <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> ecosystems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> while six Pacific countries recently gave a formal undertaking to protect the reefs in a <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">biodiversity hotspot</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Triangle" title="Coral Triangle">Coral Triangle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="261" data-file-width="660" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg/220px-White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg/330px-White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg/440px-White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:White_cliffs_of_dover_09_2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_White_Cliffs_of_Dover" title="The White Cliffs of Dover">The White Cliffs of Dover</a></div></div></div>
<p>The oceans have removed 50%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> of the anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub>, so the oceans have absorbed much of the impact of climate change. The famous <a href="/wiki/White_Cliffs_of_Dover" title="White Cliffs of Dover">White Cliffs of Dover</a> illustrate how the ocean captures and buries carbon. These limestone cliffs are formed from the skeletons of marine plankton called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccoliths" title="Coccoliths">coccoliths</a>. Similarly, petroleum formation is attributed largely to marine and aquatic plankton further illustrating the key role of the oceans in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.
</p><p>Exactly how the oceans capture and bury CO<sub>2</sub> is the subject of intense research<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> by scientists worldwide, such as the Carboocean Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The current level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_emissions" title="GHG emissions">GHG emissions</a> means that ocean acidity will continue to increase and <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystems</a> will continue to degrade and change. There are feedback mechanisms involved here. For example, warmer waters can absorb less CO<sub>2</sub>, so as ocean temperatures rise some dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> will be released back into the atmosphere. Warming also reduces nutrient levels in the <a href="/wiki/Mesopelagic_zone" title="Mesopelagic zone">mesopelagic zone</a> (about 200 to 1000 m deep). This in turn limits the growth of <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a> in favour of smaller <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> that are poorer <a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological pumps</a> of carbon. This inhibits the ability of the ocean ecosystems to sequester carbon as the oceans warm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> What is clear, is that healthy ocean and coastal ecosystems are necessary to continue the vital role of the ocean carbon sinks, as indicated, for example, by the Blue Carbon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> assessment prepared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a> and the coastal carbon sinks report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a> and growing evidence of the role of fish biomass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> in the transport of carbon from surface waters to the deep ocean.
</p><p>While the various <a href="/wiki/Carbon_finance" title="Carbon finance">carbon finance</a> instruments include restoration of forests (REDD) and producing clean energy (<a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">emissions trading</a>), few address the need to finance healthy ocean and aquatic ecosystems although these are essential for continued uptake of CO<sub>2</sub> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_emissions" title="GHG emissions">GHGs</a>. The scientific basis for <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">ocean fertilization</a> – to produce more phytoplankton to increase the uptake of CO<sub>2</sub> – has been challenged, and proposals for burial of CO<sub>2</sub> in the deep ocean have come under criticism from environmentalists.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Over-fishing">Over-fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Over-fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although there is a decline of fisheries due to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, a related cause for this decrease is due to over-fishing. Over-fishing exacerbates the effects of climate change by creating conditions that make a fishing population more sensitive to environmental changes. Studies show that the state of the ocean is causing fisheries to collapse, and in areas where fisheries have not yet collapsed, the amount of over-fishing that is done is having a significant impact on the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scorse_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scorse-44">[44]</a></sup> Over-fishing is due to having access to the open sea, it makes it very easy for people to over fish, even if it is just for fun. There is also a high demand for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_food" title="Sea food">sea food</a> by fishermen, as well modern technology that has increased the amount of fish caught during each trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scorse_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scorse-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>If there was a specific amount of fish that people were allowed to catch then this could very well solve the problem of over fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scorse_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Scorse-44">[44]</a></sup> This type of limit system is in place in a few countries including <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In these countries the limit system has successfully helped in fishing industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scorse_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Scorse-44">[44]</a></sup> These types of limit systems are called <a href="/wiki/Individual_fishing_quota" title="Individual fishing quota">Individual fishing quota</a>. This means that the areas where this quota exist, the government has legal entity over it and in these boundaries they are entitled to utilize their ocean resources as they wish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scorse_44-4"><a href="#cite_note-Scorse-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
