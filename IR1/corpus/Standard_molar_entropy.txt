<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Standard molar entropy</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the <b>standard molar entropy</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> content of one <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of substance under a <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a> (not  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">STP</a>).
</p><p>The standard molar entropy is usually given the symbol <i>S</i>°, and has units of <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> per mole <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a> (J mol<sup>−1</sup> K<sup>−1</sup>). Unlike <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_change_of_formation" title="Standard enthalpy change of formation">standard enthalpies of formation</a>, the value of <i>S</i>° is absolute.  That is, an element in its standard state has a definite, nonzero value of <i>S</i> at room temperature. The entropy of a pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> structure can be 0 J mol<sup>−1</sup> K<sup>−1</sup> only at 0<span class="nowrap"> </span>K, according to the <a href="/wiki/Third_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Third law of thermodynamics">third law of thermodynamics</a>. However, this presupposes that the material forms a 'perfect <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>' without any frozen in entropy (defects, dislocations), which is never completely true because crystals always grow at a finite temperature.  However this residual entropy is often quite negligible.delta g is negative. Entropy is find out by S =delta H / temperature
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_molar_entropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Here, <i>dq<sub>k</sub></i>/<i>T</i> represents a very small exchange of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> <i>T</i>. The total molar entropy is the sum of many small changes in molar entropy, where each small change can be considered a <a href="/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics)" title="Reversible process (thermodynamics)">reversible</a> process.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_molar_entropy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The standard molar entropy of a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">STP</a> includes contributions from:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> of one mole of the <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> from 0 K to the <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> (including heat absorbed in any changes between different <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structures</a>)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_heat_of_fusion" title="Latent heat of fusion">latent heat of fusion</a> of the solid.</li>
<li>The heat capacity of the <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> from the melting point to the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_heat_of_vaporization" title="Latent heat of vaporization">latent heat of vaporization</a> of the liquid.</li>
<li>The heat capacity of the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> from the boiling point to room <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Changes in entropy are associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_transitions" title="Phase transitions">phase transitions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_equations" title="Chemical equations">Chemical equations</a> make use of the standard molar entropy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactants" title="Reactants">reactants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a> to find the standard entropy of reaction:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Δ<i>S</i>°<sub>rxn</sub> = <i>S</i>°<sub>products</sub> – <i>S</i>°<sub>reactants</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The standard entropy of reaction helps determine whether the reaction will take place <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_process" title="Spontaneous process">spontaneously</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, a spontaneous reaction always results in an increase in total entropy of the system and its surroundings:
</p>
<dl><dd>Δ<i>S</i><sub>total</sub> = Δ<i>S</i><sub>system</sub> + Δ<i>S</i><sub>surroundings</sub> &gt; 0</dd></dl>
<p>Molar entropy is not same for all gases. Under identical conditions, it is greater for heavier gas.
</p>
