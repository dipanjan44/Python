<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coral</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Marine invertebrates of the class Anthozoa</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Coral_(disambiguation)" title="Coral (disambiguation)">Coral (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg/300px-Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg/450px-Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg/600px-Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coral_Outcrop_Flynn_Reef.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A coral outcrop on the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Corals</b> are <a href="/wiki/Marine_invertebrates" title="Marine invertebrates">marine invertebrates</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthozoa" title="Anthozoa">Anthozoa</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> <a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">Cnidaria</a>. They typically live in compact <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonies</a> of many identical individual <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyps</a>. Corals species include the important <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">reef</a> builders that inhabit tropical oceans and secrete <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> to form a hard skeleton.
</p><p>A coral "group" is a colony of myriad <a href="/wiki/Cloning" title="Cloning">genetically identical</a> polyps. Each polyp is a sac-like animal typically only a few millimeters in diameter and a few centimeters in length. A set of <a href="/wiki/Tentacle" title="Tentacle">tentacles</a> surround a central mouth opening. An <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> is excreted near the base. Over many generations, the colony thus creates a large skeleton characteristic of the species. Individual heads grow by <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a> of polyps. Corals also breed sexually by spawning: polyps of the same species release <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a> simultaneously over a period of one to several nights around a <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>.
</p><p>Although some corals are able to catch small <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> using <a href="/wiki/Cnidocyte" title="Cnidocyte">stinging cells</a> on their tentacles, most corals obtain the majority of their energy and nutrients from <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unicellular" title="Unicellular">unicellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Symbiodinium" title="Symbiodinium">Symbiodinium</a></i> that live within their tissues. These are commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Zooxanthellae" title="Zooxanthellae">zooxanthellae</a>. Such corals require sunlight and grow in clear, shallow water, typically at depths less than 60 metres (200 ft). Corals are major contributors to the physical structure of the <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> that develop in tropical and subtropical waters, such as the enormous <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia.
</p><p>Other corals do not rely on zooxanthellae and can live in much deeper water, with the cold-water <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Lophelia" title="Lophelia">Lophelia</a></i> surviving as deep as 3,300 metres (10,800 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squires_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Squires-1">[1]</a></sup> Some have been found on the <a href="/wiki/Darwin_Mounds" title="Darwin Mounds">Darwin Mounds</a>, north-west of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Wrath" title="Cape Wrath">Cape Wrath</a>, Scotland, and others as far north as off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington State</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s pupil <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> described the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corallium_rubrum" title="Corallium rubrum">red coral</a>, <i>korallion</i> in his book on stones, implying it was a mineral; but he described it as a deep-sea plant in his <i>Enquiries on Plants</i>, where he also mentions large stony plants that reveal bright flowers when under water in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Gulf of Heroes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> stated boldly that several sea creatures including sea nettles and sponges "are neither animals nor plants, but are possessed of a third nature (<i>tertius natura</i>)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen2015-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Gyllius" title="Petrus Gyllius">Petrus Gyllius</a> copied Pliny, introducing the term <i>zoophyta</i> for this third group in his 1535 book <i>On the French and Latin Names of the Fishes of the Marseilles Region</i>; it is popularly but wrongly supposed that Aristotle created the term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen2015_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen2015-3">[3]</a></sup> Gyllius further noted, following Aristotle, how hard it was to define what was a plant and what was an animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen2015_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen2015-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Persian polymath <a href="/wiki/Al-Biruni" title="Al-Biruni">Al-Biruni</a> (d. 1048) classified sponges and corals as animals, arguing that they respond to touch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egerton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egerton-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, people believed corals to be plants until the eighteenth century, when <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> used a microscope to establish that coral had the characteristic thin cell membranes of an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ep2-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Presently, corals are classified as certain species of animals within the sub-classes <a href="/wiki/Hexacorallia" title="Hexacorallia">Hexacorallia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Octocorallia" title="Octocorallia">Octocorallia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthozoa" title="Anthozoa">Anthozoa</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> <a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">Cnidaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Hexacorallia includes the stony corals and these groups have <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyps</a> that generally have a 6-fold symmetry. Octocorallia includes <a href="/wiki/Blue_coral" title="Blue coral">blue coral</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_coral" title="Soft coral">soft corals</a> and species of Octocorallia have polyps with an 8-fold symmetry, each polyp having eight tentacles and eight <a href="/wiki/Mesentery_(zoology)" title="Mesentery (zoology)">mesenteries</a>. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fire_coral" title="Fire coral">Fire corals</a> are not true corals, being in the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthomedusa" title="Anthomedusa">Anthomedusa</a> (sometimes known as Anthoathecata) of the class <a href="/wiki/Hydrozoa" title="Hydrozoa">Hydrozoa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coral_polyp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="462" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Coral_polyp.jpg/220px-Coral_polyp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Coral_polyp.jpg/330px-Coral_polyp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Coral_polyp.jpg/440px-Coral_polyp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coral_polyp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Anatomy of a stony coral polyp</div></div></div>
<p>Corals are <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(motility)" title="Sessility (motility)">sessile</a> animals and differ from most other cnidarians in not having a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medusa_(biology)" title="Medusa (biology)">medusa</a> stage in their life cycle. The body unit of the animal is a <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyp</a>. Most corals are colonial, the initial polyp budding to produce another and the colony gradually developing from this small start. In stony corals, also known as hard corals, the polyps produce a skeleton composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> to strengthen and protect the organism. This is deposited by the polyps and by the <a href="/wiki/Coenosarc" title="Coenosarc">coenosarc</a>, the living tissue that connects them. The polyps sit in cup-shaped depressions in the skeleton known as <a href="/wiki/Corallite" title="Corallite">corallites</a>. Colonies of stony coral are very variable in appearance; a single species may adopt an encrusting, plate-like, bushy, columnar or massive solid structure, the various forms often being linked to different types of habitat, with variations in light level and water movement being significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soft_corals">Soft corals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soft corals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In soft corals, there is no stony skeleton but the tissues are often toughened by the presence of tiny skeletal elements known as sclerites, which are made from calcium carbonate. Soft corals are very variable in form and most are colonial. A few soft corals are <a href="/wiki/Stolon" title="Stolon">stolonate</a>, but the polyps of most are connected by sheets of coenosarc. In some species this is thick and the polyps are deeply embedded. Some soft corals are encrusting or form lobes. Others are tree-like or whip-like and have a central axial skeleton embedded in the tissue matrix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This is composed either of a fibrous protein called <a href="/wiki/Gorgonin" title="Gorgonin">gorgonin</a> or of a calcified material. In both stony and soft corals, the polyps can be retracted, with stony corals relying on their hard skeleton and <a href="/wiki/Cnidocyte" title="Cnidocyte">cnidocytes</a> for defence against predators, and soft corals generally relying on chemical defences in the form of toxic substances present in the tissues known as <a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stony_corals">Stony corals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stony corals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1156" data-file-width="1780" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg/220px-Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg/330px-Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg/440px-Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montastrea_cavernosa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montastraea_cavernosa" title="Montastraea cavernosa">Montastraea cavernosa</a></i> polyps with tentacles extended</div></div></div>
<p>The polyps of stony corals have six-fold symmetry while those of soft corals have eight. The mouth of each polyp is surrounded by a ring of tentacles. In stony corals these are cylindrical and taper to a point, but in soft corals they are pinnate with side branches known as pinnules. In some tropical species these are reduced to mere stubs and in some they are fused to give a paddle-like appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sprung_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sprung-10">[10]</a></sup> In most corals, the tentacles are retracted by day and spread out at night to catch plankton and other small organisms. Shallow water species of both stony and soft corals can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zooxanthella" title="Zooxanthella">zooxanthellate</a>, the corals supplementing their plankton diet with the products of photosynthesis produced by these <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbionts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruppert_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ruppert-8">[8]</a></sup> The polyps interconnect by a complex and well-developed system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrovascular" title="Gastrovascular">gastrovascular</a> canals, allowing significant sharing of nutrients and symbionts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gateno_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gateno-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feeding">Feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Polyps feed on a variety of small organisms, from microscopic zooplankton to small fish. The polyp's tentacles immobilize or kill prey using their nematocysts. These cells carry <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> which they rapidly release in response to contact with another organism. A dormant nematocyst discharges in response to nearby prey touching the trigger (cnidocil). A flap (operculum) opens and its stinging apparatus fires the barb into the prey. The venom is injected through the hollow filament to immobilise the prey; the tentacles then manoeuvre the prey to the mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The tentacles then contract to bring the prey into the stomach. Once the prey is digested, the stomach reopens, allowing the elimination of waste products and the beginning of the next hunting cycle. They can scavenge drifting organic molecules and dissolved organic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murph_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-murph-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intracellular_symbionts">Intracellular symbionts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Intracellular symbionts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many corals, as well as other <a href="/wiki/Cnidaria" title="Cnidaria">cnidarian</a> groups such as  <i><a href="/wiki/Aiptasia" title="Aiptasia">Aiptasia</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Sea_anemone" title="Sea anemone">sea anemone</a>) form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbiotic" title="Symbiotic">symbiotic</a> relationship with a class of <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellate</a> <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zooxanthellae" title="Zooxanthellae">zooxanthellae</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Symbiodinium" title="Symbiodinium">Symbiodinium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murph_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-murph-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup> <i>Aiptasia</i>, a familiar pest among coral reef aquarium hobbyists, serves as a valuable <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> in the study of cnidarian-algal symbiosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Typically, each polyp harbors one species of algae, and coral species show a preference for <i><a href="/wiki/Symbiodinium" title="Symbiodinium">Symbiodinium</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Young corals are not born with zooxanthellae, but acquire the algae from the surrounding environment, including the water column and local sediment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>   Via photosynthesis, these provide energy for the coral, and aid in <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilneBay_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-MilneBay-17">[17]</a></sup> The main benefit of the zooxanthellae is their ability to photosynthesize. By using this technique, zooxanthellae are able to supply corals with  the products of photosynthesis, including glucose, glycerol, and amino acids, which the corals can use for energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As much as 30% of the tissue of a polyp may be algal material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murph_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-murph-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> Zooxanthellae also benefit corals by aiding in waste removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The algae benefit from a safe place to live and consume the polyp's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and nitrogenous waste. Due to the strain the algae can put on the polyp, stress on the coral often drives them to eject the algae. Mass ejections are known as <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>, because the algae contribute to coral's brown coloration; other colors, however, are due to host coral pigments, such as <a href="/wiki/Green_fluorescent_protein" title="Green fluorescent protein">green fluorescent proteins</a> (GFPs). Ejection increases the polyp's chance of surviving short-term stress—they can regain algae, possibly of a different species at a later time. If the stressful conditions persist, the polyp eventually dies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toller_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toller-20">[20]</a></sup> Zooxanthellae are located within the corals' cytoplasm and due to the algae's photosynthetic activity, the internal pH of the coral can be raised; this behavior indicates that the zooxanthellae are responsible to some extent for the metabolism of their host corals <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Corals can be both <a href="/wiki/Gonochorism" title="Gonochorism">gonochoristic</a> (unisexual) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditism" title="Hermaphroditism">hermaphroditic</a>, each of which can reproduce sexually and asexually. Reproduction also allows coral to settle in new areas. Reproduction is coordinated by chemical communication.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual">Sexual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sexual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="974" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg/400px-Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg/600px-Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg/800px-Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coral_Life_Cycles_ZP.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Life cycles of broadcasters and brooders</div></div></div>
<p>Corals predominantly reproduce <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexually</a>. About 25% of <a href="/wiki/Hermatypic_coral" title="Hermatypic coral">hermatypic corals</a> (stony corals) form single sex (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonochoristic" title="Gonochoristic">gonochoristic</a>) colonies, while the rest are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermaphroditic" title="Hermaphroditic">hermaphroditic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veron_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Veron-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasters">Broadcasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Broadcasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>About 75% of all hermatypic corals "broadcast spawn" by releasing <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>—into the water to spread offspring. The gametes fuse during fertilization to form a microscopic <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larva</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Planula" title="Planula">planula</a>, typically pink and elliptical in shape. A typical coral colony forms several thousand larvae per year to overcome the odds against formation of a new colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes99_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes99-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="352" data-file-width="480" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg/220px-Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg/330px-Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg/440px-Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stony_coral_spawning_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_star_coral" title="Great star coral">great star coral</a>, <i>Montastraea cavernosa</i>, releasing sperm into the water.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Reproductive_synchrony" title="Reproductive synchrony">Synchronous spawning</a> is very typical on the coral reef, and often, even when multiple <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are present, all corals spawn on the same night. This synchrony is essential so male and female gametes can meet. Corals rely on environmental cues, varying from species to species, to determine the proper time to release gametes into the water. The cues involve temperature change, <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar cycle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_length" title="Day length">day length</a>, and possibly chemical signalling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veron_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Veron-22">[22]</a></sup> Synchronous spawning may form hybrids and is perhaps involved in coral <a href="/wiki/Speciation" title="Speciation">speciation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hatta_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hatta-24">[24]</a></sup> The immediate cue is most often sunset, which cues the release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veron_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Veron-22">[22]</a></sup> The spawning event can be visually dramatic, clouding the usually clear water with gametes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brooders">Brooders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Brooders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Brooding species are most often ahermatypic (not reef-building) in areas of high current or wave action. Brooders release only sperm, which is negatively buoyant, sinking on to the waiting egg carriers who harbor unfertilized eggs for weeks. Synchronous spawning events sometimes occurs even with these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veron_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Veron-22">[22]</a></sup> After fertilization, the corals release planula that are ready to settle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MilneBay_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-MilneBay-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Planulae">Planulae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Planulae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Planula" title="Planula">Planula</a> larvae exhibit positive <a href="/wiki/Phototaxis" title="Phototaxis">phototaxis</a>, swimming towards light to reach surface waters, where they drift and grow before descending to seek a hard surface to which they can attach and begin a new colony. They also exhibit positive <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sonotaxis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sonotaxis (page does not exist)">sonotaxis</a>, moving towards sounds that emanate from the reef and away from open water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> High failure rates afflict many stages of this process, and even though millions of gametes are released by each colony, few new colonies form. The time from spawning to settling is usually two to three days, but can be up to two months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-26">[26]</a></sup> The larva grows into a polyp and eventually becomes a coral head by asexual budding and growth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asexual">Asexual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Asexual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="337" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg/170px-Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg/255px-Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orbicella_annularis_-_calices.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Basal plates (calices) of <i>Orbicella annularis</i> showing multiplication by budding (small central plate) and division (large double plate)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2046" data-file-width="2106" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg/170px-AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg/255px-AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg/340px-AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AuloporaDevonianSilicaShale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tabulate coral <i><a href="/wiki/Aulopora" title="Aulopora">Aulopora</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a>) showing initial budding</div></div></div>
<p>Within a coral head, the genetically identical polyps reproduce <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexually</a>, either by <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a> (gemmation) or by dividing, whether longitudinally or transversely.
</p><p>Budding involves splitting a smaller polyp from an adult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes99_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes99-23">[23]</a></sup> As the new polyp grows, it forms <a href="/wiki/File:Coral_polyp.jpg" title="File:Coral polyp.jpg">its body parts</a>. The distance between the new and adult polyps grows, and with it, the coenosarc (the common body of the colony). Budding can be intratentacular, from its oral discs, producing same-sized polyps within the ring of tentacles, or extratentacular, from its base, producing a smaller polyp.
</p><p>Division forms two polyps that each become as large as the original. Longitudinal division begins when a polyp broadens and then divides its coelenteron (body), effectively splitting along its length. The mouth divides and new tentacles form. The two polyps thus created then generate their missing body parts and exoskeleton. Transversal division occurs when polyps and the exoskeleton divide transversally into two parts. This means one has the basal disc (bottom) and the other has the oral disc (top); the new polyps must separately generate the missing pieces.
</p><p>Asexual reproduction offers the benefits of high reproductive rate, delaying senescence, and replacement of dead modules, as well as geographical distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colony_division">Colony division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Colony division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whole colonies can reproduce asexually, forming two colonies with the same genotype. The possible mechanisms include fission, bailout and fragmentation. Fission occurs in some corals, especially among the family <a href="/wiki/Fungiidae" title="Fungiidae">Fungiidae</a>, where the colony splits into two or more colonies during early developmental stages. Bailout occurs when a single polyp abandons the colony and settles on a different substrate to create a new colony. Fragmentation involves individuals broken from the colony during storms or other disruptions. The separated individuals can start new colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardDavy2009_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-SheppardDavy2009-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reefs">Reefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coral_reef_locations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="640" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg/380px-Coral_reef_locations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg/570px-Coral_reef_locations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/Coral_reef_locations.jpg 2x" width="380"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coral_reef_locations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Locations of coral reefs around the world</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reef</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef_fish" title="Coral reef fish">Coral reef fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_reefs" title="List of reefs">List of reefs</a></div>
<p>Many corals in the order <a href="/wiki/Scleractinia" title="Scleractinia">Scleractinia</a> are <a href="/wiki/Hermatypic_coral" title="Hermatypic coral">hermatypic</a>, meaning that they are involved in building reefs. Most such corals obtain some of their energy from <a href="/wiki/Zooxanthellae" title="Zooxanthellae">zooxanthellae</a> in the genus <i>Symbiodinium</i>. These are <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a> which require sunlight; reef-forming corals are therefore found mainly in shallow water. They secrete calcium carbonate to form hard skeletons that become the framework of the reef. However, not all reef-building corals in shallow water contain zooxanthellae, and some deep water species, living at depths to which light cannot penetrate, form reefs but do not harbour the symbionts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuhmacher_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuhmacher-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hertshoon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Hertshoon.jpg/220px-Hertshoon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Hertshoon.jpg/330px-Hertshoon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Hertshoon.jpg/440px-Hertshoon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hertshoon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Staghorn_coral" title="Staghorn coral">Staghorn coral</a> (<i>Acropora cervicornis</i>) is an important hermatypic coral from the Caribbean</div></div></div>
<p>There are various types of shallow-water coral reef, including fringing reefs, barrier reefs and atolls; most occur in tropical and subtropical seas. They are very slow-growing, adding perhaps one centimetre (0.4 in) in height each year. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> is thought to have been laid down about two million years ago. Over time, corals fragment and die, sand and rubble accumulates between the corals, and the shells of clams and other molluscs decay to form a gradually evolving calcium carbonate structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Coral reefs are extremely diverse marine <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> hosting over 4,000 species of fish, massive numbers of cnidarians, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and many other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalding_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spalding-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_history">Evolutionary history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Evolutionary history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RugosaOrdovician.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="469" data-file-width="474" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/RugosaOrdovician.jpg/220px-RugosaOrdovician.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/RugosaOrdovician.jpg/330px-RugosaOrdovician.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/RugosaOrdovician.jpg/440px-RugosaOrdovician.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RugosaOrdovician.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solitary rugose coral (<i>Grewingkia</i>) in three views; Ordovician, southeastern Indiana</div></div></div>
<p>Although corals first appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pratt_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pratt-32">[32]</a></sup> some <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=535">535</a> million years ago</span>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> are extremely rare until the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> period, 100 million years later, when <a href="/wiki/Rugosa" title="Rugosa">rugose</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabulate_coral" title="Tabulate coral">tabulate corals</a> became widespread. <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> corals often contained numerous endobiotic symbionts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VinnMotus2008_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-VinnMotus2008-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VinnMotus2012_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-VinnMotus2012-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tabulate corals occur in <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a> and calcareous <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a> of the Ordovician and <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> periods, and often form low cushions or branching masses of <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> alongside rugose corals. Their numbers began to decline during the middle of the Silurian period, and they became extinct at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> period, <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=250">250</a> million years ago</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rugose or horn corals became dominant by the middle of the Silurian period, and became extinct early in the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period. The rugose corals existed in solitary and colonial forms, and were also composed of calcite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scleractinia" title="Scleractinia">scleractinian</a> corals filled the niche vacated by the extinct rugose and tabulate species. Their fossils may be found in small numbers in rocks from the Triassic period, and became common in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and later periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Scleractinian skeletons are composed of a form of calcium carbonate known as <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ries_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ries-38">[38]</a></sup> Although they are geologically younger than the tabulate and rugose corals, the aragonite of their skeletons is less readily preserved, and their fossil record is accordingly less complete.
</p>

<p>At certain times in the geological past, corals were very abundant. Like modern corals, these ancestors built reefs, some of which ended as great structures in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rocks" title="Sedimentary rocks">sedimentary rocks</a>. Fossils of fellow reef-dwellers algae, sponges, and the remains of many <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">trilobites</a> appear along with coral fossils. This makes some corals useful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_fossil" title="Index fossil">index fossils</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Coral fossils are not restricted to reef remnants, and many solitary fossils may be found elsewhere, such as <i>Cyclocyathus</i>, which occurs in England's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gault_clay" title="Gault clay">Gault clay</a> formation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_with_coral_reefs" title="Environmental issues with coral reefs">Environmental issues with coral reefs</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats">Threats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Threats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reef0484.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="849" data-file-width="600" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Reef0484.jpg/170px-Reef0484.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Reef0484.jpg/255px-Reef0484.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Reef0484.jpg/340px-Reef0484.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reef0484.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A healthy coral reef has a striking level of biodiversity in many forms of marine life.</div></div></div>
<p>Coral reefs are under stress around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coral_reefs_around_the_world_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coral_reefs_around_the_world-42">[42]</a></sup> In particular, coral mining, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_runoff" title="Agricultural runoff">agricultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> (organic and inorganic), <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">blast fishing</a>, disease, and the digging of <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> and access into islands and bays are localized threats to coral ecosystems. Broader threats are sea temperature rise, sea level rise and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> changes from <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, all associated with <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cra10_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-cra10-43">[43]</a></sup> In 1998, 16% of the world's reefs died as a result of increased water temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately 10% of the world's coral reefs are dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleypas,_J.A._2006_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kleypas,_J.A._2006-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> About 60% of the world's reefs are at risk due to human-related activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The threat to reef health is particularly strong in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, where 80% of reefs are <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Over 50% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> may be destroyed by 2030; as a result, most nations protect them through environmental laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norlander_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norlander-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Caribbean and tropical Pacific, direct contact between ~40–70% of common seaweeds and coral causes bleaching and death to the coral via transfer of <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a>-soluble <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Seaweed and algae proliferate given adequate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> and limited grazing by <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Parrotfish" title="Parrotfish">parrotfish</a>.
</p><p>Water temperature changes of more than 1–2 °C (1.8–3.6 °F) or <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> changes can kill some species of coral. Under such environmental stresses, corals expel their <a href="/wiki/Symbiodinium" title="Symbiodinium">Symbiodinium</a>; without them coral tissues reveal the white of their skeletons, an event known as <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoegh_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoegh-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Submarine springs found along the coast of Mexico's <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> produce water with a naturally low pH (relatively high acidity) providing conditions similar to those expected to become widespread as the oceans absorb carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stephens_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stephens-53">[53]</a></sup> Surveys discovered multiple species of live coral that appeared to tolerate the acidity. The colonies were small and patchily distributed, and had not formed structurally complex reefs such as those that compose the nearby <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_Barrier_Reef_System" title="Mesoamerican Barrier Reef System">Mesoamerican Barrier Reef System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stephens_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stephens-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef_protection" title="Coral reef protection">Coral reef protection</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_Protected_Area" title="Marine Protected Area">Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosphere_reserves" title="Biosphere reserves">Biosphere reserves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_park" title="Marine park">marine parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_monument" title="National monument">national monuments</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_heritage" title="World heritage">world heritage</a> status, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fishery management</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat protection</a> can protect reefs from anthropogenic damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many governments now prohibit removal of coral from reefs, and inform coastal residents about reef protection and ecology. While local action such as habitat restoration and herbivore protection can reduce local damage, the longer-term threats of acidification, temperature change and sea-level rise remain a challenge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cra10_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-cra10-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>To eliminate destruction of corals in their indigenous regions, projects have been started to grow corals in non-tropical countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_humans">Relation to humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Relation to humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local economies near major coral reefs benefit from an abundance of fish and other marine creatures as a food source. Reefs also provide recreational <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snorkeling" title="Snorkeling">snorkeling</a> tourism. These activities can damage coral but international projects such as <a href="/wiki/Green_Fins" title="Green Fins">Green Fins</a> that encourage dive and snorkel centres to follow a Code of Conduct have been proven to mitigate these risks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewelry">Jewelry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Jewelry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:6-Strand_Necklace,_Navajo_(Native_American),_ca._1920s,_71.57.1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1406" data-file-width="554" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg/130px-6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg/195px-6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg/260px-6-Strand_Necklace%2C_Navajo_%28Native_American%29%2C_ca._1920s%2C_71.57.1.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:6-Strand_Necklace,_Navajo_(Native_American),_ca._1920s,_71.57.1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>6-strand necklace, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_people" title="Navajo people">Navajo</a> (Native American), ca. 1920s, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Precious_coral" title="Precious coral">Precious coral</a></div>
<p>Corals' many colors give it appeal for necklaces and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>. Intensely red coral is prized as a gemstone. Sometimes called fire coral, it is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Fire_coral" title="Fire coral">fire coral</a>. Red coral is very rare because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overharvesting" title="Overharvesting">overharvesting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Always considered a precious mineral, "the Chinese have long associated red coral with auspiciousness and longevity because of its color and its resemblance to deer antlers (so by association, virtue, long life, and high rank".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> It reached its height of popularity during the Manchu or Qing Dynasty (1644-1911) when it was almost exclusively reserved for the emperor's use either in the form of coral beads (often combined with pearls) for court jewelry or as decorative <a href="/wiki/Penjing" title="Penjing">Penjing</a> (decorative miniature mineral trees). Coral was known as <i>shanhu</i> in Chinese. The "early-modern 'coral network' [began in] the Mediterranean Sea [and found its way] to Qing China via the English <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> There were strict rules regarding its use in a code established by the <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a> in 1759.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2280" data-file-width="1878" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg/220px-ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg/330px-ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg/440px-ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ViennaDioscoridesCoral.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of coral in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juliana_Anicia_Codex" title="Juliana Anicia Codex">Juliana Anicia Codex</a>, a 6th-century copy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscorides" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/De_Materia_Medica" title="De Materia Medica">De Materia Medica</a></i>. The facing page states that coral can be used to treat ulcers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In medicine, chemical compounds from corals are used to treat cancer, AIDS and pain, and for other uses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Coral skeletons, e.g. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isididae" title="Isididae">Isididae</a></i>  are also used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_graft" title="Bone graft">bone grafting</a> in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiley_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-62">[62]</a></sup>
Coral Calx, known as Praval Bhasma in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, is widely used in traditional system of <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Indian medicine</a> as a supplement in the treatment of a variety of bone metabolic disorders associated with calcium deficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In classical times ingestion of pulverized coral, which consists mainly of the weak base <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, was recommended for calming stomach ulcers by <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioscorides" title="Dioscorides">Dioscorides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Syringoporid.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1144" data-file-width="1940" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Syringoporid.jpg/220px-Syringoporid.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Syringoporid.jpg/330px-Syringoporid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Syringoporid.jpg/440px-Syringoporid.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Syringoporid.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tabulate coral (a syringoporid); Boone limestone (Lower <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>) near Hiwasse, Arkansas, scale bar is 2.0 cm.</div></div></div>
<p>Coral reefs in places such as the East African coast are used as a source of <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pouwels2002_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pouwels2002-65">[65]</a></sup> Ancient (fossil) coral limestone, notably including the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Rag_Formation" title="Coral Rag Formation">Coral Rag Formation</a> of the hills around <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> (England), was once used as a building stone, and can be seen in some of the oldest buildings in that city including the Saxon tower of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Michael_at_the_Northgate" title="St Michael at the Northgate">St Michael at the Northgate</a>, St. George's Tower of <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Castle" title="Oxford Castle">Oxford Castle</a>, and the mediaeval walls of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_research">Climate research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Climate research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Annual growth bands in some corals, such as the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep sea</a> <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_coral" title="Bamboo coral">bamboo corals</a>  (<i>Isididae</i>), may be among the first signs of the effects of ocean acidification on marine life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The growth rings allow <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologists</a> to construct year-by-year chronologies, a form of <a href="/wiki/Incremental_dating" title="Incremental dating">incremental dating</a>, which underlie high-resolution records of past <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">climatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleoecology" title="Paleoecology">environmental</a> changes using <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemical</a> techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrag_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schrag-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain species form communities called <a href="/wiki/Microatoll" title="Microatoll">microatolls</a>, which are colonies whose top is dead and mostly above the water line, but whose perimeter is mostly submerged and alive. Average <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a> level limits their height. By analyzing the various growth morphologies, microatolls offer a low resolution record of sea level change. Fossilized microatolls can also be dated using <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a>. Such methods can help to reconstruct <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea levels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithers_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithers-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasing sea temperatures in tropical regions (~1 degree C) the last century have caused major coral bleaching, death, and therefore shrinking coral populations since although they are able to adapt and acclimate, it is uncertain if this evolutionary process will happen quickly enough to prevent major reduction of their numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoegh-Guldberg_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoegh-Guldberg-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though coral have large sexually-reproducing populations, their evolution can be slowed by abundant <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_et_al._71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_et_al.-71">[71]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gene_flow" title="Gene flow">Gene flow</a> is variable among coral species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_et_al._71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_et_al.-71">[71]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> of coral species gene flow cannot be counted on as a dependable source of adaptation as they are very stationary organisms. Also, coral longevity might factor into their adaptivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_et_al._71-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_et_al.-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_climate_change" title="Adaptation to climate change">adaptation to climate change</a> has been demonstrated in many cases. These are usually due to a shift in coral and zooxanthellae <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotypes</a>. These shifts in <a href="/wiki/Allele_frequency" title="Allele frequency">allele frequency</a> have progressed toward more tolerant types of zooxanthellae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parmesan_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parmesan-72">[72]</a></sup> Scientists found that a certain <a href="/wiki/Scleractinia" title="Scleractinia">scleractinian</a> zooxanthella is becoming more common where sea temperature is high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donner_et_al._74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_et_al.-74">[74]</a></sup> Symbionts able to tolerate warmer water seem to photosynthesise more slowly, implying an evolutionary trade-off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donner_et_al._74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donner_et_al.-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Gulf of Mexico, where sea temperatures are rising, cold-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Staghorn_coral" title="Staghorn coral">staghorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elkhorn_coral" title="Elkhorn coral">elkhorn coral</a> have shifted in location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parmesan_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-Parmesan-72">[72]</a></sup>
Not only have the symbionts and specific species been shown to shift, but there seems to be a certain growth rate favorable to selection. Slower-growing but more heat-tolerant corals have become more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskett_et_al._75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baskett_et_al.-75">[75]</a></sup> The changes in temperature and acclimation are complex. Some reefs in current shadows represent a refugium location that will help them adjust to the disparity in the environment even if eventually the temperatures may rise more quickly there than in other locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McClanahan_et_al._76-0"><a href="#cite_note-McClanahan_et_al.-76">[76]</a></sup> This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicariance" title="Vicariance">separation of populations</a> by climatic barriers causes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Realized_niche" title="Realized niche">realized niche</a> to shrink greatly in comparison to the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_niche" title="Fundamental niche">fundamental niche</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Geochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Corals are shallow, colonial organisms that integrate <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ<sup>18</sup>O</a> and trace elements into their skeletal <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorph</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>) crystalline structures, as they grow. Geochemistry anomalies within the crystalline structures of corals represent functions of temperature, salinity and oxygen isotopic composition. Such geochemical analysis can help with climate modeling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Strontium.2Fcalcium_ratio_anomaly"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Strontium/calcium_ratio_anomaly">Strontium/calcium ratio anomaly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Strontium/calcium ratio anomaly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Time can be attributed to coral geochemistry anomalies by correlating <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> minimums with <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature (SST)</a> maximums to data collected from <a class="external text" href="http://www.esrl.noaa.gov/psd/gcos_wgsp/Timeseries/Nino34/" rel="nofollow">NINO 3.4 SSTA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ren,_L._2002_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ren,_L._2002-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_isotope_anomaly">Oxygen isotope anomaly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Oxygen isotope anomaly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The comparison of coral strontium/calcium minimums with sea surface temperature maximums, data recorded from <a class="external text" href="http://www.esrl.noaa.gov/psd/gcos_wgsp/Timeseries/Nino34/" rel="nofollow">NINO 3.4 SSTA</a>, time can be correlated to coral strontium/calcium and <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ<sup>18</sup>O</a> variations. To confirm accuracy of the annual relationship between Sr/Ca and <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ<sup>18</sup>O</a> variations, a perceptible association to annual coral growth rings confirms the age conversion. <a href="/wiki/Geochronology" title="Geochronology">Geochronology</a> is established by the blending of Sr/Ca data, growth rings, and <a href="/wiki/Stable_isotope_ratio" title="Stable isotope ratio">stable isotope</a> data. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Nino-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Nino-Southern Oscillation">El Nino-Southern Oscillation (ENSO)</a> is directly related to climate fluctuations that influence coral <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ<sup>18</sup>O</a> ratio from local salinity variations associated with the position of the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_convergence_zone" title="South Pacific convergence zone">South Pacific convergence zone (SPCZ)</a> and can be used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">ENSO</a> modeling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ren,_L._2002_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ren,_L._2002-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_surface_temperature_and_sea_surface_salinity">Sea surface temperature and sea surface salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Sea surface temperature and sea surface salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="2876" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg/220px-Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg/330px-Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg/440px-Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Global_Sea_Surface_Temperature_-_GPN-2003-00032.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global sea surface temperature (SST)</div></div></div>
<p>The global moisture budget is primarily being influenced by tropical sea surface temperatures from the position of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> has a unique meteorological feature positioned in the southwestern Pacific Basin called the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_convergence_zone" title="South Pacific convergence zone">South Pacific Convergence Zone (SPCZ)</a>, which contains a perennial position within the Southern Hemisphere. During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">ENSO</a> warm periods, the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_convergence_zone" title="South Pacific convergence zone">SPCZ</a> reverses orientation extending from the equator down south through <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> and towards the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynesian_islands" title="Polynesian islands">Polynesian Islands</a>; and due east towards <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> affecting geochemistry of corals in tropical regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>Geochemical analysis of skeletal coral can be linked to sea surface salinity (SSS) and <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> (SST), from <a class="external text" href="http://www.esrl.noaa.gov/psd/gcos_wgsp/Timeseries/Nino34/" rel="nofollow">El Nino 3.4 SSTA</a> data, of tropical oceans to seawater <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ<sup>18</sup>O</a> ratio anomalies from corals. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">ENSO</a> phenomenon can be related to variations in sea surface salinity (SSS) and <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature (SST)</a> that can help model tropical climate activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukas,_R._1991_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lukas,_R._1991-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_climate_research_on_current_species">Limited climate research on current species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Limited climate research on current species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Porites_lutea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Porites_lutea.jpg/220px-Porites_lutea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Porites_lutea.jpg/330px-Porites_lutea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Porites_lutea.jpg/440px-Porites_lutea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Porites_lutea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Porites lutea</i></div></div></div>
<p>Climate research on live coral species is limited to a few studied species. Studying <i><a href="/wiki/Porites" title="Porites">Porites</a></i> coral provides a stable foundation for geochemical interpretations that is much simpler to physically extract data in comparison to <i><a href="/wiki/Platygyra" title="Platygyra">Platygyra</a></i> species where the complexity of <i><a href="/wiki/Platygyra" title="Platygyra">Platygyra</a></i> species skeletal structure creates difficulty when physically sampled, which happens to be one of the only multidecadal living coral records used for coral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a> modeling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukas,_R._1991_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lukas,_R._1991-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaria">Aquaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Aquaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reef_aquarium" title="Reef aquarium">Reef aquarium</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1108" data-file-width="1548" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg/220px-Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg/330px-Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg/440px-Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zoanthus-dragon-eye.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This dragon-eye zoanthid is a popular source of color in reef tanks</div></div></div>
<p>The saltwater fishkeeping hobby has expanded, over recent years, to include <a href="/wiki/Reef_aquarium" title="Reef aquarium">reef tanks</a>, fish tanks that include large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Live_rock" title="Live rock">live rock</a> on which coral is allowed to grow and spread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> These tanks are either kept in a natural-like state, with algae (sometimes in the form of an <a href="/wiki/Algae_scrubber" title="Algae scrubber">algae scrubber</a>) and a deep sand bed providing filtration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> or as "show tanks", with the rock kept largely bare of the algae and <a href="/wiki/Microfauna" title="Microfauna">microfauna</a> that would normally populate it,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> in order to appear neat and clean.
</p><p>The most popular kind of coral kept is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_coral" title="Soft coral">soft coral</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoanthid" title="Zoanthid">zoanthids</a> and mushroom corals, which are especially easy to grow and propagate in a wide variety of conditions, because they originate in enclosed parts of reefs where water conditions vary and lighting may be less reliable and direct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> More serious fishkeepers may keep small polyp <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stony_coral" title="Stony coral">stony coral</a>, which is from open, brightly lit reef conditions and therefore much more demanding, while large polyp stony coral is a sort of compromise between the two.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquaculture">Aquaculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Aquaculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_coral" title="Aquaculture of coral">Aquaculture of coral</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_coral" title="Aquaculture of coral">Coral aquaculture</a>, also known as <i>coral farming</i> or <i>coral gardening</i>, is the cultivation of corals for commercial purposes or coral reef restoration. Aquaculture is showing promise as a potentially effective tool for restoring <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>, which have been declining around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horoszowski-Fridman_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horoszowski-Fridman-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pomeroy_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pomeroy-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rinkevich_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rinkevich-88">[88]</a></sup> The process bypasses the early growth stages of corals when they are most at risk of dying. Coral fragments known as "seeds" are grown in nurseries then replanted on the reef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferse_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferse-89">[89]</a></sup> Coral is farmed by coral farmers who live locally to the reefs and farm for reef <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> or for income. It is also farmed by scientists for research, by businesses for the supply of the live and ornamental coral trade and by private <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquarium</a> hobbyists.
</p>
