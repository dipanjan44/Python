<p><b>Zirconium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Zr</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 40. The name <i>zirconium</i> is taken from the name of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>, the most important source of zirconium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is a lustrous, grey-white, strong <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> that closely resembles <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a> and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>. Zirconium is mainly used as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opacifier" title="Opacifier">opacifier</a>, although small amounts are used as an alloying agent for its strong resistance to corrosion. Zirconium forms a variety of <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic_compounds" title="Organometallic compounds">organometallic compounds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_dioxide" title="Zirconium dioxide">zirconium dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconocene_dichloride" title="Zirconocene dichloride">zirconocene dichloride</a>, respectively. Five <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> occur naturally, three of which are stable. Zirconium compounds have no known biological role.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zirconium_rod.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="665" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Zirconium_rod.jpg/90px-Zirconium_rod.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Zirconium_rod.jpg/135px-Zirconium_rod.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Zirconium_rod.jpg/180px-Zirconium_rod.jpg 2x" width="90"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zirconium_rod.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zirconium rod</div></div></div>
<p>Zirconium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luster_(mineralogy)" title="Luster (mineralogy)">lustrous</a>, greyish-white, soft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a> metal that is solid at room temperature, though it is hard and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> at lesser purities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-7">[7]</a></sup> In powder form, zirconium is highly flammable, but the solid form is much less prone to ignition. Zirconium is highly resistant to corrosion by alkalis, acids, salt water and other agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> However, it will dissolve in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, especially when <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nostrand_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nostrand-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">Alloys</a> with <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> are <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a> at less than 35 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of zirconium is 1855 °C (3371 °F), and the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> is 4371 °C (7900 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> Zirconium has an <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of 1.33 on the Pauling scale. Of the elements within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-block" title="D-block">d-block</a> with known electronegativities, zirconium has the fifth lowest electronegativity after <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>At room temperature zirconium exhibits a hexagonally close-packed crystal structure, α-Zr, which changes to β-Zr, a body-centered cubic crystal structure, at 863 °C. Zirconium exists in the β-phase until the melting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_zirconium" title="Isotopes of zirconium">Isotopes of zirconium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring zirconium is composed of five isotopes. <sup>90</sup>Zr, <sup>91</sup>Zr, <sup>92</sup>Zr and <sup>94</sup>Zr are stable, although <sup>94</sup>Zr is predicted to undergo <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a> (not observed experimentally) with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of more than 1.10×10<sup>17</sup> years. <sup>96</sup>Zr has a half-life of 2.4×10<sup>19</sup> years, and is the longest-lived radioisotope of zirconium. Of these natural isotopes, <sup>90</sup>Zr is the most common, making up 51.45% of all zirconium. <sup>96</sup>Zr is the least common, comprising only 2.80% of zirconium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Twenty-eight artificial isotopes of zirconium have been synthesized, ranging in atomic mass from 78 to 110. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconium-93" title="Zirconium-93"><sup>93</sup>Zr</a> is the longest-lived artificial isotope, with a half-life of 1.53×10<sup>6</sup> years. <sup>110</sup>Zr, the heaviest isotope of zirconium, is the most radioactive, with an estimated half-life of 30 milliseconds. Radioactive isotopes at or above mass number 93 decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">electron emission</a>, whereas those at or below 89 decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">positron emission</a>. The only exception is <sup>88</sup>Zr, which decays by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Five isotopes of zirconium also exist as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">metastable isomers</a>: <sup>83m</sup>Zr, <sup>85m</sup>Zr, <sup>89m</sup>Zr, <sup>90m1</sup>Zr, <sup>90m2</sup>Zr and <sup>91m</sup>Zr. Of these, <sup>90m2</sup>Zr has the shortest half-life at 131 nanoseconds. <sup>89m</sup>Zr is the longest lived with a half-life of 4.161 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Zirconium_minerals" title="Category:Zirconium minerals">Category:Zirconium minerals</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="750" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg/240px-Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg/360px-Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg/480px-Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zirconium_mineral_concentrates_-_world_production_trend.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World production trend of zirconium mineral concentrates</div></div></div>
<p>Zirconium has a concentration of about 130 mg/kg within the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> and about 0.026 μg/L in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_water" title="Sea water">sea water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argonne_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-argonne-13">[13]</a></sup> It is not found in nature as a <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">native metal</a>, reflecting its intrinsic instability with respect to water. The principal commercial source of zirconium is <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> (ZrSiO<sub>4</sub>), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicate_mineral" title="Silicate mineral">silicate mineral</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-6">[6]</a></sup> which is found primarily in Australia, Brazil, India, Russia, South Africa and the United States, as well as in smaller deposits around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-7">[7]</a></sup> As of 2013, two-thirds of zircon mining occurs in Australia and South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb13_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbb13-14">[14]</a></sup> Zircon resources exceed 60 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> worldwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2008-15">[15]</a></sup> and annual worldwide zirconium production is approximately 900,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-argonne_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-argonne-13">[13]</a></sup> Zirconium also occurs in more than 140 other minerals, including the commercially useful ores <a href="/wiki/Baddeleyite" title="Baddeleyite">baddeleyite</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosnarite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosnarite (page does not exist)">kosnarite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconium is relatively abundant in <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification#Class_S" title="Stellar classification">S-type stars</a>, and it has been detected in the sun and in meteorites. Lunar rock samples brought back from several <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> missions to the moon have a high zirconium oxide content relative to terrestrial rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2005zirconium.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/2005zirconium.PNG/220px-2005zirconium.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/2005zirconium.PNG/330px-2005zirconium.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/2005zirconium.PNG/440px-2005zirconium.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2005zirconium.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zirconium output in 2005</div></div></div>
<p>Zirconium is a by-product of the mining and processing of the <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> minerals <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> From 2003 to 2007, while prices for the mineral zircon steadily increased from $360 to $840 per tonne, the price for unwrought zirconium metal decreased from $39,900 to $22,700 per ton. Zirconium metal is much higher priced than zircon because the reduction processes are expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2008_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2008-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Collected from coastal waters, zircon-bearing sand is purified by <a href="/wiki/Spiral_separator#Wet_Spiral_Separators" title="Spiral separator">spiral concentrators</a> to remove lighter materials, which are then returned to the water because they are natural components of beach sand. Using <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_separation" title="Magnetic separation">magnetic separation</a>, the titanium ores <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> are removed.
</p><p>Most zircon is used directly in commercial applications, but a small percentage is converted to the metal. Most Zr metal is produced by the reduction of the <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_chloride" title="Zirconium(IV) chloride">zirconium(IV) chloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> metal in the <a href="/wiki/Kroll_process" title="Kroll process">Kroll process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> The resulting metal is <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintered</a> until sufficiently ductile for metalworking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_of_zirconium_and_hafnium">Separation of zirconium and hafnium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Separation of zirconium and hafnium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commercial zirconium metal typically contains 1–3% of <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-18">[18]</a></sup> which is usually not problematic because the chemical properties of hafnium and zirconium are very similar. Their neutron-absorbing properties differ strongly, however, necessitating the separation of hafnium from zirconium for nuclear reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stwertka_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stwertka-19">[19]</a></sup> Several separation schemes are in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-liquid_extraction" title="Liquid-liquid extraction">liquid-liquid extraction</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">thiocyanate</a>-oxide derivatives exploits the fact that the hafnium derivative is slightly more soluble in <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isobutyl_ketone" title="Methyl isobutyl ketone">methyl isobutyl ketone</a> than in water. This method is used mainly in United States.
</p><p>Zr and Hf can also be separated by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a> of potassium hexafluorozirconate (K<sub>2</sub>ZrF<sub>6</sub>), which is less soluble in water than the analogous hafnium derivative.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">Fractional distillation</a> of the tetrachlorides, also called <a href="/wiki/Extractive_distillation" title="Extractive distillation">extractive distillation</a>, is used primarily in Europe.
</p><p>The product of a quadruple VAM (vacuum arc melting) process, combined with hot extruding and different rolling applications is cured using high-pressure, high-temperature gas <a href="/wiki/Autoclave" title="Autoclave">autoclaving</a>. This produces reactor-grade zirconium that is about 10 times more expensive than the hafnium-contaminated commercial grade.
</p><p>Hafnium must be removed from zirconium for nuclear applications because hafnium has a neutron absorption cross-section 600 times greater than zirconium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-20">[20]</a></sup> The separated hafnium can be used for reactor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_rods" title="Control rods">control rods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: the categories <a href="/wiki/Category:Zirconium_compounds" title="Category:Zirconium compounds">Zirconium compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Zirconium_minerals" title="Category:Zirconium minerals">Zirconium minerals</a>.</div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a>, zirconium forms a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In general, these compounds are colourless diamagnetic solids wherein zirconium has the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> +4. Far fewer Zr(III) compounds are known, and Zr(II) is very rare.
</p>
<h3><span id="Oxides.2C_nitrides.2C_and_carbides"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides,_nitrides,_and_carbides">Oxides, nitrides, and carbides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oxides, nitrides, and carbides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common oxide is <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_dioxide" title="Zirconium dioxide">zirconium dioxide</a>, ZrO<sub>2</sub>, also known as <i>zirconia</i>. This clear to white-coloured solid has exceptional <a href="/wiki/Fracture_toughness" title="Fracture toughness">fracture toughness</a> and chemical resistance, especially in its <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic</a> form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azomzirc_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-azomzirc-23">[23]</a></sup> These properties make zirconia useful as a thermal barrier coating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> although it is also a common <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> substitute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-azomzirc_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-azomzirc-23">[23]</a></sup> Zirconium monoxide, ZrO, is also known and <a href="/wiki/S-type_star" title="S-type star">S-type stars</a> are recognised by detection of its emission lines in the visual spectrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zirconium_tungstate" title="Zirconium tungstate">Zirconium tungstate</a> has the unusual property of shrinking in all dimensions when heated, whereas most other substances expand when heated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zirconyl_chloride" title="Zirconyl chloride">Zirconyl chloride</a> is a rare water-soluble zirconium complex with the relatively complicated formula [Zr<sub>4</sub>(OH)<sub>12</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>16</sub>]Cl<sub>8</sub>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zirconium_carbide" title="Zirconium carbide">Zirconium carbide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_nitride" title="Zirconium nitride">zirconium nitride</a> are refractory solids. The carbide is used for drilling tools and cutting edges. Zirconium hydride phases are also known.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lead_zirconate_titanate" title="Lead zirconate titanate">Lead zirconate titanate</a> (PZT) is the most commonly used piezoelectric material, with applications such as ultrasonic transducers, hydrophones, common rail injectors, piezoelectric transformers and micro-actuators.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides_and_pseudohalides">Halides and pseudohalides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Halides and pseudohalides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All four common halides are known, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_fluoride" title="Zirconium(IV) fluoride">ZrF<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_chloride" title="Zirconium(IV) chloride">ZrCl<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_bromide" title="Zirconium(IV) bromide">ZrBr<sub>4</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_iodide" title="Zirconium(IV) iodide">ZrI<sub>4</sub></a>. All have polymeric structures and are far less volatile than the corresponding monomeric titanium tetrahalides. All tend to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrolyse" title="Hydrolyse">hydrolyse</a> to give the so-called oxyhalides and dioxides.
</p><p>The corresponding tetra<a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a> are also known. Unlike the halides, the alkoxides dissolve in nonpolar solvents. Dihydrogen hexafluorozirconate is used in the metal finishing industry as an etching agent to promote paint adhesion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_derivatives">Organic derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organic derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="955" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/190px-Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/285px-Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/380px-Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zirconocene-dichloride-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zirconocene dichloride, a representative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organozirconium_compound" title="Organozirconium compound">organozirconium compound</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Organozirconium_chemistry" title="Organozirconium chemistry">Organozirconium chemistry</a> is the study of compounds containing a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-zirconium bond. The first such compound was zirconocene dibromide ((C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>ZrBr<sub>2</sub>), reported in 1952 by Birmingham and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Wilkinson" title="Geoffrey Wilkinson">Wilkinson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Schwartz%27s_reagent" title="Schwartz's reagent">Schwartz's reagent</a>, prepared in 1970 by P. C. Wailes and H. Weigold,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Metallocene" title="Metallocene">metallocene</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> for transformations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hart_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hart-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconium is also a component of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalyst" title="Ziegler-Natta catalyst">Ziegler-Natta catalysts</a>, used to produce <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>. This application exploits the ability of zirconium to reversibly form bonds to carbon. Most complexes of Zr(II) are derivatives of zirconocene, one example being (C<sub>5</sub>Me<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Zr(CO)<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The zirconium-containing mineral zircon and related minerals (<a href="/wiki/Jargoon" title="Jargoon">jargoon</a>, hyacinth, <a href="/wiki/Jacinth" title="Jacinth">jacinth</a>, ligure) were mentioned in biblical writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stwertka_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stwertka-19">[19]</a></sup> The mineral was not known to contain a new element until 1789,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup> when <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Klaproth</a> analyzed a jargoon from the island of Ceylon (now Sri Lanka). He named the new element Zirkonerde (zirconia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> attempted to isolate this new element in 1808 through electrolysis, but failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-6">[6]</a></sup> Zirconium metal was first obtained in an impure form in 1824 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Berzelius</a> by heating a mixture of potassium and potassium zirconium fluoride in an iron tube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Crystal_bar_process" title="Crystal bar process">crystal bar process</a></i> (also known as the <i>Iodide Process</i>), discovered by <a href="/wiki/Anton_Eduard_van_Arkel" title="Anton Eduard van Arkel">Anton Eduard van Arkel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hendrik_de_Boer" title="Jan Hendrik de Boer">Jan Hendrik de Boer</a> in 1925, was the first industrial process for the commercial production of metallic zirconium. It involves the formation and subsequent thermal decomposition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconium_tetraiodide" title="Zirconium tetraiodide">zirconium tetraiodide</a>, and was superseded in 1945 by the much cheaper <a href="/wiki/Kroll_process" title="Kroll process">Kroll process</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/William_Justin_Kroll" title="William Justin Kroll">William Justin Kroll</a>, in which zirconium tetrachloride is reduced by magnesium:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metal1998_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-metal1998-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ZrCl<sub>4</sub> + 2 Mg → Zr + 2 MgCl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Approximately 900,000 tonnes of zirconium ores were mined in 1995, mostly as zircon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds_2">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most zircon is used directly in high-temperature applications. This material is refractory, hard, and resistant to chemical attack. Because of these properties, zircon finds many applications, few of which are highly publicized. Its main use is as an opacifier, conferring a white, opaque appearance to ceramic materials. Because of its chemical resistance, zircon is also used in aggressive environments, such as moulds for molten metals.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zirconium_dioxide" title="Zirconium dioxide">Zirconium dioxide</a> (ZrO<sub>2</sub>) is used in laboratory crucibles, in metallurgical furnaces, and as a refractory material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> Because it is mechanically strong and flexible, it can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sintered" title="Sintered">sintered</a> into <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_knife" title="Ceramic knife">ceramic knives</a> and other blades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kyo_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-kyo-32">[32]</a></sup> Zircon (ZrSiO<sub>4</sub>) and the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic zirconia</a> (ZrO<sub>2</sub>) are cut into gemstones for use in jewelry.
</p><p>Zirconia is a component in some <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasives</a>, such as grinding wheels and <a href="/wiki/Sandpaper" title="Sandpaper">sandpaper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal">Metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small fraction of the zircon is converted to the metal, which finds various niche applications. Because of zirconium's excellent resistance to corrosion, it is often used as an alloying agent in materials that are exposed to aggressive environments, such as surgical appliances, light filaments, and watch cases. The high reactivity of zirconium with oxygen at high temperatures is exploited in some specialised applications such as explosive primers and as <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">getters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a>. The same property is (probably) the purpose of including Zr nano-particles as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> material in explosive weapons such as the <a href="/wiki/BLU-97/B_Combined_Effects_Bomb" title="BLU-97/B Combined Effects Bomb">BLU-97/B Combined Effects Bomb</a>. Burning zirconium was used as a light source in some <a href="/wiki/Flash_(photography)#Flashbulbs" title="Flash (photography)">photographic flashbulbs</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_applications">Nuclear applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nuclear applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Zirconium#The_section_asserts_a_number_of_controversial_facts_that_should_be_readily_documented_if_they_are_true._Otherwise_-_?" title="Talk:Zirconium">talk page</a>. <p>Cladding for nuclear reactor fuels consumes about 1% of the zirconium supply,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-18">[18]</a></sup> mainly in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zircaloy" title="Zircaloy">zircaloys</a>. The desired properties of these alloys are a low neutron-capture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_cross-section" title="Neutron cross-section">cross-section</a> and resistance to corrosion under normal service conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madehow_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-madehow-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC2008_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-CRC2008-8">[8]</a></sup> Efficient methods for removing the hafnium impurities were developed to serve this purpose.
</p><p>One disadvantage of zirconium alloys is that zirconium reacts with water at high temperatures, producing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas and accelerated degradation of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel#Common_physical_forms_of_nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">fuel rod cladding</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Zr + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O → ZrO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> reaction is very slow below 100 °C, but at temperature above 900 °C the reaction is rapid. Most metals undergo similar reactions. The redox reaction is relevant to the instability of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">fuel assemblies</a> at high temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This reaction was responsible for a small hydrogen explosion first observed inside the reactor building of <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">Three Mile Island</a> nuclear power plant in 1979, but at that time, the containment building was not damaged. The same reaction occurred in the reactors 1, 2 and 3 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_I_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Fukushima I Nuclear Power Plant">Fukushima I Nuclear Power Plant</a> (Japan) after the reactor cooling was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">earthquake and tsunami</a> disaster of March 11, 2011 leading to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_I_nuclear_accidents" title="Fukushima I nuclear accidents">Fukushima I nuclear accidents</a>. After venting the hydrogen in the maintenance hall of those three reactors, the mixture of hydrogen with atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> exploded, severely damaging the installations and at least one of the containment buildings. To avoid explosion, the direct venting of hydrogen to the open atmosphere would have been a preferred design option. Now, to prevent the risk of explosion in many <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactor</a> (PWR) containment buildings, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Passive_autocatalytic_recombiner" title="Passive autocatalytic recombiner">recombiner</a> is installed that converts hydrogen and oxygen into water at room temperature before the hazard arises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recombiner_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-recombiner-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_and_aeronautic_industries">Space and aeronautic industries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Space and aeronautic industries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Materials fabricated from zirconium metal and ZrO<sub>2</sub> are used in space vehicles where resistance to heat is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stwertka_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stwertka-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>High temperature parts such as combustors, blades, and vanes in <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a> and stationary <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a> are increasingly being protected by thin <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> layers, usually composed of a mixture of zirconia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" title="Yttrium oxide">yttria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positron_emission_tomography_cameras">Positron emission tomography cameras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Positron emission tomography cameras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The isotope <sup>89</sup>Zr has been applied to the tracking and quantification of molecular antibodies with <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> (PET) cameras (a method called "immuno-PET"). Immuno-PET has reached a maturity of technical development and is now entering the phase of wide-scale clinical applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Until recently, radiolabeling with <sup>89</sup>Zr was a complicated procedure requiring multiple steps. In 2001–2003 an improved multistep procedure was developed using a succinylated derivative of <a href="/wiki/Deferoxamine" title="Deferoxamine">desferrioxamine B</a> (N-sucDf) as a bifunctional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelate" title="Chelate">chelate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and a better way of binding <sup>89</sup>Zr to mAbs was reported in 2009. The new method is fast, consists of only two steps, and uses two widely available ingredients: <sup>89</sup>Zr and the appropriate chelate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomedical_applications">Biomedical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biomedical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zirconium-bearing compounds are used in many biomedical applications, including dental implants and <a href="/wiki/Crown_(dentistry)" title="Crown (dentistry)">
crowns</a>, knee and hip replacements, middle-ear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ossicular" title="Ossicular">ossicular</a> chain reconstruction, and other restorative and <a href="/wiki/Prosthesis" title="Prosthesis">prosthetic</a> devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconium binds <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, a property that has been utilized extensively to the benefit of patients with <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-41">[41]</a></sup> For example, zirconium is a primary component of the <a href="/wiki/Sorbent" title="Sorbent">sorbent</a> column dependent dialysate regeneration and recirculation system known as the REDY system, which was first introduced in 1973. More than 2,000,000 <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> treatments have been performed using the sorbent column in the REDY system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Although the REDY system was superseded in the 1990s by less expensive alternatives, new sorbent-based dialysis systems are being evaluated and approved by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA). Renal Solutions developed the DIALISORB technology, a portable, low water dialysis system. Also, developmental versions of a Wearable Artificial Kidney have incorporated sorbent-based technologies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sodium_zirconium_cyclosilicate" title="Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate">Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate</a> is under investigation for oral therapy in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Hyperkalemia" title="Hyperkalemia">hyperkalemia</a>. It is a highly selective oral sorbent designed specifically to trap <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> ions in preference to other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> throughout the gastrointestinal tract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>A mixture of monomeric and polymeric Zr<sup>4+</sup> and Al<sup>3+</sup> complexes with <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_zirconium_tetrachlorohydrex_gly" title="Aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly">Aluminium zirconium tetrachlorohydrex gly</a> or AZG, is used in a preparation as an antiperspirant in many deodorant products. It is selected for its ability to obstruct pores in the skin and prevent sweat from leaving the body.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defunct_applications">Defunct applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Defunct applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zirconium carbonate (3ZrO<sub>2</sub>·CO<sub>2</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O) was used in lotions to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_ivy" title="Poison ivy">poison ivy</a> but was discontinued because it occasionally caused skin reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Although zirconium has no known biological role, the human body contains, on average, 250 milligrams of zirconium, and daily intake is approximately 4.15 milligrams (3.5 milligrams from food and 0.65 milligrams from water), depending on dietary habits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Zirconium is widely distributed in nature and is found in all biological systems, for example: 2.86 μg/g in whole wheat, 3.09 μg/g in brown rice, 0.55 μg/g in <a href="/wiki/Spinach" title="Spinach">spinach</a>, 1.23 μg/g in eggs, and 0.86 μg/g in ground beef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Further, zirconium is commonly used in commercial products (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Deodorant" title="Deodorant">deodorant</a> sticks, aerosol <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiperspirants" title="Antiperspirants">antiperspirants</a>) and also in water purification (e.g. control of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> pollution, bacteria- and pyrogen-contaminated water).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Short-term exposure to zirconium powder can cause irritation, but only contact with the eyes requires medical attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Persistent exposure to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconium_tetrachloride" title="Zirconium tetrachloride">zirconium tetrachloride</a> results in increased mortality in rats and guinea pigs and a decrease of blood <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cell</a>s in dogs. However, in a study of 20 rats given a standard diet containing ~4% zirconium oxide, there were no adverse effects on growth rate, blood and urine parameters, or mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for zirconium exposure is 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) is 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday and a short term limit of 10 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. At levels of 25 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, zirconium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> However, zirconium is not considered an industrial health hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-41">[41]</a></sup> Furthermore, reports of zirconium-related adverse reactions are rare and, in general, rigorous cause-and-effect relationships have not been established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-41">[41]</a></sup> No evidence has been validated that zirconium is carcinogenic or genotoxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the numerous radioactive isotopes of zirconium, <sup>93</sup>Zr is among the most common. It is released as a product of <sup>235</sup>U, mainly in nuclear plants and during nuclear weapons tests in the 1950s and 1960s. It has a very long half-life (1.53 million years), its decay emits only low energy radiations, and it is not considered as highly hazardous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
