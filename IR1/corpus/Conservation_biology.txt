<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Conservation biology</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Conservation biology</b> is the management of nature and of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> with the aim of protecting <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitats" title="Habitats">habitats</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> from excessive rates of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> and the erosion of biotic interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsBiol80_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConsBiol80-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is an interdisciplinary subject drawing on natural and social sciences, and the practice of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soule86_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soule86-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groom06_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Groom06-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>478</span></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" title="Conservation ethic">conservation ethic</a> is based on the findings of conservation biology.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term conservation biology and its conception as a new field originated with the convening of "The First International Conference on Research in Conservation Biology" held at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Diego" title="University of California, San Diego">University of California, San Diego</a> in La Jolla, California in 1978 led by American biologists Bruce A. Wilcox and <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Soul%C3%A9" title="Michael E. Soulé">Michael E. Soulé</a> with a group of leading university and zoo researchers and conservationists including <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Benirschke" title="Kurt Benirschke">Kurt Benirschke</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/Otto_Frankel" title="Otto Frankel">Otto Frankel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lovejoy" title="Thomas Lovejoy">Thomas Lovejoy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jared_Diamond" title="Jared Diamond">Jared Diamond</a>. The meeting was prompted by the concern over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_deforestation" title="Tropical deforestation">tropical deforestation</a>, disappearing species, eroding <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> within species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The conference and proceedings that resulted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConsBiol80_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ConsBiol80-2">[2]</a></sup> sought to initiate the bridging of a gap between theory in ecology and evolutionary genetics on the one hand and conservation policy and practice on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Conservation biology and the concept of biological diversity (<a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>) emerged together, helping crystallize the modern era of conservation science and <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">policy</a>.  The inherent multidisciplinary basis for conservation biology has led to new subdisciplines including conservation social science, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_behavior" title="Conservation behavior">conservation behavior</a> and conservation physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke_et_al_2013_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup> It stimulated further development of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_genetics" title="Conservation genetics">conservation genetics</a> which <a href="/wiki/Otto_Frankel" title="Otto Frankel">Otto Frankel</a> had originated first but is now often considered a subdiscipline as well.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rapid decline of established biological systems around the world means that conservation biology is often referred to as a "Discipline with a deadline".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Conservation biology is tied closely to <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> in researching the <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology" title="Population ecology">population ecology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/migration" title="wikt:migration">migration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">demographics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effective_population_size" title="Effective population size">effective population size</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_depression" title="Inbreeding depression">inbreeding depression</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Minimum_viable_population" title="Minimum viable population">minimum population viability</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-13">[13]</a></sup> Conservation biology is concerned with phenomena that affect the maintenance, loss, and restoration of biodiversity and the science of sustaining evolutionary processes that engender <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>, <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, and ecosystem diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groom06_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Groom06-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-13">[13]</a></sup> The concern stems from estimates suggesting that up to 50% of all species on the planet will disappear within the next 50 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koh_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koh-14">[14]</a></sup> which has contributed to poverty, starvation, and will reset the course of evolution on this planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Conservation biologists research and educate on the trends and process of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a>, species <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinctions</a>, and the negative effect these are having on our capabilities to <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustain</a> the well-being of human society. Conservation biologists work in the field and office, in government, universities, non-profit organizations and industry. The topics of their research are diverse, because this is an interdisciplinary network with professional alliances in the biological as well as social sciences. Those dedicated to the cause and profession <a href="/wiki/Advocate" title="Advocate">advocate</a> for a global response to <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">the current biodiversity crisis</a> based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morals" title="Morals">morals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>, and scientific <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>. Organizations and citizens are responding to the biodiversity crisis through conservation action plans that direct research, monitoring, and education programs that engage concerns at local through global scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soule86_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Soule86-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groom06_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Groom06-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">The conservation of natural resources is the fundamental problem. Unless we solve that problem, it will avail us little to solve all others.</div>
<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">– Theodore Roosevelt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_resource_conservation">Natural resource conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Natural resource conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conscious efforts to conserve and protect <i>global</i> biodiversity are a recent phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Natural resource conservation, however, has a history that extends prior to the age of conservation. Resource ethics grew out of necessity through direct relations with nature. Regulation or communal restraint became necessary to prevent selfish motives from taking more than could be locally sustained, therefore compromising the long-term supply for the rest of the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup> This social dilemma with respect to natural resource management is often called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_Commons" title="Tragedy of the Commons">Tragedy of the Commons</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>From this principle, conservation biologists can trace communal resource based ethics throughout cultures as a solution to communal resource conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, the Alaskan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tlingit_people" title="Tlingit people">Tlingit</a> peoples and the <a href="/wiki/Haida_people" title="Haida people">Haida</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> had resource boundaries, rules, and restrictions among clans with respect to the fishing of <a href="/wiki/Sockeye_salmon" title="Sockeye salmon">sockeye salmon</a>. These rules were guided by clan elders who knew lifelong details of each river and stream they managed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mason_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-21">[21]</a></sup> There are numerous examples in history where cultures have followed rules, rituals, and organized practice with respect to communal natural resource management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mauryan emperor <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> around 250 B.C. issued edicts restricting the slaughter of animals and certain kinds of birds, as well as opened veterinary clinics.
</p><p>Conservation ethics are also found in early religious and philosophical writings. There are examples in the <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">Tao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic" title="Islamic">Islamic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> traditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Primack_2004_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Primack_2004-24">[24]</a></sup> In Greek philosophy, Plato lamented about pasture <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>: "What is left now is, so to say, the skeleton of a body wasted by disease; the rich, soft soil has been carried off and only the bare framework of the district left."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the bible, through Moses, God commanded to let the land rest from cultivation every seventh year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Before the 18th century, however, much of European culture considered it a <a href="/wiki/Earth_religion" title="Earth religion">pagan view</a> to admire nature. Wilderness was denigrated while agricultural development was praised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans97_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans97-27">[27]</a></sup> However, as early as AD 680 a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_sanctuary" title="Wildlife sanctuary">wildlife sanctuary</a> was founded on the <a href="/wiki/Farne_Islands" title="Farne Islands">Farne Islands</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Cuthbert" title="St Cuthbert">St Cuthbert</a> in response to his religious beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_naturalists">Early naturalists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early naturalists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:White_Gerfalcons.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="448" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/White_Gerfalcons.jpg/170px-White_Gerfalcons.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/White_Gerfalcons.jpg/255px-White_Gerfalcons.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/White_Gerfalcons.jpg/340px-White_Gerfalcons.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:White_Gerfalcons.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>White <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcons</a> drawn by <a href="/wiki/John_James_Audubon" title="John James Audubon">John James Audubon</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">Natural history</a> was a major preoccupation in the 18th century, with grand expeditions and the opening of popular public displays in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. By 1900 there were 150 natural history museums in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, 250 in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, 250 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and 300 in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farber00_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farber00-28">[28]</a></sup> Preservationist or conservationist sentiments are a development of the late 18th to early 20th centuries.
</p><p>Before Charles Darwin set sail on HMS <i>Beagle</i>, most people in the world, including Darwin, believed in special creation and that all species were unchanged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup> George-Louis Leclerc was one of the first naturalist that questioned this belief. He proposed in his 44 volume natural history book that species evolve due to environmental influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup> Erasmus Darwin was also a naturalist who also suggested that species evolved. Erasmus Darwin noted that some species have vestigial structures which are anatomical structures that have no apparent function in the species currently but would have been useful for the species' ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-29">[29]</a></sup> The thinking of these early 18th century naturalist helped to change the mindset and thinking of the early 19th century naturalist.
</p><p>By the early 19th century <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a> was ignited through the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The 19th-century fascination with natural history engendered a fervor to be the first to collect rare specimens with the goal of doing so before they became extinct by other such collectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans97_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evans97-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farber00_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Farber00-28">[28]</a></sup> Although the work of many 18th and 19th century naturalists were to inspire nature enthusiasts and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">conservation organizations</a>, their writings, by modern standards, showed insensitivity towards conservation as they would kill hundreds of specimens for their collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farber00_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Farber00-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_movement">Conservation movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conservation movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">Conservation movement</a></div>
<p>The modern roots of conservation biology can be found in the late 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> period particularly in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans97_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Evans97-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cloyd_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cloyd-31">[31]</a></sup> A number of thinkers, among them notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Monboddo" title="Lord Monboddo">Lord Monboddo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cloyd_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cloyd-31">[31]</a></sup> described the importance of "preserving nature"; much of this early emphasis had its origins in <a href="/wiki/Christian_theology" title="Christian theology">Christian theology</a>.
</p><p>Scientific conservation principles were first practically applied to the forests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">British India</a>. The conservation ethic that began to evolve included three core principles: that human activity damaged the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, that there was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civic_duty" title="Civic duty">civic duty</a> to maintain the environment for future generations, and that scientific, empirically based methods should be applied to ensure this duty was carried out. Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Ranald_Martin" title="James Ranald Martin">James Ranald Martin</a> was prominent in promoting this ideology, publishing many medico-topographical reports that demonstrated the scale of damage wrought through large-scale deforestation and desiccation, and lobbying extensively for the institutionalization of forest conservation activities in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_India" title="British India">British India</a> through the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Forest_Service" title="Indian Forest Service">Forest Departments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madras" title="Madras">Madras</a> Board of Revenue started local conservation efforts in 1842, headed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gibson_(botanist)" title="Alexander Gibson (botanist)">Alexander Gibson</a>, a professional <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botanist</a> who systematically adopted a forest conservation program based on scientific principles. This was the first case of state conservation management of forests in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_India" title="Governor-General of India">Governor-General</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Broun-Ramsay,_1st_Marquess_of_Dalhousie" title="James Broun-Ramsay, 1st Marquess of Dalhousie">Lord Dalhousie</a> introduced the first permanent and large-scale forest conservation program in the world in 1855, a model that soon spread to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_empire" title="British empire">other colonies</a>, as well the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a> was opened in 1872 as the world's first national park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>conservation</i> came into widespread use in the late 19th century and referred to the management, mainly for economic reasons, of such natural resources as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, fish, game, <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastureland</a>, and minerals. In addition it referred to the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> (<a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wildlife_refuge" title="Wildlife refuge">wildlife refuge</a>), parkland, <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watersheds</a>. This period also saw the passage of the first conservation legislation and the establishment of the first nature conservation societies. The <a href="/wiki/Sea_Birds_Preservation_Act_1869" title="Sea Birds Preservation Act 1869">Sea Birds Preservation Act of 1869</a> was passed in Britain as the first nature protection law in the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> after extensive lobbying from the Association for the Protection of Seabirds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and the respected <a href="/wiki/Ornithology" title="Ornithology">ornithologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newton" title="Alfred Newton">Alfred Newton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Newton was also instrumental in the passage of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_laws" title="Game laws">Game laws</a> from 1872, which protected animals during their breeding season so as to prevent the stock from being brought close to extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the first conservation societies was the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Protection_of_Birds" title="Royal Society for the Protection of Birds">Royal Society for the Protection of Birds</a>, founded in 1889 in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> as a <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">protest group</a> campaigning against the use of <a href="/wiki/Great_crested_grebe" title="Great crested grebe">great crested grebe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black-legged_kittiwake" title="Black-legged kittiwake">kittiwake</a> skins and feathers in <a href="/wiki/Fur_clothing" title="Fur clothing">fur clothing</a>. Originally known as "the Plumage League",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> the group gained popularity and eventually amalgamated with the Fur and Feather League in Croydon, and formed the RSPB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Trust" title="National Trust">National Trust</a> formed in 1895 with the manifesto to "...promote the permanent preservation, for the benefit of the nation, of lands, ...to preserve (so far practicable) their natural aspect."
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Forest_Reserve_Act_of_1891" title="Forest Reserve Act of 1891">Forest Reserve Act of 1891</a> gave the President power to set aside forest reserves from the land in the public domain. <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> in 1892, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Zoological_Society" title="New York Zoological Society">New York Zoological Society</a> was set up in 1895. A series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Park" title="National Park">national forests and preserves</a> were established by <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> from 1901 to 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The 1916 National Parks Act, included a 'use without impairment' clause, sought by John Muir, which eventually resulted in the removal of a proposal to build a dam in <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_National_Monument" title="Dinosaur National Monument">Dinosaur National Monument</a> in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis96_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis96-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1146" data-file-width="956" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg/220px-Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg/330px-Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg/440px-Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muir_and_Roosevelt_restored.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Roosevelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">Muir</a> on <a href="/wiki/Glacier_Point" title="Glacier Point">Glacier Point</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> civil servants, including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gordon_Hewitt" title="Charles Gordon Hewitt">Charles Gordon Hewitt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Harkin" title="James Harkin">James Harkin</a> spearheaded the movement toward <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_conservation" title="Wildlife conservation">wildlife conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_conservation_efforts">Global conservation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global conservation efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-20th century, efforts arose to target individual species for conservation, notably efforts in <a href="/wiki/Big_cat" title="Big cat">big cat</a> conservation in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> led by the New York Zoological Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the early 20th century the New York Zoological Society was instrumental in developing concepts of establishing preserves for particular species and conducting the necessary conservation studies to determine the suitability of locations that are most appropriate as conservation priorities; the work of Henry Fairfield Osborn Jr., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_E._Akeley" title="Carl E. Akeley">Carl E. Akeley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archie_Carr" title="Archie Carr">Archie Carr</a> and his son Archie Carr III is notable in this era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Akeley for example, having led expeditions to the <a href="/wiki/Virunga_Mountains" title="Virunga Mountains">Virunga Mountains</a> and observed the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">mountain gorilla</a> in the wild, became convinced that the species and the area were conservation priorities. He was instrumental in persuading <a href="/wiki/Albert_I_of_Belgium" title="Albert I of Belgium">Albert I of Belgium</a> to act in defense of the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">mountain gorilla</a> and establish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_National_Park" title="Albert National Park">Albert National Park</a> (since renamed <a href="/wiki/Virunga_National_Park" title="Virunga National Park">Virunga National Park</a>) in what is now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">Democratic Republic of Congo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1970s, led primarily by work in the United States under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" title="Endangered Species Act">Endangered Species Act</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> along with the <a href="/wiki/Species_at_Risk_Act" title="Species at Risk Act">Species at Risk Act</a> (SARA) of Canada, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plans</a> developed in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, hundreds of species specific protection plans ensued. Notably the United Nations acted to conserve sites of outstanding cultural or natural importance to the common heritage of mankind. The programme was adopted by the General Conference of <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1972. As of 2006, a total of 830 sites are listed: 644 cultural, 162 natural. The first country to pursue aggressive biological conservation through national legislation was the United States, which passed back to back legislation in the Endangered Species Act<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> (1966) and <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a> (1970),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> which together injected major funding and protection measures to large-scale habitat protection and threatened species research. Other conservation developments, however, have taken hold throughout the world. India, for example, passed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_Protection_Act_of_1972" title="Wildlife Protection Act of 1972">Wildlife Protection Act of 1972</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1980, a significant development was the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Urban_wildlife" title="Urban wildlife">urban conservation</a> movement. A local organization was established in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>, a development followed in rapid succession in cities across the UK, then overseas. Although perceived as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grassroots_movement" title="Grassroots movement">grassroots movement</a>, its early development was driven by academic research into urban wildlife. Initially perceived as radical, the movement's view of conservation being inextricably linked with other human activity has now become mainstream in conservation thought. Considerable research effort is now directed at urban conservation biology. The <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Conservation_Biology" title="Society for Conservation Biology">Society for Conservation Biology</a> originated in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup>
</p><p>By 1992, most of the countries of the world had become committed to the principles of conservation of biological diversity with the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> subsequently many countries began programmes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plans</a> to identify and conserve threatened species within their borders, as well as protect associated habitats. The late 1990s saw increasing professionalism in the sector, with the maturing of organisations such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Ecology_and_Environmental_Management" title="Institute of Ecology and Environmental Management">Institute of Ecology and Environmental Management</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Environment" title="Society for the Environment">Society for the Environment</a>.
</p><p>Since 2000, the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_scale_conservation" title="Landscape scale conservation">landscape scale conservation</a> has risen to prominence, with less emphasis being given to single-species or even single-habitat focused actions. Instead an ecosystem approach is advocated by most mainstream conservationists, although concerns have been expressed by those working to protect some high-profile species.
</p><p>Ecology has clarified the workings of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>; i.e., the complex interrelationships among humans, other species, and the physical environment. The <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">burgeoning human population</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>, and the ensuing pollution, have demonstrated how easily ecological relationships can be disrupted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Extinction rates are measured in a variety of ways. Conservation biologists measure and apply <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_measure" title="Statistical measure">statistical measures</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil records</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> rates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss">habitat loss</a>, and a multitude of other variables such as loss of biodiversity as a function of the rate of habitat loss and site occupancy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> to obtain such estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Theory_of_Island_Biogeography" title="The Theory of Island Biogeography">The Theory of Island Biogeography</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> is possibly the most significant contribution toward the scientific understanding of both the process and how to measure the rate of species extinction. The current <a href="/wiki/Background_extinction_rate" title="Background extinction rate">background extinction rate</a> is estimated to be one species every few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>The measure of ongoing species loss is made more complex by the fact that most of the Earth's species have not been described or evaluated. Estimates vary greatly on how many species actually exist (estimated range: 3,600,000-111,700,000)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson00_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson00-65">[65]</a></sup> to how many have received a <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">species binomial</a> (estimated range: 1.5-8 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson00_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson00-65">[65]</a></sup> Less than 1% of all species that have been described beyond simply noting its existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson00_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson00-65">[65]</a></sup> From these figures, the IUCN reports that 23% of <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, 5% of <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> and 70% of plants that have been evaluated are designated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered" title="Endangered">endangered</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threatened" title="Threatened">threatened</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Better knowledge is being constructed by <a href="/wiki/The_Plant_List" title="The Plant List">The Plant List</a> for actual numbers of species.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematic_conservation_planning">Systematic conservation planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Systematic conservation planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Systematic conservation planning is an effective way to seek and identify efficient and effective types of reserve design to capture or sustain the highest priority biodiversity values and to work with communities in support of local ecosystems. Margules and Pressey identify six interlinked stages in the systematic planning approach:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Compile data on the biodiversity of the planning region</li>
<li>Identify conservation goals for the planning region</li>
<li>Review existing conservation areas</li>
<li>Select additional conservation areas</li>
<li>Implement conservation actions</li>
<li>Maintain the required values of conservation areas</li></ol>
<p>Conservation biologists regularly prepare detailed conservation plans for <a href="/wiki/Grant_(money)" title="Grant (money)">grant proposals</a> or to effectively coordinate their plan of action and to identify best management practices (e.g.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>). Systematic strategies generally employ the services of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_Information_Systems" title="Geographic Information Systems">Geographic Information Systems</a> to assist in the decision making process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_physiology:_a_mechanistic_approach_to_conservation">Conservation physiology: a mechanistic approach to conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conservation physiology: a mechanistic approach to conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conservation physiology was defined by <a href="/wiki/Steven_J._Cooke" title="Steven J. Cooke">Steven J. Cooke</a> and colleagues as: 'An integrative scientific discipline applying physiological concepts, tools, and knowledge to characterizing biological diversity and its ecological implications; understanding and predicting how organisms, populations, and ecosystems respond to environmental change and stressors; and solving conservation problems across the broad range of taxa (i.e. including microbes, plants, and animals). Physiology is considered in the broadest possible terms to include functional and mechanistic responses at all scales, and conservation includes the development and refinement of strategies to rebuild populations, restore ecosystems, inform conservation policy, generate decision-support tools, and manage natural resources.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooke_et_al_2013_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>  Conservation physiology is particularly relevant to practitioners in that it has the potential to generate cause-and-effect relationships and reveal the factors that contribute to population declines.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_biology_as_a_profession">Conservation biology as a profession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Conservation biology as a profession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Conservation_Biology" title="Society for Conservation Biology">Society for Conservation Biology</a> is a global community of conservation professionals dedicated to advancing the science and practice of conserving biodiversity. Conservation biology as a discipline reaches beyond biology, into subjects such as <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arts" title="Arts">arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Groom06_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Groom06-6">[6]</a></sup> Within biology, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_genetics" title="Conservation genetics">conservation genetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> are immense fields unto themselves, but these disciplines are of prime importance to the practice and profession of conservation biology.
</p><p>Is conservation biology an <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(science)" title="Objectivity (science)">objective science</a> when biologists <a href="/wiki/Advocate" title="Advocate">advocate</a> for an inherent <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">value in nature</a>? Do conservationists introduce <a href="/wiki/Bias" title="Bias">bias</a> when they support policies using qualitative description, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_degradation" title="Habitat degradation">habitat <i>degradation</i></a>, or <i>healthy</i> <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>? As all scientists hold values, so do conservation biologists. Conservation biologists advocate for reasoned and sensible management of natural resources and do so with a disclosed combination of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_(personal_and_cultural)" title="Value (personal and cultural)">values</a> in their conservation management plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup> This sort of advocacy is similar to the medical profession advocating for healthy lifestyle options, both are beneficial to human well-being yet remain scientific in their approach.
</p><p>There is a movement in conservation biology suggesting a new form of leadership is needed to mobilize conservation biology into a more effective discipline that is able to communicate the full scope of the problem to society at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The movement proposes an adaptive leadership approach that parallels an <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">adaptive management</a> approach. The concept is based on a new philosophy or leadership theory steering away from historical notions of power, authority, and dominance. Adaptive conservation leadership is reflective and more equitable as it applies to any member of society who can mobilize others toward meaningful change using communication techniques that are inspiring, purposeful, and collegial. Adaptive conservation leadership and mentoring programs are being implemented by conservation biologists through organizations such as the Aldo Leopold Leadership Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches">Approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conservation may be classified as either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_conservation" title="In-situ conservation">in-situ conservation</a>, which is protecting an endangered species in its natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex-situ_conservation" title="Ex-situ conservation">ex-situ conservation</a>, which occurs outside the natural habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-72">[72]</a></sup> In-situ conservation involves protecting or restoring the habitat. Ex-situ conservation, on the other hand, involves protection outside of an organism's natural habitat, such as on reservations or in gene banks, in circumstances where viable populations may not be present in the natural habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Also, non-interference may be used, which is termed a <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">preservationist</a> method. Preservationists advocate for giving areas of nature and species a protected existence that halts interference from the humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup> In this regard, conservationists differ from preservationists in the social dimension, as conservation biology engages society and seeks equitable solutions for both society and ecosystems. Some preservationists emphasize the potential of biodiversity in a world without humans.</p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote">
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics_and_values">Ethics and values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ethics and values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">Conservation (ethic)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_ethic" title="Land ethic">Land ethic</a></div>
<p>Conservation biologists are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> researchers that practice ethics in the biological and social sciences. Chan states<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chan08_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chan08-73">[73]</a></sup> that conservationists must advocate for biodiversity and can do so in a scientifically ethical manner by not promoting simultaneous advocacy against other competing values.
</p><p>A conservationist may be inspired by the <i>resource conservation ethic</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> which seeks to identify what measures will deliver "the greatest good for the greatest number of people for the longest time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>13</span></sup> In contrast, some conservation biologists argue that nature has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrinsic_value_(ethics)" title="Intrinsic value (ethics)">intrinsic value</a> that is independent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropocentric" title="Anthropocentric">anthropocentric</a> usefulness or <a href="/wiki/Utilitarianism" title="Utilitarianism">utilitarianism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3,12,16–17</span></sup> Intrinsic value advocates that a gene, or species, be valued because they have a utility for the ecosystems they sustain. <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> was a classical thinker and writer on such conservation ethics whose philosophy, ethics and writings are still valued and revisited by modern conservation biologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dyke08_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke08-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>16–17</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_priorities">Conservation priorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Conservation priorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BiomassCharts.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1102" data-file-width="4134" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BiomassCharts.jpg/220px-BiomassCharts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BiomassCharts.jpg/330px-BiomassCharts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/BiomassCharts.jpg/440px-BiomassCharts.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BiomassCharts.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pie chart image showing the relative biomass representation in a rain forest through a summary of children's perceptions from drawings and artwork (left), through a scientific estimate of actual biomass (middle), and by a measure of biodiversity (right). Notice that the biomass of social insects (middle) far outweighs the number of species (right).</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity#Benefits_of_biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity § Benefits of biodiversity</a></div>
<p>The International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN) has organized a global assortment of scientists and research stations across the planet to monitor the changing state of nature in an effort to tackle the extinction crisis. The IUCN provides annual updates on the status of species conservation through its Red List.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> serves as an international conservation tool to identify those species most in need of conservation attention and by providing a global index on the status of biodiversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vié09_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vié09-75">[75]</a></sup> More than the dramatic rates of species loss, however, conservation scientists note that the sixth mass extinction is a biodiversity crisis requiring far more action than a priority focus on <a href="/wiki/Rare_species" title="Rare species">rare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>. Concerns for biodiversity loss covers a broader conservation mandate that looks at ecological processes, such as migration, and a holistic examination of biodiversity at levels beyond the species, including genetic, population and ecosystem diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molnar04_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Molnar04-76">[76]</a></sup> Extensive, systematic, and rapid rates of biodiversity loss threatens the sustained well-being of humanity by limiting supply of ecosystem services that are otherwise regenerated by the complex and evolving holistic network of genetic and ecosystem diversity. While the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_status" title="Conservation status">conservation status</a> of species is employed extensively in conservation management,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vié09_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vié09-75">[75]</a></sup> some scientists highlight that it is the common species that are the primary source of exploitation and habitat alteration by humanity. Moreover, common species are often undervalued despite their role as the primary source of ecosystem services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaston10_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaston10-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kearns10_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kearns10-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>While most in the community of conservation science "stress the importance" of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustaining biodiversity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CREOcare_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-CREOcare-79">[79]</a></sup> there is debate on how to prioritize genes, species, or ecosystems, which are all components of biodiversity (e.g. Bowen, 1999). While the predominant approach to date has been to focus efforts on endangered species by conserving <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspots" title="Biodiversity hotspots">biodiversity hotspots</a></i>, some scientists (e.g)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luck03_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luck03-80">[80]</a></sup> and conservation organizations, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_Conservancy" title="Nature Conservancy">Nature Conservancy</a>, argue that it is more cost-effective, logical, and socially relevant to invest in <i>biodiversity coldspots</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The costs of discovering, naming, and mapping out the distribution of every species, they argue, is an ill-advised conservation venture. They reason it is better to understand the significance of the ecological roles of species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biodiversity hotspots and coldspots are a way of recognizing that the spatial concentration of genes, species, and ecosystems is not uniformly distributed on the Earth's surface. For example, "[...] 44% of all species of vascular plants and 35% of all species in four vertebrate groups are confined to 25 hotspots comprising only 1.4% of the land surface of the Earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Those arguing in favor of setting priorities for coldspots point out that there are other measures to consider beyond biodiversity. They point out that emphasizing hotspots downplays the importance of the social and ecological connections to vast areas of the Earth's ecosystems where <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, not biodiversity, reigns supreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> It is estimated that 36% of the Earth's surface, encompassing 38.9% of the worlds vertebrates, lacks the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_species" title="Endemic species">endemic species</a> to qualify as biodiversity hotspot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Moreover, measures show that maximizing protections for biodiversity does not capture ecosystem services any better than targeting randomly chosen regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Population level biodiversity (i.e. coldspots) are disappearing at a rate that is ten times that at the species level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luck03_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luck03-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood08_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood08-87">[87]</a></sup> The level of importance in addressing biomass versus endemism as a concern for conservation biology is highlighted in literature measuring the level of threat to global ecosystem carbon stocks that do not necessarily reside in areas of endemism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Running08_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Running08-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurz08_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kurz08-89">[89]</a></sup> A hotspot priority approach<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> would not invest so heavily in places such as <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppes</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Serengeti" title="Serengeti">Serengeti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a>. These areas contribute a great abundance of population (not species) level biodiversity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood08_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wood08-87">[87]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>, including cultural value and planetary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="float:left; border:1px solid #ccc; font-size:90%; text-align:center; padding:2px;">

<p>Those in favor of the hotspot approach point out that species are irreplaceable components of the global ecosystem, they are concentrated in places that are most threatened, and should therefore receive maximal strategic protections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> categories, which appear on Wikipedia species articles, is an example of the hotspot conservation approach in action; species that are not rare or endemic are listed the least concern and their Wikipedia articles tend to be ranked low on the importance scale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Conservation_biology#Dubious" title="Talk:Conservation biology">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> This is a hotspot approach because the priority is set to target species level concerns over population level or biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood08_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wood08-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (July 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Species richness and genetic biodiversity contributes to and engenders ecosystem stability, ecosystem processes, evolutionary <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptability</a>, and biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Both sides agree, however, that conserving biodiversity is necessary to reduce the extinction rate and identify an inherent value in nature; the debate hinges on how to prioritize limited conservation resources in the most cost-effective way.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_values_and_natural_capital">Economic values and natural capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economic values and natural capital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg/250px-Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg/375px-Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg/500px-Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Libya_4985_Tadrart_Acacus_Luca_Galuzzi_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadrart_Acacus" title="Tadrart Acacus">Tadrart Acacus</a> desert in western <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a></div>
<p>Conservation biologists have started to collaborate with leading global <a href="/wiki/Economist" title="Economist">economists</a> to determine how to measure the <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> of nature and to make these values apparent in <a href="/wiki/International_finance" title="International finance">global market transactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC08_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC08-94">[94]</a></sup> This system of accounting is called <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a></i> and would, for example, register the value of an ecosystem before it is cleared to make way for development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_For_Nature" title="World Wide Fund For Nature">WWF</a> publishes its <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Report" title="Living Planet Report">Living Planet Report</a></i> and provides a global index of biodiversity by monitoring approximately 5,000 populations in 1,686 species of vertebrate (mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, and amphibians) and report on the trends in much the same way that the stock market is tracked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLP08_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-LLP08-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p><p>This method of measuring the global economic benefit of nature has been endorsed by the <a href="/wiki/G8%2B5" title="G8+5">G8+5</a> leaders and the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC08_94-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC08-94">[94]</a></sup> Nature sustains many <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> that benefit humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEA08_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEA08-98">[98]</a></sup> Many of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_goods" title="Public goods">public goods</a> without a <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> and therefore no <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a> or <a href="/wiki/Value_(economics)" title="Value (economics)">value</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC08_94-2"><a href="#cite_note-EC08-94">[94]</a></sup> When the <i>stock market</i> registers a financial crisis, traders on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> are not in the business of trading stocks for much of the planet's living natural capital stored in ecosystems. There is no natural stock market with investment portfolios into sea horses, amphibians, insects, and other creatures that provide a sustainable supply of ecosystem services that are valuable to society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEA08_98-1"><a href="#cite_note-MEA08-98">[98]</a></sup> The ecological footprint of society has exceeded the bio-regenerative capacity limits of the planet's ecosystems by about 30 percent, which is the same percentage of vertebrate populations that have registered decline from 1970 through 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLP08_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-LLP08-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25%; ;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">The ecological credit crunch is a global challenge. The <i>Living Planet Report 2008</i> tells us that more than three-quarters of the world's people live in nations that are ecological debtors – their national consumption has outstripped their country's biocapacity. Thus, most of us are propping up our current lifestyles, and our economic growth, by drawing (and increasingly overdrawing) upon the ecological capital of other parts of the world.
</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style=""> WWF Living Planet Report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LLP08_96-2"><a href="#cite_note-LLP08-96">[96]</a></sup>
</cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>The inherent <a href="/wiki/Natural_economy" title="Natural economy">natural economy</a> plays an essential role in sustaining humanity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> including the regulation of global <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollination_management" title="Pollination management">pollinating crops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">pest control</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Soil_ecology" title="Soil ecology">cycling soil nutrients</a>, purifying our <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> supplying medicines and health benefits,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and unquantifiable quality of life improvements. There is a relationship, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlation</a>, between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markets" title="Markets">markets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_inequity_aversion" title="Social inequity aversion">social income inequity</a> and biodiversity loss. This means that there are greater rates of biodiversity loss in places where the inequity of wealth is greatest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although a direct market comparison of <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> is likely insufficient in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_value" title="Human value">human value</a>, one measure of ecosystem services suggests the contribution amounts to trillions of dollars yearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> For example, one segment of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> forests has been assigned an annual value of 250 billion dollars;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> as another example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honey-bee" title="Honey-bee">honey-bee</a> pollination is estimated to provide between 10 and 18 billion dollars of value yearly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> The value of ecosystem services on one <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> island has been imputed to be as great as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> of that region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> This planetary wealth is being lost at an incredible rate as the demands of human society is exceeding the bio-regenerative capacity of the Earth. While biodiversity and ecosystems are resilient, the danger of losing them is that humans cannot recreate many ecosystem functions through <a href="/wiki/Technological_innovation" title="Technological innovation">technological innovation</a>.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_species_concepts">Strategic species concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Strategic species concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Keystone_species">Keystone species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Keystone species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Keystone_species" title="Keystone species">Keystone species</a></div>
<p>Some species, called a <i>keystone species</i> form a central supporting hub unique to their ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Society_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Society-111">[111]</a></sup> The loss of such a species results in a collapse in ecosystem function, as well as the loss of coexisting species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup> Keystone species are usually predators due to their ability to control the population of prey in their ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Society_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-Society-111">[111]</a></sup> The importance of a keystone species was shown by the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_sea_cow" title="Steller's sea cow">Steller's sea cow</a> (<i>Hydrodamalis gigas</i>) through its interaction with <a href="/wiki/Sea_otter" title="Sea otter">sea otters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelp_bed" title="Kelp bed">Kelp beds</a> grow and form nurseries in shallow waters to shelter creatures that support the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. Sea urchins feed on kelp, while sea otters feed on sea urchins. With the rapid decline of sea otters due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overhunting" title="Overhunting">overhunting</a>, sea urchin populations <a href="/wiki/Urchin_barren" title="Urchin barren">grazed unrestricted</a> on the kelp beds and the ecosystem collapsed. Left unchecked, the urchins destroyed the shallow water kelp communities that supported the Steller's sea cow's diet and hastened their demise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> The sea otter was thought to be a keystone species because the coexistence of many ecological associates in the kelp beds relied upon otters for their survival. However this was later questioned by Turvey and Risley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17148336_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17148336-113">[113]</a></sup> who showed that hunting alone would have driven the Steller's sea cow extinct.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indicator_species">Indicator species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Indicator species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">Indicator species</a></div>
<p>An <i>indicator species</i> has a narrow set of ecological requirements, therefore they become useful targets for observing the health of an ecosystem. Some animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a> with their semi-permeable skin and linkages to <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, have an acute sensitivity to environmental harm and thus may serve as a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miner%27s_canary" title="Miner's canary">miner's canary</a></i>. Indicator species are monitored in an effort to capture environmental degradation through pollution or some other link to proximate human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup> Monitoring an indicator species is a measure to determine if there is a significant environmental impact that can serve to advise or modify practice, such as through different forest <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a> treatments and management scenarios, or to measure the degree of harm that a <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> may impart on the health of an ecosystem.
</p><p>Government regulators, consultants, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NGO" title="NGO">NGOs</a> regularly monitor indicator species, however, there are limitations coupled with many practical considerations that must be followed for the approach to be effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> It is generally recommended that multiple indicators (genes, populations, species, communities, and landscape) be monitored for effective conservation measurement that prevents harm to the complex, and often unpredictable, response from ecosystem dynamics (Noss, 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meffe97_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meffe97-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>88–89</span></sup>).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Umbrella_and_flagship_species">Umbrella and flagship species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Umbrella and flagship species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Umbrella_species" title="Umbrella species">Umbrella species</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flagship_species" title="Flagship species">Flagship species</a></div>
<p>An example of an <i>umbrella species</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarch_(butterfly)" title="Monarch (butterfly)">monarch butterfly</a>, because of its lengthy <a href="/wiki/Insect_migration" title="Insect migration">migrations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetic</a> value. The monarch migrates across North America, covering multiple ecosystems and so requires a large area to exist. Any protections afforded to the monarch butterfly will at the same time umbrella many other species and habitats. An umbrella species is often used as <i>flagship species</i>, which are species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Giant_panda" title="Giant panda">giant panda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Blue_whale" title="Blue whale">blue whale</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tiger</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gorilla" title="Mountain gorilla">mountain gorilla</a> and the monarch butterfly, that capture the public's attention and attract support for conservation measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunter96_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter96-5">[5]</a></sup> Paradoxically, however, conservation bias towards flagship species sometimes threatens other species of chief concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context_and_trends">Context and trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Context and trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conservation biologists study trends and process from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontological" title="Paleontological">paleontological</a> past to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> present as they gain an understanding of the context related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_extinction" title="Species extinction">species extinction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-1">[1]</a></sup> It is generally accepted that there have been five major global mass extinctions that register in Earth's history. These include: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_event" title="Ordovician–Silurian extinction event">Ordovician</a> (440 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a>), <a href="/wiki/Late_Devonian_extinction" title="Late Devonian extinction">Devonian</a> (370 mya), <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic</a> (245 mya), <a href="/wiki/Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic–Jurassic extinction event">Triassic–Jurassic</a> (200 mya), and <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a> (66 mya) extinction spasms. Within the last 10,000 years, human influence over the Earth's ecosystems has been so extensive that scientists have difficulty estimating the number of species lost;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-117">[117]</a></sup> that is to say the rates of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coral_reef_destruction" title="Coral reef destruction">reef destruction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threats_to_wetlands" title="Threats to wetlands">wetland draining</a> and other human acts are proceeding much faster than human assessment of species. The latest <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Report" title="Living Planet Report">Living Planet Report</a></i> by the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a> estimates that we have exceeded the bio-regenerative capacity of the planet, requiring 1.6 Earths to support the demands placed on our natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWFLPR_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWFLPR-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holocene_extinction">Holocene extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Holocene extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1976" data-file-width="1494" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg/220px-ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg/330px-ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg/440px-ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ChildrenPerceptionBiomass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An art scape image showing the relative importance of animals in a rain forest through a summary of (a) child's perception compared with (b) a scientific estimate of the importance. The size of the animal represents its importance. The child's mental image places importance on big cats, birds, butterflies, and then reptiles versus the actual dominance of social insects (such as ants).</div></div></div>
<p>Conservation biologists are dealing with and have published <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> from all corners of the planet indicating that humanity may be causing the sixth and fastest planetary <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wake08_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wake08-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> It has been suggested that we are living in an era of unprecedented numbers of species extinctions, also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction_event" title="Holocene extinction event">Holocene extinction event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The global extinction rate may be approximately 1,000 times higher than the natural background extinction rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dell'Amore_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dell'Amore-123">[123]</a></sup> It is estimated that two-thirds of all <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genera" title="Genera">genera</a> and one-half of all mammal <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> weighing at least 44 kilograms (97 lb) have gone extinct in the last 50,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17148336_113-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17148336-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> The Global Amphibian Assessment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> reports that <a href="/wiki/Decline_in_amphibian_populations" title="Decline in amphibian populations">amphibians are declining</a> on a global scale faster than any other <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> group, with over 32% of all surviving species being threatened with extinction. The surviving populations are in continual decline in 43% of those that are threatened. Since the mid-1980s the actual rates of extinction have exceeded 211 times rates measured from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCallum07_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCallum07-128">[128]</a></sup> However, "The current amphibian extinction rate may range from 25,039 to 45,474 times the background extinction rate for amphibians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCallum07_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCallum07-128">[128]</a></sup> The global extinction trend occurs in every major <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> group that is being monitored. For example, 23% of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> and 12% of all <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> are <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">Red Listed</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN), meaning they too are threatened with extinction. Even though extinction is natural, the decline in species is happening at such an incredible rate that evolution can simply not match, therefore, leading to the greatest continual mass extinction on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vince_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vince-129">[129]</a></sup> Humans have dominated the planet and our high consumption of resources, along with the pollution generated is affecting the environments in which other species live.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vince_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vince-129">[129]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tate_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tate-130">[130]</a></sup> There are a wide variety of species that humans are working to protect such as the Hawaiian Crow and the Whooping Crane of Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worrall_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worrall-131">[131]</a></sup> People can also take action on preserving species by advocating and voting for global and national policies that improve climate, under the concepts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_mitigation" title="Climate mitigation">climate mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_restoration" title="Climate restoration">climate restoration</a>. The Earth's oceans especially require attention, as climate change has altered pH levels making it inhabitable for organisms with shells, that are dissolving as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dell'Amore_123-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dell'Amore-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_of_oceans_and_reefs">Status of oceans and reefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Status of oceans and reefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reef</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">Marine conservation</a></div>
<p>Global assessments of coral reefs of the world continue to report drastic and rapid rates of decline. By 2000, 27% of the world's coral reef ecosystems had effectively collapsed. The largest period of decline occurred in a dramatic "bleaching" event in 1998, where approximately 16% of all the coral reefs in the world disappeared in less than a year. <i><a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">Coral bleaching</a></i> is caused by a mixture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_stress" title="Environmental stress">environmental stresses</a>, including increases in ocean temperatures and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidity</a>, causing both the release of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbiotic" title="Symbiotic">symbiotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and death of corals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausSOE01_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-ausSOE01-132">[132]</a></sup> Decline and extinction risk in coral reef biodiversity has risen dramatically in the past ten years. The loss of coral reefs, which are predicted to go extinct in the next century, threatens the balance of global biodiversity, will have huge economic impacts, and endangers food security for hundreds of millions of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>  Conservation biology plays an important role in international agreements covering the world's oceans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausSOE01_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-ausSOE01-132">[132]</a></sup> (and other issues pertaining to <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>).
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25%; ;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">These predictions will undoubtedly appear extreme, but it is difficult to imagine how such changes will not come to pass without fundamental changes in human behavior.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">J.B. Jackson<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11463</span></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>The oceans are threatened by acidification due to an increase in CO<sub>2</sub> levels. This is a most serious threat to societies relying heavily upon oceanic <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>. A concern is that the majority of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> species will not be able to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolve</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acclimate" title="Acclimate">acclimate</a> in response to the changes in the ocean chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p>The prospects of averting mass extinction seems unlikely when "[...] 90% of all of the large (average approximately ≥50 kg), open ocean tuna, billfishes, and sharks in the ocean"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-16">[16]</a></sup> are reportedly gone. Given the scientific review of current trends, the ocean is predicted to have few surviving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-cellular_organism" title="Multi-cellular organism">multi-cellular organisms</a> with only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbes</a> left to dominate <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups_other_than_vertebrates">Groups other than vertebrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Groups other than vertebrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Serious concerns also being raised about <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxonomic groups</a> that do not receive the same degree of social attention or attract funds as the vertebrates. These include <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungal</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>-forming species),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fungal-conservation_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-fungal-conservation-136">[136]</a></sup> invertebrate (particularly <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koh_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koh-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> <a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">communities</a> where the vast majority of biodiversity is represented. Conservation of fungi and conservation of insects, in particular, are both of pivotal importance for conservation biology. As mycorrhizal symbionts, and as decomposers and recyclers, fungi are essential for sustainability of forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fungal-conservation_136-1"><a href="#cite_note-fungal-conservation-136">[136]</a></sup> The value of insects in the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> is enormous because they outnumber all other living groups in measure of <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">species richness</a>. The greatest bulk of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> on land is found in plants, which is sustained by insect relations. This great ecological value of insects is countered by a society that often reacts negatively toward these aesthetically 'unpleasant' creatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>
</p><p>One area of concern in the insect world that has caught the public eye is the mysterious case of missing <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a> (<i>Apis mellifera</i>). Honey bees provide an indispensable ecological services through their acts of pollination supporting a huge variety of agriculture crops. The use of honey and wax have become vastly used throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> The sudden disappearance of bees leaving empty hives or <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a> (CCD) is not uncommon. However, in  16-month period from 2006 through 2007, 29% of 577 <a href="/wiki/Beekeeper" title="Beekeeper">beekeepers</a> across the United States reported CCD losses in up to 76% of their colonies. This sudden demographic loss in bee numbers is placing a strain on the agricultural sector. The cause behind the massive declines is puzzling scientists. <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">Pests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> are all being considered as possible causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another highlight that links conservation biology to insects, forests, and climate change is the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pine_beetle" title="Mountain pine beetle">mountain pine beetle</a> (<i>Dendroctonus ponderosae</i>) <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada, which has infested 470,000 km<sup>2</sup> (180,000 sq mi) of forested land since 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Running08_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Running08-88">[88]</a></sup> An action plan has been prepared by the Government of British Columbia to address this problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This impact [<i>pine beetle epidemic</i>] converted the forest from a small net carbon sink to a large net carbon source both during and immediately after the outbreak. In the worst year, the impacts resulting from the beetle outbreak in British Columbia were equivalent to 75% of the average annual direct forest fire emissions from all of Canada during 1959–1999.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Kurz <i>et al</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurz08_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kurz08-89">[89]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_biology_of_parasites">Conservation biology of parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Conservation biology of parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology_of_parasites" title="Conservation biology of parasites">Conservation biology of parasites</a></div>
<p>A large proportion of parasite species are threatened by extinction. A few of them are being eradicated as pests of humans or domestic animals, however, most of them are harmless. Threats include the decline or fragmentation of host populations, or the extinction of host species.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_to_biodiversity">Threats to biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conservation_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Threats to biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_threats" title="Biodiversity threats">Biodiversity threats</a></div>
<p>Today, many threats to Biodiversity exist. An acronym that can be used to express the top threats of present-day H.I.P.P.O stands for Habitat Loss, Invasive Species, Pollution, Human Population, and Overharvesting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-146">[146]</a></sup> The primary threats to biodiversity are <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_expansion" title="Agricultural expansion">agricultural expansion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban development</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_trade" title="Wildlife trade">wildlife trade</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">Habitat fragmentation</a> also poses challenges, because the global network of protected areas only covers 11.5% of the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup>  A significant consequence of fragmentation and lack of <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">linked protected areas</a> is the reduction of animal migration on a global scale. Considering that billions of tonnes of biomass are responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a> across the earth, the reduction of migration is a serious matter for conservation biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:25%; ;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">Human activities are associated directly or indirectly with nearly every aspect of the current extinction spasm.
</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style=""> Wake and Vredenburg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wake08_119-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wake08-119">[119]</a></sup>
</cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>However, human activities need not necessarily cause irreparable harm to the biosphere. With <a href="/wiki/Conservation_management_system" title="Conservation management system">conservation management and planning</a> for biodiversity at all levels, from <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> to ecosystems, there are examples where humans mutually coexist in a sustainable way with nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> Even with the current threats to biodiversity there are ways we can improve the current condition and start anew.
</p><p>Many of the threats to biodiversity, including disease and climate change, are reaching inside borders of protected areas, leaving them 'not-so protected' (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a>, for example, is often cited as a serious threat in this regard, because there is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loop</a> between species extinction and the release of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Running08_88-2"><a href="#cite_note-Running08-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurz08_89-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kurz08-89">[89]</a></sup> Ecosystems store and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">cycle</a> large amounts of carbon which regulates global conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> In present day, there have been major climate shifts with temperature changes making survival of some species difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_146-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-146">[146]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> add a catastrophic threat toward a mass extinction of global biological diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_158-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-158">[158]</a></sup> Conservationists have claimed that not all the species can be saved, and they have to decide which their efforts should be used to protect. This concept is known as the Conservation Triage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_146-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-146">[146]</a></sup> The extinction threat is estimated to range from 15 to 37 percent of all species by 2050,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_158-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-158">[158]</a></sup> or 50 percent of all species over the next 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koh_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Koh-14">[14]</a></sup> The current extinction rate is 100-100,000 times more rapid today than the last several billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_146-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-146">[146]</a></sup>
</p>
