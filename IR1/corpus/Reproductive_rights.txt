<p><b>Reproductive rights</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_right" title="Legal right">legal rights</a> and freedoms relating to <a href="/wiki/Human_reproduction" title="Human reproduction">reproduction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive health</a> that vary amongst countries around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COOK_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-COOK-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> defines reproductive rights as follows:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
Reproductive rights rest on the recognition of the basic right of all couples and individuals to decide freely and responsibly the number, spacing and timing of their children and to have the information and means to do so, and the right to attain the highest standard of sexual and reproductive health. They also include the right of all to make decisions concerning reproduction free of <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Women's reproductive rights may include some or all of the following: the <a href="/wiki/Abortion-rights_movements" title="Abortion-rights movements">right to legal and safe abortion</a>; the right to <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a>; freedom from <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_sterilization" title="Compulsory sterilization">coerced sterilization</a> and contraception; the right to access good-quality <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive healthcare</a>; and the right to <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">education and access</a> in order to make free and informed reproductive choices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMNESTY_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMNESTY-3">[3]</a></sup> Reproductive rights may also include the right to receive <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">education</a> about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted infections</a> and other aspects of sexuality, and protection from practices such as <a href="/wiki/Female_genital_mutilation" title="Female genital mutilation">female genital mutilation</a> (FGM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COOK_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-COOK-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMNESTY_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMNESTY-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREEDMAN_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FREEDMAN-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reproductive rights began to develop as a subset of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> at the United Nation's 1968 International Conference on Human Rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREEDMAN_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FREEDMAN-4">[4]</a></sup> The resulting <a href="/wiki/Non-binding_resolution" title="Non-binding resolution">non binding</a> Proclamation of Teheran was the first international document to recognize one of these rights when it stated that: "Parents have a basic human right to determine <a href="/wiki/Political_freedom" title="Political freedom">freely</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moral_responsibility" title="Moral responsibility">responsibly</a> the number and the spacing of their children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREEDMAN_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FREEDMAN-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teheran_proc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-teheran_proc-6">[6]</a></sup> States, though, have been slow in incorporating these rights in <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">internationally legally binding instruments</a>. Thus, while some of these rights have already been recognized in <a href="/wiki/Soft_law" title="Soft law">hard law</a>, that is, in legally binding <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a>, others have been mentioned only in non binding recommendations and, therefore, have at best the status of <a href="/wiki/Soft_law" title="Soft law">soft law</a> in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, while a further group is yet to be accepted by the international community and therefore remains at the level of <a href="/wiki/Advocacy" title="Advocacy">advocacy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Issues related to reproductive rights are some of the most vigorously contested rights' issues worldwide, regardless of the population's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">socioeconomic</a> level, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The issue of reproductive rights is frequently presented as being of vital importance in discussions and articles by <a href="/wiki/List_of_population_concern_organizations" title="List of population concern organizations">population concern organizations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Population_Matters" title="Population Matters">Population Matters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsearch_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-popsearch-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reproductive rights are a subset of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_and_reproductive_health_and_rights" title="Sexual and reproductive health and rights">sexual and reproductive health and rights</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proclamation_of_Teheran">Proclamation of Teheran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proclamation of Teheran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1945, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a> included the obligation "to promote... universal respect for, and observance of, human rights and fundamental freedoms for all without discrimination as to race, sex, language, or religion". However, the Charter did not define these rights. Three years later, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> adopted the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> (UDHR), the first international legal document to delineate <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>; the UDHR does not mention reproductive rights.  Reproductive rights began to appear as a subset of human rights in the 1968 Proclamation of Teheran, which states: "Parents have a basic human right to determine freely and responsibly the number and the spacing of their children".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teheran_proc_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-teheran_proc-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>This right was affirmed by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> in the 1969 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Declaration_on_Social_Progress_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Declaration on Social Progress and Development (page does not exist)">Declaration on Social Progress and Development</a> which states "The family as a basic unit of society and the natural environment for the growth and well-being of all its members, particularly children and youth, should be assisted and protected so that it may fully assume its responsibilities within the community. Parents have the exclusive right to determine freely and responsibly the number and spacing of their children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREEDMAN_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-FREEDMAN-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Women%27s_Year#International" title="International Women's Year">1975 UN International Women's Year Conference</a> echoed the Proclamation of Teheran.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cairo_Programme_of_Action">Cairo Programme of Action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cairo Programme of Action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The twenty-year "Cairo Programme of Action" was adopted in 1994 at the <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_Population_and_Development" title="International Conference on Population and Development">International Conference on Population and Development</a> (ICPD) in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Non-binding_resolution" title="Non-binding resolution">non-binding</a> Programme of Action asserted that governments have a responsibility to meet individuals' reproductive needs, rather than demographic targets. It recommended that <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> services be provided in the context of other reproductive health services, including services for healthy and safe childbirth, care for sexually transmitted infections, and post-abortion care. The ICPD also addressed issues such as <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_women" title="Violence against women">violence against women</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sex_trafficking" title="Sex trafficking">sex trafficking</a>, and adolescent health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudsen_2006_5–6_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knudsen_2006_5–6-11">[11]</a></sup> The Cairo Program is the first international policy document to define <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_health" title="Reproductive health">reproductive health</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudsen_2006_5–6_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knudsen_2006_5–6-11">[11]</a></sup> stating:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Reproductive health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity, in all matters relating to the reproductive system and its functions and processes. Reproductive health therefore implies that people are able to have a satisfying and safe sex life and that they have the capability to reproduce and the freedom to decide if, when and how often to do so. Implicit in this last condition are the right of men and women to be informed [about] and to have access to safe, effective, affordable and acceptable methods of family planning of their choice, as well as other methods for regulation of fertility which are not against the law, and the right of access to appropriate health-care services that will enable women to go safely through pregnancy and childbirth and provide couples with the best chance of having a healthy infant [para. 72].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COOK_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-COOK-1">[1]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Unlike previous population conferences, a wide range of interests from grassroots to government level were represented in Cairo. 179 nations attended the ICPD and overall eleven thousand representatives from governments, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a>, international agencies and citizen activists participated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudsen_2006_5–6_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Knudsen_2006_5–6-11">[11]</a></sup> The ICPD did not address the far-reaching implications of the <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology_of_HIV/AIDS" title="Epidemiology of HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS epidemic</a>. In 1999, recommendations at the ICPD+5 were expanded to include commitment to AIDS education, research, and prevention of mother-to-child transmission, as well as to the development of vaccines and microbicides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudsen_2006_7_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knudsen_2006_7-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Cairo Programme of Action was adopted by 184 UN member states. Nevertheless, many Latin American and Islamic states made <a href="/wiki/Reservation_(law)" title="Reservation (law)">formal reservations</a> to the programme, in particular, to its concept of reproductive rights and sexual freedom, to its treatment of abortion, and to its potential incompatibility with <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Implementation of the Cairo Programme of Action varies considerably from country to country. In many countries, post-ICPD tensions emerged as the <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>-based approach was implemented. Since the ICPD, many countries have broadened their reproductive health programs and attempted to integrate maternal and child health services with family planning. More attention is paid to adolescent health and the consequences of unsafe abortion. Lara Knudsen observes that the ICPD succeeded in getting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> language into governments' and population agencies' literature, but in many countries the underlying concepts are not widely put into practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudsen_2006_7_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knudsen_2006_7-12">[12]</a></sup> In two preparatory meetings for the ICPD+10 in Asia and Latin America, the United States, under the George W. Bush Administration, was the only nation opposing the ICPD's Programme of Action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beijing_Platform">Beijing Platform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Beijing Platform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1995 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_World_Conference_on_Women" title="Fourth World Conference on Women">Fourth World Conference on Women</a> in Beijing, in its <a href="/wiki/Non-binding_resolution" title="Non-binding resolution">non-binding</a> <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Declaration" title="Beijing Declaration">Declaration</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beijing_Platform_for_Action" title="Beijing Platform for Action">Platform for Action</a>, supported the Cairo Programme's definition of reproductive health, but established a broader context of reproductive rights:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The human rights of women include their right to have control over and decide freely and responsibly on matters related to their sexuality, including sexual and reproductive health, free of coercion, discrimination and violence. Equal relationships between women and men in matters of sexual relations and reproduction, including full respect for the integrity of the person, require mutual respect, consent and shared responsibility for sexual behavior and its consequences [para. 96].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COOK_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-COOK-1">[1]</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> The Beijing Platform demarcated twelve interrelated critical areas of the human rights of women that require advocacy. The Platform framed women's reproductive rights as "indivisible, universal and inalienable human rights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BUNCH_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BUNCH-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yogyakarta_Principles">Yogyakarta Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Yogyakarta Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta_Principles" title="Yogyakarta Principles">Yogyakarta Principles</a> on the Application of International Human Rights Law in relation to Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity, proposed by a group of experts in November 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YOGYABOUT_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-YOGYABOUT-16">[16]</a></sup> but not yet incorporated by States in international law,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> declares in its Preamble that "the <a href="/wiki/International_community" title="International community">international community</a> has recognized the rights of persons to decide freely and responsibly on matters related to their sexuality, including sexual and reproductive health, free from coercion, discrimination, and violence." In relation to reproductive health, Principle 9 on "The Right to Treatment with Humanity while in Detention" requires that "States shall... [p]rovide adequate access to medical care and counseling appropriate to the needs of those in custody, recognizing any particular needs of persons on the basis of their sexual orientation and gender identity, including with regard to reproductive health, access to <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> information and therapy and access to <a href="/wiki/Hormone_therapy" title="Hormone therapy">hormonal</a> or other therapy as well as to <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_therapy" title="Sex reassignment therapy">gender-reassignment treatments</a> where desired."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Nonetheless, African, Caribbean and Islamic Countries, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>, have objected to the use of these principles as Human Rights standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_abuses">State abuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: State abuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>State abuses against reproductive rights have happened both under right-wing and left-wing governments. Such abuses include attempts to forcefully increase the birth rate - one of the most notorious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natalist" title="Natalist">natalist</a> policies of the 20th century was that which occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" title="Communist Romania">communist Romania</a> in the period of 1967-1990 during communist leader <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceaușescu">Nicolae Ceaușescu</a>, who adopted a very aggressive natalist policy which included outlawing abortion and contraception, routine pregnancy tests for women, <a href="/wiki/Tax_on_childlessness" title="Tax on childlessness">taxes on childlessness</a>, and legal discrimination against childless people - as well as attempts to decrease the fertility rate - <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_child_policy" title="One child policy">one child policy</a> (1978-2015). State mandated <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> was also practiced by authoritarian governments as a way to meet population targets: the <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Rouge" title="Khmer Rouge">Khmer Rouge</a> regime in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> systematically forced people into marriages, in order to increase the population and continue the revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Some governments have implemented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugenic" title="Eugenic">eugenic</a> policies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_sterilizations" title="Forced sterilizations">forced sterilizations</a> of 'undesirable' population groups. Such policies were carried out against ethnic minorities in Europe and North America in the 20th century, and more recently in Latin America against the Indigenous population in the 1990s; in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Fujimori" title="Alberto Fujimori">Alberto Fujimori</a> (in office from 1990 to 2000) has been accused of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> as a result of a sterilization program put in place by his administration targeting indigenous people (mainly the <a href="/wiki/Quechua_people" title="Quechua people">Quechuas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aymara_people" title="Aymara people">Aymaras</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prohibition_of_forced_sterilization_and_forced_abortion">Prohibition of forced sterilization and forced abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prohibition of forced sterilization and forced abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_preventing_and_combating_violence_against_women_and_domestic_violence" title="Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence">Istanbul convention</a>, the first legally binding instrument in Europe in the field of violence against women and domestic violence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> prohibits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced_sterilization" title="Forced sterilization">forced sterilization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forced_abortion" title="Forced abortion">forced abortion</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Article 39 – Forced abortion and forced sterilisation
</p><dl><dd>Parties shall take the necessary legislative or other measures to ensure that the following intentional conducts are criminalised:</dd></dl>
<ul><li>a performing an abortion on a woman without her prior and informed consent;</li>
<li>b performing surgery which has the purpose or effect of terminating a woman’s capacity to naturally reproduce without her prior and informed consent or understanding of the procedure</li></ul>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights">Human rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Human rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Family_planning_Ethiopia_(good_effects).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3456" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG/220px-Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG/330px-Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG/440px-Family_planning_Ethiopia_%28good_effects%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Family_planning_Ethiopia_(good_effects).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Placard showing positive effects of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> (Ethiopia)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">Human rights</a></div><p>
Since most existing legally binding <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a> do not explicitly mention sexual and reproductive rights, a <a href="/wiki/Epistemic_community_(international_relations)" title="Epistemic community (international relations)">broad coalition</a> of NGOs, civil servants, and experts working in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_organizations" title="International organizations">international organizations</a> have been promoting a reinterpretation of those instruments to link the realization of the already internationally recognized human rights with the realization of reproductive rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> An example of this linkage is provided by the 1994 Cairo Programme of Action: </p><blockquote><p>
Reproductive rights embrace certain human rights that are already recognized in national laws, international human rights documents and other relevant United Nations consensus documents. These rights rest on the recognition of the basic right of all couples and individuals to decide freely and responsibly the number, spacing and timing of their children and to have the information and means to do so, and the right to attain the highest standard of sexual and reproductive health. It also includes the right of all to make decisions concerning reproduction free of discrimination, coercion and violence as expressed in human rights documents. In the exercise of this right, they should take into account the needs of their living and future children and their responsibilities towards the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> has argued that the realisation of reproductive rights is linked with the realisation of a series of recognised <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_health" title="Right to health">right to health</a>, the right to freedom from <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_privacy" title="Right to privacy">right to privacy</a>, and the right not to be subjected to torture or ill-treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMNESTY_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMNESTY-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> states that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p> "Sexual and reproductive health and rights encompass efforts to eliminate preventable maternal and neonatal mortality and morbidity, to ensure quality sexual and reproductive health services, including contraceptive services, and to address sexually transmitted infections (STI) and cervical cancer, violence against women and girls, and sexual and reproductive health needs of adolescents. Universal access to sexual and reproductive health is essential not only to achieve sustainable development but also to ensure that this new framework speaks to the needs and aspirations of people around the world and leads to realisation of their health and human rights."</p></blockquote><p>
However, not all states have accepted the inclusion of reproductive rights in the body of internationally recognized human rights. At the Cairo Conference, several states made <a href="/wiki/Reservation_(law)" title="Reservation (law)">formal reservations</a> either to the concept of reproductive rights or to its specific content.  <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, for instance, stated that: </p><blockquote><p>
With regard to the Programme of Action of the Cairo International Conference on Population and Development and in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution and laws of Ecuador and the norms of international law, the delegation of Ecuador reaffirms, inter alia, the following principles embodied in its Constitution:  the inviolability of life, the protection of children from the moment of conception, freedom of conscience and religion, the protection of the family as the fundamental unit of society, responsible paternity, the right of parents to bring up their children and the formulation of population and development plans by the Government in accordance with the principles of respect for sovereignty. Accordingly, the delegation of Ecuador enters a reservation with respect to all terms such as "regulation of fertility", "interruption of pregnancy", "reproductive health", "reproductive rights" and "unwanted children", which in one way or another, within the context of the Programme of Action, could involve abortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-un.org-13">[13]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Similar reservations were made by <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>. Islamic Countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> made broad reservations against any element of the programme that could be interpreted as contrary to the <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a>. Guatemala even questioned whether the conference could legally proclaim new human rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> (UNFPA) and the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) advocate for reproductive rights with a primary emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a>. In this respect the UN and WHO focus on a range of issues from access to <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> services, sex education, <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a>, and the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Obstetric_fistula" title="Obstetric fistula">obstetric fistula</a>, to the relationship between reproductive health and economic status.
</p><p>The reproductive rights of women are advanced in the context of the right to freedom from <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> and the social and economic status of women. The group Development Alternatives with Women for a New Era (DAWN) explained the link in the following statement:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Control over reproduction is a basic need and a basic right for all women. Linked as it is to women's health and social status, as well as the powerful social structures of religion, state control and administrative inertia, and private profit, it is from the perspective of poor women that this right can best be understood and affirmed. Women know that childbearing is a social, not a purely personal, phenomenon; nor do we deny that world population trends are likely to exert considerable pressure on resources and institutions by the end of this century. But our bodies have become a pawn in the struggles among states, religions, male heads of households, and private corporations. Programs that do not take the interests of women into account are unlikely to succeed...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FREEDMAN_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-FREEDMAN-4">[4]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Women's reproductive rights have long retained key issue status in the debate on <a href="/wiki/Overpopulation" title="Overpopulation">overpopulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsearch_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-popsearch-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The only ray of hope I can see – and it's not much – is that wherever women are put in control of their lives, both politically and socially; where medical facilities allow them to deal with birth control and where their husbands allow them to make those decisions, birth rate falls. Women don't want to have 12 kids of whom nine will die." <i><a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">David Attenborough</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></i></p></blockquote>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OHCHR" title="OHCHR">OHCHR</a>: "Women’s sexual and reproductive health is related to multiple human rights, including the right to life, the right to be free from torture, the right to health, the right to privacy, the right to education, and the prohibition of discrimination".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Attempts have been made to analyse the socioeconomic conditions that affect the realisation of a woman's reproductive rights. The term <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_justice" title="Reproductive justice">reproductive justice</a> has been used to describe these broader social and economic issues. Proponents of reproductive justice argue that while the right to <a href="/wiki/Abortion_law" title="Abortion law">legalized abortion</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and contraception applies to everyone, these choices are only meaningful to those with resources, and that there is a growing gap between access and affordability.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Men's reproductive rights have been claimed by various organizations, both for issues of reproductive health, and other rights related to sexual reproduction.
</p><p>Three international issues in men's reproductive health are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted diseases</a>, cancer and exposure to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxins" title="Toxins">toxins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NETWORK_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NETWORK-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recently men's reproductive right with regards to paternity have become subject of debate in the U.S.  The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Male_abortion" title="Male abortion">male abortion</a>" was coined by Melanie McCulley, a South Carolina attorney, in a 1998 article.  The theory begins with the premise that when a woman becomes pregnant she has the option of abortion, adoption, or parenthood; it argues, in the context of legally recognized <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>, that in the earliest stages of pregnancy the putative (alleged) father should have the right to relinquish all future parental rights and financial responsibility, leaving the informed mother with the same three options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This concept has been supported by a former president of the feminist organization <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a>, attorney <a href="/wiki/Karen_DeCrow" title="Karen DeCrow">Karen DeCrow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> argument for male reproductive choice contends that the uneven ability to choose experienced by men and women in regards to parenthood is evidence of a state-enforced <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercion</a> favoring traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_roles" title="Sex roles">sex roles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, the National Center for Men brought a case in the US, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dubay_v._Wells" title="Dubay v. Wells">Dubay v. Wells</a></i> (dubbed by some "<i><a href="/wiki/Roe_v._Wade" title="Roe v. Wade">Roe v. Wade</a></i> for men"), that argued that in the event of an unplanned pregnancy, when an unmarried woman informs a man that she is pregnant by him, he should have an opportunity to give up all paternity rights and responsibilities.  Supporters argue that this would allow the woman time to make an informed decision and give men the same reproductive rights as women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  In its dismissal of the case, the U.S. Court of Appeals (Sixth Circuit) stated that "the Fourteenth Amendment does not deny to [the] State the power to treat different classes of persons in different ways."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The opportunity to give men the right for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_Abortion" title="Paper Abortion">Paper Abortion</a> is heavily discussed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intersex_and_reproductive_rights">Intersex and reproductive rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Intersex and reproductive rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">Intersex</a></div>
<p>Intersex, in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, is a variation in <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">sex</a> characteristics including <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gonad" title="Gonad">gonads</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genital" title="Genital">genitals</a> that do not allow an individual to be distinctly identified as male or female. Such variation may involve genital ambiguity, and combinations of chromosomal <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a> and sexual <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> other than XY-male and XX-female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Money_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Money-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dreger_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dreger-40">[40]</a></sup> Intersex persons are often subjected to involuntary "sex normalizing" surgical and hormonal treatments in infancy and childhood, often also including sterilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coe1952_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-coe1952-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SenateOnSterilisation_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-SenateOnSterilisation-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swissnek_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-swissnek-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>UN agencies have begun to take note. On 1 February 2013, Juan E Mendés, the UN Special Rapporteur on torture and other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment, issued a statement condemning non-consensual surgical intervention on intersex people. His report stated, "Children who are born with atypical sex characteristics are often subject to irreversible sex assignment, involuntary sterilization, involuntary genital normalizing surgery, performed without their informed consent, or that of their parents, "in an attempt to fix their sex", leaving them with permanent, irreversible infertility and causing severe mental suffering".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-untorture_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-untorture-46">[46]</a></sup> In May 2014, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> issued a joint statement on <i>Eliminating forced, coercive and otherwise involuntary sterilization, An interagency statement</i> with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OHCHR" title="OHCHR">OHCHR</a>, <a href="/wiki/UN_Women" title="UN Women">UN Women</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNAIDS" title="UNAIDS">UNAIDS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNDP" title="UNDP">UNDP</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFPA" title="UNFPA">UNFPA</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a>. The report references the involuntary surgical "sex-normalising or other procedures" on "intersex persons". It questions the medical necessity of such treatments, patients' ability to consent, and a weak evidence base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The report recommends a range of guiding principles to prevent <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_sterilization" title="Compulsory sterilization">compulsory sterilization</a> in medical treatment, including ensuring patient autonomy in decision-making, ensuring non-discrimination, accountability and access to remedies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth_rights_and_access">Youth rights and access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Youth rights and access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">Sex education</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minors">Minors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Minors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Parental_consent" title="Parental consent">Parental consent</a></div>
<p>In many jurisdictions minors require parental consent or parental notification in order to access various reproductive services, such as contraception, abortion, gynecological consultations,  testing for STDs etc. The requirement that minors have parental consent/notification for testing for <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> is especially controversial, particularly in areas where the disease is endemic, and it is a sensitive subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Balancing minors' rights versus parental rights is considered an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethical" title="Ethical">ethical</a> problem in medicine and law, and there have been many court cases on this issue in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> An important concept recognized since 1989 by the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> is that of the <a href="/wiki/Evolving_capacities" title="Evolving capacities">
evolving capacities of a minor</a>, namely that minors should, in accordance with their maturity and level of understanding, be involved in decisions that affect them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Youth" title="Youth">Youth</a> are often denied equal access to reproductive health services because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_workers" title="Health workers">health workers</a> view adolescent sexual activity as unacceptable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mugisha_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mugisha-54">[54]</a></sup> or see sex education as the responsibility of parents.  Providers of reproductive health have little accountability to youth clients, a primary factor in denying youth access to reproductive health care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mugisha_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mugisha-54">[54]</a></sup> In many countries, regardless of legislation, minors are denied even the most basic reproductive care, if they are not accompanied by parents: in India, for instance, in 2017, a 17-year-old girl who was rejected by her family due to her pregnancy, was also rejected by hospitals and gave birth in the street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In recent years the lack of reproductive rights for adolescents has been a concern of international organizations, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFPA" title="UNFPA">UNFPA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mandatory involvement of parents in cases where the minor has sufficient maturity to understand their situation is considered by health organization as a violation of minor's rights and detrimental to their health. The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> has criticized parental consent/notification laws:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Discrimination in health care settings takes many forms and is often manifested when an individual or group is denied access to health care services that are otherwise available to others. It can also occur through denial of services that are only needed by certain groups, such as women. Examples include specific individuals or groups being subjected to physical and verbal abuse or violence; involuntary treatment; <b>breaches of confidentiality and/or denial of autonomous decision-making, such as the requirement of consent to treatment by parents, spouses or guardians</b>; and lack of free and informed consent. [...] Laws and policies must respect the principles of autonomy in health care decision-making; guarantee free and informed consent, privacy and confidentiality; prohibit mandatory HIV testing; prohibit screening procedures that are not of benefit to the individual or the public; and ban involuntary treatment and <b>mandatory third-party authorization and notification requirements</b>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>According to UNICEF: "When dealing with sexual and reproductive health, the obligation
to inform parents and obtain their consent becomes a significant barrier with consequences for adolescents’ lives and for public health in general."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnicefBAJA_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-UnicefBAJA-58">[58]</a></sup> One specific issue which is seen as a form of hypocrisy of legislators is that of having a higher age of medical consent for the purpose of reproductive and sexual health than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_sexual_consent" title="Age of sexual consent">age of sexual consent</a> - in such cases the law allows youth to engage in sexual activity, but does not allow them to consent to medical procedures that may arise from being sexually active; UNICEF states that "On sexual and reproductive health matters, the minimum age of medical consent should never be higher than the age of sexual consent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UnicefBAJA_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-UnicefBAJA-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg/220px-No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg/330px-No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg/440px-No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:No_Sex_Signage_in_Ghana.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ad promoting abstinence in <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>: <i>No Sex Ad</i> (Anti-<a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> ― <a href="/wiki/Signage" title="Signage">signage</a>). <a href="/wiki/Abstinence-only_sex_education" title="Abstinence-only sex education">Abstinence-only sex education</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a> that teaches <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abstinence" title="Sexual abstinence">not having sex</a> outside of marriage, most often excluding other types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_and_reproductive_health" title="Sexual and reproductive health">sexual and reproductive health</a> education, such as <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> and <a href="/wiki/Safe_sex" title="Safe sex">safe sex</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Sex_Education" title="Comprehensive Sex Education">Comprehensive sex education</a>, by contrast, covers the use of birth control and sexual abstinence.</div></div></div>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unintended_pregnancies" title="Unintended pregnancies">unintended pregnancies</a> stem from traditional contraceptive methods or no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraceptive" title="Contraceptive">contraceptive</a> measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukale_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lukale-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Youth sexual education in Uganda is relatively low.  <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_sex_education" title="Comprehensive sex education">Comprehensive sex education</a> is not generally taught in schools; even if it was, the majority of young people do not stay in school after the age of fifteen, so information would be limited regardless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> experiences high rates of unintended pregnancy, along with high rates of <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a>.  Young women aged 15–24 are eight times more likely to have HIV/AIDS than young men. <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> is the world region most affected by HIV/AIDS, with approximately 25 million people living with HIV in 2015. Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for two-thirds of the global total of new HIV infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Attempted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abortions" title="Abortions">abortions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsafe_abortions" title="Unsafe abortions">unsafe abortions</a> are a risk for youth in Africa.  On average, there are 2.4 million unsafe abortions in East Africa, 1.8 million in Western Africa, over 900,000 in Middle Africa, and over 100,000 in Southern Africa each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lukale_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lukale-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, abortion is illegal except to save the mother's life.  However, 78% of teenagers report knowing someone who has had an abortion and the police do not always prosecute everyone who has an abortion.  An estimated 22% of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maternal_deaths" title="Maternal deaths">maternal deaths</a> in the area stem from illegal, unsafe abortions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 85% of European women (all ages) have used some form of <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> in their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irala_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irala-62">[62]</a></sup>  Europeans as an aggregate report using <a href="/wiki/Oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Oral contraceptive pill">the pill</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condoms" title="Condoms">condoms</a> as the most commonly used contraceptives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irala_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irala-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">Family planning</a> has become prominent throughout the region and most taboos concerning sexuality have been lifted or diminished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketting_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ketting-63">[63]</a></sup>  Youth sexual and reproductive health centers have been established across most of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketting_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ketting-63">[63]</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, approximately 80% of girls and 17% of boys have visited these youth centers, which provide all or nearly all services youth need at little to no charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketting_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ketting-63">[63]</a></sup>  Sweden has the highest percentage of lifetime contraceptive use, with 96% of its inhabitants claiming to have used birth control at some point in their life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irala_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-Irala-62">[62]</a></sup> Sweden also has a high self-reported rate of <a href="/wiki/Emergency_contraception" title="Emergency contraception">postcoital pill use</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irala_62-3"><a href="#cite_note-Irala-62">[62]</a></sup>  A 2007 anonymous survey of Swedish 18-year-olds showed that three out of four youth were sexually active, with 5% reporting having had an <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> and 4% reporting the contraction of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">STI</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larsson_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larsson-64">[64]</a></sup> Similar centers exist in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketting_63-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ketting-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Views on sexual practice vary throughout the region.  For example, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (UK), sex among youth is generally looked down upon and seen as a problem in need of solution.  In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, sex between youth is viewed as normal and therefore not discussed in terms of solutions, but rather in terms of ensuring safe practices.  That being said, the UK tends to focus on stopping sexual behavior, while the Netherlands focuses on building self-esteem and healthy relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketting_63-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ketting-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_rights_in_Latin_America" title="Reproductive rights in Latin America">Reproductive rights in Latin America</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> has come to international attention due to its harsh anti-abortion laws. Latin America is home to some of the few countries of the world with a complete ban on abortion, without an exception for saving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" title="Maternal mortality">maternal life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChileCourt_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChileCourt-65">[65]</a></sup> In some of these countries, particularity in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, the enforcement of such laws is very aggressive: <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_El_Salvador" title="Abortion in El Salvador">El Salvador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Nicaragua" title="Abortion in Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> have drawn international attention for strong enforcement of their complete bans on abortion. In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Chile" title="Abortion in Chile">Chile</a> relaxed its total ban, allowing abortion to be performed when the woman’s life is in danger, when a fetus is unviable, or in cases of rape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, education and class play a large role in the definition of which young women become pregnant and which do not - 50% of young women who are illiterate get pregnant, compared to 11% of girls with secondary education.  The same is true for poorer individuals - 28% become impregnated while only 11% of young women in wealthier households do.  Furthermore, access to reproductive rights, including contraceptives, are limited, due to age and the perception of female morality.  Health care providers often discuss contraception theoretically, not as a device to be used on a regular basis.  Decisions concerning sexual activity often involve secrecy and taboos, as well as a lack of access to accurate information.  Even more telling, young women have much easier access to maternal healthcare than they do to contraceptive help, which helps explain high pregnancy rates in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goicolea_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goicolea-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolescent_pregnancy" title="Adolescent pregnancy">adolescent pregnancy</a> in Latin America number over a million each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goicolea_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goicolea-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Birth_control_in_the_United_States" title="Birth control in the United States">Birth control in the United States</a></div>
<p>Among sexually experienced teenagers, 78% of teenage females and 85% of teenage males used contraception the first time they had sex; 86% and 93% of these same females and males, respectively, reported using contraception the last time they had sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_Guttmacher_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet_Guttmacher-68">[68]</a></sup>  The male <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condom</a> is the most commonly used method during first sex, although 54% of young women in the United States rely upon the <a href="/wiki/Combined_oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Combined oral contraceptive pill">pill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fact_Sheet_Guttmacher_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fact_Sheet_Guttmacher-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Young people in the U.S. are no more sexually active than individuals in other developed countries, but they are significantly less knowledgeable about contraception and safe sex practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup> As of 2006, only twenty states required <a href="/wiki/Sex_education" title="Sex education">sex education</a> in schools - of these, only ten required information about contraception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup>  On the whole, less than 10% of American students receive sex education that includes topical coverage of <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intimate_relationships" title="Intimate relationships">relationships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, and STI prevention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-4"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstinence-only_education" title="Abstinence-only education">Abstinence-only education</a> was used throughout much of the United States in the 1990s and early 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-5"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup>  Based upon the moral principle that sex outside of marriage is unacceptable, the programs often misled students about their rights to have sex, the consequences, and prevention of pregnancy and STIs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knudson_60-6"><a href="#cite_note-Knudson-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_the_United_States" title="Abortion in the United States">Abortion in the United States</a> is legal since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> decision <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_v._Wade" title="Roe v. Wade">Roe v. Wade</a></i> which decriminalised abortion nationwide in 1973, and established a minimal period during which abortion is legal (with more or fewer restrictions throughout the pregnancy). That basic framework, modified in <i><a href="/wiki/Planned_Parenthood_v._Casey" title="Planned Parenthood v. Casey">Planned Parenthood v. Casey</a></i> (1992), remains nominally in place, although the effective availability of abortion varies significantly from state to state, as many counties have no abortion providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doan3_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doan3-69">[69]</a></sup> <i>Planned Parenthood v. Casey</i> held that a law cannot place legal restrictions imposing an <a href="/wiki/Undue_burden_standard" title="Undue burden standard">undue burden</a> for "the purpose or effect of placing a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion of a nonviable fetus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Abortion is a controversial political issue, and regular attempts to restrict it occur in most states. One such case, originating in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, led to the Supreme Court case of <i><a href="/wiki/Whole_Woman%27s_Health_v._Hellerstedt" title="Whole Woman's Health v. Hellerstedt">Whole Woman's Health v. Hellerstedt</a></i> (2016) in which several Texas restrictions were struck down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_knowledge_about_rights">Lack of knowledge about rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lack of knowledge about rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the reasons why reproductive rights are poor in many places, is that the vast majority of the population does not know what the law is. Not only are ordinary people uninformed, but so are medical doctors. A study in Brazil on medical doctors found considerable ignorance and misunderstanding of the law on abortion (which is severely restricted, but not completely illegal).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> In Ghana, abortion, while restricted, is permitted on several grounds, but only 3% of pregnant women and 6% of those seeking an abortion were aware of the legal status of abortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In Nepal, abortion was legalized in 2002, but a study in 2009 found that only half of women knew that abortion was legalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Many people also do not understand the laws on <a href="/wiki/Sexual_violence" title="Sexual violence">sexual violence</a>: in Hungary, where <a href="/wiki/Marital_rape" title="Marital rape">marital rape</a> was made illegal in 1997, in a study in 2006, 62% of people did not know that marital rape was a crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> The United Nations Development Programme states that, in order to advance gender justice, "Women must know their rights and be able to access legal systems",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> and the 1993 UN Declaration on the Elimination of Violence Against Women states at Art. 4 (d) [...] "States should also inform women of their rights in seeking redress through such mechanisms".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_equality_and_violence_against_women">Gender equality and violence against women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Gender equality and violence against women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">Gender equality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_women" title="Violence against women">Violence against women</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_violence" title="Sexual violence">Sexual violence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">Forced marriage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">Child marriage</a></div>
<p>Addressing issues of gender-based violence is crucial for attaining reproductive rights. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> refers to "<i><b>Equality and equity for men and women</b>, to enable individuals to make free and informed choices in all spheres of life, free from discrimination based on gender</i>" and "<i>Sexual and reproductive security, <b>including freedom from sexual violence and coercion</b>, and the right to privacy,</i>" as part of achieving reproductive rights,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> and states that the <b>right to liberty and security of the person</b> which is fundamental to reproductive rights obliges states to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Take measures to prevent, punish and eradicate all forms of gender-based violence</li>
<li>Eliminate female genital mutilation/cutting</li></ul>
<p>The WHO states:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>"Gender and Reproductive Rights (GRR) aims to promote and protect human rights and gender equality as they relate to sexual and reproductive health by developing strategies and mechanisms for promoting gender equity and equality and human rights in the Departments global and national activities, as well as within the functioning and priority-setting of the Department itself."</dd></dl>
<p>Amnesty International writes that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Violence against women violates women's rights to life, physical and mental integrity, to the highest attainable standard of health, to freedom from torture and it violates their sexual and reproductive rights."</dd></dl>
<p>One key issue for achieving reproductive rights is criminalization of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_violence" title="Sexual violence">sexual violence</a>. If a woman is not protected from forced sexual intercourse, she is not protected from <a href="/wiki/Forced_pregnancy" title="Forced pregnancy">forced pregnancy</a>, namely <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy_from_rape" title="Pregnancy from rape">pregnancy from rape</a>. In order for a woman to be able to have reproductive rights, she must have the right to choose with whom and when to reproduce; and first of all, decide whether, when, and under what circumstances to be sexually active.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> In many countries, these rights of women are not respected, because women do not have a choice in regard to their partner, with <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">child marriage</a> being common in parts of the world; and neither do they have any rights in regard to sexual activity, as many countries do not allow women to refuse to engage in sexual intercourse when they do not want to (because <a href="/wiki/Marital_rape" title="Marital rape">marital rape</a> is not criminalized in those countries) or to engage in consensual sexual intercourse if they want to (because sex outside marriage is illegal in those countries). In addition to legal barriers, there are also social barriers, because in many countries a complete sexual subordination of a woman to her husband is expected (for instance, in one survey 74% of women in Mali said that a husband is justified to beat his wife if she refuses to have sex with him<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>), while sexual/romantic relations disapproved by family members, or generally sex outside marriage, can result in serious violence, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honor_killings" title="Honor killings">honor killings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>According to the CDC, "HIV stands for human immunodeficiency virus. It weakens a person’s immune system by destroying important cells that fight disease and infection. No effective cure exists for HIV. But with proper medical care, HIV can be controlled."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>  HIV amelioration is an important aspect of reproductive rights because the virus can be transmitted from mother to child during pregnancy or birth, or via breast milk.
</p><p>The WHO states that: "All women, including those with HIV, have the right “to decide freely and responsibly on the number and spacing of their children and to have access to the information, education and means to enable them to exercise these rights”".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-87">[87]</a></sup> The reproductive rights of people living with HIV, and their health, are very important. The link between HIV and reproductive rights exists in regard to four main issues:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>prevention of unwanted pregnancy</li>
<li>help to plan wanted pregnancy</li>
<li>healthcare during and after pregnancy</li>
<li>access to abortion services</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_and_forced_marriage">Child and forced marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Child and forced marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">Child marriage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">Forced marriage</a></div>
<p>The WHO states that the reproductive rights and health of girls in child marriages are negatively affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">UNPF</a> calls child marriage a "human rights violation" and states that in developing countries, one in every three girls is married before reaching age 18, and one in nine is married under age 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> is a marriage in which one or more of the parties is married without his or her consent or against his or her will. The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_preventing_and_combating_violence_against_women_and_domestic_violence" title="Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence">Istanbul convention</a>, the first legally binding instrument in Europe in the field of violence against women and domestic violence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> requires countries which ratify it to prohibit <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> (Article 37) and to ensure that forced marriages can be easily voided without further victimization (Article 32).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_violence_in_armed_conflict">Sexual violence in armed conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Sexual violence in armed conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Wartime_sexual_violence" title="Wartime sexual violence">Wartime sexual violence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy_from_rape" title="Pregnancy from rape">Pregnancy from rape</a></div>
<p>Sexual violence in armed conflict is <a href="/wiki/Sexual_violence" title="Sexual violence">sexual violence</a> committed by <a href="/wiki/Combatant" title="Combatant">combatants</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflict" title="Armed conflict">armed conflict</a>, war, or <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">military occupation</a> often as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_looting" title="War looting">spoils of war</a>; but sometimes, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_conflict" title="Ethnic conflict">ethnic conflict</a>, the phenomenon has broader sociological motives. It often includes <a href="/wiki/Gang_rape" title="Gang rape">gang rape</a>. Rape is often used as a tactic of war and a threat to international security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Sexual violence in armed conflict is a violation of reproductive rights, and often leads to <a href="/wiki/Forced_pregnancy" title="Forced pregnancy">forced pregnancy</a>  and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infections" title="Sexually transmitted infections">sexually transmitted infections</a>. Such sexual violations affect mostly women and girls,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rape_of_men" title="Rape of men">rape of men</a> can also occur, such as in <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Melhado_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Melhado-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Autesserre_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Autesserre-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maternal_mortality">Maternal mortality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Maternal mortality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maternal_death" title="Maternal death">Maternal death</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="1800" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/300px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/450px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg/600px-Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maternal_mortality_rate_worldwide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maternal_Mortality_Rate" title="Maternal Mortality Rate">Maternal Mortality Rate</a> worldwide, as defined by the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births from any cause related to or aggravated by pregnancy or its management, excluding accidental or incidental causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CIA2010_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-CIA2010-96">[96]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Maternal death is defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) as "the death of a woman while pregnant or within 42 days of termination of <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>, irrespective of the duration and site of the pregnancy, from any cause related to or aggravated by the pregnancy or its management but not from accidental or incidental causes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> It is estimated that in 2015, about 303,000 women died during and following pregnancy and childbirth, and 99% of such deaths occur in developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_control">Birth control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Birth control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Forced_pregnancy" title="Forced pregnancy">Forced pregnancy</a></div>
<p>Birth control, also known as contraception and fertility control, is a method or device used to prevent <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Birth control has been used since ancient times, but effective and safe methods of birth control only became available in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-100">[100]</a></sup> Planning, making available, and using birth control is called <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OED2012_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-OED2012-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO-health-topic_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO-health-topic-102">[102]</a></sup> Some cultures limit or discourage access to birth control because they consider it to be morally, religiously, or politically undesirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins2010_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins2010-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>All birth control methods meet opposition, especially <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> opposition, in some parts of the world. Opposition does not only target modern methods, but also 'traditional' ones : for example, the <a href="/wiki/Quiverfull" title="Quiverfull">Quiverfull</a> movement, a conservative Christian ideology, encourages the maximization of procreation, and opposes all forms of birth control, including <a href="/wiki/Natural_family_planning" title="Natural family planning">natural family planning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abortion">Abortion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Abortion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Abortion_law" title="Abortion law">Abortion law</a></div>
<p>According to a study by WHO and the Guttmacher Institute worldwide, 25 million <a href="/wiki/Unsafe_abortion" title="Unsafe abortion">unsafe abortions</a> (45% of all abortions) occurred every year between 2010 and 2014. 97% of unsafe abortions occur in developing countries in Africa, Asia and Latin America. By contrast, most abortions that take place in Western and Northern Europe and North America are safe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Committee_on_the_Elimination_of_Discrimination_against_Women" title="Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women">Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women</a> considers the criminalization of abortion a "violations of women’s sexual and reproductive health and rights" and a form of "gender based violence"; paragraph 18 of its <i>General recommendation No. 35 on gender based violence against women, updating general recommendation No. 19</i> states that: "Violations of women’s sexual and reproductive health and rights, such as forced sterilizations, forced abortion, forced pregnancy, criminalisation of abortion, denial or delay of  safe  abortion  and  post abortion  care,  forced  continuation  of pregnancy,  abuse  and mistreatment  of  women  and  girls  seeking  sexual  and  reproductive  health  information, goods and services, are forms of <a href="/wiki/Violence_against_women" title="Violence against women">gender based violence</a> that, depending on   the circumstances, may amount to torture or cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tbinternet.ohchr.org_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-tbinternet.ohchr.org-106">[106]</a></sup> The same <i>General Recommendation</i> also urges countries at paragraph 31 to [...] In particular, repeal:
a) Provisions  that  allow,  tolerate  or  condone  forms  of  gender based  violence against women, including [...] legislation  that  criminalises  abortion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tbinternet.ohchr.org_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-tbinternet.ohchr.org-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>An article from the World Health Organization calls safe, legal abortion a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_right" title="Fundamental right">fundamental right</a> of women, irrespective of where they live" and <a href="/wiki/Unsafe_abortion" title="Unsafe abortion">unsafe abortion</a> a "silent <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> The article states "ending the silent pandemic of unsafe abortion is an urgent <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public-health</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-rights" title="Human-rights">human-rights</a> imperative." It also states "access to safe abortion improves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%E2%80%99s_health" title="Women’s health">women’s health</a>, and vice versa, as documented in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> during the regime of President <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceaușescu">Nicolae Ceaușescu</a>" and "legalisation of abortion on request is a necessary but insufficient step toward improving women’s health" citing that in some countries, such as India where abortion has been legal for decades, access to competent care remains restricted because of other barriers. WHO’s Global Strategy on Reproductive Health, adopted by the World Health Assembly in May 2004, noted: “As a preventable cause of maternal mortality and morbidity, unsafe abortion must be dealt with as part of the MDG on improving maternal health and other international development goals and targets." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-108">[108]</a></sup> The WHO's Development and Research Training in Human Reproduction (HRP), whose research concerns people's sexual and reproductive health and lives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> has an overall strategy to combat unsafe abortion that comprises four inter-related activities:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>to collate, synthesize and generate scientifically sound evidence on unsafe abortion prevalence and practices;</li>
<li>to develop improved technologies and implement interventions to make abortion safer;</li>
<li>to translate evidence into norms, tools and guidelines;</li>
<li>and to assist in the development of programmes and policies that reduce unsafe abortion and improve access to safe abortion and highquality postabortion care</li></ul>
<p>The UN has estimated in 2017 that repealing anti-abortion laws would save the lives of nearly 50,000 women a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Unsafe abortions take place primarily in countries where abortion is illegal, but also occur in countries where it is legal, but women cannot access it because of various reasons (<a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objectors</a> among doctors, high prices, lack of knowledge that abortion is legal). Indeed, there are countries where the law  is liberal, but in practice it is very difficult to have an abortion, due to most doctors being  conscientious objectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> The fact that is some countries where abortion is legal it is <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> very difficult to have access to one is controversial; the UN in its 2017 resolution on <i>Intensification of efforts to prevent and eliminate all forms of violence against women and girls: domestic violence</i> urged states to guarantee access to "safe abortion where such services are permitted by national law".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Safe and legal abortion services are often very difficult to access by women from rural areas or from lower socioeconomic backgrounds. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch" title="Human Rights Watch">Human Rights Watch</a> stated that "In fact, even where abortion is permitted by law, women often have severely limited access to safe abortion services because of lack of proper regulation, health services, or political will" and estimated that "Approximately 13 percent of maternal deaths worldwide are attributable to unsafe abortion—between 68,000 and 78,000 deaths annually."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Maputo_Protocol" title="Maputo Protocol">Maputo Protocol</a>, which was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> in the form of a protocol to the <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights</a>, states at  Article 14 (Health and Reproductive Rights) that: "(2). States Parties shall take all appropriate measures to: [...] c) protect the reproductive rights of women by authorising medical abortion in cases of sexual assault, rape, incest, and where the continued pregnancy endangers the mental and physical health of the mother or the life of the mother or the foetus." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> The Maputo Protocol is the first international treaty to recognize abortion, under certain conditions, as a woman's human right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p><p>The  <i>General comment No. 36 (2018) on article 6 of the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a>, on the right to life</i>, adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Committee" title="Human Rights Committee">Human Rights Committee</a> in 2018, defines, for the first time ever, a human right to abortion - in certain circumstances (however these UN general comments are considered <a href="/wiki/Soft_law" title="Soft law">soft law</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> and, as such, not legally binding).
</p>
<blockquote><p>"Although   States   parties   may  adopt measures designed to regulate voluntary terminations of pregnancy, such measures must not result in violation of the right to life of a pregnant woman or girl, or her other rights under the Covenant. Thus, restrictions on the ability of women or girls to seek abortion must not, inter alia, jeopardize their lives, subject them  to  physical  or  mental  pain  or  suffering  which violates  article  7,  discriminate  against them  or  arbitrarily  interfere  with  their  privacy.  <i>States  parties  must  provide  safe,  legal  and effective  access  to  abortion where  the  life  and  health  of  the  pregnant  woman  or girl  is  at risk,  and  where  carrying a pregnancy to term  would cause  the  pregnant  woman  or  girl substantial  pain  or  suffering,  most  notably  where  the  pregnancy  is  the  result  of  rape  or incest or is not viable.</i> [8] In addition, States parties may not regulate pregnancy or abortion in all other cases in a manner that runs contrary to their duty to ensure that women and girls do  not  have  to  undertake  unsafe  abortions,  and  they should  revise  their  abortion  laws accordingly.  [9]  For  example,  they  should  not  take measures  such  as  criminalizing pregnancies  by  unmarried  women  or  apply  criminal  sanctions  against  women  and  girls undergoing  abortion  [10]  or  against  medical  service providers  assisting  them  in  doing  so, since  taking  such  measures  compel  women  and  girls  to  resort  to  unsafe  abortion.  States parties should not introduce new barriers and should remove existing barriers [11] that deny effective  access  by  women  and  girls  to  safe  and  legal  abortion  [12],  including  barriers caused  as  a  result  of  the  exercise  of  conscientious objection  by  individual  medical providers. [13]"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>When negotiating the Cairo Programme of Action at the 1994 <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_on_Population_and_Development" title="International Conference on Population and Development">International Conference on Population and Development</a> (ICPD), the issue was so contentious that delegates eventually decided to omit any recommendation to legalize abortion, instead advising governments to provide proper post-abortion care and to invest in programs that will decrease the number of unwanted pregnancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 18, 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Assembly_of_the_Council_of_Europe" title="Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe">Parliamentary Assembly of the Council of Europe</a>, a group comprising members from 47 European countries, adopted a resolution calling for the decriminalization of abortion within reasonable gestational limits and guaranteed access to safe abortion procedures. The nonbinding resolution was passed on April 16 by a vote of 102 to 69.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p><p>During and after the ICPD, some interested parties attempted to interpret the term ‘reproductive health’ in the sense that it implies abortion as a means of family planning or, indeed, a right to abortion. These interpretations, however, do not reflect the consensus reached at the Conference.
For the European Union, where legislation on abortion is certainly less restrictive than elsewhere, the Council Presidency has clearly stated that the Council’s commitment to promote ‘reproductive health’ did not include the promotion of abortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>  Likewise, the European Commission, in response to a question from a Member of the European Parliament, clarified:
</p><p><i>“The term ‘reproductive health’ was defined by the United Nations (UN) in 1994 at the Cairo International Conference on Population and Development. All Member States of the Union endorsed the Programme of Action adopted at Cairo. The Union has never adopted an alternative definition of ‘reproductive health’ to that given in the Programme of Action, <b>which makes no reference to abortion</b>.”</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>With regard to the U.S., it should be noted that, only a few days prior to the Cairo Conference, the head of the U.S. delegation, Vice President Al Gore, had stated for the record:
</p><p><i>“Let us get a false issue off the table: the US does not seek to establish a new international right to abortion, and we do not believe that abortion should be encouraged as a method of family planning.”</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some years later, the position of the U.S. Administration in this debate was reconfirmed by U.S. Ambassador to the UN, Ellen Sauerbrey, when she stated at a meeting of the UN Commission on the Status of Women that: <i>“nongovernmental organizations are attempting to assert that Beijing  in some way creates or contributes to the creation of an internationally recognized fundamental right to abortion”</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> She added: <i>“There is no fundamental right to abortion. And yet it keeps coming up largely driven by NGOs trying to hijack the term and trying to make it into a definition”</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p><p>Collaborative research from the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Development_Studies" title="Institute of Development Studies">Institute of Development Studies</a> states that "access to safe abortion is a matter of human rights, democracy and public health, and the denial of such access is a major cause of death and impairment, with significant costs to [international] development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDS2009_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-IDS2009-126">[126]</a></sup> The research highlights the inequities of access to safe abortion both globally and nationally and emphasises the importance of global and national movements for reform to address this. The shift by campaigners of reproductive rights from an issue-based agenda (the right to abortion), to safe, legal abortion not only as a human right, but bound up with democratic and citizenship rights, has been an important way of reframing the abortion debate and reproductive justice agenda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDS2009_126-1"><a href="#cite_note-IDS2009-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p>Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> complicated the question even more through a landmark judgment (case of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._B._and_C._v._Ireland" title="A. B. and C. v. Ireland">A. B. and C. v. Ireland</a></i>), in which it is stated that the denial of abortion for health and/or well-being reasons is an interference with an individuals right to respect for private and family life under Article 8 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, an interference which in some cases can be justified.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_control">Population control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Population control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Population_control" title="Population control">Population control</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="1267" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg/250px-Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg/375px-Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg/500px-Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceausescu_Anul_Nou.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceaușescu">Nicolae Ceaușescu</a>, Romanian communist leader, enacted one of the most infamous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natalist" title="Natalist">natalist</a> policies of the 20th century</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg/250px-Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg/375px-Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg/500px-Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Danshan_Nongguang_Village_Bulletin_board.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A community bulletin board in Nonguang Village, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sichuan_province" title="Sichuan province">Sichuan province</a>, China, keeping track of the town's female population, listing recent births by name and noting that several thousand yuan of fines for unauthorized births remain unpaid from the previous year.</div></div></div>
<p>A desire to achieve certain population targets has resulted throughout history in severely abusive practices, in cases where governments ignored human rights and enacted aggressive demographic policies. In the 20th century, several authoritarian governments have sought either to increase or to decrease the births rates, often through forceful intervention. One of the most notorious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natalist" title="Natalist">natalist</a> policies is that which occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" title="Communist Romania">communist Romania</a> in the period of 1967-1990 during communist leader <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceaușescu">Nicolae Ceaușescu</a>, who adopted a very aggressive natalist policy which included outlawing abortion and contraception, routine pregnancy tests for women, <a href="/wiki/Tax_on_childlessness" title="Tax on childlessness">taxes on childlessness</a>, and legal discrimination against childless people.  Ceaușescu's policy resulted in over 9,000 women who died due to <a href="/wiki/Illegal_abortion" title="Illegal abortion">illegal abortions</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kligman_short_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kligman_short-127">[127]</a></sup> large numbers of children put into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_orphanages" title="Romanian orphanages">Romanian orphanages</a> by parents who couldn't cope with raising them, <a href="/wiki/Street_children" title="Street children">street children</a> in the 1990s (when many orphanages were closed and the children ended on the streets), and <a href="/wiki/Overcrowding" title="Overcrowding">overcrowding</a> in homes and schools. The irony of Ceaușescu's aggressive natalist policy was a generation that may not have been born would eventually lead the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Revolution" title="Romanian Revolution">Romanian Revolution</a> which would overthrow and have him <a href="/wiki/Trial_of_Nicolae_and_Elena_Ceau%C8%99escu#Execution" title="Trial of Nicolae and Elena Ceaușescu">executed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p><p>In stark opposition with Ceaușescu's natalist policy was China's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One_child_policy" title="One child policy">one child policy</a>, in effect from 1978 to 2015, which included abuses such as <a href="/wiki/Forced_abortion" title="Forced abortion">forced abortions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> This policy has also been deemed responsible for the common practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_selective_abortion" title="Sex selective abortion">sex selective abortion</a> which led to an imbalanced <a href="/wiki/Sex_ratio" title="Sex ratio">sex ratio</a> in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bulte,_Heerink,_&amp;_Zhang,_2011_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bulte,_Heerink,_&amp;_Zhang,_2011-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p>From the 1970s to 1980s, tension grew between women's health activists who advance women's reproductive rights as part of a human rights-based approach on the one hand, and population control advocates on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> At the 1984 UN World Population Conference in Mexico City population control policies came under attack from women's health advocates who argued that the policies' narrow focus led to coercion and decreased quality of care, and that these policies ignored the varied social and cultural contexts in which family planning was provided in developing countries. In the 1980s the HIV/AIDS epidemic forced a broader discussion of sex into the public discourse in many countries, leading to more emphasis on reproductive health issues beyond reducing fertility. The growing opposition to the narrow population control focus led to a significant departure in the early 1990s from past population control policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>  In the United States, abortion opponents have begun to foment <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">conspiracy theories</a> about reproductive rights advocates, accusing them of advancing a <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a> agenda of <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a>, and of trying to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> birth rate in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dewan_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dewan-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_genital_mutilation">Female genital mutilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Female genital mutilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1248" data-file-width="1548" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg/200px-FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg/300px-FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg/400px-FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FGM_prevalence_UNICEF_2016.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prevalence of FGM</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Female_genital_mutilation" title="Female genital mutilation">Female genital mutilation</a> (FGM) is defined as "all procedures that involve partial or total removal of the external female genitalia, or other injury to the female genital organs for non-medical reasons."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-134">[134]</a></sup> The procedure has no health benefits, and can cause severe bleeding and problems urinating, cysts, infections, and complications in childbirth and increased risk of newborn deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-134">[134]</a></sup> It is performed for traditional, cultural or religious reasons in many parts of the world, especially in Africa. The <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_preventing_and_combating_violence_against_women_and_domestic_violence" title="Convention on preventing and combating violence against women and domestic violence">Istanbul Convention</a> prohibits FGM (Article 38).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bride_kidnapping_or_buying_and_reproductive_slavery">Bride kidnapping or buying and reproductive slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Bride kidnapping or buying and reproductive slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1353" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg/220px-Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg/330px-Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg/440px-Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mauricio_rugendas_-_el_malon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The painting depicts a Chilean woman being kidnapped during a <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%B3n" title="Malón">malón</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Bride_kidnapping" title="Bride kidnapping">Bride kidnapping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">Bride price</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bride_kidnapping" title="Bride kidnapping">Bride kidnapping</a> or marriage by abduction, is the practice whereby a woman or girl is abducted for the purpose of a <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a>. Bride kidnapping has been practiced historically in many parts of the world, and it continues to occur today in some places, especially in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, in countries such as  Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Kazakhstan, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan and Armenia, as well as in Ethiopia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> Bride kidnapping is often preceded or followed by rape (which may result in pregnancy), in order to force the marriage - a practice also supported by "<a href="/wiki/Marry-your-rapist_law" title="Marry-your-rapist law">marry-your-rapist law</a>" (laws regarding sexual violence, abduction or similar acts, whereby the perpetrator avoids prosecution or punishment if he marries the victim<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>). Abducting of women may happen on an individual scale or on a mass scale. <a href="/wiki/Raptio" title="Raptio">Raptio</a> is a Latin term referring to the large-scale abduction of women, usually for marriage or sexual slavery, particularity during wartime.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bride_price" title="Bride price">Bride price</a>, also called bridewealth, is money, property, or other form of wealth paid by a groom or his family to the parents of the woman he marries. The practice of bride price sometimes leads to parents selling young daughters into marriage and to trafficking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup>Bride price is common across Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> Such forced marriages often lead to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_violence" title="Sexual violence">sexual violence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forced_pregnancy" title="Forced pregnancy">forced pregnancy</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ghana" title="Northern Ghana">northern Ghana</a>, for example, the payment of bride price signifies a woman's requirement to bear children, and women using birth control are at risks of threats and coercion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1956 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplementary_Convention_on_the_Abolition_of_Slavery,_the_Slave_Trade,_and_Institutions_and_Practices_Similar_to_Slavery" title="Supplementary Convention on the Abolition of Slavery, the Slave Trade, and Institutions and Practices Similar to Slavery">Supplementary Convention on the Abolition of Slavery, the Slave Trade, and Institutions and Practices Similar to Slavery</a> defines "institutions and practices similar to slavery" to include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>c) Any institution or practice whereby:
</p>
<ul><li>(i) A woman, without the right to refuse, is promised or given in marriage on payment of a consideration in money or in kind to her parents, guardian, family or any other person or group; or</li>
<li>(ii) The husband of a woman, his family, or his clan, has the right to transfer her to another person for value received or otherwise; or</li>
<li>(iii) A woman on the death of her husband is liable to be inherited by another person;</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sperm_donation">Sperm donation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Sperm donation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sperm_donation" title="Sperm donation">Sperm donation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sperm_donor_limitation_by_country" title="Sperm donor limitation by country">Sperm donor limitation by country</a></div>
<p>Laws in many countries and states require <a href="/wiki/Sperm_donation" title="Sperm donation">sperm donors</a> to be either anonymous or known to the recipient, or the laws restrict the number of children each donor may father. Although many donors choose to remain anonymous, new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technologies" title="Technologies">technologies</a> such as the Internet and DNA technology have opened up new avenues for those wishing to know more about the biological father, siblings and half-siblings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compulsory_sterilization">Compulsory sterilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Compulsory sterilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_minority_women">Ethnic minority women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Ethnic minority women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roma_people" title="Roma people">Roma people</a></div>
<p>Ethnic minority women have often been victims of forced sterilization programs, such as <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Amerindian women</a> in parts of Latin America of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roma_people" title="Roma people">Roma women</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Fujimori" title="Alberto Fujimori">Alberto Fujimori</a> (in office from 1990 to 2000) has been accused of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> as a result of the <i><a href="/wiki/Programa_Nacional_de_Poblaci%C3%B3n" title="Programa Nacional de Población">Programa Nacional de Población</a></i>, a sterilization program put in place by his administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-146">[146]</a></sup> During his presidency, Fujimori put in place a program of forced sterilizations against <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Peru" title="Indigenous peoples in Peru">indigenous people</a> (mainly the <a href="/wiki/Quechua_people" title="Quechua people">Quechuas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aymara_people" title="Aymara people">Aymaras</a>), in the name of a "<a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> plan", presented on July 28, 1995. 
</p><p>During the 20th century, forced sterilization of <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Roma</a> women in European countries, especially in former Communist countries, was practiced,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> and there are allegations that these practices continue unofficially in some countries, such as Czech Republic, Bulgaria, Hungary and Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/V._C._vs._Slovakia" title="V. C. vs. Slovakia">V. C. vs. Slovakia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Court_for_Human_Rights" title="European Court for Human Rights">European Court for Human Rights</a> ruled in favor of a Roma woman who was the victim of forced sterilization in a state hospital in <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Eugenics_in_the_United_States" title="Eugenics in the United States">Eugenics in the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_law_in_the_United_States" title="Sterilization law in the United States">Sterilization law in the United States</a></div>
<p>Forced sterilization in the United States was practiced starting with the 19th century. The United States during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_era" title="Progressive era">Progressive era</a>, ca. 1890 to 1920, was the first country to concertedly undertake compulsory sterilization programs for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_C._Leonard" title="Thomas C. Leonard">Thomas C. Leonard</a>, professor at Princeton University, describes American eugenics and sterilization as ultimately rooted in economic arguments and further as a central element of Progressivism alongside wage controls, restricted immigration, and the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard2005_153-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard2005-153">[153]</a></sup> The heads of the programs were avid proponents of eugenics and frequently argued for their programs which achieved some success nationwide mainly in the first half of the 20th Century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_sterilization_in_Canada" title="Compulsory sterilization in Canada">Compulsory sterilization in Canada</a></div>
<p>Compulsory sterilization has been practiced historically in parts of Canada. Two Canadian provinces (<a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>) performed compulsory sterilization programs in the 20th century with eugenic aims. Canadian compulsory sterilization operated via the same overall mechanisms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institutionalization" title="Institutionalization">institutionalization</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judgment" title="Judgment">judgment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> as the American system.  However, one notable difference is in the treatment of non-insane criminals. Canadian legislation never allowed for punitive sterilization of inmates.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sexual_Sterilization_Act_of_Alberta" title="Sexual Sterilization Act of Alberta">Sexual Sterilization Act of Alberta</a> was enacted in 1928 and repealed in 1972. In 1995, <a href="/wiki/Leilani_Muir" title="Leilani Muir">Leilani Muir</a> sued the Province of Alberta for forcing her to be sterilized against her will and without her permission in 1959. Since Muir’s case, the Alberta government has apologized for the forced sterilization of over 2,800 people. Nearly 850 Albertans who were sterilized under the Sexual Sterilization Act were awarded C$142 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholic_Church">Roman Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Latin_America" title="Roman Catholicism in Latin America">Roman Catholicism in Latin America</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CIA_map_of_Central_America.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1014" data-file-width="1272" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/CIA_map_of_Central_America.png/220px-CIA_map_of_Central_America.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/CIA_map_of_Central_America.png/330px-CIA_map_of_Central_America.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/CIA_map_of_Central_America.png/440px-CIA_map_of_Central_America.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CIA_map_of_Central_America.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> has very strict anti-abortion laws, and <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> has come to international attention due to its forceful enforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-156">[156]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-157">[157]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> is opposed to artificial contraception, abortion, and <a href="/wiki/Premarital_sex" title="Premarital sex">sexual intercourse outside marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> This belief dates back to the first centuries of Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-therealpresence1_161-0"><a href="#cite_note-therealpresence1-161">[161]</a></sup> While Roman Catholicism is not the only religion with such views, its religious doctrine is very powerful in influencing countries where most of the population is Catholic, and the few countries of the world with complete bans on abortion are Catholic-majority countries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChileCourt_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChileCourt-65">[65]</a></sup> and in Europe strict restrictions on abortion exist in the Catholic majority countries of <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Malta" title="Abortion in Malta">Malta</a> (complete ban), <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Abortion in the Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Andorra" title="Abortion in Andorra">Andorra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_San_Marino" title="Abortion in San Marino">San Marino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Liechtenstein" title="Abortion in Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a> and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Poland" title="Abortion in Poland">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Monaco" title="Abortion in Monaco">Monaco</a>.
</p><p>Some of the countries of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_El_Salvador" title="Abortion in El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, have also come to international attention due to very forceful enforcement of the anti-abortion laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup> El Salvador has received repeated criticism from the UN. The Office of the UN High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR) named the law "one of the most draconian abortion laws in the world", and urged liberalization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_156-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-156">[156]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Zeid_bin_Ra%27ad" title="Prince Zeid bin Ra'ad">Zeid bin Ra'ad</a>, the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights, stated that he was "appalled that as a result of El Salvador’s absolute prohibition on abortion, women are being punished for apparent miscarriages and other obstetric emergencies, accused and convicted of having induced termination of pregnancy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters.com_157-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-157">[157]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-abortion_violence">Anti-abortion violence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Anti-abortion violence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Criticism surrounds certain forms of anti-abortion activism. <a href="/wiki/Anti-abortion_violence" title="Anti-abortion violence">Anti-abortion violence</a> is a serious issue in some parts of the world, especially in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup> It is recognized as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-issue_terrorism" title="Single-issue terrorism">single-issue terrorism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csis_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-csis-166">[166]</a></sup> Numerous organizations have also recognized anti-abortion extremism as a form of <a href="/wiki/Christian_terrorism" title="Christian terrorism">Christian terrorism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interfaith_167-0"><a href="#cite_note-interfaith-167">[167]</a></sup>
</p><p>Incidents include vandalism, arson, and bombings of <a href="/wiki/Abortion_clinic" title="Abortion clinic">abortion clinics</a>, such as those committed by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Rudolph" title="Eric Rudolph">Eric Rudolph</a> (1996–98), and murders or attempted murders of physicians and clinic staff, as committed by <a href="/wiki/James_Charles_Kopp" title="James Charles Kopp">James Kopp</a> (1998), <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jennings_Hill" title="Paul Jennings Hill">Paul Jennings Hill</a> (1994), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scott_Roeder" title="Scott Roeder">Scott Roeder</a> (2009), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_F._Griffin" title="Michael F. Griffin">Michael F. Griffin</a> (1993), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_James_Knight" title="Peter James Knight">Peter James Knight</a> (2001).
Since 1978, in the US, anti-abortion violence includes at least 11 <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murders</a> of medical staff, 26 <a href="/wiki/Attempted_murder" title="Attempted murder">attempted murders</a>, 42 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombing" title="Bombing">bombings</a>, and 187 <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arsons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reproductive_rights&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_to_the_legalization_of_abortion" title="Opposition to the legalization of abortion">opponents of legalized abortion</a> view the term "reproductive rights" as a <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemism</a> to sway emotions in favor of abortion.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Right_to_Life" title="National Right to Life">National Right to Life</a> has referred to "reproductive rights" as a "fudge term" and "the code word for <i>abortion</i> rights."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRLC_169-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRLC-169">[169]</a></sup>
</p>
