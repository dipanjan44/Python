<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in New Zealand</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_New_Zealand" title="Climate of New Zealand">Climate of New Zealand</a></div>

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baringhead_co2_v9.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Baringhead_co2_v9.svg/450px-Baringhead_co2_v9.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Baringhead_co2_v9.svg/675px-Baringhead_co2_v9.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Baringhead_co2_v9.svg/900px-Baringhead_co2_v9.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baringhead_co2_v9.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric carbon dioxide record from <a href="/wiki/Baring_Head" title="Baring Head">Baring Head</a>, Wellington from 1977 to 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Climate change in New Zealand</b> refers to change in the <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_New_Zealand" title="Climate of New Zealand">climate of New Zealand</a> on the scale of years, decades, centuries and longer periods of time. New Zealand is being affected by climate change and the impacts are predicted to increase in future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate2017_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate2017-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSNZ2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSNZ2016-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_global_warming" title="Anthropogenic global warming">Anthropogenic global warming</a> during the 20th century is apparent in the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental temperature record</a>, in New Zealand's participation in international treaties, and in social and political debates.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> is being responded to in a variety of ways by civil society and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Government of New Zealand">government of New Zealand</a>. New Zealand has an <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="New Zealand Emissions Trading Scheme">emissions trading scheme</a> and from 1 July 2010, the energy and liquid fossil fuel and some industry sectors had obligations to report emissions and to obtain and surrender emissions units (carbon credits).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_of_climate_change_in_New_Zealand">Science of climate change in New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science of climate change in New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumental_records_and_effects">Instrumental records and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instrumental records and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg/450px-NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg/675px-NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg/900px-NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NZ-best-land-temp-anom.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Zealand annual average land surface temperature from 1909 to 2013 with a <a href="/wiki/Local_regression" title="Local regression">locally weighted ('Lowess')</a> trend lines. Source: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkeley_Earth_Surface_Temperature" title="Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature">Berkeley Earth Surface Temperature</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Since instrumental measurements began in the late 19th century, New Zealand's average air temperatures have fluctuated substantially year to year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roysoc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roysoc-4">[4]</a></sup> and a number of studies indicate that New Zealand's average temperature has increased.
</p><p>A significant upward trend in national average air temperature was detected of 0.11 °C per decade (for the period from 1896 to 1994) with a 95% confidence interval ± 0.035 °C. This is roughly twice the trend reported for global data, which may be due to the relative absence of sulfate aerosols in the South Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_New_Zealand" title="Royal Society of New Zealand">Royal Society of New Zealand</a>'s statement on climate change notes that between 1908 and 2006, there has been a clear upward linear trend in the country-wide average air temperature of 0.9°Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roysoc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roysoc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide">Carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>New Zealand has a long-term record of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> similar to the <a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a>. In 1970, <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Charles Keeling</a> asked David Lowe, a physics graduate from <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Wellington" title="Victoria University of Wellington">Victoria University of Wellington</a> to establish continuous atmospheric measurements at a New Zealand site. The south-facing <a href="/wiki/Baring_Head" title="Baring Head">Baring Head</a>, on the eastern entrance to <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Harbour" title="Wellington Harbour">Wellington Harbour</a>, was chosen as being representative of the atmosphere of the southern hemisphere. Lowe initially built an automatic air-sampling machine using parts from a used telephone exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modelled wind directions indicated that air flows were originating from 55 degrees south. The Baring Head data shows about the same overall rate of increase in CO<sub>2</sub> as the measurements from the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a>, but with a smaller seasonal variation. The Baring Head CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations have increased by 50 parts per million between first records in the early 1970s and 2005. The rate of increase in 2005 was 2.5 parts per million per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Baring Head record is the longest continuous record of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> in the Southern Hemisphere and it featured in the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report: Climate Change 2007 in conjunction with the better-known Mauna Loa record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Water_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research">National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research</a> has also recorded atmospheric concentrations of methane (from 1989) and nitrous oxide (from 1997) at Baring Head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Glaciers">Glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fox_Franz_Ivory_Glaciers_NZ_fig6.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="400" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/Fox_Franz_Ivory_Glaciers_NZ_fig6.gif" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fox_Franz_Ivory_Glaciers_NZ_fig6.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Measured variations in the position of the termini of the Fox, Franz Josef, and Ivory Glaciers, 1870 to 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Since 1977, the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Water_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research">National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research</a> has been using aerial surveys of late summer snowline to estimate the <a href="/wiki/Glacier_mass_balance" title="Glacier mass balance">mass balance</a> of 50 index glaciers.  The snowline marks the equilibrium line of a glacier; above the line the glacier is accumulating snow and below the line the glacier is melting. The mass balance is the net gain or loss of snow and ice.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wa_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-wa-11">[11]</a></sup> The latest survey, in 2009, indicated two years of decline in the overall mass of the index glaciers. The National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research considers that the volume of ice in New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Glaciers_of_New_Zealand" title="Glaciers of New Zealand">glaciers</a> has declined by about 50% in the last century, while New Zealand’s average temperature increased by about 1 °Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>New Zealand's largest glacier, the <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Glacier" title="Tasman Glacier">Tasman Glacier</a> has retreated about 180 metres a year on average since the 1990s and the glacier's terminal lake, <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Lake" title="Tasman Lake">Tasman Lake</a>, is expanding at the expense of the glacier. Massey University scientists expect that Lake Tasman will stabilise at a maximum size in about 10 to 19 years, and eventually the Tasman Glacier will disappear completely. In 1973 the Tasman Glacier had no terminal lake and by 2008 Tasman Lake was 7 km long, 2 km wide and 245m deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_level">Sea level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sea level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>An analysis of long term records from four New Zealand tide gauges indicates an average rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">increase in sea level</a> of 1.6 mm a year for the 100 years to 2000, which is considered to be relatively consistent with other regional and global sea level rise calculations when corrected for glacial-isostatic effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> One global average rate of sea-level rise is 1.7 ± 0.3 mm per year for the 20th century (Church and White (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Another global average rate of sea-level rise is 1.8 mm/yr ± 0.1 for the period 1880–1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2008 study of cores from salt-marshes near <a href="/wiki/Pounawea" title="Pounawea">Pounawea</a> indicated that the rate of sea level rise in the 20th century, 2.8 ± 0.5 mm per year, had been greater than rate of change in earlier centuries (0.3 ± 0.3 mm per year from AD 1500 to AD 1900) and that the 20th century sea level rise was consistent with instrumental measurements recorded since 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Twenty-first_century">Twenty-first century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Twenty-first century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>A report published by the Royal Society of New Zealand predicts sea level rise of at least 30 cm and possibly more than one metre this century. The report finds it likely that the sea level rise around New Zealand will exceed the global average and will cause coastal erosion and flooding, especially when combined with storm surges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSNZ2016_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSNZ2016-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_intensity">Emissions intensity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Emissions intensity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg/400px-Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg/600px-Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg/800px-Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Per_capita_greenhouse_gas_emissions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Zealand gross greenhouse gas emissions per capita 1990–2012 compared to United Kingdom, Europe, China, World average, India and Africa</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_for_the_Environment_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry for the Environment (New Zealand)">NZ Ministry for the Environment</a> considers that the energy intensity of New Zealand's economic output has <i>to some degree</i> decreased since 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Greenhouse gas emissions per unit of GDP have declined slowly from about 750 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent per million dollars of GDP in 1990, to 552 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to estimates from the International Energy Association, New Zealand's per capita carbon dioxide emissions roughly doubled from 1970 to 2000 and then exceeded the per capita carbon dioxide emissions of either the United Kingdom or the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  Per capita carbon dioxide emissions are in the highest quartile of global emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2007 State of the Environment Report noted that in 2005 New Zealand’s per capita emissions of the six greenhouse gases listed in the Kyoto Protocol were 18.5 tonnes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co2e" title="Co2e">CO<sub>2</sub>equivalents</a> per head of population and were the 12th highest in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the 5th highest out of the 27 OECD countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The Ministry for the Environment's 2007 <i>State of the Environment</i> report noted that New Zealand's per capita GHG emissions were high and were exceeded in the OECD only by countries such as Australia and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The greenhouse gas intensity of New Zealand's output is the second highest in the OECD (after Australia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Consequently, New Zealand is in the bottom half of the OECD in terms of greenhouse gas emissions per unit of GDP and greenhouse gas emissions per capita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Bertram and Terry (2010, p 164) state that on a per capita basis, New Zealand's GHG emissions are unambiguously a significant contribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cchallenge_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cchallenge-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg/400px-NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg/600px-NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg/800px-NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NZ_greenhouse_gases_by_sector.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Zealand’s greenhouse gas emissions projected and actual from 1990 to 2030.</div></div></div>
<p>New Zealand has a relatively unique emissions profile. In 2014, agriculture contributed 49% of total emissions, energy (including transport); 40%, industry; 6%, waste; 5%. In other Kyoto Protocol Annex 1 countries, agriculture typically contributes about 11% of total emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ghgi2016_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-ghgi2016-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1990 and 2014 New Zealand’s total (or gross) greenhouse gas emissions increased by 23.2%. Net emissions (after subtracting forest sequestration removals) increased by 53.6%. Emission increases by sector were – agriculture; 15.2%, energy; 35.5%, industry; 45.1%. waste; -0.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ghgi2016_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-ghgi2016-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>N<sub>2</sub>O emissions originate from animal excrement and from the use of nitrogenous fertiliser. Livestock produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from rumination. Methane is a more potent greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide as it can trap twenty times the heat of an equivalent volume of carbon dioxide. Since New Zealand has large stock numbers these emissions are significant. A dairy cow produces between 84 and 123 kg of methane per year from rumen fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1997, New Zealand's per capita emissions of methane were almost six times the OECD average and ten times the global average<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, the Government proposed an <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_emissions_research_levy" title="Agricultural emissions research levy">Agricultural emissions research levy</a> to fund research into reducing ruminant emissions. The proposal, popularly called a "fart tax", was strongly opposed by Federated Farmers and was later abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Livestock_Emissions_and_Abatement_Research_Network" title="Livestock Emissions and Abatement Research Network">Livestock Emissions and Abatement Research Network</a> (LEARN) was launched in 2007 to address livestock emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_Greenhouse_Gas_Research_Consortium" title="Pastoral Greenhouse Gas Research Consortium">Pastoral Greenhouse Gas Research Consortium</a> between the New Zealand government and industry groups seeks to reduce agricultural emissions through the funding of research.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/2009_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2009 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2009 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in Copenhagen, the New Zealand government announced the formation of the Global Research Alliance involving 20 other countries. New Zealand will contribute NZ$45 million over four years towards research on agricultural greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_New_Zealand">Impact on New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impact on New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The combined effects of climate change will result in a multitude of irreversible impacts on New Zealand. Some projections predict that by the end of this century New Zealand will experience higher rainfalls, more frequent extreme weather events, rising sea levels and higher temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview2018_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Overview2018-35">[35]</a></sup> Such effects will significantly impact New Zealand, with higher temperatures resulting in dry summers, consequently limiting New Zealand’s water supply and intensifying droughts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview2018_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Overview2018-35">[35]</a></sup> This will result in a myriad of effects, with droughts and lack of water affecting not only the environment, but the economy too, as New Zealand’s agricultural export sector strongly relies on an environment conducive to growing crops and livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising sea levels will further threaten coastal areas and erode and alter landscapes whilst also resulting in salt water intrusion into soils, reducing soil quality and limiting plant species growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview2018_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Overview2018-35">[35]</a></sup> Harsher climates will also affect landscapes and people’s health with mortality expected to rise due to harsher living environments and will have devastating effects on New Zealand’s flora and fauna, with climate threatening both animal and plant chances of survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Overview2018_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-Overview2018-35">[35]</a></sup> With climate change being a serious threat to New Zealand in a multitude of areas, the need to monitor it and address it is becoming increasingly pressing as the effects become more widespread and increasingly apparent.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_social_aspects">Political and social aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political and social aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_treaties">International treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: International treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Zealand ratified the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (the UNFCCC) in September 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfat_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfat-37">[37]</a></sup> The purpose of this convention was to collectively bring countries together to discuss how to best address climate change and handle the impacts of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCC_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCC-38">[38]</a></sup> The convention, which includes 192 nations and which came into force on the 21st of May 1994, is important in many ways, recognising that climate change is a serious threat and that human (anthropogenic) impact on change in climate needs to be focused on and reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCC_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCC-38">[38]</a></sup> The convention also places responsibility on developed countries to devise methods and systems to mitigate climate change and lead the way to addressing climate change for the developing world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCC_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCC-38">[38]</a></sup> The initial ratification to this convention sparked the beginning of formal commitment to climate change and the need to consider collective methods to address and adapt to the presence of the globally threatening issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCC_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCC-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kyoto_Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Kyoto Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="349" data-file-width="567" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png/220px-New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png/330px-New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png/440px-New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_Zealand%27s_comparative_emissions_1990-2012.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A comparison between New Zealand emissions (net and gross) and those of other countries from 1990 to 2012</div></div></div>
<p>New Zealand signed the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> to the UNFCCC on 22 May 1998 and ratified it on 19 December 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfatkp_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfatkp-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under Article 3.1 and Annex B of the Protocol, New Zealand has a target to ensure that 'aggregate anthropogenic carbon dioxide equivalent emissions of the greenhouse gases listed in Annex A do not exceed' 100% of 1990 gross emissions (the baseline).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>New Zealand's target is expressed as an "assigned amount" of allowed emissions over the five-year 2008–2012 commitment period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2e_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-p2e-41">[41]</a></sup> This is divided into <a href="/wiki/Assigned_amount_units" title="Assigned amount units">Assigned amount units</a> (AAUs) (denominated as 1 metric tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent) which each Annex I Party issues to itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade (New Zealand)">Ministry for Foreign Affairs and Trade</a> (MFAT) noted that it expected New Zealand to be able to increase emissions and comply with the Kyoto Protocol, and make an economic gain. Not withstanding the Annex B reduction target of 100% of the 1990 base year gross emissions, New Zealand would be able to emit more than its initial assigned amount as long as more emission units were obtained from forest carbon sinks between 2008 and 2012. MFAT predicted that New Zealand would emit between 50 and 75 million tonnes of greenhouse gases more than the 365 million tonnes of the initial assigned amount during the first commitment period. However, MFAT considered that the increase in emissions would be offset by about 110 million <a href="/wiki/Removal_Units" title="Removal Units">Removal Units</a> (RMU) originating from forests planted from 1990 onwards (post-1989 forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2e_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-p2e-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>So, specifically, for the 2008–2012 commitment period (CP1), New Zealand may either limit emissions to the assigned amount (five times the 1990 gross volume of emissions) or allow emissions to increase and add emission units from domestic afforestation or from the international carbon markets to the assigned amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfatkp_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-mfatkp-39">[39]</a></sup>  The additional units may be any of the Kyoto units; <a href="/wiki/Assigned_amount_units" title="Assigned amount units">Assigned amount units</a> (AAU), <a href="/wiki/Removal_Units" title="Removal Units">Removal Units</a> (RMU), <a href="/wiki/Emission_Reduction_Unit" title="Emission Reduction Unit">Emission Reduction Units</a> (ERU) and <a href="/wiki/Certified_Emission_Reduction" title="Certified Emission Reduction">Certified Emission Reduction</a> (CER) units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2007, New Zealand's 309,564,733 <a href="/wiki/Assigned_amount_units" title="Assigned amount units">Assigned amount units</a> were recorded in the New Zealand Emission Unit Register.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2005, a financial liability under the Kyoto Protocol for a shortfall of emission units of 36.2 million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub>-e was first recognised in the Financial Statements of the Government of New Zealand. It was estimated as a liability of $NZ310 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> New Zealand's net balance under the Kyoto Protocol remained in deficit from 2005 (a deficit of 36 million units)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> until May 2008 (a deficit of 21.7 million units).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2012, the Ministry for the Environment reported that the latest projection of New Zealand's net Kyoto position was a surplus of 35.3 million emission units valued at NZ$38 million (based on an international carbon price of 0.68 Euro per tonne).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 9 November 2012, the New Zealand Government announced it would make climate pledges for the period from 2013 to 2020 under the UNFCCC process rather than agree to a second commitment period under the Kyoto Protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Minister for Climate Change Issues, Tim Groser, argued that this was a principled decision, noting that:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"the key requirement [to address climate change] has been to refocus political and negotiating attention beyond the Kyoto Protocol to a more comprehensive agreement that is capable of dealing with the real environmental problem – the vast bulk of emissions that would never have been covered by Kyoto...That figure is 86% and will reach 90% of total global emissions in a few years. It is a matter of simple arithmetic that the only agreement that makes environmental sense long term is an agreement that deals with the bulk of emissions, not an increasingly small part of global emissions"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>This announcement was reported internationally as New Zealand avoiding legally binding obligations and as a move that angered environmentalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Green Party climate change spokesman Kennedy Graham said the Government's announcement was about hot air at talks instead of legally binding measures to reduce emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The decision was also heavily criticised by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Prime Minister John Key said New Zealand should not lead the way on climate change, but instead be a "fast follower".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The Alliance of Small Island States voiced disappointment at New Zealand's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2020_target_under_the_UNFCCC">2020 target under the UNFCCC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2020 target under the UNFCCC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Friday 16 August 2013, the Government announced a new mitigation responsibility target under the UNFCCC of −5% on 1990 emissions by the year 2020.  The Minister for Climate Change issues noted that New Zealand will also continue to honour its prior (2009) conditional offer to reduce emissions to 10 – 20% below 1990 levels but only if other countries come on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  Although −5% is more ambitious than the targets of Australia, Canada and the United States (when compared on 1990 levels),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> a number of environmental groups have noted that it is below the level recommended by scientists in order reduce the damage of anthropogenic climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>  Some groups in New Zealand have misunderstood the 2013 target, for example assuming that it replaced the target set in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris_Climate_Agreement">Paris Climate Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Paris Climate Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Climate_Agreement" title="Paris Climate Agreement">Paris Climate Agreement</a> negotiations began on 30 November and concluded 12 December 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>  The Paris Climate Agreement is the successor to the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> and has a target to keep temperature rises within 2 degrees this century, with ambitions to reduce this temperature rise to 1.5 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The key difference between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Climate_Agreement" title="Paris Climate Agreement">Paris Climate Agreement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> is the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). The Kyoto Protocol prescribed goals that were to be achieved by each signatory country and offered monetary support for developing countries (see <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> for more on this). The NDCs which are the model on which the Paris Climate Agreement is based, lets countries determine their own goals which will be within their capabilities. 
The treaty uses the terms ‘obligation’ and ‘expectations’ towards an individual country, or to the collective. For example, there are obligations to each signatory country to communicate and review every 5 years, its NDCs. Countries are expected to meet them, but there is no obligation to do so – or how countries go about achieving this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are again financial aids available to support all countries achieve their goals towards keeping the global temperature rises this century, below 2 degrees Celsius and down towards 1.5 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="New_Zealand.E2.80.99s_NDCs_to_the_Paris_Climate_Agreement"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand’s_NDCs_to_the_Paris_Climate_Agreement">New Zealand’s NDCs to the Paris Climate Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: New Zealand’s NDCs to the Paris Climate Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The executive summary of New Zealand’s NDC’s define New Zealand’s goals as a reduction of Greenhouse Gasses by 30 per cent from 2005 levels by 2030. There are three ways for New Zealand to achieve this goal.
i)	Reducing greenhouse emissions in New Zealand
ii)	Growing more trees to absorb emissions 
iii)	Buying emissions reductions from overseas carbon emissions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
It is still too early to measure New Zealand's contributions towards this now ratified treaty. There is however, evidence from the actions undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand">Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand</a>, which have banned new exploration permits and created a regional New Zealand policy aimed at planting 1 billion trees in 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Government_responses">Other Government responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other Government responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, the same year as the United Nations established the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth Labour Government of New Zealand">Fourth Labour Government of New Zealand</a> started developing policy for climate change. This was coordinated between agencies by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_for_the_Environment_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry for the Environment (New Zealand)">Ministry for the Environment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senz99c5_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-senz99c5-67">[67]</a></sup> The Government asked the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_New_Zealand" title="Royal Society of New Zealand">Royal Society of New Zealand</a> to report on the scientific basis of climate change. A short report, 'Climate Change in New Zealand', was published in 1988 and the full report 'New Zealand Climate Report 1990' was published in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1994, four months after the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) came into force, the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth National Government of New Zealand">Fourth National Government</a> announced a number of policies.
</p>
<ul><li>a target of reducing <i>net</i> emissions to 1990 volumes by the year 2020,</li>
<li>a target of slowing growth of <i>gross</i> emissions by 20%,</li>
<li>increased carbon storage in plantation forests</li>
<li>energy sector reforms</li>
<li>an energy efficiency strategy and the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Efficiency_and_Conservation_Authority" title="Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority">Energy Efficiency and Conservation Authority</a> (EECA),</li>
<li>renewable energy sources</li>
<li>use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_Management_Act" title="Resource Management Act">Resource Management Act</a>; and,</li>
<li>voluntary agreements with industry.</li></ul>
<p>The Fourth National Government said that if emissions were not stabilised at 1990 levels by the year 2000, a low-level carbon charge would be introduced in December 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-senz99c5_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-senz99c5-67">[67]</a></sup> By 1996, the Fourth National Government changed the emissions target to either no increase in 2000 net emissions of carbon dioxide from 1990 volumes or a 20% reduction if it was cost-effective and had no impact on trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 2002, the policy of the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand">Labour-led Clark Government</a> was to implement a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> in 2007. The proposed carbon tax would have applied to the whole economy, except for emissions of agricultural methane and nitrous oxide. The tax would have been $15 per tonne of carbon dioxide or equivalent, based on approximating the international price of emissions. It would have been capped at $25 per tonne. The carbon tax policy was intended to be a precursor to emissions trading when it became internationally established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, in December 2005, the Government announced that following a review of climate change policy it would not implement the proposed carbon tax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The political parties <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Future" title="United Future">United Future</a>, who were support parties of the Labour-led Government, opposed the tax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party described the withdrawal the carbon tax as "giving up on climate change" and "capitulating" to the anti-Kyoto lobby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defence_Society" title="Environmental Defence Society">Environmental Defence Society</a> described the withdrawal of the carbon tax as "pathetic" and a result of the NZ Government Climate Change Office being "captured" by vested interests such as energy intensive businesses and the Greenhouse Policy Coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">Fifth National Government of New Zealand</a> stated it is committed to further growth in New Zealand production of fossil fuels such as oil and gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_for_the_Environment_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry for the Environment (New Zealand)">Ministry for the Environment</a> consulted on a proposed zero carbon bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Interim_Climate_Change_Committee" title="Interim Climate Change Committee">Interim Climate Change Committee</a> was established in April 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_trading_scheme">Emissions trading scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Emissions trading scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="New Zealand Emissions Trading Scheme">New Zealand Emissions Trading Scheme</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg/400px-NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg/600px-NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg/800px-NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NZU-NZ-emission-unit-720by540.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Zealand Unit Prices from 2010</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>New Zealand Emissions Trading Scheme</b> (NZ ETS) is a partial-coverage all-free allocation uncapped highly internationally linked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_trading_scheme" title="Emissions trading scheme">emissions trading scheme</a>. The NZ ETS was first legislated in the <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Response_(Emissions_Trading)_Amendment_Act_2008" title="Climate Change Response (Emissions Trading) Amendment Act 2008">Climate Change Response (Emissions Trading) Amendment Act 2008</a> in September 2008 under the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand">Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker08_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-parker08-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccret_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccret-79">[79]</a></sup> and then amended in November 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smith251109_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-smith251109-80">[80]</a></sup> and in November 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-groser12_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-groser12-81">[81]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">Fifth National Government of New Zealand</a>.
</p><p>The NZ ETS covers forestry (a net sink), energy (43.4% of total 2010 emissions), industry (6.7% of total 2010 emissions) and waste (2.8% of total 2010 emissions) but not pastoral agriculture (47% of 2010 total emissions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snapshot_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-snapshot-82">[82]</a></sup> Participants in the NZ ETS must surrender one emission unit (either an international 'Kyoto' unit or a New Zealand-issued unit) for every two tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent emissions reported or they may choose to buy NZ units from the government at a fixed price of NZ$25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MfESept09_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-MfESept09-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Individual sectors of the economy have different entry dates when their obligations to report emissions and surrender emission units take effect. Forestry, which contributed net removals of 17.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megatonnes#Derived_units" title="Megatonnes">Mts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">CO<sub>2</sub>e</a> in 2010 (19% of NZ's 2008 emissions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mfe4-10_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-mfe4-10-84">[84]</a></sup>) entered the NZ ETS on 1 January 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forest_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-forest-85">[85]</a></sup> The stationary energy, industrial processes and liquid fossil fuel sectors entered the NZ ETS on 1 July 2010. The waste sector (landfill operators) will enter on 1 January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>  Methane and nitrous oxide emissions from pastoral agriculture are not included in the NZ ETS. (From November 2009, agriculture was to enter the NZ ETS on 1 January 2015<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MfESept09_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-MfESept09-83">[83]</a></sup>)
</p><p>The NZ ETS is highly linked to international carbon markets as it allows the importing of most of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> emission units. It also creates a specific domestic unit; the 'New Zealand Unit' (NZU), which will be issued by free allocation to emitters, with no auctions intended in the short term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unit_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-unit-87">[87]</a></sup> Free allocation of NZUs will vary by sector. The commercial fishery sector (who are not participants) will receive a free allocation of units on a historic basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MfESept09_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-MfESept09-83">[83]</a></sup> Owners of pre-1990 forests will receive a fixed free allocation of units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forest_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-forest-85">[85]</a></sup>  Free allocation to emissions-intensive industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ag_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-ag-89">[89]</a></sup> will be provided on an output-intensity basis. For this sector, there is no set limit on the number of units that may be allocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull12_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-bull12-90">[90]</a></sup> The number of units allocated to eligible emitters will be based on the average emissions per unit of output within a defined 'activity'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cap_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-cap-91">[91]</a></sup>  Bertram and Terry (2010, p 16) state that as the NZ ETS does not 'cap' emissions, the NZ ETS is not a cap and trade scheme as understood in the economics literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cchallenge_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-cchallenge-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some stakeholders have criticized the New Zealand Emissions Trading Scheme for its generous free allocations of emission units and the lack of a carbon price signal (the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Commissioner_for_the_Environment" title="Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment">Parliamentary Commissioner for the Environment</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pce1_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-pce1-92">[92]</a></sup> and for being ineffective in reducing emissions (<a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_Aotearoa_New_Zealand" title="Greenpeace Aotearoa New Zealand">Greenpeace Aotearoa New Zealand</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenpeace_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>The NZ ETS was reviewed in late 2011 by an independent panel, which reported to the public in September 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ETSreview_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-ETSreview-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties_stance_on_climate_change">Political parties stance on climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Political parties stance on climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the 2008 election, the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand">Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand</a> had committed itself to addressing climate change and had declared a goal for New Zealand of carbon neutrality. To achieve this, two objectives were set: generating 90 per cent of electricity from renewable sources by 2025; and, halving per capita transport emissions by 2040.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The Climate Change (Emissions Trading and Renewable Preference) Bill was introduced into Parliament by the Fifth Labour Government on 4 December 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> On 10 September 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Response_(Emissions_Trading)_Amendment_Act_2008" title="Climate Change Response (Emissions Trading) Amendment Act 2008">Climate Change Response (Emissions Trading) Amendment Act 2008</a> had its third reading in Parliament and was adopted 63 votes to 57 with support from the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand" title="Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand">Green Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker08_78-1"><a href="#cite_note-parker08-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Colin James, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">National Party</a> "herded with" the climate change skeptics up to 2006. Then in May 2007, National stopped opposing the Kyoto Protocol and adopted a policy of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 50 per cent by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> For the 2008 election, National's policy was to honour New Zealand's Kyoto Protocol obligations and the 50% reduction by 2050 emissions target. The <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Response_(Emissions_Trading)_Amendment_Act_2008" title="Climate Change Response (Emissions Trading) Amendment Act 2008">Labour Party's emissions trading scheme</a> would be amended to better balance environmental and economic concerns, and to make it fiscally neutral, flexible, aligned with the Australian Carbon Pollution Reduction Scheme and not disadvantageous to consumers and small business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand" title="Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand">Green Party</a> support lowering carbon emissions, exposing the economy to the <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externalities</a> of carbon emissions and using income from a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> to achieve a low carbon economy and to protect those on low incomes from the economic consequences of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT Party</a> went into the 2008 election with a policy that in part stated "New Zealand is not warming" and that their policy goal was to ensure: "That no New Zealand government will ever impose needless and unjustified taxation or regulation on its citizens in a misguided attempt to reduce global warming or become a world leader in carbon neutrality"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> In September 2008, <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT Party</a> Leader <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Hide" title="Rodney Hide">Rodney Hide</a> stated "that the entire climate change – global warming hypothesis is a hoax, that the data and the hypothesis do not hold together, that Al Gore is a phoney and a fraud on this issue, and that the emissions trading scheme is a worldwide scam and swindle".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> In October 2012, in response to a speech on climate change by Green Party MP Kennedy Graham, ACT leader <a href="/wiki/John_Banks_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="John Banks (New Zealand politician)">John Banks</a> said he had "never heard such claptrap in this parliament... a bogeyman tirade, humbug."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>Formed in August 2014, the <a href="/wiki/NZ_Climate_Party" title="NZ Climate Party">NZ Climate Party</a> believe that climate change is an urgent priority and should receive much greater political attention. The party believes current &amp; past New Zealand Governments have not been taking the need to reduce greenhouse gas emissions nearly seriously enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2017 New Zealand election, environmental policy presented by political parties were determined by political commentators as the elections greatest political divide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>The New Zealand Labour Party described their commitment to climate change through Jacinda Arderns comment of “My generations Nuclear free moment” with the Labour party setting a target of net zero for greenhouse gases by the year 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAOCC_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-RAOCC-105">[105]</a></sup> The Labour government further committed to creating an independent climate change commission to address carbon monitoring and budgeting, and also to provide comment and ramifications when set targets or goals weren't met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAOCC_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-RAOCC-105">[105]</a></sup> Agriculture was also a point of focus with Labour committing to including agriculture into the emissions trading scheme to ensure that the agricultural sector operates with improved environmental practise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAOCC_105-2"><a href="#cite_note-RAOCC-105">[105]</a></sup> The Labour party also pledged to create a sustainable low carbon economy, while also achieving being a leading nation in addressing climate change, successfully achieving its commitments as made under the 2015 Paris agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RAOCC_105-3"><a href="#cite_note-RAOCC-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Party in the 2017 election made a commitment to reduce carbon emissions by 30 percent below 2005 levels by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalCC_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalCC-106">[106]</a></sup> The National Party also committed to achieving 90 percent of New Zealand's energy as renewable, alongside investing 4 million dollars into New Zealand becoming closer to a low carbon economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalCC_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-NationalCC-106">[106]</a></sup> The National government also focused on transport, committing to invest in public transport, electric vehicles and cycleways to reduce use of non-renewable energy run vehicle use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalCC_106-2"><a href="#cite_note-NationalCC-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2017 general election the Green Party leader James Shaw announced that the Green Party was taking “real climate action”, focusing on climate change policy where New Zealand could best adapt to the issues that climate change is already creating in the nation, as well as focusing on international adaptation, supporting international efforts to mitigate climate change, with special focus given towards the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> The Green Party also committed to making New Zealand a carbon neutral country by the year of  2050 and to establishing an independent climate commission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPP_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPP-108">[108]</a></sup> The Green Party proposed a Kiwi Climate Fund to replace the Emissions Trading Scheme, charging individuals responsible for contributing to climate change pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPP_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPP-108">[108]</a></sup> Committment was also made to New Zealand having 100 percent renewable energy by 2030, as well as planting 1.2 billion trees, allocating 40 million dollars to native forest regeneration and creating a 100 million dollar green infrastructure fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPP_108-2"><a href="#cite_note-CPP-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Maori Party committed to setting legally binding emission reduction targets, closing all coal run power plants by 2025, support the development of renewable resources and plant 100,000 hectares of forest over the next 10 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policies_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Policies-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>The NZ First Party also committed to setting legally binding emission reduction targets and also committed to replacing the ETS with carbon budgets and require government vehicles to be electricity run by the year 2025/2026.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policies_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-Policies-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_society_and_non-governmental_organisations">Civil society and non-governmental organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Civil society and non-governmental organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of civil society groupings and NGOs have formed to lobby in favour of and against a variety of climate change policies. Individuals also express policy preferences through forums such as the Internet, Letters to the Editor and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talkback_radio" title="Talkback radio">talkback radio</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Royal_Forest_and_Bird_Protection_Society_of_New_Zealand" title="Royal Forest and Bird Protection Society of New Zealand">Forest and Bird</a>, New Zealand's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_organisation" title="Conservation organisation">conservation organisation</a>, campaigns on climate change by increasing public awareness. Climate has an effect on ecosystems and therefore on species. New Zealand has a number of endangered species that may be affected by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_New_Zealand" title="Royal Society of New Zealand">Royal Society of New Zealand</a> advocates and provides funds for science research including research into global warming. The Royal Society considered it important to be seen to be doing something about climate change, so it became carbon neutral in 2008 when it received <a href="/wiki/CarboNZero_programme" title="CarboNZero programme">carboNZero</a> certification from <a href="/wiki/Landcare_Research" title="Landcare Research">Landcare Research</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Similar to other national science organisations, the Royal Society of New Zealand has released a position statement on global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Climate_Science_Coalition" title="New Zealand Climate Science Coalition">New Zealand Climate Science Coalition</a> has the goal of refuting what it believes are unfounded claims about anthropogenic global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcshane_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcshane-113">[113]</a></sup> It was formed in May 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Vincent_R._Gray" title="Vincent R. Gray">Vincent Gray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augie_Auer" title="Augie Auer">Augie Auer</a> among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> In August 2010, the Coalition announced it had commenced legal action against the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Water_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research">National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research</a> (NIWA), asking the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_New_Zealand" title="High Court of New Zealand">High Court</a> to invalidate its official temperature record, to prevent it using the temperature record when advising Government and to require NIWA to produce a "full and accurate" temperature record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> In September 2012, the High Court dismissed the allegations of the New Zealand Climate Science Education Trust, declined to review the NIWA temperature data and awarded costs to NIWA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Greenhouse Policy Coalition represented the energy-intensive sector (aluminium, steel, forestry, coal, dairy processing and gas sectors).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>  It was set up in 1996, and represented most of New Zealand’s largest electricity users such as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Steel" title="New Zealand Steel">New Zealand Steel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Aluminium_Smelters_Limited" title="New Zealand Aluminium Smelters Limited">New Zealand Aluminium Smelters Limited</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solid_Energy" title="Solid Energy">Solid Energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fonterra" title="Fonterra">Fonterra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanex" title="Methanex">Methanex</a> New Zealand and four pulp and paper manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cook_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-cook-119">[119]</a></sup> In 2005, the Greenhouse Policy Coalition opposed the Labour Government's proposal for 2005 carbon tax.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>  The group accepts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a> and sees a need for <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> to mitigate its effects, as long as that policy is 'moderate and measured'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> The Greenhouse Policy Coalition stopped operating on 31 March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>OraTaiao: New Zealand Climate and Health is a group of senior doctors and other health professionals concerned about the effect of climate change on population health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>  It was formed to lead the New Zealand medical advocacy response, in light of recognition of the importance of this topic by the World Medical Association and leading medical journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grassroots activism also plays a part. Climate Camp Aotearoa, part of the <a href="/wiki/Camp_for_Climate_Action" title="Camp for Climate Action">Camps for Climate Action</a> held an event in Wellington in December 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> On 21 December 2009, members of the Camp for Climate Action disrupted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_stock_exchange" title="New Zealand stock exchange">New Zealand stock exchange</a> as a protest against the failure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copenhagen_climate_change" title="Copenhagen climate change">Copenhagen climate change</a> talks and 'to confront the profiteers of climate change'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/350.org" title="350.org">350.org</a> have also organised various events around New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p><p>A coalition called Climaction were active in Auckland between 2006 and 2008. They advocated system change, not climate change. They held mass direct action blockades of major streets in Auckland City to demand <a href="/wiki/Free_public_transport" title="Free public transport">free public transport</a> and a 90% reduction in greenhouse gases by the year 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2011, the climate scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> visited New Zealand for a speaking tour. Hansen drew huge crowds for his public talks which emphasized that the likely global warming catastrophe from increased fossil fuel sourced CO<sub>2</sub> emissions could be avoided as long as the world's coal resources, including seams of <a href="/wiki/Southland,_New_Zealand" title="Southland, New Zealand">Southland</a> <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, were not used. Hansen said he did not agree with cap and trade schemes, like the NZETS that included forestry offsets. "In my opinion you have to have the simplest, transparent scheme so I just say it should be a flat fee proportional to the amount of carbon in the fuel. And the money, I say, has to go to the public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> New Zealand Herald economics editor Brian Fallow described Hansen as "authoritative on the science and compelling on the ethics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2016, an individual set up a <a class="external text" href="http://climatedash.nz" rel="nofollow">Climate Dashboard NZ</a> website to provide more visual tracking of NZ's Greenhouse Gas emission reduction efforts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_reporting_and_public_opinion">Media reporting and public opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Media reporting and public opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A six-month study in 1988 on climate change reporting in the media found that 80% of stories were no worse than slightly inaccurate.   However, one story in six contained significant misreporting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p>The popular media in New Zealand often give equal weight to those supporting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a> and those who deny it. This stance is out of step with the findings of the scientific community where the vast majority support the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">climate change scenarios</a>.
</p><p>A survey carried out in 2007 on climate change gave the following responses:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In August 2012, a Horizons poll showed that 64.4% of respondents wanted Parliament to do more to respond to global warming. 67.5% of respondents wanted business to do more to address global warming. Horizons commented that the poll "makes a strong case for more political action".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Motu Economic and Public Policy Research surveyed 2200 New Zealanders (over the age of 18) and found that at least 87% of participants are “somewhat concerned” about the effects of climate change to society in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motu_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-Motu-135">[135]</a></sup>  63% also believed that climate change would affect themselves and 58% believed that climate change would affect society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motu_135-1"><a href="#cite_note-Motu-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p>
