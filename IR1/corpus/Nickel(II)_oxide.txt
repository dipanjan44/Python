<p><b>Nickel(II) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span>. It is notable as being the only well-characterized oxide of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> (although <a href="/wiki/Nickel(III)_oxide" title="Nickel(III) oxide">nickel(III) oxide</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">Ni<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> have been claimed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup>). The mineralogical form of <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span>, <a href="/wiki/Bunsenite" title="Bunsenite">bunsenite</a>, is very rare.  It is classified as a basic metal oxide.  Several million kilograms are produced in varying quality annually, mainly as an intermediate in the production of nickel alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> can be prepared by multiple methods. Upon heating above 400 °C, nickel powder reacts with oxygen to give <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span>. In some commercial processes, green nickel oxide is made by heating a mixture of nickel powder and water at 1000 °C, the rate for this reaction can be increased by the addition of <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prad_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prad-5">[5]</a></sup> The simplest and most successful method of preparation is through pyrolysis of a nickel(II) compounds such as the hydroxide, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_nitrate" title="Nickel nitrate">nitrate</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonate" title="Nickel carbonate">carbonate</a>, which yield a light green powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> Synthesis from the elements by heating the metal in oxygen can yield grey to black powders which indicates <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">nonstoichiometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> adopts the <span class="chemf nowrap">NaCl</span> structure, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral" title="Octahedral">octahedral</a> Ni<sup>2+</sup> and O<sup>2−</sup> sites. The conceptually simple structure is commonly known as the rock salt structure. Like many other binary metal oxides, <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> is often non-stoichiometric, meaning that the Ni:O ratio deviates from 1:1.  In nickel oxide this non-stoichiometry is accompanied by a color change, with the stoichiometrically correct NiO being green and the non-stoichiometric <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> being black.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_and_reactions">Applications and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> has a variety of specialized applications and generally applications distinguish between "chemical grade", which is relatively pure material for specialty applications, and "metallurgical grade", which is mainly used for the production of alloys. It is used in the ceramic industry to make frits, ferrites, and porcelain glazes. The sintered oxide is used to produce nickel steel alloys. <a href="/wiki/Charles_%C3%89douard_Guillaume" title="Charles Édouard Guillaume">Charles Édouard Guillaume</a> won the 1920 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> for his work on nickel steel alloys which he called <a href="/wiki/Invar" title="Invar">invar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elinvar" title="Elinvar">elinvar</a>.
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> was also a component in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-iron_battery" title="Nickel-iron battery">nickel-iron battery</a>, also known as the Edison Battery, and is a component in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a>.  It is the precursor to many nickel salts, for use as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specialty_chemicals" title="Specialty chemicals">specialty chemicals</a> and catalysts. More recently, <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> was used to make the NiCd rechargeable batteries found in many electronic devices until the development of the environmentally superior NiMH battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prad_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prad-5">[5]</a></sup> <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> an anodic electrochromic material, have been widely studied as counter electrodes with tungsten oxide, cathodic electrochromic material, in complementary <a href="/wiki/Electrochromic_devices" title="Electrochromic devices">electrochromic devices</a>. 
</p><p>About 4000 tons of chemical grade <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> are produced annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">[4]</a></sup>  Black <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> is the precursor to nickel salts, which arise by treatment with mineral acids. <span class="chemf nowrap">NiO</span> is a versatile hydrogenation catalyst.
</p><p>Heating nickel oxide with either hydrogen, carbon, or carbon monoxide reduces it to metallic nickel.  It combines with the oxides of sodium and potassium at high temperatures (&gt;700 °C) to form the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickelate" title="Nickelate">nickelate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prad_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Prad-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel oxide reacts with <a href="/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide" title="Chromium(III) oxide">chromium(III) oxide</a> in a basic moist environment to form nickel chromate:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_Risks">Health Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Long-term inhalation of NiO is damaging to the lungs, causing lesions and in some cases cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The calculated half-life of dissolution of NiO in blood is more than 90 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> NiO has a long retention half-time in the lungs; after administration to rodents, it persisted in the lungs for more than 3 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Nickel oxide is classified as a human carcinogen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> based on increased respiratory cancer risks observed in epidemiological studies of sulfidic ore refinery workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2-year National Toxicology Program green NiO inhalation study, some evidence of carcinogenicity in F344/N rats but equivocal evidence in female B6C3F1 mice were observed; there was no evidence of carcinogenicity in male B6C3F1 mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> Chronic inflammation without fibrosis was observed in the 2-year studies. 
</p>
