<p><b>Indium(III) oxide</b> (<a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">In<sub>2</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>3</sub></a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_structure">Crystal structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crystal structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amorphous indium oxide is insoluble in water but soluble in acids, whereas crystalline indium oxide is insoluble in both water and acids. The crystalline form exist in two phases, the cubic (<a href="/wiki/Bixbyite" title="Bixbyite">bixbyite</a> type)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marezio1966_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marezio1966-1">[1]</a></sup> and rhombohedral (<a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a> type). Both phases have a <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of about 3 eV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The parameters of the cubic phase are listed in the infobox. The rhombohedral phase is produced at high temperatures and pressures or when using non-equilibrium growth methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It has a <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> R<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span>c No. 167, <a href="/wiki/Pearson_symbol" title="Pearson symbol">Pearson symbol</a> hR30, a = 0.5487 nm, b = 0.5487 nm, c = 0.57818 nm, Z = 6 and calculated density 7.31 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conductivity_and_magnetism">Conductivity and magnetism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conductivity and magnetism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thin films of <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doped" title="Doped">doped</a> indium oxide (In<sub>2−x</sub>Cr<sub>x</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) are a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_semiconductor" title="Magnetic semiconductor">magnetic semiconductor</a> displaying high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a>, single-<a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> crystal structure, and <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> behavior with high concentration of <a href="/wiki/Charge_carrier" title="Charge carrier">charge carriers</a>. It has possible applications in <a href="/wiki/Spintronics" title="Spintronics">spintronics</a> as a material for spin injectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thin polycrystalline films of indium oxide doped with Zn are highly conductive (conductivity ~10<sup>5</sup> S/m) and even superconductive at helium temperatures. The superconducting transition temperature T<sub>c</sub> depends on the doping and film structure and is below 3.3 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bulk samples can be prepared by heating <a href="/wiki/Indium(III)_hydroxide" title="Indium(III) hydroxide">indium(III) hydroxide</a> or the nitrate, carbonate or sulfate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-downs-8">[8]</a></sup> 
Thin films of indium oxide can be prepared by <a href="/wiki/Sputter_deposition" title="Sputter deposition">sputtering</a> of indium target in <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>/<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atmosphere. They can be used as <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_barrier" title="Diffusion barrier">diffusion barriers</a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_metal" title="Barrier metal">barrier metals</a>") in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>, e.g. to inhibit <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> between <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocrystal" title="Monocrystal">Monocrystalline</a> <a href="/wiki/Nanowire" title="Nanowire">nanowires</a> were synthetized from indium oxide by laser ablation, allowing precise diameter control down to 10 nm. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_effect_transistor" title="Field effect transistor">Field effect transistors</a> were fabricated from those.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Indium oxide nanowires can serve as sensitive and specific <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">Sol-gel</a> method is another way to prepare the nanowires.
</p><p>Indium oxide can serve as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_material" title="Semiconductor material">semiconductor material</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Heterojunction" title="Heterojunction">heterojunctions</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-type_semiconductor" title="P-type semiconductor">p</a>-<a href="/wiki/Indium_phosphide" title="Indium phosphide">InP</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" title="N-type semiconductor">n</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">GaAs</a>, n-<a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a>, and other materials. A layer of indium oxide on a silicon substrate can be deposited from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indium_trichloride" title="Indium trichloride">indium trichloride</a> solution, a method useful for manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When heated to 700 °C Indium(III) oxide forms In<sub>2</sub>O, (called indium(I) oxide or indium suboxide), at 2000 °C it decomposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-downs-8">[8]</a></sup>
It is soluble in acids but not in alkali.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-downs_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-downs-8">[8]</a></sup>
With ammonia at high temperature <a href="/wiki/Indium_nitride" title="Indium nitride">indium nitride</a> is formed <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>In<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 2 NH<sub>3</sub> → 2 InN + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Potassium_oxide" title="Potassium oxide">K<sub>2</sub>O</a> and indium metal the compound K<sub>5</sub>InO<sub>4</sub> containing <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a>  InO<sub>4</sub><sup>5−</sup> ions was prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LuleiHoppe1994_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-LuleiHoppe1994-14">[14]</a></sup>
Reacting with a range of metal trioxides produced <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_(structure)" title="Perovskite (structure)">perovskites</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shannon1967_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shannon1967-15">[15]</a></sup> for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>In<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + <a href="/wiki/Chromium_trioxide" title="Chromium trioxide">Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> → 2 InCrO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Indium oxide is used in some types of batteries, <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin film</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> reflectors transparent for visible light (<a href="/wiki/Hot_mirror" title="Hot mirror">hot mirrors</a>), some <a href="/wiki/Optical_coating" title="Optical coating">optical coatings</a>, and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antistatic_coating" title="Antistatic coating">antistatic coatings</a>. In combination with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" title="Tin dioxide">tin dioxide</a>, indium oxide forms <a href="/wiki/Indium_tin_oxide" title="Indium tin oxide">indium tin oxide</a> (also called tin doped indium oxide or ITO), a material used for transparent conductive coatings.
</p><p>In semiconductors, indium oxide can be used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" title="N-type semiconductor">n-type semiconductor</a> used as a <a href="/wiki/Resistor" title="Resistor">resistive element</a> in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">histology</a>, indium oxide is used as a part of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stain_(biology)" title="Stain (biology)">stain</a> formulations.
</p>
