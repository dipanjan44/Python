<p><b>Ruthenium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ru</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 44. It is a rare <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. Like the other metals of the platinum group, ruthenium is inert to most other chemicals. The Russian-born scientist of Baltic-German ancestry and a member of the Russian Academy of Science <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ernst_Claus" title="Karl Ernst Claus">Karl Ernst Claus</a> discovered the element in 1844 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kazan_State_University" title="Kazan State University">Kazan State University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and named it after the Latin name of his homeland, <i><a href="/wiki/Ruthenia" title="Ruthenia">Ruthenia</a></i>. Ruthenium is usually found as a minor component of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> ores; the annual production is about 20 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMM_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMM-5">[5]</a></sup> Most ruthenium produced is used in wear-resistant electrical contacts and thick-film resistors. A minor application for ruthenium is in platinum <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> and as a chemistry <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a>. A new application of ruthenium is as the capping layer for extreme ultraviolet photomasks. Ruthenium is generally found in ores with the other platinum group metals in the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> and in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Small but commercially important quantities are also found in <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a> extracted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudbury,_Ontario" title="Sudbury, Ontario">Sudbury, Ontario</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Pyroxenite" title="Pyroxenite">pyroxenite</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Platinum-Geological_Survey_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Platinum-Geological_Survey-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ruthenium_crystals.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2092" data-file-width="2404" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ruthenium_crystals.jpg/220px-Ruthenium_crystals.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ruthenium_crystals.jpg/330px-Ruthenium_crystals.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ruthenium_crystals.jpg/440px-Ruthenium_crystals.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ruthenium_crystals.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gas phase grown crystals of ruthenium metal.</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)#Common_valences" title="Valence (chemistry)">polyvalent</a> hard white metal. Ruthenium is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> and is in <a href="/wiki/Group_8_element" title="Group 8 element">group 8</a> of the periodic table:

<p>Whereas all other group 8 elements have 2 electrons in the outermost shell, in ruthenium, the outermost shell has only one electron (the final electron is in a lower shell). This anomaly is observed in the neighboring metals <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a> (41), <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> (42), and <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> (45).
</p><p>Ruthenium has four crystal modifications and does not tarnish unless subject to high temperatures. Ruthenium dissolves in fused alkalis to give ruthenates (<span class="chemf nowrap">RuO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), is not attacked by acids (even <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>) but is attacked by <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> at high temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-crc-7">[7]</a></sup> Indeed, ruthenium is most readily attacked by oxidizing agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1076_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1076-8">[8]</a></sup> Small amounts of ruthenium can increase the hardness of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance of <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> is increased markedly by the addition of a small amount of ruthenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-crc-7">[7]</a></sup> The metal can be plated by <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> and by thermal decomposition. A ruthenium-<a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> alloy is known to be <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductive</a> at temperatures below 10.6 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-crc-7">[7]</a></sup> Ruthenium is the last of the 4d transition metals that can assume the group oxidation state +8, and even then it is less stable there than the heavier congener osmium: this is the first group from the left of the table where the second and third-row transition metals display notable differences in chemical behavior. Like iron but unlike osmium, ruthenium can form aqueous cations in its lower oxidation states of +2 and +3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1078_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1078-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ruthenium is the first in a downward trend in the melting and boiling points and atomization enthalpy in the 4d transition metals after the maximum seen at <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>, because the 4d subshell is more than half full and the electrons are contributing less to metallic bonding. (<a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">Technetium</a>, the previous element, has an exceptionally low value that is off the trend due to its half-filled [Kr]4d<sup>5</sup>5s<sup>2</sup> configuration, though the small amount of energy needed to excite it to a [Kr]4d<sup>6</sup>5s<sup>1</sup> configuration indicates that it is not as far off the trend in the 4d series as <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> in the 3d transition series.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1075_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1075-10">[10]</a></sup> Unlike the lighter congener iron, ruthenium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> at room temperature, as iron also is above its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curie_point" title="Curie point">Curie point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1074_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1074-11">[11]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_ruthenium" title="Isotopes of ruthenium">Isotopes of ruthenium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring ruthenium is composed of seven stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>. Additionally, 34 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotopes" title="Radioactive isotopes">radioactive isotopes</a> have been discovered. Of these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a>, the most stable are <sup>106</sup>Ru with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 373.59 days, <sup>103</sup>Ru with a half-life of 39.26 days and <sup>97</sup>Ru with a half-life of 2.9 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-n1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-n2-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fifteen other radioisotopes have been characterized with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weights</a> ranging from 89.93 <a href="/wiki/Unified_atomic_mass_unit" title="Unified atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>90</sup>Ru) to 114.928 u (<sup>115</sup>Ru). Most of these have half-lives that are less than five minutes except <sup>95</sup>Ru (half-life: 1.643 hours) and <sup>105</sup>Ru (half-life: 4.44 hours).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-n1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-n2-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> before the most abundant isotope, <sup>102</sup>Ru, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and the primary mode after is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay product</a> before <sup>102</sup>Ru is <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a> and the primary decay product after is <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-n1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-n2-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Ruthenium_minerals" title="Category:Ruthenium minerals">category:Ruthenium minerals</a></div>
<p>As the 74th <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">most abundant element in Earth's crust</a>, ruthenium is relatively rare,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-15">[15]</a></sup> found in about 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_trillion" title="Parts per trillion">parts per trillion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1071_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1071-16">[16]</a></sup> This element is generally found in ores with the other platinum group metals in the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> and in North and South America. Small but commercially important quantities are also found in <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a> extracted from <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Pyroxenite" title="Pyroxenite">pyroxenite</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. The native form of ruthenium is a very rare mineral (Ir replaces part of Ru in its structure).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-YB-2006_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-YB-2006-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS-2008_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS-2008-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roughly 12 tonnes of ruthenium are mined each year with world reserves estimated at 5,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-15">[15]</a></sup> The composition of the mined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum_group_metal" title="Platinum group metal">platinum group metal</a> (PGM) mixtures varies widely, depending on the geochemical formation. For example, the PGMs mined in South Africa contain on average 11% ruthenium while the PGMs mined in the former USSR contain only 2% (1992).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Ruthenium, osmium, and iridium are considered the minor platinum group metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1074_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1074-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ruthenium, like the other platinum group metals, is obtained commercially as a by-product from <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and platinum metals ore processing. During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_extraction_techniques#Electrorefining" title="Copper extraction techniques">electrorefining of copper</a> and nickel, noble metals such as silver, gold, and the platinum group metals precipitate as <i>anode mud</i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a> for the extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-YB-2006_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-YB-2006-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS-2008_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS-2008-18">[18]</a></sup> The metals are converted to ionized solutes by any of several methods, depending on the composition of the feedstock.  One representative method is fusion with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_peroxide" title="Sodium peroxide">sodium peroxide</a> followed by dissolution in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>, and solution in a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann-pt_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-pt-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirk-pt_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirk-pt-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">Osmium</a>, ruthenium, <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> are insoluble in aqua regia and readily precipitate, leaving the other metals in solution. Rhodium is separated from the residue by treatment with molten sodium bisulfate. The insoluble residue, containing Ru, Os, and Ir is treated with sodium oxide, in which Ir is insoluble, producing dissolved Ru and Os salts. After oxidation to the volatile oxides, <span class="chemf nowrap">RuO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> is separated from <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> by precipitation of (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>RuCl<sub>6</sub> with ammonium chloride or by distillation or extraction with organic solvents of the volatile osmium tetroxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> is used to reduce <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ruthenium chloride yielding a powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotton_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cotton-24">[24]</a></sup> The product is reduced using hydrogen, yielding the metal as a powder or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sponge_metal" title="Sponge metal">sponge metal</a> that can be treated with <a href="/wiki/Powder_metallurgy" title="Powder metallurgy">powder metallurgy</a> techniques or <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Arc_welding" title="Arc welding">arc welding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1969_126–138_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt_1969_126–138-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">Chemical compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Ruthenium_compounds" title="Category:Ruthenium compounds">Category:Ruthenium compounds</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> of ruthenium range from 0 to +8, and −2. The properties of ruthenium and osmium <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> are often similar. The +2, +3, and +4 states are the most common. The most prevalent precursor is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruthenium_trichloride" title="Ruthenium trichloride">ruthenium trichloride</a>, a red solid that is poorly defined chemically but versatile synthetically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotton_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-cotton-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_and_chalcogenides">Oxides and chalcogenides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oxides and chalcogenides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ruthenium can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidized</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(IV)_oxide" title="Ruthenium(IV) oxide">ruthenium(IV) oxide</a> (RuO<sub>2</sub>, oxidation state +4) which can in turn be oxidized by sodium metaperiodate to the volatile yellow tetrahedral <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_tetroxide" title="Ruthenium tetroxide">ruthenium tetroxide</a>, RuO<sub>4</sub>, an aggressive, strong oxidizing agent with structure and properties analogous to <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>. Like osmium tetroxide, ruthenium tetroxide is a potent fixative and stain for electron microscopy of organic materials, and is mostly used to reveal the structure of polymer samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-26">[26]</a></sup>  Dipotassium ruthenate (K<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>4</sub>, +6), and potassium perruthenate (KRuO<sub>4</sub>, +7) are also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Unlike osmium tetroxide, ruthenium tetroxide is less stable and is strong enough as an oxidising agent to oxidise dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and organic solvents like <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> at room temperature, and is easily reduced to ruthenate (<span class="chemf nowrap">RuO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) in aqueous alkaline solutions; it decomposes to form the dioxide above 100 °C. Unlike iron but like osmium, ruthenium does not form oxides in its lower +2 and +3 oxidation states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1080_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1080-28">[28]</a></sup> Ruthenium forms dichalcogenides only when reacted directly with the <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogens</a>, which are diamagnetic semiconductors crystallizing in the <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> structure and thus must contain ruthenium(II).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1080_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1080-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like iron, ruthenium does not readily form oxoanions, and prefers to achieve high coordination numbers with hydroxide ions instead. Ruthenium tetroxide is reduced by cold dilute <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> to form black potassium perruthenate, KRuO<sub>4</sub>, with ruthenium in the +7 oxidation state.  Potassium perruthenate can also be produced by oxidising potassium ruthenate, K<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>4</sub>, with chlorine gas. The perruthenate ion is unstable and is reduced by water to form the orange ruthenate. Potassium ruthenate may be synthesized by reacting ruthenium metal with potassium hydroxide and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1082_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1082-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some mixed oxides are also known, such as M<sup>II</sup>Ru<sup>IV</sup>O<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>3</sub>Ru<sup>V</sup>O<sub>4</sub>, Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Ru<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">V</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span>, and M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">II</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Ln<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">III</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ru<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">V</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1082_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1082-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides_and_oxyhalides">Halides and oxyhalides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Halides and oxyhalides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The highest known ruthenium halide is the <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_hexafluoride" title="Ruthenium hexafluoride">hexafluoride</a>, a dark brown solid that melts at 54 °C. It hydrolyzes violently upon contact with water and easily disproportionates to form a mixture of lower ruthenium fluorides, releasing fluorine gas. <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_pentafluoride" title="Ruthenium pentafluoride">Ruthenium pentafluoride</a> is a tetrameric dark green solid that is also readily hydrolyzed, melting at 86.5 °C. The yellow <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetrafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruthenium tetrafluoride (page does not exist)">ruthenium tetrafluoride</a> is probably also polymeric and can be formed by reducing the pentafluoride with <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. Among the binary compounds of ruthenium, these high oxidation states are known only in the oxides and fluorides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1083_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1083-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruthenium_trichloride" title="Ruthenium trichloride">Ruthenium trichloride</a> is a well-known compound, existing in a black α-form and a dark brown β-form: the trihydrate is red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1084_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1084-31">[31]</a></sup> Of the known trihalides, trifluoride is dark brown and decomposes above 650 °C, tetrabromide is dark-brown and decomposes above 400 °C, and triiodide is black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1083_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1083-30">[30]</a></sup> Of the dihalides, difluoride is not known, dichloride is brown, dibromide is black, and diiodide is blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1083_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1083-30">[30]</a></sup> The only known oxyhalide is the pale green ruthenium(VI) oxyfluoride, RuOF<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1084_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1084-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_and_organometallic_complexes">Coordination and organometallic complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Coordination and organometallic complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organoruthenium_chemistry" title="Organoruthenium chemistry">Organoruthenium chemistry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II)-chloride-powder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="1260" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg/220px-Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg/330px-Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg/440px-Tris%28bipyridine%29ruthenium%28II%29-chloride-powder.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II)-chloride-powder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II) chloride.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg"><img alt="Skeletal formula of Grubbs' catalyst." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="332" data-file-width="512" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg/220px-Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg/330px-Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg/440px-Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grubbs_catalyst_Gen2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grubbs' catalyst, which earned a Nobel Prize for its inventor, is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkene_metathesis" title="Alkene metathesis">alkene metathesis</a> reactions.</div></div></div>
<p>Ruthenium forms a variety of coordination complexes. Examples are the many pentammine derivatives [Ru(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub>L]<sup>n+</sup> that often exist for both Ru(II) and Ru(III). Derivatives of <a href="/wiki/Bipyridine" title="Bipyridine">bipyridine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terpyridine" title="Terpyridine">terpyridine</a> are numerous, best known being the <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">luminescent</a> <a href="/wiki/Tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II)_chloride" title="Tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II) chloride">tris(bipyridine)ruthenium(II) chloride</a>.
</p><p>Ruthenium forms a wide range compounds with carbon-ruthenium bonds. <a href="/wiki/Grubbs%27_catalyst" title="Grubbs' catalyst">Grubbs' catalyst</a> is used for alkene metathesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ruthenocene" title="Ruthenocene">Ruthenocene</a> is analogous to ferrocene structurally, but exhibits distinctive redox properties. The colorless liquid <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_pentacarbonyl" title="Ruthenium pentacarbonyl">ruthenium pentacarbonyl</a> converts in the absence of CO pressure to the dark red solid <a href="/wiki/Triruthenium_dodecacarbonyl" title="Triruthenium dodecacarbonyl">triruthenium dodecacarbonyl</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(III)_chloride" title="Ruthenium(III) chloride">Ruthenium trichloride</a> reacts with carbon monoxide to give many derivatives including RuHCl(CO)(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and Ru(CO)<sub>2</sub>(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roper%27s_complex" title="Roper's complex">Roper's complex</a>). Heating solutions of ruthenium trichloride in alcohols with <a href="/wiki/Triphenylphosphine" title="Triphenylphosphine">triphenylphosphine</a> gives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tris(triphenylphosphine)ruthenium_dichloride" title="Tris(triphenylphosphine)ruthenium dichloride">tris(triphenylphosphine)ruthenium dichloride</a> (RuCl<sub>2</sub>(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>), which converts to the hydride complex chlorohydridotris(triphenylphosphine)ruthenium(II) (RuHCl(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cotton_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-cotton-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though naturally occurring platinum alloys containing all six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum-group_metal" title="Platinum-group metal">platinum-group metals</a> were used for a long time by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> Americans and known as a material to European chemists from the mid-16th century, not until the mid-18th century was platinum identified as a pure element. That natural platinum contained palladium, rhodium, osmium and iridium was discovered in the first decade of the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks8_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks8-33">[33]</a></sup> Platinum in <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvial sands</a> of Russian rivers gave access to raw material for use in plates and medals and for the minting of <a href="/wiki/Ruble" title="Ruble">ruble</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coins" title="Coins">coins</a>, starting in 1828.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roubles_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roubles-34">[34]</a></sup> Residues from platinum production for coinage were available in the Russian Empire, and therefore most of the research on them was done in Eastern Europe.
</p><p>It is possible that the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/J%C4%99drzej_%C5%9Aniadecki" title="Jędrzej Śniadecki">Jędrzej Śniadecki</a> isolated element 44 (which he called "vestium" after the asteroid <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">Vesta</a> discovered shortly before) from South American platinum ores in 1807. He published an announcement of his discovery in 1808.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> His work was never confirmed, however, and he later withdrew his claim of discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Berzelius" title="Jöns Berzelius">Jöns Berzelius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Osann" title="Gottfried Osann">Gottfried Osann</a> nearly discovered ruthenium in 1827.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> They examined residues that were left after dissolving crude platinum from the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>. Berzelius did not find any unusual metals, but Osann thought he found three new metals, which he called pluranium, ruthenium, and polinium. This discrepancy led to a long-standing controversy between Berzelius and Osann about the composition of the residues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoRu_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoRu-37">[37]</a></sup> As Osann was not able to repeat his isolation of ruthenium, he eventually relinquished his claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoRu_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoRu-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osann2_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osann2-38">[38]</a></sup> The name "ruthenium" was chosen by Osann because the analysed samples stemmed from the Ural Mountains in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osann_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osann-39">[39]</a></sup> The name itself derives from <a href="/wiki/Ruthenia" title="Ruthenia">Ruthenia</a>, the Latin word for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rus%27_(name)" title="Rus' (name)">Rus'</a>, a historical area that included present-day <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.
</p><p>In 1844, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ernst_Claus" title="Karl Ernst Claus">Karl Ernst Claus</a>, a Russian scientist of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltic_German" title="Baltic German">Baltic German</a> descent, showed that the compounds prepared by Gottfried Osann contained small amounts of ruthenium, which Claus had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks8_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks8-33">[33]</a></sup> Claus isolated ruthenium from the platinum residues of rouble production while he was working in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kazan_University" title="Kazan University">Kazan University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazan" title="Kazan">Kazan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoRu_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoRu-37">[37]</a></sup> the same way its heavier congener osmium had been discovered four decades earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1071_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1071-16">[16]</a></sup> Claus showed that ruthenium oxide contained a new metal and obtained 6 grams of ruthenium from the part of crude platinum that is insoluble in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoRu_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoRu-37">[37]</a></sup> Choosing the name for the new element, Claus stated: "I named the new body, in honour of my Motherland, ruthenium. I had every right to call it by this name because Mr. Osann relinquished his ruthenium and the word does not yet exist in chemistry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoRu_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoRu-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because it hardens platinum and palladium alloys, ruthenium is used in <a href="/wiki/Switch#Contacts" title="Switch">electrical contacts</a>, where a thin film is sufficient to achieve the desired durability. With similar properties and lower cost than rhodium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_1969_126–138_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt_1969_126–138-25">[25]</a></sup> electric contacts are a major use of ruthenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-YB-2006_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-YB-2006-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The plate is applied to the base by electroplating<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">sputtering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ruthenium dioxide with <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> ruthenates are used in thick-film chip resistors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> These two electronic applications account for 50% of the ruthenium consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ruthenium is seldom alloyed with metals outside the platinum group, where small quantities improve some properties. The added corrosion resistance in <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> alloys led to the development of a special alloy with 0.1% ruthenium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Ruthenium is also used in some advanced high-temperature single-crystal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a>, with applications that include the turbines in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_engines" title="Jet engines">jet engines</a>. Several nickel based superalloy compositions are described, such as EPM-102 (with 3% Ru), TMS-162 (with 6% Ru), TMS-138,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and TMS-174,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> the latter two containing 6% <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">Fountain pen</a> nibs are frequently tipped with ruthenium alloy. From 1944 onward, the famous <a href="/wiki/Parker_51" title="Parker 51">Parker 51</a> fountain pen was fitted with the "RU" nib, a 14K gold nib tipped with 96.2% ruthenium and 3.8% <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ruthenium is a component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed-metal_oxide" title="Mixed-metal oxide">mixed-metal oxide</a> (MMO) anodes used for cathodic protection of underground and submerged structures, and for electrolytic cells for such processes as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_production" title="Chlorine production">generating chlorine</a> from salt water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> of some ruthenium complexes is quenched by oxygen, finding use in <a href="/wiki/Optode" title="Optode">optode</a> sensors for oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_red" title="Ruthenium red">Ruthenium red</a>, [(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub>Ru-O-Ru(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>-O-Ru(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>6+</sup>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_stain" title="Biological stain">biological stain</a> used to stain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyanion" title="Polyanion">polyanionic</a> molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleic_acids" title="Nucleic acids">nucleic acids</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_microscopy" title="Light microscopy">light microscopy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_microscopy" title="Electron microscopy">electron microscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The beta-decaying isotope 106 of ruthenium is used in radiotherapy of eye tumors, mainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant_melanoma" title="Malignant melanoma">malignant melanomas</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Uvea" title="Uvea">uvea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Ruthenium-centered complexes are being researched for possible anticancer properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Compared with platinum complexes, those of ruthenium show greater resistance to hydrolysis and more selective action on tumors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_tetroxide" title="Ruthenium tetroxide">Ruthenium tetroxide</a> exposes latent fingerprints by reacting on contact with fatty oils or fats with sebaceous contaminants and producing brown/black ruthenium dioxide pigment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysis">Catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many ruthenium-containing compounds exhibit useful catalytic properties.  The catalysts are conveniently divided into those that are soluble in the reaction medium, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homogeneous_catalyst" title="Homogeneous catalyst">homogeneous catalysts</a>, and those that are not, which are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalyst" title="Heterogeneous catalyst">heterogeneous catalysts</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Homogeneous_catalysis">Homogeneous catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Homogeneous catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Solutions containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ruthenium_trichloride" title="Ruthenium trichloride">ruthenium trichloride</a> are highly active for <a href="/wiki/Olefin_metathesis" title="Olefin metathesis">olefin metathesis</a>.  Such catalysts are used commercially for the production of polynorbornene for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KO_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-KO-59">[59]</a></sup> Well defined ruthenium <a href="/wiki/Carbene" title="Carbene">carbene</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkylidene" title="Alkylidene">alkylidene</a> complexes show comparable reactivity and provide mechanistic insights into the industrial processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Grubbs%27_catalyst" title="Grubbs' catalyst">Grubbs' catalysts</a> for example have been employed in the preparation of drugs and advanced materials.
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polynbornene.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="767" data-file-width="3661" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Polynbornene.png/440px-Polynbornene.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Polynbornene.png/660px-Polynbornene.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Polynbornene.png/880px-Polynbornene.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polynbornene.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>RuCl3-catalyzed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring-opening_metathesis_polymerization" title="Ring-opening metathesis polymerization">ring-opening metathesis polymerization</a> reaction giving polynorbornene..</div></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Ruthenium complexes are highly active catalyst for <a href="/wiki/Transfer_hydrogenation" title="Transfer hydrogenation">transfer hydrogenations</a> (sometimes referred to as "borrowing hydrogen" reactions).  This process is employed for the <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Asymmetric hydrogenation">enantioselective hydrogenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">imines</a>. This reaction exploits using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral" title="Chiral">chiral</a> ruthenium complexes introduced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ryoji_Noyori" title="Ryoji Noyori">Ryoji Noyori</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citation_21_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-citation_21-61">[61]</a></sup> For example, (cymene)Ru(S,S-Ts<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DPEN" title="DPEN">DPEN</a>) catalyzes the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Benzil" title="Benzil">benzil</a> into (<i>R,R</i>)-hydro<a href="/wiki/Benzoin_(organic_compound)" title="Benzoin (organic compound)">benzoin</a>.  In this reaction, <a href="/wiki/Formate" title="Formate">formate</a> and water/alcohol serve as the source of H<sub>2</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RuCl(S,S-TsDPEN)(cymene)-catalysed_R,R-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="155" data-file-width="511" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg/440px-RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg/660px-RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg/880px-RuCl%28S%2CS-TsDPEN%29%28cymene%29-catalysed_R%2CR-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RuCl(S,S-TsDPEN)(cymene)-catalysed_R,R-hydrobenzoin_synthesis.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>[RuCl(<i>S</i>,<i>S</i>-TsDPEN)(cymene)]-catalysed (<i>R</i>,<i>R</i>)-hydrobenzoin synthesis (yield 100%, <a href="/wiki/Enantiomeric_excess" title="Enantiomeric excess">ee</a> &gt;99%)</div></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> was awarded in 2001 to <a href="/wiki/Ry%C5%8Dji_Noyori" title="Ryōji Noyori">Ryōji Noyori</a> for contributions to the field of <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Asymmetric hydrogenation">asymmetric hydrogenation</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heterogeneous_catalysis">Heterogeneous catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Heterogeneous catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Ruthenium-promoted cobalt catalysts are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_synthesis" title="Fischer-Tropsch synthesis">Fischer-Tropsch synthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emerging_applications">Emerging applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Emerging applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some ruthenium complexes <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorb light</a> throughout the visible spectrum and are being actively researched for <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> technologies. For example, Ruthenium-based compounds have been used for light absorption in <a href="/wiki/Dye-sensitized_solar_cell" title="Dye-sensitized solar cell">dye-sensitized solar cells</a>, a promising new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-cost_solar_cell" title="Low-cost solar cell">low-cost solar cell</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many ruthenium-based oxides show very unusual properties, such as a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_critical_point" title="Quantum critical point">quantum critical point</a> behavior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> exotic <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> (in its <a href="/wiki/Strontium_ruthenate" title="Strontium ruthenate">strontium ruthenate</a> form),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
