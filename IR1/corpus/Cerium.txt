<p><b>Cerium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ce</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 58. Cerium is a <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">soft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> and silvery-white <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that tarnishes when exposed to air, and it is soft enough to be cut with a knife. Cerium is the second element in the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, and while it often shows the +3 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> characteristic of the series, it also exceptionally has a stable +4 state that does not oxidize water. It is also traditionally considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> one of the <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth elements</a>. Cerium has no biological role and is not very toxic.
</p><p>Despite always occurring in combination with the other rare-earth elements in minerals such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> groups, cerium is easy to extract from its ores, as it can be distinguished among the lanthanides by its unique ability to be oxidized to the +4 state. It is the most common of the lanthanides, followed by <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>. It is the 26th-most <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements"> abundant element</a>, making up 66 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> of the Earth's crust, half as much as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and five times as much as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.
</p><p>Cerium was the first of the lanthanides to be discovered, in <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4s" title="Bastnäs">Bastnäs</a>, Sweden by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Jöns Jakob Berzelius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hisinger" title="Wilhelm Hisinger">Wilhelm Hisinger</a> in 1803, and independently by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a> in Germany in the same year. In 1839 <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> became the first to isolate the metal. Today, cerium and its compounds have a variety of uses: for example, <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a> is used to polish glass and is an important part of <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a>. Cerium metal is used in <a href="/wiki/Ferrocerium" title="Ferrocerium">ferrocerium</a> lighters for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> properties. Cerium-doped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YAG" title="YAG">YAG</a> phosphor is used in blue <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> to produce white light.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical">Physical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cerium is the second element of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series. In the periodic table, it appears between the lanthanides <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> to its left and <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a> to its right, and above the <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">ductile</a> metal with a hardness similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup> Its 58 electrons are arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">configuration</a> [Xe]4f<sup>1</sup>5d<sup>1</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>, of which the four outer electrons are <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a>. Immediately after lanthanum, the 4f <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">orbitals</a> suddenly contract and are lowered in energy to the point that they participate readily in chemical reactions; however, this effect is not yet strong enough at cerium and thus the 5d <a href="/wiki/Electron_shell#subshells" title="Electron shell">subshell</a> is still occupied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1232_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1232-6">[6]</a></sup> Most lanthanides can use only three electrons as valence electrons, as afterwards the remaining 4f electrons are too strongly bound: cerium is an exception because of the stability of the empty f-shell in Ce<sup>4+</sup> and the fact that it comes very early in the lanthanide series, where the nuclear charge is still low enough until <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> to allow the removal of the fourth valence electron by chemical means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holleman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holleman-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1633" data-file-width="2400" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg/310px-Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg/465px-Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg/620px-Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cerium_phase_diagram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Phase diagram of cerium</div></div></div></div>
<p>Four <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropic</a> forms of cerium are known to exist at standard pressure, and are given the common labels of α to δ:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KGP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-KGP-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The high-temperature form, δ-cerium, has a bcc (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centred_cubic" title="Body-centred cubic">body-centred cubic</a>) crystal structure and exists above 726 °C.</li>
<li>The stable form below 726 °C to approximately room temperature is γ-cerium, with an fcc (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centred_cubic" title="Face-centred cubic">face-centred cubic</a>) crystal structure.</li>
<li>The dhcp (double <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_lattice_system" title="Hexagonal lattice system">hexagonal close-packed</a>) form β-cerium is the equilibrium structure approximately from room temperature to −150 °C.</li>
<li>The fcc form α-cerium is stable below about −150 °C; it has a density of 8.16 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.</li>
<li>Other solid phases occurring only at high pressures are shown on the phase diagram.</li>
<li>Both γ and β forms are quite stable at room temperature, although the equilibrium transformation temperature is estimated at around 75 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KGP_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-KGP-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Cerium has a variable <a href="/wiki/Electronic_structure" title="Electronic structure">electronic structure</a>. The energy of the 4f electron is nearly the same as that of the outer 5d and 6s electrons that are delocalized in the metallic state, and only a small amount of energy is required to change the relative occupancy of these electronic levels. This gives rise to dual valence states. For example, a volume change of about 10% occurs when cerium is subjected to high pressures or low temperatures. It appears that the valence changes from about 3 to 4 when it is cooled or compressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>At lower temperatures the behavior of cerium is complicated by the slow rates of transformation. Transformation temperatures are subject to substantial hysteresis and values quoted here are approximate. Upon cooling below −15 °C, γ-cerium starts to change to β-cerium, but the transformation involves a volume increase and, as more β forms, the internal stresses build up and suppress further transformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KGP_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-KGP-8">[8]</a></sup> Cooling below approximately −160 °C will start formation of α-cerium but this is only from remaining γ-cerium. β-cerium does not significantly transform to α-cerium except in the presence of stress or deformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KGP_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-KGP-8">[8]</a></sup> At atmospheric pressure, liquid cerium is more dense than its solid form at the melting
point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cerium" title="Isotopes of cerium">Isotopes of cerium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring cerium is made up of four isotopes: <sup>136</sup>Ce (0.19%), <sup>138</sup>Ce (0.25%), <sup>140</sup>Ce (88.4%), and <sup>142</sup>Ce (11.1%). All four are observationally stable, though the light isotopes <sup>136</sup>Ce and <sup>138</sup>Ce are theoretically expected to undergo inverse <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, and the heaviest isotope <sup>142</sup>Ce is expected to undergo double beta decay to <sup>142</sup>Nd or alpha decay to <sup>138</sup>Ba. Additionally, <sup>140</sup>Ce would release energy upon <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_fission" title="Spontaneous fission">spontaneous fission</a>. None of these decay modes have yet been observed, though the double beta decay of <sup>136</sup>Ce, <sup>138</sup>Ce, and <sup>142</sup>Ce has been experimentally searched for. The current experimental limits for their half-lives are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><sup>136</sup>Ce: &gt;3.8×10<sup>16</sup> y</dd>
<dd><sup>138</sup>Ce: &gt;1.5×10<sup>14</sup> y</dd>
<dd><sup>142</sup>Ce: &gt;5×10<sup>16</sup> y</dd></dl>
<p>All other cerium isotopes are <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">synthetic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactive</a>. The most stable of them are <sup>144</sup>Ce with a half-life of 284.9 days, <sup>139</sup>Ce with a half-life of 137.6 days, <sup>143</sup>Ce with a half-life of 33.04 days, and <sup>141</sup>Ce with a half-life of 32.5 days. All other radioactive cerium isotopes have half-lives under four days, and most of them have half-lives under ten minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-12">[12]</a></sup> The isotopes between <sup>140</sup>Ce and <sup>144</sup>Ce inclusive occur as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission products</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-12">[12]</a></sup> The primary decay mode of the isotopes lighter than <sup>140</sup>Ce is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_plus_decay" title="Beta plus decay">inverse beta decay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_lanthanum" title="Isotopes of lanthanum">isotopes of lanthanum</a>, while that of the heavier isotopes is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_praseodymium" title="Isotopes of praseodymium">isotopes of praseodymium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The great rarity of the proton-rich <sup>136</sup>Ce and <sup>138</sup>Ce is explained by the fact that they cannot be made in the most common processes of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">stellar nucleosynthesis</a> for elements beyond iron, the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a> (slow neutron capture) and the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a> (rapid neutron capture). This is so because they are bypassed by the reaction flow of the s-process, and the r-process nuclides are blocked from decaying to them by more neutron-rich stable nuclides. Such nuclei are called <a href="/wiki/P-nuclei" title="P-nuclei">p-nuclei</a>, and their origin is not yet well understood: some speculated mechanisms for their formation include proton capture as well as <a href="/wiki/Photodisintegration" title="Photodisintegration">photodisintegration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron-13">[13]</a></sup> <sup>140</sup>Ce is the most common isotope of cerium, as it can be produced in both the s- and r-processes, while <sup>142</sup>Ce can only be produced in the r-process. Another reason for the abundance of <sup>140</sup>Ce is that it is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_nucleus" title="Magic nucleus">magic nucleus</a>, having a closed neutron shell (it has 82 neutrons), and hence it has a very low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-section_(physics)" title="Cross-section (physics)">cross-section</a> towards further neutron capture. Although its proton number of 58 is not magic, it is granted additional stability, as its eight additional protons past the magic number 50 enter and complete the 1 g<sub>7/2</sub> proton orbital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron-13">[13]</a></sup> The abundances of the cerium isotopes may differ very slightly in natural sources, because <sup>138</sup>Ce and <sup>140</sup>Ce are the daughters of the long-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_radionuclide" title="Primordial radionuclide">primordial radionuclides</a> <sup>138</sup>La and <sup>144</sup>Nd, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cerium tarnishes in air, forming a <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spalling</a> oxide layer like <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> rust; a centimeter-sized sample of cerium metal corrodes completely in about a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It burns readily at 150 °C to form the pale-yellow <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a>, also known as ceria:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ce + O<sub>2</sub> → CeO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This may be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_oxide" title="Cerium(III) oxide">cerium(III) oxide</a> with hydrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1238_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1238-16">[16]</a></sup> Cerium metal is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a>, meaning that when it is ground or scratched, the resulting shavings catch fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Elements_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Elements-17">[17]</a></sup> This reactivity conforms to <a href="/wiki/Periodic_trends" title="Periodic trends">periodic trends</a>, since cerium is one of the first and hence one of the largest lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1235_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1235-18">[18]</a></sup> Cerium(IV) oxide has the <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> structure, similarly to the dioxides of praseodymium and <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a>. Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric" title="Nonstoichiometric">nonstoichiometric</a> <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogenides</a> are also known, along with the trivalent Ce<sub>2</sub>Z<sub>3</sub> (Z = <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">Se</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">Te</a>). The monochalcogenides CeZ conduct electricity and would better be formulated as Ce<sup>3+</sup>Z<sup>2−</sup>e<sup>−</sup>. While CeZ<sub>2</sub> are known, they are polychalcogenides with cerium(III): cerium(IV) chalcogenides remain unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1238_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1238-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cerium(IV)_oxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="605" data-file-width="597" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg/170px-Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg/255px-Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg/340px-Cerium%28IV%29_oxide.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cerium(IV)_oxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cerium(IV) oxide</div></div></div>
<p>Cerium is a highly electropositive metal and reacts with water. The reaction is slow with cold water but speeds up with increasing temperature, producing cerium(III) hydroxide and hydrogen gas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ce (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Ce(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Cerium metal reacts with all the halogens to give trihalides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ce (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 CeF<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Ce (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 CeCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Ce (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 CeBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Ce (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 CeI<sub>3</sub> (s) [yellow]</dd></dl>
<p>Reaction with excess fluorine produces the stable white tetrafluoride CeF<sub>4</sub>; the other tetrahalides are not known. Of the dihalides, only the bronze diiodide CeI<sub>2</sub> is known; like the diiodides of lanthanum, praseodymium, and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>, this is a cerium(III) <a href="/wiki/Electride" title="Electride">electride</a> compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1240_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1240-19">[19]</a></sup> True cerium(II) compounds are restricted to a few unusual organocerium complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cerium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the colorless Ce<sup>3+</sup> ions, which exist as a [Ce(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ce (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Ce<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>The solubility of cerium is much higher in <a href="/wiki/Methanesulfonic_acid" title="Methanesulfonic acid">methanesulfonic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kreh_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kreh-22">[22]</a></sup> Cerium(III) and terbium(III) have <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> absorption bands of relatively high intensity compared with the other lanthanides, as their configurations (one electron more than an empty or half-filled f-subshell respectively) make it easier for the extra f electron to undergo f→d transitions instead of the forbidden f→f transitions of the other lanthanides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1242_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1242-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_sulfate" title="Cerium(III) sulfate">Cerium(III) sulfate</a> is one of the few salts whose <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in water decreases with rising temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RegerGoode2009_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-RegerGoode2009-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="581" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg/220px-Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg/330px-Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg/440px-Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceric_ammonium_nitrate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ceric ammonium nitrate</div></div></div>
<p>Cerium(IV) aqueous solutions may be prepared by reacting cerium(III) solutions with the strong oxidising agents <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peroxodisulfate" title="Peroxodisulfate">peroxodisulfate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bismuthate" title="Bismuthate">bismuthate</a>. The value of <i>E</i><sup>⦵</sup>(Ce<sup>4+</sup>/Ce<sup>3+</sup>) varies widely depending on conditions due to the relative ease of complexation and hydrolysis with various anions, though +1.72 V is a usually representative value; that for <i>E</i><sup>⦵</sup>(Ce<sup>3+</sup>/Ce) is −2.34 V. Cerium is the only lanthanide which has important aqueous and coordination chemistry in the +4 oxidation state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1244_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1244-25">[25]</a></sup> Due to <a href="/wiki/Charge-transfer_complex" title="Charge-transfer complex">ligand-to-metal charge transfer</a>, aqueous cerium(IV) ions are orange-yellow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SroorEdelmann2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-SroorEdelmann2012-26">[26]</a></sup> Aqueous cerium(IV) is metastable in water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and is a strong oxidising agent that oxidizes <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1244_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1244-25">[25]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Ceric_ammonium_nitrate" title="Ceric ammonium nitrate">ceric ammonium nitrate</a> is a common oxidising agent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, releasing organic ligands from <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyls</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Belousov%E2%80%93Zhabotinsky_reaction" title="Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction">Belousov–Zhabotinsky reaction</a>, cerium oscillates between the +4 and +3 oxidation states to catalyse the reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Cerium(IV) salts, especially <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_sulfate" title="Cerium(IV) sulfate">cerium(IV) sulfate</a>, are often used as standard reagents for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volumetric_analysis" title="Volumetric analysis">volumetric analysis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cerimetry" title="Cerimetry">cerimetric titrations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The nitrate complex [Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> is the most common cerium complex encountered when using cerium(IV) is an oxidising agent: it and its cerium(III) analogue [Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> have 12-coordinate <a href="/wiki/Regular_icosahedron" title="Regular icosahedron">icosahedral</a> molecular geometry, while [Ce(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> has 10-coordinate bicapped <a href="/wiki/Snub_disphenoid" title="Snub disphenoid">dodecadeltahedral</a> molecular geometry. Cerium nitrates also form 4:3 and 1:1 complexes with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18-crown-6" title="18-crown-6">18-crown-6</a> (the ratio referring to that between cerium and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_ether" title="Crown ether">crown ether</a>). Halogen-containing complex ions such as <span class="chemf nowrap">CeF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span></span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">CeF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>, and the orange <span class="chemf nowrap">CeCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> are also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1244_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1244-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Organocerium_chemistry" title="Organocerium chemistry">Organocerium chemistry</a> is similar to that of the other <a href="/wiki/Organolanthanide_chemistry" title="Organolanthanide chemistry">lanthanides</a>, being primarily that of the <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl" title="Cyclopentadienyl">cyclopentadienyl</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclooctatetraenyl" title="Cyclooctatetraenyl">cyclooctatetraenyl</a> compounds. The cerium(III) cyclooctatetraenyl compound has the <a href="/wiki/Uranocene" title="Uranocene">uranocene</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1248_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1248-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Cerium.28IV.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cerium(IV)">Cerium(IV)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cerium(IV)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the common name of cerium(IV) compounds, the Japanese spectroscopist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Akio_Kotani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Akio Kotani (page does not exist)">Akio Kotani</a> wrote "there is no genuine example of cerium(IV)". The reason for this can be seen in the structure of ceria itself, which always contains some octahedral vacancies where oxygen atoms would be expected to go and could be better considered a <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric compound</a> with chemical formula CeO<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub>. Furthermore, each cerium atom in ceria does not lose all four of its valence electrons, but retains a partial hold on the last one, resulting in an oxidation state between +3 and +4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature-33">[33]</a></sup> Even supposedly purely tetravalent compounds such as CeRh<sub>3</sub>, CeCo<sub>5</sub>, or ceria itself have <a href="/wiki/X-ray_photoelectron_spectroscopy" title="X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy">X-ray photoemission</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-ray_absorption_spectroscopy" title="X-ray absorption spectroscopy">X-ray absorption</a> spectra more characteristic of intermediate-valence compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The 4f electron in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cerocene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cerocene (page does not exist)">cerocene</a>, Ce(C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>8</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, is poised ambiguously between being localized and delocalized and this compound is also considered intermediate-valent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_(22381131691).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="950" data-file-width="950" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg/220px-Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg/330px-Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg/440px-Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_%2822381131691%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceres_-_RC3_-_Haulani_Crater_(22381131691).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The dwarf planet <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>, after which cerium is named</div></div></div>
<p>Cerium was discovered in <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4s" title="Bastnäs">Bastnäs</a> in Sweden by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Jöns Jakob Berzelius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hisinger" title="Wilhelm Hisinger">Wilhelm Hisinger</a>, and independently in Germany by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a>, both in 1803.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-35">[35]</a></sup> Cerium was <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)#Name" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">named</a> by Berzelius after the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>, discovered two years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup> The dwarf planet itself is named after the Roman goddess of agriculture, grain crops, fertility and motherly relationships, <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(mythology)" title="Ceres (mythology)">Ceres</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cerium was originally isolated in the form of its oxide, which was named <i>ceria</i>, a term that is still used. The metal itself was too electropositive to be isolated by then-current smelting technology, a characteristic of rare-earth metals in general. After the development of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> five years later, the earths soon yielded the metals they contained. Ceria, as isolated in 1803, contained all of the lanthanides present in the cerite ore from Bastnäs, Sweden, and thus only contained about 45% of what is now known to be pure ceria. It was not until <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> succeeded in removing lanthana and <a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">"didymia"</a> in the late 1830s that ceria was obtained pure. Wilhelm Hisinger was a wealthy mine-owner and amateur scientist, and sponsor of Berzelius. He owned and controlled the mine at Bastnäs, and had been trying for years to find out the composition of the abundant heavy gangue rock (the "Tungsten of Bastnäs", which despite its name contained no <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>), now known as cerite, that he had in his mine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup> Mosander and his family lived for many years in the same house as Berzelius, and Mosander was undoubtedly persuaded by Berzelius to investigate ceria further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cerium is the most abundant of all the lanthanides, making up 66 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> of the Earth's crust; this value is just behind that of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> (68 ppm), and cerium is even more abundant than common metals such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> (13 ppm) and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> (2.1 ppm). Thus, despite its position as one of the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metal" title="Rare-earth metal">rare-earth metals</a>, cerium is actually not rare at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1294_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1294-38">[38]</a></sup> Cerium content in the soil varies between 2 and 150 ppm, with an average of 50 ppm; seawater contains 1.5 parts per trillion of cerium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup> Cerium occurs in various minerals, but the most important commercial sources are the minerals of the <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> groups, where it makes up about half of the lanthanide content. Monazite-(Ce) being is the most common representative of the monazites, with "-Ce" being the Levinson suffix informing on the dominance of the particular REE element representative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>). Also the cerium-dominant bastnäsite-(Ce) is the most important of the bastnäsites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Cerium is the easiest lanthanide to extract from its minerals because it is the only one that can reach a stable +4 oxidation state in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-44">[44]</a></sup> Because of the decreased solubility of cerium in the +4 oxidation state, cerium is sometimes depleted from rocks relative to the other rare-earth elements and is incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>, since Ce<sup>4+</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">Zr</a><sup>4+</sup> have the same charge and similar ionic radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In extreme cases, cerium(IV) can form its own minerals separated from the other rare-earth elements, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerianite" title="Cerianite">cerianite</a> (correctly named cerianite-(Ce)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>), (Ce,Th)O<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="2000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png/350px-Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png/525px-Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png/700px-Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bastnaesite_crystal_structure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of bastnäsite-(Ce). Color code: carbon, C, blue-gray; fluorine, F, green; cerium, Ce, white; oxygen, O, red.</div></div></div>
<p>Bastnäsite, Ln<sup>III</sup>CO<sub>3</sub>F, is usually lacking in <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and the heavy lanthanides beyond <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>, and hence the extraction of cerium from it is quite direct. First, the bastnäsite is purified, using dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> impurities. The ore is then roasted in the air to oxidize it to the lanthanide oxides: while most of the lanthanides will be oxidized to the sesquioxides Ln<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, cerium will be oxidized to the dioxide CeO<sub>2</sub>. This is insoluble in water and can be leached out with 0.5 M hydrochloric acid, leaving the other lanthanides behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The procedure for monazite, (Ln,Th)PO<sub>4</sub>, which usually contains all the rare earths, as well as thorium, is more involved. Monazite, because of its magnetic properties, can be separated by repeated electromagnetic separation. After separation, it is treated with hot concentrated sulfuric acid to produce water-soluble sulfates of rare earths. The acidic filtrates are partially neutralized with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> to pH 3–4. Thorium precipitates out of solution as hydroxide and is removed. After that, the solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths to their insoluble <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalates</a>. The oxalates are converted to oxides by annealing. The oxides are dissolved in nitric acid, but cerium oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub> and hence precipitates out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-11">[11]</a></sup> Care must be taken when handling some of the residues as they contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radium-228" title="Radium-228"><sup>228</sup>Ra</a>, the daughter of <sup>232</sup>Th, which is a strong gamma emitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1268" data-file-width="1002" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/170px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/255px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg/340px-Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auer_von_Welsbach.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carl Auer von Welsbach, who discovered many applications of cerium</div></div></div>
<p>The first use of cerium was in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">gas mantles</a>, invented by the Austrian chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>. In 1885, he had previously experimented with mixtures of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, lanthanum, and yttrium oxides, but these gave green-tinted light and were unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Six years later, he discovered that pure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thorium_oxide" title="Thorium oxide">thorium oxide</a> produced a much better, though blue, light, and that mixing it with cerium dioxide resulted in a bright white light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Additionally, cerium dioxide also acts as a catalyst for the combustion of thorium oxide. This resulted in great commercial success for von Welsbach and his invention, and created great demand for thorium; its production resulted in a large amount of lanthanides being simultaneously extracted as by-products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1228_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1228-54">[54]</a></sup> Applications were soon found for them, especially in the pyrophoric alloy known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mischmetall" title="Mischmetall">mischmetall</a>" composed of 50% cerium, 25% lanthanum, and the remainder being the other lanthanides, that is used widely for lighter flints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1228_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1228-54">[54]</a></sup> Usually, iron is also added to form an alloy known as <a href="/wiki/Ferrocerium" title="Ferrocerium">ferrocerium</a>, also invented by von Welsbach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-55">[55]</a></sup> Due to the chemical similarities of the lanthanides, chemical separation is not usually required for their applications, such as the mixing of mischmetall into steel to improve its strength and workability, or as catalysts for the cracking of petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood1229_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood1229-44">[44]</a></sup> This property of cerium saved the life of writer <a href="/wiki/Primo_Levi" title="Primo Levi">Primo Levi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a>, when he found a supply of ferrocerium alloy and bartered it for food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ceria is the most widely used compound of cerium. The main application of ceria is as a polishing compound, for example in <a href="/wiki/Chemical-mechanical_planarization" title="Chemical-mechanical planarization">chemical-mechanical planarization</a> (CMP). In this application, ceria has replaced other metal oxides for the production of high-quality optical surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-55">[55]</a></sup> Major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> applications for the lower sesquioxide are as a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> for the oxidation of CO and <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></a> emissions in the exhaust gases from motor vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Ceria has also been used as a substitute for its radioactive congener <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thoria</a>, for example in the manufacture of electrodes used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">gas tungsten arc welding</a>, where ceria as an alloying element improves arc stability and ease of starting while decreasing burn-off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_sulfate" title="Cerium(IV) sulfate">Cerium(IV) sulfate</a> is used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidising_agent" title="Oxidising agent">oxidising agent</a> in quantitative analysis. Cerium(IV) in <a href="/wiki/Methanesulfonic_acid" title="Methanesulfonic acid">methanesulfonic acid</a> solutions is applied in industrial scale electrosynthesis as a recyclable oxidant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Ceric ammonium nitrate is used as an oxidant in organic chemistry and in etching electronic components, and as a primary standard for quantitative analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-appl-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The photostability of pigments can be enhanced by the addition of cerium. It provides pigments with light fastness and prevents clear polymers from darkening in sunlight. Television glass plates are subject to electron bombardment, which tends to darken them by creation of <a href="/wiki/F-center" title="F-center">F-center</a> color centers. This effect is suppressed by addition of cerium oxide. Cerium is also an essential component of phosphors used in TV screens and fluorescent lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-r1-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cerium_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-cerium-63">[63]</a></sup> Cerium sulfide forms a red pigment that stays stable up to 350 °C. The pigment is a nontoxic alternative to <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_sulfide" title="Cadmium sulfide">cadmium sulfide</a> pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cerium is used as alloying element in aluminum to create castable eutectic alloys, Al-Ce alloys with 6–16 wt.% Ce, to which Mg and/or Si can be further added; these alloys have excellent high temperature strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Cerium has no known biological role in humans, but is not very toxic either; it does not accumulate in the food chain to any appreciable extent. Because it often occurs together with calcium in phosphate minerals, and bones are primarily <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphate" title="Calcium phosphate">calcium phosphate</a>, cerium can accumulate in bones in small amounts that are not considered dangerous. Cerium, like the other lanthanides, is known to affect human metabolism, lowering cholesterol levels, blood pressure, appetite, and risk of blood coagulation. <a href="/wiki/Cerium_nitrate" title="Cerium nitrate">Cerium nitrate</a> is an effective topical antimicrobial treatment for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third-degree_burn" title="Third-degree burn">third-degree burns</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> although large doses can lead to cerium poisoning and <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobinemia" title="Methemoglobinemia">methemoglobinemia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The early lanthanides act as essential cofactors for the methanol dehydrogenase of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanotrophic" title="Methanotrophic">methanotrophic</a> bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Methylacidiphilum_fumariolicum" title="Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum">Methylacidiphilum fumariolicum</a></i> SolV, for which lanthanum, cerium, praseodymium, and neodymium alone are about equally effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like all rare-earth metals, cerium is of low to moderate toxicity. A strong reducing agent, it ignites spontaneously in air at 65 to 80 °C. Fumes from cerium fires are toxic. Water should not be used to stop cerium fires, as cerium reacts with water to produce hydrogen gas. Workers exposed to cerium have experienced itching, sensitivity to heat, and skin lesions. Cerium is not toxic when eaten, but animals injected with large doses of cerium have died due to cardiovascular collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup> Cerium is more dangerous to aquatic organisms, on account of being damaging to cell membranes, but this is not an important risk because it is not very soluble in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
