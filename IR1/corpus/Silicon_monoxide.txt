<p><b>Silicon monoxide</b> is the chemical compound with the formula SiO where silicon is present in the oxidation state +2. In the vapour phase it is a diatomic molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-1">[1]</a></sup> It has been detected in stellar objects<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and it has been described as the most common oxide of silicon in the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup><br/>
When SiO gas is cooled rapidly, it condenses to form a brown/black polymeric <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glassy</a> material, (SiO)<sub><i>n</i></sub>, which is available commercially and used to deposit films of SiO.  Glassy (SiO)<sub><i>n</i></sub> is air- and moisture-sensitive.  Its surface readily oxidizes in air at room temperature, giving an SiO<sub>2</sub> surface layer that <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">protects the material from further oxidation</a>.  However, (SiO)<sub><i>n</i></sub> irreversibly <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionates</a> into SiO<sub>2</sub> and Si in a few hours between 400 and 800°C, and very rapidly between 1,000 and 1,440°C, although the reaction does not go to completion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_SiO">Formation of SiO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation of SiO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first precise report on the formation of SiO was in 1887<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vol_VI_1947_p._235_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vol_VI_1947_p._235-5">[5]</a></sup> by the chemist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_F._Maybery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charles F. Maybery (page does not exist)">Charles F. Maybery</a> (1850–1927) at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Case_School_of_Applied_Science" title="Case School of Applied Science">Case School of Applied Science</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>.  Maybery claimed that SiO formed as an amorphous greenish-yellow substance with a vitreous luster when silica was reduced with charcoal in the absence of metals in an electric furnace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The substance was always found at the interface between the charcoal and silica particles.  By investigating some of the chemical properties of the substance, its specific gravity, and a combustion analysis, Maybery deduced that the substance must be SiO.  The equation representing the partial chemical reduction of SiO<sub>2</sub> with C can be represented as:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>+ C</span> ⇌   <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO + CO</span></dd></dl>
<p>Complete reduction of SiO<sub>2</sub> with twice the amount of carbon yields elemental silicon and twice the amount of carbon monoxide.  In 1890, the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Clemens_Winkler" title="Clemens Winkler">Clemens Winkler</a> (the discoverer of germanium) was the first to attempt to synthesize SiO by heating silicon dioxide with silicon in a combustion furnace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>+ Si</span>  ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">2 SiO</span></dd></dl>
<p>However, Winkler was not able to produce the monoxide since the temperature of the mixture was only around 1000°C.  The experiment was repeated in 1905 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Noel_Potter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Noel Potter (page does not exist)">Henry Noel Potter</a> (1869–1942), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886)" title="Westinghouse Electric (1886)">Westinghouse</a> engineer.  Using an electric furnace, Potter was able to attain a temperature of 1700°C and observe the generation of SiO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vol_VI_1947_p._235_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vol_VI_1947_p._235-5">[5]</a></sup>  Potter also investigated the properties and applications of the solid form of SiO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of the volatility of SiO, silica  can be removed from ores or minerals by heating them with silicon to produce gaseous SiO in this manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-1">[1]</a></sup>  However, due to the difficulties associated with accurately measuring its vapor pressure, and because of the dependency on the specifics of the experimental design, various values have been reported in the literature for the vapor pressure of SiO (g).  For the p<sub>SiO</sub> above molten silicon in a quartz (SiO<sub>2</sub>) crucible at the melting point of silicon, one study yielded a value of 0.002 atm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  For the direct vaporization of pure, amorphous SiO solid, 0.001 atm has been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For a coating system, at the phase boundary between SiO<sub>2</sub> and a silicide, 0.01 atm was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Silica itself, or refractories containing SiO<sub>2</sub>, can be reduced with H<sub>2</sub> or CO at high temperatures, e.g.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>(s) + H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>(g) ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO(g) + H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O(g)</span></dd></dl>
<p>As the SiO product volatilizes off (is removed), the equilibrium shifts to the right, resulting in the continued consumption of SiO<sub>2</sub>.  Based on the dependence of the rate of silica weight loss on the gas flow rate normal to the interface, the rate of this reduction appears to be controlled by convective diffusion or mass transfer from the reacting surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Gaseous_.28molecular.29_SiO"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaseous_(molecular)_SiO">Gaseous (molecular) SiO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gaseous (molecular) SiO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silicon monoxide molecules have been trapped in an argon matrix cooled by helium. In these conditions, the SiO bond length is between 148.9 pm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup> and 151 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inorganic_Chemistry_2001_p._858_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inorganic_Chemistry_2001_p._858-16">[16]</a></sup> This bond length is similar to the length of Si=O double bonds (148 pm) in the matrix-isolated linear molecule <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (O=Si=O), indicative of the absence of a triple bond as in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup>  However, the SiO triple bond has a calculated bond length of 150 pm and a bond energy of 794 kJ/mol, which are also very close to those reported for SiO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inorganic_Chemistry_2001_p._858_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inorganic_Chemistry_2001_p._858-16">[16]</a></sup>  The SiO double bond structure is, notably, an exception to Lewis' <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> for molecules composed of the light main group elements, whereas the SiO triple bond satisfies this rule.  That anomaly not withstanding, the observation that monomeric SiO is short-lived and that (SiO)<sub>'n'</sub> <a href="/wiki/Oligomer" title="Oligomer">oligomers</a> with 'n' = 2,3,4,5 are known, all having closed ring structures in which the silicon atoms are connected through bridging oxygen atoms (i.e. each oxygen atom is singly bonded to two silicon atoms; no Si-Si bonds), suggests the Si=O double bond structure, with a hypovalent silicon atom, is likely for the monomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Condensing molecular SiO in argon matrix together with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide" title="Carbonyl sulfide">carbonyl sulfide</a> (COS), followed by irradiation with light, produces the planar molecules <span class="chemf nowrap">OSiF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (with Si-O distance 148 pm) and <span class="chemf nowrap">OSiCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (Si-O 149 pm), and the linear molecule <span class="chemf nowrap">OSiS</span> (Si-O 149 pm, Si-S 190 pm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Matrix-isolated molecular SiO reacts with oxygen atoms generated by microwave discharge to produce molecular <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> which has a linear structure. 
</p><p>When metal atoms (such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">Al</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">Pd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Ag</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">Au</a>) are co-deposited with SiO, triatomic molecules are produced with linear (AlSiO and PdSiO), non-linear (AgSiO and AuSiO), and ring (NaSiO) structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Solid_.28polymeric.29_SiO"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_(polymeric)_SiO">Solid (polymeric) SiO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solid (polymeric) SiO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potter reported SiO solid as yellowish-brown in color and as being an electrical and thermal insulator.  The solid burns in oxygen and decomposes water with the liberation of hydrogen.  It dissolves in warm alkali hydroxides and in hydrofluoric acid.  Even though Potter reported the heat of combustion of SiO to be 200 to 800 calories higher than that of an equilibrium mixture of Si and SiO<sub>2</sub> (which could, arguably, be used as evidence that SiO is a unique chemical compound),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> some studies characterized commercially available solid silicon monoxide materials as an inhomogeneous mixture of amorphous <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a> and amorphous <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a> with some chemical bonding at the interface of the Si and  SiO<sub>2</sub> phases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Recent spectroscopic studies in a correlation with Potter`s report suggest that commercially available solid silicon monoxide materials can not be considered as an inhomogeneous mixture of amorphous <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a> and amorphous <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
