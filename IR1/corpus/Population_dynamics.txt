<p><b>Population dynamics</b> is the branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a> that studies the size and age composition of <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">populations</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamical_systems" title="Dynamical systems">dynamical systems</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environmental</a> processes driving them (such as <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_rate" title="Death rate">death rates</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">immigration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a>). Example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scenarios" title="Scenarios">scenarios</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ageing_population" title="Ageing population">ageing populations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Population_decline" title="Population decline">population decline</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population dynamics has traditionally been the dominant branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_biology" title="Mathematical biology">mathematical biology</a>, which has a history of more than 210 years, although more recently the scope of mathematical biology has greatly expanded. The first principle of population dynamics is widely regarded as the exponential law of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malthus" title="Malthus">Malthus</a>, as modeled by the <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_growth_model" title="Malthusian growth model">Malthusian growth model</a>. The early period was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demographic</a> studies such as the work of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Gompertz" title="Benjamin Gompertz">Benjamin Gompertz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Fran%C3%A7ois_Verhulst" title="Pierre François Verhulst">Pierre François Verhulst</a> in the early 19th century, who refined and adjusted the Malthusian demographic model.
</p><p>A more general model formulation was proposed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=F.J._Richards&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="F.J. Richards (page does not exist)">F.J. Richards</a> in 1959, further expanded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Hopkins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Simon Hopkins (page does not exist)">Simon Hopkins</a>, in which the models of Gompertz, Verhulst and also <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_von_Bertalanffy" title="Ludwig von Bertalanffy">Ludwig von Bertalanffy</a> are covered as special cases of the general formulation. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotka%E2%80%93Volterra_equation" title="Lotka–Volterra equation">Lotka–Volterra predator-prey equations</a> are another famous example, as well as the alternative <a href="/wiki/Arditi%E2%80%93Ginzburg_equations" title="Arditi–Ginzburg equations">Arditi–Ginzburg equations</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_game" title="Computer game">computer game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/SimCity_(1989_video_game)" title="SimCity (1989 video game)">SimCity</a></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMORPG" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultima_Online" title="Ultima Online">Ultima Online</a>, among others, tried to <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">simulate</a> some of these population dynamics.
</p><p>In the past 30 years, population dynamics has been complemented by <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_game_theory" title="Evolutionary game theory">evolutionary game theory</a>, developed first by <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Smith" title="John Maynard Smith">John Maynard Smith</a>. Under these dynamics, evolutionary biology concepts may take a deterministic mathematical form. Population dynamics overlap with another active area of research in mathematical biology: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_modelling_in_epidemiology" title="Mathematical modelling in epidemiology">mathematical epidemiology</a>, the study of infectious disease affecting populations. Various models of viral spread have been proposed and analyzed, and provide important results that may be applied to health policy decisions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_mathematical_models">Common mathematical models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Common mathematical models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exponential_population_growth">Exponential population growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exponential population growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Exponential growth describes unregulated reproduction. It is very unusual to see this in nature. In the last 100 years, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_growth" title="Human population growth">human population growth</a> has appeared to be exponential. In the long run, however, it is not likely. Thomas Malthus believed that human population growth would lead to <a href="/wiki/Overpopulation" title="Overpopulation">overpopulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">scarcity</a> of resources. They believed that human population  would grow at rate in which they exceed the ability at which humans can find food. In the future, humans would be unable to feed large populations. The biological assumptions of exponential growth is that the per capita growth rate is constant. Growth is not limited by resource scarcity or predation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louie_Yang_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Louie_Yang-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logistic_population_growth">Logistic population growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Logistic population growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>“<a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">Logistics</a>” comes from the French word <b>logistique</b>, which means “to compute”. Population regulation is a density-dependent process, meaning that population growth rates are regulated by the density of a population. Consider an analogy with a thermostat. When the temperature is too hot, the thermostat turns on the air conditioning to decrease the temperature back to <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. When the temperature is too cold, the thermostat turns on the heater to increase the temperature back to homeostasis. Likewise with density dependence, whether the population density is high or low, population dynamics returns the population density to homeostasis. Homeostasis is the set point, or <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>, defined as K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louie_Yang_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Louie_Yang-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_and_wildlife_management">Fisheries and wildlife management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fisheries and wildlife management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">Population dynamics of fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matrix_population_models" title="Matrix population models">Matrix population models</a></div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_management" title="Wildlife management">wildlife management</a>, population is affected by three dynamic rate functions.
</p>
<ul><li>Natality or <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a>, often recruitment, which means reaching a certain size or reproductive stage. Usually refers to the age a fish can be caught and counted in nets.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_growth_rate" title="Population growth rate">Population growth rate</a>, which measures the growth of individuals in size and length. More important in fisheries, where population is often measured in biomass.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">Mortality</a>, which includes harvest mortality and natural mortality. Natural mortality includes non-human predation, disease and old age.</li></ul>

</p><p>If we measure these rates over many time intervals, we can determine how a population's density changes over time. Immigration and emigration are present, but are usually not measured.
</p><p>All of these are measured to determine the harvestable surplus, which is the number of individuals that can be harvested from a population without affecting long-term population stability or average population size. The harvest within the harvestable surplus is termed "compensatory" mortality, where the harvest deaths are substituted for the deaths that would have occurred naturally. Harvest above that level is termed "additive" mortality, because it adds to the number of deaths that would have occurred naturally. These terms are not necessarily judged as "good" and "bad," respectively, in population management. For example, a fish &amp; game agency might aim to reduce the size of a deer population through additive mortality. Bucks might be targeted to increase buck competition, or does might be targeted to reduce reproduction and thus overall population size.
</p><p>For the management of many fish and other wildlife populations, the goal is often to achieve the largest possible long-run sustainable harvest, also known as <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">maximum sustainable yield</a> (or MSY). Given a population dynamic model, such as any of the ones above, it is possible to calculate the population size that produces the largest harvestable surplus at equilibrium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While the use of population dynamic models along with statistics and optimization to set harvest limits for fish and game is controversial among scientists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> it has been shown to be more effective than the use of human judgment in computer experiments where both incorrect models and natural resource management students competed to maximize yield in two hypothetical fisheries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  To give an example of a non-intuitive result, fisheries produce more fish when there is a nearby <a href="/wiki/Refuge_(ecology)" title="Refuge (ecology)">refuge</a> from human predation in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserve</a>, resulting in higher catches than if the whole area was open to fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="For_control_applications">For control applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: For control applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_game_theory" title="Evolutionary game theory">Evolutionary game theory</a></div>
<p>Population dynamics have been widely used in several <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a> applications. With the use of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_game_theory" title="Evolutionary game theory">evolutionary game theory</a>, population games are broadly implemented for different industrial and daily-life contexts. Mostly used in multiple-input-multiple-output (<a href="/wiki/MIMO" title="MIMO">MIMO</a>) systems, although they can be adapted for use in single-input-single-output (<a href="/wiki/Single-input_single-output_system" title="Single-input single-output system">SISO</a>) systems. Some examples of applications are military campaigns, resource allocation for <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_network" title="Water supply network">water distribution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">dispatch of distributed generators</a>, lab experiments, transport problems, communication problems, among others.  Furthermore, with the adequate contextualization of industrial problems, population dynamics can be an efficient and easy-to-implement solution for control-related problems. Multiple academic research has been and is continuously carried out.
</p>
