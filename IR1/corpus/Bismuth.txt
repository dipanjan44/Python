<p><b>Bismuth</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Bi</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 83. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentavalent" title="Pentavalent">pentavalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metal</a> and one of the <a href="/wiki/Pnictogen" title="Pnictogen">pnictogens</a> with chemical properties resembling its lighter homologs <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>. Elemental bismuth may occur naturally, although its <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> form important commercial ores. The <a href="/wiki/Free_element" title="Free element">free element</a> is 86% as dense as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. It is a brittle metal with a silvery white color when freshly produced, but surface <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> can give it a pink tinge. Bismuth is marginally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a>, and the most naturally <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a> element, and has one of the lowest values of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> among metals.
</p><p>Bismuth metal has been known since ancient times, although it was often confused with lead and tin, which share some physical properties. The etymology is uncertain, but possibly comes from Arabic <i>bi ismid</i>, meaning having the properties of antimony<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or the German words <i>weiße Masse</i> or <i>Wismuth</i> ("white mass"), translated in the mid-sixteenth century to <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> <i>bisemutum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oet-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth was long considered the element with the highest atomic mass that is stable, but in 2003 it was discovered to be extremely weakly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a>: its only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_isotope" title="Primordial isotope">primordial isotope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth-209" title="Bismuth-209">bismuth-209</a>, decays via <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> more than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> times the estimated <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of its tremendously long half-life, bismuth may still be considered stable for almost all purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth compounds account for about half the production of bismuth. They are used in cosmetics, pigments, and a few pharmaceuticals, notably <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subsalicylate" title="Bismuth subsalicylate">bismuth subsalicylate</a>, used to treat diarrhea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> Bismuth's unusual propensity to expand upon freezing is responsible for some of its uses, such as in casting of printing type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> Bismuth has unusually low <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> for a heavy metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> As the toxicity of lead has become more apparent in recent years, there is an increasing use of bismuth alloys (presently about a third of bismuth production) as a replacement for lead.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png/110px-Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png/165px-Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png/220px-Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bismuth_symbol_by_Torbern_Bergman.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Alchemical_symbol" title="Alchemical symbol">Alchemical symbol</a> used by <a href="/wiki/Torbern_Bergman" title="Torbern Bergman">Torbern Bergman</a>, 1775</div></div></div>
<p>The name <i>bismuth</i> dates from around the 1660s, and is of uncertain etymology. It is one of the first 10 metals to have been discovered. Bismuth appears in the 1660s, from obsolete <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Bismuth</i></i>, <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Wismut</i></i>, <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Wissmuth</i></i> (early 16th century); perhaps related to <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i><i lang="goh" title="Old High German language text">hwiz</i></i> ("white").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oet_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-oet-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">bisemutum</i></i> (due to <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">Georgius Agricola</a>, who Latinized many German mining and technical words) is from the German <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Wismuth</i></i>, perhaps from <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">weiße Masse</i></i>, "white mass".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The element was confused in early times with <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and lead because of its resemblance to those elements. Bismuth has been known since ancient times, so no one person is credited with its discovery. <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">Agricola</a>, in <a href="/wiki/De_Natura_Fossilium" title="De Natura Fossilium">De Natura Fossilium</a> (c. 1546) states that bismuth is a distinct metal in a family of metals including tin and lead. This was based on observation of the metals and their physical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Miners in the age of alchemy also gave bismuth the name <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">tectum argenti</i>,</i> or "silver being made," in the sense of silver still in the process of being formed within the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Heinrich_Pott&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann Heinrich Pott (page does not exist)">Johann Heinrich Pott</a> in 1738,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torbern_Olof_Bergman" title="Torbern Olof Bergman">Torbern Olof Bergman</a>, the distinctness of lead and bismuth became clear, and <a href="/wiki/Claude_Fran%C3%A7ois_Geoffroy" title="Claude François Geoffroy">Claude François Geoffroy</a> demonstrated in 1753 that this metal is distinct from lead and tin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
Bismuth was also known to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incas" title="Incas">Incas</a> and used (along with the usual copper and tin) in a special <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_bronze" title="Bismuth bronze">bronze alloy</a> for knives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bismuth is a brittle metal with a white, silver-pink hue, often with an <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescent</a> <a href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_oxide" title="Bismuth(III) oxide">oxide</a> tarnish showing many colors from yellow to blue. The spiral, stair-stepped structure of bismuth crystals is the result of a higher growth rate around the outside edges than on the inside edges. The variations in the thickness of the oxide layer that forms on the surface of the crystal cause different wavelengths of light to interfere upon reflection, thus displaying a rainbow of colors. When <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">burned</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, bismuth burns with a blue <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxide" title="Bismuth oxide">its oxide</a> forms yellow <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">fumes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> is much lower than that of its neighbors in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>, such as lead, <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>.
</p><p>
No other metal is verified to be more naturally <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a> than bismuth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Superdiamagnetism" title="Superdiamagnetism">Superdiamagnetism</a> is a different physical phenomenon.) Of any metal, it has one of the lowest values of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, and maybe <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>) and the highest <a href="/wiki/Hall_effect" title="Hall effect">Hall coefficient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It has a high <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity" title="Electrical resistivity and conductivity">electrical resistivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup> When deposited in sufficiently thin layers on a substrate, bismuth is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>, despite being a <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg/220px-Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg/330px-Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg/440px-Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bismuth_sample_1B.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bismuth sample demonstrating staircase structure and iridescent properties</div></div></div><p>Elemental bismuth is <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">denser</a> in the liquid phase than the solid, a characteristic it shares with <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w768_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-w768-20">[20]</a></sup> Bismuth expands 3.32% on solidification; therefore, it was long a component of low-melting <a href="/wiki/Typesetting" title="Typesetting">typesetting</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>, where it compensated for the contraction of the other alloying components<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> to form almost isostatic <a href="/wiki/Lead-bismuth_eutectic" title="Lead-bismuth eutectic">bismuth-lead eutectic</a> alloys.
</p><p>Though virtually unseen in nature, high-purity bismuth can form distinctive, colorful <a href="/wiki/Hopper_crystal" title="Hopper crystal">hopper crystals</a>. It is relatively nontoxic and has a low melting point just above 271 °C, so crystals may be grown using a household stove, although the resulting crystals will tend to be lower quality than lab-grown crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>At ambient conditions bismuth shares the same layered structure as the metallic forms of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w767_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-w767-25">[25]</a></sup> crystallizing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_crystal_system" title="Trigonal crystal system">rhombohedral lattice</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Pearson_symbol" title="Pearson symbol">Pearson symbol</a> hR6, <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> R<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span>m No. 166), which is often classed into trigonal or hexagonal crystal systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-str_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-str-2">[2]</a></sup> When compressed at room temperature, this Bi-I structure changes first to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> Bi-II at 2.55 GPa, then to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> Bi-III at 2.7 GPa, and finally to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> Bi-IV at 7.7 GPa. The corresponding transitions can be monitored via changes in electrical conductivity; they are rather reproducible and abrupt, and are therefore used for calibration of high-pressure equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_characteristics">Chemical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bismuth is stable to both dry and moist air at ordinary temperatures. When red-hot, it reacts with water to make bismuth(III) oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s8_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-s8-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Bi + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O → Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth(V)_fluoride" title="Bismuth(V) fluoride">bismuth(V) fluoride</a> at 500 °C or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_fluoride" title="Bismuth(III) fluoride">bismuth(III) fluoride</a> at lower temperatures (typically from Bi melts); with other <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> it yields only bismuth(III) halides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w770_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-w770-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g559_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-g559-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k185_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-k185-32">[32]</a></sup> The trihalides are corrosive and easily react with moisture, forming <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyhalides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oxyhalides (page does not exist)">oxyhalides</a> with the formula BiOX.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s9_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-s9-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Bi + 3 X<sub>2</sub> → 2 BiX<sub>3</sub> (X = F, Cl, Br, I)</dd></dl>
<p>Bismuth dissolves in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to make <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_sulfate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bismuth(III) sulfate (page does not exist)">bismuth(III) sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s8_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-s8-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>6 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + 2 Bi → 6 H<sub>2</sub>O + Bi<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 3 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_nitrate" title="Bismuth(III) nitrate">bismuth(III) nitrate</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>Bi + 6 HNO<sub>3</sub> → 3 H<sub>2</sub>O + 3 NO<sub>2</sub> + Bi(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It also dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, but only with oxygen present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s8_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-s8-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Bi + 3 O<sub>2</sub> + 12 HCl → 4 BiCl<sub>3</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Transmetalation" title="Transmetalation">transmetalating</a> agent in the synthesis of alkaline-earth metal complexes:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Ba + 2 BiPh<sub>3</sub> → 3 BaPh<sub>2</sub> + 2 Bi</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_bismuth" title="Isotopes of bismuth">Isotopes of bismuth</a></div>
<p>The only primordial <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of bismuth, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth-209" title="Bismuth-209">bismuth-209</a>, was traditionally regarded as the heaviest stable isotope, but it had long been suspected<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> to be unstable on theoretical grounds. This was finally demonstrated in 2003, when researchers at the Institut d'Astrophysique Spatiale in <a href="/wiki/Orsay" title="Orsay">Orsay</a>, France, measured the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha emission</a> <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><a href="/wiki/Bismuth-209" title="Bismuth-209"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">209</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Bi<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></a></span> to be <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7026599594400000000♠</span>1.9<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>19</sup> years</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> times longer than the current estimated <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> Owing to its extraordinarily long half-life, for all presently known medical and industrial applications, bismuth can be treated as if it is stable and nonradioactive. The radioactivity is of academic interest because bismuth is one of a few elements whose radioactivity was suspected and theoretically predicted before being detected in the laboratory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> Bismuth has the longest known alpha decay half-life, although <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_tellurium" title="Isotopes of tellurium">tellurium-128</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a> half-life of over <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7031694267200000000♠</span>2.2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>24</sup> years</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-36">[36]</a></sup> Bismuth's extremely long half life means that less than one billionth of the bismuth present at the formation of the planet Earth would have decayed into thallium since then.
</p><p>Several isotopes of bismuth with short half-lives occur within the radioactive disintegration chains of <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, and more have been synthesized experimentally. Bismuth-213 is also found on the decay chain of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-233" title="Uranium-233">uranium-233</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercially, the radioactive isotope bismuth-213 can be produced by bombarding <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a> with <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">bremsstrahlung</a> photons from a <a href="/wiki/Linear_particle_accelerator" title="Linear particle accelerator">linear particle accelerator</a>. In 1997, an antibody conjugate with bismuth-213, which has a 45-minute half-life and decays with the emission of an alpha particle, was used to treat patients with leukemia. This isotope has also been tried in cancer treatment, for example, in the targeted alpha therapy (TAT) program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">Chemical compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Bismuth_compounds" title="Category:Bismuth compounds">Category:Bismuth compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Bismuth forms trivalent and pentavalent compounds, the trivalent ones being more common. Many of its chemical properties are similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>, although they are less toxic than derivatives of those lighter elements.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_and_sulfides">Oxides and sulfides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oxides and sulfides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At elevated temperatures, the vapors of the metal combine rapidly with oxygen, forming the yellow trioxide, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_oxide" title="Bismuth(III) oxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">Bi<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w768_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-w768-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g553_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-g553-40">[40]</a></sup> When molten, at temperatures above 710 °C, this oxide corrodes any metal oxide, and even platinum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k185_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-k185-32">[32]</a></sup> On reaction with base, it forms two series of <a href="/wiki/Oxyanion" title="Oxyanion">oxyanions</a>: <span class="chemf nowrap">BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, which is polymeric and forms linear chains, and <span class="chemf nowrap">BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. The anion in <span class="chemf nowrap">Li<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> is actually a cubic octameric anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">Bi<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">24−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">24</sub></span></span>, whereas the anion in <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> is tetrameric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norman1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-norman1-41">[41]</a></sup>

<p><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxychloride" title="Bismuth oxychloride">Bismuth oxychloride</a> (BiOCl, see figure at right) and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxynitrate" title="Bismuth oxynitrate">bismuth oxynitrate</a> (BiONO<sub>3</sub>) stoichiometrically appear as simple anionic salts of the bismuthyl(III) cation (BiO<sup>+</sup>) which commonly occurs in aqueous bismuth compounds. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bismuthine_and_bismuthides">Bismuthine and bismuthides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bismuthine and bismuthides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike the lighter <a href="/wiki/Pnictogen" title="Pnictogen">pnictogens</a> nitrogen, phosphorus, and arsenic, but similar to <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>, bismuth does not form a stable <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydride</a>. Bismuth hydride, <a href="/wiki/Bismuthine" title="Bismuthine">bismuthine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">BiH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> compound that spontaneously decomposes at room temperature. It is stable only below −60 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norman1_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-norman1-41">[41]</a></sup> Bismuthides are <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic" title="Intermetallic">intermetallic</a> compounds between bismuth and other metals.
</p><p>In 2014 researchers discovered that sodium bismuthide can exist as a form of matter called a “three-dimensional topological Dirac semi-metal” (3DTDS) that possess 3D <a href="/wiki/Dirac_fermion" title="Dirac fermion">Dirac fermions</a> in bulk. It is a natural, three-dimensional counterpart to <a href="/wiki/Graphene" title="Graphene">graphene</a> with similar <a href="/wiki/Electron_mobility" title="Electron mobility">electron mobility</a> and velocity. Graphene and <a href="/wiki/Topological_insulator" title="Topological insulator">topological insulators</a> (such as those in 3DTDS) are both crystalline materials that are electrically insulating inside but conducting on the surface, allowing them to function as <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> and other electronic devices. While sodium bismuthide (<span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>Bi</span>) is too unstable to be used in devices without packaging, it can demonstrate potential applications of 3DTDS systems, which offer distinct efficiency and fabrication advantages over planar graphene in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spintronics" title="Spintronics">spintronics</a> applications.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k1401_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-k1401-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_bismuth_production" title="List of countries by bismuth production">List of countries by bismuth production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bismite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="1400" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bismite.jpg/170px-Bismite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bismite.jpg/255px-Bismite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bismite.jpg/340px-Bismite.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bismite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bismite" title="Bismite">Bismite</a> mineral</div></div></div>
<p>In the Earth's crust, bismuth is about <a href="/wiki/Abundances_of_the_elements_(data_page)" title="Abundances of the elements (data page)">twice as abundant as gold</a>. The most important <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> of bismuth are <a href="/wiki/Bismuthinite" title="Bismuthinite">bismuthinite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismite" title="Bismite">bismite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup> Native bismuth is known from Australia, Bolivia, and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arizona1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-arizona1-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a>, the world mining production of bismuth in 2014 was 13,600 tonnes, with the major contributions from China (7,600 tonnes), Vietnam (4,950 tonnes) and Mexico (948 tonnes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2016_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2016-53">[53]</a></sup> The refinery production in 2010 was 16,000 tonnes, of which China produced 13,000, Mexico 850 and Belgium 800 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2010_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2010-54">[54]</a></sup> The difference reflects bismuth's status as a byproduct of extraction of other metals such as lead, copper, tin, molybdenum and tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> World bismuth production from refineries is a more complete and reliable statistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oje_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oje-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth travels in crude lead bullion (which can contain up to 10% bismuth) through several stages of refining, until it is removed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kroll-Betterton_process" title="Kroll-Betterton process">Kroll-Betterton process</a> which separates the impurities as slag, or the electrolytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Betts_process" title="Betts process">Betts process</a>. Bismuth will behave similarly with another of its major metals, copper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oje_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oje-56">[56]</a></sup> The raw bismuth metal from both processes contains still considerable amounts of other metals, foremost lead. By reacting the molten mixture with chlorine gas the metals are converted to their chlorides while bismuth remains unchanged. Impurities can also be removed by various other methods for example with fluxes and treatments yielding high-purity bismuth metal (over 99% Bi).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BiPrice.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1400" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/BiPrice.png/290px-BiPrice.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/BiPrice.png/435px-BiPrice.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/BiPrice.png/580px-BiPrice.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BiPrice.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World mine production and annual averages of bismuth price (New York, not adjusted for inflation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-59">[59]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The price for pure bismuth metal has been relatively stable through most of the 20th century, except for a spike in the 1970s. Bismuth has always been produced mainly as a byproduct of lead refining, and thus the price usually reflected the cost of recovery and the balance between production and demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Demand for bismuth was small prior to World War II and was pharmaceutical – bismuth compounds were used to treat such conditions as digestive disorders, <a href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_infection" title="Sexually transmitted infection">sexually transmitted infections</a> and burns. Minor amounts of bismuth metal were consumed in fusible alloys for <a href="/wiki/Fire_sprinkler" title="Fire sprinkler">fire sprinkler</a> systems and <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(electrical)" title="Fuse (electrical)">fuse wire</a>. During World War II bismuth was considered a <a href="/wiki/Strategic_material" title="Strategic material">strategic material</a>, used for solders, fusible alloys, medications and atomic research. To stabilize the market, the producers set the price at $1.25 per pound (2.75 $/kg) during the war and at $2.25 per pound (4.96 $/kg) from 1950 until 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1970s, the price rose rapidly as a result of increasing demand for bismuth as a metallurgical additive to aluminium, iron and steel. This was followed by a decline owing to increased world production, stabilized consumption, and the recessions of 1980 and 1981–82. In 1984, the price began to climb as consumption increased worldwide, especially in the United States and Japan. In the early 1990s, research began on the evaluation of bismuth as a nontoxic replacement for lead in ceramic glazes, fishing sinkers, food-processing equipment, free-machining <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brasses</a> for plumbing applications, lubricating greases, and shot for <a href="/wiki/Waterfowl_hunting" title="Waterfowl hunting">waterfowl hunting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s14_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-s14-60">[60]</a></sup> Growth in these areas remained slow during the middle 1990s, in spite of the backing of lead replacement by the US Government, but intensified around 2005. This resulted in a rapid and continuing increase in price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_59-3"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling">Recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most bismuth is produced as a byproduct of other metal-extraction processes including the smelting of lead, and also of tungsten and copper. Its <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> is dependent on increased recycling, which is problematic.
</p><p>It was once believed that bismuth could be practically recycled from the soldered joints in electronic equipment. Recent efficiencies in solder application in electronics mean there is substantially less solder deposited, and thus less to recycle. While recovering the silver from silver-bearing solder may remain economic, recovering bismuth is substantially less so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Next in recycling feasibility would be sizeable catalysts with a fair bismuth content, such as bismuth phosphomolybdate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, bismuth used in galvanizing, and as a free-machining metallurgical additive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Bismuth in uses where it is dispersed most widely include certain stomach medicines (<a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subsalicylate" title="Bismuth subsalicylate">bismuth subsalicylate</a>), paints (<a href="/wiki/Bismuth_vanadate" title="Bismuth vanadate">bismuth vanadate</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearlescent" title="Pearlescent">pearlescent</a> cosmetics (<a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxychloride" title="Bismuth oxychloride">bismuth oxychloride</a>), and bismuth-containing bullets. Recycling bismuth from these uses is impractical.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bismuth has few commercial applications, and those applications that use it generally require small quantities relative to other raw materials. In the United States, for example, 884 tonnes of bismuth were consumed in 2010, of which 63% went into chemicals (including pharmaceuticals, pigments, and cosmetics); 26% into metallurgical additives for casting and galvanizing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> 7% into bismuth alloys, solders and ammunition; and 4% into research and other uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2010_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2010-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some manufacturers use bismuth as a substitute in equipment for potable water systems such as valves to meet "lead-free" mandates in the U.S. (began in 2014).  This is a fairly large application since it covers all residential and commercial building construction.
</p><p>In the early 1990s, researchers began to evaluate bismuth as a nontoxic replacement for lead in various applications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicines">Medicines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Medicines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bismuth is an ingredient in some pharmaceuticals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> although the use of some of these substances is declining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k184_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-k184-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subsalicylate" title="Bismuth subsalicylate">Bismuth subsalicylate</a> is used as an <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">antidiarrheal</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> it is the <a href="/wiki/Active_ingredient" title="Active ingredient">active ingredient</a> in such "Pink Bismuth" preparations as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepto-Bismol" title="Pepto-Bismol">Pepto-Bismol</a>, as well as the 2004 reformulation of <a href="/wiki/Kaopectate" title="Kaopectate">Kaopectate</a>. It is also used to treat some other gastro-intestinal diseases and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_poisoning" title="Cadmium poisoning">cadmium poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> The mechanism of action of this substance is still not well documented, although an <a href="/wiki/Oligodynamic_effect" title="Oligodynamic effect">oligodynamic effect</a> (toxic effect of small doses of heavy metal ions on microbes) may be involved in at least some cases. <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">Salicylic acid</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of the compound is antimicrobial for toxogenic <i>E. coli,</i> an important pathogen in <a href="/wiki/Traveler%27s_diarrhea" title="Traveler's diarrhea">traveler's diarrhea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>a combination of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subsalicylate" title="Bismuth subsalicylate">bismuth subsalicylate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subcitrate" title="Bismuth subcitrate">bismuth subcitrate</a> is used to treat the bacteria causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcers</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bibrocathol" title="Bibrocathol">Bibrocathol</a> is an organic bismuth-containing compound used to treat eye infections.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subgallate" title="Bismuth subgallate">Bismuth subgallate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Active_ingredient" title="Active ingredient">active ingredient</a> in Devrom, is used as an internal deodorant to treat malodor from <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>.</li>
<li>Bismuth compounds (including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bismuth_tartrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sodium bismuth tartrate (page does not exist)">sodium bismuth tartrate</a>) were formerly used to treat syphilis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Milk of bismuth" (an aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_hydroxide" title="Bismuth hydroxide">bismuth hydroxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subcarbonate" title="Bismuth subcarbonate">bismuth subcarbonate</a>) was marketed as an alimentary cure-all in the early 20th century</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth_subnitrate" title="Bismuth subnitrate">Bismuth subnitrate</a> (Bi<sub>5</sub>O(OH)<sub>9</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subcarbonate" title="Bismuth subcarbonate">bismuth subcarbonate</a> (Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>(CO<sub>3</sub>)) are also used in medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmetics_and_pigments">Cosmetics and pigments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cosmetics and pigments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxychloride" title="Bismuth oxychloride">Bismuth oxychloride</a> (BiOCl) is sometimes used in cosmetics, as a pigment in paint for eye shadows, hair sprays and nail polishes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k184_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-k184-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Effp_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Effp-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paff_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paff-67">[67]</a></sup> This compound is found as the mineral bismoclite and in crystal form contains layers of atoms (see figure above) that refract light chromatically, resulting in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridescent" title="Iridescent">iridescent</a> appearance similar to <a href="/wiki/Nacre" title="Nacre">nacre</a> of pearl. It was used as a cosmetic in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> and in many places since. <i>Bismuth white</i> (also "Spanish white") can refer to either bismuth oxychloride or <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxynitrate" title="Bismuth oxynitrate">bismuth oxynitrate</a> (BiONO<sub>3</sub>), when used as a white pigment. Bismuth vanadate is used as a light-stable non-reactive paint pigment (particularly for artists' paints), often as a replacement for the more toxic cadmium sulfide yellow and orange-yellow pigments. The most common variety in artists' paints is a lemon yellow, visually indistinguishable from its cadmium-containing alternative.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_and_alloys">Metal and alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Metal and alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bismuth is used in metal alloys with other metals such as iron, to create alloys to go into automatic sprinkler systems for fires. It was also used to make <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_bronze" title="Bismuth bronze">bismuth bronze</a> which was used in the Bronze Age.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead_replacement">Lead replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lead replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The density difference between lead (11.32 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) and bismuth (9.78 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) is small enough that for many <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a> and weighting applications, bismuth can substitute for <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. For example, it can replace lead as a dense material in <a href="/wiki/Fishing_sinker" title="Fishing sinker">fishing sinkers</a>. It has been used as a replacement for lead in <a href="/wiki/Shot_(pellet)" title="Shot (pellet)">shot</a>, bullets and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Less-lethal" title="Less-lethal">less-lethal</a> <a href="/wiki/Riot_gun" title="Riot gun">riot gun</a> ammunition. The Netherlands, Denmark, England,  Wales, the US, and many other countries now prohibit the use of lead shot for the hunting of wetland birds, as many birds are prone to <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> owing to mistaken ingestion of lead (instead of small stones and grit) to aid digestion, or even prohibit the use of lead for all hunting, such as in the Netherlands. Bismuth-tin alloy shot is one alternative that provides similar ballistic performance to lead. (Another less expensive but also more poorly performing alternative is "steel" shot, which is actually soft iron.) Bismuth's lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleability" title="Malleability">malleability</a> does, however, make it unsuitable for use in expanding hunting bullets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for the whole paragraph (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Bismuth, as a dense element of high atomic weight, is used in bismuth-impregnated <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Latex_shield&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Latex shield (page does not exist)">latex shields</a> to shield from X-ray in medical examinations, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_computed_tomography" title="X-ray computed tomography">CTs</a>, mostly as it is considered non-toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Restriction_of_Hazardous_Substances_Directive" title="Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive">Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive</a> (RoHS) for reduction of lead has broadened bismuth's use in electronics as a component of low-melting point solders, as a replacement for traditional tin-lead solders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2010_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2010-54">[54]</a></sup> Its low toxicity will be especially important for solders to be used in food processing equipment and copper water pipes, although it can also be used in other applications including those in the automobile industry, in the EU for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lohse_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-lohse-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth has been evaluated as a replacement for lead in free-machining <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brasses</a> for <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a> applications,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> although it does not equal the performance of leaded steels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lohse_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-lohse-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_metal_uses_and_specialty_alloys">Other metal uses and specialty alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other metal uses and specialty alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many bismuth <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> have low <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> and are found in specialty applications such as <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">solders</a>. Many automatic sprinklers, electric fuses, and safety devices in fire detection and suppression systems contain the eutectic In19.1-Cd5.3-Pb22.6-Sn8.3-Bi44.7 alloy that melts at 47 °C (117 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup>  This is a convenient temperature since it is unlikely to be exceeded in normal living conditions. Low-melting alloys, such as Bi-Cd-Pb-Sn alloy which melts at 70 °C, are also used in automotive and aviation industries. Before deforming a thin-walled metal part, it is filled with a melt or covered with a thin layer of the alloy to reduce the chance of breaking. Then the alloy is removed by submerging the part in boiling water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k183_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-k183-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth is used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-machining_steel" title="Free-machining steel">free-machining steels</a> and free-machining aluminium alloys for precision machining properties. It has similar effect to lead and improves the chip breaking during machining. The shrinking on solidification in lead and the expansion of bismuth compensate each other and therefore lead and bismuth are often used in similar quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Similarly, alloys containing comparable parts of bismuth and lead exhibit a very small change (on the order 0.01%) upon melting, solidification or aging. Such alloys are used in high-precision casting, e.g. in dentistry, to create models and molds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k183_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-k183-71">[71]</a></sup> Bismuth is also used as an alloying agent in production of malleable irons and as a <a href="/wiki/Thermocouple" title="Thermocouple">thermocouple</a> material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2010_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2010-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth is also used in aluminium-silicon cast alloys in order to refine silicon morphology. However, it indicated a poisoning effect on modification of strontium (Sr).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Some bismuth alloys, such as Bi35-Pb37-Sn25, are combined with non-sticking materials such as <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>, glass and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a> because they easily wet them allowing to make joints to other parts. Addition of bismuth to caesium enhances the quantum yield of caesium cathodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k184_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-k184-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">Sintering</a> of bismuth and manganese powders at 300 °C produces a permanent magnet and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetostrictive" title="Magnetostrictive">magnetostrictive</a> material, which is used in ultrasonic generators and receivers working in the 10–100 kHz range and in magnetic memory devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s15_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-s15-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_as_compounds">Other uses as compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other uses as compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bismuthvanadat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="322" data-file-width="393" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Bismuthvanadat.jpg/220px-Bismuthvanadat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Bismuthvanadat.jpg/330px-Bismuthvanadat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/Bismuthvanadat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bismuthvanadat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bismuth vanadate, a yellow pigment</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Bismuth is included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSCCO" title="BSCCO">BSCCO</a> (bismuth strontium calcium copper oxide) which is a group of similar superconducting compounds discovered in 1988 that exhibit the highest superconducting transition temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth_subnitrate" title="Bismuth subnitrate">Bismuth subnitrate</a> is a component of <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">glazes</a> that produces an <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescence</a> and is used as a pigment in paint.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_telluride" title="Bismuth telluride">Bismuth telluride</a> is a semiconductor and an excellent <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect" title="Thermoelectric effect">thermoelectric</a> material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k184_45-4"><a href="#cite_note-k184-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Bi<sub>2</sub>Te<sub>3</sub> diodes are used in mobile refrigerators, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPU" title="CPU">CPU</a> coolers, and as detectors in <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> spectrophotometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k184_45-5"><a href="#cite_note-k184-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxide" title="Bismuth oxide">Bismuth oxide</a>, in its delta form, is a solid electrolyte for oxygen. This form normally breaks down below a high-temperature threshold, but can be electrodeposited well below this temperature in a highly alkaline solution.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bismuth_vanadate" title="Bismuth vanadate">Bismuth vanadate</a> is an opaque yellow pigment used by some artists' oil, acrylic, and watercolor paint companies, primarily as a replacement for the more toxic cadmium sulfide yellows in the greenish-yellow (lemon) to orange-toned yellow range. It performs practically identically to the cadmium pigments, such as in terms of resistance to degradation from UV exposure, opacity, tinting strength, and lack of reactivity when mixed with other pigments. The most commonly-used variety by artists' paint makers is lemon in color. In addition to being a replacement for several cadmium yellows, it also serves as a non-toxic visual replacement for the older chromate pigments made with zinc, lead, and strontium. If a green pigment and barium sulfate (for increased transparency) are added it can also serve as a replacement for barium chromate, which possesses a more greenish cast than the others. In comparison with lead chromates, it does not blacken due to hydrogen sulfide in the air (a process accelerated by UV exposure) and possesses a particularly brighter color than them, especially the lemon, which is the most translucent, dull, and fastest to blacken due to the higher percentage of lead sulfate required to produce that shade. It is also used, on a limited basis due to its cost, as an vehicle paint pigment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li>A catalyst for making acrylic fibers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>As an <a href="/wiki/Electrocatalyst" title="Electrocatalyst">electrocatalyst</a> in the conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to CO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Lubrication" title="Lubrication">lubricating</a> <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">greases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
<li>In crackling microstars (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_eggs" title="Dragon's eggs">dragon's eggs</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_oxide" title="Bismuth(III) oxide">oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subcarbonate" title="Bismuth subcarbonate">subcarbonate</a> or subnitrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicology_and_ecotoxicology">Toxicology and ecotoxicology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Toxicology and ecotoxicology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Bismuthia" title="Bismuthia">bismuthia</a>, a rare dermatological condition that results from the prolonged use of bismuth.</i></dd></dl>
<p>Scientific literature indicates that some of the compounds of bismuth are less toxic to humans via ingestion compared to other heavy metals (lead, arsenic, antimony, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Kean-7">[7]</a></sup> presumably due to the comparatively low solubility of bismuth salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-85">[85]</a></sup>  Its biological half-life for whole-body retention is reported to be 5 days but it can remain in the kidney for years in people treated with bismuth compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth poisoning can occur and has according to some reports been common in relatively recent times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenntech_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenntech-87">[87]</a></sup> As with lead, bismuth poisoning can result in the formation of a black deposit on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gingiva" title="Gingiva">gingiva</a>, known as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth_line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bismuth line (page does not exist)">bismuth line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biline_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-biline-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Poisoning may be treated with <a href="/wiki/Dimercaprol" title="Dimercaprol">dimercaprol</a>; however, evidence for benefit is unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2008_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2008-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS2016_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS2016-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bismuth's environmental impacts are not well known; it may be less likely to bioaccumulate than some other heavy metals, and this is an area of active research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioremediation">Bioremediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Bioremediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fungus <i><a href="/wiki/Marasmius_oreades" title="Marasmius oreades">Marasmius oreades</a></i> can be used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_remediation" title="Biological remediation">biological remediation</a> of bismuth in polluted soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
