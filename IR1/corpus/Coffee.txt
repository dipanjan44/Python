<p><b>Coffee</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Coffee_preparation" title="Coffee preparation">brewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">drink</a> prepared from roasted <a href="/wiki/Coffee_bean" title="Coffee bean">coffee beans</a>, the seeds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epigynous_berries" title="Epigynous berries">berries</a> from certain <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea" title="Coffea">Coffea</a></i> species. The genus <i>Coffea</i> is native to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Africa" title="Tropical Africa">tropical Africa</a> (specifically having its origin in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Comoros" title="Comoros">Comoros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Coffee plants are now cultivated in <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_coffee_production" title="List of countries by coffee production">over 70 countries</a>, primarily in the equatorial regions of <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">the Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and Africa. The two most commonly grown are <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">C. arabica</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Robusta_coffee" title="Robusta coffee">C. robusta</a></i>. Once ripe, coffee berries are picked, processed, and dried. Dried coffee seeds (referred to as "beans") are <a href="/wiki/Coffee_roasting" title="Coffee roasting">roasted</a> to varying degrees, depending on the desired flavor. Roasted beans are <a href="/wiki/Coffee_preparation" title="Coffee preparation">ground and then brewed</a> with near-boiling water to produce the beverage known as coffee.
</p><p>Coffee is darkly colored, bitter, slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> and has a <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulating</a> effect in humans, primarily due to its <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> content.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (July 2018)">medical citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is one of the most popular drinks in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnn_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mnn-3">[3]</a></sup> and it can be prepared and presented in a variety of ways (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Espresso" title="Espresso">espresso</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_press" title="French press">French press</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_latte" title="Café latte">café latte</a>). It is usually served hot, although <a href="/wiki/Iced_coffee" title="Iced coffee">iced coffee</a> is a popular alternative. Clinical studies indicate that moderate coffee consumption is benign or mildly beneficial in healthy adults, with continuing research on whether long-term consumption lowers the risk of some diseases, although those long-term studies are of generally poor quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest credible evidence of coffee-drinking appears in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Arabia" title="Southern Arabia">southern Arabia</a> in the middle of the 15th century in <a href="/wiki/Sufism" title="Sufism">Sufi</a> shrines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wein34_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wein34-5">[5]</a></sup> It was here in Arabia that coffee seeds were first roasted and brewed in a similar way to how it is now prepared.  But the coffee seeds had to be first exported from East Africa to Yemen, as the <i>Coffea arabica</i> plant is thought to have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_plant" title="Indigenous plant">indigenous</a> to the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coffee:_A_dark_history_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coffee:_A_dark_history-6">[6]</a></sup> Yemeni traders took coffee back to their homeland and began to cultivate the seed. By the 16th century, the drink had reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. From there, it spread to Europe and the rest of the world.
</p><p>As of 2016, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> was the leading grower of coffee beans, producing <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_coffee_production" title="List of countries by coffee production">one-third of the world total</a>. Coffee is a major export <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a>, being the top legal agricultural <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_coffee_exports" title="List of countries by coffee exports">export for numerous countries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnn_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mnn-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> It is one of the most valuable commodities exported by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. Green, unroasted coffee is one of the most traded agricultural commodities in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some controversy has been associated with coffee cultivation and the way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a> trade with developing nations, as well as the <a href="#Ecological_effects">impact on the environment</a> with regards to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_clearing" title="Land clearing">clearing of land</a> for coffee-growing and water use. Consequently, the markets for <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade_coffee" title="Fair trade coffee">fair trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_coffee" title="Organic coffee">organic coffee</a> are expanding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1395" data-file-width="2048" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg/220px-Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg/330px-Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg/440px-Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_beans_unroasted.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Unroasted coffee beans</div></div></div>
<p>The word "coffee" entered the English language in 1582 via the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>koffie</i>, borrowed from the <a </p><p>The Arabic word <i>qahwah</i> was traditionally held to refer to a type of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_people" title="Arab people">Arab</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicographers" title="Lexicographers">lexicographers</a> as deriving from the verb <i>qahiya</i> (قَهِيَ), "to lack hunger", in reference to the drink's reputation as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appetite_suppressant" title="Appetite suppressant">appetite suppressant</a>. It has also been proposed that the source may be the Proto-Central Semitic root q-h-h meaning "dark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye1986_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye1986-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternatively, the word <a href="/wiki/Khat" title="Khat">Khat</a>, a plant widely used as a stimulant in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> before being supplanted by coffee has been suggested as a possible origin, or the Arabic word <i>quwwah<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span></i> (meaning "strength").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It may also come from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kaffa" title="Kingdom of Kaffa">Kingdom of Kaffa</a> in southeast Ethiopia where Coffea arabica grows wild, but this is considered less likely;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye1986_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye1986-10">[10]</a></sup> in the local Kaffa language, the coffee plant is instead called "bunno".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The expression "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_break" title="Coffee break">coffee break</a>" was first attested in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-13">[13]</a></sup> The term "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_pot" title="Coffee pot">coffee pot</a>" dates from 1705.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_coffee" title="History of coffee">History of coffee</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legendary_accounts">Legendary accounts</span></h3>
<p>According to legend, ancestors of today's <a href="/wiki/Oromo_people" title="Oromo people">Oromo people</a> in a region of Kaffa in Ethiopia were believed to have been the first to recognize the energizing effect of the coffee plant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wein34_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wein34-5">[5]</a></sup> though no direct evidence has been found indicating where in Africa coffee grew or who among the native populations might have used it as a stimulant or even known about it, earlier than the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wein34_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wein34-5">[5]</a></sup> The story of <a href="/wiki/Kaldi" title="Kaldi">Kaldi</a>, the 9th-century Ethiopian goatherd who discovered coffee when he noticed how excited his goats became after eating the beans from a coffee plant, did not appear in writing until 1671 and is probably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocryphal" title="Apocryphal">apocryphal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wein34_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wein34-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other accounts attribute the discovery of coffee to Sheikh Omar. According to an ancient chronicle (preserved in the Abd-Al-Kadir manuscript), Omar, who was known for his ability to cure the sick through prayer, was once exiled from <a href="/wiki/Mocha,_Yemen" title="Mocha, Yemen">Mocha</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> to a desert cave near Ousab (modern-day Wusab, about 90 km east of <a href="/wiki/Zabid" title="Zabid">Zabid</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Starving, Omar chewed berries from nearby shrubbery but found them to be bitter. He tried roasting the seeds to improve the flavor, but they became hard. He then tried boiling them to soften the seed, which resulted in a fragrant brown liquid. Upon drinking the liquid Omar was revitalized and sustained for days. As stories of this "miracle drug" reached Mocha, Omar was asked to return and was made a saint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> From Ethiopia, the coffee plant was introduced into the Arab World through Egypt and Yemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Souza120_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Souza120-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_transmission">Historical transmission</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="744" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg/220px-Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg/330px-Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg/440px-Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mocha_Dapper_1680.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of <a href="/wiki/Mocha,_Yemen" title="Mocha, Yemen">Mocha</a>, Yemen during the second half of the 17th century</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest credible evidence of coffee-drinking or knowledge of the coffee tree appears in the middle of the 15th century in the accounts of Ahmed al-Ghaffar in Yemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wein34_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wein34-5">[5]</a></sup> It was here in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabia" title="Arabia">Arabia</a> that coffee seeds were first roasted and brewed, in a similar way to how it is now prepared. Coffee was used by Sufi circles to stay awake for their religious rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia-of-islam-kawah_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-of-islam-kawah-17">[17]</a></sup> Accounts differ on the origin of coffee (seeds) prior to its appearance in Yemen. One account credits Muhammad <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Sa%27d" title="Ibn Sa'd">Ibn Sa'd</a> for bringing the beverage to <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> from the African coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Other early accounts say Ali ben Omar of the <a href="/wiki/Shadhili" title="Shadhili">Shadhili</a> Sufi order was the first to introduce coffee to Arabia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> According to al Shardi, Ali ben Omar may have encountered coffee during his stay with the <a href="/wiki/Adal_Sultanate" title="Adal Sultanate">Adal</a> king <a href="/wiki/Sa%27ad_ad-Din_II" title="Sa'ad ad-Din II">Sadadin</a>'s companions in 1401. Famous 16th-century Islamic scholar <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Hajar_al-Haytami" title="Ibn Hajar al-Haytami">Ibn Hajar al-Haytami</a> notes in his writings of a beverage called qahwa developed from a tree in the <a href="/wiki/Zeila" title="Zeila">Zeila</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia-of-islam-kawah_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-of-islam-kawah-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg"><img alt="Relief of a young, cherub-like boy passing a cup to a reclining man with a moustache and hat. The sculpture is white with gold accents on the cup, clothes, and items." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="646" data-file-width="815" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg/220px-Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg/330px-Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg/440px-Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leipzig_coffeebaum_815.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Over the door of a <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> <a href="/wiki/Coffeehouse" title="Coffeehouse">coffeeshop</a> is a sculptural representation of a man in <a href="/wiki/Kaftan" title="Kaftan">Turkish dress</a>, receiving a cup of coffee from a boy</div></div></div>
<p>By the 16th century, it had reached the rest of the Middle East, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">northern Africa</a>.  The first coffee smuggled out of the Middle East was by Sufi <a href="/wiki/Baba_Budan" title="Baba Budan">Baba Budan</a> from Yemen to India in 1670. Before then, all exported coffee was boiled or otherwise sterilised. Portraits of Baba Budan depict him as having smuggled seven coffee seeds by strapping them to his chest. The first plants grown from these smuggled seeds were planted in <a href="/wiki/Mysore" title="Mysore">Mysore</a>. Coffee then spread to Italy, and to the rest of Europe, to <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and to the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyers_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meyers-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (August 2016)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="621" data-file-width="423" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg/220px-Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg/330px-Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mason%27s_essence_of_coffee_and_chicory_advert.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A late 19th century advertisement for coffee essence</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MODOGlendora.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1386" data-file-width="1848" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/MODOGlendora.jpg/220px-MODOGlendora.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/MODOGlendora.jpg/330px-MODOGlendora.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/MODOGlendora.jpg/440px-MODOGlendora.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MODOGlendora.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A coffee can from the first half of the 20th century. From the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Objeto_del_Objeto" title="Museo del Objeto del Objeto">Museo del Objeto del Objeto</a> collection.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1583, <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Rauwolf" title="Leonhard Rauwolf">Leonhard Rauwolf</a>, a German physician, gave this description of coffee after returning from a ten-year trip to the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A beverage as black as ink, useful against numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illness" title="Illness">illnesses</a>, particularly those of the stomach. Its consumers take it in the morning, quite frankly, in a porcelain cup that is passed around and from which each one drinks a cupful. It is composed of water and the fruit from a bush called bunnu.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Léonard Rauwolf, <i>Reise in die Morgenländer</i> (in German)</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, coffee spread to Italy. The thriving trade between <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and North Africa, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, and the Middle East brought many goods, including coffee, to the Venetian port. From Venice, it was introduced to the rest of Europe. Coffee became more widely accepted after it was deemed a Christian beverage by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VIII" title="Pope Clement VIII">Pope Clement VIII</a> in 1600, despite appeals to ban the "Muslim drink." The first European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_house" title="Coffee house">coffee house</a> opened in Rome in 1645.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyers_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meyers-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1988" data-file-width="1429" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG/220px-Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG/330px-Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG/440px-Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Washington_Coffee_New_York_Tribune.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1919 advertisement for <i>G Washington's Coffee</i>. The first instant coffee was invented by inventor <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_(inventor)" title="George Washington (inventor)">George Washington</a> in 1909.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> was the first to import coffee on a large scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Dutch later grew the crop in <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceylon" title="Ceylon">Ceylon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plant_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-plant-22">[22]</a></sup> The first exports of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_coffee" title="Indonesian coffee">Indonesian coffee</a> from Java to the Netherlands occurred in 1711.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through the efforts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honourable_East_India_Company" title="Honourable East India Company">British East India Company</a>, coffee became popular in England as well. <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a> recorded tasting the drink at <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in a diary entry of May 1637 to where it had been brought by an <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> student of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balliol_College" title="Balliol College">Balliol College</a> from Crete named Nathaniel Conopios of Crete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Oxford's <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Lane_Coffee_House" title="Queen's Lane Coffee House">Queen's Lane Coffee House</a>, established in 1654, is still in existence today. Coffee was introduced in France in 1657, and in Austria and Poland after the 1683 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">Battle of Vienna</a>, when coffee was captured from supplies of the defeated <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast9_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast9-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>When coffee reached North America during the Colonial period, it was initially not as successful as it had been in Europe as alcoholic beverages remained more popular. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>, the demand for coffee increased so much that dealers had to hoard their scarce supplies and raise prices dramatically; this was also due to the reduced availability of <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> from British merchants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast39_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast39-27">[27]</a></sup> and a general resolution among many Americans to avoid drinking tea following the 1773 <a href="/wiki/Boston_Tea_Party" title="Boston Tea Party">Boston Tea Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, during which Britain temporarily cut off access to tea imports, the Americans' taste for coffee grew.
</p><p>Coffee consumption declined in England, giving way to tea during the 18th century. The latter beverage was simpler to make, and had become cheaper with the British conquest of India and the tea industry there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast13_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast13-29">[29]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Sail" title="Age of Sail">Age of Sail</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seamen" title="Seamen">seamen</a> aboard ships of the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> made substitute coffee by dissolving burnt bread in hot water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fremont-Barnes2005_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fremont-Barnes2005-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_de_Clieu" title="Gabriel de Clieu">Gabriel de Clieu</a> took a coffee plant to the French territory of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> in the Caribbean<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, from which much of the world's cultivated arabica coffee is descended. Coffee thrived in the climate and was conveyed across the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast14_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast14-31">[31]</a></sup> Coffee was cultivated in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Domingue" title="Saint-Domingue">Saint-Domingue</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>) from 1734, and by 1788 it supplied half the world's coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast_2010_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast_2010-32">[32]</a></sup> The conditions that the slaves worked in on coffee plantations were a factor in the soon to follow <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>. The coffee industry never fully recovered there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast16_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast16-33">[33]</a></sup> It made a brief come-back in 1949 when Haiti was the world's 3rd largest coffee exporter, but fell quickly into rapid decline.
</p><p>Meanwhile, coffee had been <a href="/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Brazil#History" title="Coffee production in Brazil">introduced to Brazil</a> in 1727, although its cultivation did not gather momentum until independence in 1822.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast19_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast19-34">[34]</a></sup> After this time massive tracts of rainforest were cleared for coffee plantations, first in the vicinity of Rio de Janeiro and later São Paulo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast204_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast204-35">[35]</a></sup> Brazil went from having essentially no coffee exports in 1800, to being a significant regional producer in 1830, to being the largest producer in the world by 1852. In 1910–20, Brazil exported around 70% of the world's coffee, Colombia, Guatemala, and Venezuela, exported half of the remaining 30%, and Old World production accounted for less than 5% of world exports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats1920_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats1920-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cultivation was taken up by many countries in Central America in the latter half of the 19th century, and almost all involved the large-scale displacement and exploitation of the indigenous people. Harsh conditions led to many uprisings, coups and bloody suppression of peasants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast334_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast334-37">[37]</a></sup> The notable exception was <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, where lack of ready labor prevented the formation of large farms. Smaller farms and more egalitarian conditions ameliorated unrest over the 19th and 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast356_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast356-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rapid growth in coffee production in South America during the second half of the 19th century was matched by growth in consumption in developed countries, though nowhere has this growth been as pronounced as in the United States, where high rate of population growth was compounded by doubling of per capita consumption between 1860 and 1920. Though the United States was not the heaviest coffee-drinking nation at the time (<a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> all had comparable or higher levels of per capita consumption), due to its sheer size, it was already the largest consumer of coffee in the world by 1860, and, by 1920, around half of all coffee produced worldwide was consumed in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats1920_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-stats1920-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coffee has become a vital <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crop</a> for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. Over one hundred million people in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a> have become dependent on coffee as their primary source of income. It has become the primary export and backbone for African countries like Uganda, Burundi, Rwanda, and Ethiopia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethiopiacoffetrade_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ethiopiacoffetrade-39">[39]</a></sup> as well as many Central American countries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Coffea" title="Coffea">Coffea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_varieties" title="Coffee varieties">coffee varieties</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg"><img alt="Illustration of a single branch of a plant. Broad, ribbed leaves are accented by small white flowers at the base of the stalk. On the edge of the drawing are cutaway diagrams of parts of the plant." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2168" data-file-width="1572" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg/220px-Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg/330px-Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg/440px-Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffea_arabica_-_K%C3%B6hler%E2%80%93s_Medizinal-Pflanzen-189.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">Coffea arabica</a></i> plant and seeds</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_flowers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1152" data-file-width="2048" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Coffee_flowers.jpg/220px-Coffee_flowers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Coffee_flowers.jpg/330px-Coffee_flowers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Coffee_flowers.jpg/440px-Coffee_flowers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_flowers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robusta coffee flowers</div></div></div>
<p>Several species of shrub of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea" title="Coffea">Coffea</a></i> produce the berries from which coffee is extracted. The two main species commercially cultivated are <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_canephora" title="Coffea canephora">Coffea canephora</a></i> (predominantly a form known as 'robusta') and <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">C. arabica</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICO_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICO-40">[40]</a></sup> <i>C. arabica</i>, the most highly regarded species, is native to the southwestern highlands of Ethiopia and the <a href="/wiki/Boma_Plateau" title="Boma Plateau">Boma Plateau</a> in southeastern Sudan and possibly <a href="/wiki/Mount_Marsabit" title="Mount Marsabit">Mount Marsabit</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>C. canephora</i> is native to western and central Subsaharan Africa, from <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a> to <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> and southern Sudan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Less popular species are <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_liberica" title="Coffea liberica">C. liberica</a></i>, <i>C. stenophylla</i>, <i>C. mauritiana</i>, and <i>C. racemosa</i>.
</p><p>All coffee plants are classified in the large family <a href="/wiki/Rubiaceae" title="Rubiaceae">Rubiaceae</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> shrubs or trees that may grow 5 m (15 ft) tall when unpruned. The leaves are dark green and glossy, usually 10–15 cm (4–6 in) long and 6 cm (2.4 in) wide, simple, entire, and opposite. <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">Petioles</a> of opposite leaves fuse at the base to form interpetiolar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stipules" title="Stipules">stipules</a>, characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Rubiaceae" title="Rubiaceae">Rubiaceae</a>. The flowers are <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Morphology_.28large-scale_features.29" title="Leaf">axillary</a>, and clusters of fragrant white flowers bloom simultaneously. <a href="/wiki/Gynoecium" title="Gynoecium">Gynoecium</a> consists of an inferior ovary, also characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Rubiaceae" title="Rubiaceae">Rubiaceae</a>. The flowers are followed by oval berries of about 1.5 cm (0.6 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purdue_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-43">[43]</a></sup> When immature they are green, and they ripen to yellow, then crimson, before turning black on drying. Each berry usually contains two seeds, but 5–10% of the berries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> have only one; these are called <a href="/wiki/Peaberry" title="Peaberry">peaberries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Arabica berries ripen in six to eight months, while robusta takes nine to eleven months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PradeepkumarPradeep2008_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-PradeepkumarPradeep2008-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Coffea arabica</i> is predominantly self-pollinating, and as a result, the seedlings are generally uniform and vary little from their parents. In contrast, <i>Coffea canephora</i>, and <i>C. liberica</i> are self-incompatible and require <a href="/wiki/Outcrossing" title="Outcrossing">outcrossing</a>. This means that useful forms and hybrids must be propagated <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetatively</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CW158_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-CW158-47">[47]</a></sup> Cuttings, grafting, and budding are the usual methods of vegetative propagation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CW1612_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-CW1612-48">[48]</a></sup> On the other hand, there is great scope for experimentation in search of potential new strains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CW158_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-CW158-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Oregon_State_University" title="Oregon State University">Oregon State University</a> entomologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Poinar,_Jr." title="George Poinar, Jr.">George Poinar, Jr.</a> announced the discovery of a new plant species that's a 45-million-year-old relative of coffee found in amber.  Named <i>Strychnos electri</i>, after the Greek word for amber (electron), the flowers represent the first-ever fossils of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asterid" title="Asterid">asterid</a>, which is a clade of flowering plants that not only later gave us coffee, but also sunflowers, peppers, potatoes, mint – and deadly poisons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discovery_News_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discovery_News-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_coffee_production" title="List of countries by coffee production">List of countries by coffee production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2234" data-file-width="4500" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg/320px-Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg/480px-Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg/640px-Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carte_Coffea_robusta_arabic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing areas of coffee cultivation:<br/> r:<i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_canephora" title="Coffea canephora">Coffea canephora</a></i><br/> m:<i>Coffea canephora</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">Coffea arabica</a></i><br/> a:<i>Coffea arabica</i></div></div></div>
<p>The traditional method of planting coffee is to place 20 seeds in each hole at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy season</a>. This method loses about 50% of the seeds' potential, as about half fail to sprout. A more effective method of growing coffee, used in Brazil, is to raise seedlings in nurseries that are then planted outside at six to twelve months. Coffee is often <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercropped</a> with food crops, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beans" title="Beans">beans</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> during the first few years of cultivation as farmers become familiar with its requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purdue_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Coffea" title="Coffea">Coffee plants</a> grow within a defined area between the tropics of <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Capricorn</a>, termed the bean belt or coffee belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the two main species grown, <a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">arabica coffee</a> (from <i>C. arabica</i>) is generally more highly regarded than <a href="/wiki/Robusta_coffee" title="Robusta coffee">robusta coffee</a> (from <i>C. canephora</i>); robusta tends to be bitter and have less flavor but better body than arabica. For these reasons, about three-quarters of coffee cultivated worldwide is <i>C. arabica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICO_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICO-40">[40]</a></sup> Robusta strains also contain about 40–50% more caffeine than arabica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA763_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA763-51">[51]</a></sup> Consequently, this species is used as an inexpensive substitute for arabica in many commercial coffee blends. Good quality robusta beans are used in traditional Italian <a href="/wiki/Espresso" title="Espresso">espresso</a> blends to provide a full-bodied taste and a better foam head (known as <i>crema</i>).
</p><p>Additionally, <i>Coffea canephora</i> is less susceptible to disease than <i>C. arabica</i> and can be cultivated in lower altitudes and warmer climates where <i>C. arabica</i> will not thrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavironPonte2005_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-DavironPonte2005-52">[52]</a></sup> The robusta strain was first collected in 1890 from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lomani_River" title="Lomani River">Lomani River</a>, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a>, and was conveyed from the Congo Free State (now the Democratic Republic of the Congo) to Brussels to Java around 1900. From Java, further breeding resulted in the establishment of robusta plantations in many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In particular, the spread of the devastating coffee leaf rust (<i><a href="/wiki/Hemileia_vastatrix" title="Hemileia vastatrix">Hemileia vastatrix</a></i>), to which <i>C. arabica</i> is vulnerable, hastened the uptake of the resistant robusta. <i>Hemileia vastatrix</i> is a fungal pathogen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and results in light, rust-colored spots on the undersides of coffee plant leaves. <i>Hemileia vastatrix</i> grows exclusively on the leaves of coffee pants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Coffee leaf rust is found in virtually all countries that produce coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mycena_citricolor" title="Mycena citricolor">Mycena citricolor</a> is another threat to coffee plants, primarily in Latin America.  Mycena citricolor, commonly referred to as American Leaf Spot, is a fungus that can affect the whole coffee plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krishnan_2017_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krishnan_2017-57">[57]</a></sup> It can grow on leaves, resulting in leaves with holes that often fall from the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krishnan_2017_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Krishnan_2017-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 900 species of insect have been recorded as pests of coffee crops worldwide. Of these, over a third are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coleoptera" title="Coleoptera">beetles</a>, and over a quarter are <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">bugs</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Coffee_root-knot_nematode" title="Coffee root-knot nematode">20 species of nematodes</a>, 9 species of mites, and several snails and slugs also attack the crop. Birds and rodents sometimes eat coffee berries, but their impact is minor compared to invertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In general, <i>arabica</i> is the more sensitive species to invertebrate predation overall. Each part of the coffee plant is assailed by different animals. <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">Nematodes</a> attack the roots, <a href="/wiki/Coffee_borer_beetle" title="Coffee borer beetle">coffee borer beetles</a> burrow into stems and woody material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and the foliage is attacked by over 100 species of larvae (caterpillars) of <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">butterflies and moths</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mass spraying of insecticides has often proven disastrous, as predators of the pests are more sensitive than the pests themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Instead, integrated pest management has developed, using techniques such as targeted treatment of pest outbreaks, and managing crop environment away from conditions favouring pests. Branches infested with scale are often cut and left on the ground, which promotes scale parasites to not only attack the scale on the fallen branches but in the plant as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2-mm-long <a href="/wiki/Coffee_borer_beetle" title="Coffee borer beetle">coffee borer beetle</a> (<i>Hypothenemus hampei</i>) is the most damaging insect pest to the world's coffee industry, destroying up to 50 percent or more of the coffee berries on plantations in most coffee-producing countries. The adult female beetle nibbles a single tiny hole in a coffee berry and lays 35 to 50 eggs. Inside, the offspring grow, mate, and then emerge from the commercially ruined berry to disperse, repeating the cycle. <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">Pesticides</a> are mostly ineffective because the beetle juveniles are protected inside the berry nurseries, but they are vulnerable to predation by birds when they emerge. When groves of trees are nearby, the <a href="/wiki/American_yellow_warbler" title="American yellow warbler">American yellow warbler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rufous-capped_warbler" title="Rufous-capped warbler">rufous-capped warbler</a>, and other insectivorous birds have been shown to reduce by 50 percent the number of coffee berry borers in Costa Rica coffee plantations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beans from different countries or regions can usually be distinguished by differences in flavor, aroma, <a href="/wiki/Mouthfeel" title="Mouthfeel">body</a>, and acidity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> These taste characteristics are dependent not only on the coffee's growing region, but also on genetic subspecies (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variety_(biology)" title="Variety (biology)">varietals</a>) and processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castle_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-castle-65">[65]</a></sup> Varietals are generally known by the region in which they are grown, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_coffee" title="Colombian coffee">Colombian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_coffee" title="Java coffee">Java</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kona_coffee" title="Kona coffee">Kona</a>.
</p><p>Arabica coffee beans are cultivated mainly in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Africa" title="Eastern Africa">eastern Africa</a> or Asia, while robusta beans are grown in <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">central Africa</a>, throughout <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">southeast Asia</a>, and Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICO_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-ICO-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_effects">Ecological effects</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg"><img alt="A cluster of bushes with drooping leaves and long chains of flowers sits in a clearing, surrounded by forest" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg/220px-Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg/330px-Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg/440px-Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_Flowers_Show.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flowering <i>Coffea arabica</i> tree in a <a href="/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Brazil" title="Coffee production in Brazil">Brazilian plantation</a></div></div></div>
<p>Originally, coffee farming was done in the <a href="/wiki/Shade_(shadow)" title="Shade (shadow)">shade</a> of trees that provided a habitat for many animals and insects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janzen_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janzen-66">[66]</a></sup> Remnant forest trees were used for this purpose, but many species have been planted as well. These include leguminous trees of the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Albizia" title="Albizia">Albizia</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassia_(legume)" title="Cassia (legume)">Cassia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Erythrina" title="Erythrina">Erythrina</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gliricidia" title="Gliricidia">Gliricidia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Inga" title="Inga">Inga</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Leucaena" title="Leucaena">Leucaena</a></i>, as well as the nitrogen-fixing non-legume sheoaks of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Casuarina" title="Casuarina">Casuarina</a></i>, and the silky oak <i><a href="/wiki/Grevillea_robusta" title="Grevillea robusta">Grevillea robusta</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>This method is commonly referred to as the traditional shaded method, or "<a href="/wiki/Shade-grown_coffee" title="Shade-grown coffee">shade-grown</a>". Starting in the 1970s, many farmers switched their production method to sun cultivation, in which coffee is grown in rows under full sun with little or no forest canopy. This causes berries to ripen more rapidly and bushes to produce higher yields, but requires the clearing of trees and increased use of fertilizer and pesticides, which damage the environment and cause health problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salvesen_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salvesen-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unshaded coffee plants grown with fertilizer yield the most coffee, although unfertilized shaded crops generally yield more than unfertilized unshaded crops: the response to fertilizer is much greater in full sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> While traditional coffee production causes berries to ripen more slowly and produce lower yields, the quality of the coffee is allegedly superior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cec1999_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-cec1999-70">[70]</a></sup> In addition, the traditional shaded method provides living space for many wildlife species. Proponents of shade cultivation say environmental problems such as <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticide_pollution" title="Pesticide pollution">pesticide pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_degradation" title="Soil degradation">soil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_degradation" title="Water degradation">water degradation</a> are the side effects of the practices employed in sun cultivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janzen_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janzen-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Birding_Association" title="American Birding Association">American Birding Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Migratory_Bird_Center" title="Smithsonian Migratory Bird Center">Smithsonian Migratory Bird Center</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-migratory_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-migratory-72">[72]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Arbor_Day_Foundation" title="National Arbor Day Foundation">National Arbor Day Foundation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arbor_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-arbor-73">[73]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Alliance" title="Rainforest Alliance">Rainforest Alliance</a> have led a campaign for 'shade-grown' and <a href="/wiki/Organic_coffee" title="Organic coffee">organic coffees</a>, which can be sustainably harvested.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Shaded coffee cultivation systems show greater biodiversity than full-sun systems, and those more distant from continuous forest compare rather poorly to undisturbed native forest in terms of habitat value for some bird species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciam2000_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciam2000-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rickert2005_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rickert2005-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another issue concerning coffee is its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">use of water</a>. It takes about 140 liters (37 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_gallon" title="US gallon">U.S. gal</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">water</a> to grow the coffee beans needed to produce one cup of coffee, and coffee is often grown in countries where there is a water shortage, such as <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Used_coffee_grounds" title="Used coffee grounds">Used coffee grounds</a> may be used for <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">composting</a> or as a <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a>. They are especially appreciated by <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_pH#Examples_of_plant_preferences" title="Soil pH">acid-loving plants</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blueberries" title="Blueberries">blueberries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Some commercial coffee shops run initiatives to make better use of these grounds, including <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>' "Grounds for your Garden" project,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> and community sponsored initiatives such as "Ground to Ground".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> may significantly impact coffee yields within a few decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Botanic_Gardens,_Kew" title="Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew">Kew Royal Botanic Gardens</a> concluded that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> threatens the genetic diversity of Arabica plants found in Ethiopia and surrounding countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing">Processing</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coffee_production" title="Coffee production">Coffee production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1152" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg/220px-Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg/330px-Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg/440px-Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_drying_traditional_Indonesia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Traditional coffee beans drying in Kalibaru, Indonesia</div></div></div>
<p>Coffee berries and their seeds undergo several processes before they become the familiar roasted coffee. Berries have been traditionally selectively picked by hand; a labor-intensive method, it involves the selection of only the berries at the peak of ripeness. More commonly, crops are strip picked, where all berries are harvested simultaneously regardless of ripeness by person or machine. After picking, green coffee is processed by one of two methods—the dry process method, simpler and less labor-intensive as the berries can be strip picked, and the wet process method, which incorporates fermentation into the process and yields a mild coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>Then they are sorted by ripeness and color, and most often the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruiting" title="Fruiting">flesh</a> of the berry is removed, usually by machine, and the seeds are fermented to remove the slimy layer of <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a> still present on the seed. When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> is finished, the seeds are washed with large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> to remove the fermentation residue, which generates massive amounts of <a href="/wiki/Coffee_wastewater" title="Coffee wastewater">coffee wastewater</a>. Finally, the seeds are dried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kummer2003p38_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-kummer2003p38-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>The best (but least used) method of drying coffee is using drying tables. In this method, the pulped and fermented coffee is spread thinly on raised beds, which allows the air to pass on all sides of the coffee, and then the coffee is mixed by hand. In this method the drying that takes place is more uniform, and fermentation is less likely. Most African coffee is dried in this manner and certain coffee farms around the world are starting to use this traditional method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kummer2003p38_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-kummer2003p38-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>Next, the coffee is sorted, and labeled as green coffee. Some companies use cylinders to pump in heated air to dry the coffee seeds, though this is generally in places where the humidity is very high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kummer2003p38_84-2"><a href="#cite_note-kummer2003p38-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> coffee known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kopi_luwak" title="Kopi luwak">kopi luwak</a> undergoes a peculiar process made from coffee berries eaten by the <a href="/wiki/Asian_palm_civet" title="Asian palm civet">Asian palm civet</a>, passing through its digestive tract, with the beans eventually harvested from <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>. Coffee brewed from this process<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marcone_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-marcone-85">[85]</a></sup> is among the most expensive in the world, with bean prices reaching $160 per pound<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top10_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-top10-86">[86]</a></sup> or $30 per brewed cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-econ-87">[87]</a></sup> Kopi luwak coffee is said to have uniquely rich, slightly smoky aroma and flavor with hints of chocolate, resulting from the action of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_enzymes" title="Digestive enzymes">digestive enzymes</a> breaking down bean <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> to facilitate partial <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marcone_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-marcone-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-econ-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roasting">Roasting</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coffee_roasting" title="Coffee roasting">Coffee roasting</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_beans2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5792" data-file-width="8688" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Coffee_beans2.jpg/220px-Coffee_beans2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Coffee_beans2.jpg/330px-Coffee_beans2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Coffee_beans2.jpg/440px-Coffee_beans2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coffee_beans2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roasted coffee beans</div></div></div>
<p>The next step in the process is the roasting of the green coffee. Coffee is usually sold in a roasted state, and with rare exceptions all coffee is roasted before it is consumed. It can be sold <a href="/wiki/Coffee_roasting" title="Coffee roasting">roasted</a> by the supplier, or it can be <a href="/wiki/Home_roasting_coffee" title="Home roasting coffee">home roasted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kummer2003p37_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-kummer2003p37-88">[88]</a></sup> The roasting process influences the taste of the beverage by changing the coffee bean both physically and chemically. The bean decreases in weight as moisture is lost and increases in volume, causing it to become less dense. The density of the bean also influences the strength of the coffee and requirements for packaging.
</p><p>The actual roasting begins when the temperature inside the bean reaches approximately 200 °C (392 °F), though different varieties of seeds differ in moisture and density and therefore roast at different rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trent_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-trent-89">[89]</a></sup> During roasting, <a href="/wiki/Caramelization" title="Caramelization">caramelization</a> occurs as intense heat breaks down <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a>, changing them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sugar" title="Simple sugar">simple sugars</a> that begin to brown, which alters the color of the bean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kummer2003p261_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-kummer2003p261-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">Sucrose</a> is rapidly lost during the roasting process, and may disappear entirely in darker roasts. During roasting, aromatic oils and acids weaken, changing the flavor; at 205 °C (401 °F), other oils start to develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trent_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-trent-89">[89]</a></sup> One of these oils, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caffeol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Caffeol (page does not exist)">caffeol</a>, is created at about 200 °C (392 °F), which is largely responsible for coffee's aroma and flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plant_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-plant-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Roasting is the last step of processing the beans in their intact state. During this last treatment, while still in the bean state, more caffeine breaks down above 235 °C (455 °F). Dark roasting is the utmost step in bean processing removing the most caffeine. Although, dark roasting is not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">Decaffeination</a> process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grading_roasted_beans">Grading roasted beans</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Food_grading" title="Food grading">Food grading</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fancy_a_cupper.jpg"><img alt="Two men hold spoons over a row of cups filled with coffee." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Fancy_a_cupper.jpg/220px-Fancy_a_cupper.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Fancy_a_cupper.jpg/330px-Fancy_a_cupper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Fancy_a_cupper.jpg/440px-Fancy_a_cupper.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fancy_a_cupper.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coffee "cuppers", or professional tasters, grade the coffee</div></div></div>
<p>Depending on the color of the roasted beans as perceived by the human eye, they will be labeled as light, medium light, medium, medium dark, dark, or very dark. A more accurate method of discerning the degree of roast involves measuring the reflected light from roasted seeds illuminated with a light source in the near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> spectrum. This elaborate light meter uses a process known as <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> to return a number that consistently indicates the roasted coffee's relative degree of roast or flavor development.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roast_characteristics">Roast characteristics</span></h3>
<p>The degree of roast has an effect upon coffee flavor and body. Darker roasts are generally bolder because they have less fiber content and a more sugary flavor. Lighter roasts have a more complex and therefore perceived stronger flavor from aromatic oils and acids otherwise destroyed by longer roasting times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mauro_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mauro-91">[91]</a></sup> Roasting does not alter the amount of caffeine in the bean, but does give less caffeine when the beans are measured by volume because the beans expand during roasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>A small amount of chaff is produced during roasting from the skin left on the seed after processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bay_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bay-93">[93]</a></sup> Chaff is usually removed from the seeds by air movement, though a small amount is added to dark roast coffees to soak up oils on the seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trent_89-2"><a href="#cite_note-trent-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decaffeination">Decaffeination</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">Decaffeination</a> may also be part of the processing that coffee seeds undergo. Seeds are decaffeinated when they are still green. Many methods can remove caffeine from coffee, but all involve either soaking the green seeds in hot water (often called the "Swiss water process")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> or steaming them, then using a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> to dissolve caffeine-containing oils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plant_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-plant-22">[22]</a></sup> Decaffeination is often done by processing companies, and the extracted caffeine is usually sold to the pharmaceutical industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plant_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-plant-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jam_jar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Jam_jar.jpg/220px-Jam_jar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Jam_jar.jpg/330px-Jam_jar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Jam_jar.jpg/440px-Jam_jar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jam_jar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coffee container</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coffee_bean_storage" title="Coffee bean storage">Coffee bean storage</a></div>
<p>Coffee is best stored in an airtight container made of ceramic, glass, or non-reactive metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup> Higher quality prepackaged coffee usually has a one-way valve which prevents air from entering while allowing the coffee to release gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodnetwork-truebrew_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-foodnetwork-truebrew-96">[96]</a></sup> Coffee freshness and flavor is preserved when it is stored away from moisture, heat, and light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup> The ability of coffee to absorb strong smells from food means that it should be kept away from such smells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-2"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup> Storage of coffee in refrigerators is not recommended due to the presence of moisture which can cause deterioration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-3"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup> Exterior walls of buildings which face the sun may heat the interior of a home, and this heat may damage coffee stored near such a wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-4"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup> Heat from nearby ovens also harms stored coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Consumer_Reports_May_2013_95-5"><a href="#cite_note-Consumer_Reports_May_2013-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1931, a method of packing coffee in a sealed vacuum in cans was introduced. The roasted coffee was packed and then 99% of the air was removed, allowing the coffee to be stored indefinitely until the can was opened. Today this method is in mass use for coffee in a large part of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing">Brewing</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coffee_preparation" title="Coffee preparation">Coffee preparation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4266" data-file-width="3819" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Consumer_Reports_-_Zojirushi_coffeemaker_alt.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A contemporary automatic coffeemaker</div></div></div>
<p>Coffee beans must be ground and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drip_brew" title="Drip brew">brewed</a> to create a beverage. The criteria for choosing a method include flavor and economy. Almost all methods of preparing coffee require that the beans be ground and then mixed with hot water long enough to allow the flavor to emerge but not so long as to draw out bitter compounds. The liquid can be consumed after the spent grounds are removed. Brewing considerations include the fineness of grind, the way in which the water is used to extract the flavor, the ratio of coffee grounds to water (the brew ratio), additional flavorings such as <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, milk, and spices, and the technique to be used to separate spent grounds. Ideal holding temperatures range from 85–88 °C (185–190 °F) to as high as 93 °C (199 °F) and the ideal serving temperature is 68 to 79 °C (154 to 174 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The recommended brew ratio for non-espresso coffee is around 55 to 60 grams of grounds per litre of water, or two level <a href="/wiki/Tablespoon" title="Tablespoon">tablespoons</a> for a 5- or 6-ounce cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>The roasted coffee beans may be ground at a roastery, in a grocery store, or in the home. Most coffee is roasted and ground at a roastery and sold in packaged form, though roasted coffee beans can be ground at home immediately before consumption. It is also possible, though uncommon, to roast raw beans at home.
</p><p>Coffee beans may be ground in several ways. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burr_grinder" title="Burr grinder">burr grinder</a> uses revolving elements to shear the seed; a <a href="/wiki/Blade_grinder" title="Blade grinder">blade grinder</a> cuts the seeds with blades moving at high speed; and a mortar and pestle crushes the seeds. For most brewing methods a burr grinder is deemed superior because the grind is more even and the grind size can be adjusted.
</p><p>The type of grind is often named after the brewing method for which it is generally used. <a href="/wiki/Turkish_coffee" title="Turkish coffee">Turkish grind</a> is the finest grind, while <a href="/wiki/Coffee_percolator" title="Coffee percolator">coffee percolator</a> or <a href="/wiki/French_press" title="French press">French press</a> are the coarsest grinds. The most common grinds are between these two extremes: a medium grind is used in most home coffee-brewing machines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothstein_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rothstein-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coffee may be brewed by several methods. It may be boiled, steeped, or pressurized. Brewing coffee by boiling was the earliest method, and Turkish coffee is an example of this method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-101">[101]</a></sup> It is prepared by grinding or pounding the seeds to a fine powder, then adding it to water and bringing it to the boil for no more than an instant in a pot called a <i><a href="/wiki/Cezve" title="Cezve">cezve</a></i> or, in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, a <i>bríki</i>. This produces a strong coffee with a layer of foam on the surface and sediment (which is not meant for drinking) settling at the bottom of the cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coffee percolators and <a href="/wiki/Coffeemaker" title="Coffeemaker">automatic coffeemakers</a> brew coffee using gravity. In an automatic coffeemaker, hot water drips onto coffee grounds that are held in a paper, plastic, or perforated metal <a href="/wiki/Coffee_filter" title="Coffee filter">coffee filter</a>, allowing the water to seep through the ground coffee while extracting its oils and essences. The liquid drips through the coffee and the filter into a carafe or pot, and the spent <a href="/wiki/Coffee_preparation" title="Coffee preparation">grounds</a> are retained in the filter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a percolator, boiling water is forced into a chamber above a filter by steam pressure created by boiling. The water then seeps through the grounds, and the process is repeated until terminated by removing from the heat, by an internal timer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy_102-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-102">[102]</a></sup>
or by a thermostat that turns off the heater when the entire pot reaches a certain temperature.
</p><p>Coffee may be brewed by steeping in a device such as a French press (also known as a cafetière, coffee press or coffee plunger).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davids_1991_128_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davids_1991_128-103">[103]</a></sup> Ground coffee and hot water are combined in a cylindrical vessel and left to brew for a few minutes. A circular filter which fits tightly in the cylinder fixed to a plunger is then pushed down from the top to force the grounds to the bottom. The filter retains the grounds at the bottom as the coffee is poured from the container. Because the coffee grounds are in direct contact with the water, all the coffee oils remain in the liquid, making it a stronger beverage. This method of brewing leaves more sediment than in coffee made by an automatic coffee machine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davids_1991_128_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davids_1991_128-103">[103]</a></sup> Supporters of the French press method point out that the sediment issue can be minimized by using the right type of grinder: they claim that a rotary blade grinder cuts the coffee bean into a wide range of sizes, including a fine coffee dust that remains as sludge at the bottom of the cup, while a burr grinder uniformly grinds the beans into consistently-sized grinds, allowing the coffee to settle uniformly and be trapped by the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Within the first minute of brewing 95% of the caffeine is released from the coffee bean.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Espresso" title="Espresso">espresso</a> method forces hot pressurized and vaporized water through ground coffee. As a result of brewing under high pressure (ideally between 9–10 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>), the espresso beverage is more concentrated (as much as 10 to 15 times the quantity of coffee to water as gravity-brewing methods can produce) and has a more complex physical and chemical constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> A well-prepared espresso has a reddish-brown foam called <i>crema</i> that floats on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothstein_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rothstein-100">[100]</a></sup> Other pressurized water methods include the <a href="/wiki/Moka_pot" title="Moka pot">moka pot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_coffee_maker" title="Vacuum coffee maker">vacuum coffee maker</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_brew_coffee" title="Cold brew coffee">Cold brew coffee</a> is made by steeping coarsely ground beans in cold water for several hours, then filtering them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bonne_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bonne-106">[106]</a></sup> This results in a brew lower in acidity than most hot-brewing methods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brewed_coffee" title="Brewed coffee">Brewed coffee</a> from typical grounds prepared with tap water contains 40 mg <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> per 100 gram and no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Essential_nutrients" title="Essential nutrients">essential nutrients</a> in significant content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Espresso" title="Espresso">espresso</a>, however, likely due to its higher amount of suspended solids, there are significant contents of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niacin" title="Niacin">niacin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riboflavin" title="Riboflavin">riboflavin</a>, and 212 mg of caffeine per 100 grams of grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serving">Serving</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_coffee_beverages" title="List of coffee beverages">List of coffee beverages</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3194" data-file-width="2924" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/260px-French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/390px-French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/520px-French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:French_School_-_Enjoying_Coffee_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Enjoying coffee</i>, painting by unknown artist in the <a href="/wiki/Pera_Museum" title="Pera Museum">Pera Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>Once brewed, coffee may be served in a variety of ways. Drip-brewed, percolated, or French-pressed/cafetière coffee may be served as <i>white coffee</i> with a dairy product such as milk or cream, or dairy substitute, or as <i>black coffee</i> with no such addition. It may be sweetened with sugar or artificial sweetener. When served cold, it is called <i><a href="/wiki/Iced_coffee" title="Iced coffee">iced coffee</a></i>.
</p><p>Espresso-based coffee has a variety of possible presentations. In its most basic form, an espresso is served alone as a <i>shot</i> or <i>short black</i>, or with hot water added, when it is known as <a href="/wiki/Caff%C3%A8_Americano" title="Caffè Americano">Caffè Americano</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Long_black" title="Long black">long black</a> is made by pouring a double espresso into an equal portion of water, retaining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crema_(coffee)" title="Crema (coffee)">crema</a>, unlike Caffè Americano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castle_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Castle-109">[109]</a></sup>
Milk is added in various forms to an espresso: steamed milk makes a <a href="/wiki/Latte" title="Latte">caffè latte</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> equal parts steamed milk and milk froth make a <a href="/wiki/Cappuccino" title="Cappuccino">cappuccino</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Castle_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-Castle-109">[109]</a></sup> and a dollop of hot foamed milk on top creates a <a href="/wiki/Caff%C3%A8_macchiato" title="Caffè macchiato">caffè macchiato</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Flat_white" title="Flat white">flat white</a> is prepared by adding steamed hot milk (<a href="/wiki/Microfoam" title="Microfoam">microfoam</a>) to espresso so that the flavour is brought out and the texture is unusually velvety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenneally_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenneally-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> It has less milk than a latte but both are varieties of coffee to which the milk can be added in such a way as to create a decorative surface pattern. Such effects are known as <a href="/wiki/Latte_art" title="Latte art">latte art</a>.
</p><p>Coffee can also be incorporated with alcohol to produce a variety of beverages: it is combined with whiskey in <a href="/wiki/Irish_coffee" title="Irish coffee">Irish coffee</a>, and it forms the base of alcoholic coffee liqueurs such as <a href="/wiki/Kahl%C3%BAa" title="Kahlúa">Kahlúa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tia_Maria" title="Tia Maria">Tia Maria</a>. Darker beers such as stout and porter give a chocolate or coffee-like taste due to roasted grains even though actual coffee beans are not added to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instant_coffee">Instant coffee</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Instant_coffee" title="Instant coffee">Instant coffee</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Instant_coffee.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Instant_coffee.jpg/230px-Instant_coffee.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Instant_coffee.jpg/345px-Instant_coffee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Instant_coffee.jpg/460px-Instant_coffee.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Instant_coffee.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Instant coffee</div></div></div>
<p>A number of products are sold for the convenience of consumers who do not want to prepare their own coffee or who do not have access to coffeemaking equipment. Instant coffee is dried into soluble powder or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze_drying" title="Freeze drying">freeze-dried</a> into granules that can be quickly dissolved in hot water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Originally invented in 1907,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast119_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast119-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> it rapidly gained in popularity in many countries in the post-war period, with <a href="/wiki/Nescaf%C3%A9" title="Nescafé">Nescafé</a> being the most popular product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast195_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast195-119">[119]</a></sup> Many consumers determined that the convenience in preparing a cup of instant coffee more than made up for a perceived inferior taste,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast196_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast196-120">[120]</a></sup> although, since the late 1970s, instant coffee has been produced differently in such a way that is similar to the taste of freshly brewed coffee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Paralleling (and complementing) the rapid rise of instant coffee was the <a href="/wiki/Coffee_vending_machine" title="Coffee vending machine">coffee vending machine</a> invented in 1947 and widely distributed since the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast197_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast197-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_canned_coffee" title="Japanese canned coffee">Canned coffee</a> has been popular in Asian countries for many years, particularly in China, Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan. Vending machines typically sell varieties of flavored canned coffee, much like brewed or percolated coffee, available both hot and cold. Japanese convenience stores and groceries also have a wide availability of bottled coffee drinks, which are typically lightly sweetened and pre-blended with milk. Bottled coffee drinks are also consumed in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>Liquid coffee concentrates are sometimes used in large institutional situations where coffee needs to be produced for thousands of people at the same time. It is described as having a flavor about as good as low-grade robusta coffee, and costs about 10¢ a cup to produce. The machines can process up to 500 cups an hour, or 1,000 if the water is preheated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_and_distribution">Sale and distribution</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_coffee" title="Economics of coffee">Economics of coffee</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9,_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="485" data-file-width="688" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG/220px-Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG/330px-Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG/440px-Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9%2C_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sacos_de_caf%C3%A9,_Casa_do_Bandeirante_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Brazil" title="Coffee production in Brazil">Brazilian coffee</a> sacks</div></div></div>
<p>Coffee ingestion on average is about a third of that of <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">tap water</a> in North America and Europe. Worldwide, 6.7 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">metric tons</a> of coffee were produced annually in 1998–2000, and the forecast is a rise to seven million metric tons annually by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brazil remains the largest coffee exporting nation, however Vietnam tripled its exports between 1995 and 1999 and became a major producer of robusta seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> Indonesia is the third-largest coffee exporter overall and the largest producer of washed arabica coffee. Organic Honduran coffee is a rapidly growing emerging commodity owing to the Honduran climate and rich soil.
</p><p>In 2013, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i> reported that global coffee prices dropped more than 50 percent year-over-year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> The downward trend continued, and in September 2018 coffee futures dropped to a 12-year low, below the cost of production for many farmers in mountainous regions who cannot use mechanized harvesting methods. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, black ivory coffee beans are fed to <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a> whose digestive enzymes reduce the bitter taste of beans collected from dung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hp_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-hp-128">[128]</a></sup> These beans sell for up to $1,100 a kilogram ($500 per lb), achieving the world's most expensive coffee<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hp_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-hp-128">[128]</a></sup> some three times costlier than beans harvested from the dung of <a href="/wiki/Asian_palm_civet" title="Asian palm civet">Asian palm civets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top10_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-top10-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-econ_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-econ-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commodity_market">Commodity market</span></h3>
<p>Coffee is bought and sold as green coffee beans by roasters, investors, and price speculators as a tradable commodity in <a href="/wiki/Commodity_market" title="Commodity market">commodity markets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded funds</a>. Coffee <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futures_contracts" title="Futures contracts">futures contracts</a> for Grade 3 washed arabicas are traded on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mercantile_Exchange" title="New York Mercantile Exchange">New York Mercantile Exchange</a> under ticker symbol <b>KC</b>, with contract deliveries occurring every year in March, May, July, September, and December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> Coffee is an example of a product that has been susceptible to significant commodity futures price variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> Higher and lower grade arabica coffees are sold through other channels. Futures contracts for robusta coffee are traded on the <a href="/wiki/London_International_Financial_Futures_and_Options_Exchange" title="London International Financial Futures and Options Exchange">London International Financial Futures and Options Exchange</a> and, since 2007, on the New York <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_Exchange" title="Intercontinental Exchange">Intercontinental Exchange</a>.
</p><p>Dating to the 1970s, coffee has been incorrectly described by many, including historian <a href="/wiki/Mark_Pendergrast" title="Mark Pendergrast">Mark Pendergrast</a>, as the world's "second most legally traded commodity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergrast2009_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-pendergrast2009-132">[132]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> Instead, "coffee was the second most valuable commodity exported by developing countries," from 1970 to circa 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talbot2004_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-talbot2004-134">[134]</a></sup> This fact was derived from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Trade_and_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Trade and Development">United Nations Conference on Trade and Development</a> Commodity Yearbooks which show "Third World" commodity exports by value in the period 1970–1998 as being in order of crude oil in first place, coffee in second, followed by sugar, cotton, and others. Coffee continues to be an important commodity export for developing countries, but more recent figures are not readily available due to the shifting and politicized nature of the category "developing country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergrast2009_132-1"><a href="#cite_note-pendergrast2009-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/International_Coffee_Day" title="International Coffee Day">International Coffee Day</a>, which is claimed to have originated in Japan in 1983 with an event organized by the All Japan Coffee Association, takes place on September 29 in several countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span></h2>
<p>A 2017 review of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinical_trials" title="Clinical trials">clinical trials</a> found that drinking coffee is generally safe within usual levels of intake and is more likely to improve health outcomes than to cause harm at doses of 3 or 4 cups of coffee daily. Exceptions include possible increased risk in women having <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">bone fractures</a>, and a possible increased risk in pregnant women of <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">fetal loss</a> or <a href="/wiki/Small_for_gestational_age" title="Small for gestational age">decreased birth weight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup> Results were complicated by poor study quality, and differences in age, gender, health status, and serving size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mortality">Mortality</span></h3>
<p>In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>–<a href="/wiki/AARP" title="AARP">AARP</a> Diet and Health Study analysed the relationship between coffee drinking and <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality</a>. They found that higher coffee consumption was associated with lower risk of death, and that those who drank any coffee lived longer than those who did not. However the authors noted, "whether this was a causal or associational finding cannot be determined from our data."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEJM_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM-138">[138]</a></sup> A 2014 <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">meta-analysis</a> found that coffee consumption (4 cups/day) was inversely associated with all-cause mortality (a 16% lower risk), as well as <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a> mortality specifically (a 21% lower risk from drinking 3 cups/day), but not with <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> Additional meta-analysis studies corroborated these findings, showing that higher coffee consumption (2–4 cups per day) was associated with a reduced risk of death by all disease causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bjn_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-bjn-140">[140]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phn_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-phn-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_disease">Cardiovascular disease</span></h3>
<p>Moderate coffee consumption is not a risk factor for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_heart_disease" title="Coronary heart disease">coronary heart disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18707777_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18707777-142">[142]</a></sup> A 2012 meta-analysis concluded that people who drank moderate amounts of coffee had a lower rate of <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a>, with the biggest effect found for those who drank more than four cups a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> A 2014 meta-analysis concluded that <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, such as coronary artery disease and <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, is less likely with three to five cups of non-decaffeinated coffee per day, but more likely with over five cups per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ding2014_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ding2014-144">[144]</a></sup> A 2016 meta-analysis showed that coffee consumption was associated with a reduced risk of death in patients who have had a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p><p>Drinking four or more cups of coffee per day does not affect the risk of <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a> compared to drinking little or no coffee; however, drinking one to three cups per day may be at a slightly increased risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21450934_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21450934-146">[146]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_health">Mental health</span></h3>
<p>Long-term preliminary research, including assessment of symptoms for <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive_impairment" title="Cognitive impairment">cognitive impairment</a>, was inconclusive for coffee having an effect in the elderly, mainly due to the poor quality of the studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> There appears to be a beneficial relationship between coffee intake and <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a> as well as coffee intake and depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Parkinson.27s_disease"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Parkinson's_disease">Parkinson's disease</span></h3>
<p>Meta-analyses have consistently found that long-term coffee consumption is associated with a lower risk of <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poole_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-poole-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Type_II_diabetes">Type II diabetes</span></h3>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Systematic_review" title="Systematic review">systematic review</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">meta-analysis</a> of 28 prospective observational studies, representing over one million participants, every additional cup of caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee consumed in a day was associated, respectively, with a 9% and 6% lower risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_2_diabetes" title="Type 2 diabetes">type 2 diabetes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ding2014-24459154_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ding2014-24459154-148">[148]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer">Cancer</span></h3>
<p>The effects of coffee consumption on cancer risk remain unclear, with reviews and meta-analyses showing either no relationship<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xie_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-xie-149">[149]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-akter_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-akter-150">[150]</a></sup> or a slightly lower risk of cancer onset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bravi_151-0"><a href="#cite_note-bravi-151">[151]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> Studies suggest that coffee consumption of 2 cups per/day was associated with a 14% increased risk of developing lung cancer, but only among people who smoke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_action">Method of action</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="220" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg/220px-Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg/330px-Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg/440px-Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caffeine-2D-skeletal.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula" title="Skeletal formula">Skeletal formula</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> molecule</div></div></div>
<p>One psychoactive chemical in coffee is <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_receptor" title="Adenosine receptor">adenosine receptor</a> antagonist that is known for its <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulant</a> effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> Coffee also contains the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoamine_oxidase_inhibitors" title="Monoamine oxidase inhibitors">monoamine oxidase inhibitors</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-carboline" title="Β-carboline">β-carboline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harmane" title="Harmane">harmane</a>, which may contribute to its psychoactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAOI_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAOI-155">[155]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a healthy <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, caffeine is mostly broken down by the hepatic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsomal" title="Microsomal">microsomal</a> enzymatic system. The excreted metabolites are mostly <a href="/wiki/Paraxanthine" title="Paraxanthine">paraxanthines</a>—<a href="/wiki/Theobromine" title="Theobromine">theobromine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theophylline" title="Theophylline">theophylline</a>—and a small amount of unchanged caffeine. Therefore, the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of caffeine depends on the state of this enzymatic system of the liver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zivković_2000_33–6_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zivković_2000_33–6-156">[156]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols" title="Polyphenols">Polyphenols</a> in coffee have been shown to affect <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">free radicals</a> <a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> but there is no evidence that this effect occurs in humans. Polyphenol levels vary depending on how beans are roasted as well as for how long. As interpreted by the <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling_Institute" title="Linus Pauling Institute">Linus Pauling Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a>, dietary polyphenols, such as those ingested by consuming coffee, have little or no direct <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> value following ingestion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPI_159-0"><a href="#cite_note-LPI-159">[159]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efsa_160-0"><a href="#cite_note-efsa-160">[160]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Caffeine_content">Caffeine content</span></h2>

The caffeine content of a cup of coffee varies depending mainly on the brewing method, and also on the coffee variety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/USDA_National_Nutrient_Database" title="USDA National Nutrient Database">USDA National Nutrient Database</a>, an 8-ounce (237 ml) cup of "coffee brewed from grounds" contains 95 mg caffeine, whereas an espresso (25 ml) contains 53 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to an article in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Dietetic_Association" title="Journal of the American Dietetic Association">Journal of the American Dietetic Association</a></i>, coffee has the following caffeine content, depending on how it is prepared:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunker_162-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bunker-162">[162]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coffeehouses">Coffeehouses</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coffeehouse" title="Coffeehouse">Coffeehouse</a> for a social history of coffee, and <a href="/wiki/Caff%C3%A8" title="Caffè">caffè</a> for specifically Italian traditions</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1892" data-file-width="2806" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg/220px-1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg/330px-1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg/440px-1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1004-CoffeeSceneCairo18th.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A coffeehouse in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>, 18th century</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1488" data-file-width="1959" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg/220px-Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg/330px-Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg/440px-Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wien_Cafe_Central_2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_Central" title="Café Central">Café Central</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>. A staple of the <a href="/wiki/Viennese_coffee_house" title="Viennese coffee house">Viennese coffee house</a> tradition, it has remained open since 1876</div></div></div>
<p>Widely known as coffeehouses or cafés, establishments serving prepared coffee or other hot beverages have existed for over five hundred years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Coffeehouses in <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> became a concern as places for political gatherings to the imams who banned them, and the drink, for Muslims between 1512 and 1524. In 1530 the first coffeehouse was opened in <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup> The first coffeehouse in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> was opened in 1475<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> by traders arriving from Damascus and <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a>. Soon after, coffeehouses became part of the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_Ottoman_Empire" title="Culture of the Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Culture</a>, spreading rapidly to all regions of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.
</p><p>In the 17th century, coffee appeared for the first time in Europe outside the Ottoman Empire, and coffeehouses were established and quickly became popular. The first coffeehouses in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> appeared in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, as a result of the traffic between <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">La Serenissima</a> and the Ottomans; the very first one is recorded in 1645. The first coffeehouse in England was set up in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> in 1650 by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jewish</a> man named Jacob in the building now known as "The Grand Cafe". A plaque on the wall still commemorates this and the cafe is now a cocktail bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup> By 1675, there were more than 3,000 coffeehouses in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup>
</p><p>A legend says that after the second <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vienna" title="Battle of Vienna">Turkish siege of Vienna</a> in 1683, the Viennese discovered many bags of coffee in the abandoned Ottoman encampment. Using this captured stock, a Polish soldier named <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_Franciszek_Kulczycki" title="Jerzy Franciszek Kulczycki">Kulczycki</a> opened the first <a href="/wiki/Viennese_coffee_house" title="Viennese coffee house">coffeehouse in Vienna</a>. This story never happened. Nowadays it is proven that the first coffeehouse in Vienna was opened by the Armenian Johannes Theodat in 1685.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1672 an Armenian named Pascal established a coffee stall in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> that was ultimately unsuccessful and the city had to wait until 1689 for its first coffeehouse when <a href="/wiki/Procopio_Cut%C3%B2" title="Procopio Cutò">Procopio Cutò</a> opened the <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_Procope" title="Café Procope">Café Procope</a>. This coffeehouse still exists today and was a major meeting place of the French <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a>; <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Rousseau</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Denis Diderot</a> frequented it, and it is arguably the birthplace of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a></i>, the first modern encyclopedia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup>  America had its first coffeehouse in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, in 1676.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup> Coffee, tea and beer were often served together in establishments which functioned both as coffeehouses and taverns; one such was the Green Dragon in Boston, where <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Otis,_Jr." title="James Otis, Jr.">James Otis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Revere" title="Paul Revere">Paul Revere</a> planned rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast13_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast13-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="800" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg/220px-Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg/330px-Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg/440px-Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Espresso-machine-first-patent-angelo-moriondo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First patent for the espresso machine, Angelo Moriondo (1884)</div></div></div>
<p>The modern steamless <a href="/wiki/Espresso_machine" title="Espresso machine">espresso machine</a> was invented in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, Italy, in 1938 by <a href="/wiki/Gaggia" title="Gaggia">Achille Gaggia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast218_177-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast218-177">[177]</a></sup> and from there spread in coffeehouses and restaurants across Italy and the rest of Europe in the early 1950s. An Italian named Pino Riservato opened the first espresso bar, the Moka Bar, in <a href="/wiki/Soho" title="Soho">Soho</a> in 1952, and there were 400 such bars in London alone by 1956. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cappucino" title="Cappucino">Cappucino</a> was particularly popular among English drinkers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast219_178-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast219-178">[178]</a></sup> Similarly in the United States, the espresso craze spread. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Beach,_San_Francisco,_California" title="North Beach, San Francisco, California">North Beach</a> in San Francisco saw the opening of the Caffe Trieste in 1957, which served <a href="/wiki/Beat_Generation" title="Beat Generation">Beat Generation</a> poets such as <a href="/wiki/Allen_Ginsberg" title="Allen Ginsberg">Allen Ginsberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Kaufman" title="Bob Kaufman">Bob Kaufman</a> alongside Italian immigrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast219_178-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast219-178">[178]</a></sup> Similar such cafes existed in Greenwich Village and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast219_178-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast219-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peet%27s_Coffee_%26_Tea" title="Peet's Coffee &amp; Tea">Peet's Coffee &amp; Tea</a> store opened in 1966 in Berkeley, California by Dutch native <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Peet" title="Alfred Peet">Alfred Peet</a>. He chose to focus on roasting batches with fresher, higher quality seeds than was the norm at the time. He was a trainer and supplier to the founders of Starbuck's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTMarshall2007_179-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTMarshall2007-179">[179]</a></sup>
</p><p>The American coffeehouse chain <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>, which began as a modest business roasting and selling coffee beans in 1971, was founded by three college students, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Baldwin" title="Jerry Baldwin">Jerry Baldwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Bowker" title="Gordon Bowker">Gordon Bowker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zev_Siegl" title="Zev Siegl">Zev Siegl</a>. The first store opened on March 30, 1971 at the <a href="/wiki/Pike_Place_Market" title="Pike Place Market">Pike Place Market</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, followed by a second and third over the next two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast2523_180-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast2523-180">[180]</a></sup> Entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Howard_Schultz" title="Howard Schultz">Howard Schultz</a> joined the company in 1982 as Director of Retail Operations and Marketing, and pushed to sell premade espresso coffee. The others were reluctant, but Schultz opened Il Giornale in Seattle in April 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast301_181-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast301-181">[181]</a></sup> He bought the other owners out in March 1987 and pushed on with plans to expand—from 1987 to the end of 1991, the chain (rebranded from Il Giornale to Starbucks) expanded to over 100 outlets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast302_182-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast302-182">[182]</a></sup> The company has 25,000 stores in over 75 countries worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup>
</p><p>South Korea experienced almost 900 percent growth in the number of coffee shops in the country between 2006 and 2011. The capital city <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> now has the highest concentration of coffee shops in the world, with more than 10,000 cafes and coffeehouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asia_Today_184-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asia_Today-184">[184]</a></sup>
</p><p>A contemporary term for a person who makes coffee beverages, often a coffeehouse employee, is a <i><a href="/wiki/Barista" title="Barista">barista</a></i>. The Specialty Coffee Association of Europe and the Specialty Coffee Association of America have been influential in setting standards and providing training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coffee_culture" title="Coffee culture">Coffee culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1153" data-file-width="1455" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg/220px-Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg/330px-Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg/440px-Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner_-_Davoser_Cafe_-_1928.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Davoser Café</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner" title="Ernst Ludwig Kirchner">Ernst Ludwig Kirchner</a>, 1928</div></div></div>
<p>Coffee is often consumed alongside (or instead of) breakfast by many at home or when eating out at <a href="/wiki/Diner" title="Diner">diners</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria" title="Cafeteria">cafeterias</a>. It is often served at the end of a formal meal, normally with a dessert, and at times with an after-dinner mint, especially when consumed at a restaurant or dinner party.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Break">Break</span></h3>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_break" title="Coffee break">coffee break</a> in the United States and elsewhere is a short mid-morning rest period granted to employees in <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> and industry, corresponding with the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> terms "<a href="/wiki/Elevenses" title="Elevenses">elevenses</a>", "smoko" (in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>), "morning tea", "tea break", or even just "tea". An afternoon coffee break, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afternoon_tea" title="Afternoon tea">afternoon tea</a>, often occurs as well.
</p><p>The coffee break originated in the late 19th century in <a href="/wiki/Stoughton,_Wisconsin" title="Stoughton, Wisconsin">Stoughton, Wisconsin</a>, with the wives of Norwegian immigrants. The city celebrates this every year with the Stoughton Coffee Break Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> In 1951, <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i></a> noted that "[s]ince the war, the coffee break has been written into union contracts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup> The term subsequently became popular through a Pan-American Coffee Bureau ad campaign of 1952 which urged consumers, "Give yourself a Coffee-Break – and Get What Coffee Gives to You."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_B._Watson" title="John B. Watson">John B. Watson</a>, a behavioral psychologist who worked with <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_House" title="Maxwell House">Maxwell House</a> later in his career, helped to popularize coffee breaks within the American culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup> Coffee breaks usually last from 10 to 20 minutes and frequently occur at the end of the first third of the work shift. In some companies and some <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">civil service</a>, the coffee break may be observed formally at a set hour. In some places, a cart with hot and cold beverages and <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">breads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pastry" title="Pastry">pastries</a> arrives at the same time morning and afternoon, an employer may contract with an outside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterer" title="Caterer">caterer</a> for daily service, or coffee breaks may take place away from the actual work-area in a designated <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria" title="Cafeteria">cafeteria</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tea_room" title="Tea room">tea room</a>. More generally, the phrase "coffee break" has also come to denote any break from work.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prohibition">Prohibition</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2518" data-file-width="1576" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg/170px-John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg/255px-John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg/340px-John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Frederick_Lewis_004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Coffee Bearer</i>, <a href="/wiki/Orientalism" title="Orientalism">Orientalist</a> painting by <a href="/wiki/John_Frederick_Lewis" title="John Frederick Lewis">John Frederick Lewis</a> (1857)</div></div></div>
<p>Coffee was initially used for spiritual reasons. At least 1,100 years ago, traders brought coffee across the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> into Arabia (modern-day Yemen), where Muslim <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dervishes" title="Dervishes">dervishes</a> began cultivating the shrub in their gardens. At first, the Arabians made wine from the pulp of the fermented coffee berries. This beverage was known as <i><a href="/wiki/Qishr" title="Qishr">qishr</a></i> (<i>kisher</i> in modern usage) and was used during religious ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast5_190-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast5-190">[190]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coffee drinking was prohibited by jurists and scholars (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulema" title="Ulema">ulema</a></i>) meeting in Mecca in 1511 as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haraam" title="Haraam">haraam</a></i>, but the subject of whether it was intoxicating was hotly debated over the next 30 years until the ban was finally overturned in the mid-16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup> Use in religious rites among the <a href="/wiki/Sufism" title="Sufism">Sufi</a> branch of Islam led to coffee's being put on trial in Mecca: it was accused of being a heretical substance, and its production and consumption were briefly repressed. It was later prohibited in Ottoman Turkey under an edict by the <a href="/wiki/Murad_IV" title="Murad IV">Sultan Murad IV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins2006_192-0"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins2006-192">[192]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coffee, regarded as a Muslim drink, was prohibited by <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_in_Africa#Ethiopia" title="Coptic Orthodox Church in Africa">Ethiopian Orthodox Christians</a> until as late as 1889; it is now considered a national drink of Ethiopia for people of all faiths. Its early association in Europe with rebellious political activities led to <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> outlawing coffeehouses from January 1676 (although the uproar created forced the monarch to back down two days before the ban was due to come into force).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast13_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast13-29">[29]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" title="Frederick II of Prussia">Frederick the Great</a> banned it in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> in 1777 for nationalistic and economic reasons; concerned about the price of import, he sought to force the public back to consuming beer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast11_193-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast11-193">[193]</a></sup> Lacking coffee-producing colonies, Prussia had to import all its coffee at a great cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup>
</p><p>A contemporary example of religious prohibition of coffee can be found in <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coffeefacts_195-0"><a href="#cite_note-coffeefacts-195">[195]</a></sup> The organization holds that it is both physically and spiritually unhealthy to consume coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beliefnet-mormons_196-0"><a href="#cite_note-beliefnet-mormons-196">[196]</a></sup> This comes from the Mormon doctrine of health, given in 1833 by founder <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a> in a revelation called the <a href="/wiki/Word_of_Wisdom" title="Word of Wisdom">Word of Wisdom</a>. It does not identify coffee by name, but includes the statement that "hot drinks are not for the belly," which has been interpreted to forbid both coffee and tea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beliefnet-mormons_196-1"><a href="#cite_note-beliefnet-mormons-196">[196]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quite a number of members of the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a> also avoid caffeinated drinks. In its teachings, the Church encourages members to avoid tea, coffee, and other stimulants. Abstinence from coffee, tobacco, and alcohol by many Adventists has afforded a near-unique opportunity for studies to be conducted within that population group on the health effects of coffee drinking, free from confounding factors. One study was able to show a weak but <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">statistically significant</a> association between coffee consumption and mortality from ischemic heart disease, other <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, all cardiovascular diseases combined, and all causes of death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup>
</p><p>For a time, there had been controversy in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> community over whether the coffee seed was a <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> and therefore prohibited for <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a>. Upon petition from coffeemaker <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_House" title="Maxwell House">Maxwell House</a>, the coffee seed was classified in 1923 as a berry rather than a seed by orthodox Jewish rabbi Hersch Kohn, and therefore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosher" title="Kosher">kosher</a> for Passover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fair_trade">Fair trade</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade_coffee" title="Fair trade coffee">Fair trade coffee</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade_debate" title="Fair trade debate">Fair trade debate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CXLT_Coffee.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2187" data-file-width="1560" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CXLT_Coffee.jpg/150px-CXLT_Coffee.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CXLT_Coffee.jpg/225px-CXLT_Coffee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CXLT_Coffee.jpg/300px-CXLT_Coffee.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CXLT_Coffee.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Small-sized bag of coffee beans</div></div></div>
<p>The concept of <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a> labeling, which guarantees coffee growers a negotiated preharvest price, began in the late 1980s with the <a href="/wiki/Stichting_Max_Havelaar" title="Stichting Max Havelaar">Max Havelaar Foundation's</a> labeling program in the Netherlands. In 2004, 24,222 metric tons (of 7,050,000 produced worldwide) were fair trade; in 2005, 33,991 metric tons out of 6,685,000 were fair trade, an increase from 0.34% to 0.51%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup>
A number of <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade_impact_studies" title="Fair trade impact studies">fair trade impact studies</a> have shown that fair trade coffee produces a mixed impact on the communities that grow it. Many studies are skeptical about fair trade, reporting that it often worsens the bargaining power of those who are not part of it. Coffee was incorporated into the fair-trade movement in 1988, when the <a href="/wiki/Max_Havelaar" title="Max Havelaar">Max Havelaar</a> mark was introduced in the Netherlands. The very first fair-trade coffee was an effort to import a Guatemalan coffee into Europe as "Indio Solidarity Coffee".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201"><a href="#cite_note-201">[201]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the founding of organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Fair_Trade_Association" title="European Fair Trade Association">European Fair Trade Association</a> (1987), the production and consumption of fair trade coffee has grown as some local and national coffee chains started to offer fair trade alternatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efta2009_202-0"><a href="#cite_note-efta2009-202">[202]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-203"><a href="#cite_note-203">[203]</a></sup> For example, in April 2000, after a year-long campaign by the human rights organization <a href="/wiki/Global_Exchange" title="Global Exchange">Global Exchange</a>, Starbucks decided to carry fair-trade coffee in its stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelsmacker2005_204-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelsmacker2005-204">[204]</a></sup> Since September 2009 all Starbucks Espresso beverages in UK and Ireland are made with Fairtrade and Shared Planet certified coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sbft_205-0"><a href="#cite_note-sbft-205">[205]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2005 study done in Belgium concluded that consumers' buying behavior is not consistent with their positive attitude toward ethical products. On average 46% of European consumers claimed to be willing to pay substantially more for ethical products, including fair-trade products such as coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelsmacker2005_204-1"><a href="#cite_note-pelsmacker2005-204">[204]</a></sup> The study found that the majority of respondents were unwilling to pay the actual price premium of 27% for fair trade coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelsmacker2005_204-2"><a href="#cite_note-pelsmacker2005-204">[204]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore_and_culture">Folklore and culture</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oromo_people" title="Oromo people">Oromo people</a> would customarily plant a coffee tree on the graves of powerful sorcerers. They believed that the first coffee bush sprang up from the tears that the god of heaven shed over the corpse of a dead sorcerer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> was inspired to compose the humorous <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schweigt_stille,_plaudert_nicht" title="Schweigt stille, plaudert nicht">Coffee Cantata</a></i>, about <a href="/wiki/Caffeine_dependence" title="Caffeine dependence">dependence on the beverage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pendergrast10_207-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pendergrast10-207">[207]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impacts">Economic impacts</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_coffee_production" title="List of countries by coffee production">List of countries by coffee production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="703" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg/280px-Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg/420px-Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg/560px-Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brazil_Coffee_production_regions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of coffee areas in Brazil</div></div></div>
<p>Market volatility, and thus increased returns, during 1830 encouraged Brazilian entrepreneurs to shift their attention from gold to coffee, a crop hitherto reserved for local consumption. Concurrent with this shift was the commissioning of vital infrastructures, including approximately 7,000 km of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroads</a> between 1860 and 1885. The creation of these railways enabled the importation of workers, in order to meet the enormous need for labor. This development primarily affected the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Rio_de_Janeiro" title="State of Rio de Janeiro">State of Rio de Janeiro</a>, as well as the Southern States of Brazil, most notably <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, due to its favourable climate, soils, and terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-208"><a href="#cite_note-208">[208]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Brazil" title="Coffee production in Brazil">Coffee production</a> attracted <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Brazil" title="Immigration to Brazil">immigrants</a> in search of better economic opportunities in the early 1900s. Mainly, these were Portuguese, Italian, Spanish, German, and Japanese nationals. For instance, São Paulo received approximately 733,000 immigrants in the decade preceding 1900, whilst only receiving approximately 201,000 immigrants in the six years to 1890. The production yield of coffee increases. In 1880, São Paulo produced 1.2 million bags (25% of total production), in 1888 2.6 million (40%), in 1902 8 million bags (60%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bresileco1_209-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bresileco1-209">[209]</a></sup> Coffee is then 63% of the country's exports. The gains made by this trade allow sustained economic growth in the country.
</p><p>The four years between planting a coffee and the first harvest extends seasonal variations in the price of coffee. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Government" title="Brazilian Government">Brazilian Government</a> is thus forced, to some extent, to keep strong price subsidies during production periods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span></h3>
<p>Coffee competitions take place across the globe with people at the regional competing to achieve national titles and then compete on the international stage. World Coffee Events holds the largest of such events moving the location of the final competition each year. The competition includes the following events: Barista Championship, Brewers Cup, Latte Art and Cup Tasters.
A <a href="/wiki/World_Brewer%27s_Cup" title="World Brewer's Cup">World Brewer's Cup</a> Championship takes place in Melbourne, Australia, every year that houses contestants from around the world<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-210"><a href="#cite_note-210">[210]</a></sup> to crown the World's Coffee King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-211"><a href="#cite_note-211">[211]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-212"><a href="#cite_note-212">[212]</a></sup>
</p>
