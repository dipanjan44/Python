<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oxocarbon</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>oxocarbon</b> or <b>oxide of carbon</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> consisting only of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The simplest and most common oxocarbons are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) with IUPAC names carbon(II) oxide and carbon(IV) oxide respectively. Many other stable (practically if not thermodynamically) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> oxides of carbon are known, but they are rarely encountered, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_suboxide" title="Carbon suboxide">carbon suboxide</a> (C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=C=O) and <a href="/wiki/Mellitic_anhydride" title="Mellitic anhydride">mellitic anhydride</a> (C<sub>12</sub>O<sub>9</sub>).
</p>

<p>While textbooks will often list only the first three, and rarely the fourth, a large number of other oxides are known today, most of them synthesized since the 1960s. Some of these new oxides are stable at room temperature. Some are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> or stable only at very low temperatures, but decompose to simpler oxocarbons when warmed.  Many are inherently unstable and can be observed only momentarily as intermediates in chemical reactions or are so reactive that they can exist only in the gas phase or under <a href="/wiki/Matrix_isolation" title="Matrix isolation">matrix isolation</a> conditions.
</p><p>The inventory of oxocarbons appears to be steadily growing. The existence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphene_oxide" title="Graphene oxide">graphene oxide</a> and of other stable <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> carbon oxides with unbounded molecular structures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hummers_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hummers-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snow_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-snow-4">[4]</a></sup> suggests that many more remain to be discovered.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) occurs widely in nature, and was incidentally manufactured by humans since pre-historical times, by the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of carbon-containing substances and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> of foods such as <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a>. It was gradually  recognized as a chemical substance, formerly called <i>spiritus sylvestris</i> ("forest spirit") or "fixed air", by various chemists in the 17th and 18th centuries.
</p><p>Carbon monoxide may occur in combustion, too, and was used (though not recognized) since antiquity for the <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> from its <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>. Like the dioxide, it was described and studied in the West by various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alchemist" title="Alchemist">alchemists</a> and chemists since the Middle Ages.  Its true composition was discovered by <a href="/wiki/William_Cruickshank_(chemist)" title="William Cruickshank (chemist)">William Cruikshank</a> in 1800.
</p><p>Carbon suboxide was discovered by Brodie in 1873, by passing electric current through carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brodie1873_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-brodie1873-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The fourth "classical" oxide, <a href="/wiki/Mellitic_anhydride" title="Mellitic anhydride">mellitic anhydride</a> (C<sub>12</sub>O<sub>9</sub>), was apparently obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justus_Liebig" title="Justus Liebig">Liebig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Wöhler</a> in 1830 in their study of <a href="/wiki/Mellite" title="Mellite">mellite ("honeystone")</a>, but was characterized only in 1913, by Meyer and Steiner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brodie also discovered in 1859 a fifth compound called <a href="/wiki/Graphite_oxide" title="Graphite oxide">graphite oxide</a>, consisting of carbon and oxygen in ratios varying between 2:1 and 3:1; but the nature and molecular structure of this substance remained unknown until a few years ago, when it was renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphene_oxide" title="Graphene oxide">graphene oxide</a> and became a topic of research in <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hummers_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-hummers-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Notable examples of unstable or metastable oxides that were detected only in extreme situations are <a href="/wiki/Dicarbon_monoxide" title="Dicarbon monoxide">dicarbon monoxide</a> radical (:C=C=O), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_trioxide" title="Carbon trioxide">carbon trioxide</a> (CO<sub>3</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetroxide" title="Carbon tetroxide">carbon tetroxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamieson1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamieson1-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_pentoxide" title="Carbon pentoxide">carbon pentoxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamieson2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamieson2-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_hexoxide" title="Carbon hexoxide">carbon hexoxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jamieson3_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jamieson3-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,2-dioxetanedione" title="1,2-dioxetanedione">1,2-dioxetanedione</a> (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some of these reactive carbon oxides were detected within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_clouds" title="Molecular clouds">molecular clouds</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> by <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">rotational spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pickett_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-pickett-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many hypothetical oxocarbons have been studied by theoretical methods but have yet to be detected. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_anhydride" title="Oxalic anhydride">oxalic anhydride</a> (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> or O=(C<sub>2</sub>O)=O), <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_dione" title="Ethylene dione">ethylene dione</a> (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=O)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schroder1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-schroder1-17">[17]</a></sup>  and other linear or cyclic polymers of carbon monoxide (-CO-)<sub><i>n</i></sub> (<a href="/wiki/Polyketone" title="Polyketone">polyketones</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiao_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jiao-18">[18]</a></sup> and linear or cyclic polymers of carbon dioxide (-CO<sub>2</sub>-)<sub><i>n</i></sub>, such as the dimer <a href="/wiki/1,3-Dioxetanedione" title="1,3-Dioxetanedione">1,3-dioxetanedione</a> (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewars_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewars-19">[19]</a></sup> and the trimer <a href="/wiki/1,3,5-Trioxanetrione" title="1,3,5-Trioxanetrione">1,3,5-trioxanetrione</a> (C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>6</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewars_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-lewars-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shirel_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-shirel-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_structure">General structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Normally, carbon is <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">tetravalent</a>, while oxygen is <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">divalent</a>, and in most oxocarbons (as in most other carbon compounds) each carbon atom may be <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">bound</a> to four other atoms, while oxygen may be bound to at most two.  Moreover, while carbon can connect to other carbons to form arbitrarily large chains or networks, chains of three or more oxygens are rarely if ever observed.  Thus the known electrically neutral oxocarbons generally consist of one or more carbon skeletons (including <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">cyclic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_compound" title="Aromatic compound">aromatic</a> structures) connected and terminated by oxide (-O-, =O) or peroxide (-O-O-) groups.
</p><p>Carbon atoms with unsatisfied bonds are found in some oxides, such as the diradical C<sub>2</sub>O or :C=C=O; but these compounds are generally too reactive to be isolated in bulk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brinker_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-brinker-21">[21]</a></sup>  Loss or gain of electrons can result in monovalent negative oxygen (-<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>), trivalent positive oxygen (≡<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>), or trivalent negative carbon (≡<span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>). The last two are found in carbon monoxide, <sup>−</sup>C≡O<sup>+</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nigg_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nigg-22">[22]</a></sup>  Negative oxygen occurs in most <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon_anion" title="Oxocarbon anion">oxocarbon anions</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_carbon_dioxides">Linear carbon dioxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linear carbon dioxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One family of carbon oxides has the general formula C<sub>n</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, or O=(C=)<sub><i>n</i></sub>O — namely, a linear chain of carbon atoms, capped by oxygen atoms at both ends.  The first members are
</p>
<ul><li>CO<sub>2</sub> or O=C=O, the well-known <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.</li>
<li>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=O, the extremely unstable <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_dione" title="Ethylene dione">ethylene dione</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schroder1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-schroder1-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=C=O, the metastable <a href="/wiki/Carbon_suboxide" title="Carbon suboxide">carbon suboxide</a> or tricarbon dioxide.</li>
<li>C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=C=C=O, <a href="/wiki/Tetracarbon_dioxide" title="Tetracarbon dioxide">tetracarbon dioxide</a> or 1,2,3-Butatriene-1,4-dione<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>C<sub>5</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=C=C=C=O, <a href="/wiki/Pentacarbon_dioxide" title="Pentacarbon dioxide">pentacarbon dioxide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> stable in solution at room temp. and pure up to −90 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feastw_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-feastw-25">[25]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Some higher members of this family have been detected in trace amounts in low-pressure gas phase and/or cryogenic matrix experiments, specifically for <i>n</i> = 7<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feastw_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-feastw-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p.97</span></sup> and <i>n</i> = 17, 19, and 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p.95</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_carbon_monoxides">Linear carbon monoxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linear carbon monoxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another family of oxocarbons are the linear carbon monoxides C<sub><i>n</i></sub>O.  The first member, ordinary carbon monoxide CO, seems to be the only one that is practically stable in the pure state at room temperature (though it is not thermodynamically stable at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Boudouard_reaction" title="Boudouard reaction">Boudouard reaction</a>).  Photolysis of the linear carbon dioxides in a cryogenic matrix leads to loss of CO, resulting in detectable amounts of even-numbered monoxides such as C<sub>2</sub>O,  C<sub>4</sub>O,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brinker_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-brinker-21">[21]</a></sup> and C<sub>6</sub>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feastw_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-feastw-25">[25]</a></sup>  The members up to <i>n</i>=9 have also been obtained by electrical discharge on gaseous C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> diluted in argon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogata1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ogata1-27">[27]</a></sup> The first three members have been detected in interstellar space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogata1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ogata1-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>When <i>n</i> is even, the molecules are believed to be in the <a href="/wiki/Triplet_state" title="Triplet state">triplet</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cumulene" title="Cumulene">cumulene</a>-like) state, with the atoms connected by double bonds and an unfilled orbital in the first carbon — as in :C=C=O, :C=C=C=C=O, and, in general, :(C=)<sub><i>n</i></sub>=O.  When <i>n</i> is odd, the triplet structure is believed to <a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">resonate</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Singlet_state" title="Singlet state">singlet</a> (<a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>-type) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_molecule" title="Polar molecule">polar</a> state with a negative charge on the carbon end and a positive one on the oxygen end, as in <sup>−</sup>C≡C-C≡O<sup>+</sup>, <sup>−</sup>C≡C-C≡C-C≡O<sup>+</sup>, and, in general, <sup>−</sup>(C≡C-)<sub>(<i>n</i>-1)/2</sub>C≡O<sup>+</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogata1_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-ogata1-27">[27]</a></sup>  Carbon monoxide itself follows this pattern: its predominant form is believed to be <sup>−</sup>C≡O<sup>+</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nigg_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nigg-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radialene-type_cyclic_polyketones">Radialene-type cyclic polyketones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radialene-type cyclic polyketones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another family of oxocarbons that has attracted special attention are the cyclic <a href="/wiki/Radialene" title="Radialene">radialene</a>-type oxocarbons C<sub><i>n</i></sub>O<sub><i>n</i></sub> or (CO)<sub><i>n</i></sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seitz_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-seitz-28">[28]</a></sup> They can be regarded as cyclic polymers of carbon monoxide, or <i>n</i>-fold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keton" title="Keton">ketones</a> of <i>n</i>-carbon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycloalkanes" title="Cycloalkanes">cycloalkanes</a>. Carbon monoxide itself (CO) can be regarded as the first member. Theoretical studies indicate that ethylene dione (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> or O=C=C=O) and <a href="/wiki/Cyclopropanetrione" title="Cyclopropanetrione">cyclopropanetrione</a> C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>3</sub> do not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schroder1_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-schroder1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiao_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jiao-18">[18]</a></sup> The next three members — <a href="/wiki/Cyclobutanetetrone" title="Cyclobutanetetrone">C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentanepentone" title="Cyclopentanepentone">C<sub>5</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanehexone" title="Cyclohexanehexone">C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub></a> — are theoretically possible, but are expected to be quite unstable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiao_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-jiao-18">[18]</a></sup> and so far they have been synthesized only in trace amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wyja_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-wyja-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On the other hand, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anions" title="Anions">anions</a> of these oxocarbons are quite stable, and some of them have been known since the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seitz_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-seitz-28">[28]</a></sup> They are

<li>C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Squaric_acid" title="Squaric acid">squarate</a> (Cohen and others, 1959),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>

<li>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub><sup>2−</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Rhodizonic_acid" title="Rhodizonic acid">rhodizonate</a> (Heller, 1837).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The cyclic oxide C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> also forms the stable anions of <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone" title="Tetrahydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone">tetrahydroxy-1,4-benzoquinone</a> (C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Benzenehexol" title="Benzenehexol">benzenehexol</a> (C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub><sup>6−</sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chen_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-chen-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromaticity</a> of these anions has been studied using theoretical methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_oxides">New oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many new stable or metastable oxides have been synthesized since the 1960s, such as:
</p>
<ul><li>C<sub>10</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Benzoquinonetetracarboxylic_dianhydride" title="Benzoquinonetetracarboxylic dianhydride">benzoquinonetetracarboxylic dianhydride</a> (Hammond, 1963).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamm_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamm-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Ethylenetetracarboxylic_dianhydride" title="Ethylenetetracarboxylic dianhydride">ethylenetetracarboxylic dianhydride</a>, a stable isomer of cyclohexanehexone (Sauer and others, 1967).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sauer1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-sauer1-42">[42]</a></sup></li>

<p>Many relatives of these oxides have been investigated theoretically, and some are expected to be stable, such as other carbonate and oxalate esters of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahydroxy-1,2-benzoquinone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tetrahydroxy-1,2-benzoquinone (page does not exist)">tetrahydroxy-1,2-benzoquinone</a> and of the rhodizonic, croconic, squaric, and deltic acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jiao_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-jiao-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymeric_carbon_oxides">Polymeric carbon oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Polymeric carbon oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon suboxide spontaneously polymerizes at room temperature into a carbon-oxygen <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>, with 3:2 carbon:oxygen atomic ratio. The polymer is believed to be a linear chain of fused six-membered <a href="/wiki/Lactone" title="Lactone">lactone</a> rings, with a continuous carbon backbone of alternating single and double bonds. Physical measurements indicate that the mean number of units per molecule is about 5–6, depending on the formation temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snow_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-snow-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kibett_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-kibett-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Carbon monoxide compressed to 5 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">GPa</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cell</a> yields a somewhat similar <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonyl" title="Polycarbonyl">reddish polymer</a> with a slightly higher oxygen content, which is metastable at room conditions. It is believed that CO <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionates</a> in the cell to a mixture of CO<sub>2</sub> and C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>; the latter forms a polymer similar to the one described above (but with a more irregular structure), that traps some of the CO<sub>2</sub> in its matrix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-katz_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-katz-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evans_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-evans-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another carbon-oxygen polymer, with C:O ratio 5:1 or higher, is the classical graphite oxide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hummers_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-hummers-3">[3]</a></sup> and its single-sheet version <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphene_oxide" title="Graphene oxide">graphene oxide</a>.
</p>

<li>C<sub>120</sub>O</li>
<li>C<sub>120</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (4 isomers)</li>
<li>C<sub>70</sub>O</li>
<li>C<sub>140</sub>O</li></ul>
<p>and others.
