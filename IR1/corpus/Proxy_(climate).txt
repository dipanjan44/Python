<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Proxy (climate)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about climatic patterns. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Proxy_(disambiguation)" title="Proxy (disambiguation)">Proxy</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Preserved physical characteristics allowing reconstruction of past climatic conditions</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="1449" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/300px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/450px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/600px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of global temperature over the past 2000 years, comparing results from researchers using different proxy methods</div></div></div>
<p>In the study of past climates ("<a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>climate proxies</b> are preserved physical characteristics of the past that stand in for direct meteorological measurements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and enable scientists to reconstruct the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic conditions</a> over a longer fraction of the Earth's history. Reliable global records of climate only began in the 1880s, and proxies provide the only means for scientists to determine climatic patterns before record-keeping began.
</p><p>Examples of proxies include <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_rings" title="Tree rings">tree rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">sub-fossil pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">boreholes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">lake and ocean sediments</a>, and carbonate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speleothems" title="Speleothems">speleothems</a>. The character of deposition or rate of growth of the proxies' material has been influenced by the climatic conditions of the time in which they were laid down or grew. Chemical traces produced by climatic changes, such as quantities of particular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopes" title="Isotopes">isotopes</a>, can be recovered from proxies. Some proxies, such as gas bubbles trapped in ice, enable traces of the ancient atmosphere to be recovered and measured directly to provide a history of fluctuations in the composition of the Earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To produce the most precise results, systematic cross-verification between proxy indicators is necessary for accuracy in readings and record-keeping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Proxies can be combined to produce temperature reconstructions longer than the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental temperature record</a> and can inform discussions of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and climate history. The distribution of proxy records, just like the instrumental record, is not at all uniform, with more records in the northern hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unisci.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-unisci.com-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proxies">Proxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(statistics)" title="Proxy (statistics)">Proxy (statistics)</a></div>
<p>In science, it is sometimes necessary to study a variable which cannot be measured directly.  This can be done by "proxy methods," in which a variable which correlates with the variable of interest is measured, and then used to infer the value of the variable of interest.  Proxy methods are of particular use in the study of the past climate, beyond times when direct measurements of temperatures are available.
</p><p>Most proxy records have to be calibrated against independent temperature measurements, or against a directly calibrated proxy, during their period of overlap to estimate the relationship between temperature and the proxy. The longer history of the proxy is then used to reconstruct temperature from earlier periods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_cores">Ice cores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ice cores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drilling">Drilling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drilling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:103px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Icecore_4x.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="898" data-file-width="101" height="898" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/29/Icecore_4x.jpg" width="101"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Icecore_4x.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ice Core sample taken from drill. Photo by <a href="/wiki/Lonnie_Thompson" title="Lonnie Thompson">Lonnie Thompson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byrd_Polar_Research_Center" title="Byrd Polar Research Center">Byrd Polar Research Center</a>.</div></div></div> <p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_cores" title="Ice cores">Ice cores</a> are <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">cylindrical samples</a> from within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sheets" title="Ice sheets">ice sheets</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American" title="North American">North American</a> regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strom,_Robert_p._255_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strom,_Robert_p._255-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Core_Location_Maps._7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Core_Location_Maps.-7">[7]</a></sup> First attempts of extraction occurred in 1956 as part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>. As original means of extraction, the <a href="/wiki/Cold_Regions_Research_and_Engineering_Laboratory" title="Cold Regions Research and Engineering Laboratory">U.S. Army’s Cold Regions Research and Engineering Laboratory</a> used an 80-foot (24 m)-long modified electrodrill in 1968 at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Century" title="Camp Century">Camp Century</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Byrd_Station" title="Byrd Station">Byrd Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Their machinery could drill through 15–20 feet of <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice</a> in 40–50 minutes. From 1300 to 3,000 feet (910 m) in depth, <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">core samples</a> were 4 ¼ inches in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a> and 10 to 20 feet (6.1 m) long. Deeper <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">samples</a> of 15 to 20 feet (6.1 m) long were not uncommon. Every subsequent <a href="/wiki/Drilling" title="Drilling">drilling</a> team improves their method with each new effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Proxy">Proxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="464" height="106" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/74/Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg/220px-Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/74/Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg/330px-Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/74/Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg/440px-Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_deltaD_deltaO18.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>δ18O<sub>air</sub> and δD<sub>ice</sub> for Vostok, Antarctica ice core.</div></div></div>
<p>The ratio between the <sup>16</sup>O and <sup>18</sup>O <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water#Heavy_water_and_isotopologues" title="Properties of water">water molecule isotopologues</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> helps determine <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">past temperatures</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)#Measurement" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">snow accumulations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strom,_Robert_p._255_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strom,_Robert_p._255-6">[6]</a></sup> The heavier <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> (<sup>18</sup>O) condenses more readily as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperatures" title="Temperatures">temperatures</a> decrease and falls more easily as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, while the lighter isotope (<sup>16</sup>O) needs colder conditions to precipitate. The farther <a href="/wiki/North" title="North">north</a> one needs to go to find elevated levels of the <sup>18</sup>O isotopologue, the warmer the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to oxygen isotopes, water contains hydrogen isotopes - <sup>1</sup>H and <sup>2</sup>H, usually referred to as H and D (for <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>) - that are also used for temperature proxies. Normally, ice cores from Greenland are analyzed for δ<sup>18</sup>O and those from Antarctica for δ-deuterium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=" (November 2011)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> Those cores that analyze for both show a lack of agreement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (In the figure, δ<sup>18</sup>O is for the trapped air, not the ice. δD is for the ice.)
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_bubble" title="Air bubble">Air bubbles</a> in the ice, which contain trapped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, are also helpful in determining past climate changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strom,_Robert_p._255_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strom,_Robert_p._255-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1989-1992, the European Greenland Ice Core Drilling Project drilled in central <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> at coordinates 72° 35' N, 37° 38' W.  The ices in that core were 3840 years old at a depth of 770 m, 40,000 years old at 2521 m, and 200,000 years old or more at 3029 m <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_cores" title="Ice cores">Ice cores</a> in Antarctica can reveal the climate records for the past 650,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strom,_Robert_p._255_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strom,_Robert_p._255-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Location <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a> and a complete list of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> drilling sites can be found on the website for the <a href="/wiki/National_Ice_Core_Laboratory" title="National Ice Core Laboratory">National Ice Core Laboratory</a>: <a class="external free" href="http://icecores.org/" rel="nofollow">http://icecores.org/</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Core_Location_Maps._7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Core_Location_Maps.-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_rings">Tree rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tree rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tree_rings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Tree_rings.jpg/220px-Tree_rings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Tree_rings.jpg/330px-Tree_rings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Tree_rings.jpg/440px-Tree_rings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tree_rings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tree rings seen in a cross section of a trunk of a tree.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dendroclimatology" title="Dendroclimatology">Dendroclimatology</a></div>
<p>Dendroclimatology is the science of determining past climates from trees, primarily from properties of the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_rings" title="Tree rings">tree rings</a>. Tree rings are wider when conditions favor growth, narrower when times are difficult. Other properties of the annual rings, such as maximum latewood density (MXD) have been shown to be better proxies than simple ring width. Using tree rings, scientists have estimated many local climates for hundreds to thousands of years previous. By combining multiple tree-ring studies (sometimes with other climate proxy records), scientists have estimated past regional and global climates (see <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record_of_the_past_1000_years" title="Temperature record of the past 1000 years">Temperature record of the past 1000 years</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_leaves">Fossil leaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fossil leaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New approaches retrieve data such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> content of past atmospheres from fossil leaf <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a> and isotope composition, measuring cellular CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations. A 2014 study was able to use the <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_ratio" title="Isotope ratio">isotope ratios</a> to estimate the CO<sub>2</sub> amounts of the past 400 million years, the findings hint at a higher <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a> to CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boreholes">Boreholes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Boreholes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">Borehole</a> temperatures are used as temperature proxies. Since heat transfer through the ground is slow, temperature measurements at a series of different depths down the borehole, adjusted for the effect of rising heat from inside the Earth, can be "<a href="/wiki/Invertible_matrix" title="Invertible matrix">inverted</a>" (a mathematical formula to solve matrix equations) to produce a non-unique series of surface temperature values. The solution is "non-unique" because there are multiple possible surface temperature reconstructions that can produce the same borehole temperature profile. In addition,  due to physical limitations, the reconstructions are inevitably "smeared", and become more smeared further back in time. When reconstructing temperatures around 1,500 AD, boreholes have a temporal resolution of a few centuries. At the start of the 20th Century, their resolution is a few decades; hence they do not provide a useful check on the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental temperature record</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p80_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p80-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pollack_et_al_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pollack_et_al-13">[13]</a></sup> However, they are broadly comparable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unisci.com_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-unisci.com-5">[5]</a></sup> These confirmations have given paleoclimatologists the confidence that they can measure the temperature of 500 years ago. This is concluded by a depth scale of about 492 feet (150 meters) to measure the temperatures from 100 years ago and 1,640 feet (500 meters) to measure the temperatures from 1,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.cnn.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-archives.cnn.com-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Boreholes have a great advantage over many other proxies in that no calibration is required: they are actual temperatures. However, they record surface temperature not the near-surface temperature (1.5 meter) used for most "surface" weather observations. These can differ substantially under extreme conditions or when there is surface snow. In practice the effect on borehole temperature is believed to be generally small. A second source of error is contamination of the well by groundwater may affect the temperatures, since the water "carries" more modern temperatures with it. This effect is believed to be generally small, and more applicable at very humid sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p80_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p80-12">[12]</a></sup> It does not apply in ice cores where the site remains frozen all year.
</p><p>More than 600 boreholes, on all continents, have been used as proxies for reconstructing surface temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pollack_et_al_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-pollack_et_al-13">[13]</a></sup> The highest concentration of boreholes exist in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Their depths of drilling typically range from 200 to greater than 1,000 meters into the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> of the Earth or ice sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archives.cnn.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-archives.cnn.com-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>A small number of boreholes have been drilled in the ice sheets; the purity of the ice there permits longer reconstructions. Central Greenland borehole temperatures show "a warming over the last 150 years of approximately 1°C ± 0.2°C preceded by a few centuries of cool conditions. Preceding this was a warm period centered around A.D. 1000, which was warmer than the late 20th century by approximately 1°C." A borehole in the Antarctica icecap shows that the "temperature at A.D. 1 [was] approximately 1°C warmer than the late 20th century".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p81_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boreholes_in_Glacial_Ice_p81-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Borehole temperatures in Greenland were responsible for an important revision to the isotopic temperature reconstruction, revealing that the former assumption that "spatial slope equals temporal slope" was incorrect.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corals">Corals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Corals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bleached_Coral.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bleached_Coral.png/220px-Bleached_Coral.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bleached_Coral.png/330px-Bleached_Coral.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Bleached_Coral.png/440px-Bleached_Coral.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bleached_Coral.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coral bleached due to changes in ocean water properties</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a> <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skeletal" title="Skeletal">skeletal</a> rings, or bands, also share paleoclimatological information, similarly to tree rings. In 2002, a report was published on the findings of Drs. Lisa Greer and Peter Swart, associates of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a> at the time, in regard to <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_oxygen" title="Isotopes of oxygen">stable oxygen isotopes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> of coral. Cooler temperatures tend to cause coral to use heavier isotopes in its structure, while warmer temperatures result in more normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotopes" title="Oxygen isotopes">oxygen isotopes</a> being built into the coral structure. Denser water <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> also tends to contain the heavier isotope. Greer's coral sample from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> was taken in 1994 and dated back to 1935. Greer recalls her conclusions, "When we look at the averaged annual data from 1935 to about 1994, we see it has the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sine wave</a>. It is periodic and has a significant pattern of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope" title="Oxygen isotope">oxygen isotope</a> composition that has a peak at about every twelve to fifteen years." <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">Surface water temperatures</a> have coincided by also peaking every twelve and a half years. However, since recording this temperature has only been practiced for the last fifty years, correlation between recorded water temperature and coral structure can only be drawn so far back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollen_grains">Pollen grains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pollen grains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pollen can be found in sediments. Plants produce <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> in large quantities and it is extremely resistant to decay. It is possible to identify a plant species from its pollen grain. The identified plant community of the area at the relative time from that sediment layer, will provide information about the climatic condition. The abundance of pollen of a given <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> period or year depends partly on the weather conditions of the previous months, hence pollen density provides information on short-term climatic conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The study of prehistoric pollen is <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dinoflagellate_cysts">Dinoflagellate cysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dinoflagellate cysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="778" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png/220px-Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png/330px-Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png/440px-Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peridinium_ovatum_cyst.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cyst of a dinoflagellate <i>Peridinium ovatum</i></div></div></div>
<p>Dinoflagellates occur in most aquatic environments and during their life cycle, some species produce highly resistant organic-walled <a href="/wiki/Dinocyst" title="Dinocyst">cysts</a> for a dormancy period when environmental conditions are not appropriate for growth. Their living depth is relatively shallow (dependent upon light penetration), and closely coupled to diatoms on which they feed. Their distribution patterns in surface waters are closely related to physical characteristics of the water bodies, and nearshore assemblages can also be distinguished from oceanic assemblages. The distribution of dinocysts in sediments has been relatively well documented and has contributed to understanding the average sea-surface conditions that determine the distribution pattern and abundances of the taxa (<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>). Several studies, including <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> have compiled box and gravity cores in the North Pacific analyzing them for palynological content to determine the distribution of dinocysts and their relationships with sea surface temperature, salinity, productivity and upwelling. Similarly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> use a box core at 576.5 m of water depth from 1992 in the central Santa Barbara Basin to determine oceanographic and climatic changes during the past 40 kyr in the area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_and_ocean_sediments">Lake and ocean sediments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lake and ocean sediments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to their study on other proxies, paleoclimatologists examine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotopes" title="Oxygen isotopes">oxygen isotopes</a> in the contents of ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediments" title="Sediments">sediments</a>. Likewise, they measure the layers of <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varve</a> (deposited fine and coarse silt or clay)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> laminating lake sediments. Lake varves are primarily influenced by:
</p>
<ul><li>Summer temperature, which shows the energy available to melt seasonal snow and ice</li>
<li>Winter snowfall, which determines the level of disturbance to sediments when melting occurs</li>
<li>Rainfall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">Diatoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiolaria" title="Radiolaria">radiolarians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ostracod" title="Ostracod">ostracods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">Coccolithophores</a> are examples of biotic proxies for lake and ocean conditions that are commonly used to reconstruct past climates. The distribution of the species of these and other aquatic creatures preserved in the sediments are useful proxies. The optimal conditions for species preserved in the sediment act as clues. Researchers use these clues to reveal what the climate and environment was like when the creatures died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The oxygen isotope ratios in their shells can also be used as proxies for temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_isotopes_and_temperature_reconstruction">Water isotopes and temperature reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Water isotopes and temperature reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_lipids">Membrane lipids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Membrane lipids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A novel climate proxy obtained from <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lignites" title="Lignites">lignites</a>, ancient peat) and soils, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Membrane_lipids" title="Membrane lipids">membrane lipids</a> known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glycerol_dialkyl_glycerol_tetraether&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraether (page does not exist)">glycerol dialkyl glycerol tetraether</a> (GDGT) is helping to study paleo environmental factors, which control relative distribution of differently branched GDGT <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">isomers</a>. The study authors note, "These branched membrane lipids are produced by an as yet unknown group of anaerobic soil bacteria."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> As of  2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there is a decade of research demonstrating that in mineral soils the degree of <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylation</a> of bacteria (brGDGTs), helps to calculate mean annual air temperatures. This proxy method was used to study the <a href="/wiki/Climate_across_Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary" title="Climate across Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary">climate of the early Palaeogene</a>, at the Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary, and researchers found that annual air temperatures, over land and at mid-latitude, averaged about 23–29 °C (± 4.7 °C), which is 5–10 °C higher than most previous findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudoproxies">Pseudoproxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proxy_(climate)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pseudoproxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The skill of algorithms used to combine proxy records into an overall hemispheric temperature reconstruction may be tested using a technique known as "<a href="/wiki/Pseudoproxy" title="Pseudoproxy">pseudoproxies</a>". In this method, output from a <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate model</a> is sampled at locations corresponding to the known proxy network, and the temperature record produced is compared to the (known) overall temperature of the model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
