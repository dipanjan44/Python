<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Waste</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Unwanted or unusable materials</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>

<p><b>Waste</b> (or <b>wastes</b>) are unwanted or unusable materials. Waste is any substance which is discarded after primary use, or is worthless, defective and of no use.
</p><p>Examples include <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">municipal solid waste</a> (household trash/refuse), <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>, which contains bodily wastes (<a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>), <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a>, and others.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Environment_Program">United Nations Environment Program</span></h3>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basel_Convention_on_the_Control_of_Transboundary_Movements_of_Hazardous_Wastes_and_Their_Disposal" title="Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal">Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal</a> of 1989, Art. 2(1), "'Wastes' are substance or objects, which are disposed of or are intended to be disposed of or are required to be disposed of by the provisions of national law".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basel_Convention_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basel_Convention-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:European_legal_definition_of_waste.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="654" data-file-width="809" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/European_legal_definition_of_waste.png/230px-European_legal_definition_of_waste.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/European_legal_definition_of_waste.png/345px-European_legal_definition_of_waste.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/European_legal_definition_of_waste.png/460px-European_legal_definition_of_waste.png 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:European_legal_definition_of_waste.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic illustration of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> Legal definition of waste<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Statistics_Division">United Nations Statistics Division</span></h3>
<p>The UNSD <i>Glossary of Environment Statistics</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNSD_Glossary_of_Environment_Statistics_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNSD_Glossary_of_Environment_Statistics-2">[2]</a></sup> describes waste as "materials that are not prime products (that is, products produced for the market) for which the generator has no further use in terms of his/her own purposes of production, transformation or consumption, and of which he/she wants to dispose. Wastes may be generated during the extraction of raw materials, the processing of raw materials into intermediate and final products, the consumption of final products, and other human activities. Residuals recycled or reused at the place of generation are excluded."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European  Union</span></h3>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Waste_framework_directive" title="Waste framework directive">Waste Framework Directive 2008/98/EC</a>, Art. 3(1), the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> defines waste as "an object the holder discards, intends to discard or is required to discard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  For a more structural description of the Waste Directive, see the <a class="external text" href="http://ec.europa.eu/environment/waste/legislation/a.htm" rel="nofollow">European Commission's summary</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_waste_types" title="List of waste types">List of waste types</a></div>
<p>There are many waste types defined by modern systems of <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a>, notably including:
</p>
<ul><li>Municipal waste includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Household_waste" title="Household waste">household waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_waste" title="Commercial waste">commercial waste</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Demolition_waste" title="Demolition waste">demolition waste</a></li>
<li>Hazardous waste includes <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biomedical_waste" title="Biomedical waste">Biomedical waste</a> includes clinical waste</li>
<li>Special <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a>, explosive waste, and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_waste" title="Electronic waste">electronic waste</a> (e-waste)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reporting">Reporting</span></h2>
<p>There are many issues that surround reporting waste. It is most commonly measured by size or weight, and there is a stark difference between the two. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_waste" title="Organic waste">organic waste</a> is much heavier when it is wet, and plastic or glass bottles can have different weights but be the same size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solid_Waste_Management_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solid_Waste_Management-4">[4]</a></sup> On a global scale it is difficult to report waste because countries have different definitions of waste and what falls into waste categories, as well as different ways of reporting. Based on incomplete reports from its parties, the <a href="/wiki/Basel_Convention" title="Basel Convention">Basel Convention</a> estimated 338 million tonnes of waste was generated in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Waste_Activities_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-International_Waste_Activities-5">[5]</a></sup> For the same year, OECD estimated 4 billion tonnes from its member countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Improving_Recycling_Markets_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Improving_Recycling_Markets-6">[6]</a></sup> Despite these inconsistencies, waste reporting is still useful on a small and large scale to determine key causes and locations, and to find ways of preventing, minimizing, recovering, treating, and disposing waste.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs">Costs</span></h2>
<p><span id="Cultural_dynamics_of_waste"></span>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_costs">Environmental costs</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="1024" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg/300px-Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg/450px-Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg/600px-Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sign._Mahabalipuram.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Waste not the Waste. Sign in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>, India</div></div></div>
<p>Inappropriately managed waste can attract rodents and insects, which  can harbour gastrointestinal parasites, yellow fever, worms, the plague and other conditions for humans, and exposure to hazardous wastes, particularly when they are burned, can cause various other diseases including cancers. <a href="/wiki/Toxic_waste" title="Toxic waste">Toxic waste</a> materials can contaminate surface water, groundwater, soil, and air which causes more problems for humans, other species, and ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diaz_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diaz-7">[7]</a></sup> Waste treatment and disposal produces significant green house gas (GHG) emissions, notably methane, which are contributing significantly to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Waste_Activities_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-International_Waste_Activities-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_costs">Social costs</span></h3>
<p>Waste management is a significant <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> issue. Many of the environmental burdens cited above are more often borne by marginalized groups, such as racial minorities, women, and residents of developing nations. <a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">NIMBY</a> (not in my back yard) is the opposition of residents to a proposal for a new development because it is close to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the need for expansion and siting of waste treatment and disposal facilities is increasing worldwide. There is now a growing market in the transboundary movement of waste, and although most waste that flows between countries goes between developed nations, a significant amount of waste is moved from developed to developing nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_costs">Economic costs</span></h3>
<p>The economic costs of managing waste are high, and are often paid for by municipal governments;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> money can often be saved with more efficiently designed collection routes, modifying vehicles, and with public education. Environmental policies such as <a href="/wiki/Pay_as_you_throw" title="Pay as you throw">pay as you throw</a> can reduce the cost of management and reduce waste quantities. Waste recovery (that is, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a>) can curb economic costs because it avoids extracting raw materials and often cuts transportation costs. "Economic assessment of municipal waste management systems – case studies using a combination of <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle assessment</a> (LCA) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_costing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Life-cycle costing (page does not exist)">life-cycle costing</a> (LCC)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The location of waste treatment and disposal facilities often reduces property values due to noise, dust, pollution, unsightliness, and negative stigma. The informal waste sector consists mostly of <a href="/wiki/Waste_picker" title="Waste picker">waste pickers</a> who scavenge for metals, glass, plastic, textiles, and other materials and then trade them for a profit. This sector can significantly alter or reduce waste in a particular system, but other negative economic effects come with the disease, poverty, exploitation, and abuse of its workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_recovery">Resource recovery</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resource_recovery" title="Resource recovery">Resource recovery</a></div>
<p>Resource recovery is the retrieval of recyclable waste, which was intended for disposal, for a specific next use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  It is the processing of recyclables to extract or recover materials and resources, or convert to energy.  This process is carried out at a resource recovery facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  Resource recovery is not only important to the environment, but it can be cost effective by decreasing the amount of waste sent to the disposal stream, reduce the amount of space needed for landfills, and protect limited natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_recovery">Energy recovery</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">Energy recovery</a> from waste is using non-recyclable waste materials and extracting from it heat, electricity, or energy through a variety of processes, including <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrolyzation" title="Pyrolyzation">pyrolyzation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup> This process is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">waste-to-energy</a>.
</p><p>There are several ways to recover energy from waste. Anaerobic digestion is a naturally occurring process of <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a> where organic matter is reduced to a simpler chemical component in the absence of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">Incineration</a> or direct controlled burning of municipal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_waste" title="Solid waste">solid waste</a> to reduce waste and make energy.  Secondary recovered fuel is the energy recovery from waste that cannot be reused or recycled from mechanical and biological treatment activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup>  Pyrolysis involves heating of waste, with the absence of oxygen, to high temperatures to break down any carbon content into a mixture of gaseous and liquid fuels and solid residue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup> Gasification is the conversion of carbon rich material through high temperature with partial oxidation into a gas stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_arc" title="Plasma arc">Plasma arc</a> heating is the very high heating of municipal solid waste to temperatures ranging from 3,000-10,000 °C, where energy is released by an electrical discharge in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inert_atmosphere" title="Inert atmosphere">inert atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using waste as fuel can offer important environmental benefits.  It can provide a safe and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-effective" title="Cost-effective">cost-effective</a> option for wastes that would normally have to be dealt with through disposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup> It can help reduce carbon dioxide emissions by diverting energy use from fossil fuels, while also generating energy and using waste as fuel can reduce the <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> generated in landfills by averting waste from landfills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGD_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-IGD-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is some debate in the classification of certain biomass feedstock as wastes. Crude Tall Oil (CTO), a co-product of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper" title="Pulp and paper">pulp and papermaking</a> process, is  defined as a waste or residue in some European countries when in fact it is produced “on purpose” and has significant value add potential in industrial applications. Several companies use CTO to produce fuel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> while the pine chemicals industry maximizes it as a feedstock “producing  low-carbon, bio-based chemicals” through cascading use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_awareness">Education and awareness</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">Education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a> in the area of waste and <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> is increasingly important from a global perspective of <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">resource management</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Talloires_Declaration" title="Talloires Declaration">Talloires Declaration</a> is a declaration for <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> concerned about the unprecedented scale and speed of environmental <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">degradation</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">depletion</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>. Local, regional, and global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> pollution; accumulation and distribution of toxic wastes; destruction and depletion of forests, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>; depletion of the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> and emission of "green house" gases threaten the survival of humans and thousands of other living species, the integrity of the earth and its <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, the security of nations, and the heritage of future generations. Several universities have implemented the Talloires Declaration by establishing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_management" title="Environmental management">environmental management</a> and waste management programs, e.g. the waste management university project. <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">University</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocational" title="Vocational">vocational</a> education are promoted by various organizations, e.g. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=WAMITAB&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="WAMITAB (page does not exist)">WAMITAB</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Institution_of_Wastes_Management" title="Chartered Institution of Wastes Management">Chartered Institution of Wastes Management</a>.
</p>
