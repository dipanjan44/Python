<p>Chemically, <b>black carbon</b> (<b>BC</b>) is a component of fine <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulate matter</a> (PM ≤ 2.5 µm in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_diameter" title="Aerodynamic diameter">aerodynamic diameter</a>). Black carbon consists of pure carbon in several linked forms.  It is formed through the incomplete combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, and is emitted in both <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> and naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anenberg,_''et_al.'',_2012_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anenberg,_''et_al.'',_2012-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Black carbon causes human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morbidity" title="Morbidity">morbidity</a> and premature mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anenberg,_''et_al.'',_2012_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anenberg,_''et_al.'',_2012-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, black carbon is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a> agent. Black carbon <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">warms the Earth</a> by absorbing sunlight and heating the atmosphere and by reducing <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> when deposited on snow and ice (direct effects) and indirectly by interaction with clouds, with the total forcing of 1.1 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Black carbon stays in the atmosphere for only several days to weeks, whereas <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_lifetime" title="Atmospheric lifetime">atmospheric lifetime</a> of more than 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>black carbon</i> is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_sciences" title="Soil sciences">soil sciences</a> and geology, referring either to deposited atmospheric black carbon or to directly incorporated black carbon from vegetation fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Especially in the tropics, black carbon in soils significantly contributes to fertility as it is able to absorb important plant nutrients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-essay-like" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is written like a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_a_publisher_of_original_thought" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">personal reflection, personal essay, or argumentative essay</a></b> that states a Wikipedia editor's personal feelings or presents an original argument about a topic.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit">help improve it</a> by rewriting it in an <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Information_style_and_tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">encyclopedic style</a>.</span> <small><i>(February 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The term black carbon was coined by <a href="/wiki/Tihomir_Novakov" title="Tihomir Novakov">Tihomir Novakov</a>, referred to as "the godfather of black carbon studies" by <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a>, in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Smoke or soot was the first pollutant to be recognized as having significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> yet one of the last to be studied by the contemporary atmospheric research community.  <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Faraday</a> recognized that soot was composed of carbon and that it was produced by the incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Soot is composed of a complex mixture of organic compounds which are weakly absorbing in the visible spectral region and a highly absorbing black component which is variously called “elemental”, “graphitic” or “black carbon”. The term elemental carbon has been used in conjunction with thermal and wet chemical determinations and the term  graphitic carbon suggests the presence  of <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>-like micro-crystalline structures in soot as evidenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raman_Spectroscopy" title="Raman Spectroscopy">Raman Spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  The term black carbon is used to imply that this soot component is primarily responsible for the absorption of visible light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-11">[11]</a></sup>  The term black carbon is used as a synonym for both the elemental and graphitic component of soot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It can be measured using different types of devices based on absorption or dispersion of a light beam or derived from noise measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dekoninck_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dekoninck-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_mitigation_attempts">Early mitigation attempts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early mitigation attempts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The disastrous effects of coal pollution on human health and mortality in the early 1950s in London led to the UK <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_1956" title="Clean Air Act 1956">Clean Air Act 1956</a>. This act led to dramatic  reductions of soot concentrations in the United Kingdom which were followed by similar reductions in US cities like Pittsburgh and St. Louis. These reductions were largely achieved by the decreased use of soft coal for domestic heating by switching either to “smokeless” coals or other forms of fuel, such as fuel oil and natural gas.  The steady reduction of smoke pollution in the industrial cities of Europe and United States caused a shift in research emphasis away from soot emissions and the almost complete neglect of black carbon as a significant aerosol constituent, at least in the United States.
</p><p>In the 1970s, however, a series of studies substantially changed this picture and demonstrated that black carbon as well as the organic soot components continued to be a large component in urban aerosols across the United States and Europe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which led to improved controls of these emissions. In the less-developed regions of the world where there were limited or no controls on soot emissions the air quality continued to degrade as the population increased.  It was not generally realized until many years later that from the perspective of global effects the emissions from these regions were extremely important.
</p>
<h3><span id="Influence_on_Earth.27s_atmosphere"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Earth's_atmosphere">Influence on Earth's atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence on Earth's atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the developments mentioned above relate to air quality in urban atmospheres. The first indications of the role of black carbon in a larger, global context came from studies of the Arctic Haze phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Black carbon was identified in the Arctic haze aerosols<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen-17">[17]</a></sup> and in the Arctic snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, aerosol particles can affect the radiation balance leading to a cooling or heating effect with the magnitude and sign of the temperature change largely dependent on aerosol optical properties, aerosol concentrations, and the albedo of the underlying surface.  A purely scattering aerosol will reflect energy that would normally be absorbed by the earth-atmosphere system back to space and leads to a cooling effect. As one adds an absorbing component to the aerosol, it can lead to a heating of the earth-atmosphere system if the reflectivity of the underlying surface is sufficiently high.
</p><p>Early studies of the effects of aerosols on atmospheric radiative transfer on a global scale assumed a dominantly scattering aerosol with only a small absorbing component, since this appears to be a good representation of naturally occurring aerosols. However, as discussed above, urban aerosols have a large black carbon component and if these particles can be transported on a global scale then one would expect a heating effect over surfaces with a high surface albedo like snow or ice.  Furthermore, if these particles are deposited in the snow an additional heating effect would occur due to reductions in the surface albedo.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_and_modeling_spatial_distribution">Measuring and modeling spatial distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measuring and modeling spatial distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Levels of Black carbon are most often determined based on the modification of the optical properties of a fiber filter by deposited particles. Either filter transmittance, filter reflectance or a combination of transmittance and reflectance is measured. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aethalometers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aethalometers (page does not exist)">Aethalometers</a> are frequently used devices that optically detect the changing absorption of light transmitted through a filter ticket. The USEPA Environmental Technology Verification program evaluated both the Aethalometer <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and also the Sunset Laboratory thermal-optical analyzer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A multiangle absorption photometer takes into account both transmitted and reflected light.  Alternative methods rely on satellite based measurements of optical depth for large areas or more recently on spectral noise analysis for very local concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1970s and early 1980s surprisingly large ground level concentrations of black carbon were observed throughout the western Arctic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen-17">[17]</a></sup> Modeling studies indicated that they could lead to heating over polar ice. One of the major uncertainties in modeling the effects of the Arctic haze on the solar radiation balance was limited knowledge of the vertical distributions of black carbon.
</p><p>During 1983 and 1984 as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> AGASP program, the first measurements of such distributions in the Arctic atmosphere were obtained with an <a href="/wiki/Aethalometer" title="Aethalometer">aethalometer</a> which had the capability of measuring black carbon on a real-time basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  These measurements showed substantial concentrations of black carbon found throughout the western Arctic troposphere including the North Pole.  The vertical profiles showed either a strongly layered structure or an almost uniform distribution up to eight kilometers with concentrations within layers as large as those found at ground level in typical mid-latitude urban areas in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2-23">[23]</a></sup>  The absorption <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depths</a> associated with these vertical profiles were large as evidenced by a vertical profile over the Norwegian arctic where absorption optical depths of 0.023 to 0.052 were calculated respectively for external and internal mixtures of black carbon with the other aerosol components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosen2_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosen2-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Optical depths of these magnitudes lead to a substantial change in the solar radiation balance over the highly reflecting Arctic snow surface during the March–April time frame of these measurements modeled the Arctic aerosol for an absorption optical depth of 0.021 (which is close to the average of an internal and external mixtures for the AGASP flights), under cloud-free conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  These heating effects were viewed at the time as potentially one of the major causes of Arctic warming trends as described in Archives of Dept. of Energy, Basic Energy Sciences Accomplishments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presence_in_soils">Presence in soils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presence in soils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Up to 30% of the total carbon stored in soils is contributed by black carbon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Especially for tropical soils black carbon serves as a reservoir for nutrients. Experiments showed that soils without high amounts of black carbon are significantly less fertile than soils that contain black carbon. An example for this increased soil fertility are the <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">Terra preta</a> soils of central Amazonia, which are presumably human-made by pre-Columbian native populations. Terra Preta soils have on average three times higher soil organic matter (SOM) content, higher nutrient levels and a better nutrient retention capacity than surrounding infertile soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In this context, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> agricultural practice used in tropical regions does not only enhance productivity by releasing nutrients from the burned vegetation but also by adding black carbon to the soil. Nonetheless, for a sustainable management, a <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-char" title="Slash-and-char">slash-and-char</a> practice would be better in order to prevent high emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> and volatile black carbon. Furthermore, the positive effects of this type of agriculture are counteracted if used for large patches so that soil erosion is not prevented by the vegetation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_sources">Emission sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Emission sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_region">By region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: By region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Developed countries were once the primary source of black carbon emissions, but this began to change in the 1950s with the adoption of pollution control technologies in those countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>   Whereas the United States emits about 21% of the world’s CO<sub>2</sub>, it emits 6.1% of the world’s soot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  The European Union and United States might further reduce their black carbon emissions by accelerating implementation of black carbon regulations that currently take effect in 2015 or 2020<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and by supporting the adoption of pending International Maritime Organization (IMO) regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Existing regulations also could be expanded to increase the use of clean diesel and <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">clean coal technologies</a> and to develop second-generation technologies.
</p><p>Today, the majority of black carbon emissions are from developing countries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and this trend is expected to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reference_Jacobson_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reference_Jacobson-33">[33]</a></sup> The largest sources of black carbon are Asia, Latin America, and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  China and India together account for 25-35% of global black carbon emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan-35">[35]</a></sup> Black carbon emissions from China doubled from 2000 to 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan-35">[35]</a></sup>  Existing and well-tested technologies used by developed countries, such as clean diesel and clean coal, could be transferred to developing countries to reduce their emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Black carbon emissions are highest in and around major source regions. This results in regional hotspots of atmospheric solar heating due to black carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  Hotspot areas include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V._Ramanathan_&amp;_G._Carmichael,_id_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-V._Ramanathan_&amp;_G._Carmichael,_id-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the Indo-Gangetic plains of India</li>
<li>eastern China</li>
<li>most of Southeast Asia and Indonesia</li>
<li>equatorial regions of Africa</li>
<li>Mexico and Central America</li>
<li>most of Brazil and Peru in South America.</li></ul>
<p>Approximately three billion people live in these hotspots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V._Ramanathan_&amp;_G._Carmichael,_id_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-V._Ramanathan_&amp;_G._Carmichael,_id-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_source">By source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: By source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3008" data-file-width="2000" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG/220px-Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG/330px-Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG/440px-Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tahmand_alumiiniumpott.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Black carbon on a cooking pot. Result of a biofuel cooking.</div></div></div>
<p>Approximately 20% of black carbon is emitted from burning biofuels, 40% from fossil fuels, and 40% from open biomass burning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Similar estimates of the sources of black carbon emissions are as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>42%    Open biomass burning (forest and savanna burning)</li>
<li>18%	Residential biofuel burned with traditional technologies</li>
<li>14%	<a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">Diesel engines</a> for transportation</li>
<li>10%	<a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">Diesel engines</a> for industrial use</li>
<li>10% 	Industrial processes and power generation, usually from smaller boilers</li>
<li>6%	Residential <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> burned with traditional technologies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Black carbon sources vary by region.  For example, the majority of soot emissions in South Asia are due to biofuel cooking,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> whereas in East Asia, coal combustion for residential and industrial uses plays a larger role. In Western Europe, traffic seems to be the most important source since high concentrations coincide with proximity to major roads or participation to (motorized) traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dons_et_al._43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dons_et_al.-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fossil fuel and biofuel soot have significantly greater amounts of black carbon than climate-cooling aerosols and particulate matter, making reductions of these sources particularly powerful mitigation strategies. For example, emissions from the diesel engines and marine vessels contain higher levels of black carbon compared to other sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>  Regulating black carbon emissions from diesel engines and marine vessels therefore presents a significant opportunity to reduce black carbon’s global warming impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biomass burning emits greater amounts of climate-cooling <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> and particulate matter than black carbon, resulting in short-term cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  However, over the long-term, biomass burning may cause a net warming when CO<sub>2</sub> emissions and deforestation are considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Reducing biomass emissions would therefore reduce global warming in the long-term and provide co-benefits of reduced air pollution, CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, and deforestation.  It has been estimated that by switching to <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-char" title="Slash-and-char">slash-and-char</a> from <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> agriculture, which turns biomass into ash using open fires that release black carbon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and GHGs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> 12% of anthropogenic carbon emissions caused by land use change could be reduced annually,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> which is approximately 0.66 Gt CO<sub>2</sub>-eq. per year, or 2% of all annual global CO<sub>2</sub>-eq emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black carbon is a form of ultrafine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a>, which when released in the air causes premature human mortality and disability.  In addition, atmospheric black carbon changes the radiative energy balance of the climate system in a way that raises air and surface temperatures, causing a variety of detrimental environmental impacts on humans, on agriculture, and on plant and animal ecosystems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health_impacts">Public health impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public health impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Particulate matter is the most harmful to public health of all air pollutants in Europe. Black carbon particulate matter contains very fine <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a> and is therefore particularly harmful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is estimated that from 640,000 to 4,900,000 premature human deaths could be prevented every year by utilizing available mitigation measures to reduce black carbon in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans are exposed to black carbon by inhalation of air in the immediate vicinity of local sources. Important indoor sources include candles and biomass burning whereas traffic and occasionally forest fires are the major outdoor sources of black carbon exposure. Concentrations of black carbon decrease sharply with increasing distance from (traffic) sources which makes it an atypical component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a>. This makes it difficult to estimate exposure of populations. For particulate matter epidemiological studies have traditionally relied on single fixed site measurements or inferred residential concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dons_et_al._2013_hourly_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dons_et_al._2013_hourly-54">[54]</a></sup> Recent studies have shown that as much black carbon is inhaled in traffic and at other locations as at the home address.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dons_et_al._2011_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dons_et_al._2011-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dons_et_al._2012_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dons_et_al._2012-56">[56]</a></sup> 
High peak concentrations are encountered during car driving. High in-vehicle concentrations of black carbon have been associated with driving during rush hours, on highways and in dense traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dons_et_al._2013_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dons_et_al._2013-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Even relatively low exposure concentrations of Black Carbon have a direct effect on the lung function of adults and an inflammatory effect on the respiratory system of children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laeremans_et_al._2018_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laeremans_et_al._2018-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De_Prins_et_al._2014_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-De_Prins_et_al._2014-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The public health benefits of reduction in the amount of soot and other particulate matter has been recognized for years. However, high concentrations persist in industrializing areas in Asia and in urban areas in the West such as <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-60">[60]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> estimates that air pollution causes nearly two million premature deaths per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> By reducing black carbon, a primary component of fine particulate matter, the health risks from air pollution will decline.  In fact, public health concerns have given rise to leading to many efforts to reduce such emissions, for example, from diesel vehicles and cooking stoves.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_impacts">Climate impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Direct effect</b> Black carbon particles directly absorb sunlight and reduce the planetary albedo when suspended in the atmosphere.
</p><p><b>Semi-direct effect</b> Black carbon absorb incoming solar radiation, perturb the temperature structure of the atmosphere, and influence cloud cover. They may either increase or decrease cloud cover under different conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koch_and_Del_Genio,_2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koch_and_Del_Genio,_2010-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Snow/ice albedo effect</b> When deposited on high albedo surfaces like ice and snow, black carbon particles reduce the total surface albedo available to reflect solar energy back into space. Small initial snow albedo reduction may have a large forcing because of a positive feedback: Reduced snow albedo would increase surface temperature. The increased surface temperature would decrease the snow cover and further decrease surface albedo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR032612_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-JGR032612-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Indirect effect</b> Black carbon may also indirectly cause changes in the absorption or reflection of solar radiation through changes in the properties and behavior of clouds. Research scheduled for publication in 2013 shows black carbon plays a role second only to carbon dioxide in climate change. Effects are complex, resulting from a variety of factors, but due to the short life of black carbon in the atmosphere, about a week as compared to carbon dioxide which last centuries, control of black carbon offers possible opportunities for slowing, or even reversing, climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR032612_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-JGR032612-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT011513_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT011513-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA011713_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA011713-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_forcing">Radiative forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radiative forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Estimates of black carbon’s globally averaged direct radiative forcing vary from the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a>’s estimate of + 0.34 watts per square meter (W/m<sup>2</sup>) ± 0.25,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>  to a more recent estimate by V. Ramanathan and G. Carmichael of 0.9 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_a_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_a-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC also estimated the globally averaged snow albedo effect of black carbon at +0.1 ± 0.1 W/m<sup>2</sup>.
</p><p>Based on the IPCC estimate, it would be reasonable to conclude that the combined direct and indirect snow albedo effects for black carbon rank it as the third largest contributor to globally averaged positive radiative forcing since the pre-industrial period. In comparison, the more recent direct radiative forcing estimate by Ramanathan and Carmichael <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ramanathan_a_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ramanathan_a-67">[67]</a></sup> would lead one to conclude that black carbon has contributed the second largest globally averaged radiative forcing after carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), and that the radiative forcing of black carbon is “as much as 55% of the CO<sub>2</sub> forcing and is larger than the forcing due to the other greenhouse gasses (GHGs) such as CH<sub>4</sub>, CFCs, N<sub>2</sub>O, or tropospheric ozone.”

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_Arctic_ice_and_Himalayan_glaciers">Effects on Arctic ice and Himalayan glaciers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Effects on Arctic ice and Himalayan glaciers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the <abbr title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</abbr>, “the presence of black carbon over highly reflective surfaces, such as snow and ice, or clouds, may cause a significant positive radiative forcing.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>  The IPCC also notes that emissions from <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> burning, which usually have a negative forcing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>  have a positive forcing over snow fields in areas such as the Himalayas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Charles Zender, black carbon is a significant contributor to Arctic ice-melt, and reducing such emissions may be “the most efficient way to mitigate Arctic warming that we know of”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The “climate forcing due to snow/ice albedo change is of the order of 1.0 W/m<sup>2</sup> at middle- and high-latitude land areas in the Northern Hemisphere and over the Arctic Ocean.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>  The “soot effect on snow albedo may be responsible for a quarter of observed global warming.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> “Soot deposition increases surface melt on ice masses, and the meltwater spurs multiple radiative and dynamical feedback processes that accelerate ice disintegration,” according to <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> scientists James Hansen and Larissa Nazarenko.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> As a result of this feedback process, “BC on snow warms the planet about three times more than an equal forcing of CO<sub>2</sub>.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>  When black carbon concentrations in the Arctic increase during the winter and spring due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_Haze" title="Arctic Haze">Arctic Haze</a>, surface temperatures increase by 0.5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Black carbon emissions also significantly contribute to Arctic ice-melt, which is critical because “nothing in climate is more aptly described as a ‘tipping point’ than the 0 °C boundary that separates frozen from liquid water—the bright, reflective snow and ice from the dark, heat-absorbing ocean.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Black carbon emissions from northern Eurasia, North America, and Asia have the greatest absolute impact on Arctic warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> However, black carbon emissions actually occurring within the Arctic have a disproportionately larger impact per particle on Arctic warming than emissions originating elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> As Arctic ice melts and shipping activity increases, emissions originating within the Arctic are expected to rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some regions, such as the Himalayas, the impact of black carbon on melting snowpack and glaciers may be equal to that of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Warmer air resulting from the presence of black carbon in South and East Asia over the Himalayas contributes to a warming of approximately 0.6 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-99">[99]</a></sup> An “analysis of temperature trends on the Tibetan side of the Himalayas reveals warming in excess of 1 °C.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-99">[99]</a></sup> A summer aerosol sampling on a glacier saddle of Mt. Everest (Qomolangma) in 2003 showed industrially induced sulfate from South Asia may cross over the highly elevated Himalaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> This indicated BC in South Asia could also have the same transport mode. And such kind of signal might have been detected in at a black carbon monitoring site in the hinterland of Tibet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Snow sampling and measurement suggested black carbon deposited in some Himalayan glaciers may reduce the surface albedo by 0.01-0.02.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Black carbon record based on a shallow ice core drilled from the East Rongbuk glacier showed a dramatic increasing trend of black carbon concentrations in the ice stratigraphy since the 1990s, and simulated average radiative forcing caused by black carbon was nearly 2 W/m<sup>2</sup> in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> This large warming trend is the proposed causal factor for the accelerating retreat of Himalayan glaciers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_99-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-99">[99]</a></sup> which threatens fresh water supplies and food security in China and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> A general darkening trend in the mid-Himalaya glaciers revealed by MODIS data since 2000 could be partially attributed to black carbon and light absorbing impurities like dust in the springtime, which was later extended to the whole Hindu Kush-Kararoram-Himalaya glaciers research finding a widespread darkening trend of -0.001 yr<sup>−1</sup> over the period of 2000-2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ming,_J_2015_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ming,_J_2015-106">[106]</a></sup> The most rapid decrease in albedo (more negative than -0.0015 yr<sup>−1</sup>) occurred in the altitudes over 5500 m above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ming,_J_2015_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ming,_J_2015-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_warming">Global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its 2007 report, the IPCC estimated for the first time the direct <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> of black carbon from fossil fuel emissions at + 0.2 W/m², and the radiative forcing of black carbon through its effect on the surface albedo of snow and ice at an additional + 0.1 W/m².<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> More recent studies and public testimony by many of the same scientists cited in the IPCC’s report estimate that emissions from black carbon are the second-largest contributor to global warming after carbon dioxide emissions, and that reducing these emissions may be the fastest strategy for slowing climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-108">[108]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1950, many countries have significantly reduced black carbon emissions, especially from fossil fuel sources, primarily to improve public health from improved air quality, and “technology exists for a drastic reduction of fossil fuel related BC” throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Given black carbon’s relatively short lifespan, reducing black carbon emissions would reduce warming within weeks. Because black carbon remains in the atmosphere only for a few weeks, reducing black carbon emissions may be the fastest means of slowing climate change in the near term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-108">[108]</a></sup> Control of black carbon, particularly from fossil-fuel and biofuel sources, is very likely to be the fastest method of slowing global warming in the immediate future,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-111">[111]</a></sup> and major cuts in black carbon emissions could slow the effects of climate change for a decade or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Reducing black carbon emissions could help keep the climate system from passing the tipping points for <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate changes</a>, including significant sea-level rise from the melting of Greenland and/or Antarctic ice sheets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>“Emissions of black carbon are the second strongest contribution to current global warming, after carbon dioxide emissions”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Calculation of black carbon’s combined climate forcing at 1.0–1.2 W/m², which “is as much as 55% of the CO<sub>2</sub> forcing and is larger than the forcing due to the other [GHGs] such as CH<sub>4</sub>, CFCs, N<sub>2</sub>O or tropospheric ozone.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> Other scientists estimate the total magnitude of black carbon’s forcing between + 0.2 and 1.1 W/m² with varying ranges due to uncertainties. (See Table 1.) This compares with the IPCC’s climate forcing estimates of 1.66 W/m<sup>2</sup> for CO<sub>2</sub> and 0.48 W/m² for CH<sub>4</sub>. (See Table 2.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> In addition, black carbon forcing is two to three times as effective in raising temperatures in the Northern Hemisphere and the Arctic than equivalent forcing values of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacobson calculates that reducing fossil fuel and biofuel soot particles would eliminate about 40% of the net observed global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>  (See Figure 1.) In addition to black carbon, fossil fuel and biofuel soot contain aerosols and particulate matter that cool the planet by reflecting the sun’s radiation away from the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>  When the aerosols and particulate matter are accounted for, fossil fuel and biofuel soot are increasing temperatures by about 0.35 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p><p>Black carbon alone is estimated to have a 20-year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Warming_Potential" title="Global Warming Potential">Global Warming Potential</a> (GWP) of 4,470, and a 100-year GWP of 1,055–2,240.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>  Fossil fuel soot, as a result of mixing with cooling aerosols and particulate matter, has a lower 20-year GWP of 2,530, and a 100-year GWP of 840-1,280.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Integrated Assessment of Black Carbon and Tropospheric Ozone published in 2011 by the United Nations Environment Programme and World Meteorological Organization calculates that cutting black carbon, along with tropospheric ozone and its precursor, methane, can reduce the rate of global warming by half and the rate of warming in the Arctic by two-thirds, in combination with CO<sub>2</sub> cuts. By trimming “peak warming”, such cuts can keep current global temperature rise below 1.5 ˚C for 30 years and below 2 ˚C for 60 years, in combination with CO<sub>2</sub> cuts. (FN: UNEP-WMO 2011.) See Table 1, on page 9 of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.unep.org/dewa/Portals/67/pdf/Black_Carbon.pdf" rel="nofollow">UNEP-WMO report</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduction of CO<sub>2</sub> as well as SLCFs could keep global temperature rise under 1.5 ˚C through 2030, and below 2 ˚C through 2070, assuming CO<sub>2</sub> is also cut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_123-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-123">[123]</a></sup> See the graph on page 12 of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.unep.org/dewa/Portals/67/pdf/Black_Carbon.pdf" rel="nofollow">UNEP-WMO report</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_123-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_technologies">Control technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Control technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ramanathan notes that “developed nations have reduced their black carbon emissions from fossil fuel sources by a factor of 5 or more since 1950.  Thus, the technology exists for a drastic reduction of fossil fuel related black carbon.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacobson believes that “[g]iven proper conditions and incentives, [soot] polluting technologies can be quickly phased out.  In some small-scale applications (such as domestic cooking in developing countries), health and convenience will drive such a transition when affordable, reliable alternatives are available.  For other sources, such as vehicles or coal boilers, regulatory approaches may be required to nudge either the transition to existing technology or the development of new technology.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen states that “technology is within reach that could greatly reduce soot, restoring snow albedo to near pristine values, while having multiple other benefits for climate, human health, agricultural productivity, and environmental aesthetics.  Already soot emissions from coal are decreasing in many regions with transition from small users to power plants with scrubbers.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacobson suggests converting “[U.S.] vehicles from fossil fuel to electric, plug-in-hybrid, or hydrogen fuel cell vehicles, where the electricity or hydrogen is produced by a renewable energy source, such as wind, solar, geothermal, hydroelectric, wave, or tidal power.  Such a conversion would eliminate 160 Gg/yr (24%) of U.S. (or 1.5% of world) fossil-fuel soot and about 26% of U.S. (or 5.5% of world) carbon dioxide.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> According to Jacobson’s estimates, this proposal would reduce soot and CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by 1.63 GtCO<sub>2</sub>–eq. per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> He notes, however, “that the elimination of hydrocarbons and nitrogen oxides would also eliminate some cooling particles, reducing the net benefit by at most, half, but improving human health,” a substantial reduction for one policy in one country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p><p>For diesel vehicles in particular there are several effective technologies available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup>  Newer, more efficient <a href="/wiki/Diesel_particulate_filter" title="Diesel particulate filter">diesel particulate filters</a> (DPFs), or traps, can eliminate over 90% of black carbon emissions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> but these devices require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra-low_sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low sulfur diesel">ultra-low sulfur diesel</a> fuel (ULSD).  To ensure compliance with new particulate rules for new on-road and non-road vehicles in the U.S., the EPA first required a nationwide shift to ULSD, which allowed DPFs to be used in diesel vehicles in order to meet the standards.  Because of recent EPA regulations, black carbon emissions from diesel vehicles are expected to decline about 70 percent from 2001 to 2020.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> Overall, “BC emissions in the United States are projected to decline by 42 percent from 2001 to 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> By the time the full fleet is subject to these rules, EPA estimates that over 239,000 tons of particulate matter will be reduced annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> Outside of the US diesel oxidation catalysts are often available and DPFs will become available as ULSD is more widely commercialized.
</p><p>Another technology for reducing black carbon emissions from diesel engines is to shift fuels to compressed natural gas. In <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>, India, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a> ordered shift to compressed natural gas for all public transport vehicles, including buses, taxis, and rickshaws, resulted in a climate benefit, “largely because of the dramatic reduction of black carbon emissions from the diesel bus engines.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>  Overall, the fuel switch for the vehicles reduced black carbon emissions enough to produce a 10 percent net reduction in CO<sub>2</sub>-eq., and perhaps as much as 30 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> The main gains were from diesel bus engines whose CO<sub>2</sub>-eq. emissions were reduced 20 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>  According to a study examining these emissions reductions, “there is a significant potential for emissions reductions through the [UNFCCC] Clean Development for such fuel switching projects.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p><p>Technologies are also in development to reduce some of the 133,000 metric tons of particulate matter emitted each year from ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>  Ocean vessels use diesel engines, and particulate filters similar to those in use for land vehicles are now being tested on them.  As with current particulate filters these too would require the ships to use ULSD, but if comparable emissions reductions are attainable, up to 120,000 metric tons of particulate emissions could be eliminated each year from international shipping. That is, if particulate filters could be shown reduce black carbon emissions 90 percent from ships as they do for land vehicles, 120,000 metric tons of today’s 133,000 metric tons of emissions would be prevented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> Other efforts can reduce the amount of black carbon emissions from ships simply by decreasing the amount of fuel the ships use.  By traveling at slower speeds or by using shore side electricity when at port instead of running the ship’s diesel engines for electric power, ships can save fuel and reduce emissions.
</p><p>Reynolds and Kandlikar estimate that the shift to <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> for public transport in New Delhi ordered by the Supreme Court reduced climate emissions by 10 to 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ramanathan estimates that “providing alternative energy-efficient and smoke-free cookers and introducing transferring technology for reducing soot emissions from coal combustion in small industries could have major impacts on the radiative forcing due to soot.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>  Specifically, the impact of replacing biofuel cooking with black carbon-free cookers (solar, bio, and natural gas) in South and East Asia is dramatic: over South Asia, a 70 to 80% reduction in black carbon heating; and in East Asia, a 20 to 40% reduction.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradation">Biodegradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biodegradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Condensed aromatic ring structures indicate black carbon degradation in soil. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saprophytic_fungi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saprophytic fungi (page does not exist)">Saprophytic fungi</a> are being researched for their potential role in the degradation of black carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_options">Policy options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Policy options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many countries have existing national laws to regulate black carbon emissions, including laws that address particulate emissions.  Some examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>banning or regulating slash-and-burn clearing of forests and savannas;</li>
<li>requiring shore-based power/electrification of ships at port, regulating idling at terminals, and mandating fuel standards for ships seeking to dock at port;</li>
<li>requiring regular vehicle emissions tests, retirement, or retrofitting (e.g. adding particulate traps<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup>), including penalties for failing to meet air quality emissions standards, and heightened penalties for on-the-road "super-emitting" vehicles;</li>
<li>banning or regulating the sale of certain fuels and/or requiring the use of cleaner fuels for certain uses;</li>
<li>limiting the use of chimneys and other forms of biomass burning in urban and non-urban areas;</li>
<li>requiring permits to operate industrial, power generating, and oil refining facilities and periodic permit renewal and/or modification of equipment; and</li>
<li>requiring filtering technology and high-temperature combustion (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_steam_generator" title="Supercritical steam generator">supercritical</a> coal) for existing power generation plants, and regulating annual emissions from power generation plants.</li></ul>
<p>The International Network for Environmental Compliance &amp; Enforcement issued a Climate Compliance Alert on Black Carbon in 2008 which cited reduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a> as a cost-effective way to reduce a major cause of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup>
</p>
