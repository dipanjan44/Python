<p><b>Sulfur trioxide</b> (alternative spelling <b>sulphur trioxide</b>) is the chemical compound with the formula SO<sub>3</sub>, with a relatively narrow liquid range. In the gaseous form, this species is a significant pollutant, being the primary agent in <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is prepared on an industrial scale as a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.
</p><p>In perfectly dry apparatus, sulfur trioxide vapor is invisible, and the liquid is transparent. However, it fumes profusely even in a relatively dry atmosphere (it has been used as a smoke agent) due to formation of a sulfuric acid mist. This vapor has no odor but is extremely corrosive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lerner_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lerner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_structure_and_bonding">Molecular structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Molecular structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gaseous SO<sub>3</sub> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_planar" title="Trigonal planar">trigonal planar</a> molecule of D<sub>3h</sub> symmetry, as predicted by <a href="/wiki/VSEPR_theory" title="VSEPR theory">VSEPR theory</a>. SO<sub>3</sub> belongs to the D<sub>3h</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">point group</a>.
</p><p>In terms of electron-counting formalism, the sulfur atom has an <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of +6 and a <a href="/wiki/Formal_charge" title="Formal charge">formal charge</a> of +2. The <a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis structure</a> consists of an S=O <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bond</a> and two S–O <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative_bond" title="Dative bond">dative bonds</a> without utilizing d-orbitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The electrical <a href="/wiki/Dipole#molecular_dipoles" title="Dipole">dipole moment</a> of gaseous sulfur trioxide is zero. This is a consequence of the 120° angle between the S-O bonds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_of_solid_SO3">Structure of solid SO<sub>3</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure of solid SO3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="742" data-file-width="1100" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png/200px-Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png/300px-Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png/400px-Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sulfur-trioxide-trimer-from-xtal-1967-3D-balls-B.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ball-and-stick_model" title="Ball-and-stick model">Ball-and-stick model</a> of the <i>γ</i>-SO<sub>3</sub> molecule</div></div></div>
<p>The nature of solid SO<sub>3</sub> is complex because structural changes are caused by traces of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon condensation of the gas, absolutely pure SO<sub>3</sub> condenses into a trimer, which is often called <i>γ</i>-SO<sub>3</sub>. This molecular form is a colorless solid with a melting point of 16.8 °C. It adopts a cyclic structure described as [S(=O)<sub>2</sub>(<i>μ</i>-O)]<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cw_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cw-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>If SO<sub>3</sub> is condensed above 27 °C, then <i>α</i>-SO<sub>3</sub> forms, which has a melting point of 62.3 °C. <i>α</i>-SO<sub>3</sub> is fibrous in appearance. Structurally, it is the <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> [S(=O)<sub>2</sub>(<i>μ</i>-O)]<sub><i>n</i></sub>. Each end of the polymer is terminated with OH groups. <i>β</i>-SO<sub>3</sub>, like the alpha form, is fibrous but of different molecular weight, consisting of an hydroxyl-capped polymer, but melts at 32.5 °C. Both the gamma and the beta forms are metastable, eventually converting to the stable alpha form if left standing for sufficient time. This conversion is caused by traces of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merck-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Relative vapor pressures of solid SO<sub>3</sub> are alpha &lt; beta &lt; gamma at identical temperatures, indicative of their relative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">molecular weights</a>. Liquid sulfur trioxide has a vapor pressure consistent with the gamma form. Thus heating a crystal of  <i>α</i>-SO<sub>3</sub> to its melting point results in a sudden increase in vapor pressure, which can be forceful enough to shatter a glass vessel in which it is heated. This effect is known as the "alpha explosion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merck-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>SO<sub>3</sub> is aggressively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a>. The heat of hydration is sufficient that mixtures of SO<sub>3</sub> and wood or cotton can ignite. In such cases, SO<sub>3</sub> dehydrates these <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Merck-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>SO<sub>3</sub> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_anhydride" title="Inorganic anhydride">anhydride</a> of H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>. Thus, the following reaction occurs:
</p>
<dl><dd>SO<sub>3</sub><sub> (g)</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub> (l)</sub> → H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub><sub> (aq)</sub> (ΔH<sub>f</sub> = −200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilojoule" title="Kilojoule">kJ</a> <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol<sup>−1</sup></a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The reaction occurs both rapidly and exothermically, too violently to be used in large-scale manufacturing. At or above 340 °C, sulfuric acid, sulfur trioxide, and water coexist in significant equilibrium concentrations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Sulfur trioxide also reacts with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dichloride" title="Sulfur dichloride">sulfur dichloride</a> to yield the useful <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_chloride" title="Thionyl chloride">thionyl chloride</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>SO<sub>3</sub> + SCl<sub>2</sub> → SOCl<sub>2</sub> + SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>SO<sub>3</sub> is a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> readily forming crystalline complexes with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide_pyridine_complex" title="Sulfur trioxide pyridine complex">pyridine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxane" title="Dioxane">dioxane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trimethylamine" title="Trimethylamine">trimethylamine</a>. These adducts can be used as sulfonating agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sulfur trioxide can be prepared in the laboratory by the two-stage <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bisulfate" title="Sodium bisulfate">sodium bisulfate</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_pyrosulfate" title="Sodium pyrosulfate">Sodium pyrosulfate</a> is an intermediate product:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vries_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-vries-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Dehydration at 315 °C:
<dl><dd>2 NaHSO<sub>4</sub>  →  Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl></li>
<li>Cracking at 460 °C:
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>  →  Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + SO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl></li></ol>
<p>In contrast, KHSO<sub>4</sub> does not undergo the same reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vries_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-vries-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Industrially SO<sub>3</sub> is made by the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">Sulfur dioxide</a>, which in turn is produced by the burning of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_pyrite" title="Iron pyrite">iron pyrite</a> (a sulfide ore of iron). After being purified by electrostatic precipitation, the SO<sub>2</sub> is then oxidised by atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> at between 400 and 600 °C over a catalyst. A typical catalyst consists of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> (V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) activated with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_oxide" title="Potassium oxide">potassium oxide</a> K<sub>2</sub>O on <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">kieselguhr</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silica</a> support. <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">Platinum</a> also works very well but is too expensive and is poisoned (rendered ineffective) much more easily by impurities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The majority of sulfur trioxide made in this way is converted into <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> not by the direct addition of water, with which it forms a fine mist, but by absorption in concentrated sulfuric acid and dilution with water of the produced <a href="/wiki/Oleum" title="Oleum">oleum</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate#other_uses" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate § other uses</a></div>
<p>It was once produced industrially by heating <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sulfur trioxide is an essential reagent in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfonation" title="Sulfonation">sulfonation</a> reactions. These processes afford detergents, dyes, and pharmaceuticals. Sulfur trioxide is generated in situ from sulfuric acid or is used as a solution in the acid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with being a strong oxidizing agent, sulfur trioxide will cause serious burns on both inhalation and ingestion because it is highly corrosive and hygroscopic in nature. SO<sub>3</sub> should be handled with extreme care as it reacts violently with water and produces highly corrosive sulfuric acid. It should also be kept away from organic material due to the strong dehydrating nature of sulfur trioxide and its ability to react violently with such materials.
</p>
