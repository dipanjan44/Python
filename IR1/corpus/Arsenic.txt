<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>Arsenic</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>As</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 33. Arsenic occurs in many minerals, usually in combination with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, but also as a pure elemental <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>. Arsenic is a <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloid</a>. It has various <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropes</a>, but only the gray form, which has a metallic appearance, is important to industry.
</p><p>The primary use of arsenic is in alloys of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> (for example, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_batteries" title="Car batteries">car batteries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a>). Arsenic is a common n-type <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> electronic devices, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optoelectronic" title="Optoelectronic">optoelectronic</a> compound <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> is the second most commonly used semiconductor after doped <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. Arsenic and its compounds, especially the trioxide, are used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, treated wood products, <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a>. These applications are declining due to the toxicity of arsenic and its compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="#Bacteria">A few species of bacteria</a> are able to use arsenic compounds as respiratory <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolites</a>. Trace quantities of arsenic are an essential <a href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrient)" title="Mineral (nutrient)">dietary element</a> in rats, hamsters, goats, chickens, and presumably other species. A role in human metabolism is not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a> occurs in multicellular life if quantities are larger than needed. <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_contamination_of_groundwater" title="Arsenic contamination of groundwater">Arsenic contamination of groundwater</a> is a problem that affects millions of people across the world.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> states that all forms of arsenic are a serious risk to human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA1-9">[9]</a></sup> The United States' <a href="/wiki/Agency_for_Toxic_Substances_and_Disease_Registry" title="Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry">Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry</a> ranked arsenic as number 1 in its 2001 Priority List of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous" title="Hazardous">Hazardous</a> Substances at <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Arsenic is classified as a Group-A <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA1-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SbAs_lattice.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="846" data-file-width="1130" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/SbAs_lattice.png/220px-SbAs_lattice.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/SbAs_lattice.png/330px-SbAs_lattice.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/SbAs_lattice.png/440px-SbAs_lattice.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SbAs_lattice.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure common to <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">Sb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stibarsen" title="Stibarsen">AsSb</a> and gray As</div></div></div>
<p>The three most common arsenic <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropes</a> are gray, yellow, and black arsenic, with gray being the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norman-11">[11]</a></sup> Gray arsenic (α-As, <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> R<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span>m No. 166) adopts a double-layered structure consisting of many interlocked, ruffled, six-membered rings. Because of weak bonding between the layers, gray arsenic is brittle and has a relatively low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 3.5. Nearest and next-nearest neighbors form a distorted octahedral complex, with the three atoms in the same double-layer being slightly closer than the three atoms in the next.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg2001-12">[12]</a></sup> This relatively close packing leads to a high density of 5.73 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup> Gray arsenic is a <a href="/wiki/Semimetal" title="Semimetal">semimetal</a>, but becomes a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandgap" title="Bandgap">bandgap</a> of 1.2–1.4 eV if amorphized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Gray arsenic is also the most stable form. Yellow arsenic is soft and waxy, and somewhat similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraphosphorus" title="Tetraphosphorus">tetraphosphorus</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">P<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>). Both have four atoms arranged in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral" title="Tetrahedral">tetrahedral</a> structure in which each atom is bound to each of the other three atoms by a single bond. This unstable allotrope, being molecular, is the most volatile, least dense, and most toxic. Solid yellow arsenic is produced by rapid cooling of arsenic vapor, <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>. It is rapidly transformed into gray arsenic by light. The yellow form has a density of 1.97 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup> Black arsenic is similar in structure to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_phosphorus" title="Black phosphorus">black phosphorus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup>
Black arsenic can also be formed by cooling vapor at around 100–220 °C. It is glassy and brittle. It is also a poor electrical conductor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_arsenic" title="Isotopes of arsenic">Isotopes of arsenic</a></div>
<p>Arsenic occurs in nature as a <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic element</a>, composed of one stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>75</sup>As.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-16">[16]</a></sup>  As of 2003, at least 33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have also been synthesized, ranging in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> from 60 to 92. The most stable of these is <sup>73</sup>As with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 80.30 days. All other isotopes have half-lives of under one day, with the exception of <sup>71</sup>As (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=65.30 hours), <sup>72</sup>As (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=26.0 hours), <sup>74</sup>As (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=17.77 days), <sup>76</sup>As (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=1.0942 days), and <sup>77</sup>As (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub>=38.83 hours). Isotopes that are lighter than the stable <sup>75</sup>As tend to decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">β<sup>+</sup> decay</a>, and those that are heavier tend to decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">β<sup>−</sup> decay</a>, with some exceptions.
</p><p>At least 10 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> have been described, ranging in atomic mass from 66 to 84. The most stable of arsenic's isomers is <sup>68m</sup>As with a half-life of 111 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic has a similar electronegativity and ionization energies to its lighter congener phosphorus and as such readily forms covalent molecules with most of the nonmetals. Though stable in dry air, arsenic forms a golden-bronze tarnish upon exposure to humidity which eventually becomes a black surface layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood552_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood552-17">[17]</a></sup> When heated in air, arsenic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidizes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a>; the fumes from this reaction have an odor resembling <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>. This odor can be detected on striking <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenide</a> minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">arsenopyrite</a> with a hammer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gokcen1989_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gokcen1989-2">[2]</a></sup> It burns in oxygen to form arsenic trioxide and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentoxide" title="Arsenic pentoxide">arsenic pentoxide</a>, which have the same structure as the more well-known phosphorus compounds, and in fluorine to give <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentafluoride" title="Arsenic pentafluoride">arsenic pentafluoride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood552_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood552-17">[17]</a></sup> Arsenic (and some arsenic compounds) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimes</a> upon heating at atmospheric pressure, converting directly to a gaseous form without an intervening liquid state at 887 K (614 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gokcen1989_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gokcen1989-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> is 3.63 MPa and 1,090 K (820 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gokcen1989_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gokcen1989-2">[2]</a></sup> Arsenic makes <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_acid" title="Arsenic acid">arsenic acid</a> with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenous_acid" title="Arsenous acid">arsenous acid</a> with dilute nitric acid, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a> with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>; however, it does not react with water, alkalis, or non-oxidising acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Arsenic reacts with metals to form <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenides</a>, though these are not ionic compounds containing the As<sup>3−</sup> ion as the formation of such an anion would be highly endothermic and even the group 1 arsenides have properties of <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic" title="Intermetallic">intermetallic</a> compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood552_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood552-17">[17]</a></sup> Like <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>, which like arsenic <a href="/wiki/D-block_contraction" title="D-block contraction">succeed the 3d transition series</a>, arsenic is much less stable in the group oxidation state of +5 than its vertical neighbors phosphorus and antimony, and hence arsenic pentoxide and arsenic acid are potent oxidizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood552_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood552-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Arsenic_compounds" title="Category:Arsenic compounds">Category:Arsenic compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Compounds of arsenic resemble in some respects those of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> which occupies the same <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group</a> (column) of the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. The most common <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> for arsenic are: −3 in the <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenides</a>, which are alloy-like intermetallic compounds, +3 in the <a href="/wiki/Arsenite" title="Arsenite">arsenites</a>, and +5 in the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenates" title="Arsenates">arsenates</a> and most organoarsenic compounds. Arsenic also bonds readily to itself as seen in the square As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> ions in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Skutterudite" title="Skutterudite">skutterudite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  In the +3 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>, arsenic is typically pyramidal owing to the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norman_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norman-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inorganic_compounds">Inorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the simplest arsenic compound is the trihydride, the highly toxic, flammable, pyrophoric <a href="/wiki/Arsine" title="Arsine">arsine</a> (AsH<sub>3</sub>). This compound is generally regarded as stable, since at room temperature it decomposes only slowly. At temperatures of 250–300 °C decomposition to arsenic and hydrogen is rapid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood557_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood557-20">[20]</a></sup> Several factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, presence of light and certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> (namely <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>) facilitate the rate of decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INRS_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-INRS-21">[21]</a></sup> It oxidises readily in air to form arsenic trioxide and water, and analogous reactions take place with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood557_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood557-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic forms colorless, odorless, crystalline <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_arsenic" title="White arsenic">white arsenic</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentoxide" title="Arsenic pentoxide">As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> and readily soluble in water to form acidic solutions. <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_acid" title="Arsenic acid">Arsenic(V) acid</a> is a weak acid and the salts are called <a href="/wiki/Arsenate" title="Arsenate">arsenates</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood572_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood572-22">[22]</a></sup> the most common <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_contamination_of_groundwater" title="Arsenic contamination of groundwater">arsenic contamination of groundwater</a>, and a problem that affects many people. Synthetic arsenates include <a href="/wiki/Scheele%27s_Green" title="Scheele's Green">Scheele's Green</a> (cupric hydrogen arsenate, acidic copper arsenate), <a href="/wiki/Calcium_arsenate" title="Calcium arsenate">calcium arsenate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead_hydrogen_arsenate" title="Lead hydrogen arsenate">lead hydrogen arsenate</a>. These three have been used as <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisons</a>.
</p><p>The protonation steps between the arsenate and arsenic acid are similar to those between <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Phosphorous_acid" title="Phosphorous acid">phosphorous acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenous_acid" title="Arsenous acid">arsenous acid</a> is genuinely tribasic, with the formula As(OH)<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood572_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood572-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>A broad variety of sulfur compounds of arsenic are known. Orpiment (<a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trisulfide" title="Arsenic trisulfide">As<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub></a>) and realgar (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraarsenic_tetrasulfide" title="Tetraarsenic tetrasulfide">As<sub>4</sub>S<sub>4</sub></a>) are somewhat abundant and were formerly used as painting pigments. In As<sub>4</sub>S<sub>10</sub>, arsenic has a formal oxidation state of +2 in As<sub>4</sub>S<sub>4</sub> which features As-As bonds so that the total covalency of As is still 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Both orpiment and realgar, as well as As<sub>4</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, have selenium analogs; the analogous As<sub>2</sub>Te<sub>3</sub> is known as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Kalgoorlieite" title="Kalgoorlieite">kalgoorlieite</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and the anion As<sub>2</sub>Te<sup>−</sup> is known as a ligand in <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood578_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood578-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>All trihalides of arsenic(III) are well known except the astatide, which is unknown. <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentafluoride" title="Arsenic pentafluoride">Arsenic pentafluoride</a> (AsF<sub>5</sub>) is the only important pentahalide, reflecting the lower stability of the +5 oxidation state; even so, it is a very strong fluorinating and oxidizing agent. (The <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentachloride" title="Arsenic pentachloride">pentachloride</a> is stable only below −50 °C, at which temperature it decomposes to the trichloride, releasing chlorine gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup>)
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Arsenic is used as the group 5 element in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/III-V_semiconductor" title="III-V semiconductor">III-V semiconductors</a> <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indium_arsenide" title="Indium arsenide">indium arsenide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_arsenide" title="Aluminium arsenide">aluminium arsenide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  The valence electron count of GaAs is the same as a pair of Si atoms, but the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">band structure</a> is completely different which results distinct bulk properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Other arsenic alloys include the II-V semiconductor <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_arsenide" title="Cadmium arsenide">cadmium arsenide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organoarsenic_compounds">Organoarsenic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Organoarsenic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organoarsenic_chemistry" title="Organoarsenic chemistry">Organoarsenic chemistry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trimethylarsine-2D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="568" data-file-width="1100" height="46" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Trimethylarsine-2D.png/90px-Trimethylarsine-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Trimethylarsine-2D.png/135px-Trimethylarsine-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Trimethylarsine-2D.png/180px-Trimethylarsine-2D.png 2x" width="90"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trimethylarsine-2D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trimethylarsine</div></div></div>
<p>A large variety of organoarsenic compounds are known. Several were developed as <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical warfare agents</a> during World War I, including <a href="/wiki/Blister_agent" title="Blister agent">vesicants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">lewisite</a> and vomiting agents such as <a href="/wiki/Adamsite" title="Adamsite">adamsite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellison2007_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellison2007-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Girard2010_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Girard2010-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Somani2001_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Somani2001-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cacodylic_acid" title="Cacodylic acid">Cacodylic acid</a>, which is of historic and practical interest, arises from the <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylation</a> of arsenic trioxide, a reaction that has no analogy in phosphorus chemistry. Indeed, <a href="/wiki/Cacodyl" title="Cacodyl">cacodyl</a> was the first organometallic compound known (even though arsenic is not a true metal) and was named from the Greek <i>κακωδἰα</i> "stink" for its offensive odor; like all arsenic compounds, it is very poisonous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood584_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood584-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Arsenide_minerals" title="Category:Arsenide minerals">Arsenide minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Arsenate_minerals" title="Category:Arsenate minerals">Arsenate minerals</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Native_arsenic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="585" data-file-width="637" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Native_arsenic.jpg/200px-Native_arsenic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Native_arsenic.jpg/300px-Native_arsenic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Native_arsenic.jpg/400px-Native_arsenic.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Native_arsenic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A large sample of native arsenic</div></div></div>
<p>Arsenic comprises about 1.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> (0.00015%) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>, and is the 53rd most abundant element. Typical background concentrations of arsenic do not exceed 3 ng/m<sup>3</sup> in the atmosphere; 100 mg/kg in soil; and 10 μg/L in freshwater.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">Minerals</a> with the formula MAsS and MAs<sub>2</sub> (M = <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Ni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">Co</a>) are the dominant commercial sources of arsenic, together with <a href="/wiki/Realgar" title="Realgar">realgar</a> (an arsenic sulfide mineral) and native (elemental) arsenic. An illustrative mineral is <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">arsenopyrite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>As<a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a>), which is structurally related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_pyrite" title="Iron pyrite">iron pyrite</a>. Many minor As-containing minerals are known. Arsenic also occurs in various organic forms in the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geosphere_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-geosphere-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg/250px-World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg/375px-World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg/500px-World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Arsenic_Production_2006.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arsenic output in 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2006_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2006-34">[34]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In 2014, China was the top producer of white arsenic with almost 70% world share, followed by <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, according to the <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2016_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2016-35">[35]</a></sup> Most arsenic refinement operations in the US and Europe have closed over environmental concerns. Arsenic is found of the smelter dust from <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> smelters, and is recovered primarily from copper refinement dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSYB2007_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSYB2007-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy)" title="Roasting (metallurgy)">roasting</a> arsenopyrite in air, arsenic sublimes as arsenic(III) oxide leaving iron oxides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geosphere_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-geosphere-33">[33]</a></sup> while roasting without air results in the production of gray arsenic. Further purification from sulfur and other chalcogens is achieved by <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a> in vacuum, in a hydrogen atmosphere, or by distillation from molten lead-arsenic mixture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="340" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg/220px-Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg/330px-Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mineraly.sk_-_realgar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Realgar</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:82px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="315" data-file-width="365" height="69" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg/80px-Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg/120px-Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg/160px-Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg.png 2x" width="80"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arsenic_alchemical_symbol.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Alchemical_symbol" title="Alchemical symbol">Alchemical symbol</a> for arsenic</div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>arsenic</i> has its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> word <span dir="rtl" lang="arc" title="Aramaic language text">ܠܐ ܙܐܦܢܝܐ</span> <i>(al) zarniqa</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-38">[38]</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> word <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">زرنيخ</span> <i>zarnikh</i>, meaning "yellow" (literally "gold-colored") and hence "(yellow) <a href="/wiki/Orpiment" title="Orpiment">orpiment</a>". It was adopted into <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> as <i>arsenikon</i> (<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἀρσενικόν</span>), a form that is <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>, being the neuter form of the Greek word <i>arsenikos</i> (<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἀρσενικός</span>), meaning "male", "virile". The Greek word was adopted in Latin as <i>arsenicum</i>, which in French became <i>arsenic</i>, from which the English word arsenic is taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etymonline.com_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline.com-38">[38]</a></sup> Arsenic sulfides (orpiment, <a href="/wiki/Realgar" title="Realgar">realgar</a>) and oxides have been known and used since ancient times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curiosa_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curiosa-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zosimos_of_Panopolis" title="Zosimos of Panopolis">Zosimos</a> (circa 300 AD) describes roasting <i>sandarach</i> (realgar) to obtain <i>cloud of arsenic</i> (<a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a>), which he then <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduces</a> to gray arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> As the symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a> are not very specific, it was frequently used for <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> until the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_test" title="Marsh test">Marsh test</a>, a sensitive chemical test for its presence. (Another less sensitive but more general test is the <a href="/wiki/Reinsch_test" title="Reinsch test">Reinsch test</a>.) Owing to its use by the ruling class to murder one another and its potency and discreetness, arsenic has been called the "poison of kings" and the "king of poisons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="640" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg/220px-The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg/330px-The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg/440px-The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Arsenic_Labyrinth_at_Botallack_Mine_-_geograph.org.uk_-_490315.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The arsenic labyrinth, part of <a href="/wiki/Botallack_Mine" title="Botallack Mine">Botallack Mine</a>, Cornwall.</div></div></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, arsenic was often included in <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>, which made the alloy harder (so-called "<a href="/wiki/Arsenical_bronze" title="Arsenical bronze">arsenical bronze</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> (Albert the Great, 1193–1280) is believed to have been the first to isolate the element from a compound in 1250, by heating soap together with <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trisulfide" title="Arsenic trisulfide">arsenic trisulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuildingBlocks451-3_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuildingBlocks451-3-44">[44]</a></sup> In 1649, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Schr%C3%B6der_(physician)" title="Johann Schröder (physician)">Johann Schröder</a> published two ways of preparing arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Crystals of elemental (native) arsenic are found in nature, although rare.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cadet%27s_fuming_liquid" title="Cadet's fuming liquid">Cadet's fuming liquid</a> (impure <a href="/wiki/Cacodyl" title="Cacodyl">cacodyl</a>), often claimed as the first synthetic <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic compound</a>, was synthesized in 1760 by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Claude_Cadet_de_Gassicourt" title="Louis Claude Cadet de Gassicourt">Louis Claude Cadet de Gassicourt</a> by the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_acetate" title="Potassium acetate">potassium acetate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3099" data-file-width="2377" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg/220px-A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg/330px-A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg/440px-A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_chemist_gives_a_demonstration_involving_arsenic_Wellcome_V0011761.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satirical cartoon by <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier" title="Honoré Daumier">Honoré Daumier</a> of a chemist giving a public demonstration of arsenic, 1841</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, "arsenic" ("white arsenic" or arsenic trioxide) was mixed with <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> and eaten by women to improve the <a href="/wiki/Complexion" title="Complexion">complexion</a> of their faces, making their skin paler to show they did not work in the fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Arsenic was also rubbed into the faces and arms of women to "improve their complexion". The accidental use of arsenic in the adulteration of foodstuffs led to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bradford_Sweets_Poisoning" title="The Bradford Sweets Poisoning">the Bradford sweet poisoning</a> in 1858, which resulted in around 20 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Food_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Food-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two arsenic pigments have been widely used since their discovery – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Green" title="Paris Green">Paris Green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scheele%27s_Green" title="Scheele's Green">Scheele's Green</a>. After the toxicity of arsenic became widely known, these chemicals were used less often as pigments and more often as insecticides. In the 1860s, an arsenic byproduct of dye production, London Purple was widely used.  This was a solid mixture of arsenic trioxide, aniline, lime, and ferrous oxide, insoluble in water and very toxic by inhalation or ingestion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> But it was later replaced with Paris Green, another arsenic-based dye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> With better understanding of the toxicology mechanism, two other compounds were used starting in the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Calcium_arsenate" title="Calcium arsenate">Arsenite of lime</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenate_of_lead" title="Arsenate of lead">arsenate of lead</a> were used widely as insecticides until the discovery of <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a> in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural">Agricultural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Agricultural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roxarsone.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1468" data-file-width="1250" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Roxarsone.png/220px-Roxarsone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Roxarsone.png/330px-Roxarsone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Roxarsone.png/440px-Roxarsone.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roxarsone.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Roxarsone" title="Roxarsone">Roxarsone</a> is a controversial arsenic compound used as a feed ingredient for chickens.</div></div></div>
<p>The toxicity of arsenic to <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> led to its use as a wood preservative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In the 1930s, a process of treating wood with <a href="/wiki/Chromated_copper_arsenate" title="Chromated copper arsenate">chromated copper arsenate</a> (also known as CCA or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanalith" title="Tanalith">Tanalith</a>) was invented, and for decades, this treatment was the most extensive industrial use of arsenic. An increased appreciation of the toxicity of arsenic led to a ban of CCA in consumer products in 2004, initiated by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-round_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-round-57">[57]</a></sup> However, CCA remains in heavy use in other countries (such as on Malaysian rubber plantations).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic was also used in various agricultural insecticides and poisons. For example, <a href="/wiki/Lead_hydrogen_arsenate" title="Lead hydrogen arsenate">lead hydrogen arsenate</a> was a common insecticide on <a href="/wiki/Fruit_tree" title="Fruit tree">fruit trees</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> but contact with the compound sometimes resulted in <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a> among those working the sprayers. In the second half of the 20th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monosodium_methyl_arsenate" title="Monosodium methyl arsenate">monosodium methyl arsenate</a> (MSMA) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disodium_methyl_arsenate" title="Disodium methyl arsenate">disodium methyl arsenate</a> (DSMA) – less toxic organic forms of arsenic – replaced lead arsenate in agriculture. These organic arsenicals were in turn phased out by 2013 in all agricultural activities except cotton farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The biogeochemistry of arsenic is complex and includes various adsorption and desorption processes. The toxicity of arsenic is connected to its solubility and is affected by pH. Arsenite (<span class="chemf nowrap">AsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) is more soluble than arsenate (<span class="chemf nowrap">AsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) and is more toxic; however, at a lower pH, arsenate becomes more mobile and toxic. It was found that addition of sulfur, phosphorus, and iron oxides to high-arsenite soils greatly reduces arsenic phytotoxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic is used as a feed additive in <a href="/wiki/Poultry_farming" title="Poultry farming">poultry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pig_farming" title="Pig farming">swine production</a>, in particular in the U.S. to increase weight gain, improve <a href="/wiki/Feed_conversion_ratio" title="Feed conversion ratio">feed efficiency</a>, and to prevent disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> An example is <a href="/wiki/Roxarsone" title="Roxarsone">roxarsone</a>, which had been used as a <a href="/wiki/Broiler" title="Broiler">broiler</a> starter by about 70% of U.S. broiler growers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The Poison-Free Poultry Act of 2009 proposed to ban the use of roxarsone in industrial swine and poultry production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Alpharma, a subsidiary of Pfizer Inc., which produces roxarsone, voluntarily suspended sales of the drug in response to studies showing elevated levels of inorganic arsenic, a carcinogen, in treated chickens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAQ&amp;A_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDAQ&amp;A-65">[65]</a></sup> A successor to Alpharma, <a href="/wiki/Zoetis" title="Zoetis">Zoetis</a>, continues to sell <a href="/wiki/Nitarsone" title="Nitarsone">nitarsone</a>, primarily for use in turkeys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAQ&amp;A_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-FDAQ&amp;A-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic is intentionally added to the feed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chickens" title="Chickens">chickens</a> raised for human consumption. Organic arsenic compounds are less toxic than pure arsenic, and promote the growth of chickens. Under some conditions, the arsenic in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_feed" title="Chicken feed">chicken feed</a> is converted to the toxic inorganic form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2006 study of the remains of the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> racehorse, <a href="/wiki/Phar_Lap" title="Phar Lap">Phar Lap</a>, determined that the 1932 death of the famous champion was caused by a massive overdose of arsenic.  Sydney veterinarian Percy Sykes stated, "In those days, arsenic was quite a common tonic, usually given in the form of a solution (Fowler's Solution) ... It was so common that I'd reckon 90 per cent of the horses had arsenic in their system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_use">Medical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Medical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 18th, 19th, and 20th centuries, a number of arsenic compounds were used as medicines, including <a href="/wiki/Arsphenamine" title="Arsphenamine">arsphenamine</a> (by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich" title="Paul Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a> (by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fowler_(inventor)" title="Thomas Fowler (inventor)">Thomas Fowler</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITOM_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITOM-68">[68]</a></sup> Arsphenamine, as well as <a href="/wiki/Neosalvarsan" title="Neosalvarsan">neosalvarsan</a>, was indicated for <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trypanosomiasis" title="Trypanosomiasis">trypanosomiasis</a>, but has been superseded by modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a>.
</p><p>Arsenic trioxide has been used in a variety of ways over the past 500 years, most commonly in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, but in medications as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Fowler%27s_solution" title="Fowler's solution">Fowler's solution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>  The US <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> in the year 2000 approved this compound for the treatment of patients with <a href="/wiki/Acute_promyelocytic_leukemia" title="Acute promyelocytic leukemia">acute promyelocytic leukemia</a> that is resistant to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-trans_retinoic_acid" title="All-trans retinoic acid">all-trans retinoic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recently, researchers have been locating tumors using arsenic-74 (a positron emitter). This isotope produces clearer <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">PET scan</a> images than the previous radioactive agent, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>-124, because the body tends to transport iodine to the thyroid gland producing signal noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>In subtoxic doses, soluble arsenic compounds act as <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulants</a>, and were once popular in small doses as medicine by people in the mid-18th to 19th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys_2">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main use of arsenic is in alloying with lead. Lead components in <a href="/wiki/Automotive_battery" title="Automotive battery">car batteries</a> are strengthened by the presence of a very small percentage of arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Selective_leaching" title="Selective leaching">Dezincification</a> of brass (a copper-zinc alloy) is greatly reduced by the addition of arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> "Phosphorus Deoxidized Arsenical Copper" with an arsenic content of 0.3% has an increased corrosion stability in certain environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">Gallium arsenide</a> is an important <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> material, used in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>. Circuits made from GaAs are much faster (but also much more expensive) than those made from <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. Unlike silicon, GaAs has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_bandgap" title="Direct bandgap">direct bandgap</a>, and can be used in <a href="/wiki/Laser_diode" title="Laser diode">laser diodes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LEDs</a> to convert <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> energy directly into <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the United States built a stockpile of 20,000 tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">weaponized</a> <a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">lewisite</a> (ClCH=CHAsCl<sub>2</sub>), an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoarsenic" title="Organoarsenic">organoarsenic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicant" title="Vesicant">vesicant</a> (blister agent) and <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lung</a> irritant. The stockpile was neutralized with bleach and dumped into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the United States used <a href="/wiki/Agent_Blue" title="Agent Blue">Agent Blue</a>, a mixture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_cacodylate" title="Sodium cacodylate">sodium cacodylate</a> and its acid form, as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow_herbicides" title="Rainbow herbicides">rainbow herbicides</a> to deprive North Vietnamese soldiers of foliage cover and rice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Copper acetoarsenite was used as a green <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> known under many names, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Green" title="Paris Green">Paris Green</a> and Emerald Green. It caused numerous <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisonings</a>. <a href="/wiki/Scheele%27s_Green" title="Scheele's Green">Scheele's Green</a>, a copper arsenate, was used in the 19th century as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_dye" title="Food dye">coloring agent</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweets" title="Sweets">sweets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></li>
<li>Arsenic is used in <a href="/wiki/Bronzing" title="Bronzing">bronzing</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a>.</li>
<li>As much as 2% of produced arsenic is used in lead alloys for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_shot" title="Lead shot">lead shot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></li>
<li>Arsenic is added in small quantities to alpha-brass to make it <a href="/wiki/Selective_leaching" title="Selective leaching">dezincification-resistant</a>. This grade of brass is used in plumbing fittings and other wet environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></li>
<li>Arsenic is also used for taxonomic sample preservation.</li>
<li>Until recently, arsenic was used in optical glass. Modern glass manufacturers, under pressure from environmentalists, have ceased using both arsenic and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_biochemistry" title="Arsenic biochemistry">Arsenic biochemistry</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacteria">Bacteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Bacteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ArsenobetainePIC.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="78" data-file-width="124" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ArsenobetainePIC.svg/220px-ArsenobetainePIC.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ArsenobetainePIC.svg/330px-ArsenobetainePIC.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ArsenobetainePIC.svg/440px-ArsenobetainePIC.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ArsenobetainePIC.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Arsenobetaine" title="Arsenobetaine">Arsenobetaine</a></div></div></div>
<p>Some species of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> obtain their energy by <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizing</a> various fuels while <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> arsenate to arsenite. Under oxidative environmental conditions some bacteria oxidize arsenite to arsenate as fuel for their metabolism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> involved are known as <a href="/wiki/Arsenate_reductase_(glutaredoxin)" title="Arsenate reductase (glutaredoxin)">arsenate reductases</a> (Arr).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, bacteria were discovered that employ a version of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> in the absence of oxygen with arsenites as <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donors</a>, producing arsenates (just as ordinary photosynthesis uses water as electron donor, producing molecular oxygen). This may be classified as chemolithoautotrophic arsenite oxidation, for which oxygen is used as the terminal electron acceptor, arsenite is the electron donor, and carbon dioxide is the carbon source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Santini_Sly_Schnagl_Macy_pp._92–97_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Santini_Sly_Schnagl_Macy_pp._92–97-85">[85]</a></sup> Researchers conjecture that, over the course of history, these photosynthesizing organisms produced the arsenates that allowed the arsenate-reducing bacteria to thrive. One <a href="/wiki/Strain_(biology)" title="Strain (biology)">strain</a> PHS-1 has been isolated and is related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gammaproteobacterium" title="Gammaproteobacterium">gammaproteobacterium</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectothiorhodospira_shaposhnikovii" title="Ectothiorhodospira shaposhnikovii">Ectothiorhodospira shaposhnikovii</a></i>. The mechanism is unknown, but an encoded Arr enzyme may function in reverse to its known <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Arsenate" title="Arsenate">arsenate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> anions are similar structurally, no evidence exists for the replacement of phosphate in <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> by arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Essential_trace_element_in_higher_animals">Essential trace element in higher animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Essential trace element in higher animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some evidence indicates that arsenic is an essential trace mineral in birds (chickens), and in mammals (rats, hamsters, and goats). However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_function" title="Biological function">biological function</a> is not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heredity">Heredity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Heredity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic has been linked to <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetic changes</a>, heritable changes in gene expression that occur without changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a>. These include DNA methylation, histone modification, and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> interference. Toxic levels of arsenic cause significant DNA hypermethylation of tumor suppressor genes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P16_(gene)" title="P16 (gene)">p16</a> and <a href="/wiki/P53" title="P53">p53</a>, thus increasing risk of <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenesis" title="Carcinogenesis">carcinogenesis</a>. These epigenetic events have been studied <i>in vitro</i> using human <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> cells and <i>in vivo</i> using rat <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> cells and peripheral blood <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leukocytes" title="Leukocytes">leukocytes</a> in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Inductively_coupled_plasma_mass_spectrometry" title="Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry">Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry</a> (ICP-MS) is used to detect precise levels of intracellular arsenic and other arsenic bases involved in epigenetic modification of DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Studies investigating arsenic as an epigenetic factor can be used to develop precise biomarkers of exposure and susceptibility.
</p><p>The Chinese brake fern (<i><a href="/wiki/Pteris_vittata" title="Pteris vittata">Pteris vittata</a></i>) hyperaccumulates arsenic from the soil into its leaves and has a proposed use in <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">phytoremediation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomethylation">Biomethylation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Biomethylation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inorganic arsenic and its compounds, upon entering the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>, are progressively metabolized through a process of <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">methylation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biomethylation_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biomethylation-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> For example, the mold <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scopulariopsis_brevicaulis" title="Scopulariopsis brevicaulis">Scopulariopsis brevicaulis</a></i> produces significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Trimethylarsine" title="Trimethylarsine">trimethylarsine</a> if inorganic arsenic is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The organic compound <a href="/wiki/Arsenobetaine" title="Arsenobetaine">arsenobetaine</a> is found in some marine foods such as fish and algae, and also in mushrooms in larger concentrations. The average person's intake is about 10–50 µg/day. Values about 1000 µg are not unusual following consumption of fish or mushrooms, but there is little danger in eating fish because this arsenic compound is nearly non-toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure">Exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Naturally occurring sources of human exposure include volcanic ash, weathering of minerals and ores, and mineralized groundwater. Arsenic is also found in food, water, soil, and air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Arsenic is absorbed by all plants, but is more concentrated in leafy vegetables, rice, apple and grape juice, and seafood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> An additional route of exposure is inhalation of atmospheric gases and dusts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atsdr.cdc.gov_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-atsdr.cdc.gov-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_in_drinking_water">Occurrence in drinking water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Occurrence in drinking water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_contamination_of_groundwater" title="Arsenic contamination of groundwater">Arsenic contamination of groundwater</a></div>
<p>Extensive arsenic contamination of groundwater has led to widespread <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a> in <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_and_sanitation_in_Bangladesh" title="Water supply and sanitation in Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and neighboring countries. It is estimated that approximately 57 million people in the Bengal basin are drinking <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> with arsenic concentrations elevated above the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>'s standard of 10 <a href='/wiki/Concentration#"Parts-per"_notation' title="Concentration">parts per billion</a> (ppb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> However, a study of cancer rates in Taiwan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> suggested that significant increases in cancer mortality appear only at levels above 150 ppb. The arsenic in the groundwater is of natural origin, and is released from the sediment into the groundwater, caused by the <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">anoxic conditions</a> of the subsurface. This groundwater was used after local and western <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a> and the Bangladeshi government undertook a massive shallow tube <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">well</a> drinking-water program in the late twentieth century. This program was designed to prevent drinking of bacteria-contaminated surface waters, but failed to test for arsenic in the groundwater. Many other countries and districts in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, have geological environments that produce groundwater with a high arsenic content. <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning#Arsenicosis:_chronic_arsenic_poisoning_from_drinking_water" title="Arsenic poisoning">Arsenicosis</a> was reported in <a href="/wiki/Nakhon_Si_Thammarat" title="Nakhon Si Thammarat">Nakhon Si Thammarat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> in 1987, and the <a href="/wiki/Chao_Phraya_River" title="Chao Phraya River">Chao Phraya River</a> probably contains high levels of naturally occurring dissolved arsenic without being a public health problem because much of the public uses bottled water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> In Pakistan, more than 60 million people are exposed to arsenic polluted drinking water indicated by a recent report of <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a>. Podgorski’s team investigated more than 1200 samples and more than 66% samples exceeded the WHO minimum contamination level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, arsenic is most commonly found in the ground waters of the southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-107">[107]</a></sup> Parts of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and the Dakotas are also known to have significant concentrations of arsenic in ground water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> Increased levels of skin cancer have been associated with arsenic exposure in Wisconsin, even at levels below the 10 part per billion drinking water standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> According to a recent film funded by the US <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a>, millions of private wells have unknown arsenic levels, and in some areas of the US, more than 20% of the wells may contain levels that exceed established limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p><p>Low-level exposure to arsenic at concentrations of 100 parts per billion (i.e., above the 10 parts per billion drinking water standard) compromises the initial immune response to <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus_subtype_H1N1" title="Influenza A virus subtype H1N1">H1N1 or swine flu</a> infection according to NIEHS-supported scientists. The study, conducted in laboratory mice, suggests that people exposed to arsenic in their drinking water may be at increased risk for more serious illness or death from the virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Canadians are drinking water that contains inorganic arsenic. Private-dug–well waters are most at risk for containing inorganic arsenic. Preliminary well water analysis typically does not test for arsenic. Researchers at the Geological Survey of Canada have modeled relative variation in natural arsenic hazard potential for the province of New Brunswick. This study has important implications for potable water and health concerns relating to inorganic arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSC-2009_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSC-2009-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>Epidemiological evidence from <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> shows a dose-dependent connection between chronic arsenic exposure and various forms of cancer, in particular when other risk factors, such as cigarette smoking, are present. These effects have been demonstrated at contaminations less than 50 ppb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Arsenic is itself a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalhoutSchulz2011_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p><p>Analyzing multiple epidemiological studies on inorganic arsenic exposure suggests a small but measurable increase in risk for bladder cancer at 10 ppb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> According to Peter Ravenscroft of the Department of Geography at the University of Cambridge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> roughly 80 million people worldwide consume between 10 and 50 ppb arsenic in their drinking water. If they all consumed exactly 10 ppb arsenic in their drinking water, the previously cited multiple epidemiological study analysis would predict an additional 2,000 cases of bladder cancer alone. This represents a clear underestimate of the overall impact, since it does not include lung or skin cancer, and explicitly underestimates the exposure. Those exposed to levels of arsenic above the current WHO standard should weigh the costs and benefits of arsenic remediation.
</p><p>Early (1973) evaluations of the processes for removing dissolved arsenic from drinking water demonstrated the efficacy of co-precipitation with either iron or aluminum oxides. In particular, iron as a coagulant was found to remove arsenic with an efficacy exceeding 90%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Several adsorptive media systems have been approved for use at point-of-service in a study funded by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (US EPA) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF). A team of European and Indian scientists and engineers have set up six arsenic treatment plants in <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> based on in-situ remediation method (SAR Technology). This technology does not use any chemicals and arsenic is left in an insoluble form (+5 state) in the subterranean zone by recharging aerated water into the aquifer and developing an oxidation zone that supports arsenic oxidizing micro-organisms. This process does not produce any waste stream or sludge and is relatively cheap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another effective and inexpensive method to avoid arsenic contamination is to sink wells 500 feet or deeper to reach purer waters. A recent 2011 study funded by the US National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences' Superfund Research Program shows that deep sediments can remove arsenic and take it out of circulation. In this process, called <i>adsorption</i>, arsenic sticks to the surfaces of deep sediment particles and is naturally removed from the ground water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnetic separations of arsenic at very low magnetic field <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradients</a> with high-surface-area and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monodisperse" title="Monodisperse">monodisperse</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Nanocrystal" title="Nanocrystal">nanocrystals</a> have been demonstrated in point-of-use water purification. Using the high specific surface area of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanocrystals, the mass of waste associated with arsenic removal from water has been dramatically reduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>Epidemiological studies have suggested a correlation between chronic consumption of drinking water contaminated with arsenic and the incidence of all leading causes of mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The literature indicates that arsenic exposure is causative in the pathogenesis of diabetes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chaff-based filters have recently been shown to reduce the arsenic content of water to 3 µg/L. This may find applications in areas where the potable water is extracted from underground <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="San_Pedro_de_Atacama">San Pedro de Atacama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: San Pedro de Atacama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For several centuries, the people of <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro_de_Atacama" title="San Pedro de Atacama">San Pedro de Atacama</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> have been drinking water that is contaminated with arsenic, and some evidence suggests they have developed some immunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazard_maps_for_contaminated_groundwater">Hazard maps for contaminated groundwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Hazard maps for contaminated groundwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Around one-third of the world’s population drinks water from groundwater resources. Of this, about 10 percent, approximately 300 million people, obtains water from groundwater resources that are contaminated with unhealthy levels of arsenic or fluoride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> These trace elements derive mainly from minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_transformation_of_arsenic_in_natural_waters">Redox transformation of arsenic in natural waters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Redox transformation of arsenic in natural waters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic is unique among the trace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalloids" title="Metalloids">metalloids</a> and oxyanion-forming trace metals (e.g. As, Se, Sb, Mo, V, Cr, U, Re). It is sensitive to mobilization at pH values typical of natural waters (pH 6.5–8.5) under both oxidizing and reducing conditions. Arsenic can occur in the environment in several oxidation states (−3, 0, +3 and +5), but in natural waters it is mostly found in inorganic forms as oxyanions of trivalent arsenite [As(III)] or pentavalent arsenate [As(V)]. Organic forms of arsenic are produced by biological activity, mostly in surface waters, but are rarely quantitatively important. Organic arsenic compounds may, however, occur where waters are significantly impacted by industrial pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic may be solubilized by various processes. When pH is high, arsenic may be released from surface binding sites that lose their positive charge. When water level drops and <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> minerals are exposed to air, arsenic trapped in sulfide minerals can be released into water. When organic carbon is present in water, bacteria are fed by directly reducing As(V) to As(III) or by reducing the element at the binding site, releasing inorganic arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p><p>The aquatic transformations of arsenic are affected by pH, reduction-oxidation potential, organic matter concentration and the concentrations and forms of other elements, especially iron and manganese. The main factors are pH and the redox potential. Generally, the main forms of arsenic under oxic conditions are H<sub>3</sub>AsO<sub>4</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>AsO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>, HAsO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>, and AsO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> at pH 2, 2–7, 7–11 and 11, respectively. Under reducing conditions, H<sub>3</sub>AsO<sub>4</sub> is predominant at pH 2–9.
</p><p>Oxidation and reduction affects the migration of arsenic in subsurface environments. Arsenite is the most stable soluble form of arsenic in reducing environments and arsenate, which is less mobile than arsenite, is dominant in oxidizing environments at neutral pH. Therefore, arsenic may be more mobile under reducing conditions. The reducing environment is also rich in organic matter which may enhance the solubility of arsenic compounds. As a result, the <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> of arsenic is reduced and dissolved arsenic accumulates in groundwater. That is why the arsenic content is higher in reducing environments than in oxidizing environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of sulfur is another factor that affects the transformation of arsenic in natural water. Arsenic can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> when metal sulfides form. In this way, arsenic is removed from the water and its mobility decreases. When oxygen is present, bacteria oxidize reduced sulfur to generate energy, potentially releasing bound arsenic.
</p><p>Redox reactions involving Fe also appear to be essential factors in the fate of arsenic in aquatic systems. The reduction of iron oxyhydroxides plays a key role in the release of arsenic to water. So arsenic can be enriched in water with elevated Fe concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup>  Under oxidizing conditions, arsenic can be mobilized from <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> or iron oxides especially at elevated pH. Under reducing conditions, arsenic can be mobilized by reductive desorption or dissolution when associated with iron oxides. The reductive desorption occurs under two circumstances. One is when arsenate is reduced to arsenite which adsorbs to iron oxides less strongly.  The other results from a change in the charge on the mineral surface which leads to the desorption of bound arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some species of bacteria catalyze redox transformations of arsenic. Dissimilatory arsenate-respiring prokaryotes (DARP) speed up the reduction of As(V) to As(III). DARP use As(V) as the electron acceptor of anaerobic respiration and obtain energy to survive. Other organic and inorganic substances can be oxidized in this process. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoautotrophic" title="Chemoautotrophic">Chemoautotrophic</a> arsenite oxidizers (CAO) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophic" title="Heterotrophic">heterotrophic</a> arsenite oxidizers (HAO) convert As(III) into As(V). CAO combine the oxidation of As(III) with the reduction of oxygen or nitrate. They use obtained energy to fix produce organic carbon from CO<sub>2</sub>. HAO cannot obtain energy from As(III) oxidation. This process may be an arsenic <a href="/wiki/Detoxification" title="Detoxification">detoxification</a> mechanism for the bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p><p>Equilibrium thermodynamic calculations predict that As(V) concentrations should be greater than As(III) concentrations in all but strongly reducing conditions, i.e. where SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> reduction is occurring.  However, abiotic redox reactions of arsenic are slow. Oxidation of As(III) by dissolved O<sub>2</sub> is a particularly slow reaction. For example, Johnson and Pilson (1975) gave <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-lives</a> for the oxygenation of As(III) in seawater ranging from several months to a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> In other studies, As(V)/As(III) ratios were stable over periods of days or weeks during water sampling when no particular care was taken to prevent oxidation, again suggesting relatively slow oxidation rates. Cherry found from experimental studies that the As(V)/As(III) ratios were stable in anoxic solutions for up to 3 weeks but that gradual changes occurred over longer timescales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> Sterile water samples have been observed to be less susceptible to speciation changes than non-sterile samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup> Oremland found that the reduction of As(V) to As(III) in Mono Lake was rapidly catalyzed by bacteria with rate constants ranging from 0.02 to 0.3 day<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wood_preservation_in_the_US">Wood preservation in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Wood preservation in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2002, US-based industries consumed 19,600 metric tons of arsenic. Ninety percent of this was used for treatment of wood with <a href="/wiki/Chromated_copper_arsenate" title="Chromated copper arsenate">chromated copper arsenate</a> (CCA). In 2007, 50% of the 5,280 metric tons of consumption was still used for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSYB2007_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSYB2007-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> In the United States, the voluntary phasing-out of arsenic in production of consumer products and residential and general consumer construction products began on 31 December 2003, and alternative chemicals are now used, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline_Copper_Quaternary" title="Alkaline Copper Quaternary">Alkaline Copper Quaternary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borate" title="Borate">borates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood_preservation#Copper_azole" title="Wood preservation">copper azole</a>, cyproconazole, and <a href="/wiki/Propiconazole" title="Propiconazole">propiconazole</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although discontinued, this application is also one of the most concern to the general public. The vast majority of older <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber_treatment" title="Timber treatment">pressure-treated</a> wood was treated with CCA. CCA lumber is still in widespread use in many countries, and was heavily used during the latter half of the 20th century as a structural and outdoor <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a>. Although the use of CCA lumber was banned in many areas after studies showed that arsenic could leach out of the wood into the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> (from playground equipment, for instance), a risk is also presented by the burning of older CCA timber. The direct or indirect ingestion of wood ash from burnt CCA lumber has caused fatalities in animals and serious poisonings in humans; the lethal human dose is approximately 20 grams of ash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> Scrap CCA lumber from construction and demolition sites may be inadvertently used in commercial and domestic fires. Protocols for safe disposal of CCA lumber are not consistent throughout the world. Widespread <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> disposal of such timber raises some concern,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> but other studies have shown no arsenic contamination in the groundwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-146"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mapping_of_industrial_releases_in_the_US">Mapping of industrial releases in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Mapping of industrial releases in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One tool that maps the location (and other information) of arsenic releases in the United State is <a href="/wiki/TOXMAP" title="TOXMAP">TOXMAP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> TOXMAP is a Geographic Information System (GIS) from the Division of Specialized Information Services of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">United States National Library of Medicine</a> (NLM) funded by the US Federal Government.  With marked-up maps of the United States, TOXMAP enables users to visually explore data from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s (EPA) <a href="/wiki/Toxics_Release_Inventory" title="Toxics Release Inventory">Toxics Release Inventory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfund_Basic_Research_Program" title="Superfund Basic Research Program">Superfund Basic Research Programs</a>. TOXMAP's chemical and environmental health information is taken from NLM's Toxicology Data Network (TOXNET),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>, and from other authoritative sources.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioremediation">Bioremediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Bioremediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Physical, chemical, and biological methods have been used to remediate arsenic contaminated water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JEM-2012_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-JEM-2012-149">[149]</a></sup> Bioremediation is said to be cost effective and environmentally friendly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> Bioremediation of ground water contaminated with arsenic aims to convert arsenite, the toxic form of arsenic to humans, to arsenate. Arsenate (+5 oxidation state) is the dominant form of arsenic in surface water, while arsenite (+3 oxidation state) is the dominant form in hypoxic to anoxic environments. Arsenite is more soluble and mobile than arsenate. Many species of bacteria can transform arsenite to arsenate in anoxic conditions by using arsenite as an electron donor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> This is a useful method in ground water remediation. Another bioremediation strategy is to use plants that accumulate arsenic in their tissues via <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">phytoremediation</a> but the disposal of contaminated plant material needs to be considered.
</p><p>Bioremediation requires careful evaluation and design in accordance with existing conditions.  Some sites may require the addition of an electron acceptor while others require microbe supplementation (<a href="/wiki/Bioaugmentation" title="Bioaugmentation">bioaugmentation</a>). Regardless of the method used, only constant monitoring can prevent future contamination.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Elemental arsenic and arsenic compounds are classified as "<a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a>" and "dangerous for the environment" in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directive_67/548/EEC" title="Directive 67/548/EEC">directive 67/548/EEC</a>.
The <a href="/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="International Agency for Research on Cancer">International Agency for Research on Cancer</a> (IARC) recognizes arsenic and inorganic arsenic compounds as <a href="/wiki/List_of_IARC_Group_1_carcinogens" title="List of IARC Group 1 carcinogens">group 1 carcinogens</a>, and the EU lists arsenic trioxide, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentoxide" title="Arsenic pentoxide">arsenic pentoxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenate" title="Arsenate">arsenate</a> salts as category 1 <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>.
</p><p>Arsenic is known to cause <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning#Arsenicosis:_chronic_arsenic_poisoning_from_drinking_water" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenicosis</a> when present in drinking water, "the most common species being arsenate [<span class="chemf nowrap">HAsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>; As(V)] and arsenite [H<sub>3</sub>AsO<sub>3</sub>; As(III)]".
</p>
<h3><span id="Legal_limits.2C_food.2C_and_drink"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_limits,_food,_and_drink">Legal limits, food, and drink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Legal limits, food, and drink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States since 2006, the maximum concentration in drinking water allowed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) is 10 ppb<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> and the FDA set the same standard in 2005 for bottled water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda_metals_154-0"><a href="#cite_note-fda_metals-154">[154]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodsafetynews_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-foodsafetynews-155">[155]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (August 2013)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> The Department of Environmental Protection for New Jersey set a drinking water limit of 5 ppb in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">IDLH</a> (immediately dangerous to life and health) value for arsenic metal and inorganic arsenic compounds is 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> (5 ppb). The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> has set the <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> (PEL) to a time-weighted average (TWA) of 0.01 mg/m<sup>3</sup> (0.01 ppb), and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set the <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) to a 15-minute constant exposure of 0.002 mg/m<sup>3</sup> (0.002 ppb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PGCH_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-PGCH-157">[157]</a></sup> The PEL for organic arsenic compounds is a TWA of 0.5 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup> (0.5 ppb).
</p><p>In 2008, based on its ongoing testing of a wide variety of American foods for toxic chemicals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> set the "level of concern" for inorganic arsenic apple and pear juices at 23 ppb, based on non-carcinogenic effects, and began blocking importation of products in excess of this level; it also required recalls for non-conforming domestic products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda_metals_154-1"><a href="#cite_note-fda_metals-154">[154]</a></sup>  In 2011, the national <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dr._Oz" title="Dr. Oz">Dr. Oz</a></i> television show broadcast a program highlighting tests performed by an independent lab hired by the producers. Though the methodology was disputed (it did not distinguish between organic and inorganic arsenic) the tests showed levels of arsenic up to 36 ppb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup>  In response, FDA tested the worst brand from the <i>Dr.</i> <i>Oz</i> show and found much lower levels. Ongoing testing found 95% of the apple juice samples were below the level of concern. Later testing by <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a> showed inorganic arsenic at levels slightly above 10 ppb, and the organization urged parents to reduce consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup>  In July 2013, on consideration of consumption by children, chronic exposure, and carcinogenic effect, the FDA established an "action level" of 10 ppb for apple juice, the same as the drinking water standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda_metals_154-2"><a href="#cite_note-fda_metals-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p><p>Concern about arsenic in rice in Bangladesh was raised in 2002, but at the time only Australia had a legal limit for food (one milligram per kilogram).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup>  Concern was raised about people who were eating U.S. rice exceeding WHO standards for personal arsenic intake in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup>  In 2011, the People's Republic of China set a food standard of 150 ppb for arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States in 2012, testing by separate groups of researchers at the Children's Environmental Health and Disease Prevention Research Center at <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> (early in the year, focusing on urinary levels in children)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a> (in November)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cr_rice_168-0"><a href="#cite_note-cr_rice-168">[168]</a></sup> found levels of arsenic in rice that resulted in calls for the FDA to set limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foodsafetynews_155-1"><a href="#cite_note-foodsafetynews-155">[155]</a></sup> The FDA released some testing results in September 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> and as of July 2013, is still collecting data in support of a new potential regulation. It has not recommended any changes in consumer behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup>
</p><p>Consumer Reports recommended: 
</p>
<ol><li>That the EPA and FDA eliminate arsenic-containing fertilizer, drugs, and pesticides in food production;</li>
<li>That the FDA establish a legal limit for food;</li>
<li>That industry change production practices to lower arsenic levels, especially in food for children; and</li>
<li>That consumers test home water supplies, eat a varied diet, and cook rice with excess water, then draining it off (reducing inorganic arsenic by about one third along with a slight reduction in vitamin content).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cr_rice_168-1"><a href="#cite_note-cr_rice-168">[168]</a></sup></li>
<li>Evidence-based public health advocates also recommend that, given the lack of regulation or labeling for arsenic in the U.S., children should eat no more than 1.5 servings per week of rice and should not drink rice milk as part of their daily diet before age 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeleywellness_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-berkeleywellness-172">[172]</a></sup>  They also offer recommendations for adults and infants on how to limit arsenic exposure from rice, drinking water, and fruit juice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeleywellness_172-1"><a href="#cite_note-berkeleywellness-172">[172]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>A 2014 <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> advisory conference was scheduled to consider limits of 200–300 ppb for rice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cr_rice_168-2"><a href="#cite_note-cr_rice-168">[168]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecotoxicity">Ecotoxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Ecotoxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic is bioaccumulative in many organisms, marine species in particular, but it does not appear to biomagnify significantly in food webs. In polluted areas, plant growth may be affected by root uptake of arsenate, which is a phosphate analog and therefore readily transported in plant tissues and cells. In polluted areas, uptake of the more toxic arsenite ion (found more particularly in reducing conditions) is likely in poorly-drained soils.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_mechanism">Biological mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Biological mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic's toxicity comes from the affinity of arsenic(III) oxides for <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a>. Thiols, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a> residues and <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Lipoic_acid" title="Lipoic acid">lipoic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_A" title="Coenzyme A">coenzyme A</a>, are situated at the active sites of many important <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic disrupts <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> production through several mechanisms. At the level of the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>, arsenic inhibits <a href="/wiki/Lipoic_acid" title="Lipoic acid">lipoic acid</a>, which is a cofactor for <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_dehydrogenase" title="Pyruvate dehydrogenase">pyruvate dehydrogenase</a>. By competing with phosphate, arsenate uncouples <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a>, thus inhibiting energy-linked reduction of <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NAD+</a>, mitochondrial respiration and ATP synthesis. Hydrogen peroxide production is also increased, which, it is speculated, has potential to form reactive oxygen species and oxidative stress. These metabolic interferences lead to death from multi-system <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_failure" title="Organ failure">organ failure</a>. The organ failure is presumed to be from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necrotic" title="Necrotic">necrotic</a> cell death, not <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a>, since energy reserves have been too depleted for apoptosis to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_176-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-176">[176]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although arsenic causes toxicity it can also play a protective role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_risks_and_remediation">Exposure risks and remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Exposure risks and remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Occupational exposure and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a> may occur in persons working in industries involving the use of inorganic arsenic and its compounds, such as wood preservation, glass production, nonferrous metal alloys, and electronic semiconductor manufacturing. Inorganic arsenic is also found in coke oven emissions associated with the smelter industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup>
</p><p>The conversion between As(III) and As(V) is a large factor in arsenic environmental contamination. According to Croal, Gralnick, Malasarn and Newman, "[the] understanding [of] what stimulates As(III) oxidation and/or limits As(V) reduction is relevant for <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> of contaminated sites (Croal). The study of chemolithoautotrophic As(III) oxidizers and the heterotrophic As(V) reducers can help the understanding of the oxidation and/or reduction of arsenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treatment of chronic arsenic poisoning is possible. British anti-lewisite (<a href="/wiki/Dimercaprol" title="Dimercaprol">dimercaprol</a>) is prescribed in doses of 5 mg/kg up to 300 mg every 4 hours for the first day, then every 6 hours for the second day, and finally every 8 hours for 8 additional days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup> However the USA's <a href="/wiki/Agency_for_Toxic_Substances_and_Disease_Registry" title="Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry">Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry</a> (ATSDR) states that the long-term effects of arsenic exposure cannot be predicted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atsdr.cdc.gov_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-atsdr.cdc.gov-101">[101]</a></sup> Blood, urine, hair, and nails may be tested for arsenic; however, these tests cannot foresee possible health outcomes from the exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atsdr.cdc.gov_101-2"><a href="#cite_note-atsdr.cdc.gov-101">[101]</a></sup> Long-term exposure and consequent excretion through urine has been linked to bladder and kidney cancer in addition to cancer of the liver, prostate, skin, lungs, and nasal cavity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup>
</p>
