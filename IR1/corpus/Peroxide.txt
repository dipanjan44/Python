<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Peroxide</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Peroxide_(disambiguation)" title="Peroxide (disambiguation)">Peroxide (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peroxide_group_v.2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="1636" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Peroxide_group_v.2.png/170px-Peroxide_group_v.2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Peroxide_group_v.2.png/255px-Peroxide_group_v.2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Peroxide_group_v.2.png/340px-Peroxide_group_v.2.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peroxide_group_v.2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Types of peroxides, from top to bottom: peroxide ion, <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxide</a>, organic hydroperoxide, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peracid" title="Peracid">peracid</a>. The peroxide group is marked in <b><span style="color:blue;">blue</span></b>. R, R<sup>1</sup> and R<sup>2</sup> mark hydrocarbon <a href="/wiki/Moiety_(chemistry)" title="Moiety (chemistry)">moieties</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Peroxides</b> are a group of compounds with the structure R−O−O−R.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The O−O group in a peroxide is called the <b>peroxide group</b> or <b>peroxo group</b>. In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide ions</a>, the oxygen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> in the peroxide ion have an <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of −1.
</p><p>The most common peroxide is <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), colloquially known simply as "peroxide". It is marketed as a solution in water at various concentrations.  Since hydrogen peroxide is colorless, so are these solutions. It is mainly used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidant" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleaching" title="Bleaching">bleaching</a> agent. However, hydrogen peroxide is also biochemically produced in the human body, largely as a result of a range of <a href="/wiki/Oxidase" title="Oxidase">oxidase</a> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>   Concentrated solutions are potentially dangerous when in contact with organic compounds.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:254px;max-width:254px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H2O2_gas_structure.svg"><img alt="O−O bond length = 147.4 pm O−H bond length = 95.0 pm" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="1011" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/H2O2_gas_structure.svg/250px-H2O2_gas_structure.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/H2O2_gas_structure.svg/375px-H2O2_gas_structure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/H2O2_gas_structure.svg/500px-H2O2_gas_structure.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Structure and dimensions of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.</div></div></div></div>
<p>Aside from hydrogen peroxide, some other major classes of peroxides are these:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Peroxy_acid" title="Peroxy acid">Peroxy acids</a>, the peroxy derivatives of many familiar acids, examples being <a href="/wiki/Peroxymonosulfuric_acid" title="Peroxymonosulfuric acid">peroxymonosulfuric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peracetic_acid" title="Peracetic acid">peracetic acid</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metal_peroxide" title="Metal peroxide">Metal peroxides</a>, examples being <a href="/wiki/Barium_peroxide" title="Barium peroxide">barium peroxide</a> (BaO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_peroxide" title="Sodium peroxide">sodium peroxide</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">Organic peroxides</a>, compounds with the linkage C−O−O−C or C−O−O−H.  One example is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylhydroperoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tert-Butylhydroperoxide (page does not exist)"><i>tert</i>-butylhydroperoxide</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Main_group_peroxides" title="Main group peroxides">Main group peroxides</a>, compounds with the linkage E−O−O−E (E = <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_group_element" title="Main group element">main group element</a>), one example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potassium_peroxydisulfate" title="Potassium peroxydisulfate">potassium peroxydisulfate</a>.</li></ul>
