<p><b>Lanthanum oxide</b>, chemical formula  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> containing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a> <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. It is used in some ferroelectric materials, as a component of optical materials, and is a feedstock for certain catalysts, among other uses.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La2O3powder.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="500" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/160px-La2O3powder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/240px-La2O3powder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/320px-La2O3powder.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La2O3powder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> powder</div></div></div>
<p>Lanthanum oxide is an odorless, white solid that is insoluble in water, but soluble in dilute acid. Depending on the pH of the compound, different crystal structures can be obtained.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="It may not be the pH of the compound itself, rather the pH of the solution used to obtain the solid (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is hygroscopic; under atmosphere, it absorbs moisture over time and converts to lanthanum hydroxide. Lanthanum oxide has p-type semiconducting properties and a band gap of approximately 5.8 eV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>. Its average room temperature resistivity is 10 kΩ·cm, which decreases with an increase in temperature. La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  has the lowest lattice energy of the rare earth oxides, with very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>, ε = 27.  
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At low temperatures, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has an A-M<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> hexagonal crystal structure. The La<sup>3+</sup> metal atoms are surrounded by a 7 coordinate group of O<sup>2−</sup>atoms, the oxygen ions are in an octahedral shape around the metal atom and there is one oxygen ion above one of the octahedral faces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  On the other hand, at high temperatures the Lanthanum oxide converts to a C-M<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> cubic crystal structure.  The La<sup>3+</sup> ion is surrounded by a 6 coordinate group of O<sup>2−</sup> ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements_obtained_from_Lanthana">Elements obtained from Lanthana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elements obtained from Lanthana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several elements were discovered as a consequence of lengthy analysis and decomposition of the ore <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">Gadolinite</a>.  As the ore was progressively analysed, the residue was first given the label <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceria" title="Ceria">ceria</a> and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthana" title="Lanthana">lanthana</a> and then subsequently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">yttria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terbia" title="Terbia">terbia</a>. In order of date discovered, the list of elements includes <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">Cerium</a> 58, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">Lanthanum</a> 57, <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">Erbium</a> 68, <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">Terbium</a> 65, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Yttrium</a> 39, <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">Ytterbium</a> 70, <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">Holmium</a> 67, <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">Thulium</a> 69, <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">Scandium</a> 21, <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">Praseodymium</a> 59, <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">Neodymium</a> 60 and <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">Dysprosium</a> 66.  Several of these new elements were either discovered or isolated by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> in the 1830s and 1840s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum oxide crystallizes as several <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>.
</p><p>To produce hexagonal La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, a 0.1 M solution of LaCl<sub>3</sub> is sprayed onto a preheated substrate, usually made of metal chalcogenides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kale_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kale-5">[5]</a></sup> The process can be viewed as occurring in two steps – hydrolysis followed by dehydration:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LaCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   La(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 HCl</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 La(OH)<sub>3</sub>  →  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>An alternative route to obtaining hexagonal La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> involves precipitation of nominal La(OH)<sub>3</sub> from aqueous solution using a combination of 2.5% NH<sub>3</sub> and the surfactant <a href="/wiki/Sodium_dodecyl_sulfate" title="Sodium dodecyl sulfate">sodium dodecyl sulfate</a> followed by heating and stirring for 24 hours at 80 °C:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LaCl<sub>3</sub>+ 3 H<sub>2</sub>O + 3 NH<sub>3</sub> → La(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</dd>
<dd>LaCl<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O → La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Other routes  include:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 La<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> + 3 CO<sub>2</sub>  → 2 La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 CS<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>2 La<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + heat → 2 La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 SO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum oxide is used as an additive to develop certain ferroelectric materials, such as La-doped Bi<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>12</sub> (BLT).
Lanthanum oxide is used in optical materials; often the optical glasses are doped with La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to improve the glass' refractive index, chemical durability, and mechanical strength.
</p>
<dl><dd>3 B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → 2 La(BO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>When this 1:3 reaction is mixed into a glass composite, the high molecular weight of the lanthanum causes an increase of the homogeneous mixture of the melt which leads to a lower melting point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The addition of the La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to the glass melt leads to a higher glass transition temperature from 658 °C to 679 °C.  The addition also leads to a higher density, microhardness, and refractive index of the glass.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_and_applications">Uses and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is used to make optical glasses, to which this oxide confers increased density, refractive index, and hardness. Together with oxides of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> improves the resistance of the glass to attack by alkali. La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is an ingredient for the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" title="Piezoelectric">piezoelectric</a> and thermoelectric materials. Automobile exhaust-gas converters contain La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is also used in X-ray imaging intensifying screens, phosphors as well as dielectric and conductive ceramics. Gives off bright glow.
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has been examined for the <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_coupling_of_methane" title="Oxidative coupling of methane">oxidative coupling of methane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films can be <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(chemistry)" title="Deposition (chemistry)">deposited</a> by many different methods, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_disposition" title="Chemical vapor disposition">chemical vapor disposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_oxidation" title="Thermal oxidation">thermal oxidation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">sputtering</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spray_pyrolysis" title="Spray pyrolysis">spray pyrolysis</a>. Depositions of these films occur in a temperature range of 250–450 °C. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycrystalline" title="Polycrystalline">Polycrystalline</a> films are formed at 350 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kale_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-kale-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> tungsten electrodes are replacing thoriated tungsten electrodes in <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">Gas tungsten arc welding (TIG)</a> due to safety concerns with thorium's radioactivity.
</p>
