<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oxyanion</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>oxyanion</b>, or <b>oxoanion</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> with the generic formula <span class="chemf nowrap">A<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>x</i></sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>z</i>−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>y</i></sub></span></span> (where A represents a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> and O represents an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom). Oxyanions are formed by a large majority of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The formulae of simple oxyanions are determined by the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a>. The structures of condensed oxyanions can be rationalized in terms of AO<sub><i>n</i></sub> polyhedral units with sharing of corners or edges between polyhedra. The phosphate and polyphosphate esters adenosine monophosphate (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_monophosphate" title="Adenosine monophosphate">AMP</a>), adenosine diphosphate (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP) are important in biology.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monomeric_oxyanions">Monomeric oxyanions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Monomeric oxyanions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The formula of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomeric" title="Monomeric">monomeric</a> oxyanions, <span class="chemf nowrap">AO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>m</i>−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i></sub></span></span>, is dictated by the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of the element A and its position in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>. Elements of the first row are limited to a maximum coordination number of 4. However, none of the first row elements has a monomeric oxyanion with that coordination number. Instead, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_planar" title="Trigonal planar">trigonal planar</a> structure with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%A0_bond" title="Π bond">π bonding</a> between the central atom and the oxygen atoms. This π bonding is favoured by the similarity in size of the central atom and oxygen.
</p><p>The oxyanions of second-row elements in the <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group</a> oxidation state are <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a>. Tetrahedral SiO<sub>4</sub> units are found in <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> minerals, [Mg,Fe]SiO<sub>4</sub>, but the anion does not have a separate existence as the oxygen atoms are surrounded tetrahedrally by cations in the solid state. <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">Phosphate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), and <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) ions can be found as such in various salts. Many oxyanions of elements in lower oxidation state obey the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> and this can be used to rationalize the formulae adopted. For example, chlorine(V) has two valence electrons so it can accommodate three electron pairs from bonds with oxide ions. The charge on the ion is +5 − 3 × 2 = −1, and so the formula is <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. The structure of the ion is predicted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSEPR" title="VSEPR">VSEPR</a> theory to be pyramidal, with three bonding electron pairs and one lone pair. In a similar way,
the oxyanion of chlorine(III) has the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, and is bent with two lone pairs and two bonding pairs.
</p>

<p>In the third and subsequent rows of the periodic table, 6-coordination is possible, but isolated octahedral oxyanions are not known because they would carry too high an electrical charge. Thus molybdenum(VI) does not form <span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>, but forms the tetrahedral <a href="/wiki/Molybdate" title="Molybdate">molybdate</a> anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>. MoO<sub>6</sub> units are found in condensed molybdates. Fully protonated oxyanions with an octahedral structure are found in such species as <span class="chemf nowrap">Sn(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">Sb(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Condensation_reactions">Condensation reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Condensation reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dichromate-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="687" data-file-width="1100" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dichromate-3D-balls.png/150px-Dichromate-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dichromate-3D-balls.png/225px-Dichromate-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dichromate-3D-balls.png/300px-Dichromate-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dichromate-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The dichromate ion; two tetrahedra share one corner</div></div></div>
<p>In aqueous solution, oxyanions with high charge can undergo condensation reactions, such as in the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dichromate" title="Dichromate">dichromate</a> ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">Cr<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">CrO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">Cr<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The driving force for this reaction is the reduction of electrical charge density on the anion and the elimination of the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">H<sup>+</sup> ion</a>. The amount of order in the solution is decreased, releasing a certain amount of <a href="/wiki/Entropy_(order_and_disorder)" title="Entropy (order and disorder)">entropy</a> which makes the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> more negative and favors the forward reaction. It is an example of an <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reaction</a> with the monomeric oxyanion acting as a base and the condensed oxyanion acting as its <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a>. The reverse reaction is a <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> reaction, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_molecule" title="Water molecule">water molecule</a>, acting as a base, is split. Further condensation may occur, particularly with anions of higher charge, as occurs with adenosine phosphates.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structures_and_formulae_of_polyoxyanions">Structures and formulae of polyoxyanions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structures and formulae of polyoxyanions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">Polyoxometalate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="676" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png/200px-Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png/300px-Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png/400px-Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ammonium-metavanadate-chains-3D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Metavanadate chains in ammonium metavanadate</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>polyoxyanion</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> oxyanion in which multiple oxyanion monomers, usually regarded as MO<sub><i>n</i></sub> polyhedra, are joined by sharing corners or edges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When two corners of a polyhedron are shared the resulting structure may be a chain or a ring. Short chains occur, for example, in <a href="/wiki/Polyphosphate" title="Polyphosphate">polyphosphates</a>. Inosilicates, such as <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxenes</a>, have a long chain of SiO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra each sharing two corners. The same structure occurs in so-called meta-vanadates, such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_metavanadate" title="Ammonium metavanadate">ammonium metavanadate</a>, NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>.
</p><p>The formula of the oxyanion <span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> is obtained as follows: each nominal silicon ion (Si<sup>4+</sup>) is attached to two nominal oxide ions (O<sup>2−</sup>) and has a half share in two others. Thus the stoichiometry and charge are given by:
</p>
<dl><dd>Stoichiometry: Si + 2 O + (2 × ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>) O = SiO<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dd>Charge: +4 + (2 × −2) + (2 × (​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> × −2)) = −2.</dd></dl>
<p>A ring can be viewed as a chain in which the two ends have been joined. Cyclic <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acids_and_phosphates" title="Phosphoric acids and phosphates">triphosphate</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">P<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span></span> is an example.
</p><p>When three corners are shared the structure extends into two dimensions. In <a href="/wiki/Amphibole" title="Amphibole">amphiboles</a>, (of which  <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> is an example) two chains are linked together by sharing of a third corner on alternate places along the chain. This results in an ideal formula <span class="chemf nowrap">Si<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">11</sub></span></span> and a linear chain structure which explains the fibrous nature of these minerals. Sharing of all three corners can result in a sheet structure, as in <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">Si<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>, in which each silicon has one oxygen to itself and a half-share in three others. Crystalline mica can be cleaved into very thin sheets.
</p><p>The sharing of all four corners of the tetrahedra results in a 3-dimensional structure, such as in <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aluminosilicate" title="Aluminosilicate">Aluminosilicates</a> are minerals in which some silicon is replaced by aluminium. However, the oxidation state of aluminium is one less than that of silicon, so the replacement must be accompanied by the addition of another cation. The number of possible combinations of such a structure is very large, which is, in part, the reason why there are so many aluminosilicates.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Decavanadate_polyhedra.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1520" data-file-width="1929" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/220px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/330px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Decavanadate_polyhedra.png/440px-Decavanadate_polyhedra.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Decavanadate_polyhedra.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Decavanadate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">V<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">28</sub></span></span></div></div></div>
<p>Octahedral MO<sub>6</sub> units are common in oxyanions of the larger transition metals. Some compounds, such as salts of the chain-polymeric ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">Mo<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span> even contain both tetrahedral and octahedral units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-4">[4]</a></sup> Edge-sharing is common in ions containing octahedral building blocks and the octahedra are usually distorted to reduce the strain at the bridging oxygen atoms. This results in 3-dimensional structures called <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">polyoxometalates</a>. Typical examples occur in the <a href="/wiki/Keggin_structure" title="Keggin structure">Keggin structure</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Phosphomolybdic_acid" title="Phosphomolybdic acid">phosphomolybdate</a> ion. Edge sharing is an effective means of reducing electrical charge density, as can be seen with the hypothetical condensation reaction involving two octahedra:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i>−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> + 4 H<sup>+</sup> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Mo<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">(<i>n</i>−4)−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Here, the average charge on each M atom is reduced by 2. The efficacy of edge-sharing is demonstrated by the following reaction, which occurs when an alkaline aqueous solution of molybdate is acidified.
</p>
<dl><dd>7 <span class="chemf nowrap">MoO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 8 H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">Mo<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">24</sub></span></span> + 4 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The tetrahedral molybdate ion is converted into a cluster of 7 edge-linked octahedra<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> giving an average charge on each molybdenum of ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>6</sup>⁄<sub>7</sub></span>. The heptamolybdate cluster is so stable that clusters with between 2 and 6 molybdate units have not been detected even though they must be formed as intermediates.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heuristic_for_acidity">Heuristic for acidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heuristic for acidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pKa of the related acids can be guessed from the number of double bonds to oxygen. Thus perchloric acid is a very strong acid while hypochlorous acid is very weak. A simple rule usually works to within about 1 pH unit.
</p>
<h2><span id="Acid.E2.80.93base_properties"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid–base_properties">Acid–base properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxyanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acid–base properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most oxyanions are weak <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> and can be protonated to give acids or acid salts. For example, the phosphate ion can be successively protonated to form phosphoric acid.
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>+ H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="944" data-file-width="1100" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png/100px-Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png/150px-Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png/200px-Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sulfuric-acid-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sulfuric acid molecule</div></div></div>
<p>The extent of protonation in aqueous solution will depend on the <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constants</a> and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. For example, AMP (adenosine monophosphate) has a p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> value of 6.21,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> so at pH 7 it will be about 10% protonated. Charge neutralization is an important factor in these protonation reactions. By contrast, the univalent anions <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> ions are very difficult to protonate and so the corresponding acids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acids" title="Strong acids">strong acids</a>.
</p><p>Although acids such as phosphoric acid are written as H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, the protons are attached to oxygen atoms forming hydroxyl groups, so the formula can also be written as <span class="nowrap">OP(OH)<sub>3</sub></span> to better reflect the structure. Sulfuric acid may be written as <span class="nowrap">O<sub>2</sub>S(OH)<sub>2</sub></span>; this is the molecule observed in the gas phase.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphite" title="Phosphite">phosphite</a> ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_base" title="Strong base">strong base</a>, and so always carries at least one proton. In this case the proton is attached directly to the phosphorus atom with the structure <span class="chemf nowrap">HPO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. In forming this ion, the phosphite ion is behaving as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis base</a> and donating a pair of electrons to the Lewis acid, H<sup>+</sup>.
</p>

<ul><li>The chromate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">CrO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>, is the predominant species at high pH.  As pH rises the chromate ion becomes ever more predominant, until it is the only species in solutions with pH &gt; 6.75.</li>
<li>At pH &lt; p<i>K</i><sub>1</sub> the hydrogen chromate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">HCrO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> is predominant in dilute solution.</li>
<li>The dichromate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">Cr<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span>, is predominant in more concentrated solutions, except at high pH.</li></ul>
<p>The species H<sub>2</sub>CrO<sub>4</sub> and <span class="chemf nowrap">HCr<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span> are not shown as they are formed only at very low pH.  
</p><p>Predominance diagrams can become very complicated when many polymeric species can be formed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> such as in <a href="/wiki/Vanadate" title="Vanadate">vanadates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molybdate" title="Molybdate">molybdates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstates</a>. Another complication is that many of the higher polymers are formed extremely slowly, such that equilibrium may not be attained even in months, leading to possible errors in the equilibrium constants and the predominance diagram.
</p>
