<p>The <b>Geologic temperature record</b> are changes in <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a> as determined from <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologic</a> evidence on multi-million to billion (10<sup>9</sup>) year time scales. The study of past temperatures provides an important paleoenvironmental insight because it is a component of the climate and <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> of the time.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleothermometer" title="Paleothermometer">Paleothermometer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_isotope_stage" title="Marine isotope stage">Marine isotope stage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_glaciation" title="Timeline of glaciation">Timeline of glaciation</a></div>
<p>Evidence for past temperatures comes mainly from isotopic considerations (especially <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O"><span class="texhtml">δ</span><sup>18</sup>O</a>); the Mg/Ca ratio of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foramanifera" title="Foramanifera">foram</a> tests, and <a href="/wiki/Alkenone" title="Alkenone">alkenones</a>, are also useful. Often, many are used in conjunction to get a multi-proxy estimate for the temperature. This has proved crucial in studies on glacial/interglacial temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sigmanboyle2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sigmanboyle2000-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_the_temperature_record">Description of the temperature record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description of the temperature record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pleistocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="745" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png/350px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png/525px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png/700px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Five_Myr_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of the past 5 million years of climate history, based on <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">oxygen isotope fractionation</a> in deep sea sediment cores (serving as a proxy for the total global mass of glacial ice sheets), fitted to a model of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> (Lisiecki and Raymo 2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and to the temperature scale derived from <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station#Ice_core_drilling" title="Vostok Station">Vostok</a> ice cores following Petit et al. (1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">last 3 million years</a> have been characterized by cycles of glacials and interglacials within a gradually deepening <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Currently</a>, the Earth is in an interglacial period, beginning about <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">20,000 years ago</a> (20 kya).
</p><p>The cycles of glaciation involve the growth and retreat of continental ice sheets in the Northern Hemisphere and involve fluctuations on a number of time scales, notably on the 21 ky, 41 ky and 100 ky scales. Such cycles are usually interpreted as being <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">driven by</a> predictable changes in the Earth orbit known as <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>.  
At the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Pleistocene" title="Middle Pleistocene">Middle Pleistocene</a> (0.8 million years ago, close to the <a href="/wiki/Brunhes%E2%80%93Matuyama_reversal" title="Brunhes–Matuyama reversal">Brunhes–Matuyama</a> <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">geomagnetic reversal</a>) there has been a <a href="/wiki/100,000-year_problem" title="100,000-year problem">largely unexplained</a> switch in the dominant periodicity of glaciations from the 41 ky to the 100 ky cycle.
</p><p>The gradual intensification of this ice age over the last 3 million years has been associated with declining concentrations of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, though it remains unclear if this change is sufficiently large to have caused the changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperatures</a>. 
Decreased temperatures can cause a decrease in carbon dioxide as, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry%27s_Law" title="Henry's Law">Henry's Law</a>, carbon dioxide is more soluble in colder waters, which may account for 30ppmv of the 100ppmv decrease in carbon dioxide concentration during the last glacial maximum. <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_sigmanboyle2000"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_temperature_record#endnote_sigmanboyle2000">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, the initiation of this deepening phase also corresponds roughly to the closure of the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> by the action of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.  This prevented direct ocean flow between the Pacific and Atlantic, which would have had significant effects on ocean circulation and the distribution of heat.  However, modeling studies have been ambiguous as to whether this could be the direct cause of the intensification of the present ice age.
</p><p>This recent period of cycling climate is part of the more extended ice age that began about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=40">40</a> million years ago</span> with the glaciation of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_Eocene_thermal_maxima">Initial Eocene thermal maxima</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Initial Eocene thermal maxima">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="650" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/300px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/450px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/600px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Climate change during the last 65 million years. The true magnitude of the PETM is likely to be understated in this figure due to coarse sampling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the earliest part of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_period" title="Geologic period">period</a>, a series of abrupt thermal spikes have been observed, lasting no more than a few hundred thousand years.  The most pronounced of these, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> (PETM) is visible in the figure at right.  These are usually interpreted as caused by abrupt releases of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrates" title="Clathrates">clathrates</a> (frozen methane ices that accumulate at the bottom of the ocean), though some scientists dispute that methane would be sufficient to cause the observed changes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  During these events, temperatures in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> may have reached levels more typically associated with modern temperate (i.e. mid-latitude) oceans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
During the PETM, the global mean temperature seems to have risen by as much as 5-8 °C (9-14 °F) to an average temperature as high as 23 °C (73 °F), in contrast to the global average temperature of today at just under 15 °C (60 °F).   Geologists and paleontologists think that during much of the Paleocene and early Eocene, the poles were free of ice caps, and palm trees and crocodiles lived above the Arctic Circle, while much of the continental United States had a sub-tropical environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretaceous_thermal_optimum">Cretaceous thermal optimum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cretaceous thermal optimum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous_Thermal_Maximum" title="Cretaceous Thermal Maximum">Cretaceous Thermal Maximum</a></div>
<p>During the later portion of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>, from <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=66–100">66 to 100</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>, average global temperatures reached their highest level during the last ~200 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renne2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renne2013-6">[6]</a></sup>  This is likely to be the result of a favorable configuration of the continents during this period that allowed for improved circulation in the oceans and discouraged the formation of large scale ice sheet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Perhaps the visible anecdotal evidence of high temperatures during this period was the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Polar_forests_of_the_Cretaceous" title="Polar forests of the Cretaceous">deciduous forests extending all the way to the poles</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluctuations_during_the_remainder_of_the_Phanerozoic">Fluctuations during the remainder of the Phanerozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fluctuations during the remainder of the Phanerozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="726" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/300px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/450px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/600px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>500 Million Years of Climate Change<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eon_(geology)" title="Eon (geology)">eon</a>, encompassing the last 542 million years and almost the entire time since the origination of complex multi-cellular life, has more generally been a period of fluctuating temperature between ice ages, such as the current age, and "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_optima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Climate optima (page does not exist)">climate optima</a>", similar to what occurred in the Cretaceous.  Roughly 4 such cycles have occurred during this time with an approximately 140 million year separation between climate optima.  In addition to the present, ice ages have occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> interval and the late <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a>-early <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a>.  There is also a "cooler" interval during the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and early Cretaceous, with evidence of increased sea ice, but the lack of continents at either pole during this interval prevented the formation of continental ice sheets and consequently this is usually not regarded as a full-fledged ice age.  In between these cold period, warmer conditions were present and often referred to as climate optima.  However, it has been difficult to determine whether these warmer intervals were actually hotter or colder than occurred during the Cretaceous optima.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Proterozoic_ice_ages">Late Proterozoic ice ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late Proterozoic ice ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a> era (<span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=1000–541">1,000 to 541</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>), provides evidence of at least two and possibly more major glaciations.  The more recent of these ice ages, encompassing the Marinoan &amp; Varangian glacial maxima (about <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=560–650">560 to 650</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>), has been proposed as a <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">snowball Earth</a> event with continuous sea ice reaching nearly to the equator.  This is significantly more severe than the ice age during the Phanerozoic.  Because this ice age terminated only slightly before the rapid diversification of life during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a>, it has been proposed that this ice age (or at least its end) created conditions favorable to evolution.  The earlier Sturtian glacial maxima (~730 million years) may also have been a snowball Earth event though this is unproven.
</p><p>The changes that lead to the initiation of snowball Earth events are not well known, but it has been argued that they necessarily lead to their own end.  The widespread sea ice prevents the deposition of fresh carbonates in ocean sediment.  Since such carbonates are part of the natural process for recycling carbon dioxide, short-circuiting this process allows carbon dioxide to accumulate in the atmosphere.  This increases the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> and eventually leads to higher temperatures and the retreat of sea ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eyles2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eyles2004-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_view">Overall view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Overall view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Direct combination of these interpreted geological temperature records is not necessarily valid, nor is their combination with other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">more recent temperature records</a>, which may use different definitions.  Nevertheless, an overall perspective is useful even when imprecise. In this view time is plotted backwards from the present, taken as 2015 CE. It is scaled <i>linear</i> in five separate segments, expanding by about an order of magnitude at each vertical break.  Temperatures in the left-hand panel are very approximate, and best viewed as a qualitative indication only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  Further information is given on the <a href="/wiki/File:All_palaeotemps.png#Summary" title="File:All palaeotemps.png">graph description page</a>.
</p>

<h3><span id="Other_temperature_changes_in_Earth.27s_past"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_temperature_changes_in_Earth's_past">Other temperature changes in Earth's past</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geologic_temperature_record&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other temperature changes in Earth's past">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the Neoproterozoic, evidence of temperature changes and glaciation is usually too scattered and sporadic to draw firm conclusions though it seems likely that temperature fluctuations were also substantial during this period.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Temperature reconstructions based on oxygen and silicon isotopes from rock samples have predicted much hotter Precambrian sea temperatures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These predictions suggest ocean temperatures of 55–85 °C during the period of <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2000–3500">2,000 to 3,500</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span>, followed by cooling to more mild temperatures of between 10-40 °C by <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=1,000">1,000</a> million years ago</span>. <a href="/wiki/Ancestral_sequence_reconstruction" title="Ancestral sequence reconstruction">Reconstructed proteins</a> from Precambrian organisms has also provided evidence that the ancient world was much warmer than today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, other evidence suggests that the period of <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=2000–3000">2,000 to 3,000</a> <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">million years ago</a></span> was very generally colder and more glaciated than the last 500 million years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  This is thought to be the result of <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> radiation approximately 20% lower than today. <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">Solar luminosity</a> was 30% dimmer when the Earth formed 4.5 billion years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and it is expected to increase in luminosity approximately 10% per billion years in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>On very long time scales, the evolution of the sun is also an important factor in determining Earth's climate.  According to standard solar theories, the sun will gradually have increased in brightness as a natural part of its evolution after having started with an intensity approximately 70% of its modern value.  The initially low solar radiation, if combined with modern values of greenhouse gases, would not have been sufficient to allow for liquid oceans on the surface of the Earth.  However, evidence of liquid water at the surface has been demonstrated as far back as <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=4,400">4,400</a> million years ago</span>.  This is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faint_young_sun_paradox" title="Faint young sun paradox">faint young sun paradox</a> and is usually explained by invoking much larger greenhouse gas concentrations in Earth's early history, though such proposals are poorly constrained by existing experimental evidence.
</p>
