<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ocean acidification</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The ongoing decrease in the pH of the Earth's oceans, caused by the uptake of carbon dioxide, and its expected impacts</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png"><img alt="World map showing varying change to pH across different parts of different oceans" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1133" data-file-width="1683" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png/300px-WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png/450px-WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png/600px-WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WOA05_GLODAP_del_pH_AYool.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Estimated change in sea water <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> caused by human created <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a> between the 1700s and the 1990s, from the <a href="/wiki/Global_Ocean_Data_Analysis_Project" title="Global Ocean Data Analysis Project">Global Ocean Data Analysis Project</a> (GLODAP) and the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean_Atlas" title="World Ocean Atlas">World Ocean Atlas</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1974" data-file-width="1423" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg/220px-Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg/330px-Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg/440px-Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acidifiedupwelledwater.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NOAA provides evidence for upwelling of "acidified" water onto the Continental Shelf. In the figure above, note the vertical sections of (A) temperature, (B) aragonite saturation, (C) pH, (D) DIC, and (E) pCO<sub>2</sub> on transect line 5 off Pt. St. George, California. The potential density surfaces are superimposed on the temperature section. The 26.2 potential density surface delineates the location of the first instance in which the undersaturated water is upwelled from depths of 150 to 200 m onto the shelf and outcropping at the surface near the coast. The red dots represent sample locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Ocean acidification</b> is the ongoing decrease in the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s oceans, caused by the uptake of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cald03_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cald03-2">[2]</a></sup> Seawater is slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" title="Basic (chemistry)">basic</a> (meaning pH &gt; 7), and ocean acidification involves a shift towards pH-neutral conditions rather than a transition to acidic conditions (pH &lt; 7).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> An estimated 30–40% of the carbon dioxide from human activity released into the atmosphere dissolves into oceans, rivers and lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millero95_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-millero95-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feely04_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feely04-5">[5]</a></sup> To achieve <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibrium</a>, some of it reacts with the water to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>. Some of the resulting carbonic acid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> dissociate into a <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> ion and a hydrogen ion, thus increasing ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">H<sup>+</sup></a> ion concentration). Between 1751 and 1996, surface ocean pH is estimated to have decreased from approximately 8.25 to 8.14,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacob05_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jacob05-6">[6]</a></sup> representing an increase of almost 30% in H<sup>+</sup> ion concentration in the world's oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18536730_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18536730-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scor-int_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-scor-int-8">[8]</a></sup> Earth System Models project that, within the last decade, ocean acidity exceeded historical analogues<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and, in combination with other ocean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogeochemical" title="Biogeochemical">biogeochemical</a> changes, could undermine the functioning of marine ecosystems and disrupt the provision of many goods and services associated with the ocean beginning as early as 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasing acidity is thought to have a range of potentially harmful consequences for marine organisms, such as depressing metabolic rates and immune responses in some organisms, and causing <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleaching_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bleaching-11">[11]</a></sup> By increasing the presence of free hydrogen ions, the additional carbonic acid that forms in the oceans ultimately results in the conversion of carbonate ions into bicarbonate ions. Ocean <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> (roughly equal to [HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>] + 2[CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>]) is not changed by the process, or may increase over long time periods due to <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonate</a> dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This net decrease in the amount of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> ions available may make it more difficult for marine calcifying organisms, such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>, to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogenic" title="Biogenic">biogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, and such structures become vulnerable to dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-13">[13]</a></sup> Ongoing acidification of the oceans may threaten future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_chains" title="Food chains">food chains</a> linked with the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As members of the <a href="/wiki/InterAcademy_Panel" title="InterAcademy Panel">InterAcademy Panel</a>, 105 <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_sciences" title="Academy of sciences">science academies</a> have issued a statement on ocean acidification recommending that by 2050, global CO<sub>2</sub> emissions be reduced by at least 50% compared to the 1990 level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iap_statement_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-iap_statement-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>While ongoing ocean acidification is at least partially anthropogenic in origin, it has occurred previously in Earth's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The most notable example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> (PETM),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zachos2005_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zachos2005-18">[18]</a></sup> which occurred approximately 56 million years ago when massive amounts of carbon entered the ocean and atmosphere, and led to the dissolution of carbonate sediments in all ocean basins.
</p><p>Ocean acidification has been compared to anthropogenic climate change and called the "evil twin of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPost_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPost-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hennige_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hennige-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and "the other CO<sub>2</sub> problem".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hennige_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hennige-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Freshwater bodies also appear to be acidifying, although this is a more complex and less obvious phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gies2018_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gies2018-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiss2018_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss2018-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_cycle">Carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="806" data-file-width="1155" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/CO2_pump_hg.png/220px-CO2_pump_hg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/CO2_pump_hg.png/330px-CO2_pump_hg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/CO2_pump_hg.png/440px-CO2_pump_hg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> cycle between the atmosphere and the ocean</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> describes the fluxes of carbon dioxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) between the oceans, <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">terrestrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Human activities such as the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> changes have led to a new flux of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> into the atmosphere. About 45% has remained in the atmosphere; most of the rest has been taken up by the oceans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven99_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven99-28">[28]</a></sup> with some taken up by terrestrial plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cramer01_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-cramer01-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="557" data-file-width="698" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg/220px-CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg/330px-CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg/440px-CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CalciumAragoniteSaturation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of (A) aragonite and (B) calcite saturation depth in the global oceans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa2-30">[30]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans,_1880-2012_(US_EPA).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="536" data-file-width="670" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png/220px-Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png/330px-Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png/440px-Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans%2C_1880-2012_%28US_EPA%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Changes_in_aragonite_saturation_of_the_world%27s_oceans,_1880-2012_(US_EPA).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The map was created by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution using Community Earth System Model data. This map was created by comparing average conditions during the 1880s with average conditions during the most recent 10 years (2003–2012). Aragonite saturation has only been measured at selected locations during the last few decades, but it can be calculated reliably for different times and locations based on the relationships scientists have observed among aragonite saturation, pH, dissolved carbon, water temperature, concentrations of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, and other factors that can be measured. This map shows changes in the amount of aragonite dissolved in ocean surface waters between the 1880s and the most recent decade (2003–2012). Aragonite saturation is a ratio that compares the amount of aragonite that is actually present with the total amount of aragonite that the water could hold if it were completely saturated. The more negative the change in aragonite saturation, the larger the decrease in aragonite available in the water, and the harder it is for marine creatures to produce their skeletons and shells. The global map shows changes over time in the amount of aragonite dissolved in ocean water, which is called aragonite saturation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div></div></div>
<p>The carbon cycle involves both <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and inorganic carbon compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate ion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate ion</a>. The inorganic compounds are particularly relevant when discussing ocean acidification for they include many forms of dissolved <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> present in the Earth's oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>When <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> dissolves, it reacts with water to form a balance of ionic and non-ionic chemical species: dissolved free carbon dioxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2(aq)</sub></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>). The ratio of these species depends on factors such as <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, pressure, salinity, pH, and <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> (as shown in a <a href="/wiki/Bjerrum_plot" title="Bjerrum plot">Bjerrum plot</a>). These different forms of <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">dissolved inorganic carbon</a> are transferred from an ocean's surface to its interior by the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">solubility pump</a>.
</p><p>The resistance of an area of ocean to absorbing atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Revelle_factor" title="Revelle factor">Revelle factor</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidification">Acidification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acidification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dissolving <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> in seawater increases the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> ion (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) concentration in the ocean, and thus decreases ocean pH, as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<center> CO<sub>2</sub> <sub>(aq)</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> ⇌ HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + 2 H<sup>+</sup>. </center>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ken_Caldeira" title="Ken Caldeira">Caldeira</a> and Wickett (2003)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cald03_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-cald03-2">[2]</a></sup> placed the rate and magnitude of modern ocean acidification changes in the context of probable historical changes during the last 300 million years.
</p><p>Since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> began, it is estimated that surface ocean pH has dropped by slightly more than 0.1 units on the <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithmic</a> scale of pH, representing about a 29% increase in <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>. It is expected to drop by a further 0.3 to 0.5 pH units<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup> (an additional doubling to tripling of today's <a href="/wiki/Post-industrial_society" title="Post-industrial society">post-industrial</a> acid concentrations) by 2100 as the oceans absorb more anthropogenic <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, the impacts being most severe for <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cald03_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-cald03-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> These changes are predicted to accelerate as more anthropogenic <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is released to the atmosphere and taken up by the oceans. The degree of change to ocean chemistry, including ocean pH, will depend on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_climate_change" title="Mitigation of climate change">mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">emissions pathways</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a-b-2011_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-b-2011-34">[34]</a></sup> taken by society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the largest changes are expected in the future,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-13">[13]</a></sup> a report from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> scientists found large quantities of water undersaturated in <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> are already upwelling close to the Pacific <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> area of North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feeley08_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-feeley08-36">[36]</a></sup> Continental shelves play an important role in marine ecosystems since most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_organisms" title="Marine organisms">marine organisms</a> live or are <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawned</a> there, and though the study only dealt with the area from <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> to <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">Northern California</a>, the authors suggest that other shelf areas may be experiencing similar effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feeley08_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-feeley08-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate">Rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">

<p>One of the first detailed datasets to examine how pH varied over 8 years at a specific north <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_zone" title="Temperate zone">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> location found that acidification had strong links to <i>in situ</i> benthic species dynamics and that the  variation in ocean pH may cause calcareous species to perform more poorly than noncalcareous species in years with low pH and predicts  consequences for near-shore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lovejoy" title="Thomas Lovejoy">Thomas Lovejoy</a>, former chief biodiversity advisor to the World Bank, has suggested that "the acidity of the oceans will more than double in the next 40 years. He says this rate is 100 times faster than any changes in ocean acidity in the last 20 million years, making it unlikely that marine life can somehow adapt to the changes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009ng_acidrate_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009ng_acidrate-44">[44]</a></sup> It is predicted that, by the year 2100, If co-occurring biogeochemical changes influence the delivery of ocean goods and services, then they could also have a considerable effect on human welfare for those who rely heavily on the ocean for food, jobs, and revenues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Current rates of ocean acidification have been compared with the greenhouse event at the Paleocene–Eocene boundary (about 55 million years ago) when surface ocean temperatures rose by 5–6 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>. No catastrophe was seen in surface ecosystems, yet bottom-dwelling organisms in the deep ocean experienced a major extinction. The current acidification is on a path to reach levels higher than any seen in the last 65 million years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and the rate of increase is about ten times the rate that preceded the Paleocene–Eocene mass extinction. The current and projected acidification has been described as an almost unprecedented geological event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yale_ridgwell_acidrate_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-yale_ridgwell_acidrate-47">[47]</a></sup> A National Research Council study released in April 2010 likewise concluded that "the level of acid in the oceans is increasing at an unprecedented rate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> A 2012 paper in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> examined the geological record in an attempt to find a historical analog for current global conditions as well as those of the future. The researchers determined that the current rate of ocean acidification is faster than at any time in the past 300 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>A review by climate scientists at the <a href="/wiki/RealClimate" title="RealClimate">RealClimate</a> blog, of a 2005 report by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> of the UK similarly highlighted the centrality of the <i>rates</i> of change in the present anthropogenic acidification process, writing:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The natural pH of the ocean is determined by a need to balance the deposition and burial of <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> on the sea floor against the influx of <span class="chemf nowrap">Ca<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> into the ocean from dissolving rocks on land, called weathering. These processes stabilize the pH of the ocean, by a mechanism called <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> compensation...The point of bringing it up again is to note that if the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> concentration of the atmosphere changes more slowly than this, as it always has throughout the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station#Ice_core_drilling" title="Vostok Station">Vostok record</a>, the pH of the ocean will be relatively unaffected because <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> compensation can keep up. The [present] fossil fuel acidification is much faster than natural changes, and so the acid spike will be more intense than the earth has seen in at least 800,000 years."</p></blockquote>
<p>In the 15-year period 1995–2010 alone, acidity has increased 6 percent in the upper 100 meters of the Pacific Ocean from Hawaii to Alaska.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010aug_sciam_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010aug_sciam-53">[53]</a></sup> According to a statement in July 2012 by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Lubchenco" title="Jane Lubchenco">Jane Lubchenco</a>, head of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> "surface waters are changing much more rapidly than initial calculations have suggested. It's yet another reason to be very seriously concerned about the amount of carbon dioxide that is in the atmosphere now and the additional amount we continue to put out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPost_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPost-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2013 study claimed acidity was increasing at a rate 10 times faster than in any of the evolutionary crises in Earth's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In a synthesis report published in <i>Science</i> in 2015, 22 leading marine scientists stated that CO<sub>2</sub> from burning fossil fuels is changing the oceans' chemistry more rapidly than at any time since the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Great Dying</a>, Earth's most severe known extinction event, emphasizing that the 2 °C maximum temperature increase agreed upon by governments reflects too small a cut in emissions to prevent "dramatic impacts" on the world's oceans, with lead author Jean-Pierre Gattuso remarking that "The ocean has been minimally considered at previous climate negotiations. Our study provides compelling arguments for a radical change at the UN conference (in Paris) on climate change".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rate at which ocean acidification will occur may be influenced by the rate of surface ocean warming, because the chemical equilibria that govern seawater pH are temperature-dependent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-56">[56]</a></sup> Greater seawater warming could lead to a smaller change in pH for a given increase in CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calcification">Calcification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Calcification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changes in ocean chemistry can have extensive direct and indirect effects on organisms and their habitats. One of the most important repercussions of increasing ocean acidity relates to the production of shells and plates out of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> This process is called calcification and is important to the biology and survival of a wide range of marine organisms. Calcification involves the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of dissolved ions into solid <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a>. After they are formed, such structures are vulnerable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolution</a> unless the surrounding seawater contains <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturating</a> concentrations of carbonate ions (CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="540" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg/220px-Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg/330px-Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg/440px-Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate_system_of_seawater.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bjerrum_plot" title="Bjerrum plot">Bjerrum plot</a>: Change in carbonate system of seawater from ocean acidification.</div></div></div>
<p>Of the extra carbon dioxide added into the oceans, some remains as dissolved carbon dioxide, while the rest contributes towards making additional bicarbonate (and additional carbonic acid). This also increases the concentration of hydrogen ions, and the percentage increase in hydrogen is larger than the percentage increase in bicarbonate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-57">[57]</a></sup> creating an imbalance in the reaction HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> ⇌ CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup>. To maintain chemical equilibrium, some of the carbonate ions already in the ocean combine with some of the hydrogen ions to make further bicarbonate. Thus the ocean's concentration of carbonate ions is reduced, creating an imbalance in the reaction Ca<sup>2+</sup> + CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> ⇌ CaCO<sub>3</sub>, and making the dissolution of formed <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> structures more likely.
</p><p>The increase in concentrations of dissolved carbon dioxide and bicarbonate, and reduction in carbonate, are shown in a <a href="/wiki/Bjerrum_plot" title="Bjerrum plot">Bjerrum plot</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saturation_state">Saturation state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saturation state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Here Ω is the product of the concentrations (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_(chemistry)" title="Activity (chemistry)">activities</a>) of the reacting ions that form the mineral (<span class="chemf nowrap">Ca<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), divided by the product of the concentrations of those ions when the mineral is at <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">sp</sub></span></span>), that is, when the mineral is neither forming nor dissolving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In seawater, a natural horizontal boundary is formed as a result of temperature, pressure, and depth, and is known as the saturation horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> Above this saturation horizon, Ω has a value greater than 1, and <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> does not readily dissolve. Most calcifying organisms live in such waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> Below this depth, Ω has a value less than 1, and <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> will dissolve. However, if its production rate is high enough to offset dissolution, <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> can still occur where Ω is less than 1. The <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_compensation_depth" title="Carbonate compensation depth">carbonate compensation depth</a> occurs at the depth in the ocean where production is exceeded by dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The decrease in the concentration of CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> decreases Ω, and hence makes <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> dissolution more likely.
</p><p>Calcium carbonate occurs in two common <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> (crystalline forms): <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>. Aragonite is much more soluble than calcite, so the aragonite saturation horizon is always nearer to the surface than the calcite saturation horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> This also means that those organisms that produce aragonite may be more vulnerable to changes in ocean acidity than those that produce calcite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-13">[13]</a></sup> Increasing <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> levels and the resulting lower pH of seawater decreases the saturation state of <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> and raises the saturation horizons of both forms closer to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> This decrease in saturation state is believed to be one of the main factors leading to decreased calcification in marine organisms, as the inorganic precipitation of <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> is directly proportional to its saturation state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_impacts">Possible impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Possible impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Increasing acidity has possibly harmful consequences, such as depressing metabolic rates in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jumbo_squid" title="Jumbo squid">jumbo squid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosa_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa-62">[62]</a></sup> depressing the immune responses of blue mussels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bibby_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bibby-63">[63]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a>. However it may benefit some species, for example increasing the growth rate of the sea star, <i><a href="/wiki/Pisaster_ochraceus" title="Pisaster ochraceus">Pisaster ochraceus</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gooding_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gooding-64">[64]</a></sup> while shelled plankton species may flourish in altered oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>The report "Ocean Acidification Summary for Policymakers 2013" describes research findings and possible impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_on_oceanic_calcifying_organisms">Impacts on oceanic calcifying organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impacts on oceanic calcifying organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">natural absorption of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a> by the world's oceans helps mitigate the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climatic</a> effects of anthropogenic emissions of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, it is believed that the resulting decrease in pH will have negative consequences, primarily for oceanic <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcifying</a> organisms. These span the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> from <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> to <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a> and include organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">coccolithophores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacea" title="Crustacea">crustaceans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> As described above, under normal conditions, calcite and aragonite are stable in surface waters since the carbonate ion is at <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturating</a> concentrations. However, as ocean pH falls, the concentration of carbonate ions required for saturation to occur increases, and when carbonate becomes undersaturated, structures made of calcium carbonate are vulnerable to dissolution. Therefore, even if there is no change in the rate of calcification, the rate of dissolution of calcareous material increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Corals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gatt98_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-gatt98-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gatt99_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-gatt99-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lan05_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-lan05-71">[71]</a></sup> coccolithophore algae,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rieb00_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-rieb00-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zond01_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-zond01-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zond02_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-zond02-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delille05_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-delille05-75">[75]</a></sup> coralline algae,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuffner_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-kuffner-76">[76]</a></sup> foraminifera,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-catalyst-2007-09-13-OAtbgws_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-catalyst-2007-09-13-OAtbgws-77">[77]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaz07_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaz07-78">[78]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pteropod" title="Pteropod">pteropods</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orr05_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-orr05-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-comeau09_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-comeau09-79">[79]</a></sup> experience reduced calcification or enhanced dissolution when exposed to elevated <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> published a comprehensive overview of ocean acidification, and its potential consequences, in June 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> However, some studies have found different response to ocean acidification, with coccolithophore calcification and photosynthesis both increasing under elevated atmospheric pCO<sub>2</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buitenhuis99_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-buitenhuis99-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nimer93_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-nimer93-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iglesias2008_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iglesias2008-82">[82]</a></sup> an equal decline in primary production and calcification in response to elevated CO<sub>2</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciandra03_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciandra03-83">[83]</a></sup> or the direction of the response varying between species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-langer06_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-langer06-84">[84]</a></sup> A study in 2008 examining a <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sediment core</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a> found that while the species composition of coccolithophorids has remained unchanged for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial</a> period 1780 to 2004, the calcification of coccoliths has increased by up to 40% during the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iglesias2008_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iglesias2008-82">[82]</a></sup> A 2010 study from <a href="/wiki/Stony_Brook_University" title="Stony Brook University">Stony Brook University</a> suggested that while some areas are overharvested and other fishing grounds are being restored, because of ocean acidification it may be impossible to bring back many previous shellfish populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stony2010_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stony2010-85">[85]</a></sup> While the full ecological consequences of these changes in calcification are still uncertain, it appears likely that many calcifying species will be adversely affected.
</p><p>When exposed in experiments to pH reduced by 0.2 to 0.4, larvae of a temperate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittlestar" title="Brittlestar">brittlestar</a>, a relative of the common sea star, fewer than 0.1 percent survived more than eight days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010aug_sciam_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-2010aug_sciam-53">[53]</a></sup> There is also a suggestion that a decline in the coccolithophores may have secondary effects on climate, contributing to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> by decreasing the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> via their effects on <a href="/wiki/CLAW_hypothesis" title="CLAW hypothesis">oceanic cloud cover</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rutt06_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-rutt06-86">[86]</a></sup>  All marine ecosystems on Earth will be exposed to changes in acidification and several other ocean biogeochemical changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The fluid in the internal compartments where corals grow their <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> is also extremely important for calcification growth. When the saturation rate of aragonite in the external seawater is at ambient levels, the corals will grow their aragonite crystals rapidly in their internal compartments, hence their exoskeleton grows rapidly. If the level of aragonite in the external seawater is lower than the ambient level, the corals have to work harder to maintain the right balance in the internal compartment. When that happens, the process of growing the crystals slows down, and this slows down the rate of how much their exoskeleton is growing. Depending on how much aragonite is in the surrounding water, the corals may even stop growing because the levels of aragonite are too low to pump into the internal compartment. They could even dissolve faster than they can make the crystals to their skeleton, depending on the aragonite levels in the surrounding water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Under the current progression of carbon emissions, around 70% of North Atlantic cold-water corals will be living in corrosive waters by 2050-60.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perez2018_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perez2018-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Oceanographic_Institution" title="Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution">Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution</a> in January 2018 showed that the skeletal growth of corals under acidified conditions is primarily affected by a reduced capacity to build dense exoskeletons, rather than affecting the  linear extension of the exoskeleton. Using Global Climate Models, they show that the density of some species of corals could be reduced by over 20% by the end of this century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>An <i>in situ</i> experiment  on a 400 m<sup>2</sup> patch of the Great Barrier Reef to decrease seawater CO<sub>2</sub> level (raise pH) to close to the preindustrial value showed a 7% increase in net calcification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright2016_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albright2016-90">[90]</a></sup>
A similar experiment to raise <i>in situ</i> seawater seawater CO<sub>2</sub> level (lower pH) to a level expected soon after the middle of this century found that net calcification decreased 34%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright2018_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albright2018-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ocean acidification may force some organisms to reallocate resources away from productive endpoints such as growth in order to maintain calcification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_et_al_2008_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_et_al_2008-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some places carbon dioxide bubbles out from the sea floor, locally changing the pH and other aspects of the chemistry of the seawater. Studies of these carbon dioxide seeps have documented a variety of responses by different organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18536730_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18536730-7">[7]</a></sup>  Coral reef communities located near carbon dioxide seeps are of particular interest because of the sensitivity of some corals species to acidification.  In <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>, declining pH caused by carbon dioxide seeps is associated with declines in coral species diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>  However, in <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a> carbon dioxide seeps are not associated with reduced species diversity of corals, although bioerosion of coral skeletons is much higher at low pH sites.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_biological_impacts">Other biological impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other biological impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aside from the slowing and/or reversing of calcification, organisms may suffer other adverse effects, either indirectly through negative impacts on food resources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_33-6"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-33">[33]</a></sup> or directly as reproductive or physiological effects. For example, the elevated oceanic levels of CO<sub>2</sub> may produce <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>-induced acidification of body fluids, known as <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>. Also, increasing ocean acidity is believed to have a range of direct consequences. For example, increasing acidity has been observed to: reduce metabolic rates in jumbo squid;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosa_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa-62">[62]</a></sup> depress the immune responses of blue mussels;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bibby_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bibby-63">[63]</a></sup> and make it harder for juvenile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clownfish" title="Clownfish">clownfish</a> to tell apart the smells of non-predators and predators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dixson_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dixson-94">[94]</a></sup> or hear the sounds of their predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-95">[95]</a></sup> This is possibly because ocean acidification may alter the <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustic</a> properties of seawater, allowing sound to propagate further, and increasing ocean noise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hester_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hester-96">[96]</a></sup> This impacts all animals that use sound for <a href="/wiki/Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation">echolocation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whale_song" title="Whale song">communication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr-threat_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-threat-97">[97]</a></sup> Atlantic longfin squid eggs took longer to hatch in acidified water, and the squid's <a href="/wiki/Statocyst" title="Statocyst">statolith</a> was smaller and malformed in animals placed in sea water with a lower pH. The lower PH was simulated with 20-30 times the normal amount of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> However, as with calcification, as yet there is not a full understanding of these processes in marine organisms or <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ocean_Acidification_and_Krill_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ocean_Acidification_and_Krill-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another possible effect would be an increase in <a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">red tide</a> events, which could contribute to the accumulation of toxins (<a href="/wiki/Domoic_acid" title="Domoic acid">domoic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brevetoxin" title="Brevetoxin">brevetoxin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxitoxin" title="Saxitoxin">saxitoxin</a>) in small organisms such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchovies" title="Anchovies">anchovies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>, in turn increasing occurrences of <a href="/wiki/Amnesic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Amnesic shellfish poisoning">amnesic shellfish poisoning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Neurotoxic shellfish poisoning">neurotoxic shellfish poisoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paralytic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Paralytic shellfish poisoning">paralytic shellfish poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ocean_Acidification_Promotes_Disruptive_and_Harmful_Algal_Blooms_on_Our_Coasts_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ocean_Acidification_Promotes_Disruptive_and_Harmful_Algal_Blooms_on_Our_Coasts-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_impacts_amplified_by_ocean_warming_and_deoxygenation">Ecosystem impacts amplified by ocean warming and deoxygenation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ecosystem impacts amplified by ocean warming and deoxygenation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the full implications of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> on marine ecosystems are still being documented, there is a substantial body of research showing that a combination of ocean acidification and elevated ocean temperature, driven mainly by CO<sub>2</sub> and other greenhouse gas emissions, have a compounded effect on marine life and the ocean environment.  This effect far exceeds the individual harmful impact of either.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>  In addition, ocean warming exacerbates <a href="/wiki/Ocean_deoxygenation" title="Ocean deoxygenation">ocean deoxygenation</a>, which is an additional stressor on marine organisms, by increasing ocean stratification, through density and solubility effects, thus limiting nutrients,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> while at the same time increasing metabolic demand.
</p><p>Meta analyses have quantified the direction and magnitude of the harmful effects of ocean acidification, warming and deoxygenation on the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>  These meta-analyses have been further tested by mesocosm studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> that simulated the interaction of these stressors and found a catastrophic effect on the marine food web, i.e. that the increases in consumption from thermal stress more than negates any primary producer to herbivore increase from elevated CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonbiological_impacts">Nonbiological impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nonbiological impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leaving aside direct biological effects, it is expected that ocean acidification in the future will lead to a significant decrease in the burial of carbonate sediments for several centuries, and even the dissolution of existing carbonate sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridgwell07_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-ridgwell07-111">[111]</a></sup> This will cause an elevation of ocean <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a>, leading to the enhancement of the ocean as a reservoir for CO<sub>2</sub> with implications for climate change as more CO<sub>2</sub> leaves the atmosphere for the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tyrrell08_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-tyrrell08-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_human_industry">Impact on human industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Impact on human industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The threat of acidification includes a decline in <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial fisheries</a> and in the Arctic tourism industry and economy. Commercial fisheries are threatened because acidification harms calcifying organisms which form the base of the Arctic food webs.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pteropoda" title="Pteropoda">Pteropods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brittle_star" title="Brittle star">brittle stars</a> both form the base of the Arctic <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a> and are both seriously damaged from acidification. Pteropods shells dissolve with increasing acidification and the brittle stars lose muscle mass when re-growing appendages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> For pteropods to create shells they require aragonite which is produced through carbonate ions and dissolved calcium. Pteropods are severely affected because increasing acidification levels have steadily decreased the amount of water supersaturated with carbonate which is needed for aragonite creation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biogeosciences_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-biogeosciences-114">[114]</a></sup> Arctic waters are changing so rapidly that they will become undersaturated with aragonite as early as 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biogeosciences_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-biogeosciences-114">[114]</a></sup> Additionally the brittle star's eggs die within a few days when exposed to expected conditions resulting from Arctic acidification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> Acidification threatens to destroy Arctic food webs from the base up. Arctic food webs are considered simple, meaning there are few steps in the food chain from small organisms to larger predators. For example, pteropods are "a key prey item of a number of higher predators – larger plankton, fish, seabirds, whales".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Both pteropods and sea stars serve as a substantial food source and their removal from the simple food web would pose a serious threat to the whole ecosystem. The effects on the calcifying organisms at the base of the food webs could potentially destroy fisheries. The value of fish caught from US commercial fisheries in 2007 was valued at $3.8 billion and of that 73% was derived from calcifiers and their direct predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> Other organisms are directly harmed as a result of acidification. For example, decrease in the growth of marine calcifiers such as the <a href="/wiki/American_lobster" title="American lobster">American lobster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arctica_islandica" title="Arctica islandica">ocean quahog</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallops</a> means there is less shellfish meat available for sale and consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Red king crab fisheries are also at a serious threat because crabs are calcifiers and rely on carbonate ions for shell development. Baby red king crab when exposed to increased acidification levels experienced 100% mortality after 95 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> In 2006, red king crab accounted for 23% of the total guideline harvest levels and a serious decline in red crab population would threaten the crab harvesting industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup>  Several ocean goods and services are likely to be undermined by future ocean acidification potentially affecting the livelihoods of some 400 to 800 million people depending upon the emission scenario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mora_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mora-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_indigenous_peoples">Impact on indigenous peoples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Impact on indigenous peoples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Acidification could damage the Arctic tourism economy and affect the way of life of indigenous peoples. A major pillar of Arctic tourism is the sport fishing and hunting industry. The sport fishing industry is threatened by collapsing food webs which provide food for the prized fish. A decline in tourism lowers revenue input in the area, and threatens the economies that are increasingly dependent on tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> The rapid decrease or disappearance of marine life could also affect the diet of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous peoples</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_responses">Possible responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Possible responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Save_our_ocean,_People%27s_Climate_March,_29_April_2017_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2218" data-file-width="1568" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Save_our_ocean%2C_People%27s_Climate_March%2C_29_April_2017_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Save_our_ocean,_People%27s_Climate_March,_29_April_2017_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Demonstrator calling for action against ocean acidification at the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Climate_March_(2017)" title="People's Climate March (2017)">People's Climate March (2017)</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_CO2_emissions">Reducing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Reducing CO2 emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Members of the <a href="/wiki/InterAcademy_Panel" title="InterAcademy Panel">InterAcademy Panel</a> recommended that by 2050, global anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions be reduced less than 50% of the 1990 level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iap_statement_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-iap_statement-16">[16]</a></sup> The 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iap_statement_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-iap_statement-16">[16]</a></sup> statement also called on world leaders to:</p><blockquote>
<ul><li>Acknowledge that ocean acidification is a direct and real consequence of increasing atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations, is already having an effect at current concentrations, and is likely to cause grave harm to important marine ecosystems as CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations reach 450 [parts-per-million (ppm)] and above;</li>
<li>... Recognise that reducing the build up of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere is the only practicable solution to mitigating ocean acidification;</li>
<li>... Reinvigorate action to reduce stressors, such as <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">pollution</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a> to increase resilience to ocean acidification.</li></ul></blockquote>
<p>Stabilizing atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations at 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> would require near-term emissions reductions, with steeper reductions over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup>
</p><p>
The <a href="/wiki/German_Advisory_Council_on_Global_Change" title="German Advisory Council on Global Change">German Advisory Council on Global Change</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wbgu_ocean_acidification_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-wbgu_ocean_acidification-123">[123]</a></sup> stated:</p><blockquote><p>In order to prevent disruption of the calcification of marine organisms and the resultant risk of fundamentally altering marine food webs, the following guard rail should be obeyed: the pH of near surface waters should not drop more than 0.2 units below the pre-industrial average value in any larger ocean region (nor in the global mean).</p></blockquote>
<p>One policy target related to ocean acidity is the magnitude of future global warming. Parties to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC) adopted a target of limiting warming to below 2 °C, relative to the pre-industrial level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfccc_2 degrees_celsius_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-unfccc_2_degrees_celsius-124">[124]</a></sup> Meeting this target would require substantial reductions in anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p><p>Limiting global warming to below 2 °C would imply a reduction in surface ocean pH of 0.16 from pre-industrial levels. This would represent a substantial decline in surface ocean pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-good_2 degrees_celsius_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-good_2_degrees_celsius-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 25, 2015, USEPA denied<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> a June 30, 2015, citizens petition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> that asked EPA to regulate CO<sub>2</sub> under <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Toxic_Substances_Control_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Toxic Substances Control Act (page does not exist)">TSCA</a> in order to mitigate ocean acidification. In the denial, EPA said that risks from ocean acidification were being "more efficiently and effectively addressed" under domestic actions, e.g., under the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Climate_Action_Plan" title="Presidential Climate Action Plan">Presidential Climate Action Plan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> and that multiple avenues are being pursued to work with and in other nations to reduce emissions and deforestation and promote clean energy and energy efficiency.
</p><p>On March 28, 2017 the US by executive order rescinded the Climate Action Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> On June 1, 2017 it was announced the US would <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">withdraw from the Paris accords</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> and on June 12, 2017 that the US would abstain from the G7 Climate Change Pledge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> two major international efforts to reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_engineering">Climate engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Climate engineering</a> (mitigating temperature or pH effects of emissions) has been proposed as a possible response to ocean acidification. The IAP (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iap_statement_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-iap_statement-16">[16]</a></sup> statement cautioned against climate engineering as a policy response:</p><blockquote><p>Mitigation approaches such as adding chemicals to counter the effects of acidification are likely to be expensive, only partly effective and only at a very local scale, and may pose additional unanticipated risks to the marine environment. There has been very little research on the feasibility and impacts of these approaches. Substantial research is needed before these techniques could be applied.</p></blockquote>
<p>Reports by the WGBU (2006),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wbgu_ocean_acidification_123-1"><a href="#cite_note-wbgu_ocean_acidification-123">[123]</a></sup> the UK's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> (2009),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> (2011)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> warned of the potential risks and difficulties associated with climate engineering.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_fertilization">Iron fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_acidification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Iron fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">Iron fertilization</a> of the ocean could stimulate photosynthesis in <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_hypothesis" title="Iron hypothesis">Iron hypothesis</a>). The phytoplankton would convert the ocean's dissolved carbon dioxide into <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> and oxygen gas, some of which would sink into the deeper ocean before oxidizing. More than a dozen open-sea experiments confirmed that adding iron to the ocean increases <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> in phytoplankton by up to 30 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> While this approach has been proposed as a potential solution to the ocean acidification problem, mitigation of surface ocean acidification might increase acidification in the less-inhabited deep ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cao_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-cao-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p><p>A report by the UK's Royal Society (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> reviewed the approach for effectiveness, affordability, timeliness and safety. The rating for affordability was "medium", or "not expected to be very cost-effective". For the other three criteria, the ratings ranged from "low" to "very low" (i.e., not good). For example, in regards to safety, the report found a "[high] potential for undesirable ecological side effects", and that ocean fertilization "may increase anoxic regions of ocean ('<a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">dead zones</a>')".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uk_royal_society_table_2.8_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-uk_royal_society_table_2.8-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p>
