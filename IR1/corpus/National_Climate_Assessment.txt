<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">National Climate Assessment</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the more general term, see <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_climate_assessments&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National climate assessments (page does not exist)">National climate assessments</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>National Climate Assessment</b> (<b>NCA</b>) is a United States government interagency ongoing effort<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP2012_factsheet_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP2012_factsheet-1">[1]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> science conducted under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Global_Change_Research_Act_of_1990" title="Global Change Research Act of 1990">Global Change Research Act of 1990</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The NCA is a major product<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCANet_2012poster_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCANet_2012poster-4">[4]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program</a> (USGCRP) which coordinates a team of experts and receives input from a Federal Advisory Committee. The Fourth National Climate Assessment was released in early November, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For the Third National Climate Assessment, released in 2014, USGCRP coordinated hundreds of experts and received advice from a sixty-member <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Advisory_Committee" title="Federal Advisory Committee">Federal Advisory Committee</a>. NCA research is integrated and summarized in the mandatory ongoing National Climate Assessment Reports. The reports are "extensively reviewed by the public and experts, including federal agencies and a panel of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.
</p><p>The First National Climate Assessment was published in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA_2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCA_2000-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[Notes 1]</a></sup> Between 2002 and 2009, USGCRP previously known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="U.S. Climate Change Science Program">U.S. Climate Change Science Program</a> (CCSP), produced twenty-one Synthesis and Assessment Products (SAPs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalChange_other_assessments_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalChange_other_assessments-8">[7]</a></sup>  The second NCA was published in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalChange_other_assessments_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalChange_other_assessments-8">[7]</a></sup> and the third was released in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA2013_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCA2013-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>
NCA's overarching goal according to their May 20, 2011 engagement strategy summary, "is to enhance the ability of the U.S. to anticipate, mitigate, and adapt to changes in the global environment (NCA 2011:2)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA2011_summary_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCA2011_summary-10">[9]</a></sup> </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The vision is to advance an inclusive, broad based, and sustained process for assessing and communicating scientific knowledge of the impacts, risks, and vulnerabilities associated with a changing global climate in support of decision-making across the U.S.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>NCA May 20, 2011 page2</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>According to the USGCRP official website the NCA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP_about_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP_about-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Informs the nation about already observed changes, the current status of the climate, and anticipated trends for the future; integrates scientific information from multiple sources and sectors to highlight key findings and significant gaps in our knowledge; establishes consistent methods for evaluating climate impacts in the U.S. in the context of broader global change, and provides input to Federal science priorities and is used by U.S. citizens, communities, and businesses as they create more sustainable and environmentally sound plans for the nation’s future.
</p></blockquote>
<p>In 2013, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama%E2%80%99s_2013_climate_action_plan" title="Barack Obama’s 2013 climate action plan">President's Climate Action Plan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> released by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_Office_of_the_President" title="Executive Office of the President">Executive Office of the President</a> specifically noted the importance of the National Climate Assessments in achieving the goal of "Using Sound Science to Manage Climate Impacts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The next assessment is scheduled to be released in 2018. On August 18, 2017 a 15-member advisory committee that was tasked with writing "concrete guidance" based on the assessment was disbanded. NOAA said that the disbandment of the committee will not "impact the completion of the Fourth National Climate Assessment." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Change_Research_Act">Global Change Research Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Global Change Research Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Climate Assessment (NCA) is conducted under the auspices of the Global Change Research Act of 1990. The GCRA requires a report to the President and the Congress every four years that integrates, evaluates, and interprets the findings of the U.S. Global Change Research Program (USGCRP); analyzes the effects of global change on the natural environment, agriculture, energy production and use, land and water resources, transportation, human health and welfare, human social systems, and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biological diversity</a>; and analyzes current trends in global change, both human-induced and natural, and projects major trends for the subsequent 25 to 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Federal government is responsible for producing these reports through the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program (USGCRP)</a>, a collaboration of 13 Federal agencies and departments.
</p>
<h2><span id="National_Climate_Assessment_and_Development_Advisory_Committee_.28NCADAC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Climate_Assessment_and_Development_Advisory_Committee_(NCADAC)">National Climate Assessment and Development Advisory Committee (NCADAC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: National Climate Assessment and Development Advisory Committee (NCADAC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Climate Assessment and Development Advisory Committee (NCADAC) was a 60-person U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Advisory_Committee" title="Federal Advisory Committee">Federal Advisory Committee</a> which oversaw the development of the draft Third NCA report and made recommendations about the ongoing assessment process. The committee was sun-set in fall 2014. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a> established the NCADAC in December 2010 as per the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Advisory_Committee_Act" title="Federal Advisory Committee Act">Federal Advisory Committee Act</a> (1972). 1972. The NCADAC was supported through the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The successor  <i>Advisory Committee for the Sustained National Climate Assessment</i>, established in 2015, was sunsetted by the Trump administration on August 20, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp170820_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wp170820-17">[16]</a></sup> The fifteen-member committee was chaired by <a href="/wiki/Richard_H._Moss" title="Richard H. Moss">Richard H. Moss</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="National_Assessment_Synthesis_Team_.28NAST.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Assessment_Synthesis_Team_(NAST)">National Assessment Synthesis Team (NAST)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National Assessment Synthesis Team (NAST)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1998, the first <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Assessment_Synthesis_Team&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Assessment Synthesis Team (page does not exist)">National Assessment Synthesis Team</a> (NAST) was formed under the auspices of the Subcommittee on Global Change Research (SGCR), through the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Committee_on_Environmental_and_Natural_Resources&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Committee on Environmental and Natural Resources (page does not exist)">Committee on Environmental and Natural Resources</a> (CENR) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_and_Technology_Council" title="National Science and Technology Council">National Science and Technology Council</a> (NSTC) with members from "government, academia, and private enterprise." Its mandate was to broadly "design and conduct" "national efforts to assess the consequences of climate variability and climate change for the United States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> NAST is an advisory committee chartered under the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Advisory_Committee_Act" title="Federal Advisory Committee Act">Federal Advisory Committee Act</a> to help the US Global Change Research Program fulfill its legal mandate under the Global Change Research Act of 1990. The <a href="/wiki/National_Science_and_Technology_Council" title="National Science and Technology Council">National Science and Technology Council</a> forwarded the report to the President and Congress for their consideration as required by the Global Change Research Act. Administrative support for the US Global Change Research Program was provided by the <a href="/wiki/University_Corporation_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="University Corporation for Atmospheric Research">University Corporation for Atmospheric Research</a>, which was sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA_2000_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCA_2000-6">[6]</a></sup>  In their 2001 assessment, the NAST concluded in the United States, "natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> appear to be the most vulnerable to the harmful effects of climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAST_2001_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAST_2001-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> In their 2001 report they also described long-term major trends in climate change in the twenty-first century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAST_2001_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NAST_2001-19">[18]</a></sup> The first NAST co-chairs were Dr. Jerry M. Melillo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAST_2001_chairs_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAST_2001_chairs-21">[20]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Biological_Laboratory" title="Marine Biological Laboratory">Marine Biological Laboratory</a> in Woods Hole, Massachusetts, Tony Janetos, and Thomas Karl.
</p>
<h2><span id="First_National_Climate_Assessment_.28NCA1.29_2000"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_National_Climate_Assessment_(NCA1)_2000">First National Climate Assessment (NCA1) 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First National Climate Assessment (NCA1) 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The First National Climate Assessment prepared by National Assessment Synthesis Team (NAST), entitled "Climate Change Impacts on the United States: the Potential Consequences of Climate Variability and Change", was released in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA_2000_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NCA_2000-6">[6]</a></sup> The first National Assessment on Climate Change (NACC) was published in 2000. The report was a multidisciplinary effort to study and portray in regional detail the potential effects of human-induced global warming on the United States. The project was articulated into some 20 regional studies - each involving dozens of scientific and academic experts as well as representatives of industry and environmental groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCCRP_assessments_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-USCCRP_assessments-22">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Second_National_Climate_Assessment_.28NCA2.29_2009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_National_Climate_Assessment_(NCA2)_2009">Second National Climate Assessment (NCA2) 2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second National Climate Assessment (NCA2) 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Second National Climate Assessment, entitled "Global Climate Change Impacts in the United States", was published in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalChange_other_assessments_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalChange_other_assessments-8">[7]</a></sup> In addition to synthesizing, evaluating, and reporting on what was known about the potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consequences_of_climate_change" title="Consequences of climate change">consequences of climate change</a>, the report also sought to identify potential measures to adapt to climate change and to identify the highest research priorities for the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCCRP_2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USCCRP_2009-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Third_National_Climate_Assessment_.28NCA3.29_2014"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_National_Climate_Assessment_(NCA3)_2014">Third National Climate Assessment (NCA3) 2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Third National Climate Assessment (NCA3) 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Third National Climate Assessment report entitled "Global Climate Change Impacts in the United States"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCA2014_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCA2014-24">[23]</a></sup> was delivered to the Federal Government for review in 2013 and became available to the public in May 2014.
</p><p>The Third NCA report was written by more than 300 authors drawn from academia; local, state, tribal, and Federal governments; and the private and nonprofit sectors. The NCADAC selected these authors based on criteria that included expertise, experience, and ensuring a variety of perspectives.
</p><p>After review by the NCADAC, the draft Third NCA report was released for public review and comment on January 14, 2013.  By the time the public comment period closed on April 12, 2013, more than 4000 comments had been received from 644 government, non-profit, and commercial sector employees, educators, students, and the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USCCRP_2013_newsletter_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-USCCRP_2013_newsletter-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Concurrently, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a>, part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Academies_of_Science" title="National Academies of Science">National Academy of Sciences</a>, reviewed the draft and submitted feedback. The NCADAC produced a final draft of their report and provided it to the federal government for review in late fall of 2013; a final public version of the report was released on May 6, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>  A number of derivative products, including a printed “Highlights” document, have been produced in addition to the full interactive electronic NCA document that is available on the web.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Third_National_Climate_Assessment_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Third_National_Climate_Assessment-29">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Fourth_National_Climate_Assessment_.28NCA4.29_2017"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourth_National_Climate_Assessment_(NCA4)_2017">Fourth National Climate Assessment (NCA4) 2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fourth National Climate Assessment (NCA4) 2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Climate Science Special Report, Volume 1 of the Fourth National Climate Assessment, was released in November 2017 and described itself as "an authoritative assessment of the science of climate change, with a focus on the United States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Change_Information_System">Global Change Information System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Global Change Information System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program (USGCRP)</a> has established the <a href="/wiki/Global_Change_Information_System" title="Global Change Information System">Global Change Information System (GCIS)</a> to better coordinate and integrate the use of federal information products on changes in the global environment and the implications of those changes for society. The GCIS is an open-source, web-based resource for traceable, sound global change data, information, and products. Designed for use by scientists, decision makers, and the public, the GCIS provides coordinated links to a select group of information products produced, maintained, and disseminated by government agencies and organizations. As well as guiding users to global change research products selected by the 13 member agencies, the GCIS serves as a key access point to assessments, reports, and tools produced by the USGCRP. The GCIS is managed, integrated, and curated by USGCRP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NCAnet">NCAnet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Climate_Assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: NCAnet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In preparation for the 2014 NCA, the USGCRP began in 2011 to call for wider participation and reinforced the long-term goal of improving climate literacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGCRP2012_factsheet_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGCRP2012_factsheet-1">[1]</a></sup> Recruitment began in 2011 for NCAnet, a network of organizations working with the NCA, to further engage producers and users of assessment information across the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NACnet_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-NACnet-34">[33]</a></sup> NCAnet was officially established and registered at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a> on April 13, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCAnet2012_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCAnet2012-35">[34]</a></sup>
