<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Steam reforming</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Steam reforming</b> or <b>steam methane reforming</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a> for producing <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. This is achieved in a processing device called a reformer which reacts steam at high temperature and pressure with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> catalyst. The steam methane reformer is widely used in industry to make <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.  There is also interest in the development of much smaller units based on similar technology to produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> as a feedstock for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Small-scale steam reforming units to supply <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a> are currently the subject of research and development, typically involving the reforming of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, but other fuels are also being considered such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autogas" title="Autogas">autogas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_reforming">Industrial reforming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Industrial reforming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Steam reforming of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> is the most common method of producing commercial bulk hydrogen at about 95% of the world production<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ogden_1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ogden_1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> of 500 billion m<sup>3</sup> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Hydrogen is used in the <a href="/wiki/Ammonia_production" title="Ammonia production">industrial synthesis of ammonia</a> and other chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At high temperatures (700 – 1100 °C) and in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>), steam reacts with methane to yield <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and hydrogen. 
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">CH<sub>4</sub></a> + <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a> ⇌ <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a> + 3 <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>2</sub></a></dd></dl>
<p>Catalysts with high <a href="/wiki/Surface-area-to-volume_ratio" title="Surface-area-to-volume ratio">surface-area-to-volume ratio</a> are preferred because of <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> limitations due to high <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>. Examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> shapes used are spoked wheels, gear wheels, and rings with holes. Additionally, these shapes have a low <a href="/wiki/Pressure_drop" title="Pressure drop">pressure drop</a> which is advantageous for this application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Additional hydrogen can be obtained by reacting the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a> with water via the <a href="/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction" title="Water-gas shift reaction">water-gas shift reaction</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The first reaction is strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> (consumes heat, ΔH<sub>r</sub>= 206 kJ/mol), the second reaction is mildly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> (produces heat, ΔH<sub>r</sub>= -41 kJ/mol).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> produces nine million tons of hydrogen per year, mostly with steam reforming of natural gas. The worldwide ammonia production, using hydrogen derived from steam reforming, was 144 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_tonnes" title="Metric tonnes">metric tonnes</a> in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>This steam reforming process is quite different from and not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_reforming" title="Catalytic reforming">catalytic reforming</a> of <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> process that also produces significant amounts of hydrogen along with high <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane</a> <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.
</p><p>Steam reforming of natural gas is approximately 65–75% efficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autothermal_reforming">Autothermal reforming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Autothermal reforming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Autothermal reforming (ATR) uses oxygen and carbon dioxide or steam in a reaction with methane to form <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>. The reaction takes place in a single chamber where the methane is partially oxidized. The reaction is exothermic due to the oxidation. When the ATR uses carbon dioxide the H<sub>2</sub>:CO ratio produced is 1:1; when the ATR uses steam the H<sub>2</sub>:CO ratio produced is 2.5:1
</p><p>The reactions can be described in the following equations, using CO<sub>2</sub>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2CH<sub>4</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub>  → 3H<sub>2</sub> + 3CO + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>And using steam:
</p>
<dl><dd>4CH<sub>4</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → 10H<sub>2</sub> + 4CO</dd></dl>
<p>The outlet temperature of the syngas is between 950-1100 C and outlet pressure can be as high as 100 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main difference between SMR and ATR is that SMR only uses oxygen via air for combustion as a heat source to create steam, while ATR directly combusts oxygen. The advantage of ATR is that the H<sub>2</sub>:CO can be varied, this is particularly useful for producing certain second generation <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">DME</a> which requires a 1:1 H<sub>2</sub>:CO ratio.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_oxidation">Partial oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Partial oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Partial_oxidation" title="Partial oxidation">Partial oxidation</a></div><p>Partial oxidation (POX) is a type of chemical reaction. It occurs when a substoichiometric fuel-air mixture is partially combusted in a reformer, creating a hydrogen-rich syngas which can then be put to further use.
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The capital cost of steam reforming plants is prohibitive for small to medium size applications because the technology does not scale down well. Conventional steam reforming plants operate at pressures between 200 and 600 psi with outlet temperatures in the range of 815 to 925 °C. However, analyses have shown that even though it is more costly to construct, a well-designed SMR can produce hydrogen more cost-effectively than an ATR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reforming_for_combustion_engines">Reforming for combustion engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reforming for combustion engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gas_flare" title="Gas flare">Flared gas</a> and vented <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">VOCs</a> are known problems in the offshore industry and in the on-shore oil and gas industry, since both emit unnecessary greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Reforming for combustion engines utilizes steam reforming technology for converting waste gases into a source of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reforming for combustion engines is based on steam reforming, where non-methane hydrocarbons (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMHC" title="NMHC">NMHCs</a>) of low quality gases are converted to <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">synthesis gas</a> (H<sub>2</sub> + CO) and finally to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) - thereby improving the fuel gas quality (methane number).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast to conventional steam reforming, the process is operated at lower temperatures and with lower steam supply, allowing a high content of methane (CH<sub>4</sub>) in the produced fuel gas. The main reactions are:
</p><p>Steam reforming: 
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub><i>m</i></sub> + <i>n</i> H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ (<i>n</i> + ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup><i>m</i></sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>) H<sub>2</sub> + <i>n</i> CO</dd></dl>
<p>Methanation: 
</p>
<dl><dd>CO + 3 H<sub>2</sub> ⇌ CH<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Water-gas shift: 
</p>
<dl><dd>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ H<sub>2</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reforming_for_fuel_cells">Reforming for fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reforming for fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_of_reforming_for_supplying_fuel_cells">Advantages of reforming for supplying fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Advantages of reforming for supplying fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Steam reforming of gaseous hydrocarbons is seen as a potential way to provide fuel for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a>. The basic idea for vehicle on-board reforming is that for example a <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> tank and a steam reforming unit would replace the bulky pressurized <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_tank" title="Hydrogen tank">hydrogen tanks</a> that would otherwise be necessary. This might mitigate the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_highway" title="Hydrogen highway">distribution problems</a> associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_car" title="Hydrogen car">hydrogen vehicles</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> however the major market players discarded the approach of on-board reforming as impractical.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (At high temperatures see above).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages_of_reforming_for_supplying_fuel_cells">Disadvantages of reforming for supplying fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disadvantages of reforming for supplying fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The reformer–fuel-cell system is still being researched but in the near term, systems would continue to run on existing fuels, such as natural gas or gasoline or diesel. However, there is an active debate about whether using these fuels to make hydrogen is beneficial while global warming is an issue. Fossil fuel reforming does not eliminate carbon dioxide release into the atmosphere but reduces the carbon dioxide emissions and nearly eliminates carbon monoxide emissions as compared to the burning of conventional fuels due to increased efficiency and fuel cell characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> However, by turning the release of carbon dioxide into a <a href="/wiki/Point_source" title="Point source">point source</a> rather than distributed release, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> becomes a possibility, which would prevent the carbon dioxide's release to the atmosphere, while adding to the cost of the process.
</p><p>The cost of hydrogen production by reforming fossil fuels depends on the scale at which it is done, the capital cost of the reformer and the efficiency of the unit, so that whilst it may cost only a few dollars per kilogram of hydrogen at industrial scale, it could be more expensive at the smaller scale needed for fuel cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_challenges_with_reformers_supplying_fuel_cells">Current challenges with reformers supplying fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam_reforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current challenges with reformers supplying fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>However, there are several challenges associated with this technology:
</p>
<ul><li>The reforming reaction takes place at high temperatures, making it slow to start up and requiring costly high temperature materials.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">Sulfur</a> compounds in the fuel will poison certain catalysts, making it difficult to run this type of system from ordinary <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. Some new technologies have overcome this challenge with sulfur-tolerant catalysts.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coking" title="Coking">Coking</a> would be another cause of catalyst deactivation during steam reforming. High reaction temperatures, low steam-to-carbon ratio (S/C), and the complex nature of sulfur-containing commercial hydrocarbon fuels make coking especially favorable. Olefins, typically ethylene, and aromatics are well known carbon-precursors, hence their formation must be reduced during the SR. Additionally, catalysts with lower acidity were reported to be less prone to coking by suppressing dehydrogenation reactions. H2S, the main product in the reforming of organic sulfur, can bind to all transition metal catalysts to form metal–sulfur bonds and subsequently reduce catalyst activity by inhibiting the chemisorption of reforming reactants. Meanwhile, the adsorbed sulfur species increases the catalyst acidity, and hence indirectly promotes coking. Precious metal catalysts such as Rh and Pt have lower tendencies to form bulk sulfides than other metal catalysts such as Ni. Rh and Pt are less prone to sulfur poisoning by only chemisorbing sulfur rather than forming metal sulfides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Low temperature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymer_fuel_cell" title="Polymer fuel cell">polymer fuel cell</a> membranes can be poisoned by the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO) produced by the reactor, making it necessary to include complex CO-removal systems. <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_fuel_cell" title="Solid oxide fuel cell">Solid oxide fuel cells</a> (SOFC) and <a href="/wiki/Molten_carbonate_fuel_cell" title="Molten carbonate fuel cell">molten carbonate fuel cells</a> (MCFC) do not have this problem, but operate at higher temperatures, slowing start-up time, and requiring costly materials and bulky insulation.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_efficiency" title="Thermodynamic efficiency">thermodynamic efficiency</a> of the process is between 70% and 85% (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">LHV basis</a>) depending on the purity of the hydrogen product.</li></ul>
