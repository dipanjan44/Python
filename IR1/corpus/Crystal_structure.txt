<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Crystal structure</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

The values for the H–O–H angle and O–H distance have come from <i>Physics of Ice</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_of_Ice_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_of_Ice-1">[1]</a></sup> with uncertainties of ±1.5° and ±0.005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">Å</a>, respectively. The white box in (c) is the unit cell defined by Bernal and Fowler<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a>, <b>crystal structure</b> is a description of the ordered arrangement of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline material</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solid_State_Physics_2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solid_State_Physics_2010-3">[3]</a></sup> Ordered structures occur from the intrinsic nature of the constituent particles to form symmetric patterns that repeat along the principal directions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space_(mathematics)" title="Three-dimensional space (mathematics)">three-dimensional space</a> in matter.
</p><p>The smallest group of particles in the material that constitutes the repeating pattern is the <b>unit cell</b> of the structure. The unit cell completely defines the symmetry and structure of the entire crystal lattice, which is built up by repetitive <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">translation</a> of the unit cell along its principal axes.  The repeating patterns are said to be located at the points of the <a href="/wiki/Bravais_lattice" title="Bravais lattice">Bravais lattice</a>.
</p><p>The lengths of the principal axes, or edges, of the unit cell and the angles between them are the <a href="/wiki/Lattice_constant" title="Lattice constant">lattice constants</a>, also called <i>lattice parameters</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetry</a> properties of the crystal are described by the concept of <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space groups</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solid_State_Physics_2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Solid_State_Physics_2010-3">[3]</a></sup> All possible symmetric arrangements of particles in three-dimensional space may be described by the 230 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_groups" title="Space groups">space groups</a>.
</p><p>The crystal structure and symmetry play a critical role in determining many physical properties, such as <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)" title="Cleavage (crystal)">cleavage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">electronic band structure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crystal_optics" title="Crystal optics">optical transparency</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unit_cell">Unit cell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Unit cell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crystal structure is described in terms of the geometry of arrangement of particles in the unit cell. The unit cell is defined as the smallest repeating unit having the full symmetry of the crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The geometry of the unit cell is defined as a <a href="/wiki/Parallelepiped" title="Parallelepiped">parallelepiped</a>, providing six lattice parameters taken as the lengths of the cell edges (<i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, <i>c</i>) and the angles between them (α, β, γ).  The positions of particles inside the unit cell are described by the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_coordinates" title="Fractional coordinates">fractional coordinates</a> (<i>x<sub>i</sub></i>, <i>y<sub>i</sub></i>, <i>z<sub>i</sub></i>) along the cell edges, measured from a reference point. It is only necessary to report the coordinates of a smallest asymmetric subset of particles. This group of particles may be chosen so that it occupies the smallest physical space, which means that not all particles need to be physically located inside the boundaries given by the lattice parameters. All other particles of the unit cell are generated by the symmetry operations that characterize the symmetry of the unit cell. The collection of symmetry operations of the unit cell is expressed formally as the <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> of the crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-group_symmetry_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space-group_symmetry-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miller_indices">Miller indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Miller indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="570" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg/250px-Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg/375px-Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg/500px-Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miller_Indices_Cubes.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Planes with different Miller indices in cubic crystals</div></div></div>
<p>Vectors and planes in a crystal lattice are described by the three-value <a href="/wiki/Miller_index" title="Miller index">Miller index</a> notation. This syntax uses the indices <i>ℓ</i>, <i>m</i>, and <i>n</i> as directional orthogonal parameters, which are separated by 90°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1991_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1991-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>By definition, the syntax (<i>ℓmn</i>) denotes a plane that intercepts the three points <i>a</i><sub>1</sub>/<i>ℓ</i>, <i>a</i><sub>2</sub>/<i>m</i>, and <i>a</i><sub>3</sub>/<i>n</i>, or some multiple thereof. That is, the Miller indices are proportional to the inverses of the intercepts of the plane with the unit cell (in the basis of the lattice vectors). If one or more of the indices is zero, it means that the planes do not intersect that axis (i.e., the intercept is "at infinity"). A plane containing a coordinate axis is translated so that it no longer contains that axis before its Miller indices are determined. The Miller indices for a plane are <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integers</a> with no common factors. Negative indices are indicated with horizontal bars, as in (1<span style="text-decoration:overline;">2</span>3). In an orthogonal coordinate system for a cubic cell, the Miller indices of a plane are the Cartesian components of a vector normal to the plane.
</p><p>Considering only (<i>ℓmn</i>) planes intersecting one or more lattice points (the <i>lattice planes</i>), the distance <i>d</i> between adjacent lattice planes is related to the (shortest) <a href="/wiki/Reciprocal_lattice" title="Reciprocal lattice">reciprocal lattice</a> vector orthogonal to the planes by the formula

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planes_and_directions">Planes and directions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Planes and directions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The crystallographic directions are geometric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)" title="Line (mathematics)">lines</a> linking nodes (<a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>) of a crystal. Likewise, the crystallographic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">planes</a> are geometric <i>planes</i> linking nodes. Some directions and planes have a higher density of nodes. These high density planes have an influence on the behavior of the crystal as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solid_State_Physics_2010_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Solid_State_Physics_2010-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">Optical properties</a>: <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">Refractive index</a> is directly related to density (or periodic density fluctuations).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">Adsorption</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a>: Physical adsorption and chemical reactions occur at or near surface atoms or molecules. These phenomena are thus sensitive to the density of nodes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">Surface tension</a>: The condensation of a material means that the atoms, ions or molecules are more stable if they are surrounded by other similar species. The surface tension of an interface thus varies according to the density on the surface.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cristal_densite_surface.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="201" data-file-width="212" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Cristal_densite_surface.svg/300px-Cristal_densite_surface.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Cristal_densite_surface.svg/450px-Cristal_densite_surface.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Cristal_densite_surface.svg/600px-Cristal_densite_surface.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cristal_densite_surface.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dense crystallographic planes</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Microstructural <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">defects</a>: <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">Pores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crystallite" title="Crystallite">crystallites</a> tend to have straight grain boundaries following higher density planes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)" title="Cleavage (crystal)">Cleavage</a>: This typically occurs preferentially parallel to higher density planes.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_deformation" title="Plastic deformation">Plastic deformation</a>: <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">Dislocation</a> glide occurs preferentially parallel to higher density planes. The perturbation carried by the dislocation (<a href="/wiki/Burgers_vector" title="Burgers vector">Burgers vector</a>) is along a dense direction. The shift of one node in a more dense direction requires a lesser distortion of the crystal lattice.</li></ul>
<p>Some directions and planes are defined by symmetry of the crystal system. In monoclinic, rhombohedral, tetragonal, and trigonal/hexagonal systems there is one unique axis (sometimes called the <b>principal axis</b>) which has higher <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">rotational symmetry</a> than the other two axes. The <b>basal plane</b> is the plane perpendicular to the principal axis in these crystal systems. For triclinic, orthorhombic, and cubic crystal systems the axis designation is arbitrary and there is no principal axis.
</p>

</p>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_by_symmetry">Classification by symmetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification by symmetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crystal_system" title="Crystal system">Crystal system</a></div>
<p>The defining property of a crystal is its inherent symmetry, by which we mean that under certain 'operations' the crystal remains unchanged. All crystals have translational symmetry in three directions, but some have other symmetry elements as well. For example, rotating the crystal 180° about a certain axis may result in an atomic configuration that is identical to the original configuration. The crystal is then said to have a twofold rotational symmetry about this axis. In addition to rotational symmetries like this, a crystal may have symmetries in the form of mirror planes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translational_symmetries" title="Translational symmetries">translational symmetries</a>, and also the so-called "compound symmetries," which are a combination of translation and rotation/mirror symmetries. A full classification of a crystal is achieved when all of these inherent symmetries of the crystal are identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lattice_systems">Lattice systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lattice systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lattice_system" title="Lattice system">lattice systems</a> are a grouping of crystal structures according to the axial system used to describe their lattice. Each lattice system consists of a set of three axes in a particular geometric arrangement. There are seven lattice systems. They are similar to but not quite the same as the seven <a href="/wiki/Crystal_system" title="Crystal system">crystal systems</a>.

<p>The simplest and most symmetric, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_(crystal_system)" title="Cubic (crystal system)">cubic</a> (or isometric) system, has the symmetry of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cube_(geometry)" title="Cube (geometry)">cube</a>, that is, it exhibits four threefold rotational axes oriented at 109.5° (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_angle" title="Tetrahedral angle">tetrahedral angle</a>) with respect to each other. These threefold axes lie along the body diagonals of the cube. The other six lattice systems, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_lattice_system" title="Hexagonal lattice system">hexagonal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhombohedral_lattice_system" title="Rhombohedral lattice system">rhombohedral</a> (often confused with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_crystal_system" title="Trigonal crystal system">trigonal crystal system</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triclinic" title="Triclinic">triclinic</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bravais_lattices">Bravais lattices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bravais lattices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bravais_lattice" title="Bravais lattice">Bravais lattice</a></div>
<p>Bravais lattices, also referred to as <i>space lattices</i>, describe the geometric arrangement of the lattice points,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1991_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1991-6">[6]</a></sup> and therefore the translational symmetry of the crystal. The three dimensions of space afford 14 distinct Bravais lattices describing the translational symmetry. All crystalline materials recognized today, not including <a href="/wiki/Quasicrystal" title="Quasicrystal">quasicrystals</a>, fit in one of these arrangements. The fourteen three-dimensional lattices, classified by lattice system, are shown above.
</p><p>The crystal structure consists of the same group of atoms, the <i>basis</i>, positioned around each and every lattice point. This group of atoms therefore repeats indefinitely in three dimensions according to the arrangement of one of the Bravais lattices. The characteristic rotation and mirror symmetries of the unit cell is described by its <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_point_group" title="Crystallographic point group">crystallographic point group</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_systems">Crystal systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Crystal systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A crystal system is a set of point groups in which the point groups themselves and their corresponding space groups are assigned to a lattice system. Of the 32 point groups that exist in three dimensions, most are assigned to only one lattice system, in which case the crystal system and lattice system both have the same name. However, five point groups are assigned to two lattice systems, rhombohedral and hexagonal, because both lattice systems exhibit threefold rotational symmetry. These point groups are assigned to the trigonal crystal system. 
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Point_groups">Point groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Point groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_point_group" title="Crystallographic point group">Crystallographic point group</a></div>
<p>The crystallographic point group or <i>crystal class</i> is the mathematical group comprising the symmetry operations that leave at least one point unmoved and that leave the appearance of the crystal structure unchanged. These symmetry operations include
</p>
<ul><li><i>Reflection</i>, which reflects the structure across a <i>reflection plane</i></li>
<li><i>Rotation</i>, which rotates the structure a specified portion of a circle about a <i>rotation axis</i></li>
<li><i>Inversion</i>, which changes the sign of the coordinate of each point with respect to a <i>center of symmetry</i> or <i>inversion point</i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Improper_rotation" title="Improper rotation">Improper rotation</a></i>, which consists of a rotation about an axis followed by an inversion.</li></ul>
<p>Rotation axes (proper and improper), reflection planes, and centers of symmetry are collectively called <i>symmetry elements</i>. There are 32 possible crystal classes. Each one can be classified into one of the seven crystal systems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_groups">Space groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Space groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">Space group</a></div>
<p>In addition to the operations of the point group, the space group of the crystal structure contains translational symmetry operations. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>Pure <i>translations</i>, which move a point along a vector</li>
<li><i>Screw axes</i>, which rotate a point around an axis while translating parallel to the axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sands_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sands-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Glide planes</i>, which reflect a point through a plane while translating it parallel to the plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sands_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sands-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There are 230 distinct space groups.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atomic_coordination">Atomic coordination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Atomic coordination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By considering the arrangement of atoms relative to each other, their coordination numbers (or number of nearest neighbors), interatomic distances, types of bonding, etc., it is possible to form a general view of the structures and alternative ways of visualizing them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1994_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1994-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Close_packing">Close packing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Close packing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p>
<dl><dd>...<b>ABABABAB</b>...</dd></dl>

</p><p>If, however, all three planes are staggered relative to each other and it is not until the fourth layer is positioned directly over plane A that the sequence is repeated, then the following sequence arises:
</p>

<p>This type of structural arrangement is known as <b>cubic close packing (ccp)</b>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grain_boundaries">Grain boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Grain boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grain boundaries are interfaces where crystals of different orientations meet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1991_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1991-6">[6]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Grain_boundary" title="Grain boundary">grain boundary</a> is a single-phase interface, with crystals on each side of the boundary being identical except in orientation. The term "crystallite boundary" is sometimes, though rarely, used. Grain boundary areas contain those atoms that have been perturbed from their original lattice sites, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dislocations" title="Dislocations">dislocations</a>, and impurities that have migrated to the lower energy grain boundary.
</p><p>Treating a grain boundary geometrically as an interface of a single crystal cut into two parts, one of which is rotated, we see that there are five variables required to define a grain boundary. The first two numbers come from the unit vector that specifies a rotation axis. The third number designates the angle of rotation of the grain. The final two numbers specify the plane of the grain boundary (or a unit vector that is normal to this plane).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1994_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1994-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grain boundaries disrupt the motion of dislocations through a material, so reducing crystallite size is a common way to improve strength, as described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93Petch" title="Hall–Petch">Hall–Petch</a> relationship. Since grain boundaries are defects in the crystal structure they tend to decrease the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of the material. The high interfacial energy and relatively weak bonding in most grain boundaries often makes them preferred sites for the onset of corrosion and for the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of new phases from the solid. They are also important to many of the mechanisms of <a href="/wiki/Creep_(deformation)" title="Creep (deformation)">creep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1994_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1994-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grain boundaries are in general only a few nanometers wide. In common materials, crystallites are large enough that grain boundaries account for a small fraction of the material. However, very small grain sizes are achievable. In nanocrystalline solids, grain boundaries become a significant volume fraction of the material, with profound effects on such properties as <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plasticity</a>. In the limit of small crystallites, as the volume fraction of grain boundaries approaches 100%, the material ceases to have any crystalline character, and thus becomes an <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous solid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_1994_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Physics_1994-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defects_and_impurities">Defects and impurities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Defects and impurities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Real crystals feature <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">defects</a> or irregularities in the ideal arrangements described above and it is these defects that critically determine many of the electrical and mechanical properties of real materials. When one atom substitutes for one of the principal atomic components within the crystal structure, alteration in the electrical and thermal properties of the material may ensue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Impurities may also manifest as spin impurities in certain materials. Research on magnetic impurities demonstrates that substantial alteration of certain properties such as specific heat may be affected by small concentrations of an impurity, as for example impurities in semiconducting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> may lead to different properties as first predicted in the late 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dislocation" title="Dislocation">Dislocations</a> in the crystal lattice allow <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear</a> at lower stress than that needed for a perfect crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction_of_structure">Prediction of structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Prediction of structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure_prediction" title="Crystal structure prediction">Crystal structure prediction</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="948" data-file-width="1000" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png/220px-Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png/330px-Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png/440px-Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sodium-chloride-3D-ionic.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of sodium chloride (table salt)</div></div></div>
<p>The difficulty of predicting stable crystal structures based on the knowledge of only the chemical composition has long been a stumbling block on the way to fully computational materials design. Now, with more powerful algorithms and high-performance computing, structures of medium complexity can be predicted using such approaches as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolutionary_algorithms" title="Evolutionary algorithms">evolutionary algorithms</a>, random sampling, or metadynamics.
</p><p>The crystal structures of simple ionic solids (e.g., NaCl or table salt) have long been rationalized in terms of <a href="/wiki/Pauling%27s_rules" title="Pauling's rules">Pauling's rules</a>, first set out in 1929 by <a href="/wiki/Linus_Pauling" title="Linus Pauling">Linus Pauling</a>, referred to by many since as the "father of the chemical bond".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paulingsrules_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paulingsrules-15">[15]</a></sup> Pauling also considered the nature of the interatomic forces in metals, and concluded that about half of the five d-orbitals in the transition metals are involved in bonding, with the remaining nonbonding d-orbitals being responsible for the magnetic properties. He, therefore, was able to correlate the number of d-orbitals in bond formation with the bond length as well as many of the physical properties of the substance. He subsequently introduced the metallic orbital, an extra orbital necessary to permit uninhibited resonance of valence bonds among various electronic structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Resonating_valence_bond_theory" title="Resonating valence bond theory">resonating valence bond theory</a>, the factors that determine the choice of one from among alternative crystal structures of a metal or intermetallic compound revolve around the energy of resonance of bonds among interatomic positions. It is clear that some modes of resonance would make larger contributions (be more mechanically stable than others), and that in particular a simple ratio of number of bonds to number of positions would be exceptional. The resulting principle is that a special stability is associated with the simplest ratios or "bond numbers": ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>, ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span>, ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span>, ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span>, ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span>, etc. The choice of structure and the value of the <a href="/wiki/Axial_ratio" title="Axial ratio">axial ratio</a> (which determines the relative bond lengths) are thus a result of the effort of an atom to use its valency in the formation of stable bonds with simple fractional bond numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>After postulating a direct correlation between electron concentration and crystal structure in beta-phase alloys, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hume-Rothery" title="Hume-Rothery">Hume-Rothery</a> analyzed the trends in melting points, compressibilities and bond lengths as a function of group number in the periodic table in order to establish a system of valencies of the transition elements in the metallic state. This treatment thus emphasized the increasing bond strength as a function of group number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The operation of directional forces were emphasized in one article on the relation between bond hybrids and the metallic structures. The resulting correlation between electronic and crystalline structures is summarized by a single parameter, the weight of the d-electrons per hybridized metallic orbital. The "d-weight" calculates out to 0.5, 0.7 and 0.9 for the fcc, hcp and bcc structures respectively. The relationship between d-electrons and crystal structure thus becomes apparent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In crystal structure predictions/simulations, the periodicity is usually applied, since the system is imagined as unlimited big in all directions. Starting from a triclinic structure with no further symmetry property assumed, the system may be driven to show some additional symmetry properties by applying Newton's Second Law on particles in the unit cell and a recently developed dynamical equation for the system period vectors
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> (lattice parameters including angles), even if the system is subject to external stress.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymorphism">Polymorphism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Polymorphism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quartz,_Tibet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1596" data-file-width="1592" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg/300px-Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg/450px-Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg/600px-Quartz%2C_Tibet.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quartz,_Tibet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">Quartz</a> is one of the several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>, SiO<sub>2</sub>. The most important forms of silica include: <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">α-quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">β-quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">tridymite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cristobalite" title="Cristobalite">cristobalite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">Polymorphism</a> is the occurrence of multiple crystalline forms of a material. It is found in many crystalline materials including <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>.  According to Gibbs' rules of phase equilibria, these unique crystalline phases are dependent on intensive variables such as pressure and temperature.  Polymorphism is related to <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropy</a>, which refers to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_elements" title="Chemical elements">elemental solids</a>. The complete morphology of a material is described by polymorphism and other variables such as <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">crystal habit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous fraction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">crystallographic defects</a>. Polymorphs have different stabilities and may spontaneously convert from a metastable form (or thermodynamically unstable form) to the <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stable</a> form at a particular temperature. They also exhibit different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melting_points" title="Melting points">melting points</a>, solubilities, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a> patterns.
</p><p>One good example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>, or SiO<sub>2</sub>. In the vast majority of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicates" title="Silicates">silicates</a>, the Si atom shows tetrahedral coordination by 4 oxygens. All but one of the crystalline forms involve tetrahedral {SiO<sub>4</sub>} units linked together by shared vertices in different arrangements. In different minerals the tetrahedra show different degrees of networking and polymerization. For example, they occur singly, joined together in pairs, in larger finite clusters including rings, in chains, double chains, sheets, and three-dimensional frameworks. The minerals are classified into groups based on these structures. In each of the 7 thermodynamically stable crystalline forms or polymorphs of crystalline quartz, only 2 out of 4 of each the edges of the {SiO<sub>4</sub>} tetrahedra are shared with others, yielding the net chemical formula for silica: SiO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Another example is elemental <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> (Sn), which is malleable near ambient temperatures but is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> when cooled. This change in mechanical properties due to existence of its two major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotropes</a>, α- and β-tin. The two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotropes</a> that are encountered at normal pressure and temperature, α-tin and β-tin, are more commonly known as <i>gray tin</i> and <i>white tin</i> respectively. Two more allotropes, γ and σ, exist at temperatures above 161 °C and pressures above several GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> White tin is metallic, and is the stable crystalline form at or above room temperature. Below 13.2 °C, tin exists in the gray form, which has a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_cubic" title="Diamond cubic">diamond cubic</a> crystal structure, similar to <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>. Gray tin has no metallic properties at all, is a dull gray powdery material, and has few uses, other than a few specialized <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Although the α–β transformation temperature of tin is nominally 13.2 °C, impurities (e.g. Al, Zn, etc.) lower the transition temperature well below 0 °C, and upon addition of Sb or Bi the transformation may not occur at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crystal_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Twenty of the 32 crystal classes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" title="Piezoelectric">piezoelectric</a>, and crystals belonging to one of these classes (point groups) display <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectricity</a>. All piezoelectric classes lack a center of symmetry. Any material develops a <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> polarization when an electric field is applied, but a substance that has such a natural charge separation even in the absence of a field is called a polar material. Whether or not a material is polar is determined solely by its crystal structure. Only 10 of the 32 point groups are <a href="/wiki/Polar_point_group" title="Polar point group">polar</a>. All polar crystals are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyroelectric" title="Pyroelectric">pyroelectric</a>, so the 10 polar crystal classes are sometimes referred to as the pyroelectric classes.
</p><p>There are a few crystal structures, notably the <a href="/wiki/Perovskite_(structure)" title="Perovskite (structure)">perovskite structure</a>, which exhibit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferroelectric" title="Ferroelectric">ferroelectric</a> behavior. This is analogous to <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a>, in that, in the absence of an electric field during production, the ferroelectric crystal does not exhibit a polarization. Upon the application of an electric field of sufficient magnitude, the crystal becomes permanently polarized. This polarization can be reversed by a sufficiently large counter-charge, in the same way that a ferromagnet can be reversed. However, although they are called ferroelectrics, the effect is due to the crystal structure (not the presence of a ferrous metal).
</p>
