<p><b>Palladium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Pd</b> and atomic number 46. It is a rare and lustrous silvery-white metal discovered in 1803 by <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a>. He named it after the <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">asteroid Pallas</a>, which was itself named after the <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> of the Greek goddess <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, acquired by her when she slew <a href="/wiki/Pallas_(daughter_of_Triton)" title="Pallas (daughter of Triton)">Pallas</a>. Palladium, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> form a group of elements referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> metals (PGMs). These have similar chemical properties, but palladium has the lowest melting point and is the least dense of them.
</p><p>More than half the supply of palladium and its <a href="/wiki/Congener_(chemistry)" title="Congener (chemistry)">congener</a> platinum is used in <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a>, which convert as much as 90% of the harmful gases in automobile exhaust (<a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>) into less noxious substances (<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>). Palladium is also used in electronics, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_purification" title="Hydrogen purification">hydrogen purification</a>, chemical applications, groundwater treatment, and jewelry. Palladium is a key component of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>, which react hydrogen with oxygen to produce electricity, heat, and water.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">Ore</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposit_(geology)" title="Deposit (geology)">deposits</a> of palladium and other PGMs are rare. The most extensive deposits have been found in the norite belt of the <a href="/wiki/Bushveld_Igneous_Complex" title="Bushveld Igneous Complex">Bushveld Igneous Complex</a> covering the <a href="/wiki/Transvaal_Basin" title="Transvaal Basin">Transvaal Basin</a> in South Africa; the <a href="/wiki/Stillwater_igneous_complex" title="Stillwater igneous complex">Stillwater Complex</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, United States; the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Basin" title="Sudbury Basin">Sudbury Basin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bay_District" title="Thunder Bay District">Thunder Bay District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada; and the <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">Norilsk Complex</a> in Russia. <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">Recycling</a> is also a source, mostly from scrapped catalytic converters. The numerous applications and limited supply sources result in considerable <a href="/wiki/Palladium_as_an_investment" title="Palladium as an investment">investment</a> interest.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Palladium belongs to <a href="/wiki/Group_10_element" title="Group 10 element">group 10</a> in the periodic table, but the configuration in the outermost electron shells is atypical for group 10 (see also <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a> (41), <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> (44), and <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> (45)).  Fewer electron shells are filled than the elements directly preceding it (a phenomenon unique to palladium). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_shell" title="Valence shell">valence shell</a> has eighteen electrons – ten more than the eight found in the valence shells of the <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a> from <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a> onward.
</p>

<p>Palladium is a soft silver-white metal that resembles platinum. It is the least dense and has the lowest <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of the platinum group metals. It is soft and ductile when <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealed</a> and is greatly increased in strength and hardness when cold-worked. Palladium dissolves slowly in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, in hot, concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, and when finely ground, in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup> It dissolves readily at room temperature in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.
</p><p>Palladium does not react with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> at standard temperatures (and thus does not tarnish in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>). Palladium heated to 800 °C will produce a layer of palladium(II) oxide (PdO). It tarnishes lightly in a moist atmosphere containing <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="producing what compound? (May 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palladium films with defects produced by alpha particle bombardment at low temperature exhibit superconductivity having T<sub>c</sub>=3.2 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_palladium" title="Isotopes of palladium">Isotopes of palladium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring palladium is composed of seven <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, six of which are stable. The most stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladium-107" title="Palladium-107"><sup>107</sup>Pd</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 6.5 million years (found in nature), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pd-103" title="Pd-103"><sup>103</sup>Pd</a> with 17 days, and <sup>100</sup>Pd with 3.63 days. Eighteen other radioisotopes have been characterized with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weights</a> ranging from 90.94948(64) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>91</sup>Pd) to 122.93426(64) u (<sup>123</sup>Pd).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These have half-lives of less than thirty minutes, except <sup>101</sup>Pd (half-life: 8.47 hours), <sup>109</sup>Pd (half-life: 13.7 hours), and <sup>112</sup>Pd (half-life: 21 hours).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>For isotopes with atomic mass unit values less than that of the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>106</sup>Pd, the primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> with the primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay product</a> being rhodium. The primary mode of decay for those isotopes of Pd with atomic mass greater than 106 is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> with the primary product of this decay being <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiogenic" title="Radiogenic">Radiogenic</a> <sup>107</sup>Ag is a decay product of <sup>107</sup>Pd and was first discovered in 1978<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_Durango" title="Santa Clara, Durango">Santa Clara</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> meteorite of 1976. The discoverers suggest that the coalescence and differentiation of iron-cored small planets may have occurred 10 million years after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleosynthetic" title="Nucleosynthetic">nucleosynthetic</a> event. <sup>107</sup>Pd versus Ag correlations observed in bodies, which have been melted since accretion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, must reflect the presence of short-lived nuclides in the early solar system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><i><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Palladium_compounds" title="Category:Palladium compounds">Category:Palladium compounds</a>.</div></i></dd></dl>
<p>Palladium compounds primarily exist in the 0 and +2 oxidation state.  Other less common states are also recognized. Generally the compounds of palladium are more similar to those of platinum than those of any other element.

<h3><span id="Palladium.28II.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Palladium(II)">Palladium(II)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Palladium(II)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Palladium(II) chloride is the principal starting material for other palladium compounds. It arises by the reaction of palladium with chlorine. It is used to prepare heterogeneous palladium catalysts such as palladium on barium sulfate, palladium on carbon, and palladium chloride on carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Solutions of PdCl<sub>2</sub> in nitric acid react with <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_acetate" title="Palladium(II) acetate">palladium(II) acetate</a>, also a versatile reagent.  PdCl<sub>2</sub> reacts with ligands (L) to give square planar complexes of the type PdCl<sub>2</sub>L<sub>2</sub>. One example of such complexes is the <a href="/wiki/Benzonitrile" title="Benzonitrile">benzonitrile</a> derivative PdX<sub>2</sub>(PhCN)<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>PdCl<sub>2</sub> + 2 L → PdCl<sub>2</sub>L<sub>2</sub>  (L = PhCN, PPh<sub>3</sub>, NH<sub>3</sub>, etc)</dd></dl>
<p>The complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(II)_dichloride" title="Bis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(II) dichloride">bis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(II) dichloride</a> is a useful catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palladium(II)_acetate.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="322" data-file-width="408" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg/170px-Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg/255px-Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg/340px-Palladium%28II%29_acetate.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Palladium(II)_acetate.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_acetate" title="Palladium(II) acetate">Palladium(II) acetate</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span id="Palladium.280.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Palladium(0)">Palladium(0)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Palladium(0)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Palladium forms a range of zerovalent complexes with the formula PdL<sub>4</sub>, PdL<sub>3</sub> and PdL<sub>2</sub>.  For example, reduction of a mixture of PdCl<sub>2</sub>(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> and PPh<sub>3</sub> gives <a href="/wiki/Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)" title="Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)">tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 PdCl<sub>2</sub>(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 4 PPh<sub>3</sub> + 5 <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">N<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub></a> → 2 Pd(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub> + N<sub>2</sub> + 4 N<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub><sup>+</sup>Cl<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Another major palladium(0) complex, <a href="/wiki/Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)" title="Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)">tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)</a> (Pd<sub>2</sub>(dba)<sub>3</sub>), is prepared by reducing <a href="/wiki/Sodium_tetrachloropalladate" title="Sodium tetrachloropalladate">sodium tetrachloropalladate</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Dibenzylideneacetone" title="Dibenzylideneacetone">dibenzylideneacetone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palladium(0), as well as palladium(II), are catalysts in <a href="/wiki/Palladium-catalyzed_coupling_reactions" title="Palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions">coupling reactions</a>, as has been recognized by the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> to <a href="/wiki/Richard_F._Heck" title="Richard F. Heck">Richard F. Heck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ei-ichi_Negishi" title="Ei-ichi Negishi">Ei-ichi Negishi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Akira_Suzuki_(chemist)" title="Akira Suzuki (chemist)">Akira Suzuki</a>. Such reactions are widely practiced for the synthesis of fine chemicals. Prominent coupling reactions include the <a href="/wiki/Heck_reaction" title="Heck reaction">Heck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_reaction" title="Suzuki reaction">Suzuki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonogashira_coupling" title="Sonogashira coupling">Sonogashira coupling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stille_reaction" title="Stille reaction">Stille reactions</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kumada_coupling" title="Kumada coupling">Kumada coupling</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_acetate" title="Palladium(II) acetate">Palladium(II) acetate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)" title="Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)">tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)</a> (Pd(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>, and <a href="/wiki/Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)" title="Tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)">tris(dibenzylideneacetone)dipalladium(0)</a> (Pd<sub>2</sub>(dba)<sub>3</sub>) serve either as catalysts or precatalysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_oxidation_states">Other oxidation states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other oxidation states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although Pd(IV) compounds are comparatively rare, one example is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_hexachloropalladate(IV)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sodium hexachloropalladate(IV) (page does not exist)">sodium hexachloropalladate(IV)</a>, Na<sub>2</sub>[PdCl<sub>6</sub>]. A few <a href="/wiki/Compounds_of_palladium(III)" title="Compounds of palladium(III)">compounds of palladium(III)</a> are also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Palladium(VI) was claimed in 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11786638_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11786638-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11786632_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11786632-21">[21]</a></sup> but subsequently disproven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mixed valence palladium complexes exist, e.g. Pd<sub>4</sub>(CO)<sub>4</sub>(OAc)<sub>4</sub>Pd(acac)<sub>2</sub> forms an infinite Pd chain structure, with alternatively interconnected Pd<sub>4</sub>(CO)<sub>4</sub>(OAc)<sub>4</sub> and Pd(acac)<sub>2</sub> units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25319757_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25319757-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="561" data-file-width="432" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/170px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/255px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/340px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a></div></div></div>
<p>William Hyde Wollaston noted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovery</a> of a new noble metal in July 1802 in his lab-book and named it palladium in August of the same year. Wollaston purified enough of the material and offered it, without naming the discoverer, in a small shop in <a href="/wiki/Soho" title="Soho">Soho</a> in April 1803. After harsh criticism from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Chenevix_(chemist)" title="Richard Chenevix (chemist)">Richard Chenevix</a> that palladium is an alloy of platinum and mercury, Wollaston anonymously offered a reward of 20 British pounds for 20 grains of synthetic palladium <i>alloy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contr_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-contr-25">[25]</a></sup> Chenevix received the <a href="/wiki/Copley_Medal" title="Copley Medal">Copley Medal</a> in 1803 after he published his experiments on palladium. Wollaston published the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> in 1804 and mentions some of his work on palladium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Disco_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Disco-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He disclosed that he was the discoverer of palladium in a publication in 1805.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contr_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-contr-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was named by Wollaston in 1802 after the asteroid <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">2 Pallas</a>, which had been discovered two months earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup> Wollaston found palladium in crude platinum ore from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> by dissolving the ore in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>, neutralizing the solution with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, and precipitating platinum as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloroplatinate" title="Ammonium chloroplatinate">ammonium chloroplatinate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>. He added <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_cyanide" title="Mercuric cyanide">mercuric cyanide</a> to form the compound <a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_cyanide" title="Palladium(II) cyanide">palladium(II) cyanide</a>, which was heated to extract palladium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Disco_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Disco-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladium_chloride" title="Palladium chloride">Palladium chloride</a> was at one time prescribed as a <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> treatment at the rate of 0.065 g per day (approximately one milligram per kilogram of body weight). This treatment had many negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverse_effect_(medicine)" title="Adverse effect (medicine)">side-effects</a>, and was later replaced by more effective drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most palladium is used for <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a> in the automobile industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiel_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kiel-30">[30]</a></sup> In the run up to year 2000, the Russian supply of palladium to the global market was repeatedly delayed and disrupted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> for political reasons, the export quota was not granted on time. The ensuing market panic drove the price to an all-time high of $1100 per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">troy ounce</a> in January 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chart-all_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-chart-all-32">[32]</a></sup> Around that time, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>, fearing that automobile production would be disrupted by a palladium shortage, stockpiled the metal. When prices fell in early 2001, Ford lost nearly <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>World demand for palladium increased from 100 tons in 1990 to nearly 300 tons in 2000. The global production of palladium from mines was 222 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in 2006 according to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS07CS_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS07CS-34">[34]</a></sup> Many were concerned about a steady supply of palladium in the wake of Russia's military maneuvers in Ukraine, partly as sanctions could hamper Russian palladium exports; any restrictions on Russian palladium exports would exacerbate what is already expected to be a large palladium deficit in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Those concerns pushed palladium prices to their highest level since 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In September 2014 they soared above the $900 per ounce mark. In 2016 however palladium cost around $614 per ounce as Russia managed to maintain stable supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  In January 2018 palladium futures climbed past $1,100 per ounce for the first time on record, mainly due to the strong demand from the automotive industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2005palladium_(mined).PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG/350px-2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG/525px-2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG/700px-2005palladium_%28mined%29.PNG 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2005palladium_(mined).PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Palladium output in 2005</div></div></div>
<p>As overall mine production of palladium reached 208,000 kilograms in 2016, Russia was the top producer with 82,000 kilograms, followed by South Africa, Canada and the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Russia's company Norilsk Nickel ranks first among the largest palladium producers globally, it accounts for 39% of the world’s production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palladium can be found as a free metal alloyed with gold and other platinum-group metals in <a href="/wiki/Placer_mining" title="Placer mining">placer</a> deposits of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. For the production of palladium, these deposits play only a minor role. The most important commercial sources are <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>-<a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> deposits found in the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Basin" title="Sudbury Basin">Sudbury Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">Norilsk–Talnakh</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. The other large deposit is the <a href="/wiki/Merensky_Reef" title="Merensky Reef">Merensky Reef</a> <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> metals deposit within the <a href="/wiki/Bushveld_Igneous_Complex" title="Bushveld Igneous Complex">Bushveld Igneous Complex</a> <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Stillwater_igneous_complex" title="Stillwater igneous complex">Stillwater igneous complex</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and the Roby zone ore body of the <a href="/wiki/Lac_des_%C3%8Eles_igneous_complex" title="Lac des Îles igneous complex">Lac des Îles igneous complex</a> of Ontario are the two other sources of palladium in Canada and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS07CS_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS07CS-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS07YB_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS07YB-41">[41]</a></sup> Palladium is found in the rare minerals <a href="/wiki/Cooperite_(mineral)" title="Cooperite (mineral)">cooperite</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Polarite" title="Polarite">polarite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Many more Pd minerals are known, but all of them are very rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palladium is also produced in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> reactors and can be extracted from spent nuclear fuel (see <a href="/wiki/Synthesis_of_precious_metals" title="Synthesis of precious metals">synthesis of precious metals</a>), though this source for palladium is not used. None of the existing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">nuclear reprocessing</a> facilities are equipped to extract palladium from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-level_radioactive_waste" title="High-level radioactive waste">high-level radioactive waste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="297" data-file-width="640" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg/220px-Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg/330px-Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg/440px-Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aufgeschnittener_Metall_Katalysator_f%C3%BCr_ein_Auto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross section of a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">metal-core catalytic converter</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="816" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg/220px-25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg/330px-25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg/440px-25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:25_rubles_palladium_1989_Ivan_III.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Soviet 25-rouble commemorative <a href="/wiki/Palladium_coin" title="Palladium coin">palladium coin</a> is a rare example of the monetary usage of palladium.</div></div></div>
<p>The largest use of palladium today is in catalytic converters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unctad_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-unctad-46">[46]</a></sup> Palladium is also used in jewelry, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unctad_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-unctad-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Watch" title="Watch">watch</a> making, blood sugar test strips, aircraft <a href="/wiki/Spark_plug" title="Spark plug">spark plugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surgical_instrument" title="Surgical instrument">surgical instruments</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_contact" title="Electrical contact">electrical contacts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Palladium is also used to make professional <a href="/wiki/Transverse_flute" title="Transverse flute">transverse (concert or classical) flutes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> As a commodity, palladium <a href="/wiki/Bullion" title="Bullion">bullion</a> has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO_currency_code" title="ISO currency code">ISO currency codes</a> of XPD and 964. Palladium is one of only four metals to have such codes, the others being <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> and platinum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Because it absorbs hydrogen, palladium is a key component of the controversial <a href="/wiki/Cold_fusion" title="Cold fusion">cold fusion</a> experiments that began in 1989.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysis">Catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When it is finely divided, as with <a href="/wiki/Palladium_on_carbon" title="Palladium on carbon">palladium on carbon</a>, palladium forms a versatile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>; it speeds <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous</a> catalytic processes like <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dehydrogenation" title="Dehydrogenation">dehydrogenation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">petroleum cracking</a>. Palladium is also essential to the <a href="/wiki/Lindlar_catalyst" title="Lindlar catalyst">Lindlar catalyst</a>, also called Lindlar's Palladium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> A large number of <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">carbon–carbon bonding</a> reactions in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> are facilitated by palladium compound catalysts. For example:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Heck_reaction" title="Heck reaction">Heck reaction</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suzuki_coupling" title="Suzuki coupling">Suzuki coupling</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tsuji%E2%80%93Trost_reaction" title="Tsuji–Trost reaction">Tsuji-Trost reactions</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wacker_process" title="Wacker process">Wacker process</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Negishi_coupling" title="Negishi coupling">Negishi reaction</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stille_coupling" title="Stille coupling">Stille coupling</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sonogashira_coupling" title="Sonogashira coupling">Sonogashira coupling</a></li></ul>
<p>(See <a href="#Compounds">palladium compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palladium-catalyzed_coupling_reactions" title="Palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions">palladium-catalyzed coupling reactions</a>.)
</p><p>When dispersed on conductive materials, palladium is an excellent electrocatalyst for oxidation of primary alcohols in alkaline media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Palladium is also a versatile metal for <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_catalysis" title="Homogeneous catalysis">homogeneous catalysis</a>, used in combination with a broad variety of <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a> for highly selective chemical transformations.
</p><p>
In 2010, palladium-catalysed organic reactions were recognised by the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a>.  A 2008 study showed that palladium is an effective catalyst for carbon-fluoride bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="775" data-file-width="995" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png/400px-Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png/600px-Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png/800px-Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kumada_Catalytic_Cycle.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Catalytic cycle for Kumada cross coupling reaction, which is widely used in the synthesis of fine chemicals.</div></div></div>
<p>Palladium catalysis is primarily employed in organic chemistry and industrial applications, although its use is growing as a tool for <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic biology</a>; in 2017, effective <i>in vivo</i> catalytic activity of palladium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoparticles" title="Nanoparticles">nanoparticles</a> was demonstrated in mammals to treat disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid28699627_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid28699627-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second greatest application of palladium in electronics is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multilayer_ceramic_capacitor" title="Multilayer ceramic capacitor">multilayer ceramic capacitors</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> in which palladium (and palladium-silver alloy) is used for electrodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unctad_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-unctad-46">[46]</a></sup> Palladium (sometimes alloyed with nickel) is used for component and connector plating in consumer electronics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and in soldering materials. The electronic sector consumed 1.07 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">troy ounces</a> (33.2 tonnes) of palladium in 2006, according to a <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Matthey" title="Johnson Matthey">Johnson Matthey</a> report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-matthey_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-matthey-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen easily diffuses through heated palladium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Membrane_reactor" title="Membrane reactor">membrane reactors</a> with Pd membranes are used in the production of high purity hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Palladium is used in <a href="/wiki/Palladium-hydrogen_electrode" title="Palladium-hydrogen electrode">palladium-hydrogen electrode</a>s in electrochemical studies. <a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_chloride" title="Palladium(II) chloride">Palladium(II) chloride</a> readily catalyzes carbon monoxide gas to carbon dioxide and is useful in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_detector" title="Carbon monoxide detector">carbon monoxide detectors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_storage">Hydrogen storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hydrogen storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Palladium_hydride" title="Palladium hydride">Palladium hydride</a></div>
<p>Palladium readily <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorbs</a> hydrogen at room temperatures, forming <a href="/wiki/Palladium_hydride" title="Palladium hydride">palladium hydride</a> PdH<sub>x</sub> with x less than 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> While this property is common to many transition metals, palladium has a uniquely high absorption capacity and does not lose its ductility until x approaches 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gr_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-gr-62">[62]</a></sup> This property has been investigated in designing an efficient, inexpensive, and safe hydrogen fuel storage medium, though palladium itself is currently prohibitively expensive for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grochala_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-grochala-63">[63]</a></sup>  The content of hydrogen in palladium can be linked to <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_susceptibility" title="Magnetic susceptibility">magnetic susceptibility</a>, which decreases with the increase of hydrogen and becomes zero for PdH<sub>0.62</sub>. At any higher ratio, the <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> becomes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dentistry">Dentistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dentistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Palladium is used in small amounts (about 0.5%) in some alloys of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_amalgam" title="Dental amalgam">dental amalgam</a> to decrease corrosion and increase the <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)#Metallic_lustre" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">metallic lustre</a> of the final restoration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewelry">Jewelry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jewelry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Palladium has been used as a <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metal</a> in jewelry since 1939 as an alternative to platinum in the alloys called "<a href="/wiki/Colored_gold#White_gold" title="Colored gold">white gold</a>", where the naturally white color of palladium does not require <a href="/wiki/Plating#Rhodium_plating" title="Plating">rhodium plating</a>. Palladium is much less dense than platinum. Similar to gold, palladium can be beaten into <a href="/wiki/Metal_leaf" title="Metal leaf">leaf</a> as thin as 100 nm (​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>250,000</sub></span> in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-4">[4]</a></sup> Unlike platinum, palladium may discolor at temperatures above 400 °C (752 °F);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> it is relatively brittle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Is it relatively brittle at those temperatures? (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Palladium is one of the three most popular alloying metals in white gold (<a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and silver can also be used).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unctad_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-unctad-46">[46]</a></sup> Palladium-gold is more expensive than nickel-gold, but seldom causes allergic reactions (though certain cross-allergies with nickel may occur).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>When platinum was declared a strategic government resource during World War II, many jewelry bands were made out of palladium. Up to as recently as September 2001,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> palladium was more expensive than platinum and rarely used in jewelry because of the technical difficulty of <a href="/wiki/Casting_(metalworking)" title="Casting (metalworking)">casting</a>. Currently, the casting problem has been resolved and use in jewelry has increased because platinum has increased in price while palladium decreased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to 2004, the principal use of palladium in jewelry was the manufacture of white gold. In early 2004, when gold and platinum prices rose steeply, China began fabricating volumes of palladium jewelry, consuming 37 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in 2005. Changes in the relative price of platinum after 2008 lowered demand for palladium to 17.4 tonnes in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS09YB_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS09YB-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS06YB_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS06YB-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2010, <a href="/wiki/Hallmark" title="Hallmark">hallmarks</a> for palladium were introduced by assay offices in the United Kingdom, and hallmarking became mandatory for all jewelry advertising pure or alloyed palladium. Articles can be marked as 500, 950, or 999 parts of palladium per thousand of the alloy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">Fountain pen</a> <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nibs</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> are sometimes plated with palladium when a silver (rather than gold) appearance is desired. <a href="/wiki/Sheaffer" title="Sheaffer">Sheaffer</a> has used palladium plating for decades, either as an accent on otherwise gold nibs or covering the gold completely.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinotype" title="Platinotype">platinotype</a> printing process, photographers make fine-art black-and-white prints using platinum or palladium salts. Often used with platinum, palladium provides an alternative to silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Palladium is a metal with low toxicity. It is poorly absorbed by the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingested" title="Ingested">ingested</a>. Plants such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_hyacinth" title="Water hyacinth">water hyacinth</a> are killed by low levels of palladium salts, but most other plants tolerate it, although tests show that, at levels above 0.0003%, growth is affected. High doses of palladium could be poisonous; tests on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a> suggest it may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a>, though no clear evidence indicates the element harms humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palladium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platinum-group_metal" title="Platinum-group metal">platinum-group metals</a>, bulk Pd is quite inert. Although <a href="/wiki/Contact_dermatitis" title="Contact dermatitis">contact dermatitis</a> has been reported, data on the effects are limited. It has been shown that people with an allergic reaction to palladium also react to nickel, making it advisable to avoid the use of dental alloys containing palladium on those so allergic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiel_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kiel-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some palladium is emitted with the exhaust gases of cars with <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a>. Between 4 and 108 ng/km of palladium particulate is released by such cars, while the total uptake from food is estimated to be less than 2 µg per person a day. The second possible source of palladium is dental restoration, from which the uptake of palladium is estimated to be less than 15 µg per person per day. People working with palladium or its compounds might have a considerably greater uptake. For soluble compounds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palladium_chloride" title="Palladium chloride">palladium chloride</a>, 99% is eliminated from the body within 3 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiel_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kiel-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) of soluble palladium compounds in mice is 200 mg/kg for <a href="/wiki/Oral_administration" title="Oral administration">oral</a> and 5 mg/kg for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous_administration" title="Intravenous administration">intravenous administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiel_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kiel-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
