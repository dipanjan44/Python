<p>The  <b>Holocene extinction</b>, otherwise referred to as the <b>Sixth extinction</b> or <b>Anthropocene extinction</b>, is the ongoing <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a> of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> during the present <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a>, mainly as a result of human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldScientists_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorldScientists-1">[1]</a></sup> The large number of extinctions spans numerous families of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>. With widespread degradation of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot" title="Biodiversity hotspot">highly biodiverse</a> habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, as well as other areas, the vast majority of these extinctions are thought to be <i>undocumented</i>, as no one is even aware of the existence of the species before they go extinct, or no one has yet discovered their extinction. The current rate of extinction of species is estimated at 100 to 1,000 times higher than natural background rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawton95_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawton95-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Holocene extinction includes the disappearance of large land animals known as <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a>, starting at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last Ice Age</a>. Megafauna outside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_continent" title="African continent">African continent</a>, which did not evolve alongside humans, proved highly sensitive to the introduction of new predation, and many died out shortly after early humans began spreading and hunting across the Earth (additionally, many <a href="/wiki/List_of_African_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of African animals extinct in the Holocene">African species have also gone extinct in the Holocene</a>). These extinctions, occurring near the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>–<a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> boundary, are sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" title="Quaternary extinction event">Quaternary extinction event</a>.
</p><p>The arrival of humans on different continents coincides with megafaunal extinction. The most popular theory is that human overhunting of species added to existing stress conditions. Although there is debate regarding how much human predation affected their decline, certain population declines have been directly correlated with human activity, such as the extinction events of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_New_Zealand" title="List of extinct animals of New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_the_Hawaiian_Islands" title="List of extinct animals of the Hawaiian Islands">Hawaii</a>. Aside from humans, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> may have been a driving factor in the megafaunal extinctions, especially at the end of the Pleistocene.
</p><p>Ecologically, humanity has been noted as an unprecedented "global superpredator" that consistently preys on the adults of other <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a>, and has <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">worldwide effects</a> on <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a>. There have been extinctions of species on every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_mass" title="Land mass">land mass</a> and in every <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>: there are many famous examples <a href="/wiki/List_of_African_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of African animals extinct in the Holocene">within Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Asian_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of Asian animals extinct in the Holocene">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_Europe" title="List of extinct animals of Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_Australia" title="List of extinct animals of Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_North_American_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of North American animals extinct in the Holocene">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_South_American_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of South American animals extinct in the Holocene">South America</a>, and on smaller islands. Overall, the Holocene extinction can be linked to the <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on the environment</a>. The Holocene extinction continues into the 21st century, with <a href="/wiki/Meat#Environmental_impact" title="Meat">meat consumption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Decline_in_amphibian_populations" title="Decline in amphibian populations">decline in amphibian populations</a> being a few broader examples of an almost universal, <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan</a> <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment#Biodiversity" title="Human impact on the environment">decline in biodiversity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Human overpopulation</a> (and continued <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_growth" title="Human population growth">population growth</a>) along with <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">profligate consumption</a> are considered to be the primary drivers of this rapid decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PimmJenkins_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PimmJenkins-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Holocene extinction is also known as the "sixth extinction", as it is possibly the sixth mass extinct event, after the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_events" title="Ordovician–Silurian extinction events">Ordovician–Silurian extinction events</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Late_Devonian_extinction" title="Late Devonian extinction">Late Devonian extinction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic–Jurassic extinction event">Triassic–Jurassic extinction event</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceballos_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ceballos-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirzo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-dirzo-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz2015_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz2015-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldScientists_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WorldScientists-1">[1]</a></sup> Mass extinctions are characterized by the loss of at least 75% of species within a geologically short period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> There is no general agreement on where the Holocene, or <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>, extinction begins, and the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" title="Quaternary extinction event">Quaternary extinction event</a>, which includes climate change resulting in the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, ends, or if they should be considered separate events at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doughty,_C._E._2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doughty,_C._E._2010-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-14">[14]</a></sup> Some have suggested that anthropogenic extinctions may have begun as early as when the first modern humans spread out of Africa between 200,000 and 100,000 years ago; this is supported by rapid megafaunal extinction following recent human colonisation in <a href="/wiki/Australian_megafauna" title="Australian megafauna">Australia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_New_Zealand" title="List of extinct animals of New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_African_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of African animals extinct in the Holocene">Madagascar</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> as might be expected when any large, adaptable predator (<a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>) moves into a new ecosystem. In many cases, it is suggested that even minimal hunting pressure was enough to wipe out large fauna, particularly on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographical_isolation" title="Geographical isolation">geographically isolated</a> islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-16">[16]</a></sup> Only during the most recent parts of the extinction have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_climate_change_on_plant_biodiversity" title="Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity">plants also suffered large losses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>The Future of Life</i> (2002), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E.O._Wilson" title="E.O. Wilson">Edward Osborne Wilson</a> of Harvard calculated that, if the current rate of human disruption of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> continues, one-half of Earth's higher lifeforms will be extinct by 2100. A 1998 poll conducted by the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a> found that 70% of biologists acknowledge an ongoing anthropogenic extinction event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> At present, the rate of extinction of species is estimated at 100 to 1,000 times higher than the <a href="/wiki/Background_extinction_rate" title="Background extinction rate">background extinction rate</a>, the historically typical rate of extinction (in terms of the natural evolution of the planet);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawton95_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-lawton95-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> also, the current rate of extinction is 10 to 100 times higher than in any of the previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinctions</a> in the history of Earth. One scientist estimates the current extinction rate may be 10,000 times the <a href="/wiki/Background_extinction_rate" title="Background extinction rate">background extinction rate</a>, although most scientists predict a much lower extinction rate than this outlying estimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_ecologist" title="Theoretical ecologist">Theoretical ecologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Pimm" title="Stuart Pimm">Stuart Pimm</a> stated that the extinction rate for plants is 100 times higher than normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a pair of studies published in 2015, extrapolation from observed extinction of Hawaiian snails led to the conclusion that 7% of all species on Earth may have been lost already.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is widespread consensus among scientists that human activity is accelerating the extinction of many animal species through the destruction of habitats, the consumption of animals as resources, and the elimination of species that humans view as threats or competitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup> But some contend that this biotic destruction has yet to reach the level of the previous five mass extinctions. Stuart Pimm, for example, asserts that the sixth mass extinction "is something that hasn't happened yet – we are on the edge of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrington_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carrington-25">[25]</a></sup> In November 2017, a statement, titled "<a href="/wiki/World_Scientists%27_Warning_to_Humanity#Second_notice" title="World Scientists' Warning to Humanity">World Scientists’ Warning to Humanity: A Second Notice</a>", led by eight authors and signed by 15,364 scientists from 184 countries asserted that, among other things, "we have unleashed a mass extinction event, the sixth in roughly 540 million years, wherein many current life forms could be annihilated or at least committed to extinction by the end of this century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldScientists_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WorldScientists-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Anthropocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2481" data-file-width="3508" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg/290px-Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg/435px-Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg/580px-Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecological_Processes_Coral_Reefs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A diagram showing the ecological processes of <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> before and after the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a></div></div></div>
<p>The abundance of species extinctions considered <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>, or due to human activity, have sometimes (especially when referring to hypothesized future events) been collectively called the "Anthropocene extinction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wooldridge2008_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-wooldridge2008-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackson2008_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-jackson2008-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup> "Anthropocene" is a term introduced in 2000. Some now postulate that a new geological epoch has begun, with the most abrupt and widespread extinction of species since the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a> 66 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "anthropocene" is being used more frequently by scientists, and some commentators may refer to the current and projected future extinctions as part of a longer Holocene extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Holocene–Anthropocene boundary is contested, with some commentators asserting significant human influence on climate for much of what is normally regarded as the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">Epoch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:102_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:102-30">[30]</a></sup> Other commentators place the Holocene–Anthropocene boundary at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> and also say that "[f]ormal adoption of this term in the near future will largely depend on its utility, particularly to earth scientists working on late Holocene successions."
</p><p>It has been suggested that human activity has made the period starting from the mid-20th century different enough from the rest of the Holocene to consider it a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_epoch" title="Geological epoch">geological epoch</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> a term which was considered for inclusion in the timeline of Earth's history by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">International Commission on Stratigraphy</a> in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In order to constitute the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">extinction event</a>, scientists must determine exactly when anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas emissions</a> began to measurably alter natural atmospheric levels on a global scale, and when these alterations caused changes to global climate. Using chemical proxies from Antarctic ice cores, researchers have estimated the fluctuations of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) gases in the Earth's atmosphere during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">late Pleistocene</a> and Holocene epochs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:102_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:102-30">[30]</a></sup> Estimates of the fluctuations of these two gases in the atmosphere, using chemical proxies from Antarctic ice cores, generally indicate that the peak of the Anthropocene occurred within the previous two centuries: typically beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, when the highest greenhouse gas levels were recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:11-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition_by_humans">Competition by humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Competition by humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Megafauna#Megafaunal_mass_extinctions" title="Megafauna">Megafaunal mass extinctions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" title="Quaternary extinction event">Quaternary extinction event</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="441" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif/220px-Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif/330px-Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif/440px-Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Extinctions_Africa_Austrailia_NAmerica_Madagascar.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The percent of <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a> on different land masses over time, with the arrival of humans indicated.</div></div></div>
<p>The Holocene extinction is mainly caused by human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceballos_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ceballos-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirzo_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-dirzo-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz2015_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz2015-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-5">[5]</a></sup> Extinction of animals, plants, and other organisms caused by human actions may go as far back as the late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, over 12,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup> There is a correlation between megafaunal extinction and the arrival of humans, and human overpopulation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_growth" title="Human population growth">human population growth</a>, along with overconsumption and consumption growth, most prominently in the past two centuries, are regarded as one of the underlying causes of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceballos_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ceballos-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PimmJenkins_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-PimmJenkins-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutter_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sutter-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">Megafauna</a> were once found on every continent of the world and large islands such as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, but are now almost exclusively found on the continent of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, with notable comparisons on <a href="/wiki/Australian_megafauna" title="Australian megafauna">Australia</a> and the islands previously mentioned experiencing population crashes and <a href="/wiki/Trophic_cascade" title="Trophic cascade">trophic cascades</a> shortly after the earliest human settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-16">[16]</a></sup> It has been suggested that the African megafauna survived because they evolved alongside humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> The timing of <a href="/wiki/List_of_South_American_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of South American animals extinct in the Holocene">South American megafaunal extinction</a> appears to precede human arrival, although the possibility that human activity at the time impacted the global climate enough to cause such an extinction has been suggested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been noted, in the face of such evidence, that humans are unique in ecology as an unprecedented 'global superpredator', regularly preying on large numbers of fully grown terrestrial and marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apex_predators" title="Apex predators">apex predators</a>, and with a great deal of influence over food webs and climatic systems worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-40">[40]</a></sup> Although significant debate exists as to how much human predation and indirect effects contributed to prehistoric extinctions, certain population crashes have been directly correlated with human arrival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup> A 2018 study published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PNAS" title="PNAS">PNAS</a></i> found that since the dawn of human civilization, 83% of wild mammals, 80% of marine mammals, 50% of plants and 15% of fish have vanished. Currently, livestock make up 60% of the <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> of all mammals on earth, followed by humans (36%) and wild mammals (4%). As for birds, 70% are domesticated, such as poultry, whereas only 30% are wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrington2018_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carrington2018-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biomass2018_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biomass2018-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, some scholars assert that the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> as the dominant economic system has accelerated ecological exploitation and destruction, and has also exacerbated mass species extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CUNY" title="CUNY">CUNY</a> professor <a href="/wiki/David_Harvey" title="David Harvey">David Harvey</a>, for example, posits that the <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> era "happens to be the era of the fastest mass extinction of species in the Earth's recent history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1605" data-file-width="2000" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg/220px-Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg/330px-Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg/440px-Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edwards%27_Dodo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Dodo" title="Dodo">dodo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flightless_bird" title="Flightless bird">flightless bird</a> native to <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, became <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> during the mid- to late <a href="/wiki/17th_century" title="17th century">17th century</a> due to habitat destruction and predation by introduced mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Human civilization flourished in accordance to<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the efficiency and intensity of prevailing <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-:15-44">[44]</a></sup> Local communities that acquire more subsistence strategies increased in number to combat competitive pressures of land utilization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">Gobbledegook. Please rewrite in English.</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:102_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:102-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-:15-44">[44]</a></sup> Therefore, the Holocene developed competition on the basis of agriculture. The growth of agriculture has then introduced newer means of climate change, pollution, and ecological development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction#Human_causes" title="Habitat destruction">Habitat destruction by humans</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_resources_conservation#Human_impact" title="Marine resources conservation">oceanic devastation</a>, such as through <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> and contamination; and the modification and destruction of vast tracts of land and river systems around the world to meet solely human-centered ends (with 13 percent of Earth's ice-free land surface now used as row-crop agricultural sites, 26 percent used as pastures, and 4 percent urban-industrial areas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>), thus replacing the original local ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Other, related human causes of the extinction event include <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> the introduction in various regions of <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">non-native species</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Globalization_and_disease" title="Globalization and disease">widespread</a> <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(medicine)" title="Transmission (medicine)">transmission</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> spread through livestock and crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawton95_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-lawton95-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent investigations about <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> landscape burning has a major implication for the current debate about the timing of the Anthropocene and the role that humans may have played in the production of greenhouse gases prior to the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-:15-44">[44]</a></sup> Studies on early <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a> raises questions about the current use of population size or density as a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy</a> for the amount of land clearance and anthropogenic burning that took place in pre-industrial times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-:16-50">[50]</a></sup> Scientists have questioned the correlation between population size and early territorial alterations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-:16-50">[50]</a></sup> Ruddiman and Ellis' research paper in 2009 makes the case that early farmers involved in systems of agriculture used more land per capita than growers later in the Holocene, who intensified their labor to produce more food per unit of area (thus, per laborer); arguing that agricultural involvement in rice production implemented thousands of years ago by relatively small populations have created significant environmental impacts through large-scale means of deforestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-:15-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>While a number of human-derived factors are recognized as potentially contributing to rising atmospheric concentrations of CH<sub>4</sub> (methane) and CO<sub>2</sub> (carbon dioxide), deforestation and territorial clearance practices associated with agricultural development may be contributing most to these concentrations globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:11_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:11-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-:13-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_44-4"><a href="#cite_note-:15-44">[44]</a></sup> Scientists that are employing a variance of <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> and paleoecological data argue that the processes contributing to substantial human modification of the environment spanned many thousands of years ago on a global scale and thus, not originating as early as the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Gaining popularity on his uncommon hypothesis, palaeoclimatologist <a href="/wiki/William_Ruddiman" title="William Ruddiman">William Ruddiman</a> in 2003, stipulated that in the early Holocene 11,000 years ago, atmospheric carbon dioxide and methane levels fluctuated in a pattern which was different from the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch before it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:102_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-:102-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-:13-51">[51]</a></sup> He argued that the patterns of the significant decline of CO<sub>2</sub> levels during the last ice age of the Pleistocene inversely correlates to the Holocene where there have been dramatic increases of CO<sub>2</sub> around 8000 years ago and CH<sub>4</sub> levels 3000 years after that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-:13-51">[51]</a></sup> The correlation between the decrease of CO<sub>2</sub> in the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> and the increase of it during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> implies that the causation of this spark of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere was the growth of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">human agriculture</a> during the Holocene such as the anthropogenic expansion of (human) land use and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:102_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-:102-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_51-3"><a href="#cite_note-:13-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands">Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Human arrival in the Caribbean around 6,000 years ago is correlated with the extinction of many species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Examples include many different genera of <a href="/wiki/Pilosans_of_the_Caribbean" title="Pilosans of the Caribbean">ground and arboreal sloths</a> across all islands. These sloths were generally smaller than those found on the South American continent. <i><a href="/wiki/Megalocnus" title="Megalocnus">Megalocnus</a></i> were the largest genus at up to 90 kilograms (200 lb), <i><a href="/wiki/Acratocnus" title="Acratocnus">Acratocnus</a></i> were medium-sized relatives of modern <a href="/wiki/Two-toed_sloth" title="Two-toed sloth">two-toed sloths</a> endemic to <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Imagocnus" title="Imagocnus">Imagocnus</a></i> also of Cuba, <i><a href="/wiki/Neocnus" title="Neocnus">Neocnus</a></i> and many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent research, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archeology" title="Archeology">archaeological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontological</a> digs on 70 different Pacific islands has shown that numerous species became extinct as people moved across the Pacific, starting 30,000 years ago in the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Archipelago" title="Bismarck Archipelago">Bismarck Archipelago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It is currently estimated that among the bird species of the Pacific, some 2000 species have gone extinct since the arrival of humans, representing a 20% drop in the biodiversity of birds worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Genyornis_BW.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1002" data-file-width="800" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Genyornis_BW.jpg/170px-Genyornis_BW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Genyornis_BW.jpg/255px-Genyornis_BW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Genyornis_BW.jpg/340px-Genyornis_BW.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Genyornis_BW.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genyornis_newtoni" title="Genyornis newtoni">Genyornis newtoni</a></i>, a 2-metre (7 ft) tall flightless bird. Evidence of egg cooking in this species is the first evidence of megafaunal hunting by humans on Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-56">[56]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Hawaii" title="Ancient Hawaii">first settlers</a> are thought to have arrived in the islands between 300 and 800 CE, with European arrival in the 16th century. Hawaii is notable for its <a href="/wiki/Endemism_in_the_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Endemism in the Hawaiian Islands">endemism</a> of plants, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_birds_of_Hawaii" title="Endemic birds of Hawaii">birds</a>, insects, mollusks and <a href="/wiki/List_of_fish_of_Hawaii" title="List of fish of Hawaii">fish</a>; 30% of its organisms are endemic. Many of its species are endangered or have gone extinct, primarily due to accidentally introduced species and livestock grazing. Over 40% of its bird species have gone extinct, and it is the location of 75% of extinctions in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Extinction has increased in Hawaii over the last 200 years and is relatively well documented, with extinctions among native snails used as estimates for global extinction rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Australian_megafauna" title="Australian megafauna">Australian megafauna</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_Australia" title="List of extinct animals of Australia">List of extinct animals of Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_flora_of_Australia" title="List of extinct flora of Australia">List of extinct flora of Australia</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species_in_Australia" title="Invasive species in Australia">Invasive species in Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_clearing_in_Australia" title="Land clearing in Australia">Land clearing in Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fire-stick_farming" title="Fire-stick farming">Fire-stick farming</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> was once home to a <a href="/wiki/Australian_megafauna" title="Australian megafauna">large assemblage of megafauna</a>, with many parallels to those found on the African continent today. Australia's fauna is characterised by primarily <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupial</a> mammals, and many reptiles and birds, all existing as giant forms until recently. <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Humans</a> arrived on the continent very early, about 50,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> The extent human arrival contributed is controversial; climatic drying of Australia 40,000–60,000 years ago was an unlikely cause, as it was less severe in speed or magnitude than previous regional climate change which failed to kill off megafauna. Extinctions in Australia continued from original settlement until today in both <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_flora_of_Australia" title="List of extinct flora of Australia">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_Australia" title="List of extinct animals of Australia">animals</a>, whilst <a href="/wiki/Threatened_fauna_of_Australia" title="Threatened fauna of Australia">many more animals</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_threatened_flora_of_Australia" title="List of threatened flora of Australia">plants</a> have declined or are endangered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the older timeframe and the soil chemistry on the continent, very little <a href="/wiki/Subfossil" title="Subfossil">subfossil</a> preservation evidence exists relative to elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-59">[59]</a></sup> However, continent-wide extinction of all genera weighing over 100 kilograms, and six of seven genera weighing between 45 and 100 kilograms occurred around 46,400 years ago (4,000 years after human arrival)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and the fact that megafauna survived until a later date on the island of <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> following the establishment of a land bridge<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> suggest direct hunting or anthropogenic ecosystem disruption such as <a href="/wiki/Fire-stick_farming" title="Fire-stick farming">fire-stick farming</a> as likely causes. The first evidence of direct human predation leading to extinction in Australia was published in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Madagascar">Madagascar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Madagascar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_of_Madagascar" title="Wildlife of Madagascar">Wildlife of Madagascar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subfossil_lemur" title="Subfossil lemur">Subfossil lemur</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="875" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg/260px-Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg/390px-Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg/520px-Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subfossil_lemur_C14_ranges.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Radiocarbon dating of multiple subfossil specimens shows that now extinct <a href="/wiki/Subfossil_lemur" title="Subfossil lemur">giant lemurs</a> were present in Madagascar until after human arrival.</div></div></div>
<p>Within 500 years of the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Malagasy_people" title="Malagasy people">humans</a> between 2,500–2,000 years ago, nearly all of Madagascar's distinct, <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">geographically isolated</a> megafauna became extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The largest animals, of more than 150 kilograms (330 lb), were extinct very shortly after the first human arrival, with large and medium-sized species dying out after prolonged hunting pressure from an expanding human population moving into more remote regions of the island around 1000 years ago. Smaller fauna experienced initial increases due to decreased competition, and then subsequent declines over the last 500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-16">[16]</a></sup> All fauna weighing over 10 kilograms (22 lb) died out. The primary reasons for this are human hunting and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_in_Madagascar" title="Deforestation in Madagascar">habitat loss</a> from early <a href="/wiki/Aridification" title="Aridification">aridification</a>, both of which persist and threaten Madagascar's remaining taxa today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The eight or more species of <a href="/wiki/Elephant_bird" title="Elephant bird">elephant birds</a>, giant flightless <a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratites</a> in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Aepyornis" title="Aepyornis">Aepyornis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mullerornis" title="Mullerornis">Mullerornis</a></i>, are extinct from over-hunting,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> as well as 17 species of lemur, known as giant, <a href="/wiki/Subfossil_lemur" title="Subfossil lemur">subfossil lemurs</a>. Some of these lemurs typically weighed over 150 kilograms (330 lb), and fossils have provided evidence of human butchery on many species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_New_Zealand" title="List of extinct animals of New Zealand">List of extinct animals of New Zealand</a></div><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_of_New_Zealand" title="Biodiversity of New Zealand">Biodiversity of New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_New_Zealand_environment" title="Timeline of the New Zealand environment">Timeline of the New Zealand environment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species_in_New_Zealand" title="Invasive species in New Zealand">Invasive species in New Zealand</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> is characterised by its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_isolation" title="Geographic isolation">geographic isolation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insular_biogeography" title="Insular biogeography">island biogeography</a>, and had been isolated from mainland Australia for 80 million years. It was the last large land mass to be colonised by humans. The arrival of <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesian</a> settlers circa 12th century resulted in the extinction of all of the islands' megafaunal birds within several hundred years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kolbert_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-kolbert-65">[65]</a></sup> The last <a href="/wiki/Moa" title="Moa">moa</a>, large flightless <a href="/wiki/Ratite" title="Ratite">ratites</a>, became extinct within 200 years of the arrival of human settlers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup> The Polynesians also introduced the <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_rat" title="Polynesian rat">Polynesian rat</a>. This may have put some pressure on other birds but at the time of early European contact (18th Century) and colonisation (19th Century) the bird life was prolific. With them, the Europeans brought <a href="/wiki/Black_rat" title="Black rat">ship rats</a>, possums, cats and mustelids which decimated native bird life, some of which had adapted <a href="/wiki/Flightless_bird" title="Flightless bird">flightlessness</a> and ground nesting habits and others had no defensive behavior as a result of having no extant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> mammalian predators. The <a href="/wiki/Kakapo" title="Kakapo">kakapo</a>, the world's biggest parrot, which is flightless, now only exists in managed breeding sanctuaries. New Zealand's national emblem, the <a href="/wiki/Kiwi" title="Kiwi">kiwi</a>, is on the endangered bird list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kolbert_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-kolbert-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_North_American_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of North American animals extinct in the Holocene">List of North American animals extinct in the Holocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_South_American_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of South American animals extinct in the Holocene">List of South American animals extinct in the Holocene</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mammoth_House_(Replica).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2252" data-file-width="3842" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG/220px-Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG/330px-Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG/440px-Mammoth_House_%28Replica%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mammoth_House_(Replica).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoth</a> bone hut, based on finds in <a href="/wiki/Mezhyrich" title="Mezhyrich">Mezhyrich</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ectopistes_migratorius_(passenger_pigeon).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2640" data-file-width="2784" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg/170px-Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg/255px-Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg/340px-Ectopistes_migratorius_%28passenger_pigeon%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ectopistes_migratorius_(passenger_pigeon).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Passenger_pigeon" title="Passenger pigeon">passenger pigeon</a> was a species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon">pigeon</a> endemic to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. It experienced a rapid decline in the late 1800s due to habitat destruction and intense hunting after the arrival of Europeans. The last wild bird is thought to have been shot in 1901.</div></div></div>
<p>There has been a debate as to the extent to which the disappearance of <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a> at the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial period</a> can be attributed to human activities by hunting, or even by slaughter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> of prey populations. Discoveries at Monte Verde in South America and at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meadowcroft_Rock_Shelter" title="Meadowcroft Rock Shelter">Meadowcroft Rock Shelter</a> in Pennsylvania have caused a controversy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-67">[67]</a></sup> regarding the <a href="/wiki/Clovis_culture" title="Clovis culture">Clovis culture</a>. There likely would have been human settlements prior to the Clovis Culture, and the history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric_migration_and_settlement_of_the_Americas_from_Asia" title="Prehistoric migration and settlement of the Americas from Asia">humans in the Americas</a> may extend back many thousands of years before the Clovis culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-67">[67]</a></sup> The amount of correlation between human arrival and megafauna extinction is still being debated: for example, in <a href="/wiki/Wrangel_Island" title="Wrangel Island">Wrangel Island</a> in Siberia the extinction of dwarf <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoths</a> (approximately 2000 BCE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> did not coincide with the arrival of humans, nor did megafaunal mass extinction on the South American continent, although it has been suggested climate changes induced by anthropogenic effects elsewhere in the world may have contributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Comparisons are sometimes made between recent extinctions (approximately since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>) and the Pleistocene extinction near the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial period</a>. The latter is exemplified by the extinction of large herbivores such as the <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoth</a> and the carnivores that preyed on them. Humans of this era actively hunted the <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> but it is not known if this hunting was the cause of the subsequent massive ecological changes, widespread extinctions and climate changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doughty,_C._E._2010_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doughty,_C._E._2010-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ecosystems encountered by the first Americans had not been exposed to human interaction, and may have been far less resilient to human made changes than the ecosystems encountered by industrial era humans. Therefore, the actions of the Clovis people, despite seeming insignificant by today's standards could indeed have had a profound effect on the ecosystems and wild life which was entirely unused to human influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Afroeurasia">Afroeurasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Afroeurasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_African_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of African animals extinct in the Holocene">List of African animals extinct in the Holocene</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Asian_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene" title="List of Asian animals extinct in the Holocene">List of Asian animals extinct in the Holocene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_extinct_animals_of_Europe" title="List of extinct animals of Europe">List of extinct animals of Europe</a></div>
<p>Africa experienced the smallest decline in megafauna compared to the other continents. This is presumably due to the idea that Afroeurasian megafauna evolved alongside humans, and thus developed a healthy fear of them, unlike the comparatively tame animals of other continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Unlike other continents, the megafauna of Eurasia went extinct over a relatively long period of time, possibly due to climate fluctuations fragmenting and decreasing populations, leaving them vulnerable to over-exploitation, as with the <a href="/wiki/Steppe_bison" title="Steppe bison">steppe bison</a> (<i>Bison priscus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The warming of the arctic region caused the rapid decline of grasslands, which had a negative effect on the grazing megafauna of Eurasia. Most of what once was <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_steppe" title="Mammoth steppe">mammoth steppe</a> has been converted to <a href="/wiki/Mire" title="Mire">mire</a>, rendering the environment incapable of supporting them, notably the <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="994" data-file-width="558" height="392" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg/220px-Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg/330px-Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg/440px-Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aridity_ice_age_vs_early_holocene_vs_modern.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Top:</i> <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">Arid</a> ice age climate<div style="clear:both;"></div><i>Middle:</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_period" title="Atlantic period">Atlantic Period</a>, warm and wet<div style="clear:both;"></div><i>Bottom:</i> Potential vegetation in climate now if not for human effects like agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_paleoclimate_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL_paleoclimate-73">[73]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="770" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg/220px-Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg/330px-Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg/440px-Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bramble-cay-melomys.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bramble_Cay_melomys" title="Bramble Cay melomys">Bramble Cay melomys</a> were declared extinct in June 2016. This is likely the first mammalian extinction due to <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>One of the main theories to the extinction is <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The climate change theory has suggested that a change in climate near the end of the late Pleistocene stressed the megafauna to the point of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Some scientists favor abrupt climate change as the catalyst for the extinction of the mega-fauna at the end of the Pleistocene, but there are many who believe increased hunting from early modern humans also played a part, with others even suggesting that the two interacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> However, the annual mean temperature of the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interglacial_period" title="Interglacial period">interglacial period</a> for the last 10,000 years is no higher than that of previous interglacial periods, yet some of the same megafauna survived similar temperature increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andersen1973_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andersen1973-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashworth1980_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ashworth1980-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birks1973_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birks1973-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birks_&amp;_Birks1980_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birks_&amp;_Birks1980-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradely1985_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradely1985-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis1976_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1976-83">[83]</a></sup> In the Americas, a controversial explanation for the shift in climate is presented under the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas_impact_hypothesis" title="Younger Dryas impact hypothesis">Younger Dryas impact hypothesis</a>, which states that the impact of comets cooled global temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Firestone2006_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Firestone2006-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS07A_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS07A-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunch_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunch-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Megafaunal_extinction">Megafaunal extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Megafaunal extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Megafauna play a significant role in the lateral transport of mineral nutrients in an ecosystem, tending to translocate them from areas of high to those of lower abundance. They do so by their movement between the time they consume the nutrient and the time they release it through elimination (or, to a much lesser extent, through decomposition after death).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolf2013_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolf2013-87">[87]</a></sup> In South America's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>, it is estimated that such lateral diffusion was reduced over 98% following the megafaunal extinctions that occurred roughly 12,500 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall_2013_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall_2013-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoughtyWolf2013_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-DoughtyWolf2013-89">[89]</a></sup> Given that <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> availability is thought to limit productivity in much of the region, the decrease in its transport from the western part of the basin and from floodplains (both of which derive their supply from the uplift of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>) to other areas is thought to have significantly impacted the region's ecology, and the effects may not yet have reached their limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DoughtyWolf2013_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-DoughtyWolf2013-89">[89]</a></sup> The extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoths</a> allowed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasslands" title="Grasslands">grasslands</a> they had maintained through grazing habits to become birch forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doughty,_C._E._2010_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Doughty,_C._E._2010-13">[13]</a></sup> The new forest and the resulting forest fires may have induced <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doughty,_C._E._2010_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Doughty,_C._E._2010-13">[13]</a></sup> Such disappearances might be the result of the proliferation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_human" title="Modern human">modern humans</a>; some recent studies favor this theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>Large populations of megaherbivores have the potential to contribute greatly to the atmospheric concentration of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, which is an important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. Modern <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminant</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a> produce methane as a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Foregut_fermentation" title="Foregut fermentation">foregut fermentation</a> in digestion, and release it through belching or flatulence. Today, around 20% of annual <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> come from livestock methane release. In the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a>, it has been estimated that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sauropod" title="Sauropod">sauropods</a> could have emitted 520 million tons of methane to the atmosphere annually,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-91">[91]</a></sup> contributing to the warmer climate of the time (up to 10 °C warmer than at present).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sauropod_methane_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-sauropod_methane-92">[92]</a></sup> This large emission follows from the enormous estimated biomass of sauropods, and because methane production of individual herbivores is believed to be almost proportional to their mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_91-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recent studies have indicated that the extinction of megafaunal herbivores may have caused a reduction in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a>. This hypothesis is relatively new.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-93">[93]</a></sup> One study examined the methane emissions from the <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">bison</a> that occupied the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> of North America before contact with European settlers. The study estimated that the removal of the bison caused a decrease of as much as 2.2 million tons per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelliher_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelliher-94">[94]</a></sup> Another study examined the change in the methane concentration in the atmosphere at the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleistocene_epoch" title="Pleistocene epoch">Pleistocene epoch</a> after the extinction of megafauna in the Americas. After early humans migrated to the Americas about 13,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>, their hunting and other associated ecological impacts led to the extinction of many megafaunal species there. Calculations suggest that this extinction decreased methane production by about 9.6 million tons per year. This suggests that the absence of megafaunal methane emissions may have contributed to the abrupt climatic cooling at the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-93">[93]</a></sup> The decrease in atmospheric methane that occurred at that time, as recorded in <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, was 2–4 times more rapid than any other decrease in the last half million years, suggesting that an unusual mechanism was at work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_93-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hyperdisease hypothesis, proposed by Ross MacPhee in 1997, states that the megafaunal die-off was due to an indirect transmission of diseases by newly arriving <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">aboriginal humans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacPhee&amp;Marx1997_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacPhee&amp;Marx1997-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacPhee&amp;Marx1998_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacPhee&amp;Marx1998-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> According to MacPhee, aboriginals or animals travelling with them, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_dog" title="Domestic dog">domestic dogs</a> or livestock, introduced one or more highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virulent" title="Virulent">virulent</a> diseases into new environments whose native population had no immunity to them, eventually leading to their extinction. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-selection" title="K-selection">K-selection</a> animals, such as the now-extinct megafauna, are especially vulnerable to diseases, as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-selection" title="R-selection">r-selection</a> animals who have a shorter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gestation_period" title="Gestation period">gestation period</a> and a higher population size. Humans are thought to be the sole cause as other earlier migrations of animals into North America from Eurasia did not cause extinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacPhee&amp;Marx1997_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-MacPhee&amp;Marx1997-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many problems with this theory, as this disease would have to meet several criteria: it has to be able to sustain itself in an environment with no <a href="/wiki/Host_(biology)" title="Host (biology)">hosts</a>; it has to have a high <a href="/wiki/Infection_rate" title="Infection rate">infection rate</a>; and be extremely lethal, with a <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rate</a> of 50–75%. Disease has to be very virulent to kill off all the individuals in a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, and even such a virulent disease as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Nile_Virus" title="West Nile Virus">West Nile Virus</a> is unlikely to have caused extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lyons_et_al2004_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lyons_et_al2004-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, diseases have been the cause for some extinctions. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Avian_malaria" title="Avian malaria">avian malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avipoxvirus" title="Avipoxvirus">avipoxvirus</a>, for example, have had a negative impact on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_birds_of_Hawaii" title="Endemic birds of Hawaii">endemic birds of Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LaPointe_2012_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-LaPointe_2012-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defaunation">Defaunation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Defaunation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Susa_group,_mountain_gorilla.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1632" data-file-width="1552" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg/220px-Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg/330px-Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg/440px-Susa_group%2C_mountain_gorilla.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Susa_group,_mountain_gorilla.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>There are roughly 880 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_gorillas" title="Mountain gorillas">mountain gorillas</a> remaining in existence. 60% of <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primate</a> species face an anthropogenically driven extinction crisis and 75% have declining populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primates_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-primates-100">[100]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The loss of species from ecological communities, defaunation, is primarily driven by human activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirzo_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-dirzo-9">[9]</a></sup> This has resulted in <a href="/wiki/Empty_forest" title="Empty forest">empty forests</a>, ecological communities depleted of large vertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primack2014_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-primack2014-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpecialIssue_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-SpecialIssue-24">[24]</a></sup> This is not to be confused with extinction, as it includes both the disappearance of species and declines in abundance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Defaunation effects were first implied at the Symposium of Plant-Animal Interactions at the University of Campinas, Brazil in 1988 in the context of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neotropic_ecozone" title="Neotropic ecozone">neotropical forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eco_and_evo_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-eco_and_evo-103">[103]</a></sup> Since then, the term has gained broader usage in conservation biology as a global phenomenon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirzo_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-dirzo-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eco_and_evo_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-eco_and_evo-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Big_cat" title="Big cat">Big cat</a> populations have severely declined over the last half-century and could face extinction in the following decades. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">IUCN</a> estimates: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lions" title="Lions">lions</a> are down to 25,000, from 450,000; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopards" title="Leopards">leopards</a> are down to 50,000, from 750,000; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cheetahs" title="Cheetahs">cheetahs</a> are down to 12,000, from 45,000; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tigers" title="Tigers">tigers</a> are down to 3,000 in the wild, from 50,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigcats_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigcats-104">[104]</a></sup> A December 2016 study by the Zoological Society of London, <a href="/wiki/Panthera_Corporation" title="Panthera Corporation">Panthera Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Conservation_Society" title="Wildlife Conservation Society">Wildlife Conservation Society</a> showed that cheetahs are far closer to extinction than previously thought, with only 7,100 remaining in the wild, and crammed within only 9% of their historic range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visser_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Visser-105">[105]</a></sup> Human pressures are to blame for the cheetah population crash, including prey loss due to overhunting by people, retaliatory killing from farmers, habitat loss and the illegal wildlife trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Pollinator_decline" title="Pollinator decline">pollinator decline</a> refers to the reduction in abundance of insect and other animal pollinators in many ecosystems worldwide beginning at the end of the twentieth century, and continuing into the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Pollinators, which are necessary for 75% of food crops, are declining globally in both abundance and diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>  A 2017 study led by <a href="/wiki/Radboud_University_Nijmegen" title="Radboud University Nijmegen">Radboud University's</a> Hans de Kroon indicated that the biomass of insect life in Germany had declined by three-quarters in the previous 25 years.  Participating researcher Dave Goulson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sussex_University" title="Sussex University">Sussex University</a> stated that their study suggested that humans are making large parts of the planet uninhabitable for wildlife.  Goulson characterized the situation as an approaching "ecological Armageddon", adding that "if we lose the insects then everything is going to collapse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Various species are predicted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_critically_endangered_species" title="List of critically endangered species">become extinct in the near future</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> among them the <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceros</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> nonhuman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">primates</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-primates_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-primates-100">[100]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pangolins" title="Pangolins">pangolins</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giraffes" title="Giraffes">giraffes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suttergiraffe_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-suttergiraffe-117">[117]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">Hunting</a> alone threatens bird and mammalian populations around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> Mammals in particular have suffered such severe losses as the result of human activity that it could take several million years for them to recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> According to the WWF's 2016 <a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Index" title="Living Planet Index">Living Planet Index</a>, global wildlife populations have declined 58% since 1970, primarily due to habitat destruction, over-hunting and pollution. They project that if current trends continue, 67% of wildlife could disappear by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> In their 2018 report, the WWF found that <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a> of resources by the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">global population</a> has destroyed 60% of animals since 1970, and this continued destruction of wildlife is an emergency which threatens the survival of human civilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> 189 countries, which are signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (Rio Accord),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> have committed to preparing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plan</a>, a first step at identifying specific <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a> and habitats, country by country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_extinction">Recent extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Recent extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_extinct_in_the_wild_species" title="IUCN Red List extinct in the wild species">IUCN Red List extinct in the wild species</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_endangered_species" title="List of endangered species">List of endangered species</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_critically_endangered_species" title="List of critically endangered species">List of critically endangered species</a></div>
<p>Recent extinctions are more directly attributable to human influences, whereas prehistoric extinctions can be attributed to other factors, such as global climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceballos_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ceballos-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirzo_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-dirzo-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> (IUCN) characterises 'recent' extinction as those that have occurred past the cut-off point of 1500,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> and at least 875 species have gone extinct since that time and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> Some species, such as the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A8re_David%27s_deer" title="Père David's deer">Père David's deer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_crow" title="Hawaiian crow">Hawaiian crow</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-133">[133]</a></sup> are extinct in the wild, and survive solely in captive populations. Other species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Florida_panther" title="Florida panther">Florida panther</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Ecological_extinction" title="Ecological extinction">ecologically extinct</a>, surviving in such low numbers that they essentially have no impact on the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckinney2013_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-mckinney2013-134">[134]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>318</span></sup> Other populations are only <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">locally extinct</a> (extirpated), still existence elsewhere, but reduced in distribution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mckinney2013_134-1"><a href="#cite_note-mckinney2013-134">[134]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>75–77</span></sup> as with the extinction of <a href="/wiki/Gray_whale" title="Gray whale">gray whales</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> and of the <a href="/wiki/Leatherback_sea_turtle" title="Leatherback sea turtle">leatherback sea turtle</a> in Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_destruction">Habitat destruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Habitat destruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_global_warming" title="Extinction risk from global warming">Extinction risk from global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_and_environmental_impact_of_palm_oil" title="Social and environmental impact of palm oil">Social and environmental impact of palm oil</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg/250px-Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg/375px-Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg/500px-Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malayasia_iko_2002169.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of rainforest converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">oil palm</a> plantations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Global warming is widely accepted as being a contributor to extinction worldwide, in a similar way that <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">previous extinction events</a> have generally included a rapid change in global climate and meteorology. It is also expected to disrupt sex ratios in many reptiles which have <a href="/wiki/Temperature-dependent_sex_determination" title="Temperature-dependent sex determination">temperature-dependent sex determination</a>.
</p><p>The removal of land to clear way for <a href="/wiki/Social_and_environmental_impact_of_palm_oil" title="Social and environmental impact of palm oil">palm oil plantations</a> releases carbon emissions held in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peatland" title="Peatland">peatlands</a> of Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foster_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foster-138">[138]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_NYT_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal_NYT-139">[139]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">Palm oil</a> mainly serves as a cheap <a href="/wiki/Cooking_oil" title="Cooking oil">cooking oil</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> and also as a (<a href="/wiki/Food_vs._fuel" title="Food vs. fuel">controversial</a>) <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>. However, damage to peatland contributes to 4% of global greenhouse gas emissions, and 8% of those caused by burning <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> Palm oil cultivation has also been criticized for other impacts to the environment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenpeace_Cooking_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenpeace_Cooking-143">[143]</a></sup> including deforestation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> which has threatened critically endangered species such as the <a href="/wiki/Orangutan" title="Orangutan">orangutan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cspi_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-cspi-145">[145]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mail_Online_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mail_Online-146">[146]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Tree-kangaroo" title="Tree-kangaroo">tree-kangaroo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-147"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> The IUCN stated in 2016 that the species could go extinct within a decade if measures are not taken to preserve the rainforests in which they live.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scientists and academics assert that <a href="/wiki/Intensive_animal_farming" title="Intensive animal farming">industrial agriculture</a> and the growing demand for <a href="/wiki/Meat#Environmental_impact" title="Meat">meat</a> is contributing to significant global <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a> as this is a significant driver of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and habitat destruction; species-rich habitats, such as significant portions of the Amazon region, are being converted to agriculture for meat production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zalasiewicz2015_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zalasiewicz2015-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-149"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> A 2017 study by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> (WWF) found that 60% of biodiversity loss can be attributed to the vast scale of feed crop cultivation required to rear tens of billions of farm animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> Moreover, a 2006 report by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO) of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Livestock%27s_Long_Shadow" title="Livestock's Long Shadow">Livestock's Long Shadow</a></i>, also found that the livestock sector is a "leading player" in biodiversity loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising levels of carbon dioxide are resulting in influx of this gas into the ocean, increasing its acidity. Marine organisms which possess <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">calcium carbonate shells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeletons</a> experience physiological pressure as the carbonate reacts with acid. For example, this is already resulting in <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> on various <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> worldwide, which provide valuable habitat and maintain a high biodiversity. Marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a> and other invertebrates are also affected, as are the organisms that feed on them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to a 2018 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>, global <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">Orca</a> populations are poised to collapse due to toxic chemical and <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">PCB</a> pollution. PCBs are still leaking into the sea in spite of being banned for decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some researchers suggest that by 2050 there could be more <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution" title="Plastic pollution">plastic</a> than fish in the oceans by weight,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutter_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sutter-37">[37]</a></sup> with about 8,800,000 metric tons (9,700,000 short tons) of plastic being discharged into the oceans annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Disposable_product" title="Disposable product">Single-use plastics</a>, such as plastic shopping bags, make up the bulk of this, and can often be ingested by marine life, such as with sea turtles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> These plastics can degrade into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microplastic" title="Microplastic">microplastics</a>, smaller particles that can affect a larger array of species. Microplastics make up the bulk of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_Garbage_Patch" title="Great Pacific Garbage Patch">Great Pacific Garbage Patch</a>, and their smaller size is detrimental to cleanup efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-158"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overexploitation">Overexploitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Overexploitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Species_affected_by_poaching" title="Species affected by poaching">Species affected by poaching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="1811" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg/220px-Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg/330px-Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg/440px-Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vaquita4_Olson_NOAA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Vaquita" title="Vaquita">vaquita</a>, the world's most endangered marine mammal, has been reduced to only 30 individuals as of February 2017. They are often killed by commercial fishing nets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-159"><a href="#cite_note-159">[159]</a></sup> As of March 2018, only 12 remain, according to The International Committee for the Recovery of the Vaquita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="738" data-file-width="942" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/220px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/330px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg/440px-Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Surexploitation_morue_surp%C3%AAcheEn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The collapse of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_cod" title="Atlantic cod">Atlantic cod</a> off the coast of Newfoundland in 1992 as a result of <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Overhunting can reduce the local population of <a href="/wiki/Game_(hunting)" title="Game (hunting)">game animals</a> by more than half, as well as reducing population density, and may lead to extinction for some species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-161"><a href="#cite_note-161">[161]</a></sup> Populations located nearer to villages are significantly more at risk of depletion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peres2006_162-0"><a href="#cite_note-peres2006-162">[162]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup> Several conservationist organizations, among them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFAW" title="IFAW">IFAW</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HSUS" title="HSUS">HSUS</a>, assert that <a href="/wiki/Trophy_hunting" title="Trophy hunting">trophy hunters</a>, particularly from the United States, are playing a significant role in the decline of giraffes, which they refer to as a "silent extinction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup>
</p><p>The surge in the mass killings by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poachers" title="Poachers">poachers</a> involved in the illegal ivory trade along with habitat loss is threatening <a href="/wiki/African_elephant" title="African elephant">African elephant</a> populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sevenyears_166-0"><a href="#cite_note-sevenyears-166">[166]</a></sup> In 1979, their populations stood at 1.7 million; at present there are fewer than 400,000 remaining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup> Prior to European colonization, scientists believe Africa was home to roughly 20 million elephants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Elephant_Census" title="Great Elephant Census">Great Elephant Census</a>, 30% of African elephants (or 144,000 individuals) disappeared over a seven-year period, 2007 to 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sevenyears_166-1"><a href="#cite_note-sevenyears-166">[166]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup> African elephants could become extinct by 2035 if poaching rates continue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suttergiraffe_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-suttergiraffe-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fishing has had a devastating effect on marine organism populations for several centuries even before the explosion of destructive and highly effective fishing practices like <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-170"><a href="#cite_note-170">[170]</a></sup> Humans are unique among predators in that they regularly predate on other adult <a href="/wiki/Apex_predator" title="Apex predator">apex predators</a>, particularly in marine environments;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bluefin_tuna" title="Bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_whales" title="Blue whales">blue whales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_right_whales" title="North Atlantic right whales">North Atlantic right whales</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-171"><a href="#cite_note-171">[171]</a></sup> and various <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> in particular are particularly vulnerable to predation pressure from human fishing. A 2016 study published in <i>Science</i> concludes that humans tend to hunt larger species, and this could disrupt ocean ecosystems for millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-172"><a href="#cite_note-172">[172]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease_2">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Decline_in_amphibian_populations" title="Decline in amphibian populations">Decline in amphibian populations</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_nose_syndrome" title="White nose syndrome">White nose syndrome</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">Colony collapse disorder</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bufo_periglenes2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2385" data-file-width="3576" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bufo_periglenes2.jpg/220px-Bufo_periglenes2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bufo_periglenes2.jpg/330px-Bufo_periglenes2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Bufo_periglenes2.jpg/440px-Bufo_periglenes2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bufo_periglenes2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Golden_toad" title="Golden toad">golden toad</a> of Costa Rica, extinct since around 1989. Its disappearance has been attributed to a confluence of several factors, including <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> warming, fungus, habitat loss and the introduction of invasive species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg/220px-Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg/330px-Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg/440px-Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecnomiohyla_rabborum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Toughie_(frog)" title="Toughie (frog)">Toughie</a>, the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbs%27_fringe-limbed_treefrog" title="Rabbs' fringe-limbed treefrog">Rabbs' fringe-limbed treefrog</a>, died in September 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup> The species was killed off from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chytrid_fungus" title="Chytrid fungus">chytrid fungus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Batrachochytrium_dendrobatidis" title="Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis">Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-176"><a href="#cite_note-176">[176]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The decline of amphibian populations has also been identified as an indicator of environmental degradation. As well as habitat loss, introduced predators and pollution, <a href="/wiki/Chytridiomycosis" title="Chytridiomycosis">Chytridiomycosis</a>, a fungal infection thought to have been accidentally spread by human travel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> has caused severe population drops of several species of frogs, including (among many others) the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_toad" title="Golden toad">golden toad</a> in Costa Rica and the <a href="/wiki/Gastric-brooding_frog" title="Gastric-brooding frog">Gastric-brooding frog</a> in Australia. Many other amphibian species now face extinction, including the reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabb%27s_Fringe-limbed_Treefrog" title="Rabb's Fringe-limbed Treefrog">Rabb's fringe-limbed treefrog</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Endling" title="Endling">endling</a>, and the extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Panamanian_golden_frog" title="Panamanian golden frog">Panamanian golden frog</a> in the wild. Chytrid fungus has spread across Australia, New Zealand, Central America and Africa, including countries with high amphibian diversity such as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forests</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Batrachochytrium_salamandrivorans" title="Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans">Batrachochytrium salamandrivorans</a></i> is a similar infection currently threatening <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>. Amphibians are now the most endangered vertebrate group, having existed for more than 300 million years through three other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinctions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>17</span></sup>
</p><p>Millions of bats in the US have been dying off since 2012 due to a fungal infection spread from European bats, which appear to be immune. Population drops have been as great as 90% within five years, and extinction of at least one bat species is predicted. There is currently no form of treatment, and such declines have been described as "unprecedented" in bat evolutionary history by Alan Hicks of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Environmental_Conservation" title="New York State Department of Environmental Conservation">New York State Department of Environmental Conservation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 2007 and 2013, over ten million beehives were abandoned due to <a href="/wiki/Colony_collapse_disorder" title="Colony collapse disorder">colony collapse disorder</a>, which causes <a href="/wiki/Worker_bee" title="Worker bee">worker bees</a> to abandon the <a href="/wiki/Queen_bee" title="Queen bee">queen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-178"><a href="#cite_note-178">[178]</a></sup> This disorder has been attributed to - amongst other things - <a href="/wiki/Neonicotinoid" title="Neonicotinoid">neonicotinoids</a> consumed by worker bees from the extensive use of pesticides in human farming methods.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holocene_extinction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some leading scientists have advocated for the global community to designate as protected areas 30 percent of the planet by 2030, and 50 percent by 2050, in order to mitigate the contemporary extinction crisis as the human population is projected to grow to 10 billion by the middle of the century. Human consumption of food and water resources is also projected to double by this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup>
</p>
