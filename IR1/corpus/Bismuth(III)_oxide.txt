<p><b>Bismuth(III) oxide</b> is perhaps the most industrially important compound of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>. It is also a common starting point for bismuth chemistry.  It is found naturally as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Bismite" title="Bismite">bismite</a> (monoclinic) and sphaerobismoite (tetragonal, much more rare), but it is usually obtained as a by-product of the smelting of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> ores.  Bismuth trioxide is commonly used to produce the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_eggs" title="Dragon's eggs">Dragon's eggs</a>" effect in <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, as a replacement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_lead" title="Red lead">red lead</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structures adopted by Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  differ substantially from those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenic(III)_oxide" title="Arsenic(III) oxide">arsenic(III) oxide</a>, As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimony(III)_oxide" title="Antimony(III) oxide">antimony(III) oxide</a>, Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bi2O3_phases.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="480" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bi2O3_phases.svg/300px-Bi2O3_phases.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bi2O3_phases.svg/450px-Bi2O3_phases.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Bi2O3_phases.svg/600px-Bi2O3_phases.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bi2O3_phases.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Existence domains of the four polymorphs of Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> as a function of temperature. (a) The α-phase transforms to the δ-phase when heated above 727 °C, which remains the structure until the melting point, 824 °C, is reached. When cooled, the δ-phase transforms into either the β-phase at 650 °C, shown in (b), or the γ-phase at 639 °C, shown in (c). The β-phase transforms to the α-phase at 303 °C. The γ-phase may persist to room temperature when the cooling rate is very slow, otherwise it transforms to the α-phase at 500 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Bismuth oxide, Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has five crystallographic <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>. The room temperature phase, α-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> crystal structure. There are three high temperature phases, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> β-phase, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centred_cubic" title="Body-centred cubic">body-centred cubic</a> γ-phase, a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> δ-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> phase and an ε- phase.
The room temperature α-phase has a complex structure with layers of oxygen atoms with layers of bismuth atoms between them. The bismuth atoms are in two different environments which can be described as distorted 6 and 5 coordinate respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MalmrosFernholt1970_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MalmrosFernholt1970-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>β-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a structure related to <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wells_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>γ-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a structure related to that of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_silicon_oxide" title="Bismuth silicon oxide">Bi<sub>12</sub>SiO<sub>20</sub></a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sillenite" title="Sillenite">sillenite</a>), where a fraction of the Bi atoms occupy the position occupied by Si<sup>IV</sup>, and may be written as Bi<sub>12</sub>Bi<sub>0.8</sub>O<sub>19.2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RadaevSimonov1992_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RadaevSimonov1992-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a defective <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a>-type crystal structure in which two of the eight oxygen sites in the unit cell are vacant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harwig1978_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harwig1978-5">[5]</a></sup>
ε- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a structure related to the α- and β- phases but as the structure is fully ordered it is an ionic insulator. It can be prepared by hydrothermal means and transforms to the α- phase at 400 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RadaevSimonov1992_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RadaevSimonov1992-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> α-phase transforms to the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> δ-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> when heated above 729 °C, which remains the structure until the melting point, 824 °C, is reached. The behaviour of Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> on cooling from the δ-phase is more complex, with the possible formation of two intermediate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phases_of_matter#Metastable_phases" title="Phases of matter">metastable</a> phases; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> β-phase or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centred_cubic" title="Body-centred cubic">body-centred cubic</a> γ-phase. The γ-phase can exist at room temperature with very slow cooling rates, but α- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> always forms on cooling the β-phase. Even though when formed by heat, it reverts to α- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> when the temperature drops back below 727 °C, δ-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> can be formed directly through electrodeposition and remain relatively stable at room temperature, in an electrolyte of bismuth compounds that is also rich in sodium or potassium hydroxide so as to have a pH near 14.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conductivity">Conductivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conductivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The α-phase exhibits p-type electronic conductivity (the charge is carried by positive holes) at room temperature which transforms to n-type conductivity (charge is carried by electrons) between 550 °C and 650 °C, depending on the oxygen partial pressure.
The conductivity in the β, γ and δ-phases is predominantly <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a> with oxide ions being the main charge carrier. Of these δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has the highest reported conductivity. At 750 °C the conductivity of δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is typically about 1 Scm<sup>−1</sup>, about three orders of magnitude greater than the intermediate phases and four orders greater than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> phase. The conductivity in the β, γ and δ-phases is predominantly <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a> with oxide ions being the main charge carrier. 
δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a defective fluorite-type crystal structure in which two of the eight oxygen sites in the unit cell are vacant. These intrinsic <a href="/wiki/Vacancy_defect" title="Vacancy defect">vacancies</a> are highly mobile due to the high polarisability of the cation sub-lattice with the 6<i>s</i><sup>2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a> electrons of Bi<sup>3+</sup>. The Bi-O bonds have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent bond</a> character and are therefore weaker than purely ionic bonds, so the oxygen ions can jump into <a href="/wiki/Vacancy_defect" title="Vacancy defect">vacancies</a> more freely.
</p><p>The arrangement of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms within the unit cell of δ-Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has been the subject of much debate in the past. Three different models have been proposed. Sillén (1937) used powder X-ray diffraction on quenched samples and reported the structure of Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> was a simple <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> phase with oxygen <a href="/wiki/Vacancy_defect" title="Vacancy defect">vacancies</a> ordered along&lt;111&gt;, i.e. along the cube body diagonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sillen_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sillen-6">[6]</a></sup> Gattow and Schroder (1962) rejected this model, preferring to describe each oxygen site (8c site) in the unit cell as having 75% occupancy. In other words, the six oxygen atoms are randomly distributed over the eight possible oxygen sites in the unit cell. Currently, most experts seem to favour the latter description as a completely disordered oxygen sub-lattice accounts for the high conductivity in a better way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gattow_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gattow-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Willis (1965) used <a href="/wiki/Neutron_diffraction" title="Neutron diffraction">neutron diffraction</a> to study the fluorite (CaF<sub>2</sub>) system. He determined that it could not be described by the ideal fluorite crystal structure, rather, the fluorine atoms were displaced from regular 8c positions towards the centres of the interstitial positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willis-8">[8]</a></sup> Shuk et al. (1996)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shuk_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shuk-9">[9]</a></sup> and Sammes et al. (1999)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sammes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sammes-10">[10]</a></sup> suggest that because of the high degree of disorder in δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, the Willis model could also be used to describe its structure.
</p>
<h3><span id="Use_in_Solid-oxide_fuel_cells_.28SOFCs.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Solid-oxide_fuel_cells_(SOFCs)">Use in Solid-oxide fuel cells (SOFCs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in Solid-oxide fuel cells (SOFCs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Interest has centred on δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> as it is principally an ionic conductor. In addition to electrical properties, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> properties are very important when considering possible applications for solid electrolytes. High <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> coefficients represent large dimensional variations under heating and cooling, which would limit the performance of an electrolyte. The transition from the high-temperature δ- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to the intermediate β- Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is accompanied by a large volume change and consequently, a deterioration of the mechanical properties of the material. This, combined with the very narrow stability range of the δ-phase (727–824 °C), has led to studies on its stabilization to room temperature.
</p><p>Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> easily forms solid solutions with many other metal oxides. These doped systems exhibit a complex array of structures and properties dependent on the type of dopant, the dopant concentration and the thermal history of the sample. The most widely studied systems are those involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> metal oxides, Ln<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">yttria</a>, Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Rare earth metal cations are generally very stable, have similar chemical properties to one another and are similar in size to Bi<sup>3+</sup>, which has a radius of 1.03 Å, making them all excellent dopants. Furthermore, their ionic radii decrease fairly uniformly from La<sup>3</sup>+  (1.032 Å), through Nd<sup>3+</sup>, (0.983 Å), Gd<sup>3+</sup>,  (0.938 Å), Dy<sup>3+</sup>, (0.912 Å) and Er<sup>3+</sup>, (0.89 Å), to Lu<sup>3+</sup>, (0.861 Å) (known as the ‘<a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a>’), making them useful to study the effect of dopant size on the stability of the Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> phases.
</p><p>Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has also been used as sintering additive in the Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>- doped zirconia system for intermediate temperature SOFC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bismuth trioxide is commercially made from <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_oxynitrate" title="Bismuth oxynitrate">bismuth subnitrate</a>. The latter is produced by dissolving bismuth in hot <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>. Addition of excess <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> followed by continuous heating of the mixture precipitates bismuth(III) oxide as a heavy yellow powder. Also, the trioxide can be prepared by ignition of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_hydroxide" title="Bismuth hydroxide">bismuth hydroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">[1]</a></sup> Bismuth trioxide can be also obtained by heating bismuth subcarbonate at approximately 400 <sup>0</sup>C. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxidation with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_persulfate" title="Ammonium persulfate">ammonium persulfate</a> and dilute caustic soda gives <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bismuth tetroxide (page does not exist)">bismuth tetroxide</a>. The same product can be obtained by using other oxidizing agents such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_ferricyanide" title="Potassium ferricyanide">potassium ferricyanide</a> and concentrated caustic potash solution.
</p><p>Electrolysis of bismuth(III) oxide in hot concentrated alkali solution gives a scarlet red precipitate of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bismuth(V) oxide (page does not exist)">bismuth(V) oxide</a>. Bismuth(III) oxide reacts with mineral acids to give the corresponding bismuth(III) salts.
</p><p>
Reaction with <a href="/wiki/Acetic_anhydride" title="Acetic anhydride">acetic anhydride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a> gives bismuth trioleate.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bi2O3_models.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="349" data-file-width="500" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Bi2O3_models.png/300px-Bi2O3_models.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Bi2O3_models.png/450px-Bi2O3_models.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Bi2O3_models.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bi2O3_models.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>(a) Sillén model; vacancies ordered along&lt;111&gt;,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sillen_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-sillen-6">[6]</a></sup> (b) Gattow model; vacancies completely disordered in oxygen sub-lattice, with each oxygen site having 75% occupancy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gattow_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gattow-7">[7]</a></sup> (c) Willis model; oxygen atoms displaced from regular 8c sites (for example, the atom marked A in (b)) along&lt;111&gt; to 32f sites. The Bi<sup>3+</sup> ions labelled 1–4 in (c) correspond to those labelled 1–4 in (b).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Willis-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Atmospheric carbon dioxide or CO<sub>2</sub> dissolved in water readily reacts with Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to generate <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subcarbonate" title="Bismuth subcarbonate">bismuth subcarbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz-12">[12]</a></sup> Bismuth oxide is considered a basic oxide, which explains the high reactivity with CO<sub>2</sub>. However, when acidic cations such as Si(IV) are introduced within the structure of the bismuth oxide, the reaction with CO<sub>2</sub> do not occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ortiz-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_device_usage">Medical device usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bismuth(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Medical device usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bismuth oxide is occasionally used in dental materials to make them more opaque to X-rays than the surrounding tooth structure. In particular, bismuth (III) oxide has been used in hydraulic silicate cements (HSC), originally in "<a href="/wiki/Mineral_trioxide_aggregate" title="Mineral trioxide aggregate">MTA</a>" (a trade name, standing for the chemically-meaningless "<a href="/wiki/Mineral_trioxide_aggregate" title="Mineral trioxide aggregate">mineral trioxide aggregate</a>") from 10 to 20% by mass with a mixture of mainly di- and tri-calcium silicate powders.  Such HSC is used for dental treatments such as: apicoectomy, apexification, pulp capping, pulpotomy, pulp regeneration, internal repair of iatrogenic perforations, repair of resorption perforations, root canal sealing and obturation.  MTA sets into a hard filling material when mixed with water. Some resin-based materials also include an HSC with bismuth oxide.  Problems have allegedly arisen with bismuth oxide because it is claimed not to be inert at high pH, specifically that it slows the setting of the HSC, but also over time can lose color<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> by exposure to light or reaction with other materials that may have been used in the tooth treatment, such as sodium hypochlorite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
