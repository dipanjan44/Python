<p><b>Cadmium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Cd</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 48. This soft, bluish-white metal is chemically similar to the two other stable metals in <a href="/wiki/Group_12_element" title="Group 12 element">group 12</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>. Like zinc, it demonstrates <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> +2 in most of its compounds, and like mercury, it has a lower melting point than the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Group_3_element" title="Group 3 element">groups 3</a> through <a href="/wiki/Group_11_element" title="Group 11 element">11</a>. Cadmium and its <a href="/wiki/Congener_(chemistry)" title="Congener (chemistry)">congeners</a> in group 12 are often not considered transition metals, in that they do not have partly filled <i>d</i> or <i>f</i> electron shells in the elemental or common oxidation states. The average concentration of cadmium in Earth's crust is between 0.1 and 0.5 parts per million (ppm). It was discovered in 1817 simultaneously by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Stromeyer" title="Friedrich Stromeyer">Stromeyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Samuel_Leberecht_Hermann" title="Karl Samuel Leberecht Hermann">Hermann</a>, both in Germany, as an impurity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_carbonate" title="Zinc carbonate">zinc carbonate</a>.
</p><p>Cadmium occurs as a minor component in most zinc ores and is a byproduct of zinc production. Cadmium was used for a long time as a corrosion-resistant plating on <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, and cadmium compounds are used as red, orange and yellow <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_pigments" title="Cadmium pigments">pigments</a>, to colour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colored_glass" title="Colored glass">glass</a>, and to stabilize <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>. Cadmium use is generally decreasing because it is <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> (it is specifically listed in the European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROHS" title="ROHS">Restriction of Hazardous Substances</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">nickel-cadmium batteries</a> have been replaced with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-metal_hydride_battery" title="Nickel-metal hydride battery">nickel-metal hydride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion</a> batteries. One of its few new uses is <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a>.
</p><p>Although cadmium has no known biological function in higher organisms, a cadmium-dependent <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> has been found in marine <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cadmium is a soft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, bluish-white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divalent" title="Divalent">divalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>. It is similar in many respects to zinc but forms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complex</a> compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-5">[5]</a></sup> Unlike most other metals, cadmium is resistant to <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> and is used as a protective <a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">plate</a> on other metals. As a bulk metal, cadmium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insoluble" title="Insoluble">insoluble</a> in water and is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammable</a>; however, in its powdered form it may burn and release <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_oxide" title="Cadmium oxide">toxic fumes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATSDR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATSDR-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Cadmium_compounds" title="Category:Cadmium compounds">Category:Cadmium compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Although cadmium usually has an <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of +2, it also exists in the +1 state. Cadmium and its <a href="/wiki/Congener_(chemistry)" title="Congener (chemistry)">congeners</a> are not always considered transition metals, in that they do not have partly filled d or f electron shells in the elemental or common oxidation states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Cadmium burns in air to form brown amorphous <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_oxide" title="Cadmium oxide">cadmium oxide</a> (CdO); the <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> form of this compound is a dark red which changes color when heated, similar to <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">Hydrochloric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> dissolve cadmium by forming <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_chloride" title="Cadmium chloride">cadmium chloride</a> (CdCl<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_sulfate" title="Cadmium sulfate">cadmium sulfate</a> (CdSO<sub>4</sub>), or <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_nitrate" title="Cadmium nitrate">cadmium nitrate</a> (Cd(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>). The oxidation state +1 can be produced by dissolving cadmium in a mixture of cadmium chloride and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_chloride" title="Aluminium chloride">aluminium chloride</a>, forming the Cd<sub>2</sub><sup>2+</sup> cation, which is similar to the Hg<sub>2</sub><sup>2+</sup> cation in <a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_chloride" title="Mercury(I) chloride">mercury(I) chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Cd + CdCl<sub>2</sub> + 2 AlCl<sub>3</sub> → Cd<sub>2</sub>(AlCl<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The structures of many cadmium complexes with <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> have been determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cadmium_cutoff.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cadmium_cutoff.png/220px-Cadmium_cutoff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cadmium_cutoff.png/330px-Cadmium_cutoff.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cadmium_cutoff.png/440px-Cadmium_cutoff.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cadmium_cutoff.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The cadmium-113 total cross section clearly showing the cadmium cut-off</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cadmium" title="Isotopes of cadmium">Isotopes of cadmium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring cadmium is composed of 8 <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>. Two of them are <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radioactive</a>, and three are expected to <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">decay</a> but have not done so under laboratory conditions. The two natural radioactive isotopes are <sup>113</sup>Cd (<a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>, half-life is 7.7 × 10<sup>15</sup> years) and <sup>116</sup>Cd (two-neutrino <a href="/wiki/Double_beta_decay" title="Double beta decay">double beta decay</a>, half-life is 2.9 × 10<sup>19</sup> years). The other three are <sup>106</sup>Cd, <sup>108</sup>Cd (both <a href="/wiki/Double_electron_capture" title="Double electron capture">double electron capture</a>), and <sup>114</sup>Cd (double beta decay); only lower limits on these half-lives have been determined. At least three isotopes – <sup>110</sup>Cd, <sup>111</sup>Cd, and <sup>112</sup>Cd – are stable. Among the isotopes that do not occur naturally, the most long-lived are <sup>109</sup>Cd with a half-life of 462.6 days, and <sup>115</sup>Cd with a half-life of 53.46 hours. All of the remaining radioactive isotopes have half-lives of less than 2.5 hours, and the majority have half-lives of less than 5 minutes. Cadmium has 8 known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, with the most stable being <sup>113m</sup>Cd (<i>t</i><sub>1⁄2</sub> = 14.1 years), <sup>115m</sup>Cd (<i>t</i><sub>1⁄2</sub> = 44.6 days), and <sup>117m</sup>Cd (<i>t</i><sub>1⁄2</sub> = 3.36 hours).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The known isotopes of cadmium range in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> from 94.950 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>95</sup>Cd) to 131.946 u (<sup>132</sup>Cd). For isotopes lighter than 112 u, the primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and the dominant <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay product</a> is element 47 (<a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>). Heavier isotopes decay mostly through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a> producing element 49 (<a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>One isotope of cadmium, <sup>113</sup>Cd, absorbs neutrons with high selectivity:  With very high probability, neutrons with energy below the <i>cadmium cut-off</i> will be absorbed; those higher than the <i>cut-off will be transmitted</i>. The cadmium cut-off is about 0.5 eV, and neutrons below that level are deemed slow neutrons, distinct from intermediate and fast neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cadmium is created via the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a> in low- to medium-mass stars with masses of 0.6 to 10 <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar masses</a>, over thousands of years. In that process, a <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> atom captures a <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> and then undergoes <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="528" data-file-width="362" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg/170px-Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg/255px-Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg/340px-Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_Strohmeyer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Stromeyer" title="Friedrich Stromeyer">Friedrich Stromeyer</a></div></div></div>
<p>Cadmium (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>cadmia</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>καδμεία</i> meaning "<a href="/wiki/Calamine_(mineral)" title="Calamine (mineral)">calamine</a>", a cadmium-bearing mixture of minerals that was named after the Greek mythological character Κάδμος, <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a>, the founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Thebes_(Boeotia)" title="Ancient Thebes (Boeotia)">Thebes</a>) was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> simultaneously in 1817 by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Stromeyer" title="Friedrich Stromeyer">Friedrich Stromeyer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Samuel_Leberecht_Hermann" title="Karl Samuel Leberecht Hermann">Karl Samuel Leberecht Hermann</a>, both in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, as an impurity in zinc carbonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> Stromeyer found the new element as an impurity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_carbonate" title="Zinc carbonate">zinc carbonate</a> (calamine), and, for 100 years, Germany remained the only important producer of the metal. The metal was named after the Latin word for calamine, because it was found in this zinc ore. Stromeyer noted that some impure samples of calamine changed color when heated but pure calamine did not. He was persistent in studying these results and eventually isolated cadmium metal by <a href="/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy)" title="Roasting (metallurgy)">roasting</a> and reducing the <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_sulfide" title="Cadmium sulfide">sulfide</a>. The potential for cadmium yellow as pigment was recognized in the 1840s, but the lack of cadmium limited this application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cadold_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cadold-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Even though cadmium and its compounds are toxic in certain forms and concentrations, the <a href="/wiki/British_Pharmaceutical_Codex" title="British Pharmaceutical Codex">British Pharmaceutical Codex</a> from 1907 states that <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_iodide" title="Cadmium iodide">cadmium iodide</a> was used as a <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">medication</a> to treat "enlarged joints, scrofulous glands, and chilblains".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1907, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> defined the international <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">ångström</a> in terms of a red cadmium spectral line (1 wavelength = 6438.46963 Å).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  This was adopted by the 7th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> in 1927.  In 1960, the definitions of both the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> and ångström were changed to use <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the industrial scale production of cadmium started in the 1930s and 1940s, the major application of cadmium was the coating of iron and steel to prevent corrosion; in 1944, 62% and in 1956, 59% of the cadmium in the United States was used for <a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">plating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB1956_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-YB1956-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1956, 24% of the cadmium in the United States was used for a second application in red, orange and yellow pigments from sulfides and selenides of cadmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB1956_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-YB1956-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stabilizing effect of cadmium chemicals like the carboxylates cadmium laurate and cadmium stearate on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pvc" title="Pvc">PVC</a> led to an increased use of those compounds in the 1970s and 1980s. The demand for cadmium in pigments, coatings, stabilizers, and alloys declined as a result of environmental and health regulations in the 1980s and 1990s; in 2006, only 7% of to total cadmium consumption was used for plating, and only 10% was used for pigments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup>
At the same time, these decreases in consumption were compensated by a growing demand for cadmium for nickel-cadmium batteries, which accounted for 81% of the cadmium consumption in the United States in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="411" data-file-width="397" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg/220px-CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg/330px-CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/90/CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CadmiumMetalUSGOV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cadmium metal</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Cadmium_minerals" title="Category:Cadmium minerals">Category:Cadmium minerals</a></div>
<p>Cadmium makes up about 0.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>. It is much rarer than zinc, which makes up about 65 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> No significant deposits of cadmium-containing ores are known. The only cadmium <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> of importance, <a href="/wiki/Greenockite" title="Greenockite">greenockite</a> (Cd<a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a>), is nearly always associated with <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">sphalerite</a> (ZnS). This association is caused by geochemical similarity between zinc and cadmium, with no geological process likely to separate them. Thus, cadmium is produced mainly as a byproduct of mining, smelting, and refining sulfidic ores of zinc, and, to a lesser degree, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. Small amounts of cadmium, about 10% of consumption, are produced from secondary sources, mainly from dust generated by recycling iron and steel scrap. Production in the United States began in 1907,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cadold_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cadold-15">[15]</a></sup> but wide use began after World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-price_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-price-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifecycle_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-lifecycle-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Metallic cadmium can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Vilyuy_River" title="Vilyuy River">Vilyuy River</a> basin in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rocks mined for phosphate fertilizers contain varying amounts of cadmium, resulting in a cadmium concentration of as much as 300 mg/kg in the fertilizers and a high cadmium content in agricultural soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Coal can contain significant amounts of cadmium, which ends up mostly in flue dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Cadmium in soil can be absorbed by crops such as rice. <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="Ministry of Agriculture of the People's Republic of China">ministry of agriculture</a> measured in 2002 that 28% of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> it sampled had excess lead and 10% had excess cadmium above limits defined by law. Some plants such as <a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">willow trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplars</a> have been found to clean both lead and cadmium from soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Typical background concentrations of cadmium do not exceed 5 ng/m<sup>3</sup> in the atmosphere; 2 mg/kg in soil; 1 μg/L in freshwater and 50 ng/L in seawater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a> reports that in 2001, China was the top producer of cadmium with almost one-sixth of the world's production, closely followed by South Korea and Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cadmium is a common impurity in <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ores, and it is most often isolated during the production of zinc. Some zinc ores concentrates from sulfidic zinc ores contain up to 1.4% of cadmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cd-Trend_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cd-Trend-32">[32]</a></sup> In the 1970s, the output of cadmium was 6.5 pounds per ton of zinc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cd-Trend_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cd-Trend-32">[32]</a></sup> Zinc <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> ores are roasted in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, converting the zinc sulfide to the <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>. Zinc metal is produced either by <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> the oxide with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. Cadmium is isolated from the zinc metal by <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_distillation" title="Vacuum distillation">vacuum distillation</a> if the zinc is smelted, or cadmium sulfate is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitated</a> from the electrolysis solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lifecycle_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-lifecycle-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium is a common component of electric batteries, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_pigments" title="Cadmium pigments">pigments</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colors_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-colors-34">[34]</a></sup> coatings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fff_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-fff-35">[35]</a></sup> and electroplating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batteries">Batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NiCd_various.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="800" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/NiCd_various.jpg/220px-NiCd_various.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/NiCd_various.jpg/330px-NiCd_various.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/NiCd_various.jpg/440px-NiCd_various.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NiCd_various.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ni-Cd batteries</div></div></div>
<p>In 2009, 86% of cadmium was used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>, predominantly in <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">nickel-cadmium batteries</a>. Nickel-cadmium cells have a nominal cell potential of 1.2 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">V</a>. The cell consists of a positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_hydroxide" title="Nickel hydroxide">nickel hydroxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a> and a negative cadmium electrode plate separated by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The European Union put a limit on cadmium in electronics in 2004 of 0.01%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> with some exceptions, and reduced the limit on cadmium content to 0.002%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Another type of battery based on cadmium is the <a href="/wiki/Silver-cadmium_battery" title="Silver-cadmium battery">silver-cadmium battery</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroplating">Electroplating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electroplating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CdSeqdots.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1636" data-file-width="3000" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CdSeqdots.jpg/220px-CdSeqdots.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CdSeqdots.jpg/330px-CdSeqdots.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CdSeqdots.jpg/440px-CdSeqdots.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CdSeqdots.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A photograph and representative spectrum of <a href="/wiki/Photoluminescence" title="Photoluminescence">photoluminescence</a> from colloidal CdSe <a href="/wiki/Quantum_dot" title="Quantum dot">quantum dots</a></div></div></div>
<p>Cadmium <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a>, consuming 6% of the global production, is used in the aircraft industry to reduce <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> of steel components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup> This coating is passivated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromate" title="Chromate">chromate</a> salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fff_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-fff-35">[35]</a></sup> A limitation of cadmium plating is <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">hydrogen embrittlement</a> of high-strength steels from the electroplating process. Therefore, steel parts heat-treated to tensile strength above 1300 MPa (200 ksi) should be coated by an alternative method (such as special low-embrittlement cadmium electroplating processes or physical vapor deposition).
</p><p>Titanium embrittlement from cadmium-plated tool residues resulted in banishment of those tools (and the implementation of routine tool testing to detect cadmium contamination) in the A-12/SR-71, U-2, and subsequent aircraft programs that use titanium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fission">Nuclear fission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nuclear fission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cadmium is used in the control rods of nuclear reactors, acting as a very effective "neutron poison" to control <a href="/wiki/Neutron_flux" title="Neutron flux">neutron flux</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup> When cadmium rods are inserted in the core of a nuclear reactor, cadmium absorbs neutrons, preventing them from creating additional fission events, thus controlling the amount of reactivity. The <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactor</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse Electric Company</a> uses an alloy consisting of 80% silver, 15% indium, and 5% cadmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Televisions">Televisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Televisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quantum_dot_display" title="Quantum dot display">QLED TVs</a> have been starting to include cadmium in construction. Some companies have been looking to reduce the environmental impact of human exposure and pollution of the material in televisions during production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg/220px-Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg/330px-Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg/440px-Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tyne_and_Wear_Metro_train_4001_at_Pelaw_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Train painted with <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_pigments" title="Cadmium pigments">cadmium orange</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cadmium_oxide" title="Cadmium oxide">Cadmium oxide</a> was used in black and white television phosphors and in the blue and green phosphors of color television cathode ray tubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_sulfide" title="Cadmium sulfide">Cadmium sulfide</a> (CdS) is used as a photoconductive surface coating for photocopier drums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cadmium_sulfide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="500" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cadmium_sulfide.jpg/110px-Cadmium_sulfide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cadmium_sulfide.jpg/165px-Cadmium_sulfide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cadmium_sulfide.jpg/220px-Cadmium_sulfide.jpg 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cadmium_sulfide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cadmium sulfide</div></div></div>
<p>Various cadmium salts are used in paint pigments, with CdS as a <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_pigments" title="Cadmium pigments">yellow pigment</a> being the most common. <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_selenide" title="Cadmium selenide">Cadmium selenide</a> is a red pigment, commonly called <i>cadmium red</i>. To painters who work with the pigment, cadmium provides the most brilliant and durable yellows, oranges, and reds — so much so that during production, these colors are significantly toned down before they are ground with oils and binders or blended into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor">watercolors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gouache" title="Gouache">gouaches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_paint" title="Acrylic paint">acrylics</a>, and other paint and pigment formulations. Because these pigments are potentially toxic, users should use a <a href="/wiki/Barrier_cream" title="Barrier cream">barrier cream</a> on the hands to prevent absorption through the skin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colors_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-colors-34">[34]</a></sup> even though the amount of cadmium absorbed into the body through the skin is reported to be less than 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATSDR_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATSDR-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a>, cadmium was used as heat, light, and weathering stabilizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Currently, cadmium stabilizers have been completely replaced with barium-zinc, calcium-zinc and organo-tin stabilizers. Cadmium is used in many kinds of <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">solder</a> and bearing alloys, because it has a low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_friction" title="Coefficient of friction">coefficient of friction</a> and fatigue resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup> It is also found in some of the lowest-melting <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Wood%27s_metal" title="Wood's metal">Wood's metal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_uses">Laboratory uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Laboratory uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HeCd_laser.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1166" data-file-width="1452" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/HeCd_laser.jpg/220px-HeCd_laser.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/HeCd_laser.jpg/330px-HeCd_laser.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/HeCd_laser.jpg/440px-HeCd_laser.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HeCd_laser.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Violet light from a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> cadmium metal vapor <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>. The highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> color arises from the 441.563 nm transition <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">line</a> of cadmium.</div></div></div>
<p>Helium–cadmium lasers are a common source of blue-ultraviolet laser light. They operate at either 325 or 422 nm in <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_microscope" title="Fluorescence microscope">fluorescence microscopes</a> and various laboratory experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Cadmium selenide <a href="/wiki/Quantum_dot" title="Quantum dot">quantum dots</a> emit bright <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">luminescence</a> under UV excitation (He-Cd laser, for example). The color of this luminescence can be green, yellow or red depending on the particle size. Colloidal solutions of those particles are used for imaging of biological tissues and solutions with a <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_microscope" title="Fluorescence microscope">fluorescence microscope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cadmium is a component of some compound <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a>, such as cadmium sulfide, cadmium selenide, and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a>, used for light detection and <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HgCdTe" title="HgCdTe">HgCdTe</a> is sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HgCdPb_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-HgCdPb-36">[36]</a></sup> light and can be used as an infrared detector, motion detector, or switch in remote control devices.
</p><p>In molecular biology, cadmium is used to block voltage-dependent calcium channels from fluxing calcium ions, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a> research to stimulate <a href="/wiki/Proteasome" title="Proteasome">proteasome</a>-dependent degradation of <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia-inducible_factors" title="Hypoxia-inducible factors">Hif-1α</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadmium-selective_sensors">Cadmium-selective sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cadmium-selective sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cadmium-selective sensors based on the <a href="/wiki/Fluorophore" title="Fluorophore">fluorophore</a> <a href="/wiki/BODIPY" title="BODIPY">BODIPY</a> have been developed for imaging and sensing of cadmium in cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> One of the most popular way to monitor cadmium in aqueous environments is the use of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, one example is by attaching a <a href="/wiki/Self-assembled_monolayer" title="Self-assembled monolayer">self-assembled monolayer</a> that can help obtain a cadmium selective electrode with a <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">ppt</a>-level sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role_and_research">Biological role and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biological role and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cadmium has no known function in higher organisms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> but a cadmium-dependent <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> has been found in some marine <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diatom_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diatom-53">[53]</a></sup>  The diatoms live in environments with very low zinc concentrations and cadmium performs the function normally carried out by zinc in other anhydrases. This was discovered with X-ray absorption fluorescence spectroscopy (XAFS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diatom_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Diatom-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The highest concentration of cadmium is absorbed in the kidneys of humans, and up to about 30 mg of cadmium is commonly inhaled throughout human childhood and adolescence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Cadmium is under preliminary research for its <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> in humans, potentially affecting mechanisms and risks of <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">osteoporosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The biogeochemistry of cadmium and its release to the environment has been the subject of review, as has the speciation of cadmium in the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Individuals and organizations have been reviewing cadmium's bioinorganic aspects for its toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The most dangerous form of occupational exposure to cadmium is inhalation of fine dust and fumes, or ingestion of highly soluble cadmium compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> Inhalation of cadmium fumes can result initially in <a href="/wiki/Metal_fume_fever" title="Metal fume fever">metal fume fever</a> but may progress to chemical <a href="/wiki/Pneumonitis" title="Pneumonitis">pneumonitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a>, and death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cadmium is also an environmental hazard. Human exposure is primarily from fossil fuel combustion, phosphate fertilizers, natural sources, iron and steel production, cement production and related activities, nonferrous metals production, and municipal solid waste incineration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> Bread, root crops, and vegetables also contribute to the cadmium in modern populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.yahoo.com_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.yahoo.com-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been a few instances of general population poisoning as the result of long-term exposure to cadmium in contaminated food and water, and research into an estrogen mimicry that may induce breast cancer is ongoing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.yahoo.com_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.yahoo.com-65">[65]</a></sup> In the decades leading up to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, mining operations contaminated the <a href="/wiki/Jinz%C5%AB_River" title="Jinzū River">Jinzū River</a> in Japan with cadmium and traces of other toxic metals. As a consequence, cadmium accumulated in the rice crops along the riverbanks downstream of the mines. Some members of the local agricultural communities consumed the contaminated rice and developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itai-itai" title="Itai-itai">itai-itai</a> disease and renal abnormalities, including <a href="/wiki/Proteinuria" title="Proteinuria">proteinuria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucosuria" title="Glucosuria">glucosuria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jinzu_River.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Jinzu_River.jpg/220px-Jinzu_River.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Jinzu_River.jpg/330px-Jinzu_River.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Jinzu_River.jpg/440px-Jinzu_River.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jinzu_River.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jinz%C5%AB_River" title="Jinzū River">Jinzū River</a> area, which was contaminated with cadmium</div></div></div>
<p>The victims of this poisoning were almost exclusively post-menopausal women with low iron and other mineral body stores. Similar general population cadmium exposures in other parts of the world have not resulted in the same health problems because the populations maintained sufficient iron and other mineral levels. Thus, although cadmium is a major factor in the itai-itai disease in Japan, most researchers have concluded that it was one of several factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-4">[4]</a></sup> Cadmium is one of six substances banned by the European Union's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROHS" title="ROHS">Restriction on Hazardous Substances</a> (RoHS) directive, which regulates hazardous substances in electrical and electronic equipment but allows for certain exemptions and exclusions from the scope of the law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
The International Agency for Research on Cancer has classified cadmium and cadmium compounds as carcinogenic to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Although occupational exposure to cadmium is linked to lung and prostate cancer, there is still a substantial controversy about the carcinogenicity of cadmium in low environmental exposure. Recent data from epidemiological studies suggest that intake of cadmium through diet associates to higher risk of endometrial, breast and prostate cancer as well as to osteoporosis in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> A recent study has demonstrated that endometrial tissue is characterized by higher levels of cadmium in current and former smoking females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cadmium exposure is a risk factor associated with a large number of illnesses including kidney disease,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zn-cd.diseases_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-zn-cd.diseases-74">[74]</a></sup> early atherosclerosis, hypertension, and cardiovascular diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Although studies show a significant correlation between cadmium exposure and occurrence of disease in human populations, a necessary molecular mechanism has not been identified. One hypothesis holds that cadmium is an <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptor</a> and some experimental studies have shown that it can interact with different <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormonal</a> signaling pathways. For example, cadmium can bind to the <a href="/wiki/Estrogen_receptor" title="Estrogen receptor">estrogen receptor</a> alpha,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and affect signal transduction along the <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MAPK" title="MAPK">MAPK</a> signaling pathways at low doses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_plant" title="Tobacco plant">tobacco plant</a> readily absorbs and accumulates <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, such as cadmium from the surrounding soil into its leaves. These are readily absorbed into the user's body following smoke inhalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Tobacco smoking is the most important single source of cadmium exposure in the general population. An estimated 10% of the cadmium content of a cigarette is inhaled through smoking. Absorption of cadmium through the lungs is more effective than through the gut, and as much as 50% of the cadmium inhaled in cigarette smoke may be absorbed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
On average, cadmium concentrations in the blood of smokers is 4 times 5 times greater and in the kidney, 2–3 times greater than non-smokers. Despite the high cadmium content in cigarette smoke, there seems to be little exposure to cadmium from <a href="/wiki/Passive_smoking" title="Passive smoking">passive smoking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a non-smoking population, food is the greatest source of exposure. High quantities of cadmium can be found in <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">mollusks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> products. However, grains, vegetables, and starchy roots and tubers are consumed in much greater quantity in the US, and are the source of the greatest dietary exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efsa.europa.eu_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-efsa.europa.eu-84">[84]</a></sup> Most plants bio-accumulate metal toxins like Cd, and when composted to form organic fertilizers yield a product which can often contain high amounts (e.g., over 0.5 mg) of metal toxins for every kilo of fertilizer. Fertilizers made from animal dung (e.g., cow dung) or urban waste can contain similar amounts of Cd. The Cd added to the soil from fertilizers (rock phosphates or organic fertilizers) become bio-available and toxic only if the soil pH is low (i.e., acidic soils).  Zinc is chemically similar to cadmium and some evidence indicates the presence of Zn ions reduces cadmium toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc, Cu, Ca, and Fe ions, and selenium with vitamin C are used to treat Cd intoxication, though it is not easily reversed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zn-cd.diseases_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-zn-cd.diseases-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of the adverse effects of cadmium on the environment and human health, the supply and use of cadmium is restricted in Europe under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/REACH_Regulation" title="REACH Regulation">REACH Regulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>The EFSA Panel on Contaminants in the Food Chain specifies that 2.5 μg/kg body weight is a tolerable weekly intake for humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-efsa.europa.eu_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-efsa.europa.eu-84">[84]</a></sup> The Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives has declared 7 μg/kg bw to be the provisional tolerable weekly intake level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>The US <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> (PEL) for cadmium at a time-weighted average (TWA) of 0.005 ppm. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has not set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) and has designated cadmium as a known human carcinogen. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">IDLH</a> (immediately dangerous to life and health) level for cadmium is 9 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_recalls">Product recalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Product recalls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2006, a sale of the seats from <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal F.C.</a>'s old stadium, <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_Stadium" title="Arsenal Stadium">Highbury</a> in London, England was cancelled when the seats were discovered to contain trace amounts of cadmium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Reports of high levels of cadmium use in children's jewelry in 2010 led to a US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_Product_Safety_Commission" title="Consumer Product Safety Commission">Consumer Product Safety Commission</a> investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The U.S. CPSC issued specific recall notices for cadmium content in jewelry sold by <a href="/wiki/Claire%27s" title="Claire's">Claire's</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> stores.
</p><p>In June 2010, <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> voluntarily recalled more than 12 million promotional "Shrek Forever After 3D" Collectable Drinking Glasses because of the cadmium levels in paint pigments on the glassware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> The glasses were manufactured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arc_International_(tableware)" title="Arc International (tableware)">Arc International</a>, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millville,_NJ" title="Millville, NJ">Millville, NJ</a>, USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
