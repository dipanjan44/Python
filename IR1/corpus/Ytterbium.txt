<p><b>Ytterbium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Yb</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 70. It is the fourteenth and penultimate element in the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, which is the basis of the relative stability of its +2 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>. However, like the other lanthanides, its most common oxidation state is +3, as in its <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a>, and other compounds. In <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>, like compounds of other late lanthanides, soluble ytterbium compounds form complexes with nine water molecules. Because of its closed-shell electron configuration, its density and melting and boiling points differ significantly from those of most other lanthanides.
</p><p>In 1878, the Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a> separated from the rare earth "erbia" another independent component, which he called "ytterbia", for <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a>, the village in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> near where he found the new component of <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>. He suspected that ytterbia was a compound of a new element that he called "ytterbium" (in total, four elements were named after the village, the others being <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>). In 1907, the new earth "lutecia" was separated from ytterbia, from which the element "lutecium" (now <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>) was extracted by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a>. After some discussion, Marignac's name "ytterbium" was retained. A relatively pure sample of the metal was not obtained until 1953. At present, ytterbium is mainly used as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> of stainless steel or <a href="/wiki/Active_laser_medium" title="Active laser medium">active laser media</a>, and less often as a <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> source.
</p><p>Natural ytterbium is a mixture of seven stable isotopes, which altogether are present at concentrations of 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a>. This element is mined in China, the United States, Brazil, and India in form of the minerals <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>. The ytterbium concentration is low because it is found only among many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_elements" title="Rare earth elements">rare earth elements</a>; moreover, it is among the least abundant. Once extracted and prepared, ytterbium is somewhat hazardous as an eye and skin irritant. The metal is a fire and explosion hazard.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium is a soft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleability" title="Malleability">malleable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> that displays a bright silvery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luster_(mineralogy)" title="Luster (mineralogy)">luster</a> when pure. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a>, and it is readily dissolved by the strong <a href="/wiki/Mineral_acid" title="Mineral acid">mineral acids</a>. It <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">reacts</a> slowly with cold <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidizes</a> slowly in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ytterbium has three <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropes</a> labeled by the Greek letters alpha, beta and gamma; their transformation temperatures are −13 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> and 795 °C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup> although the exact transformation temperature depends on the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpha-Yb_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-alpha-Yb-7">[7]</a></sup> The beta allotrope (6.966 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) exists at room temperature, and it has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centered_cubic" title="Face-centered cubic">face-centered cubic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a>. The high-temperature gamma allotrope (6.57 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> crystalline structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup> The alpha allotrope (6.903 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal</a> crystalline structure and is stable at low temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup> The beta allotrope has a metallic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> at normal atmospheric pressure, but it becomes a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> when exposed to a pressure of about 16,000 <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheres</a> (1.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigapascal" title="Gigapascal">GPa</a>). Its electrical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> increases ten times upon compression to 39,000 atmospheres (3.9 GPa), but then drops to about 10% of its room-temperature resistivity at about 40,000 atm (4.0 GPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast with the other rare-earth metals, which usually have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> properties at low <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a>, ytterbium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> at temperatures above 1.0 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the alpha allotrope is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alpha-Yb_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-alpha-Yb-7">[7]</a></sup> With a <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of 824 °C and a <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> of 1196 °C, ytterbium has the smallest liquid range of all the metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Contrary to most other lanthanides, which have a close-packed hexagonal lattice, ytterbium crystallizes in the face-centered cubic system. Ytterbium has a density of 6.973 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, which is significantly lower than those of the neighboring lanthanides, <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a> (9.32 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> (9.841 g/cm<sup>3</sup>). Its melting and boiling points are also significantly lower than those of thulium and lutetium. This is due to the closed-shell electron configuration of ytterbium ([Xe] 4f<sup>14</sup> 6s<sup>2</sup>), which causes only the two 6s electrons to be available for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">metallic bonding</a> (in contrast to the other lanthanides where three electrons are available) and increases ytterbium's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_radius" title="Metallic radius">metallic radius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium metal tarnishes slowly in air. Finely dispersed ytterbium readily oxidizes in air and under oxygen. Mixtures of powdered ytterbium with <a href="/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene" title="Polytetrafluoroethylene">polytetrafluoroethylene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hexachloroethane" title="Hexachloroethane">hexachloroethane</a> burn with a luminous emerald-green flame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Ytterbium reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to form various <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydrides</a>. Ytterbium dissolves slowly in water, but quickly in acids, liberating hydrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ytterbium is quite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a>, and it reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form ytterbium(III) hydroxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Yb (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Yb(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Ytterbium reacts with all the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Yb (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 YbF<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Yb (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 YbCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Yb (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 YbBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Yb (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 YbI<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd></dl>
<p>The ytterbium(III) ion absorbs light in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_infrared" title="Near infrared">near infrared</a> range of wavelengths, but not in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>, so <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ytterbia" title="Ytterbia">ytterbia</a>, Yb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, is white in color and the salts of ytterbium are also colorless. Ytterbium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions that contain the colorless Yb(III) ions, which exist as nonahydrate complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webelements_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-webelements-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Yb (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) + 18 <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> (l) → 2 [Yb(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<h3><span id="Yb.28II.29_vs._Yb.28III.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Yb(II)_vs._Yb(III)">Yb(II) vs. Yb(III)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yb(II) vs. Yb(III)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although usually trivalent, ytterbium readily forms divalent compounds. This behavior is unusual for <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, which almost exclusively form compounds with an oxidation state of +3. The +2 state has a valence <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configuration</a> of 4<i>f</i><sup>14</sup> because the fully filled <i>f</i>-shell gives more stability. The yellow-green ytterbium(II) ion is a very strong <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a> and decomposes water, releasing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas, and thus only the colorless ytterbium(III) ion occurs in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>. <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">Samarium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a> also behave this way in the +2 state, but <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>(II) is stable in aqueous solution. Ytterbium metal behaves similarly to europium metal and the alkaline earth metals, dissolving in ammonia to form blue <a href="/wiki/Electride" title="Electride">electride</a> salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_ytterbium" title="Isotopes of ytterbium">Isotopes of ytterbium</a></div>
<p>Natural ytterbium is composed of seven stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: <sup>168</sup>Yb, <sup>170</sup>Yb, <sup>171</sup>Yb, <sup>172</sup>Yb, <sup>173</sup>Yb, <sup>174</sup>Yb, and <sup>176</sup>Yb, with <sup>174</sup>Yb being the most common, at 31.8% of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">natural abundance</a>). 27 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been observed, with the most stable ones being <sup>169</sup>Yb with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 32.0 days, <sup>175</sup>Yb with a half-life of 4.18 days, and <sup>166</sup>Yb with a half-life of 56.7 hours. All of its remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives that are less than two hours and most of these have half-lives are less than 20 minutes. Ytterbium also has 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, with the most stable being <sup>169m</sup>Yb (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> 46 seconds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucleonica_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nucleonica-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IS_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-IS-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The isotopes of ytterbium range in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> from 147.9674 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass unit</a> (u) for <sup>148</sup>Yb to 180.9562 u for <sup>181</sup>Yb. The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> of ytterbium isotopes lighter than the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>174</sup>Yb, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, and the primary decay mode for those heavier than <sup>174</sup>Yb is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> of ytterbium isotopes lighter than <sup>174</sup>Yb are <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a> isotopes, and the primary decay products of ytterbium isotopes with heavier than <sup>174</sup>Yb are <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a> isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nucleonica_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nucleonica-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IS_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-IS-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Euxenite_-_Vegusdal,_Norvegia_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg/220px-Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg/330px-Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg/440px-Euxenite_-_Vegusdal%2C_Norvegia_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Euxenite_-_Vegusdal,_Norvegia_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">Euxenite</a></div></div></div>
<p>Ytterbium is found with other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a> in several rare <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. It is most often recovered commercially from <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> sand (0.03% ytterbium). The element is also found in <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>. The main mining areas are <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Reserves of ytterbium are estimated as one million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. Ytterbium is normally difficult to separate from other rare earths, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvent_extraction" title="Solvent extraction">solvent extraction</a> techniques developed in the mid- to late 20th century have simplified separation. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">Compounds</a> of ytterbium are rare and have not yet been well characterized. The abundance of ytterbium in the Earth's crust is about 3 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As an even-numbered lanthanide, in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oddo-Harkins_rule" title="Oddo-Harkins rule">Oddo-Harkins rule</a>, ytterbium is significantly more abundant than its immediate neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>, which occur in the same concentrate at levels of about 0.5% each. The world production of ytterbium is only about 50 tonnes per year, reflecting that it has few commercial applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup> Microscopic traces of ytterbium are used as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YAG#Yb:YAG" title="YAG">Yb:YAG laser</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state laser</a> in which ytterbium is the element that undergoes <a href="/wiki/Stimulated_emission" title="Stimulated emission">stimulated emission</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ytterbium is often the most common substitute in <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> minerals. In very few known cases/occurrences ytterbium prevails over yttrium, as, e.g., in <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>-(Yb). A report of native ytterbium from the Moon's regolith is known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is relatively difficult to separate ytterbium from other lanthanides due to its similar properties. As a result, the process is somewhat long. First, minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> are dissolved into various acids, such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. Ytterbium can then be separated from other lanthanides by <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>, as can other lanthanides. The solution is then applied to a <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a>, which different lanthanides bind in different matters. This is then dissolved using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complexing_agent" title="Complexing agent">complexing agents</a>, and due to the different types of bonding exhibited by the different lanthanides, it is possible to isolate the compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ytterbium is separated from other rare earths either by <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> or by reduction with sodium amalgam. In the latter method, a buffered acidic solution of trivalent rare earths is treated with molten sodium-mercury alloy, which reduces and dissolves Yb<sup>3+</sup>. The alloy is treated with hydrochloric acid. The metal is extracted from the solution as oxalate and converted to oxide by heating. The oxide is reduced to metal by heating with <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> in high vacuum. The metal is purified by sublimation and collected over a condensed plate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ytterbium(III)_oxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="408" data-file-width="414" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Ytterbium%28III%29_oxide.jpg/220px-Ytterbium%28III%29_oxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Ytterbium%28III%29_oxide.jpg/330px-Ytterbium%28III%29_oxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Ytterbium%28III%29_oxide.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ytterbium(III)_oxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_oxide" title="Ytterbium(III) oxide">Ytterbium(III) oxide</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Ytterbium_compounds" title="Category:Ytterbium compounds">Category:Ytterbium compounds</a></div>
<p>The chemical behavior of ytterbium is similar to that of the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>. Most ytterbium compounds are found in the +3 oxidation state, and its salts in this oxidation state are nearly colorless. Like <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a>, the trihalides of ytterbium can be reduced to the dihalides by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> dust, or by the addition of metallic ytterbium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup> The +2 oxidation state occurs only in solid compounds and reacts in some ways similarly to the <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a> compounds; for example, ytterbium(II) oxide (YbO) shows the same structure as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (CaO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="963" data-file-width="963" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png/220px-Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png/330px-Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png/440px-Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kristallstruktur_Lanthanoid-C-Typ.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_oxide" title="Ytterbium(III) oxide">ytterbium(III) oxide</a></div></div></div>
<p>Ytterbium forms both dihalides and trihalides with the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. The dihalides are susceptible to oxidation to the trihalides at room temperature and disproportionate to the trihalides and metallic ytterbium at high temperature:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3 YbX<sub>2</sub> → 2 YbX<sub>3</sub> + Yb (X = <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I</a>)</dd></dl>
<p>Some ytterbium halides are used as <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_chloride" title="Ytterbium(III) chloride">ytterbium(III) chloride</a> (YbCl<sub>3</sub>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> and can be used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Aldol_reaction" title="Aldol reaction">Aldol</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Diels%E2%80%93Alder_reaction" title="Diels–Alder reaction">Diels–Alder reactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium(II)_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ytterbium(II) iodide (page does not exist)">Ytterbium(II) iodide</a> (YbI<sub>2</sub>) may be used, like <a href="/wiki/Samarium(II)_iodide" title="Samarium(II) iodide">samarium(II) iodide</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coupling_reactions" title="Coupling reactions">coupling reactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_fluoride" title="Ytterbium(III) fluoride">Ytterbium(III) fluoride</a> (YbF<sub>3</sub>) is used as an inert and non-toxic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tooth_filling" title="Tooth filling">tooth filling</a> as it continuously releases <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> ions, which are good for dental health, and is also a good <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_contrast_agent" title="X-ray contrast agent">X-ray contrast agent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enghag_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enghag-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides">Oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium reacts with oxygen to form <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_oxide" title="Ytterbium(III) oxide">ytterbium(III) oxide</a> (Yb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>), which crystallizes in the "rare-earth C-type sesquioxide" structure which is related to the <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> structure with one quarter of the anions removed, leading to ytterbium atoms in two different six coordinate (non-octahedral) environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Ytterbium(III) oxide can be reduced to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ytterbium(II) oxide (page does not exist)">ytterbium(II) oxide</a> (YbO) with elemental ytterbium, which crystallizes in the same structure as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="349" height="334" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg/220px-Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg/330px-Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galissard_de_Marignac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a></div></div></div>
<p>Ytterbium was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> by the Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a> in the year 1878. While examining samples of <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>, Marignac found a new component in the earth then known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a>, and he named it ytterbia, for <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> village near where he found the new component of erbium. Marignac suspected that ytterbia was a compound of a new element that he called "ytterbium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enghag_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Enghag-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1907, the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a> separated Marignac's ytterbia into two components: neoytterbia and lutecia. Neoytterbia later became known as the element ytterbium, and lutecia became known as the element <a href="/wiki/Lutetium" title="Lutetium">lutetium</a>. The Austrian chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a> independently isolated these elements from ytterbia at about the same time, but he called them aldebaranium and cassiopeium;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup> the American chemist <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a> also independently isolated these elements at about the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Urbain and Welsbach accused each other of publishing results based on the other party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1st_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-1st-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fra_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fra-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deu_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deu-28">[28]</a></sup> The Commission on Atomic Mass, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Wigglesworth_Clarke" title="Frank Wigglesworth Clarke">Frank Wigglesworth Clarke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a>, and Georges Urbain, which was then responsible for the attribution of new element names, settled the dispute in 1909 by granting priority to Urbain and adopting his names as official ones, based on the fact that the separation of lutetium from Marignac's ytterbium was first described by Urbain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1st_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-1st-26">[26]</a></sup> After Urbain's names were recognized, neoytterbium was reverted to ytterbium.
</p><p>The chemical and physical properties of ytterbium could not be determined with any precision until 1953, when the first nearly pure ytterbium metal was produced by using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup> The price of ytterbium was relatively stable between 1953 and 1998 at about US $1,000/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_of_gamma_rays">Source of gamma rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Source of gamma rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <sup>169</sup>Yb <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> (with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 32 days), which is created along with the short-lived <sup>175</sup>Yb isotope (half-life 4.2 days) by <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">neutron activation</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Irradiation" title="Irradiation">irradiation</a> of ytterbium in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>, has been used as a <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> source in portable <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> machines. Like X-rays, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a> emitted by the source pass through soft tissues of the body, but are blocked by bones and other dense materials. Thus, small <sup>169</sup>Yb samples (which emit gamma rays) act like tiny X-ray machines useful for <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a> of small objects. Experiments show that radiographs taken with a <sup>169</sup>Yb source are roughly equivalent to those taken with X-rays having energies between 250 and 350 keV. <sup>169</sup>Yb is also used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-stability_atomic_clocks">High-stability atomic clocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: High-stability atomic clocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium clocks hold the record for stability with ticks stable to within less than two parts in 1 quintillion (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6982200000000000000♠</span>2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−18</sup></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The clocks developed at the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)</a> rely on about 10,000 rare-earth atoms cooled to 10 microkelvin (10 millionths of a degree above absolute zero) and trapped in an optical lattice—a series of pancake-shaped wells made of laser light. Another laser that "ticks" 518 trillion times per second provokes a transition between two energy levels in the atoms. The large number of atoms is key to the clocks' high stability.
</p><p>Visible light waves oscillate faster than microwaves, and therefore optical clocks can be more precise than <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_clocks" title="Atomic clocks">atomic clocks</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Physikalisch-Technische_Bundesanstalt" title="Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt">Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt</a> is working on several such optical clocks. The model with one single ytterbium ion caught in an ion trap is highly accurate. The optical clock based on it is exact to 17 digits after the decimal point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
A pair of experimental atomic clocks based on ytterbium atoms at the National Institute of Standards and Technology has set a record for stability. NIST physicists reported in the August 22, 2013 issue of Science Express that the ytterbium clocks' ticks are stable to within less than two parts in 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quintillion" title="Quintillion">quintillion</a> (1 followed by 18 zeros), roughly 10 times better than the previous best published results for other atomic clocks. The clocks would be accurate within a second for a period comparable to the age of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doping_of_stainless_steel">Doping of stainless steel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Doping of stainless steel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium can also be used as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> to help improve the grain refinement, strength, and other mechanical properties of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. Some ytterbium <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> have rarely been used in <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-history-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ytterbium_as_dopant_of_active_media">Ytterbium as dopant of active media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ytterbium as dopant of active media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ytterbium +3 <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductors)" title="Doping (semiconductors)">doping material</a> in <a href="/wiki/Active_laser_medium" title="Active laser medium">active laser media</a>, specifically in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_laser" title="Solid state laser">solid state lasers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_clad_fiber" title="Double clad fiber">double clad fiber</a> lasers. Ytterbium lasers are highly efficient, have long lifetimes and can generate short pulses; ytterbium can also easily be incorporated into the material used to make the laser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Ytterbium lasers commonly radiate in the 1.06–1.12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a> band being optically pumped at wavelength 900 nm–1 µm, dependently on the host and application. The small <a href="/wiki/Quantum_defect" title="Quantum defect">quantum defect</a> makes ytterbium a prospective dopant for efficient lasers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_scaling" title="Power scaling">power scaling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The kinetic of excitations in ytterbium-doped materials is simple and can be described within the concept of <a href="/wiki/McCumber_relation" title="McCumber relation">effective cross-sections</a>; for most ytterbium-doped laser materials (as for many other optically pumped gain media), the <a href="/wiki/McCumber_relation" title="McCumber relation">McCumber relation</a> holds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kouz05_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-kouz05-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mc-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-B-38">[38]</a></sup> although the application to the ytterbium-doped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_materials" title="Composite materials">composite materials</a> was under discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCumberA_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCumberA-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCumberB_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCumberB-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Usually, low concentrations of ytterbium are used. At high concentrations, the ytterbium-doped materials show <a href="/wiki/Photodarkening" title="Photodarkening">photodarkening</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photodarkening_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-photodarkening-41">[41]</a></sup>
(glass fibers) or even a switch to broadband emission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avalanche_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-avalanche-42">[42]</a></sup> (crystals and ceramics) instead of efficient laser action. This effect may be related with not only overheating, but also with conditions of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Charge_compensation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Charge compensation (page does not exist)">charge compensation</a> at high concentrations of ytterbium ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Much progress has been made in the power scaling lasers and amplifiers produced with ytterbium (Yb) doped optical fibers. Power levels have increased from the 1 kW regimes due to the advancements in components as well as the Yb-doped fibers. Fabrication of Low NA, Large Mode Area fibers enable achievement of near perfect beam qualities (M2&lt;1.1) at power levels of 1.5 kW to greater than 2 kW at ~1064 nm in a broadband configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Ytterbium-doped LMA fibers also have the advantages of a larger mode field diameter, which negates the impacts of nonlinear effects such as stimulated <a href="/wiki/Brillouin_scattering" title="Brillouin scattering">Brillouin scattering</a> and stimulated <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering" title="Raman scattering">Raman scattering</a>, which limit the achievement of higher power levels, and provide a distinct advantage over single mode ytterbium-doped fibers.
</p><p>In order to achieve even higher power levels in ytterbium-based fiber systems. all factors of the fiber must be considered. These can be achieved only via optimization of all the ytterbium fiber parameters, ranging from the core background losses to the geometrical properties, in order to reduce the splice losses within the cavity. Power scaling also requires optimization of matching passive fibers within the optical cavity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The optimization of the ytterbium-doped glass itself through host glass modification of various dopants also plays a large part in reducing the background loss of the glass, improvements in slope efficiency of the fiber, and improved photodarkening performance, all of which contribute to increased power levels in 1 µm systems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ytterbium metal increases its electrical resistivity when subjected to high stresses. This property is used in stress gauges to monitor ground deformations from earthquakes and explosions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-appl-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, ytterbium is being investigated as a possible replacement for <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> in high density pyrotechnic payloads for kinematic <a href="/wiki/Flare_(countermeasure)" title="Flare (countermeasure)">infrared decoy flares</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium(III)_oxide" title="Ytterbium(III) oxide">ytterbium(III) oxide</a> has a significantly higher <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> in the infrared range than <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, a higher radiant intensity is obtained with ytterbium-based payloads in comparison to those commonly based on <a href="/wiki/Magnesium/Teflon/Viton" title="Magnesium/Teflon/Viton">magnesium/Teflon/Viton</a> (MTV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ytterbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although ytterbium is fairly stable chemically, it is stored in airtight containers and in an inert atmosphere such as a nitrogen-filled dry box to protect it from air and moisture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> All compounds of ytterbium are treated as highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a>, although studies appear to indicate that the danger is minimal. However, ytterbium compounds cause irritation to human skin and eyes, and some might be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teratogenic" title="Teratogenic">teratogenic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Metallic ytterbium dust can spontaneously combust,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and the resulting fumes are hazardous. Ytterbium fires cannot be extinguished using water, and only dry chemical class D <a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher" title="Fire extinguisher">fire extinguishers</a> can extinguish the fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
