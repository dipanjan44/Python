<p><b>Thallium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Tl</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 81. It is a gray <a href="/wiki/Post-transition_metal" title="Post-transition metal">post-transition metal</a> that is not found free in nature. When isolated, thallium resembles <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, but discolors when exposed to air. Chemists <a href="/wiki/William_Crookes" title="William Crookes">William Crookes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude-Auguste_Lamy" title="Claude-Auguste Lamy">Claude-Auguste Lamy</a> discovered thallium independently in 1861, in residues of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> production. Both used the newly developed method of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flame_spectroscopy" title="Flame spectroscopy">flame spectroscopy</a>, in which thallium produces a notable green spectral line. Thallium, from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">θαλλός</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">thallós</i>, meaning "a green shoot or twig", was named by Crookes. It was isolated by both Lamy and Crookes in 1862; Lamy by electrolysis, and Crookes by precipitation and melting of the resultant powder. Crookes exhibited it as a powder precipitated by zinc at the International exhibition, which opened on 1 May that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thallium tends to oxidize to the +3 and +1 oxidation states as ionic salts. The +3 state resembles that of the other elements in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron_Group" title="Boron Group">group 13</a> (<a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a>). However, the +1 state, which is far more prominent in thallium than the elements above it, recalls the chemistry of <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a>, and thallium(I) ions are found geologically mostly in potassium-based ores, and (when ingested) are handled in many ways like potassium ions (K<sup>+</sup>) by ion pumps in living cells.
</p><p>Commercially, thallium is produced not from potassium ores, but as a byproduct from refining of heavy-metal sulfide ores. Approximately 60–70% of thallium production is used in the <a href="/wiki/Electronics_industry" title="Electronics industry">electronics industry</a>, and the remainder is used in the <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical industry</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass manufacturing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2001_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sl2001-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Infrared_detector" title="Infrared detector">infrared detectors</a>. The radioisotope thallium-201 (as the soluble chloride TlCl) is used in small, nontoxic amounts as an agent in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> scan, during one type of nuclear <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_stress_test" title="Cardiac stress test">cardiac stress test</a>.
</p><p>Soluble thallium salts (many of which are nearly tasteless) are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a>, and they were historically used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rat_poison" title="Rat poison">rat poisons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a>. Use of these compounds has been restricted or banned in many countries, because of their nonselective toxicity. Thallium poisoning usually results in hair loss, although this characteristic symptom does not always surface. Because of its historic popularity as a <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> weapon, thallium has gained notoriety as "the poisoner's poison" and "inheritance powder" (alongside <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A thallium atom has 81 electrons, arranged in the electron configuration [Xe]4f<sup>14</sup>5d<sup>10</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>6p<sup>1</sup>; of these, the three outermost electrons in the sixth shell are valence electrons. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Inert_pair_effect" title="Inert pair effect">inert pair effect</a>, the 6s electron pair is relativistically stabilised and it is more difficult to get them involved in chemical bonding than for the heavier elements. Thus, very few electrons are available for metallic bonding, similar to the neighboring elements <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, and hence thallium, like its congeners, is a soft, highly electrically conducting metal with a low melting point of 304 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of standard electrode potentials, depending on the reaction under study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> are reported for thallium, reflecting the greatly decreased stability of the +3 oxidation state:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<td>Tl<sup>3+</sup> + 3 e<sup>−</sup></td>
<td>↔ Tl
</td></tr>

<p>Thallium is the first element in group 13 where the reduction of the +3 oxidation state to the +1 oxidation state is spontaneous under standard conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood222_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood222-7">[7]</a></sup> Since bond energies decrease down the group, by thallium, the energy released in forming two additional bonds and attaining the +3 state is not always enough to outweigh the energy needed to involve the 6s-electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood224_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood224-9">[9]</a></sup> Accordingly, thallium(I) oxide and hydroxide are more basic and thallium(III) oxide and hydroxide are more acidic, showing that thallium conforms to the general rule of elements being more electropositive in their lower oxidation states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood224_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood224-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thallium is <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">malleable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sectility" title="Sectility">sectile</a> enough to be cut with a knife at room temperature. It has a metallic luster that, when exposed to air, quickly tarnishes to a bluish-gray tinge, resembling lead. It may be preserved by immersion in oil. A heavy layer of oxide builds up on thallium if left in air. In the presence of water, thallium <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> is formed. Sulfuric and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> dissolve thallium rapidly to make the <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_sulfate" title="Thallium(I) sulfate">sulfate</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium(I)_nitrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thallium(I) nitrate (page does not exist)">nitrate</a> salts, while <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> forms an insoluble <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_chloride" title="Thallium(I) chloride">thallium(I) chloride</a> layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_thallium" title="Isotopes of thallium">Isotopes of thallium</a></div>
<p>Thallium has 25 <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> which have <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic masses</a> that range from 184 to 210. <sup>203</sup>Tl and <sup>205</sup>Tl are the only stable isotopes and make up nearly all of natural thallium. <sup>204</sup>Tl is the most stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotope</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.78 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-11">[11]</a></sup> It is made by the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">neutron activation</a> of stable thallium in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The most useful radioisotope, <sup>201</sup>Tl (half-life 73 hours), decays by electron capture, emitting X-rays (~70–80 keV), and photons of 135 and 167 keV in 10% total abundance;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-11">[11]</a></sup> therefore, it has good imaging characteristics without excessive patient radiation dose. It is the most popular isotope used for thallium nuclear <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_stress_test" title="Cardiac stress test">cardiac stress tests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Thallium_compounds" title="Category:Thallium compounds">Category:Thallium compounds</a>.</div>
<h3><span id="Thallium.28III.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thallium(III)">Thallium(III)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thallium(III)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thallium(III) compounds resemble the corresponding aluminium(III) compounds. They are moderately strong oxidizing agents and are usually unstable, as illustrated by the positive reduction potential for the Tl<sup>3+</sup>/Tl couple. Some mixed-valence compounds are also known, such as Tl<sub>4</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and TlCl<sub>2</sub>, which contain both thallium(I) and thallium(III). <a href="/wiki/Thallium(III)_oxide" title="Thallium(III) oxide">Thallium(III) oxide</a>, Tl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, is a black solid which decomposes above 800 °C, forming the thallium(I) oxide and oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The simplest possible thallium compound, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thallane" title="Thallane">thallane</a> (TlH<sub>3</sub>), is too unstable to exist in bulk, both due to the instability of the +3 oxidation state as well as poor overlap of the valence 6s and 6p orbitals of thallium with the 1s orbital of hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The trihalides are more stable, although they are chemically distinct from those of the lighter group 13 elements and are still the least stable in the whole group. For instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thallium(III)_fluoride" title="Thallium(III) fluoride">thallium(III) fluoride</a>, TlF<sub>3</sub>, has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth(III)_fluoride" title="Bismuth(III) fluoride">β-BiF<sub>3</sub></a> structure rather than that of the lighter group 13 trifluorides, and does not form the <span class="chemf nowrap">TlF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> complex anion in aqueous solution. The trichloride and tribromide <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionate</a> just above room temperature to give the monohalides, and <a href="/wiki/Thallium_triiodide" title="Thallium triiodide">thallium triiodide</a> contains the linear <a href="/wiki/Triiodide" title="Triiodide">triiodide</a> anion (<span class="chemf nowrap">I<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) and is actually a thallium(I) compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood239_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood239-15">[15]</a></sup> Thallium(III) sesquichalcogenides do not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood254_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood254-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Thallium.28I.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thallium(I)">Thallium(I)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thallium(I)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thallium_halides" title="Thallium halides">thallium(I) halides</a> are stable. In keeping with the large size of the Tl<sup>+</sup> cation, the chloride and bromide have the <a href="/wiki/Caesium_chloride" title="Caesium chloride">caesium chloride</a> structure, while the fluoride and iodide have distorted <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> structures. Like the analogous silver compounds, TlCl, TlBr, and TlI are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosensitive" title="Photosensitive">photosensitive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood241_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood241-17">[17]</a></sup> The stability of thallium(I) compounds demonstrates its differences from the rest of the group: a stable <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_oxide" title="Thallium(I) oxide">oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_hydroxide" title="Thallium(I) hydroxide">hydroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_carbonate" title="Thallium(I) carbonate">carbonate</a> are known, as are many chalcogenides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood246_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood246-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Double_salt" title="Double salt">double salt</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">Tl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> has been shown to have hydroxyl-centred triangles of thallium, <span class="chemf nowrap">[Tl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>(OH)]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, as a recurring motif throughout its solid structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organothallium_compounds">Organothallium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organothallium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Organothallium compounds tend to be thermally unstable, in concordance with the trend of decreasing thermal stability down group 13. The chemical reactivity of the Tl–C bond is also the lowest in the group, especially for ionic compounds of the type R<sub>2</sub>TlX. Thallium forms the stable [Tl(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]<sup>+</sup> ion in aqueous solution; like the isoelectronic <a href="/wiki/Dimethylmercury" title="Dimethylmercury">Hg(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></a> and [Pb(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>, it is linear. Trimethylthallium and triethylthallium are, like the corresponding gallium and indium compounds, flammable liquids with low melting points. Like indium, thallium <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl" title="Cyclopentadienyl">cyclopentadienyl</a> compounds contain thallium(I), in contrast to gallium(III).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood262_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood262-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thallium (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">θαλλός</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">thallos</i>, meaning "a green shoot or twig")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> was discovered by flame spectroscopy in March 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The name comes from thallium's bright <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a> spectral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the publication of the improved method of flame spectroscopy by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> in the years 1859 to 1860, flame spectroscopy became an approved method to determine the composition of minerals and chemical products. <a href="/wiki/William_Crookes" title="William Crookes">William Crookes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude-Auguste_Lamy" title="Claude-Auguste Lamy">Claude-Auguste Lamy</a> both started to use the new method. William Crookes used it to make spectroscopic determinations for <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> on selenium compounds deposited in the <a href="/wiki/Lead_chamber_process" title="Lead chamber process">lead chamber</a> of a sulfuric acid production plant near <a href="/wiki/Abberode" title="Abberode">Tilkerode</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Harz" title="Harz">Harz mountains</a>. He had obtained the samples for his research on selenium cyanide from <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann" title="August Wilhelm von Hofmann">August Hofmann</a> years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> By 1862, Crookes was able to isolate small quantities of the new element and determine the properties of a few compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeKosky_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeKosky-27">[27]</a></sup> Claude-Auguste Lamy used a spectrometer that was similar to Crookes' to determine the composition of a selenium-containing substance which was deposited during the production of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>. He also noticed the new green line in the spectra and concluded that a new element was present. Lamy had received this material from the sulfuric acid plant of his friend Fréd Kuhlmann and this by-product was available in large quantities. Lamy started to isolate the new element from that source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The fact that Lamy was able to work ample quantities of thallium enabled him to determine the properties of several compounds and in addition he prepared a small ingot of metallic thallium which he prepared by remelting thallium he had obtained by electrolysis of thallium salts.
</p><p>As both scientists discovered thallium independently and a large part of the work, especially the isolation of the metallic thallium was done by Lamy, Crookes tried to secure his own priority on the work. Lamy was awarded a medal at the International Exhibition in London 1862: <i>For the discovery of a new and abundant source of thallium</i> and after heavy protest Crookes also received a medal: <i>thallium, for the discovery of the new element.</i> The controversy between both scientists continued through 1862 and 1863. Most of the discussion ended after Crookes was elected <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a> in June 1863.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-James-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murder_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murder-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The dominant use of thallium was the use as poison for <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>. After several accidents the use as poison was banned in the United States by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">Presidential Executive Order</a> 11643 in February 1972. In subsequent years several other countries also banned its use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS1972_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1972-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although thallium is a modestly abundant element in the Earth's crust, with a concentration estimated to be about 0.7 mg/kg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS2010_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS2010-32">[32]</a></sup> mostly in association with <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granites</a>, thallium is not generally economically recoverable from these sources. The major source of thallium for practical purposes is the trace amount that is found in <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and other heavy-metal-<a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vira_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vira-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg"><img alt="A close view of a rock crusted with groups of glassy, lustrous, silvery-blue hutchinsonite, in tight clusters of loosely aligned needle-like crystals, among smaller clusters of tiny orange-brown crystals" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="400" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg/220px-Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg/330px-Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hutchinsonite-131710.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystals of <a href="/wiki/Hutchinsonite" title="Hutchinsonite">hutchinsonite</a> (TlPbAs<sub>5</sub>S<sub>9</sub>)</div></div></div>
<p>Thallium is found in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Crookesite" title="Crookesite">crookesite</a> TlCu<sub>7</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Hutchinsonite" title="Hutchinsonite">hutchinsonite</a> TlPbAs<sub>5</sub>S<sub>9</sub>, and <a href="/wiki/Lor%C3%A1ndite" title="Lorándite">lorándite</a> TlAsS<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Thallium also occurs as a trace element in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_pyrite" title="Iron pyrite">iron pyrite</a>, and thallium is extracted as a by-product of roasting this mineral for the production of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2001_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sl2001-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downs_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Downs-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thallium can also be obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> of lead and zinc ores. <a href="/wiki/Manganese_nodule" title="Manganese nodule">Manganese nodules</a> found on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_floor" title="Ocean floor">ocean floor</a> contain some thallium, but the collection of these nodules has been prohibitively expensive. There is also the potential for damaging the oceanic environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In addition, several other thallium minerals, containing 16% to 60% thallium, occur in nature as complexes of sulfides or selenides that primarily contain <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, copper, lead, and/or <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>. These minerals are rare, and they have had no commercial importance as sources of thallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS2010_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS2010-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Allchar_deposit" title="Allchar deposit">Allchar deposit</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Macedonia</a> was the only area where thallium was actively mined. This deposit still contains an estimated 500 tonnes of thallium, and it is a source for several rare thallium minerals, for example <a href="/wiki/Lor%C3%A1ndite" title="Lorándite">lorándite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (USGS) estimates that the annual worldwide production of thallium is about 10 metric tonnes as a by-product from the smelting of copper, zinc, and lead ores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS2010_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS2010-32">[32]</a></sup> Thallium is either extracted from the dusts from the smelter flues or from residues such as <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a> that are collected at the end of the smelting process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-CS2010_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-CS2010-32">[32]</a></sup> The raw materials used for thallium production contain large amounts of other materials and therefore a purification is the first step. The thallium is leached either by the use of a base or sulfuric acid from the material. The thallium is precipitated several times from the solution to remove impurities. At the end it is converted to thallium sulfate and the thallium is extracted by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> on <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> plates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Downs_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Downs-36">[36]</a></sup> The production of thallium decreased by about 33% in the period from 1995 to 2009 – from about 15 metric <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> to about 10 tonnes. Since there are several small deposits or ores with relatively high thallium content, it would be possible to increase the production if a new application, such as a hypothetical thallium-containing <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity" title="High-temperature superconductivity">high-temperature superconductor</a>, becomes practical for widespread use outside of the laboratory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_uses">Historic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Historic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odorless</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">tasteless</a> <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_sulfate" title="Thallium(I) sulfate">thallium sulfate</a> was once widely used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rat_poison" title="Rat poison">rat poison</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a> killer. Since 1972 this use has been prohibited in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> due to safety concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS1972_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1972-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2001_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-sl2001-5">[5]</a></sup> Many other countries followed this example in subsequent years. Thallium salts were used in the treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringworm" title="Ringworm">ringworm</a>, other <a href="/wiki/Skin_infection" title="Skin infection">skin infections</a> and to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_sweat" title="Night sweat">night sweating</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> patients. This use has been limited due to their narrow <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_index" title="Therapeutic index">therapeutic index</a>, and the development of improved medicines for these conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optics">Optics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Optics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_bromide" title="Thallium(I) bromide">Thallium(I) bromide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_iodide" title="Thallium(I) iodide">thallium(I) iodide</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> have been used as infrared optical materials, because they are harder than other common infrared optics, and because they have transmission at significantly longer wavelengths. The trade name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KRS-5" title="KRS-5">KRS-5</a> refers to this material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_oxide" title="Thallium(I) oxide">Thallium(I) oxide</a> has been used to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> that have a high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">index of refraction</a>. Combined with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> or <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, thallium has been used in the production of high-<a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> glasses that have low <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a> in the range of 125 and 150 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a>. These glasses have room temperature properties that are similar to ordinary glasses and are durable, insoluble in water and have unique <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refractive_indices" title="Refractive indices">refractive indices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg"><img alt="A heavily pitted, blackish cylindrical rod, with extensive, crumbling brown-and-white corrosion" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="924" data-file-width="1548" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg/220px-Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg/330px-Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg/440px-Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thallium_rod_corroded.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Corroded thallium rod</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thallium(I)_sulfide" title="Thallium(I) sulfide">Thallium(I) sulfide</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> changes with exposure to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_light" title="Infrared light">infrared light</a> therefore making this compound useful in <a href="/wiki/Photoresistor" title="Photoresistor">photoresistors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-40">[40]</a></sup> Thallium selenide has been used in a <a href="/wiki/Bolometer" title="Bolometer">bolometer</a> for infrared detection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Doping selenium semiconductors with thallium improves their performance, thus it is used in trace amounts in selenium rectifiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-40">[40]</a></sup> Another application of thallium doping is the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a> crystals in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a> detection devices. In these, the sodium iodide crystals are doped with a small amount of thallium to improve their  efficiency as <a href="/wiki/Scintillation_(physics)" title="Scintillation (physics)">scintillation</a> generators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Some of the electrodes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_analyzer" title="Oxygen analyzer">dissolved oxygen analyzers</a> contain thallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2001_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-sl2001-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-temperature_superconductivity">High-temperature superconductivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: High-temperature superconductivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research activity with thallium is ongoing to develop high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting</a> materials for such applications as <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a>, storage of magnetic energy, <a href="/wiki/Linear_motor" title="Linear motor">magnetic propulsion</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_power_generation" title="Electric power generation">electric power generation</a> and transmission. The research in applications started after the discovery of the first <a href="/wiki/Thallium_barium_calcium_copper_oxide" title="Thallium barium calcium copper oxide">thallium barium calcium copper oxide</a> superconductor in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Thallium <a href="/wiki/Cuprate" title="Cuprate">cuprate</a> superconductors have been discovered that have transition temperatures above 120 K. Some mercury-doped thallium-cuprate superconductors have transition temperatures above 130 K at ambient pressure, nearly as high as the world-record-holding mercury cuprates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the widespread application of <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m">technetium-99m</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thallium-201" title="Thallium-201">thallium-201</a>, with a half-life of 73 hours, was the main substance for <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_perfusion_imaging" title="Myocardial perfusion imaging">nuclear cardiography</a>. The nuclide is still used for stress tests for risk stratification in patients with <a href="/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease" title="Coronary artery disease">coronary artery disease</a> (CAD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This isotope of thallium can be generated using a transportable generator, which is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m_generator" title="Technetium-99m generator">technetium-99m generator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The generator contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead-201" title="Lead-201">lead-201</a> (half-life 9.33 hours), which decays by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> to the thallium-201. The lead-201 can be produced in a <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotron</a> by the bombardment of thallium with <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteron" title="Deuteron">deuterons</a> by the (p,3n) and (d,4n) reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thallium_stress_test">Thallium stress test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Thallium stress test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A thallium stress test is a form of <a href="/wiki/Scintigraphy" title="Scintigraphy">scintigraphy</a>, where the amount of thallium in tissues correlates with tissue blood supply. Viable cardiac cells have normal <a href="/wiki/Na%2B/K%2B-ATPase" title="Na+/K+-ATPase">Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> ion-exchange pumps</a>. The Tl<sup>+</sup> cation binds the K<sup>+</sup> pumps and is transported into the cells. Exercise or <a href="/wiki/Dipyridamole" title="Dipyridamole">dipyridamole</a> induces widening (<a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a>) of arteries in the body. This produces <a href="/wiki/Coronary_steal" title="Coronary steal">coronary steal</a> by areas where arteries are maximally dilated. Areas of infarct or <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemic tissue</a> will remain "cold". Pre- and post-stress thallium may indicate areas that will benefit from myocardial <a href="/wiki/Revascularization" title="Revascularization">revascularization</a>. Redistribution indicates the existence of coronary steal and the presence of ischemic <a href="/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease" title="Coronary artery disease">coronary artery disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A mercury–thallium alloy, which forms a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> at 8.5% thallium, is reported to freeze at −60 °C, some 20 °C below the freezing point of mercury. This alloy is used in thermometers and low-temperature switches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-40">[40]</a></sup> In organic synthesis, thallium(III) salts, as thallium trinitrate or triacetate, are useful reagents for performing different transformations in aromatics, ketones and olefins, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Thallium is a constituent of the alloy in the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> plates of magnesium seawater batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sl2001_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-sl2001-5">[5]</a></sup> Soluble thallium salts are added to <a href="/wiki/Gold_plating" title="Gold plating">gold plating</a> baths to increase the speed of plating and to reduce grain size within the gold layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>A saturated solution of equal parts of thallium(I) <a href="/wiki/Formate" title="Formate">formate</a> (Tl(CHO<sub>2</sub>)) and thallium(I) <a href="/wiki/Malonate" title="Malonate">malonate</a> (Tl(C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)) in water is known as <a href="/wiki/Clerici_solution" title="Clerici solution">Clerici solution</a>. It is a mobile, odorless liquid which changes from yellowish to colourless upon reducing the concentration of the thallium salts. With a density of 4.25 g/cm<sup>3</sup> at 20 °C, Clerici solution is one of the heaviest aqueous solutions known. It was used in the 20th century for measuring the density of minerals by the <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">flotation</a> method, but its use has discontinued due to the high toxicity and corrosiveness of the solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jahns_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-jahns-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thallium iodide is frequently used as an additive in <a href="/wiki/Metal-halide_lamp" title="Metal-halide lamp">metal-halide lamps</a>, often together with one or two halides of other metals. It allows optimization of the lamp temperature and color rendering,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and shifts the spectral output to the green region, which is useful for underwater lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Thallium and its compounds are extremely toxic, and should be handled with care. There are numerous recorded cases of fatal thallium poisoning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for thallium exposure in the workplace as 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> skin exposure over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) also set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> skin exposure over an 8-hour workday. At levels of 15 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, thallium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>Contact with skin is dangerous, and adequate ventilation should be provided when melting this metal. Thallium(I) compounds have a high aqueous solubility and are readily absorbed through the skin. Exposure by inhalation should not exceed 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> in an 8-hour time-weighted average (40-hour work week).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Thallium will readily absorb through the skin, and care should be taken to avoid this route of exposure, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cutaneous" title="Cutaneous">cutaneous</a> absorption can exceed the absorbed dose received by inhalation at the <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> (PEL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Thallium is a suspected human <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebEl_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebEl-66">[66]</a></sup> For a long time thallium compounds were readily available as rat poison. This fact and that it is water-soluble and nearly tasteless led to frequent intoxication caused by accident or criminal intent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murder_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Murder-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the main methods of removing thallium (both radioactive and normal) from humans is to use <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">Prussian blue</a>, a material which absorbs thallium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Up to 20 grams per day of Prussian blue is fed by mouth to the person, and it passes through their digestive system and comes out in the <a href="/wiki/Human_feces" title="Human feces">stool</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hemodialysis" title="Hemodialysis">Hemodialysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemoperfusion" title="Hemoperfusion">hemoperfusion</a> are also used to remove thallium from the blood serum. At later stage of the treatment additional potassium is used to mobilize thallium from the tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA), man-made sources of thallium pollution include gaseous emission of <a href="/wiki/Cement#Heavy_metal_emissions_in_the_air" title="Cement">cement factories</a>, coal-burning power plants, and metal sewers. The main source of elevated thallium concentrations in water is the leaching of thallium from ore processing operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vira_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vira-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
