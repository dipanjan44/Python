<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anthropization</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <b>anthropization</b> is the conversion of open spaces, landscapes, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environments</a> by human action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Anthropic erosion is the process of human action degrading <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>.
</p><p>An area may be classified as anthropized even though it looks natural, such as <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> that have been <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforested</a> by humans. It can be difficult to determine how much a site has been anthropized in the case of <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> because one must be able to estimate the state of the landscape before significant human action.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The earliest known stages of anthropization can be found as early as the Neolithic era and the basic farmland created in that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>.
With the continually-growing population of humans, the land that the Earth provides has been appropriated over the years.  The ecological footprint created by anthropization is continually growing despite efficiency and technique improvements made in anthropization..
</p><p>Whether anthropized or not, all land seldom a few <a href="/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_Antarctica" title="Territorial claims in Antarctica">locations</a> has been claimed.  Outside of the largely inhospitable <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic</a> circles and large portions of other uninhabitable landscapes, much of the globe has been used or altered in some direct way by humans.  Land has been appropriated for many different reasons, but ultimately the outcome is typically a short-term benefit for humans.  An area is anthropized is some way to make land available for housing, to harvest the resources, to create space for some anthropological reason, or many other possibilities.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The root of many early forms of civilization, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> has been a primary reason for anthropization. To cultivate food or breed animals, humans must alter land—till soil or build structures—to facilitate agriculture.  This can lead to <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions, etc.), and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a> and overall an increased <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>. Agriculture and industry often overlap, and industry produces many of these effects too.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_development">Urban development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Urban development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Especially with approximately 7.5 Billion humans inhabiting the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> this typically aligns with an increase in residences worldwide.  Over the years, humans have built on land to meet their needs and wants. These actions range from small villages to massive factories, water parks, and apartments.  <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">Urbanization</a> and development of human residences can significantly affect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.  Not only does the physical space of buildings fragment habitats and possibly endanger <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">species</a>, but it fundamentally alters the habitat for any other living being.  For some species, this effect can be inconsequential, but for many this can have a dramatic impact.  The biosphere is very much interconnected, and this means that if one organism is affected, then as a result the other organisms within this ecosystem and food chain are also affected.  
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg/220px-Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg/330px-Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg/440px-Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Athabasca_oil_sands.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_oil_sands" title="Athabasca oil sands">Athabasca</a> oil sands are an example of anthropization as a result of the harvest and transport of a non-renewable resource, <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>As well, within the last century, any urbanized area requires roads for transportation.  This transportation is a continued source of pollution, and the roads can be a source of soil erosion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_and_technology">Industry and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To support humans, industrial buildings and processes are apparently essential. Urban development and agriculture require that people produce, refined, or construct many things. Key to this is that factories require that people gather the materials they need to create a product. The wide range of products in this anthropological age use a plethora of substances that must be harvested or produced.  Many of these materials are <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable</a> (e.g., fossil fuel, metal ores, etc.) and the harvest of these results in relatively permanent anthropization. For resources that depend on in high quantity, this can also mean temporary depletion or damage to the source of the resource (e.g., depletion or pollution of fresh water reserves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> improper or inefficient <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a>, etc.).  Even sustainable or renewable industrial anthropization still affects the environment.  While the resource in question may not be in jeopardy, the harvest and processing can still change and damage the environment.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Anthropization can also be a result of scientific endeavours.  This can manifest as construction of structures to aid in scientific discovery and observation.  This can range from structures such as <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatories</a>, or on the opposite scale the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a>.  These and many other things are built and used to enhance knowledge of sciences. They do however require space and energy.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To power the ever-growing human race, energy is needed.  Power-harvesting structures are built to harness energy, such as dams, windmills, and nuclear reactors.  These sources of energy ultimately fuel the rest of anthropological activity and are essential in this way.  However many of these methods have consequences.  With dams, construction aside, they can cause flooding, habitat fragmentation, and other effects.  With nuclear reactors, they have a lasting effect in that typically a lifespan of one of these is around 50 years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and afterwards the nuclear waste must be dealt with, and the structure itself must be shut down and cannot be used further.  To safely dispose of this even low-level waste can take hundreds of years, ranging upwards with increased radioactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  To produce and as a result of this production of energy, it requires a lot of anthropized land.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_anthropization">Evolution of anthropization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evolution of anthropization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:297px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2720" data-file-width="8000" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg/295px-All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg/442px-All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg/589px-All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg 2x" width="295"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:All_pyramids_of_Giza_panorama_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of ancient anthropization; Giza pyramid complex, Egypt.</div></div></div>
<p>Changes in population directly effect anthropological impact—but changes in technology and knowledge have greatly changed anthropization throughout the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>. The tools and methods that humans use to anthropize have changed drastically.  For examples, the great <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> were not constructed by some large machine, but instead by thousands of humans.  They were still able to build massive monuments, but the efficiency of their efforts and environmental damage is much different than today.  This shows that the environmental effect of modern anthropization is generally greater, not just because of the increase in population. Pollution and loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> in Egypt was largely natural, not man-made, and anthropization existed on a much lower level.
</p><p>As the human population of Earth increase, this anthropization will continue to evolve.
</p>
