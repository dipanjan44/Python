<p><b>Nickel</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ni</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 28. It is a silvery-white lustrous <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> with a slight golden tinge. Nickel belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> and is hard and <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>. Pure nickel, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulverization" title="Pulverization">powdered</a> to maximize the reactive <a href="/wiki/Surface_area" title="Surface area">surface area</a>, shows a significant chemical activity, but larger pieces are slow to react with air under <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard conditions</a> because an oxide layer forms on the surface and prevents further corrosion (<a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a>). Even so, pure <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">native</a> nickel is found in Earth's crust only in tiny amounts, usually in <a href="/wiki/Ultramafic_rock" title="Ultramafic rock">ultramafic rocks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in the interiors of larger <a href="/wiki/Iron_meteorite" title="Iron meteorite">nickel–iron meteorites</a> that were not exposed to oxygen when outside Earth's atmosphere.
</p><p>Meteoric nickel is found in combination with <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, a reflection of the origin of those elements as major end products of <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">supernova nucleosynthesis</a>. An iron–nickel mixture is thought to compose Earth's <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">inner core</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Use of nickel (as a natural <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">meteoric</a> nickel–iron alloy) has been traced as far back as 3500 BCE. Nickel was first isolated and classified as a chemical element in 1751 by <a href="/wiki/Axel_Fredrik_Cronstedt" title="Axel Fredrik Cronstedt">Axel Fredrik Cronstedt</a>, who initially mistook the <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a>, in the cobalt mines of <a href="/wiki/Los,_Sweden" title="Los, Sweden">Los, Hälsingland, Sweden</a>. The element's name comes from a mischievous sprite of German miner mythology, Nickel (similar to <a href="/wiki/Devil_in_Christianity#Christian_tradition" title="Devil in Christianity">Old Nick</a>), who personified the fact that copper-nickel ores resisted refinement into copper. An economically important source of nickel is the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> ore <a href="/wiki/Limonite" title="Limonite">limonite</a>, which often contains 1–2% nickel. Nickel's other important ore minerals include <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a> and a mixture of Ni-rich natural silicates known as <a href="/wiki/Garnierite" title="Garnierite">garnierite</a>. Major production sites include the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Basin" title="Sudbury Basin">Sudbury region</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (which is thought to be of <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteoric</a> origin), <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">Norilsk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.
</p><p>Nickel is slowly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidized</a> by air at room temperature and is considered corrosion-resistant. Historically, it has been used for plating iron and <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>, coating chemistry equipment, and manufacturing certain <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> that retain a high silvery polish, such as <a href="/wiki/Nickel_silver" title="Nickel silver">German silver</a>. About 9% of world nickel production is still used for corrosion-resistant nickel plating. Nickel-plated objects sometimes provoke <a href="/wiki/Nickel_allergy" title="Nickel allergy">nickel allergy</a>. Nickel has been widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coins" title="Coins">coins</a>, though its rising price has led to some replacement with cheaper metals in recent years.
</p><p>Nickel is one of four elements (the others are <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CoeySkumryev1999_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CoeySkumryev1999-7">[7]</a></sup> that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> at approximately room temperature. <a href="/wiki/Alnico" title="Alnico">Alnico</a> permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnets" title="Magnets">magnets</a> based partly on nickel are of intermediate strength between iron-based permanent magnets and <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_magnet" title="Rare-earth magnet">rare-earth magnets</a>. The metal is valuable in modern times chiefly in <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>; about 68% of world production is used in stainless steel. A further 10% is used for nickel-based and copper-based alloys, 7% for alloy steels, 3% in foundries, 9% in plating and 4% in other applications, including the fast-growing battery sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8">[8]</a></sup> As a compound, nickel has a number of niche chemical manufacturing uses, such as a <a href="/wiki/Raney_nickel" title="Raney nickel">catalyst for hydrogenation</a>, cathodes for batteries, pigments and metal surface treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Nickel is an essential nutrient for some microorganisms and plants that have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> with nickel as an <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">active site</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atomic_and_physical_properties">Atomic and physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atomic and physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ni@CNT2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="455" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Ni%40CNT2.jpg/220px-Ni%40CNT2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Ni%40CNT2.jpg/330px-Ni%40CNT2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Ni%40CNT2.jpg/440px-Ni%40CNT2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ni@CNT2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">Electron micrograph</a> of a Ni nanocrystal inside a single wall <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotube</a>; scale bar 5 nm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel-pV.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Nickel-pV.svg/220px-Nickel-pV.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Nickel-pV.svg/330px-Nickel-pV.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Nickel-pV.svg/440px-Nickel-pV.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel-pV.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molar volume vs. pressure at room temperature</div></div></div>
<p>Nickel is a silvery-white metal with a slight golden tinge that takes a high polish. It is one of only four elements that are magnetic at or near room temperature, the others being iron, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie temperature</a> is 355 °C (671 °F), meaning that bulk nickel is non-magnetic above this temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The unit cell of nickel is a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">face-centered cube</a> with the lattice parameter of 0.352 nm, giving an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic radius</a> of 0.124 nm. This crystal structure is stable to pressures of at least 70 GPa. Nickel belongs to the transition metals and is hard and <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electron_configuration_dispute">Electron configuration dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electron configuration dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The nickel atom has two <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configurations</a>, [Ar] 3d<sup>8</sup> 4s<sup>2</sup> and [Ar] 3d<sup>9</sup> 4s<sup>1</sup>, which are very close in energy – the symbol [Ar] refers to the <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>-like core structure. There is some disagreement on which configuration has the lowest energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scerri_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scerri-12">[12]</a></sup> Chemistry textbooks quote the electron configuration of nickel as [Ar] 4s<sup>2</sup> 3d<sup>8</sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which can also be written [Ar] 3d<sup>8</sup> 4s<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This configuration agrees with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madelung_rule" title="Madelung rule">Madelung energy ordering rule</a>, which predicts that 4s is filled before 3d. It is supported by the experimental fact that the lowest energy state of the nickel atom is a 3d<sup>8</sup> 4s<sup>2</sup> energy level, specifically the 3d<sup>8</sup>(<sup>3</sup>F) 4s<sup>2</sup> <sup>3</sup>F, <i>J</i> = 4 level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, each of these two configurations splits into several energy levels due to <a href="/wiki/Fine_structure" title="Fine structure">fine structure</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-15">[15]</a></sup> and the two sets of energy levels overlap. The average energy of states with configuration [Ar] 3d<sup>9</sup> 4s<sup>1</sup> is actually lower than the average energy of states with configuration [Ar] 3d<sup>8</sup> 4s<sup>2</sup>. For this reason, the research literature on atomic calculations quotes the ground state configuration of nickel as [Ar] 3d<sup>9</sup> 4s<sup>1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scerri_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scerri-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_nickel" title="Isotopes of nickel">Isotopes of nickel</a></div>
<p>The isotopes of nickel range in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> from 48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">48</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>) to 78 u (<span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">78</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>).
</p><p>Naturally occurring nickel is composed of five stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>; <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">58</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">60</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">61</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">62</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">64</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>, with <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">58</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> being the most abundant (68.077% <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">natural abundance</a>). Isotopes heavier than <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">62</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> cannot be formed by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> without losing energy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nickel-62" title="Nickel-62">Nickel-62</a> has the highest mean <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_binding_energy" title="Nuclear binding energy">nuclear binding energy</a> per nucleon of any <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a>, at 8.7946 MeV/nucleon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Its binding energy is greater than both <a href="/wiki/Iron-56" title="Iron-56"><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">56</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Fe</span></a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron-58" title="Iron-58"><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">58</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Fe</span></a>, more abundant elements often incorrectly cited as having the most tightly-bound nuclides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aip1995_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-aip1995-17">[17]</a></sup> Although this would seem to predict nickel-62 as the most abundant heavy element in the universe, the relatively high rate of photodisintegration of nickel in stellar interiors causes iron to be by far the most abundant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aip1995_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-aip1995-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Stable isotope nickel-60 is the daughter product of the <a href="/wiki/Extinct_radionuclide" title="Extinct radionuclide">extinct radionuclide</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron-60" title="Iron-60"><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">60</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Fe</span></a>, which decays with a half-life of 2.6 million years. Because <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">60</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Fe</span> has such a long half-life, its persistence in materials in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a> may generate observable variations in the isotopic composition of <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">60</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>. Therefore, the abundance of <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">60</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> present in extraterrestrial material may provide insight into the origin of the solar system and its early history.
</p><p>Some 18 nickel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been characterised, the most stable being <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">59</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 76,000 years, <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">63</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> with 100 years, and <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">56</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> with 6 days. All of the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives that are less than 60 hours and the majority of these have half-lives that are less than 30 seconds. This element also has one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Radioactive nickel-56 is produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_burning_process" title="Silicon burning process">silicon burning process</a> and later set free in large quantities during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_IIn" title="Type IIn">type Ia</a> <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>. The shape of the <a href="/wiki/Light_curve" title="Light curve">light curve</a> of these supernovae at intermediate to late-times corresponds to the decay via <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> of nickel-56 to <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>-56 and ultimately to iron-56.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nucleos_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nucleos-19">[19]</a></sup> Nickel-59 is a long-lived <a href="/wiki/Cosmogenic_nuclide" title="Cosmogenic nuclide">cosmogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclide</a> with a half-life of 76,000 years. <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">59</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> has found many applications in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_geology" title="Isotope geology">isotope geology</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">59</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> has been used to date the terrestrial age of <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a> and to determine abundances of extraterrestrial dust in ice and <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>. Nickel-78's half-life was recently measured at 110 milliseconds, and is believed an important isotope in <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">supernova nucleosynthesis</a> of elements heavier than iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The nuclide <sup>48</sup>Ni, discovered in 1999, is the most proton-rich heavy element isotope known. With 28 <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and 20 <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> <sup>48</sup>Ni is "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_magic" title="Double magic">double magic</a>", as is <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">78</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span> with 28 protons and 50 neutrons. Both are therefore unusually stable for nuclides with so large a proton-neutron imbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ore_genesis" title="Ore genesis">Ore genesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Nickel_minerals" title="Category:Nickel minerals">Category:Nickel minerals</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Widmanstatten_hand.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Widmanstatten_hand.jpg/220px-Widmanstatten_hand.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Widmanstatten_hand.jpg/330px-Widmanstatten_hand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Widmanstatten_hand.jpg/440px-Widmanstatten_hand.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Widmanstatten_hand.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Widmanst%C3%A4tten_pattern" title="Widmanstätten pattern">Widmanstätten pattern</a> showing the two forms of nickel-iron, kamacite and taenite, in an octahedrite meteorite</div></div></div>
<p>On Earth, nickel occurs most often in combination with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and iron in <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> in <a href="/wiki/Millerite" title="Millerite">millerite</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Nickeline" title="Nickeline">nickeline</a>, and with arsenic and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> in nickel <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Nickel is commonly found in <a href="/wiki/Iron_meteorite" title="Iron meteorite">iron meteorites</a> as the alloys <a href="/wiki/Kamacite" title="Kamacite">kamacite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taenite" title="Taenite">taenite</a>.
</p><p>The bulk of the nickel is mined from two types of <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> deposits. The first is <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">laterite</a>, where the principal ore mineral mixtures are nickeliferous <a href="/wiki/Limonite" title="Limonite">limonite</a>, (Fe,Ni)O(OH), and <a href="/wiki/Garnierite" title="Garnierite">garnierite</a> (a mixture of various hydrous nickel and nickel-rich silicates). The second is magmatic <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> deposits, where the principal ore mineral is <a href="/wiki/Pentlandite" title="Pentlandite">pentlandite</a>: <span class="chemf nowrap">(Ni,Fe)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span></span>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> have the biggest estimate reserves (45% all together).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Identified land-based resources throughout the world averaging 1% nickel or greater comprise at least 130 million tons of nickel (about the double of known reserves). About 60% is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laterites" title="Laterites">laterites</a> and 40% in sulfide deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2012_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysical</a> evidence, most of the nickel on Earth is believed to be in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">inner cores</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kamacite" title="Kamacite">Kamacite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taenite" title="Taenite">taenite</a> are naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> of iron and nickel. For kamacite, the alloy is usually in the proportion of 90:10 to 95:5, although impurities (such as <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>) may be present, while for taenite the nickel content is between 20% and 65%. Kamacite and taenite are also found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_iron_meteorite" title="Nickel iron meteorite">nickel iron meteorites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Nickel_compounds" title="Category:Nickel compounds">Category:Nickel compounds</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png"><img alt="A nickel atom with four single bonds to carbonyl (carbon triple-bonded to oxygen; bonds via the carbon) groups that are laid out tetrahedrally around it" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1075" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png/170px-Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png/255px-Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png/340px-Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel-carbonyl-2D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tetracarbonyl nickel</div></div></div>
<p>The most common <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of nickel is +2, but compounds of Ni<sup>0</sup>, Ni<sup>+</sup>, and Ni<sup>3+</sup> are well known, and the exotic oxidation states Ni<sup>2−</sup>, Ni<sup>1−</sup>, and Ni<sup>4+</sup> have been produced and studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Nickel.280.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickel(0)">Nickel(0)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nickel(0)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nickel_tetracarbonyl" title="Nickel tetracarbonyl">Nickel tetracarbonyl</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">(Ni(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), discovered by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Mond" title="Ludwig Mond">Ludwig Mond</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MondNa_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MondNa-26">[26]</a></sup> is a volatile, highly toxic liquid at room temperature. On heating, the complex decomposes back to nickel and carbon monoxide:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">Ni(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> ⇌ Ni + 4 CO</dd></dl>
<p>This behavior is exploited in the <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a> for purifying nickel, as described above. The related nickel(0) complex <a href="/wiki/Bis(cyclooctadiene)nickel(0)" title="Bis(cyclooctadiene)nickel(0)">bis(cyclooctadiene)nickel(0)</a> is a useful catalyst in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organonickel_chemistry" title="Organonickel chemistry">organonickel chemistry</a> because the <a href="/wiki/1,5-Cyclooctadiene" title="1,5-Cyclooctadiene">cyclooctadiene</a> (or <i>cod</i>) ligands are easily displaced.
</p>
<h3><span id="Nickel.28I.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickel(I)">Nickel(I)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nickel(I)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nickel(I) complexes are uncommon, but one example is the tetrahedral complex NiBr(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>. Many nickel(I) complexes feature Ni-Ni bonding, such as the dark red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>[Ni<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>(CN)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup of <span class="chemf nowrap">K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup This compound is oxidised in water, liberating <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChemH_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChemH-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is thought that the nickel(I) oxidation state is important to nickel-containing enzymes, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NiFe_Hydrogenase" title="NiFe Hydrogenase">[NiFe]-hydrogenase</a>, which catalyzes the reversible reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a> to <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housecroft_4th_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housecroft_4th-28">[28]</a></sup>

<h3><span id="Nickel.28II.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickel(II)">Nickel(II)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nickel(II)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Nickel(II) forms compounds with all common anions, including <a href="/wiki/Nickel_sulfide" title="Nickel sulfide">sulfide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_sulfate" title="Nickel sulfate">sulfate</a>, carbonate, hydroxide, carboxylates, and halides. <a href="/wiki/Nickel(II)_sulfate" title="Nickel(II) sulfate">Nickel(II) sulfate</a> is produced in large quantities by dissolving nickel metal or oxides in sulfuric acid, forming both a hexa- and heptahydrates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> useful for <a href="/wiki/Nickel_electroplating" title="Nickel electroplating">electroplating</a> nickel. Common salts of nickel, such as the chloride, nitrate, and sulfate, dissolve in water to give green solutions of the <a href="/wiki/Metal_aquo_complex" title="Metal aquo complex">metal aquo complex</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">[Ni(H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.
</p><p>The four halides form nickel compounds, which are solids with molecules that feature octahedral Ni centres. <a href="/wiki/Nickel(II)_chloride" title="Nickel(II) chloride">Nickel(II) chloride</a> is most common, and its behavior is illustrative of the other halides. Nickel(II) chloride is produced by dissolving nickel or its oxide in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>. It is usually encountered as the green hexahydrate, the formula of which is usually written NiCl<sub>2</sub>•6H<sub>2</sub>O.  When dissolved in water, this salt forms the <a href="/wiki/Metal_aquo_complex" title="Metal aquo complex">metal aquo complex</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">[Ni(H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>. Dehydration of NiCl<sub>2</sub>•6H<sub>2</sub>O gives the yellow anhydrous <span class="chemf nowrap">NiCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>Some tetracoordinate nickel(II) complexes, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bis(triphenylphosphine)nickel_chloride" title="Bis(triphenylphosphine)nickel chloride">bis(triphenylphosphine)nickel chloride</a>, exist both in tetrahedral and square planar geometries. The tetrahedral complexes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>, whereas the square planar complexes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>. In having properties of magnetic equilibrium and formation of octahedral complexes, they contrast with the divalent complexes of the heavier group 10 metals, palladium(II) and platinum(II), which form only square-planar geometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nickelocene" title="Nickelocene">Nickelocene</a> is known; it has an <a href="/wiki/Electron_counting" title="Electron counting">electron count</a> of 20, making it relatively unstable.
</p>

<h3><span id="Nickel.28III.29_and_.28IV.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickel(III)_and_(IV)">Nickel(III) and (IV)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nickel(III) and (IV)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous Ni(III) compounds are known, with the first such examples being Nickel(III) trihalophosphines (Ni<sub>III</sub>(PPh<sub>3</sub>)X<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jensen_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen-30">[30]</a></sup> Further, Ni(III) forms simple salts with fluoride<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ni(III)F_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ni(III)F-31">[31]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Nickel(III)_oxide" title="Nickel(III) oxide">oxide</a> ions. Ni(III) can be stabilized by σ-donor ligands such as <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphine" title="Phosphine">phosphines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChemH_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChemH-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ni(IV) is present in the mixed oxide <span class="chemf nowrap">BaNiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, while Ni(III) is present in <a href="/wiki/Nickel_oxide_hydroxide" title="Nickel oxide hydroxide">nickel oxide hydroxide</a>, which is used as the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable batteries</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium" title="Nickel-cadmium">nickel-cadmium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-iron_battery" title="Nickel-iron battery">nickel-iron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_hydrogen_battery" title="Nickel hydrogen battery">nickel hydrogen</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-metal_hydride_battery" title="Nickel-metal hydride battery">nickel-metal hydride</a>, and used by certain manufacturers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li-ion" title="Li-ion">Li-ion</a> batteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Ni(IV) remains a rare oxidation state of nickel and very few compounds are known to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NiIV_Science_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NiIV_Science-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NiIV_dap_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NiIV_dap-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the ores of nickel are easily mistaken for ores of silver, understanding of this metal and its use dates to relatively recent times. However, the unintentional use of nickel is ancient, and can be traced back as far as 3500 BCE. <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">Bronzes</a> from what is now Syria have been found to contain as much as 2% nickel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Some ancient Chinese manuscripts suggest that "white copper" (<a href="/wiki/Cupronickel" title="Cupronickel">cupronickel</a>, known as <i>baitong</i>) was used there between 1700 and 1400 BCE. This Paktong white copper was exported to Britain as early as the 17th century, but the nickel content of this alloy was not discovered until 1822.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNeil_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil-38">[38]</a></sup> Coins of nickel-copper alloy were minted by the Bactrian kings <a href="/wiki/Agathocles_of_Bactria" title="Agathocles of Bactria">Agathocles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_II" title="Euthydemus II">Euthydemus II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pantaleon" title="Pantaleon">Pantaleon</a> in the 2nd Century BCE, possibly out of the Chinese cupronickel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickeline.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1789" data-file-width="2452" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Nickeline.jpg/220px-Nickeline.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Nickeline.jpg/330px-Nickeline.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Nickeline.jpg/440px-Nickeline.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickeline.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>nickeline/niccolite</div></div></div>
<p>In medieval Germany, a red mineral was found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erzgebirge" title="Erzgebirge">Erzgebirge</a> (Ore Mountains) that resembled copper ore. However, when miners were unable to extract any copper from it, they blamed a mischievous sprite of German mythology, Nickel (similar to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_teaching_about_the_Devil" title="Christian teaching about the Devil">Old Nick</a></i>), for besetting the copper. They called this ore <i>Kupfernickel</i> from the German <i>Kupfer</i> for copper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JEC-I_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-JEC-I-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JEC-II_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-JEC-II-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JEC-III_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-JEC-III-43">[43]</a></sup> This ore is now known to be <a href="/wiki/Nickeline" title="Nickeline">nickeline</a> (aka niccolite), a nickel <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenide</a>. In 1751, Baron <a href="/wiki/Axel_Fredrik_Cronstedt" title="Axel Fredrik Cronstedt">Axel Fredrik Cronstedt</a> tried to extract copper from kupfernickel at a <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> mine in the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> village of <a href="/wiki/Los,_Sweden" title="Los, Sweden">Los</a>, and instead produced a white metal that he named after the spirit that had given its name to the mineral, nickel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In modern German, Kupfernickel or Kupfer-Nickel designates the alloy <a href="/wiki/Cupronickel" title="Cupronickel">cupronickel</a>.
</p><p>Originally, the only source for nickel was the rare Kupfernickel. Beginning in 1824, nickel was obtained as a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt blue</a> production. The first large-scale smelting of nickel began in Norway in 1848 from nickel-rich <a href="/wiki/Pyrrhotite" title="Pyrrhotite">pyrrhotite</a>. The introduction of nickel in steel production in 1889 increased the demand for nickel, and the nickel deposits of <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, discovered in 1865, provided most of the world's supply between 1875 and 1915. The discovery of the large deposits in the <a href="/wiki/Sudbury_Basin" title="Sudbury Basin">Sudbury Basin</a>, Canada in 1883, in <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">Norilsk-Talnakh</a>, Russia in 1920, and in the <a href="/wiki/Merensky_Reef" title="Merensky Reef">Merensky Reef</a>, South Africa in 1924, made large-scale production of nickel possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNeil_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coinage">Coinage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Coinage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="667" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Nickel2.jpg/220px-Nickel2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Nickel2.jpg/330px-Nickel2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Nickel2.jpg/440px-Nickel2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dutch_guilder" title="Dutch guilder">Dutch coins</a> made of pure nickel</div></div></div>
<p>Aside from the aforementioned Bactrian coins, nickel was not a component of coins until the mid-19th century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nickel_(Canadian_coin)" title="Nickel (Canadian coin)">99.9% nickel five-cent coins</a> were struck in Canada (the world's largest nickel producer at the time) during non-war years from 1922–1981; the metal content made these coins magnetic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> During the wartime period 1942–45, most or all nickel was removed from Canadian and U.S. coins to save it for manufacturing armor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JEC-I_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-JEC-I-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Canada used 99.9% nickel from 1968 in its higher-value coins until 2000.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">Switzerland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Switzerland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coins of nearly pure nickel were first used in 1881 in Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anna_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-anna-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Birmingham forged nickel coins in about 1833 for trading in Malaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the term "nickel" or "nick" originally applied to the copper-nickel <a href="/wiki/Flying_Eagle_cent" title="Flying Eagle cent">Flying Eagle cent</a>, which replaced copper with 12% nickel 1857–58, then the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Head_cent" title="Indian Head cent">Indian Head cent</a> of the same alloy from 1859–1864. Still later, in 1865, the term designated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three-cent_piece_(United_States_coin)" title="Three-cent piece (United States coin)">three-cent nickel</a>, with nickel increased to 25%. In 1866, the <a href="/wiki/Nickel_(United_States_coin)#Shield_nickel_(1866–1883)" title="Nickel (United States coin)">five-cent shield nickel</a> (25% nickel, 75% copper) appropriated the designation. Along with the alloy proportion, this term has been used to the present in the United States.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_use">Current use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Current use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 21st century, the high price of nickel has led to some replacement of the metal in coins around the world. Coins still made with nickel alloys include one- and two-<a href="/wiki/Euro_coins" title="Euro coins">euro coins</a>, 5¢, 10¢, 25¢ and 50¢ <a href="/wiki/Coins_of_the_United_States_dollar" title="Coins of the United States dollar">U.S. coins</a>, and 20p, 50p, £1 and £2 <a href="/wiki/Coins_of_the_pound_sterling" title="Coins of the pound sterling">UK coins</a>. Nickel-alloy in 5p and 10p UK coins was replaced with nickel-plated steel began in 2012, causing allergy problems for some people and public controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anna_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-anna-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_production">World production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: World production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_world_production.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="750" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nickel_world_production.svg/310px-Nickel_world_production.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nickel_world_production.svg/465px-Nickel_world_production.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nickel_world_production.svg/620px-Nickel_world_production.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_world_production.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Time trend of nickel production<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg&amp;lang=en"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1192" data-file-width="1703" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg/310px-Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg/465px-Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg/620px-Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evolution_minerai_nickel.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nickel ores grade evolution in some leading nickel producing countries.</div></div></div>
<p>Around 2 million tonnes of nickel are produced annually worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> are the world's largest producers of nickel, as reported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Geological_Survey" title="US Geological Survey">US Geological Survey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSCS2012_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGSCS2012-23">[23]</a></sup> The largest deposits of nickel in non-Russian Europe are located in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. Identified land-based resources averaging 1% nickel or greater contain at least 130 million tons of nickel. About 60% is in laterites and 40% is in sulfide deposits. In addition, extensive deep-sea resources of nickel are in manganese crusts and nodules covering large areas of the ocean floor, particularly in the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs1-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The one locality in the United States where nickel has been profitably mined is <a href="/wiki/Riddle,_Oregon" title="Riddle, Oregon">Riddle, Oregon</a>, where several square miles of nickel-bearing <a href="/wiki/Garnierite" title="Garnierite">garnierite</a> surface deposits are located. The mine closed in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Eagle_mine_project" title="Eagle mine project">Eagle mine project</a> is a new nickel mine in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Upper_Peninsula_of_Michigan" title="Upper Peninsula of Michigan">upper peninsula</a>. Construction was completed in 2013, and operations began in the third quarter of 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-54">[54]</a></sup> In the first full year of operation, Eagle Mine produced 18,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eagle_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-eagle-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction_and_purification">Extraction and purification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Extraction and purification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Nickel_extraction.svg&amp;lang=en"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1503" data-file-width="1623" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Nickel_extraction.svg/220px-Nickel_extraction.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Nickel_extraction.svg/330px-Nickel_extraction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Nickel_extraction.svg/440px-Nickel_extraction.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_extraction.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evolution of the annual nickel extraction, according to ores.</div></div></div>
<p>Nickel is obtained through <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">extractive metallurgy</a>: it is extracted from the ore by conventional roasting and reduction processes that yield a metal of greater than 75% purity. In many <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> applications, 75% pure nickel can be used without further purification, depending on the impurities.
</p><p>Traditionally, most sulfide ores have been processed using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrometallurgical" title="Pyrometallurgical">pyrometallurgical</a> techniques to produce a <a href="/wiki/Matte_(metallurgy)" title="Matte (metallurgy)">matte</a> for further refining. Recent advances in <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">hydrometallurgical techniques</a> resulted in significantly purer metallic nickel product. Most sulfide deposits have traditionally been processed by concentration through a <a href="/wiki/Froth_flotation" title="Froth flotation">froth flotation</a> process followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrometallurgical" title="Pyrometallurgical">pyrometallurgical</a> extraction. In hydrometallurgical processes, nickel sulfide ores are concentrated with flotation (differential flotation if Ni/Fe ratio is too low) and then smelted. The nickel matte is further processed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_extraction_techniques#Recovery_from_nickel-cobalt_sulfide_concentrates_(Sherritt_process)" title="Cobalt extraction techniques">Sherritt-Gordon process</a>. First, copper is removed by adding <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, leaving a concentrate of cobalt and nickel. Then, solvent extraction is used to separate the cobalt and nickel, with the final nickel content greater than 99%.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/220px-Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/330px-Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg/440px-Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_electrolytic_and_1cm3_cube.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">Electrolytically refined</a> nickel nodule, with green, crystallized nickel-electrolyte salts visible in the pores.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrorefining">Electrorefining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Electrorefining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A second common refining process is leaching the metal matte into a nickel salt solution, followed by the electro-winning of the nickel from solution by plating it onto a cathode as electrolytic nickel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mond_process">Mond process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mond process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_kugeln.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nickel_kugeln.jpg/220px-Nickel_kugeln.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nickel_kugeln.jpg/330px-Nickel_kugeln.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Nickel_kugeln.jpg/440px-Nickel_kugeln.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nickel_kugeln.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Highly purified nickel spheres made by the <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a></div>
<p>The purest metal is obtained from nickel oxide by the <a href="/wiki/Mond_process" title="Mond process">Mond process</a>, which achieves a purity of greater than 99.99%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The process was patented by Ludwig Mond and has been in industrial use since before the beginning of the 20th century. In this process, nickel is reacted with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> in the presence of a sulfur catalyst at around 40–80 °C to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl" title="Nickel carbonyl">nickel carbonyl</a>. Iron gives <a href="/wiki/Iron_pentacarbonyl" title="Iron pentacarbonyl">iron pentacarbonyl</a>, too, but this reaction is slow. If necessary, the nickel may be separated by distillation. <a href="/wiki/Dicobalt_octacarbonyl" title="Dicobalt octacarbonyl">Dicobalt octacarbonyl</a> is also formed in nickel distillation as a by-product, but it decomposes to <a href="/wiki/Tetracobalt_dodecacarbonyl" title="Tetracobalt dodecacarbonyl">tetracobalt dodecacarbonyl</a> at the reaction temperature to give a non-volatile solid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann-1_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-1-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel is obtained from nickel carbonyl by one of two processes. It may be passed through a large chamber at high temperatures in which tens of thousands of nickel spheres, called pellets, are constantly stirred. The carbonyl decomposes and deposits pure nickel onto the nickel spheres. In the alternate process, nickel carbonyl is decomposed in a smaller chamber at 230 °C to create a fine nickel powder. The byproduct carbon monoxide is recirculated and reused. The highly pure nickel product is known as "carbonyl nickel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_value">Metal value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Metal value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The market price of nickel surged throughout 2006 and the early months of 2007; as of April 5, 2007, the metal was trading at <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US</a>$52,300/<a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonne</a> or $1.47/oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LME_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-LME-62">[62]</a></sup> The price subsequently fell dramatically, and as of September 2017, the metal was trading at $11,000/tonne, or $0.31/oz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nickel_(United_States_coin)" title="Nickel (United States coin)">US nickel coin</a> contains 0.04 ounces (1.1 g) of nickel, which at the April 2007 price was worth 6.5 cents, along with 3.75 grams of copper worth about 3 cents, with a total metal value of more than 9 cents. Since the face value of a nickel is 5 cents, this made it an attractive target for melting by people wanting to sell the metals at a profit. However, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">United States Mint</a>, in anticipation of this practice, implemented new interim rules on December 14, 2006, subject to public comment for 30 days, which criminalized the melting and export of cents and nickels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Violators can be punished with a fine of up to $10,000 and/or imprisoned for a maximum of five years.
</p><p>As of September 19, 2013, the melt value of a U.S. nickel (copper and nickel included) is $0.045, which is 90% of the face value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1258" data-file-width="1961" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg/220px-Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg/330px-Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg/440px-Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turbinenschaufel_RB199.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nickel superalloy jet engine (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RB199" title="RB199">RB199</a>) turbine blade</div></div></div>
<p>The global production of nickel is presently used as follows: 68% in stainless steel; 10% in nonferrous <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>; 9% in <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a>; 7% in alloy steel; 3% in foundries; and 4% other uses (including batteries).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel is used in many specific and recognizable industrial and consumer products, including <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alnico" title="Alnico">alnico</a> magnets, coinage, <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable batteries</a>, electric guitar strings, microphone capsules, plating on plumbing fixtures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> and special alloys such as <a href="/wiki/Permalloy" title="Permalloy">permalloy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elinvar" title="Elinvar">elinvar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Invar" title="Invar">invar</a>. It is used for plating and as a green tint in glass. Nickel is preeminently an alloy metal, and its chief use is in nickel steels and nickel cast irons, in which it typically increases the tensile strength, toughness, and elastic limit. It is widely used in many other alloys, including nickel brasses and bronzes and alloys with copper, chromium, aluminium, lead, cobalt, silver, and gold (<a href="/wiki/Inconel" title="Inconel">Inconel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incoloy" title="Incoloy">Incoloy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monel" title="Monel">Monel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nimonic" title="Nimonic">Nimonic</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MagnetEZ.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="470" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MagnetEZ.jpg/220px-MagnetEZ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MagnetEZ.jpg/330px-MagnetEZ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/MagnetEZ.jpg/440px-MagnetEZ.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MagnetEZ.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A "horseshoe magnet" made of <a href="/wiki/Alnico" title="Alnico">alnico</a> nickel alloy.</div></div></div>
<p>Because it is resistant to corrosion, nickel was occasionally used as a substitute for decorative silver. Nickel was also occasionally used in some countries after 1859 as a cheap coinage metal (see above), but in the later years of the 20th century was replaced by cheaper <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> (i.e., iron) alloys, except in the United States and Canada.
</p><p>Nickel is an excellent alloying agent for certain precious metals and is used in the <a href="/wiki/Metallurgical_assay" title="Metallurgical assay">fire assay</a> as a collector of <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group elements</a> (PGE). As such, nickel is capable of fully collecting all six PGE elements from ores, and of partially collecting gold. High-throughput nickel mines may also engage in PGE recovery (primarily <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>); examples are Norilsk in Russia and the Sudbury Basin in Canada.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Metal_foam" title="Metal foam">Nickel foam</a> or nickel mesh is used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_diffusion_electrode" title="Gas diffusion electrode">gas diffusion electrodes</a> for <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_fuel_cell" title="Alkaline fuel cell">alkaline fuel cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel and its alloys are frequently used as catalysts for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> reactions. <a href="/wiki/Raney_nickel" title="Raney nickel">Raney nickel</a>, a finely divided nickel-aluminium alloy, is one common form, though related catalysts are also used, including Raney-type catalysts.
</p><p>Nickel is a naturally magnetostrictive material, meaning that, in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, the material undergoes a small change in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Magnetostriction" title="Magnetostriction">magnetostriction</a> of nickel is on the order of 50 ppm and is negative, indicating that it contracts.
</p><p>Nickel is used as a binder in the cemented <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a> or hardmetal industry and used in proportions of 6% to 12% by weight. Nickel makes the tungsten carbide magnetic and adds corrosion-resistance to the cemented parts, although the hardness is less than those with a cobalt binder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">63</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Ni</span>, with its <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 100.1 years, is useful in <a href="/wiki/Krytron" title="Krytron">krytron</a> devices as a <a href="/wiki/Beta_particle" title="Beta particle">beta particle</a> (high-speed <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>) emitter to make <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> by the keep-alive electrode more reliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Around 27% of all nickel production is destined for engineering, 10% for building and construction, 14% for tubular products, 20% for metal goods, 14% for transport, 11% for electronic goods, and 5% for other uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although not recognized until the 1970s, nickel is known to play an important role in the biology of some plants, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eubacteria" title="Eubacteria">eubacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaebacteria" title="Archaebacteria">archaebacteria</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigel_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sigel-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sydor_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sydor-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Nickel enzymes such as <a href="/wiki/Urease" title="Urease">urease</a> are considered virulence factors in some organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Urease catalyzes the hydrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> to form ammonia and carbamate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sydor_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sydor-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigel_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sigel-74">[74]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/NiFe_hydrogenase" title="NiFe hydrogenase">NiFe hydrogenases</a> can catalyze the oxidation of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> to form protons and electrons, and can also catalyze the reverse reaction, the reduction of protons to form hydrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sydor_75-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sydor-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sigel_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sigel-74">[74]</a></sup> A nickel-tetrapyrrole coenzyme, <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_F430" title="Cofactor F430">cofactor F430</a>, is present in methyl <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_M" title="Coenzyme M">coenzyme M</a> reductase, which can catalyze the formation of methane, or the reverse reaction, in <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> One of the carbon monoxide dehydrogenase enzymes consists of an Fe-Ni-S cluster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Other nickel-bearing enzymes include a rare bacterial class of <a href="/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase" title="Superoxide dismutase">superoxide dismutase</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyoxalase_I" title="Glyoxalase I">glyoxalase I</a> enzymes in bacteria and several parasitic eukaryotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trypanosomatid" title="Trypanosomatid">trypanosomal</a> parasites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> (in higher organisms, including yeast and mammals, this enzyme contains divalent Zn<sup>2+</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aronsson_1978_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-aronsson_1978-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ridderstroem_1996_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-ridderstroem_1996-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dietary nickel may affect human health through infections by nickel-dependent bacteria, but it is also possible that nickel is an essential nutrient for bacteria residing in the large intestine, in effect functioning as a <a href="/wiki/Prebiotic_(nutrition)" title="Prebiotic (nutrition)">prebiotic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> The U.S. Institute of Medicine has not confirmed that nickel is an essential nutrient for humans, so neither a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recommended_Dietary_Allowance" title="Recommended Dietary Allowance">Recommended Dietary Allowance</a> (RDA) nor an Adequate Intake have been established. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolerable_upper_intake_levels" title="Tolerable upper intake levels">Tolerable Upper Intake Level</a> of dietary nickel is 1000 µg/day as soluble nickel salts. Dietary intake is estimated at 70 to 100 µg/day, with less than 10% absorbed. What is absorbed is excreted in urine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Relatively large amounts of nickel – comparable to the estimated average ingestion above – <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(chemistry)" title="Leaching (chemistry)">leach</a> into food cooked in stainless steel. For example, the amount of nickel leached after 10 cooking cycles into one serving of tomato sauce averages 88 µg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel released from <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Traps" title="Siberian Traps">Siberian Traps</a> volcanic eruptions is suspected of assisting the growth of <i><a href="/wiki/Methanosarcina" title="Methanosarcina">Methanosarcina</a></i>, a genus of euryarchaeote archaea that produced methane during the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a>, the biggest extinction event on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The major source of nickel exposure is oral consumption, as nickel is essential to plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Nickel is found naturally in both food and water, and may be increased by human <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. For example, nickel-plated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faucet" title="Faucet">faucets</a> may contaminate water and soil; <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and smelting may dump nickel into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste-water" title="Waste-water">waste-water</a>; nickel–steel <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> cookware and nickel-pigmented dishes may release nickel into food. The atmosphere may be polluted by nickel ore refining and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> combustion. Humans may absorb nickel directly from <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a> and skin contact with jewelry, <a href="/wiki/Shampoo" title="Shampoo">shampoos</a>, detergents, and <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a>. A less-common form of chronic exposure is through <a href="/wiki/Hemodialysis" title="Hemodialysis">hemodialysis</a> as traces of nickel ions may be absorbed into the plasma from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelating" title="Chelating">chelating</a> action of <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a>.
</p><p>The average daily exposure does not pose a threat to human health. Most of the nickel absorbed every day by humans is removed by the kidneys and passed out of the body through urine or is eliminated through the gastrointestinal tract without being absorbed. Nickel is not a cumulative poison, but larger doses or chronic inhalation exposure may be toxic, even <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a>, and constitute an <a href="/wiki/Occupational_hazard" title="Occupational hazard">occupational hazard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nickel compounds are classified as human carcinogens<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-99">[99]</a></sup> based on increased respiratory cancer risks observed in epidemiological studies of sulfidic ore refinery workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-100">[100]</a></sup> This is supported by the positive results of the NTP bioassays with Ni sub-sulfide and Ni oxide in rats and mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> The human and animal data consistently indicate a lack of carcinogenicity via the oral route of exposure and limit the carcinogenicity of nickel compounds to respiratory tumours after inhalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Nickel metal is classified as a suspect carcinogen;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_98-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-98">[98]</a></sup> there is consistency between the absence of increased respiratory cancer risks in workers predominantly exposed to metallic nickel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-100">[100]</a></sup> and the lack of respiratory tumours in a rat lifetime inhalation carcinogenicity study with nickel metal powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-105">[105]</a></sup> In the rodent inhalation studies with various nickel compounds and nickel metal, increased lung inflammations with and without bronchial lymph node hyperplasia or fibrosis were observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_105-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> In rat studies, oral ingestion of water-soluble nickel salts can trigger perinatal mortality effects in pregnant animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Whether these effects are relevant to humans is unclear as epidemiological studies of highly exposed female workers have not shown adverse developmental toxicity effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to nickel in the workplace by inhalation, ingestion, and contact with skin or eye. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for the workplace at 1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> per 8-hour workday, excluding nickel carbonyl. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) specifies the <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 0.015 mg/m<sup>3</sup> per 8-hour workday. At 10 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, nickel is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl" title="Nickel carbonyl">Nickel carbonyl</a> [<span class="chemf nowrap">Ni(CO)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>] is an extremely toxic gas. The toxicity of metal carbonyls is a function of both the toxicity of the metal and the off-gassing of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> from the carbonyl functional groups; nickel carbonyl is also explosive in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sensitization_(immunology)" title="Sensitization (immunology)">Sensitized</a> individuals may show a skin contact <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_allergy_(nickel_allergic_contact_dermatitis)" title="Nickel allergy (nickel allergic contact dermatitis)">allergy to nickel</a> known as a contact <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>. Highly sensitized individuals may also react to foods with high nickel content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-aad-115">[115]</a></sup> Sensitivity to nickel may also be present in patients with <a href="/wiki/Dyshidrosis" title="Dyshidrosis">pompholyx</a>. Nickel is the top confirmed contact allergen worldwide, partly due to its use in jewelry for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierced_ear" title="Pierced ear">pierced ears</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Nickel allergies affecting pierced ears are often marked by itchy, red skin. Many earrings are now made without nickel or low-release nickel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> to address this problem. The amount allowed in products that contact human skin is now regulated by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. In 2002, researchers found that the nickel released by 1 and 2 Euro coins was far in excess of those standards. This is believed to be the result of a <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanic</a> reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Nickel was voted <a href="/wiki/Allergen_of_the_Year" title="Allergen of the Year">Allergen of the Year</a> in 2008 by the American Contact Dermatitis Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> In August 2015, the American Academy of Dermatology adopted a position statement on the safety of nickel: "Estimates suggest that contact dermatitis, which includes nickel sensitization, accounts for approximately $1.918 billion and affects nearly 72.29 million people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aad_115-1"><a href="#cite_note-aad-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reports show that both the nickel-induced activation of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF-1) and the up-regulation of hypoxia-inducible genes are caused by depletion of intracellular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbate" title="Ascorbate">ascorbate</a>. The addition of ascorbate to the culture medium increased the intracellular ascorbate level and reversed both the metal-induced stabilization of HIF-1- and HIF-1α-dependent gene expression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p>
