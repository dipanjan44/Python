<p><b>Attribution of recent climate change</b> is the effort to scientifically ascertain mechanisms responsible for recent <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate changes</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, commonly known as '<a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>. The effort has focused on changes observed during the period of <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental temperature record</a>, particularly in the last 50 years. This is the period when human activity has grown fastest and observations of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">atmosphere above the surface</a> have become available. According to the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC), it is "extremely likely"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> that human influence was the dominant cause of global warming between 1951 and 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The best estimate is that observed warming since 1951 has been entirely human caused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the main human activities that contribute to global warming are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>increasing <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a></li>
<li>global changes to land surface, such as <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a></li>
<li>increasing atmospheric concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In addition to human activities, some natural mechanisms can also cause <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, including for example, <a href="/wiki/Climate_oscillation" title="Climate oscillation">climate oscillations</a>, changes in <a href="/wiki/Solar_activity_and_climate" title="Solar activity and climate">solar activity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> activity.
</p><p>Multiple lines of evidence support attribution of recent climate change to human activities:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_endangerment_fact_sheet_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_endangerment_fact_sheet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A basic <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a> understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> system: greenhouse gas concentrations have increased and their warming properties are well-established.</li>
<li>Historical estimates of past climate changes suggest that the recent changes in global surface temperature are unusual.</li>
<li>Computer-based <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a> are unable to replicate the observed warming unless human greenhouse gas emissions are included.</li>
<li>Natural forces alone (such as solar and volcanic activity) cannot explain the observed warming.</li></ul>
<p>The IPCC's attribution of recent global warming to human activities is a view shared by the scientific community,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2001_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2001-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and is also supported by 196 other scientific organizations worldwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opr_list_of_scientific_organizations_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-opr_list_of_scientific_organizations-12">[12]</a></sup> (see also: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific opinion on climate change</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change#Causes" title="Climate change">Climate change § Causes</a></div>
<p>Factors affecting <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s climate can be broken down into <a href="/wiki/Global_warming#Feedback" title="Global warming">feedbacks</a> and forcings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2008_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup> A forcing is something that is imposed externally on the climate system. External forcings include natural phenomena such as volcanic eruptions and variations in the sun's output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Human activities can also impose forcings, for example, through changing the composition of the atmosphere.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">Radiative forcing</a> is a measure of how various factors alter the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">energy balance</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A positive radiative forcing will tend to increase the energy of the Earth-atmosphere system, leading to a warming of the system. Between the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in 1750, and the year 2005, the increase in the atmospheric concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a>: CO<sub>2</sub>) led to a positive radiative forcing, averaged over the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Surface_area" title="Surface area">surface area</a>, of about 1.66 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> per square metre (abbreviated W m<sup>−2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_radiative_forcing_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_radiative_forcing-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate feedbacks can either amplify or dampen the response of the climate to a given forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2008_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup>
There are many feedback mechanisms in the climate system that can either amplify (a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>) or diminish (a <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>) the effects of a change in climate forcing.
</p><p>The climate system will vary in response to changes in forcings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
The climate system will show internal variability both in the presence and absence of forcings imposed on it, (see images opposite). This internal variability is a result of complex interactions between components of the climate system, such as the <a href="/wiki/Coupling_(physics)" title="Coupling (physics)">coupling</a> between the atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> (see also the later section on <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#Internal_climate_variability_and_global_warming" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Internal climate variability and global warming</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> An example of internal variability is the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño–Southern Oscillation">El Niño–Southern Oscillation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_vs._attribution">Detection vs. attribution</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:427px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_(NOAA_NCDC).png"><img alt="Refer to caption and adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1235" data-file-width="2137" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png/425px-Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png/638px-Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png/850px-Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_%28NOAA_NCDC%29.png 2x" width="425"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Detection_and_attribution_of_climate_change_(NOAA_NCDC).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In detection and attribution, the natural factors considered usually include changes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">Sun's output</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> eruptions, as well as natural modes of variability such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">El Niño</a> and La Niña. Human factors include the emissions of heat-trapping <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">"greenhouse" gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">particulates</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">clearing of forests</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land-use</a> changes. Figure source: NOAA NCDC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Detection and attribution of climate signals, as well as its common-sense meaning, has a more precise definition within the climate change literature, as expressed by the IPCC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Detection of a climate signal does not always imply significant attribution. The IPCC's <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report</a> says "it is <i>extremely likely</i> that human activities have exerted a substantial net warming influence on climate since 1750," where "extremely likely" indicates a probability greater than 95%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>Detection</i> of a signal requires demonstrating that an observed change is <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">statistically significantly</a> different from that which can be explained by natural internal variability.
</p><p><i>Attribution</i> requires demonstrating that a signal is:
</p>
<ul><li>unlikely to be due entirely to internal variability;</li>
<li>consistent with the estimated responses to the given combination of anthropogenic and natural forcing</li>
<li>not consistent with alternative, physically plausible explanations of recent climate change that exclude important elements of the given combination of forcings.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_attributions">Key attributions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> is the primary <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> that is contributing to recent climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup> <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is absorbed and emitted naturally as part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, through animal and plant <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic eruptions</a>, and ocean-atmosphere exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup> Human activities, such as the burning of fossil fuels and changes in land use (see below), release large amounts of carbon to the atmosphere, causing <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> concentrations in the atmosphere to rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The high-accuracy measurements of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration, initiated by <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Charles David Keeling</a> in 1958, constitute the master time series documenting the changing composition of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-le_treut_2007_co2_fingerprint_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-le_treut_2007_co2_fingerprint-23">[23]</a></sup> These data have iconic status in climate change science as evidence of the effect of human activities on the chemical composition of the global atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-le_treut_2007_co2_fingerprint_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-le_treut_2007_co2_fingerprint-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Along with CO<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> are also major forcing contributors to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> lists these together with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbon" title="Hydrofluorocarbon">hydrofluorocarbon</a> (HFCs), <a href="/wiki/Fluorocarbon" title="Fluorocarbon">perfluorocarbons</a> (PFCs), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">sulfur hexafluoride</a> (SF<sub>6</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kyoto_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-kyoto-24">[24]</a></sup> which are entirely artificial gases, as contributors to radiative forcing. The chart at right attributes anthropogenic greenhouse gas <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">emissions</a> to eight main economic sectors, of which the largest contributors are <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> (many of which burn <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>), <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> processes, <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transportation</a> <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a> (generally <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> by-products (mainly methane from <a href="/wiki/Enteric_fermentation" title="Enteric fermentation">enteric fermentation</a> and nitrous oxide from <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> use).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_vapor">Water vapor</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png"><img alt="Refer to adjacent text" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="603" data-file-width="650" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png/350px-Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png/525px-Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenhouse_Gas_by_Sector.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Emission Database for Global Atmospheric Research version 3.2, fast track 2000 project</div></div></div>
<p>Water vapor is the most abundant greenhouse gas and is the largest contributor to the natural greenhouse effect, despite having a short atmospheric lifetime<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup> (about 10 days).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rc_wv_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rc_wv-26">[26]</a></sup> Some human activities can influence local water vapor levels. However, on a global scale, the concentration of water vapor is controlled by temperature, which influences overall rates of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup> Therefore, the global concentration of water vapor is not substantially affected by direct human emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-epa_role_of_greenhouse_gases-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span></h3>
<p>Climate change is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> for two main reasons. Between 1750 and 2007, about two-thirds of anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> emissions were produced from burning fossil fuels, and about one-third of emissions from changes in land use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> primarily <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Technical_Summary_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Technical_Summary-28">[28]</a></sup> Deforestation both reduces the amount of carbon dioxide absorbed by deforested regions and releases greenhouse gases directly, together with aerosols, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">biomass burning</a> that frequently accompanies it.
</p><p>A second reason that climate change has been attributed to land use is that the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">terrestrial albedo</a> is often altered by use, which leads to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>. This effect is more significant locally than globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Technical_Summary_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Technical_Summary-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Livestock_and_land_use">Livestock and land use</span></h4>
<p>Worldwide, livestock production occupies 70% of all land used for agriculture, or 30% of the ice-free land surface of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livestock_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-livestock-29">[29]</a></sup>
More than 18% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions are attributed to livestock and livestock-related activities such as deforestation and increasingly fuel-intensive farming practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livestock_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-livestock-29">[29]</a></sup> Specific attributions to the livestock sector include:
</p>
<ul><li>9% of global anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions</li>
<li>35–40% of global anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> (chiefly due to <a href="/wiki/Enteric_fermentation" title="Enteric fermentation">enteric fermentation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>)</li>
<li>64% of global anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> emissions, chiefly due to <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-livestock_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-livestock-29">[29]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerosols">Aerosols</span></h3>
<p>With virtual certainty, scientific consensus has attributed various forms of climate change, chiefly cooling effects, to <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>, which are small particles or droplets suspended in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WG1-2007-FM_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-WG1-2007-FM-30">[30]</a></sup>
Key sources to which anthropogenic aerosols are attributed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> include:
</p>
<ul><li>biomass burning such as <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> deforestation. Aerosols produced are primarily <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>.</li>
<li>industrial <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, which produces <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> and airborne <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a></li>
<li>dust produced by <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> effects such as <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attribution_of_20th_century_climate_change">Attribution of 20th century climate change</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Climate_Change_Attribution.png"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="573" data-file-width="500" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png/250px-Climate_Change_Attribution.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png/375px-Climate_Change_Attribution.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Climate_Change_Attribution.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Climate_Change_Attribution.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate model</a>'s reconstruction of temperature change during the 20th century as the result of five studied forcing factors and the amount of temperature change attributed to each.</div></div></div>
<p>Over the past 150 years human activities have released increasing quantities of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. This has led to increases in mean global temperature, or <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Other human effects are relevant—for example, sulphate <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> are believed to have a cooling effect. Natural factors also contribute. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_temperature_record" title="Historical temperature record">historical temperature record</a> of the last century, the Earth's near-surface air temperature has risen around 0.74 <a href="/wiki/Plus-minus_sign" title="Plus-minus sign">±</a> 0.18 °Celsius (1.3 ± 0.32 °Fahrenheit).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>A historically important question in climate change research has regarded the relative importance of human activity and non-<a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> causes during the period of <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental record</a>. In the 1995 <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">Second Assessment Report</a> (SAR), the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> made the widely quoted statement that "The balance of evidence suggests a discernible human influence on global climate". The phrase "balance of evidence" suggested the (English) common-law standard of proof required in civil as opposed to criminal courts: not as high as "beyond reasonable doubt". In 2001 the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">Third Assessment Report</a> (TAR) refined this, saying "There is new and stronger evidence that most of the warming observed over the last 50 years is attributable to human activities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WG1-2001-SPM_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WG1-2001-SPM-33">[33]</a></sup> The 2007 <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report</a> (AR4) strengthened this finding:
</p>
<ul><li>"Anthropogenic warming of the climate system is widespread and can be detected in temperature observations taken at the surface, in the free atmosphere and in the oceans. Evidence of the effect of external influences, both anthropogenic and natural, on the climate system has continued to accumulate since the TAR."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Other findings of the IPCC Fourth Assessment Report include:
</p>
<ul><li>"It is <i>extremely unlikely</i> (&lt;5%)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_uncertainty_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_uncertainty-35">[35]</a></sup> that the global pattern of warming during the past half century can be explained without external forcing (i.e., it is inconsistent with being the result of internal variability), and <i>very unlikely</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_uncertainty_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_uncertainty-35">[35]</a></sup> that it is due to known natural external causes alone. The warming occurred in both the ocean and the atmosphere and took place at a time when natural external forcing factors would likely have produced cooling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hegerl_2007_executive_summary_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-hegerl_2007_executive_summary-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>"From new estimates of the combined anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">forcing</a> due to greenhouse gases, <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land surface changes</a>, it is <i>extremely likely</i> (&gt;95%)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_uncertainty_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_uncertainty-35">[35]</a></sup> that human activities have exerted a substantial net warming influence on climate since 1750."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_2007_drivers_of_climate_change_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_2007_drivers_of_climate_change-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>"It is <i>virtually certain</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ar4_uncertainty_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-ar4_uncertainty-35">[35]</a></sup> that anthropogenic aerosols produce a net negative <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> (cooling influence) with a greater magnitude in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> than in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solomon_2007_drivers_of_climate_change_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-solomon_2007_drivers_of_climate_change-37">[37]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Over the past five decades there has been a global warming of approximately 0.65 °C (1.17 °F) at the Earth's surface (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_temperature_record" title="Historical temperature record">historical temperature record</a>). Among the possible factors that could produce changes in global mean temperature are internal variability of the climate system, external forcing, an increase in concentration of greenhouse gases, or any combination of these. Current studies indicate that the increase in greenhouse gases, most notably CO<sub>2</sub>, is mostly responsible for the observed warming. Evidence for this conclusion includes:
</p>
<ul><li>Estimates of internal variability from climate models, and reconstructions of past temperatures, indicate that the warming is unlikely to be entirely natural.</li>
<li>Climate models forced by natural factors <i>and</i> increased greenhouse gases and aerosols reproduce the observed global temperature changes; those forced by natural factors alone do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WG1-2001-SPM_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-WG1-2001-SPM-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Fingerprint" methods (see <a href='/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#"Fingerprint"_studies' title="Attribution of recent climate change">below</a>) indicate that the pattern of change is closer to that expected from greenhouse gas-forced change than from natural change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>The plateau in warming from the 1940s to 1960s can be attributed largely to sulphate aerosol cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Details_on_attribution">Details on attribution</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1088" data-file-width="1449" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/250px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/375px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png/500px-2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2000_Year_Temperature_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>For Northern Hemisphere temperature, recent decades appear to be the warmest since at least about 1000AD, and the warming since the late 19th century is unprecedented over the last 1000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_nh_temp_for_last_1300_years_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_nh_temp_for_last_1300_years-40">[40]</a></sup> Older data are insufficient to provide reliable hemispheric temperature estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_nh_temp_for_last_1300_years_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_nh_temp_for_last_1300_years-40">[40]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Recent scientific assessments find that most of the warming of the Earth's surface over the past 50 years has been caused by human activities (see also the section on <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#Scientific_literature_and_opinion" title="Attribution of recent climate change">scientific literature and opinion</a>). This conclusion rests on multiple lines of evidence. Like the warming "signal" that has gradually emerged from the "noise" of natural climate variability, the scientific evidence for a human influence on global climate has accumulated over the past several decades, from many hundreds of studies. No single study is a "<a href="/wiki/Smoking_gun" title="Smoking gun">smoking gun</a>." Nor has any single study or combination of studies undermined the large body of evidence supporting the conclusion that human activity is the primary driver of recent warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first line of evidence is based on a physical understanding of how greenhouse gases trap heat, how the climate system responds to increases in greenhouse gases, and how other human and natural factors influence climate. The second line of evidence is from indirect estimates of climate changes over the last 1,000 to 2,000 years. These records are obtained from living things and their remains (like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>) and from physical quantities (like the ratio between lighter and heavier <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>), which change in measurable ways as climate changes. The lesson from these data is that global surface temperatures over the last several decades are clearly unusual, in that they were higher than at any time during at least the past 400 years. For the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, the recent temperature rise is clearly unusual in at least the last 1,000 years (see graph opposite).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The third line of evidence is based on the broad, qualitative consistency between observed changes in climate and the computer model simulations of how climate would be expected to change in response to human activities. For example, when climate models are run with historical increases in greenhouse gases, they show gradual warming of the Earth and ocean surface, increases in ocean heat content and the temperature of the lower atmosphere, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">rise</a> in global sea level, retreat of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover, cooling of the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, an increase in the amount of atmospheric water vapor, and changes in large-scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure patterns</a>. These and other aspects of modelled climate change are in agreement with observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Finally, there is extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical" title="Statistical">statistical</a> evidence from so-called "fingerprint" studies. Each factor that affects climate produces a unique pattern of climate response, much as each person has a unique fingerprint. Fingerprint studies exploit these unique signatures, and allow detailed comparisons of modelled and observed climate change patterns. Scientists rely on such studies to attribute observed changes in climate to a particular cause or set of causes. In the real world, the climate changes that have occurred since the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> are due to a complex mixture of human and natural causes. The importance of each individual influence in this mixture changes over time. Of course, there are not multiple Earths, which would allow an experimenter to change one factor at a time on each Earth, thus helping to isolate different fingerprints. Therefore, climate models are used to study how individual factors affect climate. For example, a single factor (like greenhouse gases) or a set of factors can be varied, and the response of the modelled climate system to these individual or combined changes can thus be studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>For example, when climate model simulations of the last century include all of the major influences on climate, both human-induced and natural, they can reproduce many important features of observed climate change patterns. When human influences are removed from the model experiments, results suggest that the surface of the Earth would actually have cooled slightly over the last 50 years (see graph, opposite). The clear message from fingerprint studies is that the observed warming over the last half-century cannot be explained by natural factors, and is instead caused primarily by human factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another fingerprint of human effects on climate has been identified by looking at a slice through the layers of the atmosphere, and studying the pattern of temperature changes from the surface up through the stratosphere (see the section on <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#Solar_activity" title="Attribution of recent climate change">solar activity</a>). The earliest fingerprint work focused on changes in surface and atmospheric temperature. Scientists then applied fingerprint methods to a whole range of climate variables, identifying human-caused climate signals in the heat content of the oceans, the height of the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> (the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, which has shifted upward by hundreds of feet in recent decades), the geographical patterns of precipitation, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, surface pressure, and the <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> from major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_basin" title="River basin">river basins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies published after the appearance of the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> in 2007 have also found human fingerprints in the increased levels of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> (both close to the surface and over the full extent of the atmosphere), in the decline of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent, and in the patterns of changes in <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> surface temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The message from this entire body of work is that the climate system is telling a consistent story of increasingly dominant human influence – the changes in temperature, ice extent, moisture, and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">circulation</a> patterns fit together in a physically consistent way, like pieces in a complex puzzle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasingly, this type of fingerprint work is shifting its emphasis. As noted, clear and compelling scientific evidence supports the case for a pronounced human influence on global climate. Much of the recent attention is now on climate changes at continental and regional scales, and on variables that can have large impacts on societies. For example, scientists have established causal links between human activities and the changes in <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpack</a>, maximum and minimum (<a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal</a>) temperature, and the seasonal timing of runoff over <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountainous</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">western United States</a>. Human activity is likely to have made a substantial contribution to ocean surface temperature changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> formation regions. Researchers are also looking beyond the physical climate system, and are beginning to tie changes in the distribution and seasonal behaviour of plant and animal species to human-caused changes in temperature and precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>For over a decade, one aspect of the climate change story seemed to show a significant difference between models and observations. In the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, all models predicted that with a rise in greenhouse gases, the troposphere would be expected to warm more rapidly than the surface. Observations from weather balloons, satellites, and surface thermometers seemed to show the opposite behaviour (more rapid warming of the surface than the troposphere). This issue was a stumbling block in understanding the causes of climate change. It is now largely resolved. Research showed that there were large uncertainties in the <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a> data. When uncertainties in models and observations are properly accounted for, newer observational data sets (with better treatment of known problems) are in agreement with climate model results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>This does not mean, however, that all remaining differences between models and observations have been resolved. The observed changes in some climate variables, such as Arctic sea ice, some aspects of precipitation, and patterns of surface pressure, appear to be proceeding much more rapidly than models have projected. The reasons for these differences are not well understood. Nevertheless, the bottom-line conclusion from climate fingerprinting is that most of the observed changes studied to date are consistent with each other, and are also consistent with our scientific understanding of how the climate system would be expected to respond to the increase in heat-trapping gases resulting from human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_weather_events">Extreme weather events</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="689" data-file-width="2062" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/220px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/330px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png/440px-Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shifting_Distribution_of_Summer_Temperature_Anomalies2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frequency of occurrence (vertical axis) of local June–July–August temperature anomalies (relative to 1951–1980 mean) for Northern Hemisphere land in units of local <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> (horizontal axis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-50">[50]</a></sup> According to Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-50">[50]</a></sup> the distribution of anomalies has shifted to the right as a consequence of global warming, meaning that unusually hot summers have become more common. This is analogous to the rolling of a dice: cool summers now cover only half of one side of a six-sided die, white covers one side, red covers four sides, and an extremely hot (red-brown) anomaly covers half of one side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climate_dice_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-climate_dice-50">[50]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>One of the subjects discussed in the literature is whether or not extreme weather events can be attributed to human activities. Seneviratne <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> stated that attributing individual extreme weather events to human activities was challenging. They were, however, more confident over attributing changes in long-term trends of extreme weather. For example, Seneviratne <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> concluded that human activities had likely led to a warming of extreme daily minimum and maximum temperatures at the global scale.
</p><p>Another way of viewing the problem is to consider the effects of human-induced climate change on the probability of future extreme weather events. Stott <i>et al.</i> (2003),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stott_2003_european_heat_wave_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-stott_2003_european_heat_wave-53">[53]</a></sup> for example, considered whether or not human activities had increased the risk of severe <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, like the one <a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">experienced in 2003</a>. Their conclusion was that human activities had very likely more than doubled the risk of heat waves of this magnitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stott_2003_european_heat_wave_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-stott_2003_european_heat_wave-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>An analogy can be made between an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sportsperson" title="Sportsperson">athlete</a> on <a href="/wiki/Doping_in_sport" title="Doping in sport">steroids</a> and human-induced climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the same way that an athlete's performance may increase from using steroids, human-induced climate change increases the risk of some extreme weather events.
</p><p>Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> suggested that human activities have greatly increased the risk of summertime heat waves. According to their analysis, the land area of the Earth affected by very hot summer temperature anomalies has greatly increased over time (refer to graphs on the left). In the base period 1951-1980, these anomalies covered a few tenths of 1% of the global land area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_discussion_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_discussion-56">[56]</a></sup> In recent years, this has increased to around 10% of the global land area. With high confidence, Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_discussion_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_discussion-56">[56]</a></sup> attributed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_Russian_heat_wave" title="2010 Russian heat wave">2010 Moscow</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9312_Southern_United_States_drought" title="2010–12 Southern United States drought">2011 Texas heat waves</a> to human-induced global warming.
</p><p>An earlier study by Dole <i>et al.</i> (2011)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dole_2011_russian_heat_wave_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-dole_2011_russian_heat_wave-57">[57]</a></sup> concluded that the 2010 Moscow heatwave was mostly due to natural weather variability. While not directly citing Dole <i>et al.</i> (2011),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dole_2011_russian_heat_wave_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-dole_2011_russian_heat_wave-57">[57]</a></sup> Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_discussion_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_discussion-56">[56]</a></sup> rejected this type of explanation. Hansen <i>et al.</i> (2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_discussion_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_discussion-56">[56]</a></sup> stated that a combination of natural weather variability and human-induced global warming was responsible for the Moscow and Texas heat waves.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_literature_and_opinion">Scientific literature and opinion</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">Scientific opinion on climate change</a></div>
<p>There are a number of examples of published and informal support for the consensus view. As mentioned earlier, the IPCC has concluded that most of the observed increase in globally averaged temperatures since the mid-20th century is "very likely" due to human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_2007_attribution_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_2007_attribution-58">[58]</a></sup> The IPCC's conclusions are consistent with those of several reports produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2001_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2001-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
A report published in 2009 by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Global_Change_Research_Program" title="U.S. Global Change Research Program">U.S. Global Change Research Program</a> concluded that "[global] warming is unequivocal and primarily human-induced."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
A number of scientific organizations have issued statements that support the consensus view. Two examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>a joint statement made in 2005 by the national science academies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G8" title="G8">G8</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li>a joint statement made in 2008 by the <a href="/wiki/Network_of_African_Science_Academies" title="Network of African Science Academies">Network of African Science Academies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_and_attribution_studies">Detection and attribution studies</span></h3>
<p>The IPCC Fourth Assessment Report (2007), concluded that attribution was possible for a number of observed changes in the climate (see <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>). However, attribution was found to be more difficult when assessing changes over smaller regions (less than continental scale) and over short time periods (less than 50 years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hegerl_2007_executive_summary_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-hegerl_2007_executive_summary-36">[36]</a></sup>
Over larger regions, averaging reduces natural variability of the climate, making detection and attribution easier.
</p>
<ul><li>In 1996, in a paper in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> titled "A search for human influences on the thermal structure of the atmosphere", <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_D._Santer" title="Benjamin D. Santer">Benjamin D. Santer</a> et al. wrote: "The observed spatial patterns of temperature change in the free atmosphere from 1963 to 1987 are similar to those predicted by state-of-the-art climate models incorporating various combinations of changes in carbon dioxide, anthropogenic sulphate aerosol and stratospheric ozone concentrations. The degree of pattern similarity between models and observations increases through this period. It is likely that this trend is partially due to human activities, although many uncertainties remain, particularly relating to estimates of natural variability."</li>
<li>A 2002 paper in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Geophysical_Research" title="Journal of Geophysical Research">Journal of Geophysical Research</a></i> says "Our analysis suggests that the early twentieth century warming can best be explained by a combination of warming due to increases in greenhouse gases and natural forcing, some cooling due to other anthropogenic forcings, and a substantial, but not implausible, contribution from internal variability. In the second half of the century we find that the warming is largely caused by changes in greenhouse gases, with changes in sulphates and, perhaps, volcanic aerosol offsetting approximately one third of the warming."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 2005 review of detection and attribution studies by the International <a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">Ad Hoc</a> Detection and Attribution Group<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> found that "natural drivers such as solar variability and volcanic activity are at most partially responsible for the large-scale temperature changes observed over the past century, and that a large fraction of the warming over the last 50 yr can be attributed to greenhouse gas increases. Thus, the recent research supports and strengthens the IPCC Third Assessment Report conclusion that 'most of the global warming over the past 50 years is likely due to the increase in greenhouse gases.'"</li>
<li>Barnett and colleagues (2005) say that the observed warming of the oceans "cannot be explained by natural internal climate variability or solar and volcanic forcing, but is well simulated by two <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenically</a> forced climate models," concluding that "it is of human origin, a conclusion robust to observational sampling and model differences".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
<li>Two papers in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> in August 2005<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> resolve the problem, evident at the time of the TAR, of <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">tropospheric</a> temperature trends (see also the section on <a href='/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#"Fingerprint"_studies' title="Attribution of recent climate change">"fingerprint" studies</a>) . The UAH version of the record contained errors, and there is evidence of spurious cooling trends in the radiosonde record, particularly in the tropics. See <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">satellite temperature measurements</a> for details; and the 2006 US CCSP report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li>Multiple independent reconstructions of the <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record_of_the_past_1000_years" title="Temperature record of the past 1000 years">temperature record of the past 1000 years</a> confirm that the late 20th century is probably the warmest period in that time (see the preceding section -<a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change#Details_on_attribution" title="Attribution of recent climate change">details on attribution</a>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviews_of_scientific_opinion">Reviews of scientific opinion</span></h3>
<ul><li>An essay in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> surveyed 928 <a href="/wiki/Abstract_(summary)" title="Abstract (summary)">abstracts</a> related to climate change, and concluded that most journal reports accepted the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">consensus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> This is discussed further in <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change#Surveys" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific opinion on climate change</a>.</li>
<li>A 2010 paper in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a> found that among a pool of roughly 1,000 researchers who work directly on climate issues and publish the most frequently on the subject, 97% agree that anthropogenic climate change is happening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li>A 2011 paper from <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_University" title="George Mason University">George Mason University</a> published in the <i>International Journal of Public Opinion Research</i>, "The Structure of Scientific Opinion on Climate Change," collected the opinions of scientists in the earth, space, atmospheric, oceanic or hydrological sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change-73">[73]</a></sup> The 489 survey respondents—representing nearly half of all those eligible according to the survey's specific standards – work in academia, government, and industry, and are members of prominent professional organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change-73">[73]</a></sup> The study found that 97% of the 489 scientists surveyed agreed that global temperatures have risen over the past century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change-73">[73]</a></sup> Moreover, 84% agreed that "human-induced greenhouse warming" is now occurring."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change_73-3"><a href="#cite_note-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change-73">[73]</a></sup> Only 5% disagreed with the idea that human activity is a significant cause of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change_73-4"><a href="#cite_note-Structure_of_Scientific_Opinion_on_Climate_Change-73">[73]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>As described above, a small minority of scientists do disagree with the consensus: see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_scientists_opposing_global_warming_consensus" title="List of scientists opposing global warming consensus">list of scientists opposing global warming consensus</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Willie_Soon" title="Willie Soon">Willie Soon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Richard Lindzen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> say that there is insufficient proof for anthropogenic attribution. Generally this position requires new physical mechanisms to explain the observed warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_activity">Solar activity</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Global_surface_temperature_(top,_blue)_and_the_Sun%27s_energy_received_at_the_top_of_Earth%27s_atmosphere_(red,_bottom)._Solar_energy_has_been_measured_by_satellites_since_1978.gif"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="835" data-file-width="800" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Global_surface_temperature_%28top%2C_blue%29_and_the_Sun%27s_energy_received_at_the_top_of_Earth%27s_atmosphere_%28red%2C_bottom%29._Solar_energy_has_been_measured_by_satellites_since_1978.gif/220px-thumbnail.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Global_surface_temperature_%28top%2C_blue%29_and_the_Sun%27s_energy_received_at_the_top_of_Earth%27s_atmosphere_%28red%2C_bottom%29._Solar_energy_has_been_measured_by_satellites_since_1978.gif/330px-thumbnail.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Global_surface_temperature_%28top%2C_blue%29_and_the_Sun%27s_energy_received_at_the_top_of_Earth%27s_atmosphere_%28red%2C_bottom%29._Solar_energy_has_been_measured_by_satellites_since_1978.gif/440px-thumbnail.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Global_surface_temperature_(top,_blue)_and_the_Sun%27s_energy_received_at_the_top_of_Earth%27s_atmosphere_(red,_bottom)._Solar_energy_has_been_measured_by_satellites_since_1978.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar radiation at the top of our atmosphere, and global temperature</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors,_1958-1999_(USGCRP).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="997" data-file-width="907" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png/220px-Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png/330px-Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png/440px-Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors%2C_1958-1999_%28USGCRP%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evidence_of_human-induced_global_warming_-_patterns_of_temperature_change_produced_by_various_atmospheric_factors,_1958-1999_(USGCRP).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modelled simulation of the effect of various factors (including GHGs, Solar irradiance) singly and in combination, showing in particular that solar activity produces a small and nearly uniform warming, unlike what is observed.</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_sunspot_maximum" title="Solar sunspot maximum">Solar sunspot maximum</a> occurs when the magnetic field of the sun collapses and reverse as part of its average 11 year <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a> (22 years for complete North to North restoration).
</p><p>The role of the sun in recent climate change has been looked at by climate scientists. Since 1978, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">output from the Sun</a> has been measured by <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2008_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> significantly more accurately than was previously possible from the surface. These measurements indicate that the Sun's total <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">solar irradiance</a> has not increased since 1978, so the warming during the past 30 years cannot be directly attributed to an increase in total solar energy reaching the Earth (see graph above, left). In the three decades since 1978, the combination of solar and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_activity" title="Volcanic activity">volcanic activity</a> probably had a slight cooling influence on the climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate models have been used to examine the role of the sun in recent climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
Models are unable to reproduce the rapid warming observed in recent decades when they only take into account variations in total solar irradiance and volcanic activity. Models are, however, able to simulate the observed 20th century changes in temperature when they include all of the most important external forcings, including human influences and natural forcings. As has already been stated, Hegerl <i>et al.</i> (2007) concluded that greenhouse gas forcing had "very likely" caused most of the observed global warming since the mid-20th century. In making this conclusion, Hegerl <i>et al.</i> (2007) allowed for the possibility that climate models had been underestimated the effect of solar forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>The role of solar activity in climate change has also been calculated over <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">longer time periods</a> using "proxy" datasets, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_rings" title="Tree rings">tree rings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
Models indicate that solar and volcanic forcings can explain periods of relative warmth and cold between <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">A.D.</a> 1000 and 1900, but human-induced forcings are needed to reproduce the late-20th century warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another line of evidence against the sun having caused recent climate change comes from looking at how temperatures at different levels in the Earth's atmosphere have changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
Models and observations (see figure above, middle) show that greenhouse gas results in warming of the lower atmosphere at the surface (called the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>) but cooling of the upper atmosphere (called the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Depletion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> by chemical <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a> has also resulted in a cooling effect in the stratosphere. If the sun was responsible for observed warming, warming of the troposphere at the surface and warming at the top of the stratosphere would be expected as increase solar activity would replenish ozone and oxides of nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> The stratosphere has a reverse temperature gradient than the troposphere so as the temperature of the troposphere cools with altitude, the stratosphere rises with altitude. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_cells" title="Hadley cells">Hadley cells</a> are the mechanism by which equatorial generated ozone in the tropics (highest area of UV irradiance in the stratosphere) is moved poleward. Global climate models suggest that climate change may widen the Hadley cells and push the jetstream northward thereby expanding the tropics region and resulting in warmer, dryer conditions in those areas overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-consensus_views">Non-consensus views</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg"><img alt="Refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="600" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/220px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/330px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/440px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contribution of natural factors and human activities to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_radiative_forcing_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_radiative_forcing-15">[15]</a></sup> Radiative forcing values are for the year 2005, relative to the pre-industrial era (1750).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_radiative_forcing_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_radiative_forcing-15">[15]</a></sup> The contribution of solar irradiance to radiative forcing is 5% the value of the combined radiative forcing due to increases in the atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_solar_activity_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_solar_activity-85">[85]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">Climate change denial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_atmospheres" title="Extraterrestrial atmospheres">extraterrestrial atmospheres</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habibullo_Abdussamatov" title="Habibullo Abdussamatov">Habibullo Abdussamatov</a> (2004), head of space research at St. Petersburg's <a href="/wiki/Pulkovo_Observatory" title="Pulkovo Observatory">Pulkovo Astronomical Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, has argued that the sun is responsible for recently observed climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">Journalists</a> for <a href="/wiki/News" title="News">news</a> sources <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada.com" title="Canada.com">canada.com</a> (Solomon, 2007b),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geographic_News" title="National Geographic News">National Geographic News</a> (Ravillious, 2007),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-88">[88]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LiveScience" title="LiveScience">LiveScience</a> (Than, 2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-than_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-than-89">[89]</a></sup> reported on the story of warming on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. In these articles, Abdussamatov was quoted. He stated that warming on Mars was evidence that global warming on Earth was being caused by changes in the sun.
</p><p>Ravillious (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-88">[88]</a></sup> quoted two scientists who disagreed with Abdussamatov: Amato Evan, a climate scientist at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin-Madison" title="University of Wisconsin-Madison">University of Wisconsin-Madison</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a>, and Colin Wilson, a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary physicist</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>. According to Wilson, "Wobbles in the orbit of Mars are the main cause of its climate change in the current era" (see also <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Than (2007) quoted Charles Long, a climate physicist at <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest_National_Laboratory" title="Pacific Northwest National Laboratory">Pacific Northwest National Laboratories</a> in the US, who disagreed with Abdussamatov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-than_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-than-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Than (2007) pointed to the view of <a href="/wiki/Benny_Peiser" title="Benny Peiser">Benny Peiser</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_anthropologist" title="Social anthropologist">social anthropologist</a> at <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_John_Moores_University" title="Liverpool John Moores University">Liverpool John Moores University</a> in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-than_89-2"><a href="#cite_note-than-89">[89]</a></sup> In his <a href="/wiki/Newsletter" title="Newsletter">newsletter</a>, Peiser had cited a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> that had commented on warming observed on several planetary bodies in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">Solar system</a>. These included <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>'s moon <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> and Mars. In an e-mail interview with Than (2007), Peiser stated that:</p><blockquote><p>"I think it is an intriguing coincidence that warming trends have been observed on a number of very diverse planetary bodies in our solar system, (...) Perhaps this is just a fluke."</p></blockquote><p>Than (2007) provided alternative explanations of why warming had occurred on Triton, Pluto, Jupiter and Mars.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="US Environmental Protection Agency">US Environmental Protection Agency</a> (US EPA, 2009) responded to public comments on climate change attribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_solar_activity_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa_solar_activity-85">[85]</a></sup> A number of commenters had argued that recent climate change could be attributed to changes in solar irradiance. According to the US EPA (2009), this attribution was not supported by the bulk of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_literature" title="Scientific literature">scientific literature</a>. Citing the work of the IPCC (2007), the US EPA pointed to the low contribution of solar irradiance to radiative forcing since the start of the Industrial Revolution in 1750. Over this time period (1750 to 2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> the estimated contribution of solar irradiance to radiative forcing was 5% the value of the combined radiative forcing due to increases in the atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide (see graph opposite).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_cosmic_rays">Effect of cosmic rays</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henrik_Svensmark" title="Henrik Svensmark">Henrik Svensmark</a> has suggested that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a> activity of the sun deflects <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>, and that this may influence the generation of <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a>, and thereby have an effect on the climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The website <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ScienceDaily" title="ScienceDaily">ScienceDaily</a> reported on a 2009 study that looked at how past changes in climate have been affected by the Earth's magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> Geophysicist Mads Faurschou Knudsen, who co-authored the study, stated that the study's results supported Svensmark's theory. The authors of the study also acknowledged that CO<sub>2</sub> plays an important role in climate change.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus_view_on_cosmic_rays">Consensus view on cosmic rays</span></h4><p>
The view that cosmic rays could provide the mechanism by which changes in solar activity affect climate is not supported by the literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_cosmic_rays_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa_cosmic_rays-97">[97]</a></sup> Solomon <i>et al.</i> (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> state:</p><blockquote><p>[..] the cosmic ray time series does not appear to correspond to global total cloud cover after 1991 or to global low-level cloud cover after 1994. Together with the lack of a proven physical mechanism and the plausibility of other causal factors affecting changes in cloud cover, this makes the association between galactic cosmic ray-induced changes in aerosol and cloud formation controversial</p></blockquote>
<p>Studies by Lockwood and Fröhlich (2007)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> and Sloan and Wolfendale (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> found no relation between warming in recent decades and cosmic rays. Pierce and Adams (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pierce_adams_2009_cosmic_rays_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-pierce_adams_2009_cosmic_rays-101">[101]</a></sup> used a model to simulate the effect of cosmic rays on cloud properties. They concluded that the hypothesized effect of cosmic rays was too small to explain recent climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pierce_adams_2009_cosmic_rays_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-pierce_adams_2009_cosmic_rays-101">[101]</a></sup> Pierce and Adams (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> noted that their findings did not rule out a possible connection between cosmic rays and climate change, and recommended further research.
</p><p>
Erlykin <i>et al.</i> (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> found that the evidence showed that connections between solar variation and climate were more likely to be mediated by direct variation of insolation rather than cosmic rays, and concluded: "Hence within our assumptions, the effect of varying solar activity, either by direct solar irradiance or by varying cosmic ray rates, must be less than 0.07 °C since 1956, i.e. less than 14% of the observed global warming." Carslaw (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> and Pittock (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> review the recent and historical literature in this field and continue to find that the link between cosmic rays and climate is tenuous, though they encourage continued research. US EPA (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_cosmic_rays_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa_cosmic_rays-97">[97]</a></sup> commented on research by Duplissy <i>et al.</i> (2009):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p><blockquote><p>The CLOUD experiments at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> are interesting research but do not provide conclusive evidence that cosmic rays can serve as a major source of cloud seeding. Preliminary results from the experiment (Duplissy et al., 2009) suggest that though there was some evidence of ion mediated nucleation, for most of the nucleation events observed the contribution of ion processes appeared to be minor. These experiments also showed the difficulty in maintaining sufficiently clean conditions and stable temperatures to prevent spurious aerosol bursts. There is no indication that the earlier Svensmark experiments could even have matched the controlled conditions of the CERN experiment. We find that the Svensmark results on cloud seeding have not yet been shown to be robust or sufficient to materially alter the conclusions of the assessment literature, especially given the abundance of recent literature that is skeptical of the cosmic ray-climate linkage</p></blockquote>
