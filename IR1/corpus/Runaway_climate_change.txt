<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Runaway climate change</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="540" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg/220px-2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg/330px-2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg/440px-2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2007_Arctic_Sea_Ice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The record-breaking decline of <a href="/wiki/Arctic_ice_pack" title="Arctic ice pack">Arctic Sea ice</a> has been reported as a "tipping point"</div></div></div>
<p><b>Runaway climate change</b> or <b>runaway global warming</b> is hypothesized to follow a <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping point</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_system" title="Climate system">climate system</a>, after accumulated <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> initiates a reinforcing <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>. This rapid acceleration in climate change may lead to potentially irreversible damage to the climate system, making timely mitigation efforts unfeasible. This is thought to cause the climate to rapidly change until it reaches a new stable condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown06_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown06-1">[1]</a></sup>  These phrases may be used with reference to concerns about rapid <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown06_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown06-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monbiot_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monbiot-2">[2]</a></sup> Some astronomers use the expression runaway <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> to describe a situation where the climate deviates catastrophically and permanently from the original state—as happened on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although these terms are rarely used in the peer-reviewed climatological literature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi10.1146/annurev.energy.32.041706.124700_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1146/annurev.energy.32.041706.124700-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BA05_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BA05-6">[6]</a></sup> that literature does use the similar phrase "<a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a>", which refers specifically to climate changes that cause a planetary body's water to boil off.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms">Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>At a <i>tipping level</i> or <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)"><i>tipping point</i></a> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a> reaches a point such that no additional forcing is required for large climate change and impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_1208_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_1208-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>At a <i>point of no return</i>, climate impacts that are irreversible on a practical time scale occur. An example of such an impact is the disintegration of a large ice sheet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_1208_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_1208-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Runaway_greenhouse_effect">Runaway greenhouse effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Runaway greenhouse effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">Runaway greenhouse effect</a></div>
<p>The <i>runaway greenhouse effect</i> has several meanings. At the least extreme, this implies global warming sufficient to induce out-of-control amplifying feedbacks, such as ice sheet disintegration and melting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">methane hydrates</a>. At the most extreme, a Venus-like planet with crustal carbon baked into the atmosphere and a surface temperature of several hundred degrees, an irreversible <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a>.
</p><p>Between these two is the <i>moist greenhouse</i>, which occurs if the climate forcing is large enough to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O) a major <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmospheric constituent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In principle, an extreme moist greenhouse might cause an instability with water vapour preventing radiation to space of all absorbed solar energy, resulting in very high surface temperature and evaporation of the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, simulations indicate that no plausible human-made <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> (GHG) forcing can cause an instability and baked-crust runaway greenhouse effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Conceivable levels of human-made climate forcing could yield the<i> low-end</i> runaway greenhouse. A forcing of 12–16 W m<sup>−2</sup> would require <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) levels to increase 8–16 times. If the forcing were due only to CO<sub>2</sub> change, this would raise the global mean temperature by 16–24 °C with much larger polar warming. A warming of 16–24 °C produces a moderately moist greenhouse, with water vapour increasing to about 1% of the atmosphere's mass, thus increasing the rate of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> escape to space. If such a forcing were entirely due to CO<sub>2</sub>, the weathering process would remove the excess atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> on a time scale of 10<sup>4</sup>–10<sup>5</sup> years, well before the ocean was significantly depleted. Venus-like conditions on the Earth require a large long-term forcing that is unlikely to occur until the sun brightens by a few tens of percents, which will take a few billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_habitability">Global habitability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global habitability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burning all fossil fuels would adversely affect the ability of humans to live on the planet. If non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N2O" title="N2O">N<sub>2</sub>O</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) were to increase with global warming at the same rate as in the palaeoclimate record and atmospheric chemistry simulations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> they would provide approximately 25% of the greenhouse forcing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What is the other 75%? The initial assumption does not state anything about increasing CO2 levels. (March 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The remaining forcing requires approximately 4.8 times current CO<sub>2</sub> levels, corresponding to fossil fuel emissions as much as approximately 10,000 Gt C for a conservative assumption of a CO<sub>2</sub> airborne fraction averaging one-third over the 1000 years following a peak emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calculated global warming in this case is 16 °C, with warming at the poles approximately 30 °C. Calculated warming over land areas averages approximately 20 °C. Such temperatures would eliminate grain production in almost all agricultural regions in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Increased stratospheric water vapour would diminish the stratospheric ozone layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Global warming of that magnitude would make most of the planet uninhabitable by humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood_2010-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The human body generates about 100 W of metabolic heat that must be carried away to maintain a core body temperature near 37 °C, which implies that sustained <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet bulb temperatures</a> above 35 °C can result in lethal hyperthermia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_2010_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood_2010-16">[16]</a></sup> Today, the summer temperature varies widely over the Earth's surface, but wet bulb temperature is more narrowly confined by the effect of humidity, with the most common value of approximately 26–27 °C and the highest approximately of 31 °C. A warming of 10–12 °C would put most of today's world population in regions with a wetbulb temperature above 35 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood_2010_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood_2010-16">[16]</a></sup> Given the 20 °C warming that occurs with 4.8 times current CO<sub>2</sub> levels, such a climate forcing would produce intolerable climatic conditions even if the true climate sensitivity is significantly less than the Russell sensitivity,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What is the Russell sensitivity? (March 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or, if the Russell sensitivity is accurate, the CO<sub>2</sub> forcing required to produce intolerable conditions for humans is less than this amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedback_effects">Feedback effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feedback effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">Climate change feedback</a></div>
<p>The core of the concept of runaway climate change is the idea of a large positive feedback within the climate system. When a change in global temperature causes an event to occur which itself changes global temperature, this is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> effect. If this effect acts in the same direction as the original temperature change, it is a destabilising <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> (e.g. warming causing more warming); and if in the opposite direction, it is a stabilising <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> (e.g. warming causing a cooling effect). If a sufficiently strong net positive feedback occurs, it is said that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_tipping_point" title="Climate tipping point">climate tipping point</a> has been passed and the temperature will continue to change until the changed conditions result in negative feedbacks that restabilise the climate on a new, stable equilibrium manifold.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>An example of a negative feedback is that radiation leaving the Earth increases in proportion to the fourth <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">power</a> of temperature, in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a>. This feedback is always operational; therefore, while it may be overridden by positive feedbacks for comparatively small temperature changes it will dominate for larger temperature changes. An example of a positive feedback is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>, in which increasing temperature causes ice to melt, which increases the amount of heat that Earth absorbs. This feedback only operates in a restricted range of temperatures (those for which ice exists, and does not cover the whole surface; once all the ice has melted, the feedback ceases to operate).
</p><p>Climate feedback effects can involve positive feedback in a type of forcing, such as the release of methane due to rising <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">methane levels</a>; other greenhouse gases, as when CO<sub>2</sub> causes the release of methane; or other variables, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>.
</p><p>Without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_feedback" title="Climate feedback">climate feedbacks</a>, a doubling in atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration would result in a global average temperature increase of around 1.2 °C. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback" title="Water vapor feedback">Water vapor</a> amount and <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> are probably the most important global climate feedbacks. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palaeoclimatology" title="Palaeoclimatology">Historical information</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a> indicate a <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a> of 1.5 to 4.5 °C, with a best estimate of 3 °C. This is an amplification of the carbon dioxide forcing by a factor of 2.5. Some studies suggest a lower climate sensitivity, but other studies indicate a sensitivity above this range. Partly because of the difficulty in modeling the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_feedback" title="Cloud feedback">cloud feedback</a>, the true climate sensitivity remains uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slow">Slow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Slow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slow feedback effects—especially changes in the sizes of ice sheets and levels of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>—amplify the sensitivity of the total Earth system by an amount that depends on the time scale considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are known examples of the Earth's climate producing a large response to small forcings. The CO<sub>2</sub> feedback effect is believed to be part of the transition between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">glacial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> periods, with <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> providing the initial trigger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2006 book chapter by Cox et al. considers the possibility of a future runaway climate feedback due to changes in the land <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>Here we use a simple land carbon balance model to analyse the conditions required for a land sink-to-source transition, and address the question; could the land carbon cycle lead to a runaway climate feedback? [...] The simple land carbon balance model has effective parameters representing the sensitivities of climate and <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> to CO<sub>2</sub>, and the sensitivities of <a href="/wiki/Soil_respiration" title="Soil respiration">soil respiration</a> and photosynthesis to temperature. This model is used to show that (a) a carbon sink-to-source transition is inevitable beyond some finite critical CO<sub>2</sub> concentration provided a few simple conditions are satisfied, (b) the value of the critical CO<sub>2</sub> concentration is poorly known due to uncertainties in land carbon cycle parameters and especially in the climate sensitivity to CO<sub>2</sub>, and (c) that a true runaway land carbon-climate feedback (or linear instability) in the future is unlikely given that the land masses are currently acting as a carbon sink.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fast">Fast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general, fast feedback climate sensitivity depends on the initial climate state. Fast feedback effects include changes in levels of water vapour and aerosols, as well as changes in cloud cover and the extent of sea ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_deposits_and_clathrates">Methane deposits and clathrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Methane deposits and clathrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Potentially unstable methane deposits exists in <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> regions, which are expected to retreat as a result of global warming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrates" title="Clathrates">clathrates</a>, with the clathrate effect probably taking millennia to fully act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MInv04_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MInv04-22">[22]</a></sup> The potential role of methane from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrates</a> in near-future runaway scenarios is not certain, as studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OceanEsc06_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-OceanEsc06-23">[23]</a></sup> show a slow release of methane, which may not be regarded as 'runaway' by all commentators. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun" title="Clathrate gun">clathrate gun</a> runaway effect may be used to describe more rapid methane releases. Methane in the atmosphere has a high <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a>, but breaks down relatively quickly to form CO<sub>2</sub>, which is also a greenhouse gas. Therefore, slow methane release will have the long-term effect of adding CO<sub>2</sub> to the atmosphere.
</p><p>In order to model clathrates and other reservoirs of greenhouse gases and their precursors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a> would have to be 'coupled' to a carbon cycle model. Most current global climate models do not include modelling of methane deposits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_risk">Current risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific consensus in the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> is that "Anthropogenic warming could lead to some effects that are abrupt or irreversible, depending upon the rate and magnitude of the climate change." Note however that this statement is about situations weaker than "runaway change".  Text prepared for the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> states that "a 'runaway greenhouse effect'—analogous to Venus—appears to have virtually no chance of being induced by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> activities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Estimates of the size of the total carbon reservoir in Arctic <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrates" title="Clathrates">clathrates</a> vary widely. It is suggested that at least 900 gigatonnes of carbon in permafrost exists worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Furthermore, there are believed to be another 400 gigatonnes of carbon in methane clathrates in permafrost regions <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac90_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mac90-27">[27]</a></sup> with 10,000 to 11,000 gigatonnes worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac90_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mac90-27">[27]</a></sup> This is large enough that if 10% of the stored methane were released, it would have an effect equivalent to a factor of 10 increase in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer2007_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Archer2007-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a> is a potent <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> with a higher <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> than CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Worries about the release of this methane and carbon dioxide is linked to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" title="Arctic shrinkage">arctic shrinkage</a>. Recent years have seen record low Arctic sea ice. It has been suggested that rapid melting of the sea ice may initiate a feedback loop that rapidly melts arctic <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_01_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_01-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Methane clathrates on the sea-floor have also been predicted to destabilise, but much more slowly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mac90_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mac90-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>A release of methane from clathrates, however, is believed to be slow and chronic rather than catastrophic and that 21st-century effects of such a release are therefore likely to be 'significant but not catastrophic'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer2007_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Archer2007-28">[28]</a></sup> It is further noted that 'much methane from dissociated gas hydrate may never reach the atmosphere',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden-31">[31]</a></sup> as it can be dissolved into the ocean and be broken down biologically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden-31">[31]</a></sup> Other research<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> demonstrates that a release to the atmosphere can occur during large releases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These sources suggest that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun" title="Clathrate gun">clathrate gun</a> effect alone will not be sufficient to cause 'catastrophic'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Archer2007_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Archer2007-28">[28]</a></sup> climate change within a human lifetime.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">Hansen</a> et al. 2013 suggests that the Earth could become in large parts uninhabitable and note that this may not even require burning of all fossil fuels, because of higher climate sensitivity (3–4 °C or 5.4–7.2 °F) based on a 550 ppm scenario. Burning all fossil fuels would warm land areas on average about 20 °C (36 °F) and warm the poles 30 °C (54 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_et_al_2013_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_et_al_2013-10">[10]</a></sup> Earlier estimates were based on the assumption that fossil-fuel use would continue until reserves were exhausted, and predicted a <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a>, a climate similar to that on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Ongoing research determines if such a <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a> is possible on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Paleoclimatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</a></div>
<p>Events that could be described as runaway climate change may have occurred in the past.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clathrate_gun">Clathrate gun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Clathrate gun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun" title="Clathrate gun">Clathrate gun</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun" title="Clathrate gun">clathrate gun</a> hypothesis suggests an <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a> due to a massive release of methane gas from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_clathrates" title="Methane clathrates">methane clathrates</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafloor" title="Seafloor">seafloor</a>. It has been speculated that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic extinction event</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> were caused by massive clathrate release.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowball_Earth">Snowball Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Snowball Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowball_earth" title="Snowball earth">Snowball earth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">Ice-albedo feedback</a></div>
<p>Geological evidence shows that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a> caused sea ice advance to near the equator at several points in Earth history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoffman_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman-39">[39]</a></sup> Modeling work shows that such an event would indeed be a result of a self-sustaining ice-albedo effect,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budyko_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Budyko-40">[40]</a></sup> and that such a condition could be escaped via the accumulation of CO<sub>2</sub> from volcanic outgassing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirschvink_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirschvink-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
