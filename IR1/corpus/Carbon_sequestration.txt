<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon sequestration</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="50" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/50px-Mergefrom.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/75px-Mergefrom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Mergefrom.svg/100px-Mergefrom.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">It has been suggested that <i><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">Carbon dioxide removal</a></i> be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Merging" title="Wikipedia:Merging">merged</a> into this article. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="530" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/220px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/330px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg/440px-Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_sequestration-2009-10-07.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic showing both terrestrial and geological sequestration of carbon dioxide emissions from a coal-fired plant.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon sequestration</b> is the process involved in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon capture</a> and the long-term storage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide" title="Atmospheric carbon dioxide">atmospheric carbon dioxide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SedjoSohngen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SedjoSohngen-1">[1]</a></sup> or other forms of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigate or defer global warming</a>. It has been proposed as a way to slow the atmospheric and marine accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, which are released by burning <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodrien_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hodrien-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) is naturally captured from the atmosphere through biological, chemical, and physical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> Artificial processes have been devised to produce similar effects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> including large-scale, artificial capture and sequestration of industrially produced <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> using subsurface <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, reservoirs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_water" title="Ocean water">ocean water</a>, aging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon sequestration is the process involved in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon capture</a> and the long-term storage of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SedjoSohngen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SedjoSohngen-1">[1]</a></sup> and may refer specifically to:
</p>
<ul><li>"The process of removing carbon from the atmosphere and depositing it in a reservoir."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  When carried out deliberately, this may also be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a>, which is a form of <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a>, where carbon dioxide is removed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gases" title="Flue gases">flue gases</a> (e.g., at <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>) before being stored in underground reservoirs.</li>
<li>Natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycling" title="Biogeochemical cycling">biogeochemical cycling</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and reservoirs, such as by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">chemical weathering</a> of rocks.</li></ul>
<p>Carbon dioxide may be captured as a pure by-product in processes related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleum_refining" title="Petroleum refining">petroleum refining</a> or from flue gases from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_generation" title="Power generation">power generation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> sequestration includes the storage part of carbon capture and storage, which refers to large-scale, artificial capture and sequestration of industrially produced <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> using subsurface <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquifers" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a>, reservoirs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_water" title="Ocean water">ocean water</a>, aging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>.
</p><p>Carbon sequestration describes long-term storage of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> or other forms of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigate or defer global warming</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">avoid dangerous climate change</a>. It has been proposed as a way to slow the atmospheric and marine accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, which are released by burning <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodrien_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hodrien-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide is naturally captured from the atmosphere through biological, chemical or physical processes. Some artificial sequestration techniques exploit these natural processes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossary_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-3">[3]</a></sup> while some use entirely artificial processes.
</p><p>There are three ways that this sequestration can be carried out; post-combustion capture, pre-combustion capture, and oxy-combustion. A wide variety of separation techniques is being pursued, including gas phase separation, absorption into a liquid, and adsorption on a solid, as well as hybrid processes, such as adsorption/membrane systems. These above processes basically will capture carbon emitting from power plants, factories, fuel burning industries and so on.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_processes">Biological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2600" data-file-width="2000" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg/170px-Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg/255px-Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg/340px-Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phytoplankton_SoAtlantic_20060215.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An oceanic <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">phytoplankton bloom</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Ocean" title="South Atlantic Ocean">South Atlantic Ocean</a>, off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Encouraging such blooms with iron fertilization could lock up carbon on the seabed.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">Biosequestration</a> or carbon sequestration through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_processes" title="Biological processes">biological processes</a> affects the global <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>. Examples include major climatic fluctuations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Azolla_event" title="Azolla event">Azolla event</a>, which created the current <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> climate. Such processes created <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. By manipulating such processes, geoengineers seek to enhance sequestration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_production">Peat production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peat production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_bogs" title="Peat bogs">Peat bogs</a> are a very important carbon store. Peat bogs act as a sink for carbon due to the accumulation of partially decayed biomass that would otherwise continue to decay completely. There is a variance on how much the peatlands act as a carbon sink or carbon source that can be linked to varying climates in different areas of the world and different times of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  By creating new bogs, or enhancing existing ones, the amount of carbon that is sequestered by bogs would increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry">Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">Reforestation</a> is the replanting of trees on marginal crop and <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> lands to incorporate carbon from atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> into <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For this process to succeed the carbon must not return to the atmosphere from mass burning or rotting when the trees die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To this end, land allotted to the trees must not be converted to other uses and management of the frequency of disturbances might be necessary in order to avoid extreme events. Alternatively, the wood from them must itself be sequestered, e.g., via <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, bio-energy with carbon storage (<a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">BECS</a>), landfill or 'stored' by use in e.g. construction.  Short of growth in perpetuity, however, reforestation with long-lived trees (&gt;100 years) will sequester carbon for a more graduated release, minimizing impact during the expected <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbon crisis (page does not exist)">carbon crisis</a> of the 21st century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_Forestry">Urban Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Urban Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_Forestry" title="Urban Forestry">Urban Forestry</a> increases the amount of carbon taken up in cities by adding new tree sites and the sequestration of carbon occurs over the lifetime of the tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is generally practiced and maintained on smaller scales, like in cities.  The results of urban forestry can have different results depending on the type of vegetation that is being used, so it can function as a sink but can also function as a source of emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urb_For_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Urb_For-11">[11]</a></sup> Along with sequestration by the plants which is difficult to measure but seems to have little effect on the overall amount of carbon dioxide that is uptaken, the vegetation can have indirect effects on carbon by reducing need for energy consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urb_For_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Urb_For-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wetland_restoration">Wetland restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wetland restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wetland soil is an important carbon sink; 14.5% of the world’s <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a> is found in wetlands, while only 6% of the world’s land is composed of wetlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nelson_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nelson-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_farming" title="Carbon farming">Carbon farming</a></div>
<p>Compared to natural vegetation, cropland soils are depleted in soil organic carbon (SOC). When a soil is converted from natural land or semi natural land, such as forests, woodlands, grasslands, steppes and savannas, the SOC content in the soil reduces with about 30–40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">[13]</a></sup> This loss is due to the removal of plant material containing carbon, in terms of harvests. When the land use changes, the carbon in the soil will either increase or decrease, this change will continue until the soil reaches a new equilibrium. Deviations from this equilibrium can also be affected by variated climate .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The decreasing of SOC content can be counteracted by increasing the carbon input, this can be done with several strategies, e.g. leave harvest residues on the field, use manure as fertiliser or include perennial crops in the rotation. Perennial crops have larger below ground biomass fraction, which increases the SOC content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">[13]</a></sup> Globally, soils are estimated to contain approximately 1,500 gigatons of organic carbon to 1 m depth, more than the amount in vegetation and the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modification of agricultural practices is a recognized method of carbon sequestration as soil can act as an effective carbon sink offsetting as much as 20% of 2010 carbon dioxide emissions annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No-till" title="No-till">No-till</a>)
</p><p>Carbon emission reduction methods in agriculture can be grouped into two categories: reducing and/or displacing emissions and enhancing carbon removal. Some of these reductions involve increasing the efficiency of farm operations (e.g. more fuel-efficient equipment) while some involve interruptions in the natural carbon cycle. Also, some effective techniques (such as the elimination of <a href="/wiki/Stubble_burning" title="Stubble burning">stubble burning</a>) can negatively impact other environmental concerns (increased herbicide use to control weeds not destroyed by burning).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_Soil">Deep Soil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deep Soil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soils hold four times the amount of carbon stored in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> About half of this is found deep within soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> About 90% of this deep soil C is stabilized by mineral-organic associations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_emissions">Reducing emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reducing emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increasing yields and efficiency generally reduces emissions as well, since more food results from the same or less effort. Techniques include more accurate use of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, less soil disturbance, better <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, and crop strains bred for locally beneficial traits and increased yields.
</p><p>Replacing more energy <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a> operations can also reduce emissions. Reduced or <a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">no-till farming</a> requires less machine use and burns correspondingly less fuel per acre. However, no-till usually increases use of weed-control chemicals and the residue now left on the soil surface is more likely to release its <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> to the atmosphere as it decays, reducing the net carbon reduction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In practice, most farming operations that incorporate post-harvest crop residues, wastes and byproducts back into the soil provide a carbon storage benefit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This is particularly the case for practices such as field burning of stubble – rather than releasing almost all of the stored <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> to the atmosphere, tillage incorporates the biomass back into the soil.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancing_carbon_removal">Enhancing carbon removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Enhancing carbon removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All crops absorb <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> during growth and release it after harvest. The goal of agricultural carbon removal is to use the crop and its relation to the carbon cycle to permanently sequester carbon within the soil. This is done by selecting farming methods that return biomass to the soil and enhance the conditions in which the carbon within the plants will be reduced to its elemental nature and stored in a stable state. Methods for accomplishing this include:
</p>
<ul><li>Use <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">cover crops</a> such as grasses and weeds as temporary cover between planting seasons</li>
<li>Concentrate livestock in small paddocks for days at a time so they graze lightly but evenly. This encourages roots to grow deeper into the soil. Stock also till the soil with their hooves, grinding old grass and manures into the soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-australia-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cover bare paddocks with hay or dead vegetation. This protects soil from the sun and allows the soil to hold more water and be more attractive to carbon-capturing microbes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-australia-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Restore degraded land, which slows carbon release while returning the land to agriculture or other use.</li></ul>
<p>Agricultural sequestration practices may have positive effects on <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, air, and water quality, be beneficial to <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>, and expand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a>. On degraded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croplands" title="Croplands">croplands</a>, an increase of 1 ton of soil carbon pool may increase crop yield by 20 to 40 kilograms per hectare of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, 10 to 20 kg/ ha for maize, and 0.5 to 1 kg/ha for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowpeas" title="Cowpeas">cowpeas</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The effects of soil sequestration can be reversed. If the soil is disrupted or tillage practices are abandoned, the soil becomes a net source of greenhouse gases. Typically after 15 to 30 years of sequestration, soil becomes saturated and ceases to absorb carbon. This implies that there is a global limit to the amount of carbon that soil can hold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many factors affect the costs of carbon sequestration including soil quality, transaction costs and various externalities such as leakage and unforeseen environmental damage. Because reduction of atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is a long-term concern, farmers can be reluctant to adopt more expensive agricultural techniques when there is not a clear crop, soil, or economic benefit. Governments such as Australia and New Zealand are considering allowing farmers to sell carbon credits once they document that they have sufficiently increased soil carbon content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-australia_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-australia-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean-related">Ocean-related</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ocean-related">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_fertilization">Iron fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iron fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">Iron fertilization</a></div>
<p>Ocean iron fertilization is an example of such a geoengineering technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel.de_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel.de-28">[28]</a></sup> Iron fertilization<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> attempts to encourage <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">phytoplankton growth</a>, which removes carbon from the atmosphere for at least a period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This technique is controversial due to limited understanding of its complete effects on the marine <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> including side effects and possibly large deviations from expected behavior. Such effects potentially include release of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alertnet.org_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-alertnet.org-33">[33]</a></sup> and disruption of the ocean's nutrient balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel.de_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel.de-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural iron fertilisation events (e.g., deposition of iron-rich dust into ocean waters) can enhance carbon sequestration. Sperm whales act as agents of iron fertilisation when they transport iron from the deep ocean to the surface during prey consumption and defecation. Sperm whales have been shown to increase the levels of primary production and carbon export to the deep ocean by depositing iron rich feces into surface waters of the Southern Ocean. The iron rich feces causes phytoplankton to grow and take up more carbon from the atmosphere. When the phytoplankton dies, some of it sinks to the deep ocean and takes the atmospheric carbon with it. By reducing the abundance of sperm whales in the Southern Ocean, whaling has resulted in an extra 200,000 tonnes of carbon remaining in the atmosphere each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Urea_fertilization">Urea fertilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Urea fertilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_nourishment" title="Ocean nourishment">Ocean nourishment</a></div>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Jones_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ian Jones (scientist) (page does not exist)">Ian Jones</a> proposes fertilizing the ocean with <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> rich substance, to encourage <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Australian company Ocean Nourishment Corporation (ONC) plans to sink hundreds of tonnes of urea into the ocean to boost <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>-absorbing phytoplankton growth as a way to combat climate change. In 2007, Sydney-based ONC completed an experiment involving 1 tonne of nitrogen in the Sulu Sea off the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixing_layers">Mixing layers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mixing layers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Encouraging various ocean layers to mix can move nutrients and dissolved gases around, offering avenues for <a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">geoengineering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovelock07_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lovelock07-37">[37]</a></sup> Mixing may be achieved by placing large vertical pipes in the oceans to pump nutrient rich water to the surface, triggering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algal_blooms" title="Algal blooms">blooms of algae</a>, which store carbon when they grow and export carbon when they die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovelock07_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lovelock07-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This produces results somewhat similar to iron fertilization. One side-effect is a short-term rise in <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, which limits its attractiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seaweed">Seaweed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Seaweed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">Seaweed</a> grows very fast and can theoretically be harvested and processed to generate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomethane" title="Biomethane">biomethane</a>, via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_Digestion" title="Anaerobic Digestion">Anaerobic Digestion</a> to generate electricity, via <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Cogeneration/CHP</a> or as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. One study suggested that if seaweed farms covered 9% of the ocean they could produce enough biomethane to supply Earth's equivalent demand for fossil fuel energy, remove 53 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigatonnes" title="Gigatonnes">gigatonnes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> per year from the atmosphere and sustainably produce 200 kg per year of fish, per person, for 10 billion people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Ideal species for such farming and conversion include <i><a href="/wiki/Laminaria_digitata" title="Laminaria digitata">Laminaria digitata</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fucus_serratus" title="Fucus serratus">Fucus serratus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharina_latissima" title="Saccharina latissima">Saccharina latissima</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass-related">Biomass-related</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biomass-related">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage_.28BECCS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage_(BECCS)">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>BECCS refers to biomass in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations">power stations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boilers" title="Boilers">boilers</a> that use carbon capture and storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The carbon sequestered by the biomass would be captured and stored, thus removing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>This technology is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon storage</a>, BECS, though this term can also refer to the carbon sequestration potential in other technologies, such as biochar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial">Burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Burying biomass (such as trees)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> directly, mimics the natural processes that created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">Landfills</a> also represent a physical method of sequestration.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochar_burial">Biochar burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Biochar burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">Biochar</a> is <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> waste. The resulting material is added to a <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> or used as a soil improver to create <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Addition of pyrogenic organic carbon (biochar) is a novel strategy to increase the soil-C stock for the long-term and to mitigate global-warming by offsetting the atmospheric C (up to 9.5 Pg C annually).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the soil, the carbon is unavailable for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> to <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and consequential atmospheric release. This is one technique advocated by <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a>, creator of the <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia hypothesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> According to Simon Shackley, "people are talking more about something in the range of one to two billion tonnes a year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mechanisms related to biochar are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon storage</a>, BECS.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_storage">Ocean storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ocean storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If CO<sub>2</sub> were to be injected to the ocean bottom, the pressures would be great enough for CO<sub>2</sub> to be in its liquid phase.  The idea behind ocean injection would be to have stable, stationary pools of CO<sub>2</sub> at the ocean floor.  The ocean could potentially hold over a thousand billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub>. However, this avenue of sequestration isn’t being as actively pursued because of concerns about the impact on ocean life, and concerns about its stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>River mouths bring large quantities of nutrients and dead material from upriver into the ocean as part of the process that eventually produces fossil fuels. Transporting material such as crop waste out to sea and allowing it to sink exploits this idea to increase carbon storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> International regulations on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_dumping" title="Marine dumping">marine dumping</a> may restrict or prevent use of this technique.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_sequestration">Geological sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Geological sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>Geological sequestration refers to the storage of CO<sub>2</sub> underground in depleted oil and gas reservoirs, saline formations, or deep, un-minable coal beds.
</p><p>Once CO<sub>2</sub> is captured from a gas or coal-fired power plant, it would be compressed to ≈100 bar so that it would be a supercritical fluid.  In this fluid form, the CO<sub>2</sub> would be easy to transport via pipeline to the place of storage.  The CO<sub>2</sub> would then be injected deep underground, typically around 1 km, where it would be stable for hundreds to millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-53">[53]</a></sup> At these storage conditions, the density of supercritical CO<sub>2</sub> is 600 to 800 kg / m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-55">[55]</a></sup> For consumers, the cost of electricity from a coal-fired power plant with carbon capture and storage (CCS) is estimated to be 0.01–0.05 $ / kWh higher than without CCS. For reference, the average cost of electricity in the US in 2004 was 0.0762 $ / kWh. In other terms, the cost of CCS would be 20–70 $/ton of CO<sub>2</sub> captured. The transportation and injection of CO<sub>2</sub> is relatively cheap, with the capture costs accounting for 70–80% of CCS costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The important parameters in determining a good site for carbon storage are: rock porosity, rock permeability, absence of faults, and geometry of rock layers.  The medium in which the CO<sub>2</sub> is to be stored ideally has a high porosity and permeability, such as sandstone or limestone. Sandstone can have a permeability ranging from 1 to 10<sup>−5</sup> Darcy, and can have a porosity as high as ≈30%. The porous rock must be capped by a layer of low permeability which acts as a seal, or caprock, for the CO<sub>2</sub>.  Shale is an example of a very good caprock, with a permeability of 10<sup>−5</sup> to 10<sup>−9</sup> Darcy. Once injected, the CO<sub>2</sub> plume will rise via buoyant forces, since it is less dense than its surroundings. Once it encounters a caprock, it will spread laterally until it encounters a gap. If there are fault planes near the injection zone, there is a possibility the CO<sub>2</sub> could migrate along the fault to the surface, leaking into the atmosphere, which would be potentially dangerous to life in the surrounding area. Another danger related to carbon sequestration is induced seismicity. If the injection of CO<sub>2</sub> creates pressures that are too high underground, the formation will fracture, causing an earthquake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>While trapped in a rock formation, CO<sub>2</sub> can be in the supercritical fluid phase or dissolve in groundwater/brine.  It can also react with minerals in the geologic formation to precipitate carbonates. See <a href="/wiki/CarbFix" title="CarbFix">CarbFix</a>.
</p><p>Worldwide storage capacity in oil and gas reservoirs is estimated to be 675–900 Gt CO<sub>2</sub>, and in un-minable coal seams is estimated to be 15–200 Gt CO<sub>2</sub>.  Deep saline formations have the largest capacity, which is estimated to be 1,000–10,000 Gt CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-55">[55]</a></sup> In the US, there is an estimated 160 Gt CO<sub>2</sub> storage capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are a number of large-scale carbon capture and sequestration projects that have demonstrated the viability and safety of this method of carbon storage, which are summarized here <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> by the Global CCS Institute. The dominant monitoring technique is seismic imaging, where vibrations are generated that propagate through the subsurface.  The geologic structure can be imaged from the refracted/reflected waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first large-scale <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> sequestration project which began in 1996 is called <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner</a>, and is located in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> where Norway's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StatoilHydro" title="StatoilHydro">StatoilHydro</a> strips carbon dioxide from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> with <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> and disposed of this carbon dioxide in a deep <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>. In 2000, a coal-fueled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_natural_gas" title="Synthetic natural gas">synthetic natural gas</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Beulah,_North_Dakota" title="Beulah, North Dakota">Beulah, North Dakota</a>, became the world's first coal-using plant to capture and store carbon dioxide, at the <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> has been used extensively in enhanced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> recovery operations in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> beginning in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hodrien_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hodrien-2">[2]</a></sup> There are in excess of 10,000 wells that inject <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> alone. The gas comes in part from anthropogenic sources, but is principally from large naturally occurring geologic formations of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. It is transported to the oil-producing fields through a large network of over 5,000 kilometres (3,100 mi) of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> pipelines. The use of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> (EOR) methods in heavy oil reservoirs in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Canadian_Sedimentary_Basin" title="Western Canadian Sedimentary Basin">Western Canadian Sedimentary Basin</a> (WCSB) has also been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> However, transport cost remains an important hurdle. An extensive <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> pipeline system does not yet exist in the WCSB. <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_oil_sands" title="Athabasca oil sands">Athabasca oil sands</a> mining that produces <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is hundreds of kilometers north of the subsurface <a href="/wiki/Heavy_crude_oil" title="Heavy crude oil">Heavy crude oil</a> reservoirs that could most benefit from <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> injection.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_processes">Chemical processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chemical processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Developed in the Netherlands, an electrocatalysis by a copper complex helps <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">reduce carbon dioxide</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouwman_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwman-60">[60]</a></sup> This conversion <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">uses carbon dioxide as a feedstock</a> to generate oxalic acid.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_carbonation">Mineral carbonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Mineral carbonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon, in the form of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> can be removed from the atmosphere by chemical processes, and stored in stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_mineral" title="Carbonate mineral">carbonate mineral</a> forms. This process is known as 'carbon sequestration by mineral <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>' or mineral sequestration. The process involves reacting carbon dioxide with abundantly available metal oxides–either <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> (MgO) or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (CaO)–to form stable carbonates. These reactions are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> and occur naturally (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> of rock over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_time" title="Geologic time">geologic time</a> periods).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herzog_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-herzog-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldberg_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldberg-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>CaO + <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>MgO + <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">MgCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Calcium and magnesium are found in nature typically as calcium and magnesium <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">silicates</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a>) and not as binary oxides. For forsterite and serpentine the reactions are:
</p>

<p>These reactions are slightly more favorable at low temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-herzog_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-herzog-61">[61]</a></sup> This process occurs naturally over geologic time frames and is responsible for much of the Earth's surface <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. The reaction rate can be made faster however, by reacting at higher temperatures and/or pressures, although this method requires some additional energy. Alternatively, the mineral could be milled to increase its surface area, and exposed to water and constant abrasion to remove the inert Silica as could be achieved naturally by dumping Olivine in the high energy surf of beaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Experiments suggest the weathering process is reasonably quick (one year) given porous basaltic rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matter-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> naturally reacts with <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> rock in surface exposures of <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">ophiolites</a>, notably in <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>. It has been suggested that this process can be enhanced to carry out natural <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(geology)" title="Mineralization (geology)">mineralisation</a> of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>When CO2 is dissolved in water and injected into hot basaltic rocks underground it has been shown that the CO2 reacts with the basalt to form solid carbonate minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> A test plant in Iceland started up in October 2017, extracting up to 50 tons of CO2 a year from the atmosphere and storing it underground in basaltic rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers from <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, developed a low cost process for the production of <a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a>, which can sequester CO2 from the air, or at the point of air pollution, eg. at a power plant. The crystals are naturally occurring, but accumulation is usually very slow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_use">Industrial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Industrial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditional cement manufacture releases large amounts of carbon dioxide, but newly developed cement types from Novacem<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> can absorb <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> from ambient air during hardening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> A similar technique was pioneered by TecEco, which has been producing "EcoCement" since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A Canadian startup CarbonCure takes captured CO2 and injects it into concrete as it's being mixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Estonia, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> <a href="/wiki/Bottom_ash" title="Bottom ash">ash</a>, generated by power stations could be used as <a href="/wiki/Sorbent" title="Sorbent">sorbents</a> for <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> mineral sequestration. The amount of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> captured averaged 60 to 65% of the carbonaceous <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and 10 to 11% of the total <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uibu_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-uibu-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_scrubbers">Chemical scrubbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Chemical scrubbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_air_capture" title="Carbon air capture">Carbon air capture</a></div>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon dioxide scrubbing</a> processes have been proposed to remove <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> from the air, usually using a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">Kraft process</a>. Carbon dioxide scrubbing variants exist based on <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a>, which can be used to create liquid fuels, or on <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> These notably include artificial trees proposed by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Lackner" title="Klaus Lackner">Klaus Lackner</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> using chemical <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean-related_2">Ocean-related</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Ocean-related">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Basalt_storage">Basalt storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Basalt storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Carbon dioxide sequestration in <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> involves the injecting of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> into deep-sea formations. The <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> first mixes with seawater and then reacts with the basalt, both of which are alkaline-rich elements. This reaction results in the release of <span class="chemf nowrap">Ca<sup>2+</sup></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">Mg<sup>2+</sup></span> ions forming stable carbonate minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Underwater basalt offers a good alternative to other forms of oceanic carbon storage because it has a number of trapping measures to ensure added protection against leakage. These measures include “geochemical, sediment, gravitational and <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrate</a> formation.” Because <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> hydrate is denser than <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> in seawater, the risk of leakage is minimal. Injecting the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> at depths greater than 2,700 meters (8,900 ft) ensures that the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> has a greater density than seawater, causing it to sink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-environmentalresearchweb.org_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-environmentalresearchweb.org-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>One possible injection site is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_de_Fuca_plate" title="Juan de Fuca plate">Juan de Fuca plate</a>. Researchers at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamont-Doherty_Earth_Observatory" title="Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory">Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory</a> found that this plate at the western coast of the United States has a possible storage capacity of 208 gigatons. This could cover the entire <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions_by_the_United_States" title="Greenhouse gas emissions by the United States">current U.S. carbon emissions</a> for over 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-environmentalresearchweb.org_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-environmentalresearchweb.org-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>This process is undergoing tests as part of the <a href="/wiki/CarbFix" title="CarbFix">CarbFix</a> project, resulting in 95% of the injected 250 tonnes of CO2 to solidify into <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> in 2 years, using 25 tonnes of water per tonne of CO2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matter-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid_neutralisation">Acid neutralisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Acid neutralisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Carbon dioxide forms <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> when dissolved in water, so <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> is a significant consequence of elevated carbon dioxide levels, and limits the rate at which it can be absorbed into the ocean (the <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">solubility pump</a>).  A variety of different <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> have been suggested that could neutralize the acid and thus increase <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>  For example, adding crushed <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> to oceans enhances the absorption of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>  Another approach is to add <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> to oceans which is produced by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of salt water or brine, while eliminating the waste <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> by reaction with a volcanic silicate rock such as <a href="/wiki/Enstatite" title="Enstatite">enstatite</a>, effectively increasing the rate of natural weathering of these rocks to restore ocean pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Obstruction">Obstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Obstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Danger_of_leaks">Danger of leaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Danger of leaks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide may be stored deep underground.  At depth, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_pressure" title="Hydrostatic pressure">hydrostatic pressure</a> acts to keep it in a liquid state. Reservoir design faults, rock fissures and tectonic processes may act to release the gas stored into the ocean or atmosphere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_costs">Financial costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Financial costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of the technology would add an additional 1–5 cents of cost per kilowatt hour, according to estimate made by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. The financial costs of modern coal technology would nearly double if use of CCS technology were to be required by regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>  The cost of CCS technology differs with the different types of capture technologies being used and with the different sites that it is implemented in, but the costs tend to increase with CCS capture implementation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> One study conducted predicted that with new technologies these costs could be lowered but would remain slightly higher than prices without CCS technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_requirements">Energy requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sequestration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Energy requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The energy requirements of sequestration processes may be significant. In one paper, sequestration consumed 25 percent of the plant's rated 600 megawatt output capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>After adding CO<sub>2</sub> capture and compression, the capacity of the coal-fired power plant is reduced to 457 MW.</i></dd></dl>
