<p><b>Scandium(III) oxide</b>, <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">Sc</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub>, or scandia, is a high melting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> oxide. It is used in the preparation of other scandium compounds as well as in high-temperature systems (for its resistance to heat and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">thermal shock</a>), electronic ceramics, and glass composition (as a helper material).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_physical_properties">Structure and physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scandium(III) oxide adopts a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> crystal structure (<a href="/wiki/Point_group" title="Point group">point group</a>: tetrahedral (T<sub>h</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a>: Ia<span style="text-decoration:overline;">3</span>) containing 6-coordinate metal centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Powder_diffraction" title="Powder diffraction">Powder diffraction</a> analysis shows Sc−O bond distances of 2.159–2.071 Å.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scandium Oxide is an insulator with a <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of 6.0 eV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scandium oxide is the primary form of refined <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> produced by the mining industry. Scandium-rich ores, such as <a href="/wiki/Thortveitite" title="Thortveitite">thortveitite</a> (Sc,Y)<sub>2</sub>(Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Kolbeckite" title="Kolbeckite">kolbeckite</a> ScPO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O are rare, however trace amounts of scandium are present in many other minerals. Scandium oxide is therefore predominately produced as a by-product from the extraction of other elements.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scandium oxide is the primary form of refined <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">scandium</a> produced by the mining industry, making it the start point for all scandium chemistry.
</p><p>Scandium oxide reacts with most acids upon heating, to produce the expected <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrated</a> product. For example, heating in excess aqueous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCl" title="HCl">HCl</a> produces hydrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium(III)_chloride" title="Scandium(III) chloride">ScCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O</a>. This can be rendered anhydrous by evaporation to dryness in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</a>, with the mixture then being purified by removal of NH<sub>4</sub>Cl by <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a> at 300-500 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The presence of NH<sub>4</sub>Cl is required, as the hydrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium(III)_chloride" title="Scandium(III) chloride">ScCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O</a> would otherwise form a mixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxychloride" title="Oxychloride">oxychloride</a> upon drying.
</p>
<dl><dd>Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">HCl</a> + <i>x</i> H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 ScCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>ScCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O + <i>n</i> <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</a> → ScCl<sub>3</sub> + <i>n</i> H<sub>2</sub>O + <i>n</i> <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</a></dd></dl>
<p>Likewise, it is converted into hydrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium(III)_triflate" title="Scandium(III) triflate">scandium(III) triflate</a> (Sc(OTf)<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O) by a reaction with <a href="/wiki/Triflic_acid" title="Triflic acid">triflic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Metallic scandium is produced industrially by the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of scandium oxide; this proceeds via conversion to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandium(III)_fluoride" title="Scandium(III) fluoride">scandium fluoride</a> followed by a reduction with metallic <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>. This process is in some ways similar to the <a href="/wiki/Kroll_process" title="Kroll process">Kroll process</a> for the production of metallic <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.
</p><p>Scandium oxide forms scandate salts with alkalis, unlike its higher homologues <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum_oxide" title="Lanthanum oxide">lanthanum oxide</a>, for example forming K<sub>3</sub>Sc(OH)<sub>6</sub> with KOH. In this, scandium shows more similarity with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>.
</p>
