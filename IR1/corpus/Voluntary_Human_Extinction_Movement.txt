<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Voluntary Human Extinction Movement</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Voluntary Human Extinction Movement</b> (<b>VHEMT</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[A]</a></sup>) is an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> that calls for all people to abstain from reproduction to cause the gradual voluntary <a href="/wiki/Human_extinction" title="Human extinction">extinction of humankind</a>. VHEMT supports human extinction primarily because, in the group's view, it would prevent <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>. The group states that a decrease in the human population would prevent a significant amount of human-caused suffering. The extinctions of non-human species and the scarcity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resources" title="Resources">resources</a> required by humans are frequently cited by the group as evidence of the harm caused by <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human overpopulation</a>.
</p><p>VHEMT was founded in 1991 by Les U. Knight, an American activist who became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement_in_the_United_States" title="Environmental movement in the United States">environmental movement</a> in the 1970s and thereafter concluded that human extinction was the best solution to the problems facing the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> and humanity. Knight publishes the group's newsletter and serves as its spokesman. Although the group is promoted by a website and represented at some environmental events, it relies heavily on coverage from outside media to spread its message. Many commentators view its platform as unacceptably extreme, though other writers have applauded VHEMT's perspective. In response to VHEMT, some journalists and academics have argued that humans can develop <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">sustainable lifestyles</a> or can <a href="/wiki/Human_population_planning" title="Human population planning">reduce their population</a> to sustainable levels. Others maintain that, whatever the merits of the idea, the human reproductive drive will prevent humankind from ever voluntarily seeking extinction.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Voluntary Human Extinction Movement was founded by Les U. Knight,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[B]</a></sup> a high school substitute teacher living in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> After becoming involved in the environmental movement as a college student in the 1970s, Knight attributed most of the dangers faced by the planet to human overpopulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> He joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_Population_Growth" title="Zero Population Growth">Zero Population Growth</a> organization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> and chose to be <a href="/wiki/Vasectomy" title="Vasectomy">vasectomised</a> at age 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> He later concluded that the extinction of humanity would be the best solution to the Earth's environmental problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> He believes that this idea has also been held by some people throughout human history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011142_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011142-8">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="286" data-file-width="432" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg/220px-Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg/330px-Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Voluntary Human Extinction Movement cartoon by <a href="/wiki/Nina_Paley" title="Nina Paley">Nina Paley</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1991, Knight began publishing VHEMT's newsletter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> known as <i>These Exit Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> In the newsletter, he asked readers to further human extinction by not procreating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> VHEMT has also published cartoons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ta_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ta-9">[7]</a></sup> including a comic strip titled <i>Bonobo Baby</i>, featuring a woman who forgoes childbearing in favor of adopting a <a href="/wiki/Bonobo" title="Bonobo">bonobo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> In 1996, Knight created a website for VHEMT;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup> it was available in 11 languages by 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310-11">[9]</a></sup> VHEMT's logo features the letter "V" (for voluntary) and a picture of the Earth with north at the bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vhemtlogo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-vhemtlogo-12">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[C]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization_and_promotion">Organization and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>VHEMT functions as a loose network rather than a formal organization,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011142–3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011142–3-14">[11]</a></sup> and does not compile a list of members. Daniel Metz of <a href="/wiki/Willamette_University" title="Willamette University">Willamette University</a> stated in 1995 that VHEMT's mailing list had just under 400 subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> Six years later, <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a> said the list had only 230 subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-fox-15">[12]</a></sup> Knight says that anyone who agrees with his ideology is a member of the movement;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> and that this includes "millions of people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aboutvhemt_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-aboutvhemt-16">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[D]</a></sup>
</p><p>Knight serves as the spokesman for VHEMT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> He attends environmental conferences and events, where he publicizes information about population growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310-11">[9]</a></sup> VHEMT's message has, however, primarily been spread through coverage by media outlets, rather than events and its newsletter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup> VHEMT sells buttons and T-shirts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Bumper_sticker" title="Bumper sticker">bumper stickers</a> that read "Thank you for not breeding".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Knight argues that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a> is far greater than the Earth can handle, and that the best thing for Earth's biosphere is for humans to voluntarily cease reproducing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_Technology_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Technology-20">[16]</a></sup> He says that humans are "incompatible with the biosphere"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> and that human existence is causing environmental damage which will eventually bring about the extinction of humans (as well as other organisms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-21">[17]</a></sup> According to Knight, the vast majority of human societies have not lived <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_living" title="Sustainable living">sustainable lifestyles</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> and attempts to live environmentally friendly lifestyles do not change the fact that human existence has ultimately been destructive to the Earth and many of its non-human organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> Voluntary human extinction is promoted on the grounds that it will prevent human suffering and the <a href="/wiki/Extinction#Modern_extinctions" title="Extinction">extinction of other species</a>; Knight points out that many species are threatened by the increasing human population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-fox-15">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_Technology_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Technology-20">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>James Ormrod, a psychologist who profiled the group in the journal <i>Psychoanalysis, Culture &amp; Society</i>, notes that the "most fundamental belief" of VHEMT is that "human beings should stop reproducing", and that some people consider themselves members of the group but do not actually support human extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup> Knight, however, believes that even if humans become more environmentally friendly, they could still return to environmentally destructive lifestyles and hence should eliminate themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> Residents of <a href="/wiki/First_World" title="First World">First World</a> countries bear the most responsibility to change, according to Knight, as they consume the largest proportion of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-og_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-og-22">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Knight believes that Earth's non-human organisms have a higher overall value than humans and their accomplishments, such as art: "The plays of Shakespeare and the work of Einstein can't hold a candle to a tiger".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> He argues that species higher in the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> are less important than lower species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> His ideology is drawn in part from <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a>, and he sometimes refers to the Earth as <a href="/wiki/Gaianism" title="Gaianism">Gaia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158-23">[19]</a></sup> He notes that human extinction is unavoidable, and that it is better to become extinct soon to avoid causing the extinction of other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_Technology_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Technology-20">[16]</a></sup> The potential for evolution of other organisms is also cited as a benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Knight sees abstinence from reproduction as an <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruistic</a> choice<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> – a way to prevent involuntary human suffering<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsci_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsci-24">[20]</a></sup> – and cites the deaths of children from preventable causes as an example of needless suffering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> Knight claims that non-reproduction would eventually allow humans to lead idyllic lifestyles in an environment comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-maclean-25">[21]</a></sup> and maintains that the last remaining humans would be proud of their accomplishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010312_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010312-26">[22]</a></sup> Other benefits of ceasing human reproduction that he cites include the end of abortion, war, and starvation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-maclean-25">[21]</a></sup> Knight argues that "procreation today is de facto child abuse".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158-23">[19]</a></sup> He maintains that the standard of human life will worsen if resources are consumed by a growing population rather than spent solving existing issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158-23">[19]</a></sup> He speculates that if people ceased to reproduce, they would use their energy for other pursuits,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> and suggests adoption and foster care as outlets for people who desire children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>VHEMT rejects government-mandated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_control" title="Human population control">human population control</a> programs in favor of voluntary population reduction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> supporting only the use of <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> and willpower to prevent pregnancies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> Knight states that coercive tactics are unlikely to permanently lower the human population, citing the fact that humanity has survived catastrophic wars, famines, and viruses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeisman2010310-11">[9]</a></sup> Though their newsletter's name recalls the suicide manual <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Exit" title="Final Exit">Final Exit</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-21">[17]</a></sup> the idea of mass suicide is rejected,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-og_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-og-22">[18]</a></sup> and they have adopted the slogan "May we live long and die out".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> A 1995 survey of VHEMT members found that a majority of them felt a strong moral obligation to protect the Earth, distrusted the ability of political processes to prevent harm to the environment, and were willing to surrender some of their rights for their cause. VHEMT members who strongly believed that "Civilization [is] headed for collapse" were most likely to embrace these views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis1998267_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis1998267-27">[23]</a></sup> However, VHEMT does not take any overt political stances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011143-10">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>VHEMT promotes a more extreme ideology than <a href="/wiki/Population_Action_International" title="Population Action International">Population Action International</a>, a group that argues humanity should reduce—but not eliminate—its population to care for the Earth. However, the VHEMT platform is more moderate and serious than the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Euthanasia" title="Church of Euthanasia">Church of Euthanasia</a>, which advocates population reduction by suicide and cannibalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-fox-15">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-maclean-25">[21]</a></sup> The 1995 survey found that 36% considered themselves members of <a href="/wiki/Earth_First!" title="Earth First!">Earth First!</a> or had donated to the group in the previous five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis1998382–3_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis1998382–3-28">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Knight states his group's ideology runs counter to contemporary society's <a href="/wiki/Natalism" title="Natalism">natalism</a>. He believes this pressure has stopped many people from supporting, or even discussing, population control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> He admits that his group is unlikely to succeed, but contends that attempting to reduce the Earth's population is the only moral option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reception of Knight's idea in the mainstream media has been mixed. Writing in the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i>, Gregory Dicum states that there is an "undeniable logic" to VHEMT's arguments, but he doubts whether Knight's ideas can succeed, arguing that many people desire to have children and cannot be dissuaded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfc_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-sfc-7">[5]</a></sup> Stephen Jarvis echoes this skepticism in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i>, noting that VHEMT faces great difficulty owing to the basic human reproductive drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup> At <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s website, Guy Dammann applauds the movement's aim as "in many ways laudable", but argues that it is absurd to believe that humans will voluntarily seek extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gd_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-gd-29">[25]</a></sup> Freelance writer Abby O'Reilly writes that since having children is frequently viewed as a measure of success, VHEMT's goal is difficult to attain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tg02_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-tg02-30">[26]</a></sup> Knight contends in response to these arguments that though <a href="/wiki/Sexual_desire" title="Sexual desire">sexual desire</a> is natural, human desire for children is a product of <a href="/wiki/Enculturation" title="Enculturation">enculturation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-ti-4">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_New_York" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New York">Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New York</a> has criticized Knight's platform, arguing that the existence of humanity is divinely ordained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-fox-15">[12]</a></sup> Ormrod claims that Knight "arguably abandons <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> in favour of straightforward <a href="/wiki/Misanthropy" title="Misanthropy">misanthropy</a>". He notes that Knight's claim that the last humans in an extinction scenario would have an abundance of resources promotes his cause based on "benefits accruing to humans". Ormrod sees this type of argument as counter-intuitive, arguing that it borrows the language of "late-modern consumer societies". He faults Knight for what he sees as a failure to develop a consistent and unambiguous ideology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOrmrod2011158-23">[19]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> characterizes Knight's claim that voluntary human extinction is advisable due to limited resources as "Malthusian bosh". The paper further states that compassion for the planet does not necessarily require the pursuit of human extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Furedi" title="Frank Furedi">Frank Furedi</a> also deems VHEMT to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malthusian" title="Malthusian">Malthusian</a> group, classifying them as a type of environmental organization that "[thinks] the worst about the human species".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spike_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-spike-31">[27]</a></sup> Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/Spiked_(magazine)" title="Spiked (magazine)">Spiked</a></i>, Josie Appleton argues that the group is indifferent to humanity, rather than "anti-human".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiked1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-spiked1-32">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brian Bethune writes in <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> that Knight's logic is "as absurd as it's unassailable". However, he doubts Knight's claim that the last survivors of the human race would have pleasant lives and suspects that a "collective loss of the will to live" would prevail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-maclean-25">[21]</a></sup> In response to Knight's platform, journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sheldon_Richman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sheldon Richman (page does not exist)">Sheldon Richman</a> argues that humans are "active agents" and can change their behavior. He contends that people are capable of solving the problems facing Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_Technology_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Technology-20">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alan_Weisman" title="Alan Weisman">Alan Weisman</a>, author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_World_Without_Us" title="The World Without Us">The World Without Us</a></i>, suggests a limit of one child per family as a preferable alternative to abstinence from reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maclean_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-maclean-25">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Katharine_Mieszkowski" title="Katharine Mieszkowski">Katharine Mieszkowski</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a> recommends that childless people adopt VHEMT's arguments when facing "probing questions" about their childlessness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdc-33">[29]</a></sup> Writing in the <i>Journal for Critical Animal Studies</i>, Carmen Dell'Aversano notes that VHEMT seeks to renounce children as a symbol of perpetual human progress. She casts the movement as a form of "queer oppositional politics" because it rejects perpetual reproduction as a form of motivation. She argues that the movement seeks to come to a new definition of "civil order", as <a href="/wiki/Lee_Edelman" title="Lee Edelman">Lee Edelman</a> suggested that <a href="/wiki/Queer_theory" title="Queer theory">queer theory</a> should. Dell'Aversano believes that VHEMT fulfills Edelman's mandate because they embody the <a href="/wiki/Death_drive" title="Death drive">death drive</a> rather than ideas that focus on the reproduction of the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-da_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-da-34">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Knight's organization has been featured in a book titled <i><a href="/wiki/Donna_Kossy#Kooks_(1994)" title="Donna Kossy">Kooks: A Guide to the Outer Limits of Human Belief</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-te_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-te-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>The Guardian</i> journalist <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Burkeman" title="Oliver Burkeman">Oliver Burkeman</a> notes that in a phone conversation Knight seems "rather sane and self-deprecating".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tg_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-tg-35">[31]</a></sup> Weisman echoes this sentiment, characterizing Knight as "thoughtful, soft-spoken, articulate, and quite serious".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiked1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-spiked1-32">[28]</a></sup> Philosophers <a href="/wiki/Steven_Best" title="Steven Best">Steven Best</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Kellner" title="Douglas Kellner">Douglas Kellner</a> view VHEMT's stance as extreme, but they note that the movement formed in response to extreme stances found in "modern <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBestKellner2001268–9_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner2001268–9-36">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voluntary_Human_Extinction_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1973, D. Keith Mano published a science fiction novel entitled <i>The Bridge</i> in which mankind has decided that its presence is environmentally intolerable and that all human beings must die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
