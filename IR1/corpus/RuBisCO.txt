<p><b>Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase</b>, commonly known by the abbreviations <b>RuBisCO, RuBPCase</b>, or <b>RuBPco</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> involved in the first major step of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon fixation</a>, a process by which atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is converted by plants and other <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> organisms to <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">energy-rich</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>. In chemical terms, it catalyzes the <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate" title="Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate">ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate</a> (also known as <i>RuBP</i>). It is probably the most abundant <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feller_2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feller_2008-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vs._alternative_carbon_fixation_pathways">Vs. alternative carbon fixation pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Vs. alternative carbon fixation pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>RuBisCO is important <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biologically</a> because it catalyzes the primary <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> by which <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">inorganic carbon</a> enters the organic <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>.  While many autotrophic bacteria and archaea fix carbon via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reductive_acetyl_CoA_Pathway" title="Reductive acetyl CoA Pathway">reductive acetyl CoA pathway</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-hydroxypropionate_cycle" title="3-hydroxypropionate cycle">3-hydroxypropionate cycle</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Reverse_Krebs_cycle" title="Reverse Krebs cycle">reverse Krebs cycle</a>, these pathways are relatively smaller contributors to global carbon fixation than that catalyzed by RuBisCO. <a href="/wiki/Phosphoenolpyruvate_carboxylase" title="Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase">Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase</a>, unlike RuBisCO, only temporarily fixes carbon. Reflecting its importance, RuBisCO is the most abundant protein in <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a>, accounting for 50% of soluble leaf protein in <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C<sub>3</sub> plants</a> (20–30% of total leaf nitrogen) and 30% of soluble leaf protein in <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> plants</a> (5–9% of total leaf nitrogen).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feller_2008_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feller_2008-2">[2]</a></sup> Given its important role in the biosphere, the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> of RuBisCO in crops is of continuing interest (see <a href="#Genetic_engineering">below</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="475" data-file-width="483" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg/220px-Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg/330px-Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg/440px-Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of the <i>rbcL</i> gene in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroplast_genome" title="Chloroplast genome">chloroplast genome</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> (positions ca. 55-56.4 kb). <i>rbcL</i> is one of the 21 protein-coding genes involved in photosynthesis (green boxes).</div></div></div>
<p>In plants, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemoautotrophic</a> <a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">proteobacteria</a>, the enzyme usually consists of two types of protein subunit, called the large chain (<b>L</b>, about 55,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">Da</a>) and the small chain (<b>S</b>, about 13,000 Da). The <i>large-chain</i> gene (<i>rbcL</i>) is encoded by the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> DNA in plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There are typically several related <i>small-chain</i> genes in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a> of plant cells, and the small chains are imported to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stromal" title="Stromal">stromal</a> compartment of chloroplasts from the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a> by crossing the outer <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast_membrane" title="Chloroplast membrane">chloroplast membrane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15067115_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15067115-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11401297_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11401297-5">[5]</a></sup> The enzymatically active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(biochemistry)" title="Substrate (biochemistry)">substrate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ribulose" title="Ribulose">ribulose</a> 1,5-bisphosphate) <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">binding sites</a> are located in the large <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">chains</a> that form <a href="/wiki/Protein_dimer" title="Protein dimer">dimers</a> as shown in Figure 1 (above, right) in which <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> from each large chain contribute to the binding sites. A total of eight large-chains (= 4 dimers) and eight small chains assemble into a larger complex of about 540,000 Da.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In some <a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">proteobacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a>, enzymes consisting of only large subunits have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Mg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) are needed for enzymatic activity. Correct positioning of <span class="chemf nowrap">Mg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> in the <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">active site</a> of the enzyme involves addition of an "activating" carbon dioxide molecule (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>) to a <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a> in the active site (forming a <a href="/wiki/Carbamate" title="Carbamate">carbamate</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Mg<sup>2+</sup> operates by driving deprotonation of the Lys210 residue, causing the Lys residue to rotate by 120 degrees to the <i>trans</i> conformer, decreasing the distance between the nitrogen of Lys and the carbon of CO<sub>2</sub>. The close proximity allows for the formation of a covalent bond, resulting in the carbamate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup>  Mg<sup>2+</sup> is first enabled to bind to the active site by the rotation of His335 to an alternate conformation. Mg<sup>2+</sup> is then coordinated by the His residues of the active site (His300, His302, His335), and is partially neutralized by the coordination of three water molecules and their conversion to <sup>−</sup>OH (Figure 2).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup> This coordination results in an unstable complex, but produces a favorable environment for the binding of Mg<sup>2+</sup>. Formation of the carbamate is favored by an <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkaline</a> <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. The pH and the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of magnesium ions in the fluid compartment (in plants, the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">stroma of the chloroplast</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>) increases in the light. The role of changing pH and magnesium ion levels in the regulation of RuBisCO enzyme activity is discussed <a href="#Regulation_of_its_enzymatic_activity">below</a>. Once the carbamate is formed, His335 finalizes the activation by returning to its initial position through thermal fluctuation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enzymatic_activity">Enzymatic activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enzymatic activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="836" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/300px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/450px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/600px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 3</b>. Overview of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a> and carbon fixation</div></div></div>
<p>As shown in Figure 2 (left), RuBisCO is one of many enzymes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Substrates">Substrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Substrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During carbon fixation, the substrate molecules for RuBisCO are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate" title="Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate">ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (distinct from the "activating" carbon dioxide).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> RuBisCO also catalyses a reaction between ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">molecular oxygen</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) instead of carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinguishing_between_substrates">Distinguishing between substrates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distinguishing between substrates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the overall neutral charge and lack of dipole in both substrates, discriminating between and binding the substrates at the active site rely on the interaction between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrupole_moment" title="Quadrupole moment">quadrupole moments</a> of both substrates and a high electrostatic field gradient from the enzyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">[9]</a></sup> This gradient is established by the dimer form of the minimally active RuBisCO, which with its two components provides a combination of oppositely charged domains required for the enzyme's interaction with O<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub>. These conditions help explain the low turnover rate found in RuBisCO: In order to increase the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> necessary for sufficient interaction with the substrates’ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrupole_moment" title="Quadrupole moment">quadrupole moments</a>, the C- and N- terminal segments of the enzyme must be closed off, allowing the active site to be isolated from the solvent and lowering the dielectric constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This isolation has a significant entropic cost, and results in the poor turnover rate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When carbon dioxide is the substrate, the product of the carboxylase reaction is a highly unstable six-carbon phosphorylated intermediate known as 3-keto-2-carboxyarabinitol-1,5-bisphosphate, which decays virtually instantaneously into two molecules of glycerate-3-phosphate. The extremely unstable molecule created by the initial carboxylation was unknown until 1988, when it was isolated. The 3-phosphoglycerate can be used to produce larger molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>.
Also, Rubisco <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_promiscuity" title="Enzyme promiscuity">side activities</a> can lead to useless or inhibitory by-products; one such product is xylulose-1,5-bisphosphate, which inhibits Rubisco activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>When molecular oxygen is the substrate, the products of the oxygenase reaction are phosphoglycolate and 3-phosphoglycerate. Phosphoglycolate is recycled through a sequence of reactions called <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>, which involves enzymes and cytochromes located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peroxisomes" title="Peroxisomes">peroxisomes</a> (this is a case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolite_damage_and_its_repair_or_pre-emption" title="Metabolite damage and its repair or pre-emption">metabolite repair</a>). In this process, two molecules of phosphoglycolate are converted to one molecule of carbon dioxide and one molecule of 3-phosphoglycerate, which can reenter the Calvin cycle. Some of the phosphoglycolate entering this pathway can be retained by plants to produce other molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>. At ambient levels of carbon dioxide and oxygen, the ratio of the reactions is about 4 to 1, which results in a net carbon dioxide fixation of only 3.5.  Thus, the inability of the enzyme to prevent the reaction with oxygen greatly reduces the photosynthetic capacity of many plants. Some plants, many algae, and photosynthetic bacteria have overcome this limitation by devising means to increase the concentration of carbon dioxide around the enzyme, including <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> carbon fixation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">crassulacean acid metabolism</a>, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Pyrenoid" title="Pyrenoid">pyrenoid</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate_of_enzymatic_activity">Rate of enzymatic activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rate of enzymatic activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some enzymes can carry out thousands of chemical reactions each second. However, RuBisCO is slow, being able to fix only 3-10 carbon dioxide molecules each second per molecule of enzyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The reaction catalyzed by RuBisCO is, thus, the primary rate-limiting factor of the Calvin cycle during the day. Nevertheless, under most conditions, and when light is not otherwise limiting photosynthesis, the speed of RuBisCO responds positively to increasing carbon dioxide concentration.
However, our descriptive knowledge will become more usable when we can translate them into quantitative models that can enable us to calculate the outcome of the reaction under a given condition.  Since RubisCO reacts with RuBP (ribulose 1,5 bisphosphate) first to produce enediol and next with CO<sub>2</sub> that after some intermediate changes produces PGA (3-phosphoglycerate), a biochemical model is developed  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> to represent the  effects of  these steps quantitatively. Since carboxylation or fixation of CO<sub>2</sub> is possible only after the synthesis of enediol, thus it is suggested that the role of RubisCO is to produce enediol that is carboxylase and oxygenase (EnCO). Accordingly, RubisCO is called enolase-phosphoglycerase (EPGase) since it is neither carboxylase nor oxygenase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_of_its_enzymatic_activity">Regulation of its enzymatic activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regulation of its enzymatic activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>RuBisCO is usually only active during the day as ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate is not regenerated in the dark. This is due to the regulation of several other enzymes in the Calvin cycle. In addition, the activity of RuBisCO is coordinated with that of the other enzymes of the Calvin cycle in several ways.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_ions">Regulation by ions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Regulation by ions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon illumination of the chloroplasts, the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Stroma_(fluid)" title="Stroma (fluid)">stroma</a> rises from 7.0 to 8.0 because of the proton (hydrogen ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) gradient created across the <a href="/wiki/Thylakoid" title="Thylakoid">thylakoid</a> membrane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> At the same time, magnesium ions (<span class="chemf nowrap">Mg<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) move out of the thylakoids, increasing the concentration of magnesium in the stroma of the chloroplasts. RuBisCO has a high optimal pH (can be &gt;9.0, depending on the magnesium ion concentration) and, thus, becomes "activated" by the addition of carbon dioxide and magnesium to the active sites as described above.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_RuBisCO_activase">Regulation by RuBisCO activase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Regulation by RuBisCO activase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In plants and some algae, another enzyme, <b>RuBisCO activase</b>, is required to allow the rapid formation of the critical <a href="/wiki/Carbamate" title="Carbamate">carbamate</a> in the active site of RuBisCO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16245090_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16245090-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15236471_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15236471-18">[18]</a></sup> RuBisCO activase is required because the <a href="/wiki/Ribulose_1,5-bisphosphate" title="Ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate">ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate</a> (RuBP) substrate binds more strongly to the active sites lacking the carbamate and markedly slows down the "activation" process. In the light, RuBisCO activase promotes the release of the inhibitory (or — in some views — storage) RuBP from the catalytic sites. Activase is also required in some plants (e.g., tobacco and many beans) because, in darkness, RuBisCO is inhibited (or protected from hydrolysis) by a competitive inhibitor synthesized by these plants, a substrate analog <a href="/wiki/2-Carboxy-D-arabitinol_1-phosphate" title="2-Carboxy-D-arabitinol 1-phosphate">2-Carboxy-D-arabitinol 1-phosphate</a> (CA1P).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7818481_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7818481-19">[19]</a></sup> CA1P binds tightly to the active site of carbamylated RuBisCO and inhibits catalytic activity. In the light, RuBisCO activase also promotes the release of CA1P from the catalytic sites. After the CA1P is released from RuBisCO, it is rapidly converted to a non-inhibitory form by a light-activated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CA1P-phosphatase" title="CA1P-phosphatase">CA1P-phosphatase</a>. Finally, once every several hundred reactions, the normal reactions with carbon dioxide or oxygen are not completed, and other inhibitory substrate analogs are formed in the active site.  Once again, RuBisCO activase can promote the release of these analogs from the catalytic sites and maintain the enzyme in a catalytically active form. The properties of activase limit the photosynthetic potential of plants at high temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11069297_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11069297-20">[20]</a></sup> CA1P has also been shown to keep RuBisCO in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_conformation" title="Chemical conformation">conformation</a> that is protected from <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">proteolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> At high temperatures, RuBisCO activase aggregates and can no longer activate RuBisCO. This contributes to the decreased carboxylating capacity observed during heat stress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11706186_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11706186-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Regulation_by_ATP.2FADP_and_stromal_reduction.2Foxidation_state_through_the_activase"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_ATP/ADP_and_stromal_reduction/oxidation_state_through_the_activase">Regulation by ATP/ADP and stromal reduction/oxidation state through the activase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regulation by ATP/ADP and stromal reduction/oxidation state through the activase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The removal of the inhibitory RuBP, CA1P, and the other inhibitory substrate analogs by activase requires the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>. This reaction is inhibited by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a>, and, thus, activase activity depends on the ratio of these compounds in the chloroplast stroma. Furthermore, in most plants, the sensitivity of activase to the ratio of ATP/ADP is modified by the stromal reduction/oxidation (<a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a>) state through another small regulatory protein, <a href="/wiki/Thioredoxin" title="Thioredoxin">thioredoxin</a>. In this manner, the activity of activase and the activation state of RuBisCO can be modulated in response to light intensity and, thus, the rate of formation of the ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate substrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_phosphate">Regulation by phosphate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Regulation by phosphate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cyanobacteria, inorganic <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> (P<sub>i</sub>) participates in the co-ordinated regulation of photosynthesis. P<sub>i</sub> binds to the RuBisCO active site and to another site on the large chain where it can influence transitions between activated and less active conformations of the enzyme. Activation of bacterial RuBisCO might be particularly sensitive to P<sub>i</sub> levels, which can act in the same way as RuBisCO activase in higher plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_by_carbon_dioxide">Regulation by carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation by carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since carbon dioxide and oxygen <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_inhibitor" title="Enzyme inhibitor">compete</a> at the active site of RuBisCO, carbon fixation by RuBisCO can be enhanced by increasing the carbon dioxide level in the compartment containing RuBisCO (chloroplast stroma). Several times during the evolution of plants, mechanisms have evolved for increasing the level of carbon dioxide in the stroma (see <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> carbon fixation</a>). The use of oxygen as a substrate appears to be a puzzling process, since it seems to throw away captured energy. However, it may be a mechanism for preventing overload during periods of high light flux.  This weakness in the enzyme is the cause of photorespiration, such that healthy leaves in bright light may have zero net carbon fixation when the ratio of <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> to CO<sub>2</sub> reaches a threshold at which oxygen is fixed instead of carbon. This phenomenon is primarily temperature-dependent. High temperature decreases the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> dissolved in the moisture in the leaf tissues.  This phenomenon is also related to water stress. Since plant leaves are evaporatively cooled, limited water causes high leaf temperatures. C<sub>4</sub> plants use the enzyme <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">PEP carboxylase</a> initially, which has a higher affinity for CO<sub>2</sub>. The process first makes a 4-carbon intermediate compound, which is shuttled into a site of C<sub>3</sub> photosynthesis then de-carboxylated, releasing CO<sub>2</sub> to boost the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>, hence the name C<sub>4</sub> plants.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">Crassulacean acid metabolism</a> (CAM) plants keep their stomata (on the underside of the leaf) closed during the day, which conserves water but prevents the light-independent reactions (a.k.a. the Calvin Cycle) from taking place, since these reactions require CO<sub>2</sub> to pass by gas exchange through these openings. Evaporation through the upper side of a leaf is prevented by a layer of wax.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_engineering">Genetic engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Genetic engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since RuBisCO is often rate-limiting for photosynthesis in plants, it may be possible to improve <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic_efficiency" title="Photosynthetic efficiency">photosynthetic efficiency</a> by modifying RuBisCO genes in plants to increase catalytic activity and/or decrease oxygenation rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This could improve <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">biosequestration</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> and be both an important <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> strategy and a strategy to increase crop yields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-26">[26]</a></sup> Approaches under investigation include transferring RuBisCO genes from one organism into another organism, engineering Rubisco activase from thermophilic cyanobacteria into temperature sensitive plants, increasing the level of expression of RuBisCO subunits, expressing RuBisCO small chains from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastome" title="Plastome">chloroplast DNA</a>, and altering RuBisCO genes to increase specificity for carbon dioxide or otherwise increase the rate of carbon fixation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Although, as the levels of CO2 rise, efforts to increase specificity for CO2 may be unnecessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutagenesis_in_plants">Mutagenesis in plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mutagenesis in plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general, <a href="/wiki/Site-directed_mutagenesis" title="Site-directed mutagenesis">site-directed mutagenesis</a> of RuBisCO has been mostly unsuccessful,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-26">[26]</a></sup> though mutated forms of the protein have been achieved in tobacco plants with subunit C<sub>4</sub> species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and a RuBisCO with more C<sub>4</sub>-like kinetic characteristics have been attained in rice via nuclear transformation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>One avenue is to introduce RuBisCO variants with naturally high specificity values such as the ones from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_alga" title="Red alga">red alga</a> <i>Galdieria partita</i> into plants. This may improve the photosynthetic efficiency of crop plants, although possible negative impacts have yet to be studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Advances in this area include the replacement of the tobacco enzyme with that of the purple photosynthetic bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodospirillum_rubrum" title="Rhodospirillum rubrum">Rhodospirillum rubrum</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2014, two transplastomic tobacco lines with functional RuBisCO from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyanobacterium" title="Cyanobacterium">cyanobacterium</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Synechococcus" title="Synechococcus">Synechococcus</a> elongatus</i> PCC7942 (Se7942) were created by replacing the RuBisCO with the large and small subunit genes of the Se7942 enzyme, in combination with either the corresponding Se7942 assembly chaperone, RbcX, or an internal carboxysomal protein, CcmM35. Both mutants had increased CO<sub>2</sub> fixation rates when measured as carbon molecules per RuBisCO.  However, the mutant plants grew more slowly than wild-type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent theory explores the trade-off between the relative specificity (i.e., ability to favour CO<sub>2</sub> fixation over <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> incorporation, which leads to the energy-wasteful process of <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>) and the rate at which product is formed. The authors conclude that RuBisCO may actually have evolved to reach a point of 'near-perfection' in many plants (with widely varying substrate availabilities and environmental conditions), reaching a compromise between specificity and reaction rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tcherkez06_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tcherkez06-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since photosynthesis is the single most effective natural regulator of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_the_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in the Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide in the Earth's atmosphere</a>, a biochemical model of RuBisCO reaction is used as the core module of climate change models. Thus, a correct model of this reaction is essential to the basic understanding of the relations and interactions of environmental models. A new theory and model of the biochemical reaction of photosynthesis and the drawbacks of today's most widely used model of photosynthesis is discussed in volume 29 of Advances in Photosynthesis and Respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expression_in_bacterial_hosts">Expression in bacterial hosts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Expression in bacterial hosts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There currently are very few effective methods for expressing functional plant RuBisCO in bacterial hosts for genetic manipulation studies. This is largely due to RuBisCO's requirement of complex cellular machinery for its biogenesis and metabolic maintenance including the nuclear-encoded RbcS subunits, which are typically imported into <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> as unfolded proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Furthermore, sufficient expression and interaction with RuBisCO activase are major challenges as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> One successful method for expression of RuBisCO in <a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a> involves the coexpression of multiple chloroplast chaperones, though this has only been shown in <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depletion_in_proteomic_studies">Depletion in proteomic studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Depletion in proteomic studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its high abundance in plants (generally 40% of the total protein content), RuBisCO often impedes analysis of important signaling proteins such as <a href="/wiki/Transcription_factor" title="Transcription factor">transcription factors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kinase" title="Kinase">kinase</a>s, and regulatory proteins found in lower abundance (10-100 molecules per cell) within plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-40">[40]</a></sup> For example, using <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> on plant protein mixtures would result in multiple intense RuBisCO subunit peaks that interfere and hide those of other proteins.
</p><p>Recently, one efficient method for precipitating out RuBisCO involves the usage of <a href="/wiki/Protamine_sulfate" title="Protamine sulfate">protamine sulfate</a> solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Other existing methods for depleting RuBisCO and studying lower abundance proteins include <a href="/wiki/Fractionation" title="Fractionation">fractionation</a> techniques with calcium and phytate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gel_electrophoresis" title="Gel electrophoresis">gel electrophoresis</a> with polyethylene glycol,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Affinity_chromatography" title="Affinity chromatography">affinity chromatography</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and aggregation using <a href="/wiki/Dithiothreitol" title="Dithiothreitol">DTT</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> though these methods are more time-consuming and less efficient when compared to protamine sulfate precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogenetic_studies">Phylogenetic studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Phylogenetic studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chloroplast gene <i>rbc</i>L, which codes for the large subunit of RuBisCO has been widely used as an appropriate <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">locus</a> for analysis of <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plant_taxonomy" title="Plant taxonomy">plant taxonomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChase_et_al1993_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChase_et_al1993-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_RuBisCO">Evolution of RuBisCO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Evolution of RuBisCO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the evolution of the C<sub>4</sub>-fixation pathway in certain species of plants, C<sub>3</sub> RuBisCO evolved to have faster turnover of CO<sub>2</sub> in exchange for lower specificity as a result of the greater localization of CO<sub>2</sub> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesophyll_cell" title="Mesophyll cell">mesophyll cells</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bundle_sheath_cells" title="Bundle sheath cells">bundle sheath cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This was achieved through enhancement of conformational flexibility of the “open-closed” transition in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_Cycle" title="Calvin Cycle">Calvin Cycle</a>. Laboratory-based phylogenetic studies have shown that this evolution was constrained by the trade-off between stability and activity brought about by the series of necessary <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> for C<sub>4</sub> RuBisCO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-50">[50]</a></sup> Moreover, in order to sustain the destabilizing mutations, the evolution to C<sub>4</sub> RuBisCO was preceded by a period in which mutations granted the enzyme increased stability, establishing a buffer to sustain and maintain the mutations required for C<sub>4</sub> RuBisCO. To assist with this buffering process, the newly-evolved enzyme was found to have further developed a series of stabilizing mutations. While RuBisCO has always been accumulating new mutations, most of these mutations that have survived have not had significant effects on protein stability. The destabilizing C<sub>4</sub> mutations on RuBisCO has been sustained by environmental pressures such as low CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations, requiring a sacrifice of stability for new adaptive functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_term">History of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: History of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "RuBisCO" was coined humorously in 1979, by <a href="/wiki/David_Eisenberg" title="David Eisenberg">David Eisenberg</a> at a seminar honouring the retirement of the early, prominent RuBisCO researcher, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Wildman" title="Sam Wildman">Sam Wildman</a>, and also alluded to the snack food trade name "<a href="/wiki/Nabisco" title="Nabisco">Nabisco</a>" in reference to Wildman's attempts to create an edible protein supplement from tobacco leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
