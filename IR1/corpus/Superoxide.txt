<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Superoxide</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Superoxide.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="260" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Superoxide.svg/150px-Superoxide.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Superoxide.svg/225px-Superoxide.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Superoxide.svg/300px-Superoxide.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Superoxide.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis electron configuration</a> of superoxide. The six outer-shell electrons of each <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom are shown in black; one electron pair is shared (middle); the unpaired electron is shown in the upper-left; and the additional electron conferring a negative charge is shown in red.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>superoxide</b> is a compound that contains the superoxide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>, which has the chemical formula <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The systematic name of the anion is <b>dioxide(1−)</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen anion</a> superoxide is particularly important as the product of the one-electron <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxygen" title="Dioxygen">dioxygen</a> O<sub>2</sub>, which occurs widely in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Molecular oxygen (dioxygen) is a <a href="/wiki/Diradical" title="Diradical">diradical</a> containing two <a href="/wiki/Unpaired_electron" title="Unpaired electron">unpaired electrons</a>, and superoxide results from the addition of an electron which fills one of the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_level" title="Degenerate energy level">degenerate</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">molecular orbitals</a>, leaving a charged ionic species with a single unpaired electron and a net negative charge of −1.  Both dioxygen and the superoxide anion are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals">free radicals</a> that exhibit <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivatives">Derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The superoxide anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, and its protonated form, the <a href="/wiki/Hydroperoxyl" title="Hydroperoxyl">hydroperoxyl</a> radical HO<sub>2</sub>, are in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pka_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pka-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ HO<sub>2</sub> + OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Given that the hydroperoxyl radical has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a> of around 4.8,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> at neutral pH superoxide predominantly exists in the anionic form.
</p><p>Superoxides form salts with <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a>.  The salts <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caesium_superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Caesium superoxide (page does not exist)">CsO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium_superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rubidium superoxide (page does not exist)">RbO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_superoxide" title="Potassium superoxide">KO<sub>2</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_superoxide" title="Sodium superoxide">NaO<sub>2</sub></a> are prepared by the reaction of O<sub>2</sub> with the respective alkali metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alkali salts of <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> are orange-yellow in color and quite stable, provided they are kept dry. Upon dissolution of these salts in water, however, the dissolved <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> undergoes <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> (dismutation) extremely rapidly (in a pH-dependent manner):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4 <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>  + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O  →  3 O<sub>2</sub>  +  4 OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction (with moisture and carbon dioxide in exhaled air) is the basis of the use of potassium superoxide as an oxygen source in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oxygen_generator" title="Chemical oxygen generator">chemical oxygen generators</a>, such as those used on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>. Superoxides are also used in <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighters</a>' <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_tank" title="Oxygen tank">oxygen tanks</a> in order to provide a readily available source of oxygen.
</p><p>In this process <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> acts as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction_theories" title="Acid-base reaction theories">Brønsted base</a>, initially forming the radical <a href="/wiki/Hydroperoxyl" title="Hydroperoxyl">HO<sub>2</sub>·</a>. But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a> of its <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_superoxide" title="Hydrogen superoxide">hydrogen superoxide</a> (HO<sub>2</sub>·, also known as "<a href="/wiki/Hydroperoxyl" title="Hydroperoxyl">hydroperoxyl</a>" or "perhydroxy radical"), is 4.88 so that at neutral <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> 7 all but 0.3% of superoxide is in the anionic form, <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>Potassium superoxide is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a> (facilitated by crown ethers) and is stable as long as protons are not available. Superoxide can also be generated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aprotic" title="Aprotic">aprotic</a> solvents by <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_voltammetry" title="Cyclic voltammetry">cyclic voltammetry</a>.
</p><p>Salts also decompose in the solid state, but this process requires heating:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaO<sub>2</sub>  →  Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Superoxide and hydroperoxyl (HO<sub>2</sub>) are discussed interchangeably, although superoxide predominates at physiological pHs.  Both superoxide and hydroperoxyl are classified as <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is generated by the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> to kill invading <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Phagocyte" title="Phagocyte">phagocytes</a>, superoxide is produced in large quantities by the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> <a href="/wiki/NADPH_oxidase" title="NADPH oxidase">NADPH oxidase</a> for use in oxygen-dependent killing mechanisms of invading pathogens. Mutations in the gene coding for the NADPH oxidase cause an immunodeficiency syndrome called <a href="/wiki/Chronic_granulomatous_disease" title="Chronic granulomatous disease">chronic granulomatous disease</a>, characterized by extreme susceptibility to infection, especially catalase-positive organisms. In turn, micro-organisms genetically engineered to lack <a href="/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase" title="Superoxide dismutase">superoxide dismutase</a> (SOD) lose <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a>. Superoxide is also deleterious when produced as a byproduct of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a> (most notably by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_I" title="Complex I">Complex I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_III" title="Complex III">Complex III</a>), as well as several other enzymes, for example <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_oxidase" title="Xanthine oxidase">xanthine oxidase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17640558_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17640558-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because superoxide is toxic at high concentrations, nearly all organisms living in the presence of oxygen contain superoxide-scavenging enzymes called <a href="/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase" title="Superoxide dismutase">superoxide dismutase</a> (SOD). SOD efficiently catalyzes the <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> of superoxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 HO<sub>2</sub>  →  O<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Other proteins that can be both oxidized and reduced by superoxide (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin#Iron.27s_oxidation_state_in_oxyhemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>) have weak SOD-like activity. Genetic inactivation ("<a href="/wiki/Gene_knockout" title="Gene knockout">knockout</a>") of SOD produces deleterious <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypes</a> in organisms ranging from bacteria to mice and have provided important clues as to the mechanisms of toxicity of superoxide in vivo.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Yeast#Science" title="Yeast">Yeast</a> lacking both mitochondrial and cytosolic SOD grow very poorly in air, but quite well under anaerobic conditions. Absence of cytosolic SOD causes a dramatic increase in mutagenesis and genomic instability. Mice lacking mitochondrial SOD (MnSOD) die around 21 days after birth due to neurodegeneration, cardiomyopathy, and lactic acidosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17640558_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17640558-10">[10]</a></sup> Mice lacking cytosolic SOD (CuZnSOD) are viable but suffer from multiple pathologies, including reduced lifespan, <a href="/wiki/Hepatocellular_carcinoma" title="Hepatocellular carcinoma">liver cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle_atrophy" title="Muscle atrophy">muscle atrophy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataracts" title="Cataracts">cataracts</a>, thymic involution, haemolytic anemia and a very rapid age-dependent decline in female fertility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17640558_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17640558-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Superoxide may contribute to the pathogenesis of many diseases (the evidence is particularly strong for <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> poisoning and <a href="/wiki/Hyperoxia" title="Hyperoxia">hyperoxic</a> injury), and perhaps also to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging" title="Aging">aging</a> via the oxidative damage that it inflicts on cells. While the action of superoxide in the pathogenesis of some conditions is strong (for instance, mice and rats overexpressing CuZnSOD or MnSOD are more resistant to strokes and heart attacks), the role of superoxide in aging must be regarded as unproven for now. In <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organisms</a> (yeast, the fruit fly Drosophila and mice), genetically <a href="/wiki/Gene_knockout" title="Gene knockout">knocking out</a> CuZnSOD shortens lifespan and accelerates certain features of aging (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataracts" title="Cataracts">cataracts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscle_atrophy" title="Muscle atrophy">muscle atrophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macular_degeneration" title="Macular degeneration">macular degeneration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thymic_involution" title="Thymic involution">thymic involution</a>).  But the converse, increasing the levels of CuZnSOD, does not seem (except perhaps in <i><a href="/wiki/Drosophila" title="Drosophila">Drosophila</a></i>), to consistently increase lifespan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17640558_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17640558-10">[10]</a></sup> The most widely accepted view is that oxidative damage (resulting from multiple causes, including superoxide) is but one of several factors limiting lifespan.
</p><p>The binding of O<sub>2</sub> by heme proteins involves formation of Fe(III) superoxide complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Assay_in_biological_systems">Assay in biological systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Assay in biological systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The assay of superoxide generated in biological systems is a difficult task because of its high reactivity and short half-life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8074285_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8074285-12">[12]</a></sup> One approach that has been used in quantitative assays converts superoxide to <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, which is relatively stable. Hydrogen peroxide is then assayed by a fluorimetric method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8074285_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8074285-12">[12]</a></sup> As a free radical, superoxide has a strong <a href="/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance" title="Electron paramagnetic resonance">EPR</a> signal, and it is possible to detect superoxide directly using this method when its abundance is high enough. For practical purposes, this can be achieved only in vitro under non-physiological conditions, such as high pH (which slows the spontaneous dismutation) with the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_oxidase" title="Xanthine oxidase">xanthine oxidase</a>. Researchers have developed a series of tool compounds termed "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin_trap" title="Spin trap">spin traps</a>"(see <a href="/wiki/Spin_trapping" title="Spin trapping">spin trapping</a>) that can react with superoxide, forming a meta-stable radical (half-life 1–15 minutes), which can be more readily detected by EPR. Superoxide spin trapping was initially carried out with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DMPO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DMPO (page does not exist)">DMPO</a>, but, more recently, phosphorus derivatives with improved half-lives, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DEPPMPO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DEPPMPO (page does not exist)">DEPPMPO</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DIPPMPO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DIPPMPO (page does not exist)">DIPPMPO</a>, have become more widely used.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding_and_structure">Bonding and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bonding and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Superoxides are compounds in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation_number" title="Oxidation number">oxidation number</a> of oxygen is −​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>. Whereas molecular oxygen (dioxygen) is a <a href="/wiki/Diradical" title="Diradical">diradical</a> containing two <a href="/wiki/Unpaired_electron" title="Unpaired electron">unpaired electrons</a>, the addition of a second electron fills one of its two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_level" title="Degenerate energy level">degenerate</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">molecular orbitals</a>, leaving a charged ionic species with single unpaired electron and a net negative charge of −1.  Both dioxygen and the superoxide anion are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals">free radicals</a> that exhibit <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetism" title="Paramagnetism">paramagnetism</a>.  
</p><p>The derivatives of dioxygen have characteristic O–O distances that correlate with the <a href="/wiki/Bond_order" title="Bond order">order</a> of the O–O bond.
