<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Molar mass</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the <b>molar mass</b> <i>M</i> is a physical property defined as the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of a given substance (<a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a>) divided by the <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount of substance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenBook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBook-1">[1]</a></sup> The base <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">SI unit</a> for molar mass is <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>. However, for historical reasons, molar masses are almost always expressed in g/mol.
</p><p>As an example, the molar mass of water: <i>M</i>(H<sub>2</sub>O) ≈ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998180150000000000♠</span>18.015 g/mol</span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molar_masses_of_elements">Molar masses of elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Molar masses of elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Relative_atomic_mass" title="Relative atomic mass">Relative atomic mass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">Standard atomic weight</a></div>
<p>The molar mass of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> is given by the <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">Standard atomic weight</a> of the element<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtWt_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AtWt-2">[2]</a></sup> multiplied by the <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass_constant" title="Molar mass constant">molar mass constant</a>, <i>M</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">u</sub></span> = 1 × 10<sup>−3</sup> kg/mol = 1 g/mol:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CODATA2010_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CODATA2010-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>M</i>(H) = 1.007 97(7) × 1 g/mol = 1.007 97(7) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(S) = 32.065(5) × 1 g/mol = 32.065(5) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(Cl) = 35.453(2) × 1 g/mol = 35.453(2) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(Fe) = 55.845(2) × 1 g/mol = 55.845(2) g/mol.</dd></dl>
<p>Multiplying by the molar mass constant ensures that the calculation is <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensionally</a> correct: standard relative atomic masses are dimensionless quantities (i.e., pure numbers) whereas molar masses have units (in this case, grams/mole).
</p><p>Some elements are usually encountered as <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> (S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span>), <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>). The molar mass of molecules of these elements is the molar mass of the atoms multiplied by the number of atoms in each molecule:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>M</i>(H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>) = 2 × 1.007 97(7) × 1 g/mol = 2.015 88(14) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span>) = 8 × 32.065(5) × 1 g/mol = 256.52(4) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>) = 2 × 35.453(2) × 1 g/mol = 70.906(4) g/mol.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molar_masses_of_compounds">Molar masses of compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Molar masses of compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The molar mass of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> is given by the sum of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weight</a> (namely, the standard relative atomic mass) of the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> which form the compound multiplied by the <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass_constant" title="Molar mass constant">molar mass constant</a>, <i>M</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">u</sub></span>:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>M</i>(NaCl) = [22.989 769 28(2) + 35.453(2)] × 1 g/mol = 58.443(2) g/mol</dd>
<dd><i>M</i>(C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">22</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">11</sub></span>) = ([12 × 12.0107(8)] + [22 × 1.007 94(7)] + [11 × 15.9994(3)]) × 1 g/mol = 342.297(14) g/mol.</dd></dl>
<p>An average molar mass may be defined for mixtures of compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenBook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBook-1">[1]</a></sup> This is particularly important in <a href="/wiki/Polymer_science" title="Polymer science">polymer science</a>, where different polymer molecules may contain different numbers of <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> units (non-uniform polymers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_quantities">Related quantities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related quantities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molar mass is closely related to the <b>relative molar mass</b> (<i>M</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">r</sub></span>) of a compound, to the older term <b>formula weight</b> (F.W.), and to the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">standard atomic masses</a> of its constituent elements. However, it should be distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> (also known as molecular weight), which is the mass of <i>one</i> molecule (of any <i>single</i> isotopic composition) and is not directly related to the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a>, the mass of <i>one</i> atom (of any <i>single</i> isotope). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">dalton</a>, symbol Da, is also sometimes used as a unit of molar mass, especially in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, with the definition 1 Da = 1 g/mol, despite the fact that it is strictly a unit of mass (1 Da = 1 u = 1.660 538 921(73)×10<sup>−27</sup> kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CODATA2010_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CODATA2010-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Gram atomic mass</b> is another term for the mass, in grams, of one mole of atoms of that element. "Gram atom" is a former term for a mole.
</p><p><b>Molecular weight</b> (M.W.) is an older term for what is now more correctly called the <b>relative molar mass</b> (<i>M</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">r</sub></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This is a <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensionless</a> quantity (i.e., a pure number, without units) equal to the molar mass divided by the <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass_constant" title="Molar mass constant">molar mass constant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_mass">Molecular mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Molecular mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">Molecular mass</a></div>
<p>The molecular mass (<i>m</i>) is the mass of a given molecule: it is measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass units</a> (amu) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">daltons</a> (Da).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup> Different molecules of the same compound may have different molecular masses because they contain different <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of an element. The molar mass is a measure of the average molecular mass of all the molecules in a sample, and is usually the more appropriate measure when dealing with macroscopic (weigh-able) quantities of a substance.
</p><p>Molecular masses are calculated from the <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weights</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> of each <a href="/wiki/Nuclide" title="Nuclide">nuclide</a>, while molar masses are calculated from the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> of each <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a>. The atomic mass takes into account the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic distribution</a> of the element in a given sample (usually assumed to be "normal"). For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> has a molar mass of 18.0153(3) g/mol, but individual water molecules have molecular masses which range between 18.010 564 6863(15) u (<sup>1</sup>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span><sup>16</sup>O) and 22.027 7364(9) u (D<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span><sup>18</sup>O).
</p><p>The distinction between molar mass and molecular mass is important because relative molecular masses can be measured directly by <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a>, often to a precision of a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Part_per_million" title="Part per million">parts per million</a>. This is accurate enough to directly determine the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> of a molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_synthesis_usage">DNA synthesis usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: DNA synthesis usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The term <b>formula weight</b> (F.W.) has a specific meaning when used in the context of DNA synthesis: whereas an individual <a href="/wiki/Phosphoramidite" title="Phosphoramidite">phosphoramidite</a> nucleobase to be added to a DNA polymer has protecting groups and has its <i>molecular weight</i> quoted including these groups, the amount of molecular weight that is ultimately added by this nucleobase to a DNA polymer is referred to as the nucleobase's <i>formula weight</i> (i.e., the molecular weight of this nucleobase within the DNA polymer, minus protecting groups).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precision_and_uncertainties">Precision and uncertainties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Precision and uncertainties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The precision to which a molar mass is known depends on the precision of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic masses</a> from which it was calculated. Most atomic masses are known to a precision of at least one part in ten-thousand, often much better<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtWt_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AtWt-2">[2]</a></sup> (the atomic mass of <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> is a notable, and serious,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> exception). This is adequate for almost all normal uses in chemistry: it is more precise than most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_analysis" title="Chemical analysis">chemical analyses</a>, and exceeds the purity of most laboratory reagents.
</p><p>The precision of atomic masses, and hence of molar masses, is limited by the knowledge of the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic distribution</a> of the element. If a more accurate value of the molar mass is required, it is necessary to determine the isotopic distribution of the sample in question, which may be different from the standard distribution used to calculate the standard atomic mass. The isotopic distributions of the different elements in a sample are not necessarily independent of one another: for example, a sample which has been <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a> will be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopic_enrichment" title="Isotopic enrichment">enriched</a> in the lighter <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of all the elements present. This complicates the calculation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_uncertainty" title="Standard uncertainty">standard uncertainty</a> in the molar mass.
</p><p>A useful convention for normal laboratory work is to quote molar masses to two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_place" title="Decimal place">decimal places</a> for all calculations. This is more accurate than is usually required, but avoids <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rounding_error" title="Rounding error">rounding errors</a> during calculations. When the molar mass is greater than 1000 g/mol, it is rarely appropriate to use more than one decimal place. These conventions are followed in most tabulated values of molar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molar_mass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molar masses are almost never measured directly. They may be calculated from standard atomic masses, and are often listed in chemical catalogues and on <a href="/wiki/Safety_data_sheet" title="Safety data sheet">safety data sheets</a> (SDS). Molar masses typically vary between:
</p>
<dl><dd>1–238 g/mol for atoms of naturally occurring elements;</dd>
<dd>10–1000 g/mol for <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">simple chemical compounds</a>;</dd>
<dd>1000–5,000,000 g/mol for <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> fragments, etc.</dd></dl>
<p>While molar masses are almost always, in practice, calculated from atomic weights, they can also be measured in certain cases. Such measurements are much less precise than modern <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometric</a> measurements of atomic weights and molecular masses, and are of mostly historical interest. All of the procedures rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colligative_property" title="Colligative property">colligative properties</a>, and any <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> of the compound must be taken into account.
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boiling-point_elevation" title="Boiling-point elevation">Boiling-point elevation</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> of an involatile <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solute</a> is higher than that of the pure <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>, and the boiling-point elevation (Δ<i>T</i>) is directly proportional to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amount_concentration" title="Amount concentration">amount concentration</a> for dilute solutions. When the composition is expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Molality" title="Molality">molality</a>, the proportionality constant is known as the <a href="/wiki/Ebullioscopic_constant" title="Ebullioscopic constant">ebullioscopic constant</a> (<i>K</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">b</sub></span>) and is characteristic for each solvent.
