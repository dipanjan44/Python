<p>This article is about <b><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> </b>and<b> <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a></b>. Future climate change is expected to affect particular <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_SYR_2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_SYR_2007-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unchecked global warming could affect most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregions" title="Terrestrial ecoregions">terrestrial ecoregions</a>. Increasing global temperature means that ecosystems will change; some <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> are being forced out of their habitats (<a href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_global_warming" title="Extinction risk from global warming">possibly to extinction</a>) because of changing conditions, while others are flourishing. Secondary effects of global warming, such as lessened snow cover, rising sea levels, and weather changes, may influence not only human activities but also the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.
</p><p>For the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a>, experts assessed the literature on the impacts of climate change on ecosystems. Rosenzweig <i>et al</i>. (2007) concluded that over the last three decades, human-induced warming had likely had a discernible influence on many physical and biological systems (p. 81).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007) concluded, with very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confidence_(statistics)" title="Confidence (statistics)">confidence</a>, that regional temperature trends had already affected species and ecosystems around the world (p. 792).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With high confidence, they concluded that climate change would result in the extinction of many species and a reduction in the diversity of ecosystems (p. 792).
</p>
<ul><li><b>Terrestrial ecosystems and biodiversity</b>: With a warming of 3 °C, relative to 1990 levels, it is likely that global terrestrial vegetation would become a net source of carbon (Schneider <i>et al</i>., 2007:792). With high confidence, Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007:788) concluded that a global mean temperature increase of around 4 °C (above the 1990-2000 level) by 2100 would lead to major extinctions around the globe.</li>
<li><b>Marine ecosystems and biodiversity</b>: With very high confidence, Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007:792) concluded that a warming of 2 °C above 1990 levels would result in mass mortality of coral reefs globally. In addition, several studies dealing with planktonic organisms and modelling have shown that temperature plays a transcendental role in marine microbial food webs, which may have a deep influence on the biological carbon pump of marine planktonic pelagic and mesopelagic ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Freshwater ecosystems</b>: Above about a 4 °C increase in global mean temperature by 2100 (relative to 1990-2000), Schneider <i>et al</i>. (2007:789) concluded, with high confidence, that many freshwater species would become extinct.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extinction_risk_from_global_warming" title="Extinction risk from global warming">Extinction risk from global warming</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_climate_change_on_plant_biodiversity" title="Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity">Effect of climate change on plant biodiversity</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_marine_mammals" title="Effects of climate change on marine mammals">Effects of climate change on marine mammals</a></div>
<p>Studying the association between Earth climate and extinctions over the past 520 million years, scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_York" title="University of York">University of York</a> write, "The global temperatures predicted for the coming centuries may trigger a new ‘mass extinction event’, where over 50 per cent of animal and plant species would be wiped out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the species at risk are Arctic and Antarctic fauna such as <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_penguin" title="Emperor penguin">emperor penguins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the Arctic, the waters of <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> are ice-free for three weeks longer than they were thirty years ago, affecting <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bears</a>, which prefer to hunt on sea ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Species that rely on cold weather conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Gyrfalcon" title="Gyrfalcon">gyrfalcons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Snowy_owl" title="Snowy owl">snowy owls</a> that prey on lemmings that use the cold winter to their advantage may be hit hard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Marine invertebrates enjoy peak growth at the temperatures they have adapted to, regardless of how cold these may be, and <a href="/wiki/Poikilotherm" title="Poikilotherm">cold-blooded</a> animals found at greater <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> generally grow faster to compensate for the short growing season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Warmer-than-ideal conditions result in higher <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and consequent reductions in body size despite increased foraging, which in turn elevates the risk of <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a>. Indeed, even a slight increase in temperature during development impairs growth efficiency and survival rate in <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_trout" title="Rainbow trout">rainbow trout</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising temperatures are beginning to have a noticeable impact on birds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a> have shifted their ranges northward by 200 km in Europe and North America. Plants lag behind, and larger animals' migration is slowed down by cities and roads. In Britain, spring butterflies are appearing an average of 6 days earlier than two decades ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2002 article in <i>Nature</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> surveyed the scientific literature to find recent changes in range or seasonal behaviour by plant and animal species. Of species showing recent change, 4 out of 5 shifted their ranges towards the poles or higher altitudes, creating "refugee species". Frogs were breeding, flowers blossoming and birds migrating an average 2.3 days earlier each decade; butterflies, birds and plants moving towards the poles by 6.1 km per decade. A 2005 study concludes human activity is the cause of the temperature rise and resultant changing species behaviour, and links these effects with the predictions of <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate models</a> to provide validation for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Scientists have observed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_hair_grass" title="Antarctic hair grass">Antarctic hair grass</a> is colonizing areas of Antarctica where previously their survival range was limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mechanistic studies have documented <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinctions</a> due to recent climate change: McLaughlin <i>et al.</i> documented two populations of <a href="/wiki/Bay_checkerspot_butterfly" title="Bay checkerspot butterfly">Bay checkerspot butterfly</a> being threatened by precipitation change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-22">[22]</a></sup>
Parmesan states, "Few studies have been conducted at a scale that encompasses an entire species"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and McLaughlin <i>et al.</i> agreed "few mechanistic studies have linked extinctions to recent climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-22">[22]</a></sup> Daniel Botkin and other authors in one study believe that projected rates of extinction are overestimated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  For "recent" extinctions, see <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a>.
</p><p>Many species of freshwater and saltwater plants and animals are dependent on glacier-fed waters to ensure a cold water habitat that they have adapted to. Some species of freshwater fish need cold water to survive and to reproduce, and this is especially true with <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cutthroat_trout" title="Cutthroat trout">cutthroat trout</a>. Reduced glacier runoff can lead to insufficient stream flow to allow these species to thrive. Ocean <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>, a cornerstone species, prefer cold water and are the primary food source for aquatic mammals such as the <a href="/wiki/Blue_whale" title="Blue whale">blue whale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Alterations to the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>, due to increased freshwater inputs from glacier melt, and the potential alterations to <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> of the worlds oceans, may affect existing fisheries upon which humans depend as well.
</p><p>The <i>white lemuroid possum</i>, only found in the Daintree mountain forests of northern Queensland, may be the first mammal species to be driven extinct by global warming in Australia. In 2008, the white possum has not been seen in over three years. The possums cannot survive extended temperatures over 30 °C (86 °F), which occurred in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 27-year study of the largest colony of <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_penguin" title="Magellanic penguin">Magellanic penguins</a> in the world, published in 2014, found that extreme weather caused by climate change is responsible for killing 7% of penguin chicks per year on average, and in some years studied climate change accounted for up to 50% of all chick deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fountain_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fountain-28">[28]</a></sup> Since 1987, the number of breeding pairs in the colony has reduced by 24%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fountain_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fountain-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Climate change is leading to a mismatch between the <a href="/wiki/Snow_camouflage" title="Snow camouflage">snow camouflage</a> of arctic animals such as <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe_hare" title="Snowshoe hare">snowshoe hares</a> with the increasingly snow-free landscape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forests">Forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">Wildfire</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2945" data-file-width="4119" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif/250px-Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif/375px-Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif/500px-Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northern_Forest_Trend_in_Photosynthetic_Activity.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Change in Photosynthetic Activity in Northern Forests 1982-2003; <a href="/wiki/NASA_Earth_Observatory" title="NASA Earth Observatory">NASA Earth Observatory</a></div></div></div>
<p>Pine forests in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> have been devastated by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pine_beetle" title="Pine beetle">pine beetle</a> infestation, which has expanded unhindered since 1998 at least in part due to the lack of severe winters since that time; a few days of extreme cold kill most mountain pine beetles and have kept outbreaks in the past naturally contained. The infestation, which (by November 2008) has killed about half of the province's lodgepole pines (33 million acres or 135,000 km²)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robbins_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-robbins-31">[31]</a></sup> is an order of magnitude larger than any previously recorded outbreak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kurz_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-kurz-32">[32]</a></sup> One reason for unprecedented host tree mortality may be due to that the mountain pine beetles have higher reproductive success in lodgepole pine trees growing in areas where the trees have not experienced frequent beetle epidemics, which includes much of the current outbreak area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cudmore_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cudmore-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2007 the outbreak spread, via unusually strong winds, over the continental divide to <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>. An epidemic also started, be it at a lower rate, in 1999 in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>. The United States forest service predicts that between 2011 and 2013 virtually all 5 million acres (20,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of Colorado’s lodgepole pine trees over five inches (127 mm) in diameter will be lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robbins_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-robbins-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>As the northern forests are a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a>, while dead forests are a major carbon source, the loss of such large areas of forest has a positive feedback on global warming. In the worst years, the carbon emission due to beetle infestation of forests in British Columbia alone approaches that of an average year of forest fires in all of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> or five years worth of emissions from that country's transportation sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kurz_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-kurz-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Besides the immediate ecological and economic impact, the huge dead forests provide a fire risk. Even many healthy forests appear to face an increased risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fire" title="Forest fire">forest fires</a> because of warming climates. The 10-year average of boreal forest burned in North America, after several decades of around 10,000 km² (2.5 million acres), has increased steadily since 1970 to more than 28,000 km² (7 million acres) annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Though this change may be due in part to changes in forest management practices, in the western U.S., since 1986, longer, warmer summers have resulted in a fourfold increase of major wildfires and a sixfold increase in the area of forest burned, compared to the period from 1970 to 1986. A similar increase in wildfire activity has been reported in Canada from 1920 to 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Forest fires in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> have dramatically increased since 1997 as well. These fires are often actively started to clear forest for agriculture. They can set fire to the large peat bogs in the region and the CO₂released by these peat bog fires has been estimated, in an average year, to be 15% of the quantity of CO₂produced by fossil fuel combustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2018 study found that trees grow faster due to increased carbon dioxide levels, however, the trees are also eight to twelve percent lighter and denser since 1900. The authors note, "Even though a greater volume of wood is being produced today, it now contains less material than just a few decades ago."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountains">Mountains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mountains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">Mountains</a> cover approximately 25 percent of earth's surface and provide a home to more than one-tenth of global human population. Changes in global climate pose a number of potential risks to mountain habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Researchers expect that over time, climate change will affect mountain and lowland ecosystems, the frequency and intensity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">forest fires</a>, the diversity of wildlife, and the distribution of water.
</p><p>Studies suggest that a warmer climate in the United States would cause lower-elevation habitats to expand into the higher alpine zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Such a shift would encroach on the rare alpine meadows and other high-altitude habitats. High-elevation plants and animals have limited space available for new habitat as they move higher on the mountains in order to adapt to long-term changes in regional climate.
</p><p>Changes in climate will also affect the depth of the mountains snowpacks and glaciers. Any changes in their seasonal melting can have powerful impacts on areas that rely on freshwater <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> from mountains. Rising temperature may cause snow to melt earlier and faster in the spring and shift the timing and distribution of runoff. These changes could affect the availability of freshwater for natural systems and human uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_productivity">Ecological productivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecological productivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>According to a paper by Smith and Hitz (2003:66), it is reasonable to assume that the relationship between increased global mean temperature and ecosystem productivity is <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabolic</a>. Higher carbon dioxide concentrations will favourably affect plant growth and demand for water. Higher temperatures could initially be favourable for plant growth. Eventually, increased growth would peak then decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>According to IPCC (2007:11), a global average temperature increase exceeding 1.5–2.5 °C (relative to the period 1980–99), would likely have a predominantly negative impact on ecosystem goods and services, e.g., water and food supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li>Research done by the <a class="external text" href="http://pages.unibas.ch/botschoen/scc/index.shtml" rel="nofollow">Swiss Canopy Crane Project</a> suggests that slow-growing trees only are stimulated in growth for a short period under higher CO<sub>2</sub> levels, while faster growing plants like <a href="/wiki/Liana" title="Liana">liana</a> benefit in the long term. In general, but especially in <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, this means that liana become the prevalent species; and because they decompose much faster than trees their carbon content is more quickly returned to the atmosphere. Slow growing trees incorporate atmospheric carbon for decades.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Species_migration">Species migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Species migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, a <a href="/wiki/Gray_whale" title="Gray whale">gray whale</a> was found in the Mediterranean Sea, even though the species had not been seen in the North Atlantic Ocean since the 18th century.  The whale is thought to have migrated from the Pacific Ocean via the Arctic.  Climate Change &amp; European Marine Ecosystem Research (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CLAMER&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CLAMER (page does not exist)">CLAMER</a>) has also reported that the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neodenticula_seminae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neodenticula seminae (page does not exist)">Neodenticula seminae</a> alga has been found in the North Atlantic, where it had gone extinct nearly 800,000 years ago.  The alga has drifted from the Pacific Ocean through the Arctic, following the reduction in polar ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Siberian <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a>, species migration is contributing to another warming albedo-feedback, as needle-shedding larch trees are being replaced with dark-foliage evergreen conifers which can absorb some of the solar radiation that previously reflected off the snowpack beneath the forest canopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> It has been projected many fish species will migrate towards the North and South poles as a result of climate change, and that many species of fish near the Equator will go extinct as a result of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Migratory birds are especially at risk for endangerment due to the extreme dependability on temperature and air pressure for migration, foraging, growth, and reproduction. Much research has been done on the effects of climate change on birds, both for future predictions and for conservation. The species said to be most at risk for endangerment or extinction are populations that are not of conservation concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> It is predicted that a 3.5 degree increase in surface temperature will occur by year 2100, which could result in between 600 and 900 extinctions, which mainly will occur in the tropical environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_and_ecosystems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Droughts have been occurring more frequently because of global warming and they are expected to become more frequent and intense in Africa, southern Europe, the Middle East, most of the Americas, Australia, and Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Their impacts are aggravated because of increased water demand, population growth, urban expansion, and environmental protection efforts in many areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Droughts result in crop failures and the loss of pasture grazing land for livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_corn_prices.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="449" data-file-width="610" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/North_American_corn_prices.png/220px-North_American_corn_prices.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/North_American_corn_prices.png/330px-North_American_corn_prices.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/North_American_corn_prices.png/440px-North_American_corn_prices.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_American_corn_prices.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Price of corn in North America, in U.S. dollars per bushel, 2004-2012.</div></div></div>
<p>Droughts are becoming more frequent and intense in arid and semiarid western North America as temperatures have been rising, advancing the timing and magnitude of spring snow melt floods and reducing river flow volume in summer. Direct effects of climate change include increased heat and water stress, altered crop <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a>, and disrupted symbiotic interactions. These effects may be exacerbated by climate changes in river flow, and the combined effects are likely to reduce the abundance of native trees in favor of non-native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> and drought-tolerant competitors, reduce the habitat quality for many native animals, and slow litter decomposition and nutrient cycling. Climate change effects on human water demand and irrigation may intensify these effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> By 2012, North American corn prices had risen to a record $8.34 per bushel in August, leaving 20 of the 211 U.S. ethanol fuel plants idle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usacorn_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-usacorn-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
