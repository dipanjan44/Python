<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Public opinion on global warming</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>Public opinion on global warming</b> is the aggregate of <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitudes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">beliefs</a> held by the adult population concerning the science, economics, and politics of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. It is affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_coverage_of_climate_change" title="Media coverage of climate change">media coverage of climate change</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_individual_opinion">Influences on individual opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Influences on individual opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_region">Geographic region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a list of countries and their opinion, see <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_opinion_by_country" title="Climate change opinion by country">Climate change opinion by country</a>.</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:224px;max-width:224px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:222px;max-width:222px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png"><img alt="United States, Europe, and Australia are the darkest while Africa, the Middle East, and Oceania are the lightest." data-file-height="1640" data-file-width="3600" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png/220px-Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png/330px-Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png/440px-Climate_change_awareness_by_country_2008-2009.png 2x" width="220"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Proportion reporting knowing "something" or "a great deal" about global warming in 2007–08. <p>A 2007–2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallup_Poll" title="Gallup Poll">Gallup Poll</a> surveyed individuals in 128 countries. This poll queried whether the respondent knew of global warming. Those who had a basic concept of global warming didn’t necessarily connect it to human activities, revealing that a knowledge of global warming and the knowledge that it’s human-induced are two separate things. Over a third of the world's population were unaware of global warming. <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">Developing countrie</a>s have less awareness than <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed</a>, and Africa the least aware. Of those aware, residents of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and developed countries in Asia led the belief that climate change is a result of human activities while Africa, parts of Asia and the Middle East, and a few countries from the <a href="/wiki/Post-Soviet_states" title="Post-Soviet states">former Soviet Union</a> led in the opposite. Opinion within the United Kingdom was divided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelham2009Awareness_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelham2009Awareness-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first major worldwide poll, conducted by Gallup in 2008–2009 in 127 countries, found that some 62% of people worldwide said they knew about global warming. In the <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">industrialized countries</a> of North America, Europe and Japan, 90% or more knew about it (97% in the U.S., 99% in Japan); in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, especially in Africa, fewer than a quarter knew about it, although many had noticed local weather changes. Among those who knew about global warming, there was a wide variation between nations in belief that the warming was a result of human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adults in Asia, with the exception of those in developed countries, are the least likely to perceive global warming as a threat. In the western world, individuals are the most likely to be aware and perceive it as a very or somewhat serious threat to themselves and their families;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuglieseJulie2009AwarenessThreat_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PuglieseJulie2009AwarenessThreat-3">[3]</a></sup> although Europeans are more concerned about climate change than those in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the public in Africa, where individuals are the most vulnerable to global warming while producing the least carbon dioxide, is the least aware – which translates into a low perception that it is a threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuglieseJulie2009AwarenessThreat_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PuglieseJulie2009AwarenessThreat-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>These variations pose a challenge to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policymaker" title="Policymaker">policymakers</a>, as different countries travel down different paths, making an agreement over an appropriate response difficult. While Africa may be the most vulnerable and produce the least amount of greenhouse gases, they are the most ambivalent. The top five emitters (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PRC" title="PRC">China</a>, the United States, India, Russia, and Japan), who together emit half the world's greenhouse gases, vary in both awareness and concern. The United States, Russia, and Japan are the most aware at over 85% of the population. Conversely, only two-thirds of people in China and one-third in India are aware. Japan expresses the greatest concern, which translates into support for environmental policies. People in China, Russia, and the United States, while varying in awareness, have expressed a similar proportion of aware individuals concerned. Similarly, those aware in India are likely to be concerned, but India faces challenges spreading this concern to the remaining population as its energy needs increase over the next decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>An online survey on environmental questions conducted in 20 countries by Ipsos MORI, "Global Trends 2014", shows broad agreement, especially on climate change and if it is caused by humans, though the U.S. ranked lowest with 54% agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It has been suggested that the low U.S. ranking is tied to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial#Denial_networks" title="Climate change denial">denial campaigns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2010 survey of 14 <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">industrialized countries</a> found that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_skeptics" title="Climate change skeptics">skepticism</a> about the danger of global warming was highest in Australia, Norway, New Zealand and the United States, in that order, correlating positively with <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">per capita emissions of carbon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_United_States" title="Climate change in the United States">Climate change in the United States</a></div>
<p>In 2009 <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> conducted a study identifying global warming's 'Six Americas'. The report identifies six audiences with different opinions about global warning: The alarmed (18%), the concerned (33%), the cautious (19%), the disengaged (12%), the doubtful (11%) and the dismissive (7%). The alarmed and concerned make out the largest percentage and think something should be done about global warming. The cautious, disengaged and doubtful are less likely to take action. The dismissive are convinced global warming is not happening. These audiences can be used to define the best approaches for environmental action. The theory of the 'Six Americas' is also used for marketing purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Opinions in the United States vary intensely enough to be considered a <a href="/wiki/Culture_war" title="Culture war">culture war</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a January 2013 survey, Pew found that 69% of Americans say there is solid evidence that the Earth's average temperature has gotten warmer over the past few decades, up six points since November 2011 and 12 points since 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>A Gallup poll in 2014 concluded that 51 percent of Americans were a little or not at all worried about climate change, 24 percent a great deal and 25 percent a fair amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riffkin_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riffkin-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, 32 percent or Americans were worried about global warming as a great deal, 37 percent in 2016, and 45 percent in 2017. A poll taken in 2016 shows that 52% of Americans believe climate change to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Causes_of_global_warming" title="Causes of global warming">caused by human activity</a>, while 34% state it is caused by natural changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Data is increasingly showing that 62 percent of Americans believe that the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a> are happening now in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 <a href="/wiki/Gallup_(company)" title="Gallup (company)">GALLUP</a> found that 64% of Americans are worried about global warming, 59% believed that global warming is already happening and 65% is convinced that global warming is caused by human activities. These numbers show that awareness of global warming is increasing in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In countries varying in awareness, an educational gap translates into a gap in awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gap_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-gap-17">[17]</a></sup> However an increase in awareness does not always result in an increase in perceived threat. In China, 98% of those who have completed four years or more of college education reported knowing something or a great deal of climate change while only 63% of those who have completed nine years of education reported the same. Despite the differences in awareness in China, all groups perceive a low level of threat from global warming. In India those who are educated are more likely to be aware, and those who are educated there are far more likely to report perceiving global warming as a threat than those who are not educated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries-5">[5]</a></sup> In Europe, individuals who have attained a higher level of education perceive climate change as a serious threat. There is also a strong association between education and Internet use. Europeans who use the Internet more are more likely to perceive climate change as a serious threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurobarometer2009p13_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurobarometer2009p13-18">[18]</a></sup> However, a survey of American adults found that "...there is in fact little disagreement among culturally diverse citizens<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> on what science knows about climate change. The source of the climate-change controversy and like disputes over societal risks is the contamination of the science-communication environment with forms of cultural status competition that make it impossible for diverse citizens to express their reason as both collective-knowledge acquirers and cultural-identity protectors at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Residential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">demographics</a> affect perceptions of global warming. In China, 77% of those who live in urban areas are aware of global warming compared to 52% in rural areas. This trend is mirrored in India with 49% to 29% awareness, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-PuglieseJulie2009TopEmittingCountries-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the countries where at least half the population is aware of global warming, those with the majority who believe that global warming is due to human activities have a greater national GDP per unit energy—or, a greater energy efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelham2009Energy_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelham2009Energy-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Europe, individuals under fifty-five are more likely to perceive both "poverty, lack of food and drinking water" and climate change as a serious threat than individuals over fifty-five. Male individuals are more likely to perceive climate change as a threat than female individuals. Managers, white collar workers, and students are more likely to perceive climate change as a greater threat than house persons and retired individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurobarometer2009p13_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eurobarometer2009p13-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, conservative white men are more likely than other Americans to deny climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_identification">Political identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, support for environmental protection was relatively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-partisan" title="Non-partisan">non-partisan</a> in the twentieth century. Republican <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> established national parks whereas Democrat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a> established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_Conservation_Service" title="Soil Conservation Service">Soil Conservation Service</a>. Republican <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> was instrumental in founding the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>, and tried to install a third pillar of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> dealing with environmental challenges such as acid rain and greenhouse effect. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patrick_Moynihan" title="Daniel Patrick Moynihan">Daniel Patrick Moynihan</a> was Nixon's NATO delegate for the topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hüne_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>This non-partisanship began to erode during the 1980s, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> described environmental protection as an economic burden. Views over global warming began to seriously diverge between Democrats and Republicans during the negotiations that led up to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> in 1998. In a 2008 Gallup poll of the American public, 76% of Democrats and only 41% of Republicans said that they believed global warming was already happening. The opinions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_elite" title="Political elite">political elites</a>, such as members of Congress, tends to be even more polarized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlap2008RepublicanDemocraticGap_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlap2008RepublicanDemocraticGap-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Europe, opinion is not strongly divided among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-right_politics" title="Left-right politics">left and right parties</a>. Although European political parties on the left, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_parties" title="Green parties">Green parties</a>, strongly support measures to address climate change, conservative European political parties maintain similar sentiments, most notably in Western and Northern Europe. For example, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, never a friend of the coal mining industry, was a strong supporter of an active climate protection policy and was instrumental in founding the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> and the British <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Some speeches, as to the Royal Society on 27 September 1988<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and to the UN general assembly in November 1989 helped to put climate change, acid rain, and general pollution in the British mainstream.. After her career, however, Thatcher was less of a climate activist, as she called climate action a "marvelous excuse for supranational socialism," and called Al Gore an "apocalyptic hyperbole".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> France's center-right <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">President Chirac</a> pushed key environmental and climate change policies in France in 2005–2007. Conservative German administrations (under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a>) in the past two decades have supported <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> climate change initiatives; concern about <a href="/wiki/Forest_dieback" title="Forest dieback">forest dieback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> regulation were initiated under Kohl's archconservative minister of the interior <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Zimmermann" title="Friedrich Zimmermann">Friedrich Zimmermann</a>. In the period after former President George W. Bush announced that the United States was leaving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_Treaty" title="Kyoto Treaty">Kyoto Treaty</a>, European media and newspapers on both the left and right criticized the move. The conservative Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/La_Raz%C3%B3n_(Madrid)" title="La Razón (Madrid)">La Razón</a></i>, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Times" title="Irish Times">Irish Times</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Irish_Independent" title="Irish Independent">Irish Independent</a></i>, the Danish <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlingske_Tidende" title="Berlingske Tidende">Berlingske Tidende</a></i>, and the Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Kathimerini" title="Kathimerini">Kathimerini</a></i> all condemned the Bush administration's decision, as did left-leaning newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchreursAndTiberghien2007_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchreursAndTiberghien2007-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Norway, a 2013 poll conducted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TNS_Gallup" title="TNS Gallup">TNS Gallup</a> found that 92% of those who vote for the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Left_Party_(Norway)" title="Socialist Left Party (Norway)">Socialist Left Party</a> and 89% of those who vote for the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Norway)" title="Liberal Party (Norway)">Liberal Party</a> believe that global warming is caused by humans, while the percentage who held this belief is 60% among voters for the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(Norway)" title="Conservative Party (Norway)">Conservative Party</a> and 41% among voters for the <a href="/wiki/Progress_Party_(Norway)" title="Progress Party (Norway)">Progress Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The shared sentiments between the political left and right on climate change further illustrate the divide in perception between the United States and Europe on climate change. As an example, conservative German Prime Ministers <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> have differed with other parties in Germany only on how to meet emissions reduction targets, not whether or not to establish or fulfill them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchreursAndTiberghien2007_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-SchreursAndTiberghien2007-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2017 study found that those who changed their opinion on climate change between 2010 and 2014 did so "primarily to align better with those who shared their party identification and political ideology. This conforms with the theory of motivated reasoning: Evidence consistent with prior beliefs is viewed as strong and, on politically salient issues, people strive to bring their opinions into conformance with those who share their political identity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_risk_assessment_and_assignment">Individual risk assessment and assignment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Individual risk assessment and assignment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_and_global_warming" title="Ozone depletion and global warming">Ozone depletion and global warming</a></div>
<p>The IPCC attempts to orchestrate global (climate) change research to shape a worldwide consensus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, the consensus approach has been dubbed more a liability than an asset in comparison to other environmental challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The linear model of policy-making, based on a <i>more knowledge we have, the better the political response will be</i> is said to have not been working and is in the meantime rejected by sociology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLT_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLT-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sheldon Ungar, a Canadian sociologist, compares the different public reactions towards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_and_global_warming" title="Ozone depletion and global warming">ozone depletion and global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-36">[36]</a></sup> The public opinion failed to tie climate change to concrete events which could be used as a threshold or beacon to signify immediate danger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-36">[36]</a></sup> Scientific predictions of a temperature rise of two to three degrees Celsius over several decades do not respond with people, e.g. in North America, that experience similar swings during a single day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-36">[36]</a></sup> As scientists define global warming a problem of the future, a liability in "<a href="/wiki/Attention_economy" title="Attention economy">attention economy</a>", pessimistic outlooks in general and assigning extreme weather to climate change have often been discredited or ridiculed (compare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gore_effect" title="Gore effect">Gore effect</a>) in the public arena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> While the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_se_(Latin)" title="Per se (Latin)">per se</a></i> is essential for life on earth, the case was quite different with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_shield" title="Ozone shield">ozone shield</a> and other metaphors about the ozone depletion. The scientific assessment of the ozone problem also had large uncertainties. But the metaphors used in the discussion (ozone shield, ozone hole) reflected better with lay people and their concerns.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The idea of rays penetrating a damaged “shield” meshes nicely with abiding and resonant cultural motifs, including “Hollywood affinities.” These range from the shields on the Starship Enterprise to Star Wars, ... It is these pre-scientific bridging metaphors built around the penetration of a deteriorating shield that render the ozone problem relatively simple. That the ozone threat can be linked with Darth Vader means that it is encompassed in common sense understandings that are deeply ingrained and widely shared. (Sheldon Ungar 2000)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) regulation attempts of the end of the 1980s profited from those easy-to-grasp metaphors and the personal risk assumptions taken from them. As well the fate of celebrities like President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>, which had skin cancer removal in 1985 and 1987, was of high importance. In case of the public opinion on climate change, no imminent danger is perceived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ungar_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ungar-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology">Ideology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ideology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a> is an effective predictor of <a href="/wiki/Party_identification" title="Party identification">party identification</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservatives</a> are more prevalent among <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republicans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moderate" title="Moderate">moderates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberals</a> among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(voter)" title="Independent (voter)">independents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  A shift in ideology is often associated with in a shift in political views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad2009ConservativeTopIdeologicalGroup_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad2009ConservativeTopIdeologicalGroup-39">[39]</a></sup> For example, when the number of conservatives rose from 2008 to 2009, the number of individuals who felt that global warming was being exaggerated in the media also rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad2009GlobalWarmingExaggerated_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad2009GlobalWarmingExaggerated-40">[40]</a></sup>
The 2006 BBC World Service poll found that when asked about various policy options to reduce greenhouse gas emissions - tax incentives for alternative energy research and development, installment of taxes to encourage energy conservation, and reliance on nuclear energy to reduce fossil fuels. The majority of those asked felt that tax incentives were the path of action that they preferred.
</p><p>As of May 2016, polls have repeatedly found that a majority of Republican voters, particularly young ones, believe the government should take action to reduce carbon dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pursuit of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy#Green_energy_and_green_power" title="Sustainable energy">green energy</a> is an ideology that defines <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_dams" title="Hydroelectric dams">hydroelectric dams</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_power_plant" title="Natural gas power plant">natural gas power plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> as unacceptable <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energies</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining#Production" title="Coal mining">eight billion tons of coal</a> burnt each year. While there is popular support for wind, solar, biomass, and geothermal energy, all these sources combined only supplied 1.3% of global energy in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charts">Charts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Charts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2018 study found that individuals were more likely to accept that global temperatures were increasing if they were shown the information in a chart rather than in text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">Scientific opinion on climate change</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> on climate change exists, as recognized by national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences" title="Academy of Sciences">academies of science</a> and other authoritative bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JointScienceAcademies2005_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-JointScienceAcademies2005-47">[47]</a></sup>  The opinion gap between scientists and the public in 2009 stands at 84% to 49% that global temperatures are increasing because of human-activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PewResearchCenter2009PublicPraiseScientistsFault_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-PewResearchCenter2009PublicPraiseScientistsFault-48">[48]</a></sup> However, more recent research has identified substantial geographical variation in the public's understanding of the scientific consensus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_global_warming" title="Economics of global warming">Economics of global warming</a></div>
<p>Economic debates weigh the benefits of limiting industrial emissions of mitigating global warming against the costs that such changes would entail. While there is a greater amount of agreement over whether global warming exists, there is less agreement over the appropriate response. Electric or petroleum distribution may be government owned or utilities may be regulated by government. The government owned or regulated utilities may, or may not choose to make lower emissions a priority over economics, in unregulated counties industry follows economic priorities. An example of the economic priority is <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell#Environmental_pollution" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell PLC</a> reporting CO2 emissions of 81 million metric tonnes in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_coverage_of_climate_change" title="Media coverage of climate change">Media coverage of climate change</a></div>
<p>The popular media in the U.S. gives greater attention to skeptics relative to the scientific community as a whole, and the level of agreement within the scientific community has not been accurately communicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> US popular media coverage differs from that presented in other countries, where reporting is more consistent with the scientific literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Some journalists attribute the difference to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">climate change denial</a> being propagated, mainly in the US, by business-centered organizations employing tactics worked out previously by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_lobby" title="Tobacco lobby">US tobacco lobby</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The efforts of <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> and other environmental campaigns have focused on the effects of global warming and have managed to increase awareness and concern, but despite these efforts as of 2007, the number of Americans believing humans are the cause of global warming was holding steady at 61%, and those believing the popular media was understating the issue remained about 35%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad2007HollywoodHeathUpConcern_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad2007HollywoodHeathUpConcern-57">[57]</a></sup>  Between 2010 and 2013, the number of Americans who believe the media under-reports the seriousness of global warming has been increasing, and the number who think media over-states it has been falling.   According to a 2013 Gallup US opinion poll, 57% believe global warming is at least as bad as portrayed in the media (with 33% thinking media has downplayed global warming and 24% saying coverage is accurate).  Less than half of Americans (41%) think the problem is not as bad as media portrays it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>September 2011 <a href="/wiki/Angus_Reid_Public_Opinion" title="Angus Reid Public Opinion">Angus Reid Public Opinion</a> poll found that Britons (43%) are less likely than Americans (49%) or Canadians (52%) to say that "global warming is a fact and is mostly caused by emissions from vehicles and industrial facilities."  The same poll found that 20% of Americans, 20% of Britons and 14% of Canadians think "global warming is a theory that has not yet been proven."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>A March 2013 <a href="/wiki/Public_Policy_Polling" title="Public Policy Polling">Public Policy Polling</a> poll about widespread and infamous <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">conspiracy theories</a> found that 37% of American voters believe that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_conspiracy_theory" title="Global warming conspiracy theory">global warming is a hoax</a>, while 51% do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2013 poll in Norway  conducted by TNS Gallup found that 66% of the population believe that climate change is caused by humans, while 17% do not believe this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_opinion_on_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">Politics of global warming</a></div>
<p>Public opinion impacts on the issue of climate change because governments need willing electorates and citizens in order to implement policies that address climate change. Further, when climate change perceptions differ between the populace and governments, the communication of risk to the public becomes problematic. Finally, a public that is not aware of the issues surrounding climate change may resist or oppose climate change policies, which is of considerable importance to politicians and state leaders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LorenzoniAndPidgeon2006_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-LorenzoniAndPidgeon2006-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Public support for action to forestall global warming is as strong as public support has been historically for many other government actions; however, it is not "intense" in the sense that it overrides other priorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LorenzoniAndPidgeon2006_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-LorenzoniAndPidgeon2006-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fix36-46_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fix36-46-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2009 Eurobarometer survey found that, on the average, Europeans rate climate change as the second most serious problem facing the world today, between "poverty, the lack of food and drinking water" and "a major global economic downturn." 87% of Europeans consider climate change to be a "serious" or "very serious" problem, while 10% "do not consider it a serious problem." However, the proportion who believe it to be a problem has dropped in the period 2008/9 when the surveys were conducted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> While the small majority believe climate change is a serious threat, 55% percent believe the EU is doing too little and 30% believe the EU is going the right amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> As a result of European Union climate change perceptions, "climate change is an issue that has reached such a level of social and political acceptability across the EU that it enables (indeed, forces) the EU Commission and national leaders to produce all sorts of measures, including taxes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchreursAndTiberghien2007_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-SchreursAndTiberghien2007-29">[29]</a></sup> Despite the persistent high level of personal involvement of European citizens, found in another Eurobarometer survey in 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> EU leaders have begun to downscale climate policy issues on the political agenda since the beginning of the Eurozone crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The proportion of Americans who believe that the effects of global warming have begun or will begin in a few years rose to a peak in 2008 where it then declined, and a similar trend was found regarding the belief that global warming is a threat to their lifestyle within their lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newman2010AmericanConcernDrop_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newman2010AmericanConcernDrop-68">[68]</a></sup> Concern over global warming often corresponds with economic downturns and national crisis such as 9/11 as Americans prioritize the economy and national security over environmental concerns. However the drop in concern in 2008 is unique compared to other environmental issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad2009GlobalWarmingExaggerated_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saad2009GlobalWarmingExaggerated-40">[40]</a></sup> Considered in the context of environmental issues, Americans consider global warming as a less critical concern than the pollution of rivers, lakes, and drinking water; toxic waste; fresh water needs; air pollution; damage to the ozone layer; and the loss of tropical rain forests. However, Americans prioritize global warming over species extinction and acid rain issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Since 2000 the partisan gap has grown as Republican and Democratic views diverge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunlap2008PartisanGap_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlap2008PartisanGap-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
