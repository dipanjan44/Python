<p>The <b>People's Climate March</b> (PCM) was a large-scale <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activist</a> event to advocate global action against <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, which took place on Sunday, September 21, 2014, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, along with a series of companion actions worldwide, many of which also took the name People's Climate March. With an estimated 311,000 participants, the New York event was the largest climate change march in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-1">[1]</a></sup>  Described as "an invitation to change everything," the march was called in May 2014 by <a href="/wiki/350.org" title="350.org">350.org</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avaaz" title="Avaaz">Avaaz</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental</a> organization founded by writer/activist <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brodine_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brodine-2">[2]</a></sup> and it was endorsed by "over 1,500 organizations, including many international and national unions, churches, schools and community and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental justice</a> organizations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was conceived as a response to (but <i>not</i> a protest against)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCM_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCM-4">[4]</a></sup> the scheduled U.N. <a href="/wiki/Climate_Summit" title="Climate Summit">Climate Summit</a> of world leaders to take place in New York City two days later, on September 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKibben_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKibben-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although based in New York, the event was global in scope and implication, with "companion demonstrations" worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brodine_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brodine-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Organizers intended the march to be "the largest single event on climate that has been organized to date… one so large and diverse that it cannot be ignored."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCM_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCM-4">[4]</a></sup> The entire PCM project consisted of "numerous events, actions, symposia, presentations, and more organized over the course of the days leading up to the Summit, and in the days following," of which the march was intended to be "the anchor event."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCM_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-PCM-4">[4]</a></sup> Worldwide, nearly 600,000 people were estimated to have marched on September 21, including those in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2014-09-22_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-2014-09-22-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_invitation_to_the_march">Public invitation to the march</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Public invitation to the march">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 21, McKibben published an article on the website of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine (later appearing in the magazine's print issue of June 5), entitled "A Call to Arms,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKibben_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-McKibben-5">[5]</a></sup> which invited readers to a major climate march in New York City for the weekend of September 20–21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[note 1]</a></sup> In the article, McKibben calls climate change "the biggest crisis our civilization has ever faced," and predicts that the march will be "the largest demonstration yet of human resolve in the face of climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKibben_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-McKibben-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>After criticizing world leaders, including <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">President Obama</a>, for not moving fast enough or going far enough to combat climate change, McKibben cites increasing evidence of environmental deterioration, including the melting of Arctic and Antarctic ice, the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidification</a> of the oceans, and violent weather and quotes one climate scientist as exclaiming "We're all sitting ducks." He blames this state of affairs primarily on the fossil-fuel industry, which "by virtue of being perhaps the richest enterprise in human history, has been able to delay effective action, almost to the point where it's too late." Although he claims that local, small-scale activism is crucial, the global climate justice movement sometimes "needs to come together and show the world how big it's gotten," and to allow for "opening up space for change." Writes McKibben: "A loud movement – one that gives our 'leaders' permission to actually lead, and then scares them into doing so – is the only hope of upending" the "prophecy" that it's already too late to reverse the problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKibben_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-McKibben-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Staging_area_and_route">Staging area and route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Staging area and route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg/300px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg/450px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg/600px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March-.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view of one section of the march waiting to start, north of Columbus Circle on Broadway, NYC</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Staging_area" title="Staging area">staging area</a> in which marchers for the New York City event were to assemble on the morning of September 21, as agreed upon by the march organizers and local authorities, was located in a blocked-off area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Park_West" title="Central Park West">Central Park West</a> between 59th and 86th Streets, north of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan's</a> <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Circle" title="Columbus Circle">Columbus Circle</a>, which is the official starting point of the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup> The area was divided into six sections, and participating groups were to gather in the particular section appropriate to the mission and purpose of each group. These were:
</p>
<ul><li>"Frontline" groups ("Frontlines of Crisis, Forefront of Change"), located between 59th and 65th Streets – This area includes those people who are first and most impacted by climate change, including <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a>, environmental justice organizations and other communities;</li>
<li>Generational groups ("We Can Build the Future"), located between 65th and 72nd Streets – This area includes <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">labor</a> organizations, families – parents and their children, students, elders, etc.;</li>
<li>Environmental groups ("We Have Solutions"), located between 72nd and 77th Streets – This area includes <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food and water justice</a> and general <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">environmental organizations</a>;</li>
<li>Protest groups ("We Know Who Is Responsible"), located between 77th and 81st Streets – This area includes <a href="/wiki/Anti-corporate_activism" title="Anti-corporate activism">anti-corporate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace and justice</a> groups as well as similar organizations;</li>
<li><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg/300px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg/450px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg/600px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--4.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Participating students march down Central Park West.</div></div></div>Science ("The Debate Is Over"), located between 81st and 82nd Streets – This area includes scientists, <a href="/wiki/Interfaith_dialogue" title="Interfaith dialogue">interfaith groups</a> and related organizations;</li>
<li>Interfaith group, (Lutheran, Hindu), located on 58th Street between 8th and 9th Avenues.</li>
<li>Miscellaneous groups ("To Change Everything, We Need Everyone"), located between 82nd and 86th Street – This area includes <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBTQ</a> groups and various geographic entities, including New York City boroughs and communities, other U.S. city and state groups and groups from non-U.S. countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The drop-off point for charter buses of marchers arriving from other places in the U.S. was on 86th Street between Broadway and Central Park West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup> The staging area is most easily accessible by subway via the <a href="/wiki/1_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="1 (New York City Subway service)">1 line</a> (66th, 72nd and 79th Street stops) and the <a href="/wiki/C_(New_York_City_Subway_service)" title="C (New York City Subway service)">C line</a> (72nd, 81st and 86th Street stops).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>There were only five permitted entry points to the staging area; all other streets were blocked off. The five permitted entry points were 65th, 72nd, 77th, 81st and 86th Streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup> It was anticipated by some organizers that parts or all of the staging area might be closed off by the police well before the march is due to begin at 11:30 am, which means that those who arrive late in the morning might be placed at the rear of the march. In addition, those who leave the area before the march begins might not be permitted by the police to return. Therefore, some organizers advised marchers to arrive with sufficient food, water and sunscreen, as, after a certain point, they might not be able to leave the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STAGING_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-STAGING-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The march began at 11:30 am at Manhattan's Columbus Circle. The march itinerary included 59th Street (moving east), 6th Avenue (moving south), 42nd Street (moving west) and 11th Avenue (moving south), ending at 34th Street and 11th Avenue, near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacob_K._Javits_Convention_Center" title="Jacob K. Javits Convention Center">Jacob K. Javits Convention Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peripheral_events">Peripheral events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peripheral events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of peripheral events were planned for before, during or after the weekend of the march. They included the following:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_March_for_Climate_Action">Great March for Climate Action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Great March for Climate Action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_March_for_Climate_Action" title="Great March for Climate Action">Great March for Climate Action</a> consists of a cross-country march from <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, California, to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, which began on March 1, 2014, and is scheduled to conclude on November 1, 2014. The march participants plan to take part in the PCM by interrupting their march for four days and traveling by bus from <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> to New York City. The purpose of the convergence is to combine two historic climate marches – the largest (PCM) and the longest (The Great March). The marchers plan to return to Ohio beginning on the evening of September 21, hopefully bringing with them hundreds of new marchers from the PCM for the final six weeks of their journey. The Great March is scheduled to end just prior to the November 5 American <a href="/wiki/United_States_elections,_2014" title="United States elections, 2014">mid-term elections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCHEDULE_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCHEDULE-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Disruption_.28movie.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Disruption_(movie)"><i>Disruption</i> (movie)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disruption (movie)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Disruption</i> is a film, approximately 52 minutes long, directed by Kelly Nyks and Jared P. Scott,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABOUT_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABOUT-13">[12]</a></sup> which is being screened at various public venues, as well as in self-hosted screenings (for example, in libraries, campuses and community centers) on September 7, 2014, exactly two weeks before the People's Climate March, and on subsequent dates prior to the march.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> The film was partly produced by the organization 350.org, which called the PCM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup> On the evening of September 7, one of the free public screenings, which will take place at <a href="/wiki/The_New_School" title="The New School">The New School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, "will include a follow-up Q&amp;A with some of the climate movement's most recognized leaders, some of whom are featured in the film."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to 350.org, the film's title refers to "the dangerous environmental tipping points after which the entire climate system could spiral out of control, as well as the need for a mass social movement to disrupt the status quo and business-as-usual approach which is inhibiting the bold actions necessary to protect the planet's future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the event organizers, the film "takes the viewer on a fast-paced journey through the tangled world of climate change: the science, the politics, the solutions, and the stories that define this crisis at this pivotal point in human history. This is a movie by and for the movement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The film also seeks to answer the question "When it comes to climate change, why do we do so little when we know so much?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABOUT_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABOUT-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The film also argues that the environment crisis impacts other movements, such as that for social justice, as marginalized people stand to suffer most from the ravages of climate change. In a clip from the film, Eddie Bautista, executive director for the New York City Environmental Justice Alliance and one of the PCM's leading organizers, states, "This is not just about the environment, it's about the community, it's about jobs, it's about justice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup> Author <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Klein" title="Naomi Klein">Naomi Klein</a> says in the film, "We have a responsibility to rise to our historic moment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-COMMON_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-COMMON-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The film can be viewed online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_City_Climate_Convergence">New York City Climate Convergence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New York City Climate Convergence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4288" data-file-width="2848" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg/220px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg/330px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg/440px-9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:9-21_People%27s_Climate_March--8.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sign promoting related <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> hashtag #FloodWallStreet.</div></div></div>
<p>The New York City Climate Convergence is a coalition formed by two political activist environmental organizations, System Change Not Climate Change (SCNCC) and the Global Climate Convergence (GCC). During the weekend of the climate march, the organization will hold two plenaries and a number of workshops that will focus on what the organizers claim are the economic causes of climate change, and on ways in which the system can be altered, so that the world will begin to value "people, planet and peace over profit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> According to the coalition's website, the "GCC recently rose to prominence when it organized a series of climate actions connecting to the labour and environmental movements (the social and ecological) in a campaign called 'Mother Earth Day to May Day 2014.' SCNCC, an 'ecosocialist' coalition arose out of multiple socialist and activists [sic] organizations in the aftermath of Hurricane Sandy which devastated New York city [sic]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The coalition, as well as other groups espousing various forms of direct action, has been cited as furnishing "opportunities to build a stronger and more effective climate justice movement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The coalition is demanding:
</p>
<ul><li>"Millions of jobs in renewable energy, conservation, and public transit</li>
<li>A just transition from fossil fuels and nuclear power</li>
<li>New food, water, and sanitation systems</li>
<li>An emergency transition to a new kind of economy</li>
<li>Tax the rich and slash the military budget."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The Opening Plenary for the New York City Climate Convergence, to be held on Friday evening, September 19 in midtown Manhattan, will include as speakers <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_water" title="Right to water">water rights</a> activist <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Olivera" title="Oscar Olivera">Oscar Olivera</a>, Philippine <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unionist</a> leader Josua Mata, Erica Violet Lee of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_rights" title="Indigenous rights">indigenous rights</a> group <a href="/wiki/Idle_No_More" title="Idle No More">Idle No More</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> artist and activist <a href="/wiki/Immortal_Technique" title="Immortal Technique">Immortal Technique</a>, Anne Petermann of the Global Justice Ecology Project and Nastaran Mohit of the New York State Nurses Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The Closing Plenary, to be held on the following night, will include as speakers the well-known author and activist Naomi Klein (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Shock_Doctrine" title="The Shock Doctrine">The Shock Doctrine</a></i>), Jacqui Patterson of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Colored_People" title="National Association for the Advancement of Colored People">NAACP</a> Environmental &amp; Climate Justice Program, Desmond D'Sa, the 2014 Goldman Prize recipient from <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and Olga Bautista of the Southeast Side Coalition Against Petcoke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Workshops will also be held during the day on September 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_Wall_Street">Flood Wall Street</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Climate_March&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Flood Wall Street">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The day after the People's Climate March, on September 22, several thousand protesters participated in Flood Wall Street, which blocked traffic in New York's Financial District for much of the day. The action was presented as a response to a call by the Climate Justice Alliance, a network of groups on the front-lines of climate change, to call corporate America to account for its role in pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> Explaining the rationale, journalist (and Flood Wall Street participant) <a href="/wiki/Nathan_Schneider" title="Nathan Schneider">Nathan Schneider</a> wrote in <i>Al Jazeera America</i>, "Climate change is war – and Wall Street is winning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Approximately 100 people were arrested by the end of the day, of which 10 faced trial and were found not guilty on March 5, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The BBC reported an estimated 600,000 people turned out for events in 156 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> The following are other major global marches related to the PCM.
