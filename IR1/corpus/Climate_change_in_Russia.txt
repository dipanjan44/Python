<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Russia</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>
<b>Global warming in Russia</b> describes the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> related issues in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. This includes climate politics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Causes_of_global_warming" title="Causes of global warming">contribution to global warming</a> and the influence of global warming in Russia. In 2009 Russia was ready to reduce emissions 20–25% from its 1990 emission levels by the year 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yle.fi_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-yle.fi-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agreements">Agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kyoto_protocol">Kyoto protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kyoto protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Russia underlined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> it came in force on 16 February 2005. Russia ratified the agreement three months earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This agreement did not cause emission cuts for Russia due to an earlier drop in emissions compared to year 1990 for other reasons, mainly a significant drop in economic growth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="G8">G8</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: G8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Six G8 countries would have been ready for the agreement to “at least halve global <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> emissions by 2050” in 2007. Russia and the United States did not agree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_climate_change_on_Russia">Effects of climate change on Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects of climate change on Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">Regional effects of global warming</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IPCC">IPCC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: IPCC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> (2007) climate change effected temperature increase which is greater at higher northern latitudes in lots of ways. For example agricultural and forestry management at Northern Hemisphere higher latitudes, such as earlier spring planting of crops, higher frequency of wildfires, alterations in disturbance of forests due to pests, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">increased health risks due to heat-waves</a>, changes in infectious diseases and allergenic pollen and changes to human activities in the Arctic, e.g. hunting and travel over snow and ice. From 1900 to 2005, precipitation increased in northern Europe and northern and central Asia. Recently these have resulted in fairly significant increases in GDP. Changes may affect inland flash floods, more frequent coastal flooding and increased erosion, reduced snow cover and species losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permafrost">Permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> thawing may be a serious cause for concern. It is believed that <a href="/wiki/Permafrost_carbon_cycle" title="Permafrost carbon cycle">carbon storage in</a> permafrost globally is approximately 1600 gigatons; equivalent to twice the atmospheric pool. Protecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peatlands" title="Peatlands">peatlands</a> from drainage and clearance slows down the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> and gives benefits for <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> is soil that has been frozen for two or more years. In most Arctic areas it is from a few to several hundred metres thick. Thawing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">permafrost soils</a> releases <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Methane has 25 times the warming potential of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Recent <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> of the world’s soils were estimated between 150 and 250 million metric tons (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP2008-6">[6]</a></sup>
Estimated annual net methane emission rates at the end of the 20th century for the northern region was 51 million metric tons. Net methane emissions from permafrost regions north included 64% from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, 11% from Canada and 7% from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> (2004). The business-as-usual scenarios estimate the Arctic methane emissions from permafrost thawing and rising temperatures to range from 54 to 105 million metric tons of methane per year (2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP2008_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP2008-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildfires">Wildfires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wildfires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> higher temperatures may increase the frequency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildfires" title="Wildfires">wildfires</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-4">[4]</a></sup> In Russia this includes the risk of <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peatland</a> fires. Peat fire emissions may be more harmful for human health than forest fires. Scientists alarmed by the peatfires in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> in 2004 concluded that “burning peat could be a major contributor to the as yet unexplained accelerating build-up of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere since 1998.” In October 2004 in Borneo regionally the atmosphere was covered in thick smoke, visibility was 100 metres, schools shut and flights were cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 
According to <a href="/wiki/Wetlands_International" title="Wetlands International">Wetlands International</a> the wild fires in Moscow July 2010 were mainly 80–90% from dewatered peatlands. According to UN dewatered bogs cause 6% of human global warming emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Moscow air was filled with peat fire emissions in July 2010 and regionally visibility was below 300 metres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_contribution">Statistical contribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Statistical contribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Russia was the fourth top emitter of CO<sub>2</sub> in 2009, 5.2% of the global total behind <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_China" title="Climate change in China">China</a> 25.4%, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_United_States" title="Climate change in the United States">US</a> 17.8% and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_India" title="Climate change in India">India</a> 5.3%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Russia was the 9th top emitter of all greenhouse gas emissions including building and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in 2005: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_China" title="Energy in China">China</a> 16.4%, <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">US</a>: 15.7%, 3. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Brazil" title="Energy in Brazil">Brazil</a> 6.5%, 4. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Indonesia" title="Energy in Indonesia">Indonesia</a> 4.6%,  5. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_India" title="Energy in India">India</a> 4.2%, 6. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Japan" title="Energy in Japan">Japan</a> 3.1%, 7. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Germany" title="Energy in Germany">Germany</a> 2.3%, 8. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_Canada" title="Energy in Canada">Canada</a> 1.8%, and 9. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Russia" title="Energy in Russia">Russia</a>:1.6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the cumulative emissions between 1850 and 2007 the top emitters were: 1. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">US</a> 28.8%, 2. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_China" title="Energy in China">China</a>: 9.0%, 3. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Russia" title="Energy in Russia">Russia</a>: 8.0%, 4. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Germany" title="Energy in Germany">Germany</a>  6.9%, 5. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Energy in the United Kingdom">UK</a> 5.8%, 6. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Japan" title="Energy in Japan">Japan</a>: 3.9%, 7. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_France" title="Energy in France">France</a>: 2.8%, 8. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_India" title="Energy in India">India</a>  2.4%, 9. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_in_Canada" title="Energy in Canada">Canada</a>: 2.2% and 10. <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Ukraine" title="Energy in Ukraine">Ukraine</a> 2.2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian2010_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sectors">Sectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Energy:_Peat.2C_oil_shale_and_coal"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy:_Peat,_oil_shale_and_coal">Energy:  Peat, oil shale and coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Energy: Peat, oil shale and coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_Russia" title="Energy policy of Russia">Energy policy of Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal_in_Russia" title="Coal in Russia">Coal in Russia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electricity_sector_in_Russia" title="Electricity sector in Russia">Electricity sector in Russia</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> and coal are among the most harmful energy sources in respect to global warming emissions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a> is often a more harmful energy source than coal for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Peat burning has been used as an energy source in Russia. In 2005 peat-based energy production was 8% of the total energy production in Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Russia has also used <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> for energy.
</p><p>Russia produced, used and imported coal in 2008. In addition to the environmental and human health issues from harmful emissions of coal, coal mining was also dangerous work in Russia in 2008. Serious accidents have been reported.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  According to <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">IEA</a> the share of coal and <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> electricity was 19% of the gross electricity production in 2008 in Russia (187 TWh / 1,038 TWh).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Russia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> was 20% of warming emissions in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since August 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup>
</p><p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> (2007) more than half of the world forests are in five countries: Russia, Brazil, Canada, US and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarriHakala_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-HarriHakala-15">[15]</a></sup> 2/3 of world forests are in 10 countries: Russia, Brazil, Canada, US, China, Australia, Kongo<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, Indonesia, Peru and India. Russian challenges for forests include control of <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a>, corruption, forest fires and land use.
</p>
