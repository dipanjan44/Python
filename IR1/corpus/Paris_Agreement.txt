<p>The <b>Paris Agreement</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Accord de Paris</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> is an agreement within the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC), dealing with <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">greenhouse-gas-emissions mitigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_adaptation" title="Climate change adaptation">adaptation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, starting in the year 2020. The agreement's language was negotiated by representatives of 196 state parties at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">21st Conference of the Parties of the UNFCCC</a> in <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget" title="Le Bourget">Le Bourget</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and adopted by consensus on 12 December 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As of September 2018, 195 UNFCCC members have signed the agreement, and 181 have become party to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-depo_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-depo-1">[1]</a></sup> The Paris Agreement's long-term goal is to keep the increase in global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels; and to limit the increase to 1.5 °C, since this would substantially reduce the risks and effects of climate change.
</p><p>Under the Paris Agreement, each country must determine, plan, and regularly report on the contribution that it undertakes to mitigate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Article3_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Article3-6">[6]</a></sup> No mechanism forces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reguly2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-reguly2015-7">[7]</a></sup> a country to set a specific target by a specific date,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Kinver_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Kinver-8">[8]</a></sup> but each target should go beyond previously set targets. In June 2017, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> announced his intention to <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">withdraw</a> his country from the agreement. Under the agreement, the earliest effective date of withdrawal for the U.S. is November 2020, shortly before the end of President Trump's current term. In practice, changes in United States policy that are contrary to the Paris Agreement have already been put in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2017 French Environment Minister <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Hulot" title="Nicolas Hulot">Nicolas Hulot</a> announced a plan to ban all petrol and diesel vehicles in France by 2040 as part of the Paris Agreement. Hulot also stated that France would no longer use coal to produce electricity after 2022 and that up to €4 billion will be invested in boosting energy efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> To reach the agreement's emission targets, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> will ban the sale of petrol- and diesel-powered cars by 2025;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> will do the same by 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Electric trains running on the <a href="/wiki/Nederlandse_Spoorwegen" title="Nederlandse Spoorwegen">Dutch national rail network</a> are already entirely powered by wind energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_(Netherlands)" title="House of Representatives (Netherlands)">House of Representatives of the Netherlands</a> passed a bill in June 2018 mandating that the Netherlands will cut by 2050 its 1990 greenhouse-gas emissions level by 95%—exceeding the Paris Agreement goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aims">Aims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The aim of the agreement is described in its Article 2, "enhancing the implementation" of the UNFCCC through:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>(a) Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels, recognizing that this would significantly reduce the risks and impacts of climate change;
</p><p>(b) Increasing the ability to adapt to the adverse impacts of climate change and foster climate resilience and low greenhouse gas emissions development, in a manner that does not threaten food production;
</p><p>(c) Making finance flows consistent with a pathway towards low greenhouse gas emissions and climate-resilient development.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Countries furthermore aim to reach "global peaking of greenhouse gas emissions as soon as possible". The agreement has been described as an incentive for and driver of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_divestment" title="Fossil fuel divestment">fossil fuel divestment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Paris deal is the world's first comprehensive climate agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationally_determined_contributions"><span id="Nationally_determined_contributions_and_their_limits"></span> Nationally determined contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nationally determined contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="540" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/275px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/413px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg/550px-CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg.png 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_emission_pie_chart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a> by jurisdiction.</div></div></div>
<p>Contributions each individual country should make to achieve the worldwide goal are determined by all countries individually and are called <i>nationally determined contributions</i> (NDCs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Article3_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Article3-6">[6]</a></sup> Article 3 requires them to be "ambitious", "represent a progression over time" and set "with the view to achieving the purpose of this Agreement". The contributions should be reported every five years and are to be registered by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Secretariat" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">UNFCCC Secretariat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Each further ambition should be more ambitious than the previous one, known as the principle of 'progression'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Countries can cooperate and pool their nationally determined contributions. The <a href="/wiki/Intended_Nationally_Determined_Contributions" title="Intended Nationally Determined Contributions">Intended Nationally Determined Contributions</a> pledged during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2015_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 Climate Change Conference">2015 Climate Change Conference</a> serve—unless provided otherwise—as the initial Nationally determined contribution.
</p><p>The level of NDCs set by each country<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Kinver_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Kinver-8">[8]</a></sup> will set that country's targets. However the 'contributions' themselves are not binding as a matter of international law, as they lack the specificity, normative character, or obligatory language necessary to create binding norms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Furthermore, there will be no mechanism to force<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reguly2015_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-reguly2015-7">[7]</a></sup> a country to set a target in their NDC by a specific date and no enforcement if a set target in an NDC is not met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Kinver_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Kinver-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> There will be only a "name and shame" system<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> or as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_P%C3%A1sztor_(diplomat)" title="János Pásztor (diplomat)">János Pásztor</a>, the U.N. assistant secretary-general on climate change, told CBS News (US), a "name and encourage" plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As the agreement provides no consequences if countries do not meet their commitments, consensus of this kind is fragile. A trickle of nations exiting the agreement may trigger the withdrawal of more governments, bringing about a total collapse of the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="external text" href="https://ndcpartnership.org/" rel="nofollow">The NDC Partnership</a> was launched at COP22 in Marrakesh to enhance cooperation so that countries have access to the technical knowledge and financial support they need to achieve large-scale climate and sustainable development targets. The NDC Partnership is guided by a <a class="external text" href="https://ndcpartnership.org/about-us/governance" rel="nofollow">Steering Committee</a> composed of developed and developing nations and international institutions, and facilitated by a Support Unit hosted by <a class="external text" href="http://www.wri.org/" rel="nofollow">World Resources Institute</a> and based in Washington, DC and Bonn, Germany. The NDC Partnership is co-chaired by the governments of Germany and Morocco and includes <a class="external text" href="https://ndcpartnership.org/partners" rel="nofollow">71 member countries,13 institutional partners and two associate members.</a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_global_temperature">Effects on global temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects on global temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The negotiators of the agreement, however, stated that the NDCs and the 2 °C reduction target were insufficient; instead, a 1.5 °C target is required, noting "with concern that the estimated aggregate greenhouse gas emission levels in 2025 and 2030 resulting from the intended nationally determined contributions do not fall within least-cost 2 °C scenarios but rather lead to a projected level of 55 gigatonnes in 2030", and recognizing furthermore "that much greater emission reduction efforts will be required in order to hold the increase in the global average temperature to below 2 °C by reducing emissions to 40 gigatonnes or to 1.5 °C."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though not the sustained temperatures over the long term that the Agreement addresses, in the first half of 2016 average temperatures were about 1.3 °C (2.3 degrees Fahrenheit)  above the average in 1880, when global record-keeping began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>When the agreement achieved enough signatures to cross the threshold on 5 October 2016, <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">US President Barack Obama</a> claimed that "Even if we meet every target ... we will only get to part of where we need to go."  He also said that "this agreement will help delay or avoid some of the worst consequences of climate change. It will help other nations ratchet down their emissions over time, and set bolder targets as technology advances, all under a strong system of transparency that allows each nation to evaluate the progress of all other nations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obamaclimate_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-obamaclimate-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_stocktake">Global stocktake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Global stocktake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="940" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg/220px-CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg/330px-CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg/440px-CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_responsibility_1950-2000.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of cumulative per capita anthropogenic atmospheric CO2 emissions by country. Cumulative emissions include land use change, and are measured between the years 1950 and 2000.</div></div></div>
<p>The global stocktake will kick off with a "facilitative dialogue" in 2018. At this convening, parties will evaluate how their NDCs stack up to the nearer-term goal of peaking global emissions and the long-term goal of achieving net zero emissions by the second half of this century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The implementation of the agreement by all member countries together will be evaluated every 5 years, with the first evaluation in 2023. The outcome is to be used as input for new nationally determined contributions of member states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The stocktake will not be of contributions/achievements of individual countries but a collective analysis of what has been achieved and what more needs to be done.
</p><p>The stocktake works as part of the Paris Agreement's effort to create a "ratcheting up" of ambition in emissions cuts. Because analysts have agreed that the current NDCs will not limit rising temperatures below 2 degrees Celsius, the global stocktake reconvenes parties to assess how their new NDCs must evolve so that they continually reflect a country's "highest possible ambition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>While ratcheting up the ambition of NDCs is a major aim of the global stocktake, it assesses efforts beyond mitigation. The 5 year reviews will also evaluate adaptation, climate finance provisions, and technology development and transfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Paris Agreement has a 'bottom up' structure in contrast to most international environmental law treaties, which are 'top down', characterised by standards and targets set internationally, for states to implement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Unlike its predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, which sets commitment targets that have legal force, the Paris Agreement, with its emphasis on consensus-building, allows for voluntary and nationally determined targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup> The specific climate goals are thus politically encouraged, rather than legally bound. Only the processes governing the reporting and review of these goals are mandated under international law. This structure is especially notable for the United States—because there are no legal mitigation or finance targets, the agreement is considered an "executive agreement rather than a treaty". Because the UNFCCC treaty of 1992 received the consent of the Senate, this new agreement does not require further legislation from Congress for it to take effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another key difference between the Paris Agreement and the Kyoto Protocol is their scopes. While the Kyoto Protocol differentiated between Annex-1 and non-Annex-1 countries, this bifurcation is blurred in the Paris Agreement, as all parties will be required to submit emissions reductions plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup> While the Paris Agreement still emphasizes the principle of "Common but Differentiated Responsibility and Respective Capabilities"—the acknowledgement that different nations have different capacities and duties to climate action—it does not provide a specific division between developed and developing nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-34">[34]</a></sup> It therefore appears that negotiators will have to continue to deal with this issue in future negotiation rounds, even though the discussion on differentiation may take on a new dynamic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_provisions_and_carbon_markets">Mitigation provisions and carbon markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mitigation provisions and carbon markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Article 6 has been flagged as containing some of the key provisions of the Paris Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">[36]</a></sup> Broadly, it outlines the <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Mechanisms_under_Article_6_of_the_Paris_Agreement" title="Cooperative Mechanisms under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement">cooperative approaches</a> that parties can take in achieving their nationally determined carbon emissions reductions. In doing so, it helps establish the Paris Agreement as a framework for a global carbon market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Linkage_of_trading_systems_and_international_transfer_of_mitigation_outcomes_.28ITMOs.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Linkage_of_trading_systems_and_international_transfer_of_mitigation_outcomes_(ITMOs)">Linkage of trading systems and international transfer of mitigation outcomes (ITMOs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Linkage of trading systems and international transfer of mitigation outcomes (ITMOs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Mechanisms_under_Article_6_of_the_Paris_Agreement#Article_6.2:_Cooperative_Approaches" title="Cooperative Mechanisms under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement">Paragraphs 6.2</a> and 6.3 establish a framework to govern the international transfer of mitigation outcomes (ITMOs). The Agreement recognizes the rights of Parties to use emissions reductions outside of their own jurisdiction toward their NDC, in a system of carbon accounting and trading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>This provision requires the "linkage" of various carbon emissions trading systems—because measured emissions reductions must avoid "double counting", transferred mitigation outcomes must be recorded as a gain of emission units for one party and a reduction of emission units for the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-36">[36]</a></sup> Because the NDCs, and domestic carbon trading schemes, are heterogeneous, the ITMOs will provide a format for global linkage under the auspices of the UNFCCC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-38">[38]</a></sup> The provision thus also creates a pressure for countries to adopt emissions management systems—if a country wants to use more cost-effective <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Mechanisms_under_Article_6_of_the_Paris_Agreement" title="Cooperative Mechanisms under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement">cooperative approaches</a> to achieve their NDCs, they will need to monitor carbon units for their economies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_Development_Mechanism">Sustainable Development Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sustainable Development Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Mechanisms_under_Article_6_of_the_Paris_Agreement#Article_6.4:_Mechanism_to_promote_climate_action_and_support_sustainable_development" title="Cooperative Mechanisms under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement">Paragraphs 6.4</a>-6.7 establish a mechanism "to contribute to the mitigation of greenhouse gases and support sustainable development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Though there is no specific name for the mechanism as yet, many Parties and observers have informally coalesced around the name "Sustainable Development Mechanism" or "SDM".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The SDM is considered to be the successor to the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a>, a flexible mechanism under the Kyoto Protocol, by which parties could collaboratively pursue emissions reductions for their <a href="/wiki/Intended_Nationally_Determined_Contributions" title="Intended Nationally Determined Contributions">Intended Nationally Determined Contributions</a>. The Sustainable Development Mechanism lays the framework for the future of the Clean Development Mechanism post-Kyoto (in 2020).
</p><p>In its basic aim, the SDM will largely resemble the Clean Development Mechanism, with the dual mission to 1. contribute to global GHG emissions reductions and 2. support sustainable development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Though the structure and processes governing the SDM are not yet determined, certain similarities and differences from the Clean Development Mechanism can already be seen. Notably, the SDM, unlike the Clean Development Mechanism, will be available to all parties as opposed to only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annex_1" title="Annex 1">Annex-1</a> parties, making it much wider in scope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the Kyoto Protocol went into force, the Clean Development Mechanism has been criticized for failing to produce either meaningful emissions reductions or sustainable development benefits in most instances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It has also suffered from the low price of Certified Emissions Reductions (CERs), creating less demand for projects. These criticisms have motivated the recommendations of various stakeholders, who have provided through working groups and reports, new elements they hope to see in SDM that will bolster its success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-38">[38]</a></sup> The specifics of the governance structure, project proposal modalities, and overall design are expected to come during the next<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conference_of_the_Parties_(Kyoto_Protocol)" title="Conference of the Parties (Kyoto Protocol)">Conference of the Parties</a> in <a href="/wiki/Marrakesh" title="Marrakesh">Marrakesh</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptation_provisions">Adaptation provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Adaptation provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adaptation issues garnered more focus in the formation of the Paris Agreement. Collective, long-term adaptation goals are included in the Agreement, and countries must report on their adaptation actions, making adaptation a parallel component of the agreement with mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-46">[46]</a></sup> The adaptation goals focus on enhancing adaptive capacity, increasing resilience, and limiting vulnerability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ensuring_finance">Ensuring finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ensuring finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a></div>
<p>At the Paris Conference in 2015 where the Agreement was negotiated, the developed countries reaffirmed the commitment to mobilize $100 billion a year in climate finance by 2020, and agreed to continue mobilizing finance at the level of $100 billion a year until 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The commitment refers to the pre-existing plan to provide US$100 billion a year in aid to developing countries for actions on climate change adaptation and mitigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though both mitigation and adaptation require increased climate financing, adaptation has typically received lower levels of support and has mobilised less action from the private sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-46">[46]</a></sup> A 2014 report by the OECD found that just 16 percent of global finance was directed toward climate adaptation in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Paris Agreement called for a balance of climate finance between adaptation and mitigation, and specifically underscoring the need to increase adaptation support for parties most vulnerable to the effects of climate change, including Least Developed Countries and Small Island Developing States. The agreement also reminds parties of the importance of public grants, because adaptation measures receive less investment from the public sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-46">[46]</a></sup> John Kerry, as Secretary of State, announced that the U.S. would double its grant-based adaptation finance by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some specific outcomes of the elevated attention to adaptation financing in Paris include the G7 countries' announcement to provide US $420 million for Climate Risk Insurance, and the launching of a Climate Risk and Early Warning Systems (CREWS) Initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
In early March 2016, the Obama administration gave a $500 million grant to the "<a href="/wiki/Green_Climate_Fund" title="Green Climate Fund">Green Climate Fund</a>" as "the first chunk of a $3<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion commitment made at the Paris climate talks."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFrameworkConvention_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFrameworkConvention-54">[54]</a></sup> So far, the Green Climate Fund has now received over $10 billion in pledges. Notably, the pledges come from developed nations like France, the US, and Japan, but also from developing countries such as Mexico, Indonesia, and Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_and_damage">Loss and damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Loss and damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A new issue that emerged<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as a focal point in the Paris negotiations rose from the fact that many of the worst effects of climate change will be too severe or come too quickly to be avoided by adaptation measures. The Paris Agreement specifically acknowledges the need to address loss and damage of this kind, and aims to find appropriate responses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldResourcesInstitute_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorldResourcesInstitute-55">[55]</a></sup> It specifies that loss and damage can take various forms—both as immediate impacts from extreme weather events, and slow onset impacts, such as the loss of land to sea-level rise for low-lying islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The push to address loss and damage as a distinct issue in the Paris Agreement came from the Alliance of Small Island States and the Least Developed Countries, whose economies and livelihoods are most vulnerable to the negative impacts of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup> Developed countries, however, worried that classifying the issue as one separate and beyond adaptation measures would create yet another climate finance provision, or might imply legal liability for catastrophic climate events.
</p><p>In the end, all parties acknowledged the need for "averting, minimizing, and addressing loss and damage" but notably, any mention of compensation or liability is excluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup> The agreement also adopts the Warsaw International Mechanism for Loss and Damage, an institution that will attempt to address questions about how to classify, address, and share responsibility for loss and damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldResourcesInstitute_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-WorldResourcesInstitute-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_transparency_framework">Enhanced transparency framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Enhanced transparency framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While each Party's NDC is not legally binding, the Parties are legally bound to have their progress tracked by technical expert review to assess achievement toward the NDC, and to determine ways to strengthen ambition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-:9-56">[56]</a></sup> Article 13 of the Paris Agreement articulates an "enhanced transparency framework for action and support" that establishes harmonized monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) requirements. Thus, both developed and developing nations must report every two years on their mitigation efforts, and all parties will be subject to both technical and peer review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:9_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-:9-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexibility_mechanisms">Flexibility mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Flexibility mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the enhanced transparency framework is universal, along with the global stocktaking to occur every 5 years, the framework is meant to provide "built-in flexibility" to distinguish between developed and developing countries' capacities. In conjunction with this, the Paris Agreement has provisions for an enhanced framework for capacity building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-:10-57">[57]</a></sup> The agreement recognizes the varying circumstances of some countries, and specifically notes that the technical expert review for each country consider that country's specific capacity for reporting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-:10-57">[57]</a></sup> The agreement also develops a Capacity-Building Initiative for Transparency to assist developing countries in building the necessary institutions and processes for complying with the transparency framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-:10-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several ways that flexibility mechanisms can be incorporated into the enhanced transparency framework. The scope, level of detail, or frequency of reporting may all be adjusted and tiered based on a country's capacity. The requirement for in-country technical reviews could be lifted for some less developed or small island developing countries. Ways to assess capacity include financial and human resources in a country necessary for NDC review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:10_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-:10-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Paris Agreement was opened for signature on 22 April 2016 (<a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a>) at a ceremony in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-historic-58">[58]</a></sup> After several European Union states ratified the agreement in October 2016, there were enough countries that had ratified the agreement that produce enough of the world's greenhouse gases for the agreement to enter into force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-europeancommission_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-europeancommission-59">[59]</a></sup> The agreement went into effect on 4 November 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dateffect_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dateffect-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations">Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, legal instruments may be adopted to reach the goals of the convention. For the period from 2008 to 2012, greenhouse gas reduction measures were agreed in the Kyoto Protocol in 1997. The scope of the protocol was extended until 2020 with the Doha Amendment to that protocol in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Durban_Platform_for_Enhanced_Action" title="Durban Platform for Enhanced Action">Durban Platform</a> (and the Ad Hoc Working Group on the Durban Platform for Enhanced Action) was established with the aim to negotiate a legal instrument governing <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> mitigation measures from 2020. The resulting agreement was to be adopted in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption_2">Adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_(23430273715).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1042" data-file-width="1946" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg/220px-COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg/330px-COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg/440px-COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_%2823430273715%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:COP21_participants_-_30_Nov_2015_(23430273715).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Heads of delegations at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in Paris.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a></div>
<p>At the conclusion of COP 21 (the 21st meeting of the Conference of the Parties, which guides the Conference), on 12 December 2015, the final wording of the Paris Agreement was adopted by consensus by all of the 195 UNFCCC participating member states and the European Union<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">[4]</a></sup> to reduce emissions as part of the method for reducing greenhouse gas. In the 12 page Agreement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFrameworkConvention_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNFrameworkConvention-54">[54]</a></sup> the members promised to reduce their carbon output "as soon as possible" and to do their best to keep global warming "to well below 2 degrees C" [3.6 degrees F].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signature_and_entry_into_force">Signature and entry into force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Signature and entry into force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Secretary_Kerry_Holds_Granddaughter_Dobbs-Higginson_on_Lap_While_Signing_COP21_Climate_Change_Agreement_at_UN_General_Assembly_Hall_in_New_York_(26512345421).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3144" data-file-width="4752" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Secretary_Kerry_Holds_Granddaughter_Dobbs-Higginson_on_Lap_While_Signing_COP21_Climate_Change_Agreement_at_UN_General_Assembly_Hall_in_New_York_%2826512345421%29.jpg/220px-thumbnail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Secretary_Kerry_Holds_Granddaughter_Dobbs-Higginson_on_Lap_While_Signing_COP21_Climate_Change_Agreement_at_UN_General_Assembly_Hall_in_New_York_%2826512345421%29.jpg/330px-thumbnail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Secretary_Kerry_Holds_Granddaughter_Dobbs-Higginson_on_Lap_While_Signing_COP21_Climate_Change_Agreement_at_UN_General_Assembly_Hall_in_New_York_%2826512345421%29.jpg/440px-thumbnail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Secretary_Kerry_Holds_Granddaughter_Dobbs-Higginson_on_Lap_While_Signing_COP21_Climate_Change_Agreement_at_UN_General_Assembly_Hall_in_New_York_(26512345421).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signing by <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Hall" title="United Nations General Assembly Hall">United Nations General Assembly Hall</a> for the United States</div></div></div>
<p>The Paris Agreement was open for signature by states and regional economic integration organizations that are parties to the UNFCCC (the Convention) from 22 April 2016 to 21 April 2017 at the UN Headquarters in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The agreement stated that it would enter into force (and thus become fully effective) only if 55 countries that produce at least 55% of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="List of countries by greenhouse gas emissions">the world's greenhouse gas emissions</a> (according to a list produced in 2015)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ratpct_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-ratpct-64">[64]</a></sup> ratify, accept, approve or accede to the agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-66">[66]</a></sup> On 1 April 2016, the United States and China, which together represent almost 40% of global emissions, issued a joint statement confirming that both countries would sign the Paris Climate Agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> 175 Parties (174 states and the European Union) signed the agreement on the first date it was open for signature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historic_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-historic-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> On the same day, more than 20 countries issued a statement of their intent to join as soon as possible with a view to joining in 2016. With ratification by the European Union, the Agreement obtained enough parties to enter into effect as of 4 November 2016.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union_and_its_member_states">European Union and its member states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: European Union and its member states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both the EU and its member states are individually responsible for ratifying the Paris Agreement. A strong preference was reported that the EU and its 28 member states deposit their instruments of ratification at the same time to ensure that neither the EU nor its member states engage themselves to fulfilling obligations that strictly belong to the other,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurat_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurat-70">[70]</a></sup> and there were fears that disagreement over each individual member state's share of the EU-wide reduction target, as well as Britain's <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">vote to leave the EU</a> might delay the Paris pact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-71">[71]</a></sup> However, the European Parliament approved ratification of the Paris Agreement on 4 October 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-europeancommission_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-europeancommission-59">[59]</a></sup> and the EU deposited its instruments of ratification on 5 October 2016, along with several individual EU member states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process of translating the Paris Agreement into national agendas and implementation has started. One example is the commitment of the least developed countries (LDCs). The LDC Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency Initiative for Sustainable Development, known as LDC REEEI, is set to bring sustainable, clean energy to millions of energy-starved people in LDCs, facilitating improved energy access, the creation of jobs and contributing to the achievement of the <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals" title="Sustainable Development Goals">Sustainable Development Goals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D+C_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-D+C-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Per analysis from the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)</a> a carbon "budget" based upon total carbon dioxide emissions in the atmosphere (versus the rate of annual emission) to limit global warming to 1.5 °C was estimated to be was 2.25 trillion tonnes of overall emitted carbon dioxide from the period since 1870. This number is a notable increase from the number estimated by the original Paris Climate accord estimates (of around 2 trillion tonnes total) total carbon emission limit to meet the 1.5 °C global warming target, a target that would be met in the year 2020 at current rates of emission. Additionally, the annual emission of carbon is estimated to be currently at 40 billion tonnes emitted per year. The revised IPCC budget for this was based upon <a href="/wiki/Coupled_model_intercomparison_project" title="Coupled model intercomparison project">CMIP5</a> climate model. Estimate models using different base-years also provide other slightly adjusted estimates of a carbon "budget".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parties_and_signatories">Parties and signatories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Parties and signatories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_parties_to_the_Paris_Agreement" title="List of parties to the Paris Agreement">List of parties to the Paris Agreement</a></div>
<p>As of September 2018, 194 states and the European Union have signed the Agreement. 180 states and the EU, representing more than 87% of global greenhouse gas emissions, have ratified or acceded to the Agreement, including China, the United States and India, the countries with three of the four largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> of the UNFCC members total (about 42% together).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-depo_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-depo-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC03.09.2016_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC03.09.2016-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Withdrawal_from_Agreement"><span id="US_withdrawal"></span> Withdrawal from Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Withdrawal from Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement</a></div>
<p>Article 28 of the agreement enables parties to withdraw from the agreement after sending a withdrawal notification to the depositary, but notice can be given no earlier than three years after the agreement goes into force for the country. Withdrawal is effective one year after the depositary is notified. Alternatively, the Agreement stipulates that withdrawal from the UNFCCC, under which the Paris Agreement was adopted, would also withdraw the state from the Paris Agreement. The conditions for withdrawal from the UNFCCC are the same as for the Paris Agreement. The agreement does not specify provisions for noncompliance.
</p><p>On August 4, 2017, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_administration" title="Trump administration">Trump administration</a> delivered an official notice to the United Nations that the U.S. intends to withdraw from the Paris Agreement as soon as it is legally eligible to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UScom_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-UScom-76">[76]</a></sup>  The formal notice of withdrawal cannot be submitted until the agreement is in force for 3 years for the US, in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In accordance with Article 28, as the agreement entered into force in the United States on 4 November 2016, the earliest possible effective withdrawal date for the United States is 4 November 2019. If it chooses to withdraw by way of withdrawing from the UNFCCC, notice could be given immediately (the UNFCCC entered into force for the US in 1994), and be effective one year later.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Just_not_enough">Just not enough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Just not enough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="600" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png/150px-Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png/225px-Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png/300px-Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_agreement_emission_reduction_targets.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paris climate accord emission reduction targets and current real-life reductions offered</div></div></div>
<p>A pair of  studies in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> have said that as of 2017, none of the major industrialized nations were implementing the policies they had envisioned and have not met their pledged emission reduction targets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-empty_promises_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-empty_promises-79">[79]</a></sup> and even if they had, the sum of all member pledges (as of 2016) would not keep global temperature rise "well below 2 °C".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>  According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a> the emission cut targets in November 2016 will result in temperature rise by 3 °C above preindustrial levels, far above the 2 °C of the Paris climate agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, an MIT News article written on April 22, 2016 discussed recent MIT studies on the true impact that the Paris Agreement had on global temperature increase. Using their Integrated Global System Modeling (IGSM) to predict temperature increase results in 2100, they used a wide range of scenarios that included no effort towards climate change past 2030, and full extension of the Paris Agreement past 2030. They concluded that the Paris Agreement would cause temperature decrease by about 0.6 to 1.1 degrees Celsius, with only a 0.1 C change in 2050 for all scenarios. They concluded that, although beneficial, there was strong evidence that the goal provided by the Paris Agreement could not be met in the future under the current circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Difference_of_Paris_Agreement_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Difference_of_Paris_Agreement-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2018 published study points at a threshold at which temperatures could rise to 4 or 5 degrees compared to the pre-industrial levels, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_feedback" title="Climate feedback">self-reinforcing feedbacks</a> in the climate system, suggesting this threshold is below the 2 degree temperature target, agreed upon by the Paris climate deal. Study author Katherine Richardson stresses, "We note that the Earth has never in its history had a quasi-stable state that is around 2C warmer than the preindustrial and suggest that there is substantial risk that the system, itself, will ‘want’ to continue warming because of all of these other processes – even if we stop emissions. This implies not only reducing emissions but much more.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the same time, another 2018 published study notes that even at a 1.5C level of warming, important increases in the occurrence of high river flows would be expected in India, South and Southeast Asia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>. Yet, the same study points out that under a 2.0C of warming various areas in South America, central Africa, western Europe, and the Mississippi area in the United States would see more high flows; thus increasing flood risks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_binding_enforcement_mechanism">Lack of binding enforcement mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Lack of binding enforcement mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the agreement was lauded by many, including French President François Hollande and UN Secretary General Ban Ki-moon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-66">[66]</a></sup> criticism has also surfaced. For example, <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a>, a former NASA scientist and a climate change expert, voiced anger that most of the agreement consists of "promises" or aims and not firm commitments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_fraud_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_fraud-86">[86]</a></sup> He called the Paris talks a fraud with 'no action, just promises' and feels that only an across the board tax on CO2 emissions, something not part of the Paris Agreement, would force CO2 emissions down fast enough to avoid the worst effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_fraud_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_fraud-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p><p>Institutional asset owners associations and think-tanks have also observed that the stated objectives of the Paris Agreement are implicitly "predicated upon an assumption – that member states of the United Nations, including high polluters such as China, the US, India, Japan, Brazil, Canada, Russia, Indonesia and Australia, which generate more than half the world's greenhouse gas emissions, will somehow drive down their carbon pollution voluntarily and assiduously without any binding enforcement mechanism to measure and control CO<sub>2</sub> emissions at any level from factory to state, and without any specific penalty gradation or fiscal pressure (for example a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a>) to discourage bad behaviour."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto3_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
