<p><b>Beryllium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Be</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 4. It is a <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements#Universe" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">relatively rare</a> element in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, usually occurring as a product of the <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spallation</a> of larger atomic nuclei that have collided with <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. Within the cores of stars beryllium is depleted as it is fused and creates larger elements. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divalent" title="Divalent">divalent</a> element which occurs naturally only in combination with other elements in minerals. Notable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemstones" title="Gemstones">gemstones</a> which contain beryllium include <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquamarine_(gemstone)" title="Aquamarine (gemstone)">aquamarine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Chrysoberyl" title="Chrysoberyl">chrysoberyl</a>. As a free <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> it is a steel-gray, strong, lightweight and brittle <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a>.
</p><p>Beryllium improves many physical properties when added as an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloying</a> element to <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> (notably the alloy <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_copper" title="Beryllium copper">beryllium copper</a>), <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Beryllium does not form oxides until it reaches very high temperatures. Tools made of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_copper" title="Beryllium copper">beryllium copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> are strong and hard and do not create sparks when they strike a steel surface. In structural applications, the combination of high <a href="/wiki/Flexural_rigidity" title="Flexural rigidity">flexural rigidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_stability" title="Thermal stability">thermal stability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> and low <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> (1.85 times that of water) make beryllium metal a desirable <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> material for aircraft components, <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellites" title="Satellites">satellites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Because of its low density and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a>, beryllium is relatively transparent to X-rays and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>; therefore, it is the most common window material for X-ray equipment and components of <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">particle detectors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> The high thermal conductivities of beryllium and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_oxide" title="Beryllium oxide">beryllium oxide</a> have led to their use in thermal management applications.
</p><p>The commercial use of beryllium requires the use of appropriate dust control equipment and industrial controls at all times because of the <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> of inhaled beryllium-containing dusts that can cause a chronic life-threatening allergic disease in some people called <a href="/wiki/Berylliosis" title="Berylliosis">berylliosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h3>
<p>Beryllium is a steel gray and hard <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that is brittle at room temperature and has a close-packed hexagonal <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> It has exceptional <a href="/wiki/Stiffness" title="Stiffness">stiffness</a> (<a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young's modulus">Young's modulus</a> 287 GPa) and a reasonably high <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modulus_of_elasticity" title="Modulus of elasticity">modulus of elasticity</a> of beryllium is approximately 50% greater than that of steel. The combination of this modulus and a relatively low density results in an unusually fast <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">sound conduction speed</a> in beryllium – about 12.9 km/s at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">ambient conditions</a>. Other significant properties are high specific heat (1925 J·kg<sup>−1</sup>·K<sup>−1</sup>) and thermal conductivity (216 W·m<sup>−1</sup>·K<sup>−1</sup>), which make beryllium the metal with the best heat dissipation characteristics per unit weight. In combination with the relatively low coefficient of linear <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> (11.4×10<sup>−6</sup> K<sup>−1</sup>), these characteristics result in a unique stability under conditions of thermal loading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_properties">Nuclear properties</span></h3>
<p>Naturally occurring beryllium, save for slight contamination by the cosmogenic radioisotopes, is isotopically pure beryllium-9, which has a <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">nuclear spin</a> of <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">3</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>. Beryllium has a large scattering cross section for high-energy neutrons, about 6 <a href="/wiki/Barn_(unit)" title="Barn (unit)">barns</a> for energies above approximately 10 keV. Therefore, it works as a neutron reflector and <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderator</a>, effectively slowing the neutrons to the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_temperature" title="Neutron temperature">thermal energy</a> range of below 0.03 eV, where the total cross section is at least an order of magnitude lower – exact value strongly depends on the purity and size of the crystallites in the material.
</p><p>The single primordial beryllium isotope <sup>9</sup>Be also undergoes a (n,2n) neutron reaction with neutron energies over about 1.9 MeV, to produce <sup>8</sup>Be, which almost immediately breaks into two alpha particles. Thus, for high-energy neutrons, beryllium is a neutron multiplier, releasing more neutrons than it absorbs. style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> is an alpha particle and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a> nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeMelurgy_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-BeMelurgy-8">[8]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Beryllium also releases neutrons under bombardment by gamma rays. Thus, natural beryllium bombarded either by alphas or gammas from a suitable radioisotope is a key component of most radioisotope-powered <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reaction</a> <a href="/wiki/Neutron_source" title="Neutron source">neutron sources</a> for the laboratory production of free neutrons.
</p><p>Small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> are liberated when <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>Be<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> nuclei absorb low energy neutrons in the three-step nuclear reaction
</p>

Tritium is a radioisotope of concern in nuclear reactor waste streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a metal, beryllium is <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">transparent</a> to most wavelengths of <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a>, making it useful for the output windows of <a href="/wiki/X-ray_tube" title="X-ray tube">X-ray tubes</a> and other such apparatus.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes_and_nucleosynthesis">Isotopes and nucleosynthesis</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_beryllium" title="Isotopes of beryllium">Isotopes of beryllium</a></div>
<p>Both stable and unstable isotopes of beryllium are created in stars, but the radioisotopes do not last long. It is believed that most of the stable beryllium in the universe was originally created in the interstellar medium when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> induced fission in heavier elements found in interstellar gas and dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Primordial beryllium contains only one stable isotope, <sup>9</sup>Be, and therefore beryllium is a <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic element</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Activity_Proxies.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="361" data-file-width="559" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Solar_Activity_Proxies.png/300px-Solar_Activity_Proxies.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Solar_Activity_Proxies.png/450px-Solar_Activity_Proxies.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/60/Solar_Activity_Proxies.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Activity_Proxies.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plot showing variations in solar activity, including variation in sunspot number (red) and <sup>10</sup>Be concentration (blue). Note that the beryllium scale is inverted, so increases on this scale indicate lower <sup>10</sup>Be levels</div></div></div>
<p>Radioactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmogenic" title="Cosmogenic">cosmogenic</a> <sup>10</sup>Be is produced in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere of the Earth</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation" title="Cosmic ray spallation">cosmic ray spallation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200156_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200156-11">[11]</a></sup> <sup>10</sup>Be accumulates at the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> surface, where its relatively long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> (1.36 million years) permits a long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Residence_time_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Residence time (fluid dynamics)">residence time</a> before decaying to <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>-10. Thus, <sup>10</sup>Be and its daughter products are used to examine natural <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_formation" title="Soil formation">soil formation</a> and the development of <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">lateritic soils</a>, and as a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy</a> for measurement of the variations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar activity</a> and the age of <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The production of <sup>10</sup>Be is inversely proportional to solar activity, because increased <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> during periods of high solar activity decreases the flux of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_cosmic_ray" title="Galactic cosmic ray">galactic cosmic rays</a> that reach the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200156_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200156-11">[11]</a></sup> Nuclear explosions also form <sup>10</sup>Be by the reaction of fast neutrons with <sup>13</sup>C in the carbon dioxide in air. This is one of the indicators of past activity at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_test" title="Nuclear weapon test">nuclear weapon test</a> sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
The isotope <sup>7</sup>Be (half-life 53 days) is also cosmogenic, and shows an atmospheric abundance linked to sunspots, much like <sup>10</sup>Be.
</p><p><sup>8</sup>Be has a very short half-life of about 7<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">17</span></sup> s that contributes to its significant cosmological role, as elements heavier than beryllium could not have been produced by nuclear fusion in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This is due to the lack of sufficient time during the Big Bang's <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a> phase to produce carbon by the fusion of <sup>4</sup>He nuclei and the very low concentrations of available <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-8" title="Beryllium-8">beryllium-8</a>. The British <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> Sir <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> first showed that the energy levels of <sup>8</sup>Be and <sup>12</sup>C allow carbon production by the so-called <a href="/wiki/Triple-alpha_process" title="Triple-alpha process">triple-alpha process</a> in helium-fueled stars where more nucleosynthesis time is available. This process allows carbon to be produced in stars, but not in the Big Bang. Star-created carbon (the basis of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_life" title="Carbon-based life">carbon-based life</a>) is thus a component in the elements in the gas and dust ejected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AGB_star" title="AGB star">AGB stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">Big Bang nucleosynthesis</a>), as well as the creation of all other elements with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic numbers</a> larger than that of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2s electrons of beryllium may contribute to chemical bonding. Therefore, when <sup>7</sup>Be decays by L-<a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, it does so by taking electrons from its <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">atomic orbitals</a> that may be participating in bonding. This makes its decay rate dependent to a measurable degree upon its chemical surroundings – a rare occurrence in nuclear decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The shortest-lived known isotope of beryllium is <sup>13</sup>Be which decays through <a href="/wiki/Neutron_emission" title="Neutron emission">neutron emission</a>. It has a half-life of 2.7 × 10<sup>−21</sup> s. <sup>6</sup>Be is also very short-lived with a half-life of 5.0 × 10<sup>−21</sup> s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-crc-17">[17]</a></sup> The exotic isotopes <sup>11</sup>Be and <sup>14</sup>Be are known to exhibit a <a href="/wiki/Halo_nucleus" title="Halo nucleus">nuclear halo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This phenomenon can be understood as the nuclei of <sup>11</sup>Be and <sup>14</sup>Be have, respectively, 1 and 4 neutrons orbiting substantially outside the classical Fermi 'waterdrop' model of the nucleus.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beryl-130023.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="253" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beryl-130023.jpg/120px-Beryl-130023.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beryl-130023.jpg/180px-Beryl-130023.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Beryl-130023.jpg/240px-Beryl-130023.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beryl-130023.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">Emerald</a> is a naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> of beryllium.</div></div></div>
<p>The Sun has a concentration of 0.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">parts per billion</a> (ppb) of beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Beryllium has a concentration of 2 to 6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> (ppm) in the Earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merck-20">[20]</a></sup>  It is most concentrated in the soils, 6 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159-21">[21]</a></sup> Trace amounts of <sup>9</sup>Be are found in the Earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159-21">[21]</a></sup> The concentration of beryllium in sea water is 0.2–0.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_trillion" title="Parts per trillion">parts per trillion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200159-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In stream water, however, beryllium is more abundant with a concentration of 0.1 ppb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium is found in over 100 minerals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> but most are uncommon to rare. The more common beryllium containing minerals include: <a href="/wiki/Bertrandite" title="Bertrandite">bertrandite</a> (Be<sub>4</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>Be<sub>3</sub>Si<sub>6</sub>O<sub>18</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Chrysoberyl" title="Chrysoberyl">chrysoberyl</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>BeO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Phenakite" title="Phenakite">phenakite</a> (Be<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>). Precious forms of beryl are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquamarine_(gemstone)" title="Aquamarine (gemstone)">aquamarine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_beryl" title="Red beryl">red beryl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> 
The green color in gem-quality forms of beryl comes from varying amounts of chromium (about 2% for emerald).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two main ores of beryllium, beryl and bertrandite, are found in Argentina, Brazil, India, Madagascar, Russia and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup> Total world reserves of beryllium ore are greater than 400,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<p>The extraction of beryllium from its compounds is a difficult process due to its high affinity for oxygen at elevated temperatures, and its ability to reduce water when its oxide film is removed. The United States, China and Kazakhstan are the only three countries involved in the industrial-scale extraction of beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Beryllium production technology is in early stages of development in Russia after a 20-year hiatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium is most commonly extracted from the mineral <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a>, which is either <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintered</a> using an extraction agent or melted into a soluble mixture. The sintering process involves mixing beryl with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluorosilicate" title="Sodium fluorosilicate">sodium fluorosilicate</a> and soda at 770 °C (1,420 °F) to form <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoroberyllate" title="Tetrafluoroberyllate">sodium fluoroberyllate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_hydroxide" title="Beryllium hydroxide">Beryllium hydroxide</a> is precipitated from a solution of sodium fluoroberyllate and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> in water. Extraction of beryllium using the melt method involves grinding beryl into a powder and heating it to 1,650 °C (3,000 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> The melt is quickly cooled with water and then reheated 250 to 300 °C (482 to 572 °F) in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, mostly yielding <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_sulfate" title="Beryllium sulfate">beryllium sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_sulfate" title="Aluminium sulfate">aluminium sulfate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Aqueous <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> is then used to remove the aluminium and sulfur, leaving beryllium hydroxide.
</p><p>Beryllium hydroxide created using either the sinter or melt method is then converted into <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_fluoride" title="Beryllium fluoride">beryllium fluoride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_chloride" title="Beryllium chloride">beryllium chloride</a>. To form the fluoride, aqueous ammonium hydrogen fluoride is added to beryllium hydroxide to yield a precipitate of ammonium tetrafluoroberyllate, which is heated to 1,000 °C (1,830 °F) to form beryllium fluoride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Heating the fluoride to 900 °C (1,650 °F) with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> forms finely divided beryllium, and additional heating to 1,300 °C (2,370 °F) creates the compact metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Heating beryllium hydroxide forms the oxide, which becomes beryllium chloride when combined with carbon and chlorine. <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">Electrolysis</a> of molten beryllium chloride is then used to obtain the metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Beryllium_compounds" title="Category:Beryllium compounds">Category:Beryllium compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Beryllium's chemical behavior is largely a result of its small <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic</a> radii. It thus has very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionization_potential" title="Ionization potential">ionization potentials</a> and strong polarization while bonded to other atoms, which is why all of its compounds are <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> It is more chemically similar to aluminium than its close neighbors in the periodic table due to having a similar charge-to-radius ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup>
An oxide layer forms around beryllium that prevents further reactions with air unless heated above 1000 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup> Once ignited, beryllium burns brilliantly forming a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_oxide" title="Beryllium oxide">beryllium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_nitride" title="Beryllium nitride">beryllium nitride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup> Beryllium dissolves readily in non-<a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_acid" title="Oxidizing acid">oxidizing acids</a>, such as HCl and diluted H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, but not in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> or water as this forms the oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> This behavior is similar to that of aluminium metal. Beryllium also dissolves in alkali solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BeHydrolysis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="282" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1f/BeHydrolysis.png/220px-BeHydrolysis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/1f/BeHydrolysis.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BeHydrolysis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beryllium hydrolysis as a function of pH<br/>Water molecules attached to Be are omitted</div></div></div>
<p>The beryllium atom has the electronic configuration [He] 2s<sup>2</sup>. The two valence electrons give beryllium a +2 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> and thus the ability to form two <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a>; the only evidence of lower valence of beryllium is in the solubility of the metal in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beryllium_dichloride" title="Beryllium dichloride">BeCl<sub>2</sub></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-31">[31]</a></sup> and in two neutral beryllium bis(carbene) compounds in which the Be center bears a formal oxidation state of zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a>, atoms tend to seek a valence of 8 in order to resemble a <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a>. Beryllium tries to achieve a <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination number</a> of 4 because its two covalent bonds fill half of this octet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Tetracoordination allows beryllium compounds, such as the fluoride or chloride, to form polymers.
</p><p>This characteristic is employed in analytical techniques using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a> as a ligand. EDTA preferentially forms <a href="/wiki/Octahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Octahedral molecular geometry">octahedral complexes</a> – thus absorbing other cations such as Al<sup>3+</sup> which might interfere – for example, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-liquid_extraction" title="Liquid-liquid extraction">solvent extraction</a> of a complex formed between Be<sup>2+</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Acetylacetone" title="Acetylacetone">acetylacetone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Beryllium(II) readily forms complexes with strong donating ligands such as phosphine oxides and arsine oxides. There have been extensive studies of these complexes which show the stability of the O-Be bond.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Solutions of beryllium salts, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_sulfate" title="Beryllium sulfate">beryllium sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_nitrate" title="Beryllium nitrate">beryllium nitrate</a>, are acidic because of hydrolysis of the [Be(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> ion.
</p>
<dl><dd>[Be(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ [Be(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>3</sub>(OH)]<sup>+</sup> + H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Other products of hydrolysis include the <a href="/wiki/Trimer_(chemistry)" title="Trimer (chemistry)">trimeric</a> ion [Be<sub>3</sub>(OH)<sub>3</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_hydroxide" title="Beryllium hydroxide">Beryllium hydroxide</a>, Be(OH)<sub>2</sub>, is insoluble even in acidic solutions with pH less than 6, that is at biological pH. It is <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a> and dissolves in strongly <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkaline</a> solutions.
</p><p>Beryllium forms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_compound" title="Binary compound">binary compounds</a> with many non-metals. <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">Anhydrous</a> <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> are known for <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I</a>. <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_fluoride" title="Beryllium fluoride">BeF<sub>2</sub></a> has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>-like structure with corner-shared BeF<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra. <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_chloride" title="Beryllium chloride">BeCl<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_bromide" title="Beryllium bromide">BeBr<sub>2</sub></a> have chain structures with edge-shared tetrahedra. All beryllium halides have a linear monomeric molecular structure in the gas phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beryllium_difluoride" title="Beryllium difluoride">Beryllium difluoride</a>, BeF<sub>2</sub>, is different than the other <a href="/wiki/Difluoride" title="Difluoride">difluorides</a>.  In general, beryllium has a tendency to bond covalently, much more so than the other <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earths</a> and its fluoride is partially covalent (although still more ionic than its other halides).  BeF<sub>2</sub> has many similarities to <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a> (<a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>) a mostly covalently bonded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_solid" title="Network solid">network solid</a>.  BeF<sub>2</sub> has tetrahedrally coordinated metal and forms glasses (is difficult to crystallize).  When crystalline, beryllium fluoride has the same room temperature crystal structure as quartz and shares many higher temperature structures also.  Beryllium difluoride is very soluble in water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dissolve_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-dissolve-34">[34]</a></sup> unlike the other alkaline earth difluorides. (Although they are strongly ionic, they do not dissolve because of the especially strong <a href="/wiki/Madelung_constant" title="Madelung constant">lattice energy</a> of the fluorite structure.)  However, BeF<sub>2</sub> has much lower electrical conductivity when in solution or when molten than would be expected if it were fully ionic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beryllium_halide_and_pseudohalides_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beryllium_halide_and_pseudohalides-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beryllium_chemistry_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beryllium_chemistry-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMackayMackayHenderson2002243–244_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMackayMackayHenderson2002243–244-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-General_analytical_chemistry_of_beryllium_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-General_analytical_chemistry_of_beryllium-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Beryllium_oxide" title="Beryllium oxide">Beryllium oxide</a>, BeO, is a white <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> solid, which has the <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">wurtzite</a> crystal structure and a thermal conductivity as high as in some metals. BeO is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>. Salts of beryllium can be produced by treating Be(OH)<sub>2</sub> with acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup> Beryllium <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenide" title="Selenide">selenide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telluride_(chemistry)" title="Telluride (chemistry)">telluride</a> are known, all having the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system#Zincblende_structure" title="Cubic crystal system">zincblende structure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Beryllium_nitride" title="Beryllium nitride">Beryllium nitride</a>, Be<sub>3</sub>N<sub>2</sub> is a high-melting-point compound which is readily hydrolyzed. Beryllium azide, BeN<sub>6</sub> is known and beryllium phosphide, Be<sub>3</sub>P<sub>2</sub> has a similar structure to Be<sub>3</sub>N<sub>2</sub>. Basic beryllium nitrate and <a href="/wiki/Basic_beryllium_acetate" title="Basic beryllium acetate">basic beryllium acetate</a> have similar tetrahedral structures with four beryllium atoms coordinated to a central oxide ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-31">[31]</a></sup> A number of beryllium <a href="/wiki/Boride" title="Boride">borides</a> are known, such as Be<sub>5</sub>B, Be<sub>4</sub>B, Be<sub>2</sub>B, BeB<sub>2</sub>, BeB<sub>6</sub> and BeB<sub>12</sub>. <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_carbide" title="Beryllium carbide">Beryllium carbide</a>, Be<sub>2</sub>C, is a refractory brick-red compound that reacts with water to give <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-31">[31]</a></sup> No beryllium <a href="/wiki/Silicide" title="Silicide">silicide</a> has been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The mineral <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a>, which contains beryllium, has been used at least since the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a> of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968535_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968535-39">[39]</a></sup> In the first century <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>, Roman naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> mentioned in his encyclopedia <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">Natural History</a></i> that beryl and <a href="/wiki/Emerald" title="Emerald">emerald</a> ("smaragdus") were similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968536_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968536-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Papyrus_Graecus_Holmiensis" title="Papyrus Graecus Holmiensis">Papyrus Graecus Holmiensis</a>, written in the third or fourth century CE, contains notes on how to prepare artificial emerald and beryl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968536_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968536-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="758" data-file-width="708" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg/170px-Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg/255px-Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg/340px-Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis-Nicolas_Vauquelin" title="Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin">Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin</a> discovered beryllium</div></div></div>
<p>Early analyses of emeralds and beryls by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torbern_Olof_Bergman" title="Torbern Olof Bergman">Torbern Olof Bergman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franz_Karl_Achard" title="Franz Karl Achard">Franz Karl Achard</a>, and Johann Jakob Bindheim always yielded similar elements, leading to the fallacious conclusion that both substances are <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_silicate" title="Aluminium silicate">aluminium silicates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968537_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968537-41">[41]</a></sup> Mineralogist <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Just_Ha%C3%BCy" title="René Just Haüy">René Just Haüy</a> discovered that both crystals are geometrically identical, and he asked chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis-Nicolas_Vauquelin" title="Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin">Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin</a> for a chemical analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968535_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968535-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 1798 paper read before the <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_France" title="Institut de France">Institut de France</a>, Vauquelin reported that he found a new "earth" by dissolving <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">aluminium hydroxide</a> from emerald and beryl in an additional <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The editors of the journal <i>Annales de Chimie et de Physique</i> named the new earth "glucine" for the sweet taste of some of its compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Klaproth preferred the name "beryllina" due to the fact that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">yttria</a> also formed sweet salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968538_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968538-45">[45]</a></sup>  The name "beryllium" was first used by Wöhler in 1828.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1158" data-file-width="891" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg/170px-Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg/255px-Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg/340px-Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler_Stich.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> was one of the men who independently isolated beryllium</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Bussy" title="Antoine Bussy">Antoine Bussy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> independently isolated beryllium in 1828 by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> of metallic <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> with <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_chloride" title="Beryllium chloride">beryllium chloride</a>, as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>BeCl<sub>2</sub> + 2 K → 2 KCl + Be</dd></dl>
<p>Using an alcohol lamp, Wöhler heated alternating layers of beryllium chloride and potassium in a wired-shut platinum crucible. The above reaction immediately took place and caused the crucible to become white hot. Upon cooling and washing the resulting gray-black powder he saw that it was made of fine particles with a dark metallic luster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968539_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968539-49">[49]</a></sup> The highly reactive potassium had been produced by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of its compounds, a process discovered 21 years before. The chemical method using potassium yielded only small grains of beryllium from which no ingot of metal could be cast or hammered.
</p><p>The direct electrolysis of a molten mixture of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_fluoride" title="Beryllium fluoride">beryllium fluoride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a> by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lebeau" title="Paul Lebeau">Paul Lebeau</a> in 1898 resulted in the first pure (99.5 to 99.8%) samples of beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968539_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeeks1968539-49">[49]</a></sup> However, industrial production started only after the First World War. The original industrial involvement included subsidiaries and scientists related to the Union Carbide and Carbon Corporation in Cleveland OH and Siemens &amp; Halske AG in Berlin. In the US, the process was ruled by Hugh S. Cooper, director of The Kemet Laboratories Company. In Germany, the first commercially successful process for producing beryllium was developed in 1921 by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stock" title="Alfred Stock">Alfred Stock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Goldschmidt" title="Hans Goldschmidt">Hans Goldschmidt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>A sample of beryllium was bombarded with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_ray" title="Alpha ray">alpha rays</a> from the decay of <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a> in a 1932 experiment by <a href="/wiki/James_Chadwick" title="James Chadwick">James Chadwick</a> that uncovered the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup> This same method is used in one class of radioisotope-based laboratory <a href="/wiki/Neutron_source" title="Neutron source">neutron sources</a> that produce 30 neutrons for every million α particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merck_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merck-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium production saw a rapid increase during World War II, due to the rising demand for hard beryllium-copper alloys and <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_light" title="Fluorescent light">fluorescent lights</a>. Most early fluorescent lamps used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zinc_orthosilicate" title="Zinc orthosilicate">zinc orthosilicate</a> with varying content of beryllium to emit greenish light. Small additions of magnesium <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a> improved the blue part of the spectrum to yield an acceptable white light. Halophosphate-based phosphors replaced beryllium-based phosphors after beryllium was found to be toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Electrolysis of a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_fluoride" title="Beryllium fluoride">beryllium fluoride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a> was used to isolate beryllium during the 19th century. The metal's high melting point makes this process more energy-consuming than corresponding processes used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_metals" title="Alkali metals">alkali metals</a>. Early in the 20th century, the production of beryllium by the thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_iodide" title="Beryllium iodide">beryllium iodide</a> was investigated following the success of a similar process for the production of <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>, but this process proved to be uneconomical for volume production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pure beryllium metal did not become readily available until 1957, even though it had been used as an alloying metal to harden and toughen copper much earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup> Beryllium could be produced by reducing beryllium compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_chloride" title="Beryllium chloride">beryllium chloride</a> with metallic potassium or sodium. Currently most beryllium is produced by reducing beryllium fluoride with purified <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>. The price on the American market for <a href="/wiki/Casting_(metalworking)" title="Casting (metalworking)">vacuum-cast</a> beryllium ingots was about $338 per pound ($745 per kilogram) in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1998 and 2008, the world's production of beryllium had decreased from 343 to about 200 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>, of which 176 tonnes (88%) came from the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSMCS2000_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSMCS2000-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSMCS2010_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSMCS2010-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>Early precursors of the word <i>beryllium</i> can be traced to many languages, including <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">beryllus</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i lang="fr" title="French language text">béry</i>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">βήρυλλος</span>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">bērullos</i>, 'beryl'; <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a> <span lang="pra" title="Prakrit languages collective text">वॆरुलिय‌</span> (<i lang="pra-Latn" title="Prakrit languages collective text">veruliya</i>); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="Pāli">Pāli</a> <span lang="pi" title="Pali language text">वेलुरिय</span> (<i lang="pi-Latn" title="Pali language text">veḷuriya</i>), <span lang="pi" title="Pali language text">भेलिरु</span> (<i lang="pi-Latn" title="Pali language text">veḷiru</i>) or <span lang="pi" title="Pali language text">भिलर्</span> (<i lang="pi-Latn" title="Pali language text">viḷar</i>) – "to become pale", in reference to the pale semiprecious gemstone beryl. The original source is probably the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word <span lang="sa" title="Sanskrit language text">वैडूर्य</span> (<i lang="sa-Latn" title="Sanskrit language text">vaidurya</i>), which is of <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">South Indian</a> origin and could be related to the name of the modern city of <a href="/wiki/Belur,_Karnataka" title="Belur, Karnataka">Belur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> For about 160 years, beryllium was also known as <span id="Glucinium"></span> <b>glucinum</b> or <b>glucinium</b> (with the accompanying chemical symbol "<b>Gl</b>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> or "<b>G</b>" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>), the name coming from the Ancient Greek word for sweet: <span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%BB%CF%85%CE%BA%CF%8D%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:γλυκύς">γλυκύς</a></span>, due to the sweet taste of beryllium <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_windows">Radiation windows</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beryllium_target.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2700" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Beryllium_target.jpg/220px-Beryllium_target.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Beryllium_target.jpg/330px-Beryllium_target.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Beryllium_target.jpg/440px-Beryllium_target.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beryllium_target.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beryllium target which "converts" a proton beam into a neutron beam</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Be_foil_square.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1003" data-file-width="1192" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Be_foil_square.jpg/220px-Be_foil_square.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Be_foil_square.jpg/330px-Be_foil_square.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Be_foil_square.jpg/440px-Be_foil_square.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Be_foil_square.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A square beryllium foil mounted in a steel case to be used as a window between a vacuum chamber and an <a href="/wiki/X-ray_microscope" title="X-ray microscope">X-ray microscope</a>. Beryllium is highly transparent to X-rays owing to its low <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Because of its low atomic number and very low absorption for X-rays, the oldest and still one of the most important applications of beryllium is in radiation windows for <a href="/wiki/X-ray_tube" title="X-ray tube">X-ray tubes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup> Extreme demands are placed on purity and cleanliness of beryllium to avoid artifacts in the X-ray images. Thin beryllium foils are used as radiation windows for X-ray detectors, and the extremely low absorption minimizes the heating effects caused by high intensity, low energy X-rays typical of <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">synchrotron</a> radiation. Vacuum-tight windows and beam-tubes for radiation experiments on synchrotrons are manufactured exclusively from beryllium. In scientific setups for various X-ray emission studies (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Energy-dispersive_X-ray_spectroscopy" title="Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy">energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy</a>) the sample holder is usually made of beryllium because its emitted X-rays have much lower energies (~100 eV) than X-rays from most studied materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Low <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> also makes beryllium relatively transparent to energetic <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">particles</a>. Therefore, it is used to build the <a href="/wiki/Beamline" title="Beamline">beam pipe</a> around the collision region in <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> setups, such as all four main detector experiments at the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Large_Ion_Collider_Experiment" title="A Large Ion Collider Experiment">ALICE</a>, <a href="/wiki/ATLAS_experiment" title="ATLAS experiment">ATLAS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compact_Muon_Solenoid" title="Compact Muon Solenoid">CMS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LHCb" title="LHCb">LHCb</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Tevatron" title="Tevatron">Tevatron</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLAC" title="SLAC">SLAC</a>. The low density of beryllium allows collision products to reach the surrounding detectors without significant interaction, its stiffness allows a powerful vacuum to be produced within the pipe to minimize interaction with gases, its thermal stability allows it to function correctly at temperatures of only a few degrees above <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>, and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a> nature keeps it from interfering with the complex multipole magnet systems used to steer and <a href="/wiki/Strong_focusing" title="Strong focusing">focus</a> the <a href="/wiki/Particle_beam" title="Particle beam">particle beams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_applications">Mechanical applications</span></h3>
<p>Because of its stiffness, light weight and dimensional stability over a wide temperature range, beryllium metal is used for lightweight structural components in the defense and <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> industries in high-speed <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guided_missile" title="Guided missile">guided missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-fuel_rocket" title="Liquid-fuel rocket">liquid-fuel rockets</a> have used <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_nozzle" title="Rocket engine nozzle">rocket nozzles</a> made of pure beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Beryllium powder was itself studied as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuel</a>, but this use has never materialized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup> A small number of extreme high-end <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_frame" title="Bicycle frame">bicycle frames</a> have been built with beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museum_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-museum-64">[64]</a></sup> From 1998 to 2000, the <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> team used <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> engines with beryllium-aluminium-<a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> pistons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The use of beryllium engine components was banned following a protest by <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Scuderia Ferrari</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mixing about 2.0% beryllium into <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> forms an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> called <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_copper" title="Beryllium copper">beryllium copper</a> that is six times stronger than copper alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGraw-Hill2004_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-Hill2004-67">[67]</a></sup> Beryllium alloys are used in many applications because of their combination of elasticity, high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a>, high strength and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardness_(materials_science)" title="Hardness (materials science)">hardness</a>, nonmagnetic properties, as well as good <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue resistance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> These applications include non-sparking tools that are used near flammable gases (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beryllium nickel (page does not exist)">beryllium nickel</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">springs</a> and membranes (beryllium nickel and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_iron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beryllium iron (page does not exist)">beryllium iron</a>) used in surgical instruments and high temperature devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200158-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> As little as 50 parts per million of beryllium alloyed with liquid <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> leads to a significant increase in oxidation resistance and decrease in flammability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="276" data-file-width="994" height="61" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg/220px-Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg/330px-Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg/440px-Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beryllium_Copper_Adjustable_Wrench.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beryllium Copper Adjustable Wrench</div></div></div>
<p>The high elastic stiffness of beryllium has led to its extensive use in precision instrumentation, e.g. in  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_guidance" title="Inertial guidance">inertial guidance</a> systems and in the support mechanisms for optical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup> Beryllium-copper alloys were also applied as a hardening agent in "Jason pistols", which were used to strip the paint from the hulls of ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium was also used for cantilevers in high performance phonograph cartridge styli, where its extreme stiffness and low density allowed for tracking weights to be reduced to 1 gram, yet still track high frequency passages with minimal distortion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>An earlier major application of beryllium was in <a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">brakes</a> for military <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a> because of its hardness, high melting point, and exceptional ability to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_dissipation" title="Heat dissipation">dissipate heat</a>. Environmental considerations have led to substitution by other materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>To reduce costs, beryllium can be <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloyed</a> with significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/AlBeMet" title="AlBeMet">AlBeMet</a> alloy  (a trade name). This blend is cheaper than pure beryllium, while still retaining many desirable properties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mirrors">Mirrors</span></h3>
<p>Beryllium <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a> are of particular interest. Large-area mirrors, frequently with a <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb_mirror" title="Honeycomb mirror">honeycomb support structure</a>, are used, for example, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_satellite" title="Meteorological satellite">meteorological satellites</a> where low weight and long-term dimensional stability are critical. Smaller beryllium mirrors are used in <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">optical guidance</a> systems and in <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">fire-control systems</a>, e.g. in the German-made <a href="/wiki/Leopard_1" title="Leopard 1">Leopard 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leopard_2" title="Leopard 2">Leopard 2</a> <a href="/wiki/Main_battle_tank" title="Main battle tank">main battle tanks</a>. In these systems, very rapid movement of the mirror is required which again dictates low mass and high rigidity. Usually the beryllium mirror is coated with hard <a href="/wiki/Electroless_nickel_plating" title="Electroless nickel plating">electroless nickel plating</a> which can be more easily polished to a finer optical finish than beryllium. In some applications, though, the beryllium blank is polished without any coating. This is particularly applicable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> operation where thermal expansion mismatch can cause the coating to buckle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> will have 18 hexagonal beryllium sections for its mirrors. Because JWST will face a temperature of 33 K, the mirror is made of gold-plated beryllium, capable of handling extreme cold better than glass. Beryllium contracts and deforms less than glass – and remains more uniform – in such temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> For the same reason, the optics of the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a> are entirely built of beryllium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_applications">Magnetic applications</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG/220px-BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG/330px-BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG/440px-BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BERYLLIUM_-_KUGEL_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sphere Beryllium B52 - <a href="/wiki/Gyrocompass" title="Gyrocompass">Gyrocompass</a></div></div></div>
<p>Beryllium is non-magnetic. Therefore, tools fabricated out of beryllium-based materials are used by naval or military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_ordnance_disposal" title="Explosive ordnance disposal">explosive ordnance disposal</a> teams for work on or near <a href="/wiki/Naval_mine" title="Naval mine">naval mines</a>, since these mines commonly have <a href="/wiki/Fuze" title="Fuze">magnetic fuzes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> They are also found in maintenance and construction materials near <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) machines because of the high magnetic fields generated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In the fields of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_communication" title="Radio communication">radio communications</a> and powerful (usually military) <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radars</a>, hand tools made of beryllium are used to tune the highly magnetic <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystrons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetron" title="Magnetron">magnetrons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traveling_wave_tube" title="Traveling wave tube">traveling wave tubes</a>, etc., that are used for generating high levels of <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> power in the <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_applications">Nuclear applications</span></h3>
<p>Thin plates or foils of beryllium are sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_design" title="Nuclear weapon design">nuclear weapon designs</a> as the very outer layer of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonium_pit" title="Plutonium pit">plutonium pits</a> in the primary stages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_bomb" title="Thermonuclear bomb">thermonuclear bombs</a>, placed to surround the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> material. These layers of beryllium are good "pushers" for the <a href="/wiki/Implosion_(mechanical_process)" title="Implosion (mechanical process)">implosion</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-239" title="Plutonium-239">plutonium-239</a>, and they are good <a href="/wiki/Neutron_reflector" title="Neutron reflector">neutron reflectors</a>, just as in beryllium-moderated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactors" title="Nuclear reactors">nuclear reactors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weapons_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-weapons-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium is also commonly used in some <a href="/wiki/Neutron_source" title="Neutron source">neutron sources</a> in laboratory devices in which relatively few neutrons are needed (rather than having to use a nuclear reactor, or a <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Neutron_generator" title="Neutron generator">neutron generator</a>). For this purpose, a target of beryllium-9 is bombarded with energetic alpha particles from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotope</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>-210, <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>-226, <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>-238, or <a href="/wiki/Americium" title="Americium">americium</a>-241. In the nuclear reaction that occurs, a beryllium nucleus is <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_transmutation" title="Nuclear transmutation">transmuted</a> into carbon-12, and one free neutron is emitted, traveling in about the same direction as the alpha particle was heading. Such <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> driven beryllium neutron sources, named <a href="/wiki/Modulated_neutron_initiator" title="Modulated neutron initiator">"urchin"</a> neutron initiators, were used in some early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bombs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weapons_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-weapons-76">[76]</a></sup> Neutron sources in which beryllium is bombarded with <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_decay" title="Gamma decay">gamma decay</a> radioisotope, are also used to produce laboratory neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:251px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="139" data-file-width="249" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg" width="249"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CANDU_fuel_bundles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two CANDU fuel bundles: Each about 50 cm in length and 10 cm in diameter.  Notice the small appendages on the fuel clad surfaces</div></div></div>
<p>Beryllium is also used in fuel fabrication for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CANDU" title="CANDU">CANDU</a> reactors.  The fuel elements have small appendages that are resistance brazed to the fuel cladding using an induction brazing process with Be as the braze filler material.  Bearing pads are brazed in place to prevent fuel bundle to pressure tube contact, and inter-element spacer pads are brazed on to prevent element to element contact.
</p><p>Beryllium is also used at the <a href="/wiki/Joint_European_Torus" title="Joint European Torus">Joint European Torus</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear-fusion research laboratory</a>, and it will be used in the more advanced <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> to condition the components which face the plasma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Beryllium has also been proposed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cladding_(nuclear_fuel)" title="Cladding (nuclear fuel)">cladding</a> material for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_rod" title="Nuclear fuel rod">nuclear fuel rods</a>, because of its good combination of mechanical, chemical, and nuclear properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_fluoride" title="Beryllium fluoride">Beryllium fluoride</a> is one of the constituent salts of the eutectic salt mixture <a href="/wiki/FLiBe" title="FLiBe">FLiBe</a>, which is used as a solvent, moderator and coolant in many hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Molten_salt_reactor" title="Molten salt reactor">molten salt reactor</a> designs, including the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fluoride_thorium_reactor" title="Liquid fluoride thorium reactor">liquid fluoride thorium reactor</a> (LFTR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">Acoustics</span></h3>
<p>The low weight and high rigidity of beryllium make it useful as a material for high-frequency <a href="/wiki/Speaker_driver" title="Speaker driver">speaker drivers</a>. Because beryllium is expensive (many times more than <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>), hard to shape due to its brittleness, and toxic if mishandled, beryllium <a href="/wiki/Tweeter" title="Tweeter">tweeters</a> are limited to high-end home,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro_audio" title="Pro audio">pro audio</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">public address</a> applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Some high-fidelity products have been fraudulently claimed to be made of the material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some high-end <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_cartridge" title="Magnetic cartridge">phonograph cartridges</a> used beryllium cantilevers to improve tracking by reducing mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic">Electronic</span></h3>
<p>Beryllium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-type_semiconductor" title="P-type semiconductor">p-type</a> <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in <a href="/wiki/List_of_semiconductor_materials" title="List of semiconductor materials">III-V compound semiconductors</a>. It is widely used in materials such as <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">GaAs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AlGaAs" title="AlGaAs">AlGaAs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGaAs" title="InGaAs">InGaAs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InAlAs" title="InAlAs">InAlAs</a> grown by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_beam_epitaxy" title="Molecular beam epitaxy">molecular beam epitaxy</a> (MBE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Cross-rolled beryllium sheet is an excellent structural support for <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit boards</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surface-mount_technology" title="Surface-mount technology">surface-mount technology</a>. In critical electronic applications, beryllium is both a structural support and <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">heat sink</a>. The application also requires a coefficient of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">thermal expansion</a> that is well matched to the alumina and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass-reinforced_plastic" title="Glass-reinforced plastic">polyimide-glass</a> <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrates</a>. The beryllium-beryllium oxide <a href="/wiki/Metal_matrix_composite" title="Metal matrix composite">composite</a> "<a href="/wiki/E-Material" title="E-Material">E-Materials</a>" have been specially designed for these electronic applications and have the additional advantage that the thermal expansion coefficient can be tailored to match diverse substrate materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Be_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Be-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Beryllium_oxide" title="Beryllium oxide">Beryllium oxide</a> is useful for many applications that require the combined properties of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_insulator" title="Electrical insulator">electrical insulator</a> and an excellent heat conductor, with high strength and hardness, and a very high melting point. Beryllium oxide is frequently used as an insulator base plate in <a href="/wiki/Power_semiconductor_device" title="Power semiconductor device">high-power</a> <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a> for telecommunications. Beryllium oxide is also being studied for use in increasing the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> pellets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Beryllium compounds were used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_light" title="Fluorescent light">fluorescent lighting</a> tubes, but this use was discontinued because of the disease <a href="/wiki/Berylliosis" title="Berylliosis">berylliosis</a> which developed in the workers who were making the tubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span></h3>
<p>Beryllium is a component of several <a href="/wiki/Dental_material" title="Dental material">dental alloys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElshahawyWatanabe2014_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-ElshahawyWatanabe2014-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_safety_and_health">Occupational safety and health</span></h2>
<p>Beryllium is a health and safety issue for workers. Exposure to beryllium in the workplace can lead to a sensitization immune response and can over time develop <a href="/wiki/Berylliosis" title="Berylliosis">chronic beryllium disease</a> (CBD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-92">[92]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) in the United States researches these effects in collaboration with a major manufacturer of beryllium products. The goal of this research is to prevent sensitization and CBD by developing a better understanding of the work processes and exposures that may present a potential risk for workers, and to develop effective interventions that will reduce the risk for adverse health effects. NIOSH also conducts genetic research on sensitization and CBD, independently of this collaboration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cdc.gov_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-cdc.gov-92">[92]</a></sup> The NIOSH Manual of Analytical Methods contains methods for measuring occupational exposures to beryllium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Approximately 35 micrograms of beryllium is found in the average human body, an amount not considered harmful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157-95">[95]</a></sup> Beryllium is chemically similar to <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and therefore can displace it from <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, which causes them to malfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157-95">[95]</a></sup> Because Be<sup>2+</sup> is a highly charged and small ion, it can easily get into many tissues and cells, where it specifically targets cell nuclei, inhibiting many enzymes, including those used for synthesizing DNA. Its toxicity is exacerbated by the fact that the body has no means to control beryllium levels, and once inside the body the beryllium cannot be removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venugopal_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venugopal-96">[96]</a></sup> Chronic <a href="/wiki/Berylliosis" title="Berylliosis">berylliosis</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary" title="Pulmonary">pulmonary</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Granuloma" title="Granuloma">granulomatous</a> disease caused by inhalation of dust or fumes contaminated with beryllium; either large amounts over a short time or small amounts over a long time can lead to this ailment. Symptoms of the disease can take up to five years to develop; about a third of patients with it die and the survivors are left disabled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157_95-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley200157-95">[95]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer" title="International Agency for Research on Cancer">International Agency for Research on Cancer</a> (IARC) lists beryllium and beryllium compounds as <a href="/wiki/List_of_IARC_Group_1_carcinogens" title="List of IARC Group 1 carcinogens">Category 1 carcinogens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has designated a <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> (PEL) in the workplace with a time-weighted average (TWA) 0.002 mg/m<sup>3</sup>  and a constant exposure limit of 0.005 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over 30 minutes, with a maximum peak limit of 0.025 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of constant 0.0005 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">IDLH</a> (immediately dangerous to life and health) value is 4 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>The toxicity of finely divided beryllium (dust or powder, mainly encountered in industrial settings where beryllium is produced or machined) is very well-documented. Solid beryllium metal does not carry the same hazards as airborne inhaled dust, but any hazard associated with physical contact is poorly documented. Workers handling finished beryllium pieces are routinely advised to handle them with gloves, both as a precaution and because many if not most applications of beryllium cannot tolerate residue of skin contact such as fingerprints.
</p><p>Acute beryllium disease in the form of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_pneumonitis" title="Chemical pneumonitis">chemical pneumonitis</a> was first reported in Europe in 1933 and in the United States in 1943. A survey found that about 5% of workers in plants manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a> in 1949 in the United States had beryllium-related lung diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley20015_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley20015-99">[99]</a></sup> Chronic berylliosis resembles <a href="/wiki/Sarcoidosis" title="Sarcoidosis">sarcoidosis</a> in many respects, and the <a href="/wiki/Differential_diagnosis" title="Differential diagnosis">differential diagnosis</a> is often difficult. It killed some early workers in nuclear weapons design, such as <a href="/wiki/Herbert_L._Anderson" title="Herbert L. Anderson">Herbert L. Anderson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beryllium may be found in coal slag. When the slag is formulated into an abrasive agent for blasting paint and rust from hard surfaces, the beryllium can become airborne and become a source of exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early researchers tasted beryllium and its various compounds for sweetness in order to verify its presence. Modern diagnostic equipment no longer necessitates this highly risky procedure and no attempt should be made to ingest this highly toxic substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deGruyter_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-deGruyter-5">[5]</a></sup> Beryllium and its compounds should be handled with great care and special precautions must be taken when carrying out any activity which could result in the release of beryllium dust (<a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> is a possible result of prolonged exposure to beryllium-laden dust). Although the use of beryllium compounds in fluorescent lighting tubes was discontinued in 1949, potential for exposure to beryllium exists in the nuclear and aerospace industries and in the refining of beryllium metal and melting of beryllium-containing alloys, the manufacturing of electronic devices, and the handling of other beryllium-containing material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>A successful test for beryllium in air and on surfaces has been recently developed and published as an international voluntary consensus standard ASTM D7202. The procedure uses dilute <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_bifluoride" title="Ammonium bifluoride">ammonium bifluoride</a> for dissolution and fluorescence detection with beryllium bound to sulfonated hydroxybenzoquinoline, allowing up to 100 times more sensitive detection than the recommended limit for beryllium concentration in the workplace. Fluorescence increases with increasing beryllium concentration. The new procedure has been successfully tested on a variety of surfaces and is effective for the dissolution and ultratrace detection of refractory beryllium oxide and siliceous beryllium (ASTM D7458).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
