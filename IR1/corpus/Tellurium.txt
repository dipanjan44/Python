<p><b>Tellurium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Te</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 52. It is a brittle, mildly toxic, rare, silver-white <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloid</a>. Tellurium is chemically related to <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>. It is occasionally found in native form as elemental crystals. Tellurium is far more common in the universe as a whole than on Earth. Its extreme <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">rarity</a> in the Earth's crust, comparable to that of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, is due partly to its high atomic number, but also to its formation of a volatile <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydride</a> which caused it to be lost to space as a gas during the hot nebular formation of the planet.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gold_chalcogenides" title="Gold chalcogenides">Tellurium-bearing compounds</a> were first discovered in 1782 in a gold mine in <a href="/wiki/Zlatna" title="Zlatna">Zlatna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> by <a href="/wiki/Austrians" title="Austrians">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogist</a> <a href="/wiki/Franz-Joseph_M%C3%BCller_von_Reichenstein" title="Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein">Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein</a>, although it was <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a> who named the new element in 1798 after the Latin word for "earth", <i>tellus</i>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_telluride" title="Gold telluride">Gold telluride</a> minerals are the most notable natural gold compounds. However, they are not a commercially significant source of tellurium itself, which is normally extracted as a by-product of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> production.
</p><p>Commercially, the primary use of tellurium is copper and steel <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>, where it improves <a href="/wiki/Machinability" title="Machinability">machinability</a>. Applications in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CdTe_solar_panel" title="CdTe solar panel">CdTe solar panels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">semiconductors</a> also consume a considerable portion of tellurium production.
</p><p>Tellurium has no biological function, although fungi can use it in place of sulfur and selenium in <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">tellurocysteine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">telluromethionine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tellurium-fungi_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-tellurium-fungi-6">[6]</a></sup> In humans, tellurium is partly metabolized into <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_telluride" title="Dimethyl telluride">dimethyl telluride</a>, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Te, a gas with a <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>-like odor exhaled in the breath of victims of tellurium exposure or poisoning.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tellurium has two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotropes</a>, crystalline and amorphous. When <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a>, tellurium is silvery-white with a metallic luster. It is a brittle and easily pulverized metalloid. Amorphous tellurium is a black-brown powder prepared by precipitating it from a solution of <a href="/wiki/Tellurous_acid" title="Tellurous acid">tellurous acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Telluric_acid" title="Telluric acid">telluric acid</a> (Te(OH)<sub>6</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lan_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lan-7">[7]</a></sup> Tellurium is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> that shows a greater electrical conductivity in certain directions depending on <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> alignment; the conductivity increases slightly when exposed to light (<a href="/wiki/Photoconductivity" title="Photoconductivity">photoconductivity</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> When molten, tellurium is corrosive to copper, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. Of the <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogens</a>, tellurium has the highest melting and boiling points, at 722.66 K (841.12 °F) and 1,261 K (1,810 °F), respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tellurium adopts a polymeric structure consisting of zig-zag chains of Te atoms. This gray material resists oxidation by air and is not volatile.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_tellurium" title="Isotopes of tellurium">Isotopes of tellurium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring tellurium has eight isotopes. Six of those isotopes, <sup>120</sup>Te, <sup>122</sup>Te, <sup>123</sup>Te, <sup>124</sup>Te, <sup>125</sup>Te, and <sup>126</sup>Te, are stable. The other two, <sup>128</sup>Te and <sup>130</sup>Te, have been found to be slightly radioactive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tellurium_128_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tellurium_128-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> with extremely long half-lives, including 2.2 × 10<sup>24</sup> years for <sup>128</sup>Te.  This is the longest known half-life among all <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and is about 160 <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">trillion</a> (10<sup>12</sup>) times the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the known universe</a>. Stable isotopes comprise only 33.2% of naturally occurring tellurium.
</p><p>A further 30 artificial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> of tellurium are known, with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic masses</a> ranging from 105 to 142 and with half-lives of 19 days or less. Also, 17 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> are known, with half-lives up to 154 days. Tellurium (<sup>106</sup>Te to <sup>110</sup>Te ) are among the lightest elements known to undergo alpha decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NUBASE_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The atomic mass of tellurium (127.60 g·mol<sup>−1</sup>) exceeds that of iodine (126.90 g·mol<sup>−1</sup>), the next element in the periodic table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emsley_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>With an abundance in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> comparable to that of platinum (about 1 µg/kg), tellurium is one of the rarest stable solid elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  In comparison, even the rarest of the stable <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> have crustal abundances of 500 µg/kg (see <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">Abundance of the chemical elements</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>This rarity of tellurium in the Earth's crust is not a reflection of its cosmic abundance.  Tellurium is more abundant than <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> in the cosmos, though rubidium is 10,000 times more abundant in the Earth's crust. The rarity of tellurium on Earth is thought to be caused by conditions during the formation of the Earth, when the stable form of certain elements, in the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, was controlled by the reductive power of free <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. Under this scenario, certain elements that form volatile <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydrides</a>, such as tellurium, were severely depleted through evaporation of these hydrides. Tellurium and selenium are the heavy elements most depleted by this process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Tellurium is sometimes found in its native (i.e., elemental) form, but is more often found as the tellurides of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Calaverite" title="Calaverite">calaverite</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Krennerite" title="Krennerite">krennerite</a> (two different <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> of AuTe<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Petzite" title="Petzite">petzite</a>, Ag<sub>3</sub>AuTe<sub>2</sub>, and <a href="/wiki/Sylvanite" title="Sylvanite">sylvanite</a>, AgAuTe<sub>4</sub>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Telluride,_Colorado" title="Telluride, Colorado">Telluride, Colorado</a>, was named in hope of a strike of gold telluride (which never materialized, though gold metal ore was found). Gold itself is usually found uncombined, but when found as a chemical compound, it is most often combined with tellurium.
</p><p>Although tellurium is found with gold more often than in uncombined form, it is found even more often combined as tellurides of more common metals (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Melonite" title="Melonite">melonite</a>, NiTe<sub>2</sub>). Natural <a href="/wiki/Tellurite" title="Tellurite">tellurite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tellurate" title="Tellurate">tellurate</a> minerals also occur, formed by oxidation of tellurides near the Earth's surface. In contrast to selenium, tellurium does not usually replace sulfur in minerals because of the great difference in ion radii. Thus, many common sulfide minerals contain substantial quantities of selenium and only traces of tellurium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the gold rush of 1893, miners in <a href="/wiki/Kalgoorlie" title="Kalgoorlie">Kalgoorlie</a> discarded a pyritic material as they searched for pure gold, and it was used to fill in potholes and build sidewalks. In 1896, that tailing was discovered to be <a href="/wiki/Calaverite" title="Calaverite">calaverite</a>, a telluride of gold, and it sparked a second gold rush that included mining the streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg"><img alt="Oval black and white engraving of a man looking left with a scarf and a coat with large buttons." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1108" data-file-width="848" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg/220px-Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg/330px-Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg/440px-Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Klaproth</a> named the new element and credited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_M%C3%BCller_von_Reichenstein" title="Franz Joseph Müller von Reichenstein">von Reichenstein</a> with its discovery</div></div></div>
<p>Tellurium (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>tellus</i> meaning "earth") was discovered in the 18th century in a gold ore from the mines in <a href="/wiki/Zlatna" title="Zlatna">Zlatna</a>, near today's city of <a href="/wiki/Alba_Iulia" title="Alba Iulia">Alba Iulia</a>, Romania. This ore was known as "Faczebajer weißes blättriges Golderz" (white leafy gold ore from Faczebaja, German name of Facebánya, now Fața Băii in <a href="/wiki/Alba_County" title="Alba County">Alba County</a>) or <i>antimonalischer Goldkies</i> (antimonic gold pyrite), and according to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anton_von_Rupprecht&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anton von Rupprecht (page does not exist)">Anton von Rupprecht</a>, was <i>Spießglaskönig</i> (<i>argent molybdique</i>), containing native <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1782 <a href="/wiki/Franz-Joseph_M%C3%BCller_von_Reichenstein" title="Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein">Franz-Joseph Müller von Reichenstein</a>, who was then serving as the Austrian chief inspector of mines in Transylvania, concluded that the ore did not contain antimony but was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bismuth_sulfide" title="Bismuth sulfide">bismuth sulfide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The following year, he reported that this was erroneous and that the ore contained mostly gold and an unknown metal very similar to antimony. After a thorough investigation that lasted three years and included more than fifty tests, Müller determined the <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> of the mineral and noted that when heated, the new metal gives off a white smoke with a <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radish</a>-like odor; that it imparts a red color to <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>; and that when this solution is diluted with water, it has a black precipitate. Nevertheless, he was not able to identify this metal and gave it the names <i>aurum paradoxium</i> (paradoxical gold) and <i>metallum problematicum</i> (problem metal), because it did not exhibit the properties predicted for antimony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChiuZ_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChiuZ-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1789, a Hungarian scientist, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1l_Kitaibel" title="Pál Kitaibel">Pál Kitaibel</a>, discovered the element independently in an ore from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deutsch-Pilsen" title="Deutsch-Pilsen">Deutsch-Pilsen</a> that had been regarded as argentiferous <a href="/wiki/Molybdenite" title="Molybdenite">molybdenite</a>, but later he gave the credit to Müller. In 1798, it was named by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heinrich_Klaproth" title="Martin Heinrich Klaproth">Martin Heinrich Klaproth</a>, who had earlier isolated it from the mineral <a href="/wiki/Calaverite" title="Calaverite">calaverite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChiuZ_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChiuZ-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks2-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1960s brought an increase in thermoelectric applications for tellurium (as <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_telluride" title="Bismuth telluride">bismuth telluride</a>), and in free-machining <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> alloys, which became the dominant use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2007Yb_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2007Yb-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principal source of tellurium is from <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> <a href="/wiki/Sludge" title="Sludge">sludges</a> from the electrolytic refining of blister <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. It is a component of dusts from <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> refining of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. Treatment of 1000 tons of copper ore typically yields one kilogram (2.2 pounds) of tellurium. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg"><img alt="Grey and white world map with four countries colored to show the percentage of worldwide tellurium production. US to produce 40%; Peru 30%; Japan 20% and Canada 10%." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg/310px-World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg/465px-World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg/620px-World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Tellurium_Production_2006.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tellurium production 2006</div></div></div>
<p>The anode sludges contain the <a href="/wiki/Selenide" title="Selenide">selenides</a> and tellurides of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_metals" title="Noble metals">noble metals</a> in compounds with the formula M<sub>2</sub>Se or M<sub>2</sub>Te (M = Cu, Ag, Au). At temperatures of 500 °C the anode sludges are roasted with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> under air. The metal ions are reduced to the metals, while the telluride is converted to <a href="/wiki/Sodium_tellurite" title="Sodium tellurite">sodium tellurite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg2001_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg2001-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>M<sub>2</sub>Te + O<sub>2</sub> + Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>TeO<sub>3</sub> + 2 M + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tellurite_(ion)" title="Tellurite (ion)">Tellurites</a> can be leached from the mixture with water and are normally present as hydrotellurites HTeO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> in solution. <a href="/wiki/Selenite_(ion)" title="Selenite (ion)">Selenites</a> are also formed during this process, but they can be separated by adding <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. The hydrotellurites are converted into the insoluble <a href="/wiki/Tellurium_dioxide" title="Tellurium dioxide">tellurium dioxide</a> while the selenites stay in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg2001_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg2001-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">HTeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + OH<sup>−</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → TeO<sub>2</sub> + <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The metal is produced from the oxide (reduced) either by electrolysis or by reacting the <a href="/wiki/Tellurium_dioxide" title="Tellurium dioxide">tellurium dioxide</a> with sulfur dioxide in sulfuric acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiberg2001_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiberg2001-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>TeO<sub>2</sub> + 2 SO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → Te + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 4 H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Commercial-grade tellurium is usually marketed as 200-<a href="/wiki/Mesh_(scale)" title="Mesh (scale)">mesh</a> powder but is also available as slabs, ingots, sticks, or lumps. The year-end price for tellurium in 2000 was <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>14 per pound. In recent years, the tellurium price was driven up by increased demand and limited supply, reaching as high as <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>100 per pound in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>  Despite the expectation that improved production methods will double production, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> (DoE) anticipates a supply shortfall of tellurium by 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tellurium is produced mainly in the United States, Peru, Japan and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bypr_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-bypr-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a> gives the following production numbers for 2009: United States 50 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">t</a>, Peru 7 t, Japan 40 t and Canada 16 t.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BGS_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-BGS-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: the categories <a href="/wiki/Category:Tellurium_compounds" title="Category:Tellurium compounds">Tellurium compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Telluride_minerals" title="Category:Telluride minerals">Telluride minerals</a>.</div>
<p>Tellurium belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogen</a> (group 16) family of elements on the periodic table, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>:  Tellurium and selenium compounds are similar. Tellurium exhibits the oxidation states −2, +2, +4 and +6, with +4 being most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lan_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lan-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Tellurides</dt></dl>
<p>Reduction of Te metal produces the <a href="/wiki/Telluride_(chemistry)" title="Telluride (chemistry)">tellurides</a> and polytellurides, Te<sub>n</sub><sup>2−</sup>. The −2 oxidation state is exhibited in binary compounds with many metals, such as zinc telluride, <span class="chemf nowrap">ZnTe</span>, produced by heating tellurium with zinc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roscoe_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-roscoe-34">[34]</a></sup> Decomposition of <span class="chemf nowrap">ZnTe</span> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> yields <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_telluride" title="Hydrogen telluride">hydrogen telluride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Te</span>), a highly unstable analogue of the other chalcogen hydrides, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)"><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide"><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S</span></a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_selenide" title="Hydrogen selenide"><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Se</span></a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>ZnTe + 2 HCl → <span class="chemf nowrap">ZnCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Te</span></dd></dl>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Te</span> is unstable, whereas salts of its conjugate base [TeH]<sup>−</sup> are stable.
</p>
<dl><dt>Halides</dt></dl>
<p>The +2 oxidation state is exhibited by the dihalides, <span class="chemf nowrap">TeCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">TeBr<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">TeI<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. The dihalides have not been obtained in pure form,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sykes1990_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-sykes1990-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>274</span></sup> although they are known decomposition products of the tetrahalides in organic solvents, and the derived tetrahalotellurates are well-characterized:
</p>
<dl><dd>Te + <span class="chemf nowrap">X<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">X<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">TeX<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></dd></dl>

<p>Tellurium does not readily form analogues of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a>, with the functional group –TeH, that are called <a href="/wiki/Tellurol" title="Tellurol">tellurols</a>. The –TeH functional group is also attributed using the prefix <i>tellanyl-</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Like <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_telluride" title="Hydrogen telluride">H<sub>2</sub>Te</a>, these species are unstable with respect to loss of hydrogen. Telluraethers (R–Te–R) are more stable, as are <a href="/wiki/Telluroxide" title="Telluroxide">telluroxides</a>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metallurgy">Metallurgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Metallurgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest consumer of tellurium is <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and lead alloys. The addition to steel and copper produces an alloy more machinable than otherwise. It is alloyed into <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> for promoting chill for spectroscopy, where the presence of electrically conductive free graphite tends to interfere with spark emission testing results. In lead, tellurium improves strength and durability, and decreases the corrosive action of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS2007Yb_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS2007Yb-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductor_and_electronic_industry_uses">Semiconductor and electronic industry uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Semiconductor and electronic industry uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NREL_Array.jpg"><img alt="Solar panels, angled at about 30 degrees, reflect the blue sky from above a grassy field." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NREL_Array.jpg/200px-NREL_Array.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NREL_Array.jpg/300px-NREL_Array.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NREL_Array.jpg/400px-NREL_Array.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NREL_Array.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">CdTe</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_array" title="Photovoltaic array">photovoltaic array</a></div></div></div>
<p>Tellurium is used in <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a> (CdTe) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a>. <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> lab tests of tellurium demonstrated some of the greatest efficiencies for solar cell electric power generators. Massive commercial production of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride_photovoltaics" title="Cadmium telluride photovoltaics">CdTe solar panels</a> by <a href="/wiki/First_Solar" title="First Solar">First Solar</a> in recent years has significantly increased tellurium demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Replacing some of the cadmium in CdTe by <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_zinc_telluride" title="Cadmium zinc telluride">(Cd,Zn)Te</a>, produces a solid-state <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> detector, providing an alternative to single-use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_badge" title="Film badge">film badges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a> sensitive <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> material is formed by alloying tellurium with cadmium and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HgCdTe" title="HgCdTe">mercury cadmium telluride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotellurium_compound" title="Organotellurium compound">Organotellurium compounds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_telluride" title="Dimethyl telluride">dimethyl telluride</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diethyl_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diethyl telluride (page does not exist)">diethyl telluride</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diisopropyl_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diisopropyl telluride (page does not exist)">diisopropyl telluride</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diallyl_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diallyl telluride (page does not exist)">diallyl telluride</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_allyl_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Methyl allyl telluride (page does not exist)">methyl allyl telluride</a> are precursors for synthesizing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapor_phase_epitaxy" title="Metalorganic vapor phase epitaxy">metalorganic vapor phase epitaxy</a> growth of II-VI <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_semiconductor" title="Compound semiconductor">compound semiconductors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Diisopropyl telluride (DIPTe) is the preferred precursor for low-temperature growth of CdHgTe by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOVPE" title="MOVPE">MOVPE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The greatest purity <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalorganics" title="Metalorganics">metalorganics</a> of both <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and tellurium are used in these processes. The compounds for semiconductor industry and are prepared by <a href="/wiki/Adduct_purification" title="Adduct purification">adduct purification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tellurium, as tellurium suboxide, is used in the media layer of rewritable <a href="/wiki/Optical_disc" title="Optical disc">optical discs</a>, including <a href="/wiki/CD-RW" title="CD-RW">ReWritable Compact Discs</a> (<a href="/wiki/CD-RW" title="CD-RW">CD-RW</a>), ReWritable Digital Video Discs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DVD-RW" title="DVD-RW">DVD-RW</a>), and ReWritable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray Discs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tellurium_dioxide" title="Tellurium dioxide">Tellurium dioxide</a> is used to create <a href="/wiki/Acousto-optic_modulator" title="Acousto-optic modulator">acousto-optic modulators</a> (AOTFs and AOBSs) for confocal microscropy.
</p><p>Tellurium is used in the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_change_memory" title="Phase change memory">phase change memory</a> chips<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> developed by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_telluride" title="Bismuth telluride">Bismuth telluride</a> (Bi<sub>2</sub>Te<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Lead_telluride" title="Lead telluride">lead telluride</a> are working elements of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoelectric" title="Thermoelectric">thermoelectric</a> devices.  <a href="/wiki/Lead_telluride" title="Lead telluride">Lead telluride</a> is used in far-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> detectors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Tellurium compounds are used as pigments for <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>Selenides and tellurides greatly increase the optical refraction of glass widely used in <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">glass optical fibers</a> for telecommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mixtures of selenium and tellurium are used with <a href="/wiki/Barium_peroxide" title="Barium peroxide">barium peroxide</a> as an oxidizer in the delay powder of electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blasting_cap" title="Blasting cap">blasting caps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>Organic tellurides have been employed as initiators for living radical polymerization and electron-rich mono- and di-tellurides possess <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> activity.</li>
<li>Rubber can be vulcanized with tellurium instead of sulfur or selenium. The rubber produced in this way shows improved heat resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tellurite_agar" title="Tellurite agar">Tellurite agar</a> is used to identify members of the <a href="/wiki/Corynebacterium" title="Corynebacterium">corynebacterium</a> genus, most typically <a href="/wiki/Corynebacterium_diphtheriae" title="Corynebacterium diphtheriae">Corynebacterium diphtheriae</a>, the pathogen responsible for <a href="/wiki/Diphtheria" title="Diphtheria">diphtheria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>Tellurium is a key constituent of high performing mixed oxide catalysts for the heterogeneous catalytic selective oxidation of propane to acrylic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The surface elemental composition changes dynamically and reversibly with the reaction conditions. In the presence of steam the surface of the catalyst is enriched in tellurium and vanadium which translates into the enhancement of the acrylic acid production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">Neutron</a> bombardment of tellurium is the most common way to produce <a href="/wiki/Iodine-131" title="Iodine-131">iodine-131</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> This in turn is used to treat some <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> conditions, and as a tracer compound in <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a>, among other applications.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tellurium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tellurium has no known biological function, although fungi can incorporate it in place of sulfur and selenium into amino acids such as telluro-<a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a> and telluro-<a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tellurium-fungi_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-tellurium-fungi-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Organisms have shown a highly variable tolerance to tellurium compounds. Many bacteria, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudomonas_aeruginosa" title="Pseudomonas aeruginosa">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</a></i>, take up tellurite and reduce it to elemental tellurium, which accumulates and causes a characteristic and often dramatic darkening of cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In yeast, this reduction is mediated by the sulfate assimilation pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Tellurium accumulation seems to account for a major part of the toxicity effects. Many organisms also metabolize tellurium partly to form dimethyl telluride, although dimethyl ditelluride is also formed by some species. Dimethyl telluride has been observed in hot springs at very low concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Tellurium and tellurium compounds are considered to be mildly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> and need to be handled with care, although acute poisoning is rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-75">[75]</a></sup> Tellurium poisoning is particularly difficult to treat as many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelating_agents" title="Chelating agents">chelation agents</a> used in the treatment of metal poisoning will increase the toxicity of tellurium. Tellurium is not reported to be carcinogenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans exposed to as little as 0.01 mg/m<sup>3</sup> or less in air exude a foul <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>-like odor known as "tellurium breath".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-76">[76]</a></sup>
This is caused by the body converting tellurium from any oxidation state to <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_telluride" title="Dimethyl telluride">dimethyl telluride</a>, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Te. This is a volatile compound with a pungent garlic-like smell. Even though the metabolic pathways of tellurium are not known, it is generally assumed that they resemble those of the more extensively studied <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> because the final methylated metabolic products of the two elements are similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to tellurium in the workplace by inhalation, ingestion, skin contact, and eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) limits (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) tellurium exposure in the workplace to 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an eight-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set the <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) at 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup>  over an eight-hour workday. In concentrations of 25 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, tellurium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">immediately dangerous to life and health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
