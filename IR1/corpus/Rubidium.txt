<p><b>Rubidium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Rb</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 37. Rubidium is a soft, silvery-white <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metallic</a> element of the <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a> group, with a <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weight</a> of 85.4678. Elemental rubidium is highly reactive, with properties similar to those of other alkali metals, including rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>. On Earth, natural rubidium comprises two <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: 72% is the stable isotope, <sup>85</sup>Rb; 28% is the slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <sup>87</sup>Rb, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 49 billion years—more than three times longer than the estimated <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a>.
</p><p>German chemists <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> discovered rubidium in 1861 by the newly developed technique, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_emission_spectroscopy#Flame_emission_spectroscopy" title="Atomic emission spectroscopy">flame spectroscopy</a>.
</p><p>Rubidium's compounds have various chemical and electronic applications. Rubidium metal is easily vaporized and has a convenient spectral absorption range, making it a frequent target for <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> manipulation of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>.
</p><p>Rubidium is not a known nutrient for any <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">living organisms</a>. However, rubidium <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> have the same charge as potassium ions, and are actively taken up and treated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_cell" title="Animal cell">animal cells</a> in similar ways.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RbH.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/RbH.JPG/220px-RbH.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/RbH.JPG/330px-RbH.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/RbH.JPG/440px-RbH.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RbH.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Partially molten rubidium metal in an ampoule</div></div></div>
<p>Rubidium is a very soft, <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a>, silvery-white metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohly_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ohly-6">[6]</a></sup> It is the second most <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electropositive</a> of the stable alkali metals and melts at a temperature of 39.3 °C (102.7 °F). Like other alkali metals, rubidium metal reacts violently with water. As with potassium (which is slightly less reactive) and caesium (which is slightly more reactive), this reaction is usually vigorous enough to ignite the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas it produces. Rubidium has also been reported to ignite spontaneously in air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohly_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ohly-6">[6]</a></sup> It forms <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgams</a> with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, but not <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> (even though rubidium and lithium are in the same group).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg/220px-Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg/330px-Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg/440px-Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rubidium crystals (silvery) compared to <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> crystals (golden)</div></div></div>
<p>Rubidium has a very low <a href="/wiki/Ionization_energy" title="Ionization energy">ionization energy</a> of only 406 kJ/mol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Rubidium and potassium show a very similar purple color in the <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a>, and distinguishing the two elements requires more sophisticated analysis, such as spectroscopy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Rubidium_compounds" title="Category:Rubidium compounds">Category:Rubidium compounds</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rb9O2_cluster.png"><img alt="The ball-and-stick diagram shows two regular octahedra which are connected to each other by one face. All nine vertices of the structure are purple spheres representing rubidium, and at the centre of each octahedron is a small red sphere representing oxygen." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="909" data-file-width="669" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Rb9O2_cluster.png/110px-Rb9O2_cluster.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Rb9O2_cluster.png/165px-Rb9O2_cluster.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Rb9O2_cluster.png/220px-Rb9O2_cluster.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rb9O2_cluster.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><span class="chemf nowrap">Rb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> cluster</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rubidium_chloride" title="Rubidium chloride">Rubidium chloride</a> (RbCl) is probably the most used rubidium compound: among several other chlorides, it is used to induce living cells to take up <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>; it is also used as a biomarker, because in nature, it is found only in small quantities in living organisms and when present, replaces potassium. Other common rubidium compounds are the corrosive <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_hydroxide" title="Rubidium hydroxide">rubidium hydroxide</a> (RbOH), the starting material for most rubidium-based chemical processes; <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_carbonate" title="Rubidium carbonate">rubidium carbonate</a> (Rb<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>), used in some optical glasses, and rubidium copper sulfate, Rb<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>·CuSO<sub>4</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O. <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_silver_iodide" title="Rubidium silver iodide">Rubidium silver iodide</a> (RbAg<sub>4</sub>I<sub>5</sub>) has the highest <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">conductivity</a> of any known <a href="/wiki/Ionic_crystal" title="Ionic crystal">ionic crystal</a>, a property exploited in thin film <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> and other applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium forms a number of <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_oxide" title="Rubidium oxide">oxides</a> when exposed to air, including rubidium monoxide (Rb<sub>2</sub>O), Rb<sub>6</sub>O, and Rb<sub>9</sub>O<sub>2</sub>; rubidium in excess oxygen gives the <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> RbO<sub>2</sub>. Rubidium forms salts with halides, producing <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_fluoride" title="Rubidium fluoride">rubidium fluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_chloride" title="Rubidium chloride">rubidium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_bromide" title="Rubidium bromide">rubidium bromide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_iodide" title="Rubidium iodide">rubidium iodide</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_rubidium" title="Isotopes of rubidium">Isotopes of rubidium</a></div>
<p>Although rubidium is <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic</a>, rubidium in the Earth's crust is composed of two isotopes: the stable <sup>85</sup>Rb (72.2%) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <sup>87</sup>Rb (27.8%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-11">[11]</a></sup> Natural rubidium is radioactive, with specific activity of about 670 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">Bq</a>/g, enough to significantly expose a <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> in 110 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Twenty four additional rubidium isotopes have been synthesized with half-lives of less than 3 months; most are highly radioactive and have few uses.
</p><p>Rubidium-87 has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010488000000000000♠</span>48.8<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>9</sup></span> years, which is more than three times the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">age of the universe</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010137990000000000♠</span>(13.799<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.021)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>9</sup></span> years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planck_2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planck_2015-14">[14]</a></sup> making it a <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclide</a>. It readily substitutes for <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, and is therefore fairly widespread. Rb has been used extensively in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_dating" title="Rock dating">dating rocks</a>; <sup>87</sup>Rb <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decays</a> to stable <sup>87</sup>Sr. During <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(geology)" title="Fractional crystallization (geology)">fractional crystallization</a>, Sr tends to concentrate in <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">plagioclase</a>, leaving Rb in the liquid phase. Hence, the Rb/Sr ratio in residual <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> may increase over time, and the progressing <a href="/wiki/Igneous_differentiation" title="Igneous differentiation">differentiation</a> results in rocks with elevated Rb/Sr ratios. The highest ratios (10 or more) occur in <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatites</a>. If the initial amount of Sr is known or can be extrapolated, then the age can be determined by measurement of the Rb and Sr concentrations and of the <sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratio. The dates indicate the true age of the minerals only if the rocks have not been subsequently altered (see <a href="/wiki/Rubidium%E2%80%93strontium_dating" title="Rubidium–strontium dating">rubidium–strontium dating</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rubidium-82" title="Rubidium-82">Rubidium-82</a>, one of the element's non-natural isotopes, is produced by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron-capture</a> decay of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strontium-82" title="Strontium-82">strontium-82</a> with a half-life of 25.36 days. With a half-life of 76 seconds, rubidium-82 decays by positron emission to stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krypton-82" title="Krypton-82">krypton-82</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audi_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Audi-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rubidium is the twenty-third <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">most abundant element in the Earth's crust</a>, roughly as abundant as <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and rather more common than <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">[17]</a></sup> It occurs naturally in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Leucite" title="Leucite">leucite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollucite" title="Pollucite">pollucite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnallite" title="Carnallite">carnallite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinnwaldite" title="Zinnwaldite">zinnwaldite</a>, which contain as much as 1% rubidium <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lepidolite" title="Lepidolite">Lepidolite</a> contains between 0.3% and 3.5% rubidium, and is the commercial source of the element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> minerals and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chlorides</a> also contain the element in commercially significant quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">Seawater</a> contains an average of 125 µg/L of rubidium compared to the much higher value for potassium of 408 mg/L and the much lower value of 0.3 µg/L for caesium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of its large <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radius</a>, rubidium is one of the "<a href="/wiki/Incompatible_element" title="Incompatible element">incompatible elements</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(geology)" title="Fractional crystallization (geology)">magma crystallization</a>, rubidium is concentrated together with its heavier analogue caesium in the liquid phase and crystallizes last. Therefore, the largest deposits of rubidium and caesium are zone <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatite</a> ore bodies formed by this enrichment process. Because rubidium substitutes for <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> in the crystallization of magma, the enrichment is far less effective than that of caesium. Zone pegmatite ore bodies containing mineable quantities of caesium as <a href="/wiki/Pollucite" title="Pollucite">pollucite</a> or the lithium minerals <a href="/wiki/Lepidolite" title="Lepidolite">lepidolite</a> are also a source for rubidium as a by-product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two notable sources of rubidium are the rich deposits of <a href="/wiki/Pollucite" title="Pollucite">pollucite</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bernic_Lake" title="Bernic Lake">Bernic Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, Canada, and the <a href="/wiki/Rubicline" title="Rubicline">rubicline</a> ((Rb,K)AlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>) found as impurities in pollucite on the Italian island of <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a>, with a rubidium content of 17.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Both of those deposits are also sources of caesium.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although rubidium is more abundant in Earth's crust than caesium, the limited applications and the lack of a mineral rich in rubidium limits the production of rubidium compounds to 2 to 4 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">[17]</a></sup> Several methods are available for separating potassium, rubidium, and caesium. The <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a> of a rubidium and caesium alum (Cs,Rb)Al(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·12H<sub>2</sub>O yields after 30 subsequent steps pure rubidium alum. Two other methods are reported, the chlorostannate process and the ferrocyanide process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>For several years in the 1950s and 1960s, a by-product of potassium production called Alkarb was a main source for rubidium. Alkarb contained 21% rubidium, with the rest being potassium and a small amount of caesium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Today the largest producers of caesium, such as the <a href="/wiki/Tanco_Mine" title="Tanco Mine">Tanco Mine</a>, Manitoba, Canada, produce rubidium as a by-product from pollucite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg/130px-Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg/195px-Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg/260px-Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Die_Flammenf%C3%A4rbung_des_Rubidium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flame test for rubidium</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg"><img alt="Three middle-aged men, with the one in the middle sitting down. All wear long jackets, and the shorter man on the left has a beard." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="392" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg/220px-Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg/330px-Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> (left) and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a> (center) discovered rubidium by spectroscopy. <i>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Enfield_Roscoe" title="Henry Enfield Roscoe">Henry Enfield Roscoe</a> is on the right side.)</i></div></div></div>
<p>Rubidium was discovered in 1861 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a>, in Heidelberg, Germany, in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Lepidolite" title="Lepidolite">lepidolite</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscopy</a>. Because of the bright red lines in its <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">emission spectrum</a>, they chose a name derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>rubidus</i>, meaning "deep red".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuKi1861_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuKi1861-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium is a minor component in lepidolite. Kirchhoff and Bunsen processed 150 kg of a lepidolite containing only 0.24% rubidium oxide (Rb<sub>2</sub>O). Both potassium and rubidium form insoluble salts with <a href="/wiki/Chloroplatinic_acid" title="Chloroplatinic acid">chloroplatinic acid</a>, but those salts show a slight difference in solubility in hot water. Therefore, the less-soluble rubidium <a href="/wiki/Hexachloroplatinate" title="Hexachloroplatinate">hexachloroplatinate</a> (Rb<sub>2</sub>PtCl<sub>6</sub>) could be obtained by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry)" title="Fractional crystallization (chemistry)">fractional crystallization</a>. After reduction of the hexachloroplatinate with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, the process yielded 0.51 grams of <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_chloride" title="Rubidium chloride">rubidium chloride</a> for further studies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bunsen and Kirchhoff began their first large-scale isolation of caesium and rubidium compounds with 44,000 litres (12,000 US gal) of mineral water, which yielded 7.3 grams of <a href="/wiki/Caesium_chloride" title="Caesium chloride">caesium chloride</a> and 9.2 grams of <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_chloride" title="Rubidium chloride">rubidium chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuKi1861_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-BuKi1861-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-26">[26]</a></sup> Rubidium was the second element, shortly after caesium, to be discovered by spectroscopy, just one year after the invention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscope" title="Spectroscope">spectroscope</a> by Bunsen and Kirchhoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two scientists used the rubidium chloride to estimate that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> of the new element was 85.36 (the currently accepted value is 85.47).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuKi1861_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-BuKi1861-25">[25]</a></sup> They tried to generate elemental rubidium by electrolysis of molten rubidium chloride, but instead of a metal, they obtained a blue homogeneous substance which "neither under the naked eye nor under the microscope showed the slightest trace of metallic substance." They presumed it was a <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">subchloride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">Rb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>Cl</span>); however, the product was probably a <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloidal</a> mixture of the metal and rubidium chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In a second attempt to produce metallic rubidium, Bunsen was able to reduce rubidium by heating charred rubidium tartrate. Although the distilled rubidium was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a>, they were able to determine the density and the melting point. The quality of this research in the 1860s can be appraised by the fact that their determined density differs less than 0.1 g/cm<sup>3</sup> and the melting point by less than 1 °C  from the presently accepted values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The slight radioactivity of rubidium was discovered in 1908, but that was before the theory of isotopes was established in 1910, and the low level of activity (half-life greater than 10<sup>10</sup> years) made interpretation complicated. The now proven decay of <sup>87</sup>Rb to stable <sup>87</sup>Sr through <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> was still under discussion in the late 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium had minimal industrial value before the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Since then, the most important use of rubidium is research and development, primarily in chemical and electronic applications. In 1995, rubidium-87 was used to produce a <a href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate" title="Bose–Einstein condensate">Bose–Einstein condensate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> for which the discoverers, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Allin_Cornell" title="Eric Allin Cornell">Eric Allin Cornell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Edwin_Wieman" title="Carl Edwin Wieman">Carl Edwin Wieman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Ketterle" title="Wolfgang Ketterle">Wolfgang Ketterle</a>, won the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USNO_rubidium_fountain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="250" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/USNO_rubidium_fountain.jpg/220px-USNO_rubidium_fountain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/USNO_rubidium_fountain.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USNO_rubidium_fountain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A rubidium fountain <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">atomic clock</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Observatory" title="United States Naval Observatory">United States Naval Observatory</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rubidium compounds are sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a> to give them a purple color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Rubidium has also been considered for use in a <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_generator" title="Thermoelectric generator">thermoelectric generator</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">magnetohydrodynamic</a> principle, where hot rubidium ions are passed through a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> These conduct electricity and act like an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armature_(electrical_engineering)" title="Armature (electrical engineering)">armature</a> of a generator thereby generating an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. Rubidium, particularly vaporized <sup>87</sup>Rb, is one of the most commonly used atomic species employed for <a href="/wiki/Laser_cooling" title="Laser cooling">laser cooling</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensation" title="Bose–Einstein condensation">Bose–Einstein condensation</a>. Its desirable features for this application include the ready availability of inexpensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diode_laser" title="Diode laser">diode laser</a> light at the relevant <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>, and the moderate temperatures required to obtain substantial vapor pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> For cold atom applications requiring tunable interactions, <sup>85</sup>Rb is preferable due to its rich <a href="/wiki/Feshbach_resonance" title="Feshbach resonance">Feshbach spectrum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium has been used for polarizing <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3"><sup>3</sup>He</a>, producing volumes of magnetized <sup>3</sup>He gas, with the nuclear spins aligned rather than random. Rubidium vapor is optically pumped by a laser and the polarized Rb polarizes <sup>3</sup>He through the <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">hyperfine</a> interaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Spin_polarization" title="Spin polarization">spin-polarized</a> <sup>3</sup>He cells are useful for neutron polarization measurements and for producing polarized neutron beams for other purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>The resonant element in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">atomic clocks</a> utilizes the <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">hyperfine structure</a> of rubidium's energy levels, and rubidium is useful for high-precision timing.  It is used as the main component of secondary frequency references (rubidium oscillators) in cell site transmitters and other electronic transmitting, networking, and test equipment. These <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_standard" title="Rubidium standard">rubidium standards</a> are often used with <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> to produce a "primary frequency standard" that has greater accuracy and is less expensive than caesium standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clock_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clock-43">[43]</a></sup> Such rubidium standards are often mass-produced for the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a> industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other potential or current uses of rubidium include a working fluid in vapor turbines, as a <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">getter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a>, and as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photocell" title="Photocell">photocell</a> component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Rubidium is also used as an ingredient in special types of glass, in the production of <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> by burning in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, in the study of <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion_channel" title="Ion channel">ion channels</a> in biology, and as the vapor in atomic <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAG_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAG-46">[46]</a></sup> In particular, <sup>87</sup>Rb is used with other alkali metals in the development of spin-exchange relaxation-free <a href="/wiki/SERF" title="SERF">(SERF) magnetometers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAG_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-MAG-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rubidium-82" title="Rubidium-82">Rubidium-82</a> is used for <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a>. Rubidium is very similar to potassium and tissue with high potassium content will also accumulate the radioactive rubidium. One of the main uses is <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_perfusion_imaging" title="Myocardial perfusion imaging">myocardial perfusion imaging</a>. As a result of changes in the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a> in brain tumors, rubidium collects more in brain tumors than normal brain tissue, allowing the use of radioisotope rubidium-82 in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a> to locate and image brain tumors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  Rubidium-82 has a very short half-life of 76 seconds, and the production from decay of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strontium-82" title="Strontium-82">strontium-82</a> must be done close to the patient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium was tested for the influence on manic depression and depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manic_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-manic-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Dialysis patients suffering from depression show a depletion in rubidium and therefore a supplementation may help during depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In some tests the rubidium was administered as rubidium chloride with up to 720 mg per day for 60 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn1-58890-299-4_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-58890-299-4-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Rubidium reacts violently with water and can cause fires. To ensure safety and purity, this metal is usually kept under a dry <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a> or sealed in glass ampoules in an inert atmosphere. Rubidium forms <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxides</a> on exposure even to small amount of air diffused into the oil, and storage is subject to similar precautions as the storage of metallic <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rubidium, like sodium and potassium, almost always has +1 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> when dissolved in water, even in biological contexts. The human body tends to treat Rb<sup>+</sup> ions as if they were potassium ions, and therefore concentrates rubidium in the body's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intracellular_fluid" title="Intracellular fluid">intracellular fluid</a> (i.e., inside cells).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The ions are not particularly toxic; a 70 kg person contains on average 0.36 g of rubidium, and an increase in this value by 50 to 100 times did not show negative effects in test persons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> of rubidium in humans measures 31–46 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manic_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-manic-49">[49]</a></sup> Although a partial substitution of potassium by rubidium is possible, when more than 50% of the potassium in the muscle tissue of rats was replaced with rubidium, the rats died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
