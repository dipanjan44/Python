<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Sweden</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>The issue of <b>climate change in Sweden</b> has received significant public and political attention and the mitigation of its effects has been high on the agenda of the three latest <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Sweden" title="Government of Sweden">Governments of Sweden</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Persson_Cabinet" title="Persson Cabinet">Cabinet of Göran Persson</a> (−2006), the previous <a href="/wiki/Reinfeldt_Cabinet" title="Reinfeldt Cabinet">Cabinet of Fredrik Reinfeldt</a> (2006–2014) and the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%B6fven" title="Löfven">Löfven</a> cabinet (2014–). Sweden aims for an energy supply system with zero net atmospheric greenhouse gas emissions by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_History_of_Sweden">Climate History of Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate History of Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:412px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sweden_Temp_(1750-2013)BE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1814" data-file-width="2378" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg/410px-Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg/615px-Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg/820px-Sweden_Temp_%281750-2013%29BE.jpg 2x" width="410"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sweden_Temp_(1750-2013)BE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Temperature rise in Sweden's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> (1750–2013)</div></div></div>
<p>Since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> time <a href="/wiki/Era" title="Era">period</a> approximately 2,5 million years BP (before present), Sweden's climate has been alternating between glacial periods and interglacial periods. The glacial periods lasted for up to 100,000 years with <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperatures</a> possibly 20 °C lower than today's. Colder temperatures resulted in ice sheets covering most or all of Sweden. The interglacial periods were shorter, lasting 10,000–15,000 years. During these periods the climate was similar to today's with extensive forests and ice-free summers. The latest of these glacial periods was the <a href="/wiki/Weichselian_glaciation" title="Weichselian glaciation">Weichselian glaciation</a>, lasting from about 115,000 years BP until about 11,500 years BP. At its peak 20,000–17,000 years BP, it extended into the northern parts of Germany and Poland. The transition to the current <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> period was marked by a retreat of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">Ice sheet</a>s and gradually warmer temperatures. By 6,000–7,000 years BP, the temperature was slightly warmer than today and most of the southern half of the country was covered in <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous forests</a>. The temperature has fluctuated since then with a weak cooling trend, leading to a relative increase in coniferous tree-cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions">Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Germanwatch" title="Germanwatch">Germanwatch</a> <i>Climate Change Performance Index 2010</i> Sweden ranked as the second best country after Brazil in addressing greenhouse gas emissions and policy formulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sweden has also been ranked first in both the 2014 and 2016 editions of the <i>Global Green Economy Index (GGEI)</i> where Sweden performs well overall and within the topic of climate change performance where it is one of the top developed countries due to the relatively low emissions intensity of the Swedish economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the total <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions in 2009 (without other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gases</a> or land use) Sweden ranked 87th to 83rd top out of 216 countries: 50.56 tonnes (t) below Libya 55.0 t, Serbia 52.3 t and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Finland" title="Climate change in Finland">Finland</a> 52.15 t. For the per capita carbon dioxide emissions in 2009, Sweden ranked 82nd to 83rd top out of 216 countries having the same emissions as <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Ukraine" title="Energy in Ukraine">Ukraine</a> 5.58 tonnes per capita (t/capita). This was only slightly below the carbon dioxide emissions per capita in <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_China" title="Climate change in China">China</a> 5.83 t/capita (with 80th top position).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardianlist222_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardianlist222-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, Sweden ranked seventy sixth out of 185 countries for the per capita <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions when taking any land use changes into account. Without considering <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> changes the country ranked at fifty eighth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EU_Renewable_Targets">EU Renewable Targets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: EU Renewable Targets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sweden met its EU member-agreed binding renewable 2020 target in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference">2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/2014_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Society_for_Nature_Conservation" title="Swedish Society for Nature Conservation">Swedish Society for Nature Conservation</a> (SSNC) demanded:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Swedish government should confirm the given election promises of 40% emission decline by 2020</li>
<li>International 5-years goals</li>
<li>Binding for every country</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">SEK</a>4 billion Kr Swedish green fund for 2015–2018</li>
<li>Actively aim concrete agreement text already in Lima 2014</li></ol>
<p>181 students took initiative to work in 2015 for two weeks to improve Swedish climate change <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> in relation to green food, solar energy, bicycles, customs, consumption and wastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy">Policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sweden has applied policy instruments and measures for climate change mitigation since the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The instruments used include economic instruments (such as CO<sub>2</sub> tax, subsidies, penalties), legislation, voluntary agreements and a dialogue between the state and business enterprise. The main instruments are described below:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_tax_instrument">Carbon dioxide tax instrument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide tax instrument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Sweden, there are so far three different taxes levied on energy products (mainly fossil fuels), namely energy tax, sulphur tax and CO<sub>2</sub> tax. Energy taxation has been used as a policy instrument ever since the oil crisis of the 1970s to support <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> and nuclear power. Energy tax was reduced by half in 1991 during the tax reform, simultaneously with the introduction of a CO<sub>2</sub> tax on fossil fuels, with exceptions on ethanol, methanol, other biofuels, peat and wastes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy_certificate_system">Renewable energy certificate system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Renewable energy certificate system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As one part of the Government's long-term energy policy to reduce GHG emissions, the Swedish government introduced a voluntary international system for trading "green certificates", i.e. the renewable energy certificate system (RECS). With effect from 1 May 2003, RECS intends to encourage and increase the proportion of electricity produced from renewable energy sources. This will be done by payment of a levy in proportion to certain fraction of their electricity during the year. For example, during the first year (2003), users will be required to buy 7.4 per cent of the electricity generated from renewable sources
</p>
<h3><span id="Renewable_energy_subsidies_and_continuous_investment_on_R.26D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy_subsidies_and_continuous_investment_on_R&amp;D">Renewable energy subsidies and continuous investment on R&amp;D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Renewable energy subsidies and continuous investment on R&amp;D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1991, Sweden started many programs to encourage the use of renewable energy and new technology development, e.g. Energy Policy program (Long and short term programs that focus on ways to increase the supply of renewable electricity, to reduce electricity consumption, and to promote energy efficiency), Green Certificate Scheme (Generators using solar, wind, biomass, geothermal, wave or small hydro are awarded one certificate for each MWh produced, and all consumers are obliged to buy enough certificates to cover a set proportion of their use).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_collaboration_and_carbon_trading_systems">International collaboration and carbon trading systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International collaboration and carbon trading systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sweden also shows its leadership in international cooperation and competence on the climate change issues. Sweden actively took part in some international climate policy programs, such as Prototype Carbon Funds (PCF) and Activities Implemented Jointly (AIJ)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_participations">Public participations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Public participations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Public participation is quite important in addressing climate change and its effects and developing adequate responses. Without the support of the public, it is impossible to implement a new policy instrument successfully. For example, one cannot anticipate that bio ethanol and bio diesel could be widely consumed without support and understanding from the general population. Therefore, information to raise the level of knowledge concerning the climate issue to the public is necessary.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_Phase-out">Oil Phase-out</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Oil Phase-out">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Making_Sweden_an_Oil-Free_Society" title="Making Sweden an Oil-Free Society">Making Sweden an Oil-Free Society</a></div><p>The government created a Commission on Oil Independence (<i>Kommissionen för att bryta oljeberoendet i Sverige till år 2020</i>) and in 2006 it proposed the following targets for 2020:
</p><ul><li>Consumption of oil in road transport to be reduced by 40–50 per cent;</li>
<li>Consumption of oil in industry to be cut by 25–40 per cent;</li>
<li>Heating buildings with oil, a practice already cut by 70% since the 1973 oil crisis, should be phased out;</li>
<li>Overall, energy should be used 20% more efficiently</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Perception_of_Climate_Change">Public Perception of Climate Change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public Perception of Climate Change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2002 survey showed that over 95% of respondents said that the use of tax money for addressing climate change was either "Very important" or "Fairly important". A little over half of the respondents were willing to change the use of hot water, electricity consumption and travel arrangement in order to reduce the impact of climate change. A little under half did not want to decrease internal building temperatures as a means of reducing climate change impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A201
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry">Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Forest owners and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> professionals don't seem to be worried about <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> affecting forests in Sweden. For example, Forest owners in Kronoberg believe that climate change effects are distant and long-term. Stakeholders focus more on personal experience rather than results of how climate change will affect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">forests</a> in the future. Another forest professional says that nothing they can do today can affect the changes that will happen in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_Disasters_Prevention">Natural Disasters Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Natural Disasters Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sweden has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomical" title="Socioeconomical">socioeconomical</a> advantages that help higher the safety <a href="/wiki/Awareness" title="Awareness">awareness</a> to prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_disasters" title="Natural disasters">natural disasters</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Civil_Contingencies_Agency" title="Swedish Civil Contingencies Agency">Swedish Civil Contingencies Agency</a> (MSB) creates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guidelines" title="Guidelines">guidelines</a> and strategies to help the society adapt to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Floods">Floods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Floods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Sweden is affected every year by damaging floods, the MSB maintains and compiles general flood <a href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">inundation</a> <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a>, which are used for risk vulnerability analysis, emergency preparedness and in land use planning by municipalities. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_prevention" title="Flood prevention">Flood prevention</a> can include pumping equipment, <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">embankments</a> and dykes, or devices to shut down <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_systems" title="Sewage systems">sewage systems</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_Fires">Forest Fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Forest Fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MSB has created a national information system for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_brigades" title="Fire brigades">fire brigades</a>. The <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> is found on the Internet and it provides information about how the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> can affect vegetation <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire risks</a>. It provides data that helps with prevention and can assist in <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storms">Storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MSB provides <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">generators</a> that can be borrowed by areas that are hit by a storm and have lost <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">power</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landslides">Landslides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Landslides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of Sweden's location and the <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> of the ground, landslides can affect some areas. The MSB provides general stability mapping for areas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Susceptible" title="Susceptible">susceptible</a> to landslides. The maps show which areas can be affected and which areas are in need of a detailed geotechnical surveys.
</p><p>Areas where consequences of a storm can be serious, the government grants 40 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_kronor" title="Swedish kronor">Swedish kronor</a> per year for preventions. Municipalities that have preventive measures can apply for <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidy</a> from these allocated funds. A municipality that has been affected by a natural emergency has the right to ask the state for compensation to cover the exceeding costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energimyndigheten.a-w2m.se_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-energimyndigheten.a-w2m.se-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storms_2">Storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Winter storms <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gudrun" title="Cyclone Gudrun">Gudrun</a> in 2005 and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Per" title="Cyclone Per">Per</a> in 2007 in southern Sweden overthrew huge volumes of forest and caused power cuts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Storm Per on 14 January 2007 affected 440,000 electricity users and Gudrun 620,000 customers. The reallocation of capital due to power disruptions during and after storm Per was estimated to be between SEK 1 800 and 3 400 million. The network operators cost was ca SEK 1 400 million, of which SEK 750 million compensation for affected customers. The costs for electricity consumers was estimated to SEK 180–1 800 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:353px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="669" data-file-width="674" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png/351px-Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png/527px-Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png 2x" width="351"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erwin_Jan_8_2005.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">Cyclone</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gudrun_(storm)" title="Gudrun (storm)">Gudrun</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> 8 January 2005</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gudrun_Storm">Gudrun Storm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gudrun Storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gudrun" title="Gudrun">Gudrun</a> storm hit Sweden on 8 January 2005. Before the wind speeds stopped, they had reached a maximum of 43 m/s. Wind speeds were at their strongest in the Bay of <a href="/wiki/Han%C3%B6" title="Hanö">Hanö</a> where they reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> level of 33 m/s with gusts of 42 m/s. Areas like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A5ne" title="Skåne">Skåne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blekinge" title="Blekinge">Blekinge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halland" title="Halland">Halland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kronoberg" title="Kronoberg">Kronoberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6nk%C3%B6ping" title="Jönköping">Jönköping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalmar" title="Kalmar">Kalmar</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%A4stra_G%C3%B6taland" title="Västra Götaland">Västra Götaland</a> counties were hit with winds reaching 30 m/s or more. Additionally, gusts of winds hit Södermanland coast, Lake Mälaren, Lake Hjälmaren and southern parts of Stockholm County. A total of eleven counties were strongly affected by the storm.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Estimates" title="Estimates">Estimates</a> of about 730,000 users were without electricity the night of 8 January. The storm also damaged distribution networks of <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a>, Kreab Öst and other smaller companies. All the electricity damage also affected telephone and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_networks" title="Computer networks">computer networks</a>.
</p><p>Despite the storm occurring in January, the weather at the time was mild which made the need for heat less than usual. District heating systems in urban areas did not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffer" title="Suffer">suffer</a> from long power cuts to cause problems. However, smaller areas did suffer from heating systems failures.
</p><p>Millions of trees were torn by the roots and others were cut at the trunk. Trees blocked roads and seized traffic. The lack of <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a> in the ground caused spruce trees to be vulnerable to the high winds. 75 million cubic meters of forest was felled which is equal to several years of normal felling in the affected areas.
</p><p>A major problem was telephone systems failure which delayed the clearing of roads and repair of overhead lines.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">Nursing</a> homes and elderly care services were also affected as individual safety alarms did not work. People were stranded in their cars on blocked roads. Seven people were killed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accidents" title="Accidents">accidents</a> and others were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injured" title="Injured">injured</a> on the night of 8 January. Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaths" title="Deaths">deaths</a> occurred after the storm, for example, one man was killed while attempting to fix his roof. In addition, people suffered from PTSDs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energimyndigheten.a-w2m.se_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-energimyndigheten.a-w2m.se-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_climate_in_Sweden">Future climate in Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Future climate in Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the end of the century, Sweden's climate will be different from today's. There are uncertainties regarding the exact scope of the change one of which is the world's political trajectory regarding climate policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the 2080s average temperatures are set to rise by 3–5 °C. The climate in the Mälardalen region will be similar to that of northern France. Winter temperatures are likely to see a greater increase than spring, summer and autumn temperatures. By the end of the century winters could be up to 7 °C warmer than today on average. The Norrland coast will probably be the region that sees the highest increases in temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> In May 2018 mean temperature was more than in average +5 °C in most Sweden and +2.5 °C in most Europe. In July 2018 mean temperature was more than in average +3-4 °C in most Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In July 2018 Italy, Norway and France sent help to fight the dozens of forest fires in Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation">Precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sweden's future climate is expected to be wetter, with an increase in intense rain events. Most of the increase in precipitation will be during winter and a larger proportion will fall as rain. Summers will be drier and see a reduction in heavy rain events, particularly in the southern parts of Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate models differ on whether Sweden's climate will get windier or not. Some models predict an increase in average wind speed, whilst others predict a decrease. The predictions of one climate model able to resolve wind gusts show an increase in the speed of wind gusts in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltic_Sea">Baltic Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Baltic Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The surface temperature of the Baltic Sea will increase as the air temperature increases. Some models predict up to 4 °C increases in surface water temperature. Sea ice cover is expected to decrease and be localized to the northern Gulf of Bothnia by the end on the century. The salinity of the Baltic Sea is predicted to fall in some climate models as a result of increased influx of freshwater from the mainland, though other models differ significantly with some even showing an increase in salinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_Concerns">Health Concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Health Concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> may affect the health status of the population in several ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0222_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0222-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Warmer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_conditions" title="Weather conditions">weather conditions</a> can affect the elderly and vulnerable people in periods of extremely warm weather.</li>
<li>Spread of vector-borne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases" title="Diseases">diseases</a>, in particular, tick-borne infections like Lyme disease (borreliosis) and tick-borne encephalitis. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ticks" title="Ticks">Ticks</a> have spread northwards and can now even be found in the regions of <a href="/wiki/Norrland" title="Norrland">Norrland</a>. Also, there are still five species of mosquitoes in Sweden that may spread the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malaria_parasite" title="Malaria parasite">malaria parasite</a>. With the last indigenous case reported in the 1930s, a few new cases of malaria may appear with increasing temperature. The anopheles species of mosquitoes that spread the malaria parasite are sensitive to weather conditions that can be affected by climate change. Increased precipitation affects the number of hatching places of the mosquitoes, while an increase in humidity and temperature increases the life span of the mosquitoes and the development of the malaria parasite inside the mosquito.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Quality of water and food can be affected by higher temperatures in the summer.</li>
<li>Air quality may be changed with a different composition of dust and pollen grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0222_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0222-19">[19]</a></sup> Studies of future scenarios project that the spreading of ragweed northwards may cause new cases of allergy and asthma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>
