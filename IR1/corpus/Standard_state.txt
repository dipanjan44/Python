<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Standard state</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the <b>standard state</b> of a material (pure <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a>, mixture or <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>) is a reference point used to calculate its properties under different conditions. In principle, the choice of standard state is arbitrary, although the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) recommends a conventional set of standard states for general use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GB1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GB1-1">[1]</a></sup> IUPAC recommends using a standard pressure <i>p</i><sup>⦵</sup> = 10<sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Strictly speaking, temperature is not part of the definition of a standard state. For example, as discussed below, the standard state of a gas is conventionally chosen to be unit pressure (usually in bar) <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>, regardless of the temperature. However, most tables of thermodynamic quantities are compiled at specific temperatures, most commonly 298.15 K (25.00 °C; 77.00 °F) or, somewhat less commonly, 273.15 K (0.00 °C; 32.00 °F).
</p><p>The standard state should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> (STP) for gases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> nor with the <a href="/wiki/Standard_solution" title="Standard solution">standard solutions</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">analytical chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>For a given material or substance, the standard state is the <b>reference state</b> for the material's thermodynamic state properties such as <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a>, and for many other material standards. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_change_of_formation" title="Standard enthalpy change of formation">standard enthalpy change of formation</a> for an element in its standard state is zero, and this convention allows a wide range of other thermodynamic quantities to be calculated and tabulated. The standard state of a substance does not have to exist in nature: for example, it is possible to calculate values for <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> at 298.15 K and 10<sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>, although steam does not exist (as a gas) under these conditions. The advantage of this practice is that tables of thermodynamic properties prepared in this way are self-consistent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventional_standard_states">Conventional standard states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conventional standard states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many standard states are non-physical states, often referred to as "hypothetical states". Nevertheless, their thermodynamic properties are well-defined, usually by an extrapolation from some limiting condition, such as zero pressure or zero concentration, to a specified condition (usually unit concentration or pressure) using an ideal extrapolating function, such as ideal solution or ideal gas behavior, or by empirical measurements.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gases">Gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The standard state for a gas is the hypothetical state it would have as a pure substance obeying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_equation" title="Ideal gas equation">ideal gas equation</a> at standard pressure (10<sup>5</sup> Pa, or 1 bar). No real gas has perfectly ideal behavior, but this definition of the standard state allows corrections for non-ideality to be made consistently for all the different gases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquids_and_solids">Liquids and solids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liquids and solids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The standard state for liquids and solids is simply the state of the pure substance subjected to a total pressure of 10<sup>5</sup> Pa. For most elements, the reference point of Δ<i>H</i><sub>f</sub><sup>⦵</sup> = 0 is defined for the most stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotrope" title="Allotrope">allotrope</a> of the element, such as <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> in the case of carbon, and the β-phase (white tin) in the case of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>. An exception is white <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, the most common allotrope of phosphorus, which is defined as the standard state despite the fact that it is only <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutes">Solutes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solutes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For a substance in solution (solute), the standard state is the hypothetical state it would have at the standard state <a href="/wiki/Molality" title="Molality">molality</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amount_concentration" title="Amount concentration">amount concentration</a> but exhibiting infinite-dilution behavior. The reason for this unusual definition is that the behavior of a solute at the limit of infinite dilution is described by equations which are very similar to the equations for ideal gases. Hence taking infinite-dilution behavior to be the standard state allows corrections for non-ideality to be made consistently for all the different solutes. Standard state molality is 1 mol kg<sup>−1</sup>, while standard state amount concentration is 1 mol dm<sup>−3</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typesetting">Typesetting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_state&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Typesetting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of development in the nineteenth century, the superscript <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plimsoll_symbol" title="Plimsoll symbol">Plimsoll symbol</a> (<sup>⦵</sup>) was adopted to indicate the non-zero nature of the standard state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> recommends in the 3rd edition of <i>Quantities, Units and Symbols in Physical Chemistry</i> a symbol which seems to be a degree sign (°) as a substitute for the plimsoll mark. In the very same publication the plimsoll mark appears to be constructed by combining a horizontal stroke with a degree sign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A range of similar symbols are used in the literature: a stroked lowercase letter O (<sup><s>o</s></sup>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> a superscript zero (<sup>0</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or a circle with a horizontal bar either where the bar extends the boundaries of the circle (<span class="nowrap"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r861301850">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">U+</a>29B5</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">⦵</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">CIRCLE WITH HORIZONTAL BAR</span>) or is enclosed by the circle, dividing the circle in half (<span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+2296</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">⊖</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">CIRCLED MINUS</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When compared to the plimsoll symbol used on vessels, the horizontal bar should however extend the boundaries of the circle; care should be taken not to confuse the symbol with the Greek letter <a href="/wiki/Theta" title="Theta">theta</a> (uppercase Θ or ϴ, lowercase θ ).
</p>
