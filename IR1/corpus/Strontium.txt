<p><b>Strontium</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Sr</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 38. An <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a>, strontium is a soft silver-white yellowish <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metallic</a> element that is highly <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">chemically reactive</a>. The metal forms a dark oxide layer when it is exposed to air. Strontium has physical and chemical properties similar to those of its two vertical neighbors in the periodic table, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>. It occurs naturally mainly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> <a href="/wiki/Celestine_(mineral)" title="Celestine (mineral)">celestine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontianite" title="Strontianite">strontianite</a>, and is mostly mined from these. While natural strontium is stable, the synthetic <a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90"><sup>90</sup>Sr</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> is radioactive and is one of the most dangerous components of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear fallout</a>, as strontium is absorbed by the body in a similar manner to calcium. Natural stable strontium, on the other hand, is not hazardous to health.
</p><p>Both strontium and strontianite are named after <a href="/wiki/Strontian" title="Strontian">Strontian</a>, a village in Scotland near which the mineral was discovered in 1790 by <a href="/wiki/Adair_Crawford" title="Adair Crawford">Adair Crawford</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Cruickshank_(chemist)" title="William Cruickshank (chemist)">William Cruickshank</a>; it was identified as a new element the next year from its crimson-red <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a> color. Strontium was first isolated as a metal in 1808 by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> using the then-newly discovered process of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>. During the 19th century, Strontium was mostly used in the production of sugar from <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Strontian_process" title="Strontian process">strontian process</a>). At the peak of production of television <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tubes" title="Cathode ray tubes">cathode ray tubes</a>, as much as 75 percent of strontium consumption in the United States was used for the faceplate glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-5">[5]</a></sup> With the replacement of cathode ray tubes with other display methods, consumption of strontium has dramatically declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Strontium_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="376" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Strontium_1.jpg/170px-Strontium_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Strontium_1.jpg/255px-Strontium_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Strontium_1.jpg/340px-Strontium_1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Strontium_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxidized <a href="/wiki/Dendrite_(crystal)" title="Dendrite (crystal)">dendritic</a> strontium</div></div></div>
<p>Strontium is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divalent" title="Divalent">divalent</a> silvery metal with a pale yellow tint whose properties are mostly intermediate between and similar to those of its group neighbors <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood112_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood112-6">[6]</a></sup> It is softer than calcium and harder than barium. Its melting (777 °C) and boiling (1655 °C) points are lower than those of calcium (842 °C and 1757 °C respectively); barium continues this downward trend in the melting point (727 °C), but not in the boiling point (2170 °C). The density of strontium (2.64 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) is similarly intermediate between those of calcium (1.54 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) and barium (3.594 g/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup> Three <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropes</a> of metallic strontium exist, with <a href="/wiki/Transition_point" title="Transition point">transition points</a> at 235 and 540 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a> for the Sr<sup>2+</sup>/Sr couple is −2.89 V, approximately midway between those of the Ca<sup>2+</sup>/Ca (−2.84 V) and Ba<sup>2+</sup>/Ba (−2.92 V) couples, and close to those of the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood111_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood111-9">[9]</a></sup> Strontium is intermediate between calcium and barium in its reactivity toward water, with which it reacts on contact to produce <a href="/wiki/Strontium_hydroxide" title="Strontium hydroxide">strontium hydroxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas. Strontium metal burns in air to produce both <a href="/wiki/Strontium_oxide" title="Strontium oxide">strontium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontium_nitride" title="Strontium nitride">strontium nitride</a>, but since it does not react with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> below 380 °C, at room temperature, it forms only the oxide spontaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup> Besides the simple oxide SrO, the <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxide</a> SrO<sub>2</sub> can be made by direct oxidation of strontium metal under a high pressure of oxygen, and there is some evidence for a yellow <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a> Sr(O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Strontium_hydroxide" title="Strontium hydroxide">Strontium hydroxide</a>, Sr(OH)<sub>2</sub>, is a strong base, though it is not as strong as the hydroxides of barium or the alkali metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> All four dihalides of strontium are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the large size of the heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-block" title="S-block">s-block</a> elements, including strontium, a vast range of coordination numbers is known, from 2, 3, or 4 all the way to 22 or 24 in SrCd<sub>11</sub> and SrZn<sub>13</sub>. The Ca<sup>2+</sup> ion is quite large, so that high coordination numbers are the rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The large size of strontium and barium plays a significant part in stabilising strontium complexes with <a href="/wiki/Denticity" title="Denticity">polydentate</a> <a href="/wiki/Macrocycle" title="Macrocycle">macrocyclic</a> ligands such as <a href="/wiki/Crown_ether" title="Crown ether">crown ethers</a>: for example, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/18-crown-6" title="18-crown-6">18-crown-6</a> forms relatively weak complexes with calcium and the alkali metals, its strontium and barium complexes are much stronger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organostrontium compounds contain one or more strontium–carbon bonds. They have been reported as intermediates in <a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier-type</a> reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Although strontium is in the same group as magnesium, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organomagnesium_compound" title="Organomagnesium compound">organomagnesium compounds</a> are very commonly used throughout chemistry, organostrontium compounds are not similarly widespread because they are more difficult to make and more reactive. Organostrontium compounds tend to be more similar to organo<a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a> or organo<a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> compounds due to the similar <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radii</a> of these elements (Sr<sup>2+</sup> 118 pm; Eu<sup>2+</sup> 117 pm; Sm<sup>2+</sup> 122 pm). Most of these compounds can only be prepared at low temperatures; bulky ligands tend to favor stability. For example, strontium di<a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl" title="Cyclopentadienyl">cyclopentadienyl</a>, Sr(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, must be made by directly reacting strontium metal with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mercurocene&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mercurocene (page does not exist)">mercurocene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadiene" title="Cyclopentadiene">cyclopentadiene</a> itself; replacing the C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>5</sub> ligand with the bulkier C<sub>5</sub>(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>5</sub> ligand on the other hand increases the compound's solubility, volatility, and kinetic stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of its extreme reactivity with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and water, strontium occurs naturally only in compounds with other elements, such as in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Strontianite" title="Strontianite">strontianite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celestine_(mineral)" title="Celestine (mineral)">celestine</a>. It is kept under a liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mineral_oil" title="Mineral oil">mineral oil</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> to prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>; freshly exposed strontium metal rapidly turns a yellowish color with the formation of the oxide. Finely powdered strontium metal is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a>, meaning that it will ignite spontaneously in air at room temperature. Volatile strontium salts impart a bright red color to flames, and these salts are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic" title="Pyrotechnic">pyrotechnics</a> and in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flare_(pyrotechnic)" title="Flare (pyrotechnic)">flares</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup> Like calcium and barium, as well as the alkali metals and the divalent <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a> <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>, strontium metal dissolves directly in liquid <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to give a dark blue solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood112_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood112-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural strontium is a mixture of four stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: <sup>84</sup>Sr, <sup>86</sup>Sr, <sup>87</sup>Sr, and <sup>88</sup>Sr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup> Their abundance increases with increasing <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> and the heaviest, <sup>88</sup>Sr, makes up about 82.6% of all natural strontium, though the abundance varies due to the production of radiogenic <sup>87</sup>Sr as the daughter of long-lived beta-decaying <sup>87</sup><a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">Rb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Of the unstable isotopes, the primary decay mode of the isotopes lighter than <sup>85</sup>Sr is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a> to isotopes of rubidium, and that of the isotopes heavier than <sup>88</sup>Sr is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_emission" title="Electron emission">electron emission</a> to isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>. Of special note are <a href="/wiki/Strontium-89" title="Strontium-89"><sup>89</sup>Sr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90"><sup>90</sup>Sr</a>. The former has a half-life of 50.6 days and is used to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_cancer" title="Bone cancer">bone cancer</a> due to strontium's chemical similarity and hence ability to replace calcium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HalperinPerez2008_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HalperinPerez2008-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaumanCharette2005_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-BaumanCharette2005-21">[21]</a></sup> While <sup>90</sup>Sr (half-life 28.90 years) has been used similarly, it is also an isotope of concern in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">fallout</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_accidents" title="Nuclear accidents">nuclear accidents</a> due to its production as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission product</a>. Its presence in bones can cause bone cancer, cancer of nearby tissues, and <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chernobyl_accident" title="Chernobyl accident">1986 Chernobyl nuclear accident</a> contaminated about 30,000 km<sup>2</sup> with greater than 10 kBq/m<sup>2</sup> with <sup>90</sup>Sr, which accounts for 5% of the core inventory of <sup>90</sup>Sr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OECD02-Ch1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-OECD02-Ch1-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1467" data-file-width="483" height="395" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png/130px-Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png/195px-Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png/260px-Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png 2x" width="130"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flammenf%C3%A4rbungSr.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flame test for strontium</div></div></div>
<p>Strontium is named after the Scottish village of <a href="/wiki/Strontian" title="Strontian">Strontian</a> (Gaelic <i>Sròn an t-Sìthein</i>), where it was discovered in the ores of the lead mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Originally named strontianite by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles_Hope" title="Thomas Charles Hope">Thomas Charles Hope</a> the name was soon after shortened to strontium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1790, <a href="/wiki/Adair_Crawford" title="Adair Crawford">Adair Crawford</a>, a physician engaged in the preparation of barium, and his colleague <a href="/wiki/William_Cruickshank_(chemist)" title="William Cruickshank (chemist)">William Cruickshank</a>, recognised that the Strontian ores exhibited properties that differed from those in other "heavy spars" sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This allowed Adair to conclude on page 355 "... it is probable indeed, that the scotch mineral is a new species of earth which has not hitherto been sufficiently examined." The physician and mineral collector <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Gabriel_Sulzer" title="Friedrich Gabriel Sulzer">Friedrich Gabriel Sulzer</a> analysed together with <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Blumenbach" title="Johann Friedrich Blumenbach">Johann Friedrich Blumenbach</a> the mineral from Strontian and named it strontianite. He also came to the conclusion that it was distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Witherite" title="Witherite">witherite</a> and contained a new earth (neue Grunderde).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1793 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles_Hope" title="Thomas Charles Hope">Thomas Charles Hope</a>, a professor of chemistry at the University of Glasgow proposed the name   <i>strontites</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  He confirmed the earlier work of Crawford and recounted: "... Considering it a peculiar earth I thought it necessary to give it an name. I have called it Strontites, from the place it was found; a mode of derivation in my opinion, fully as proper as any quality it may possess, which is the present fashion." The element was eventually isolated by Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> in 1808 by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of a mixture containing <a href="/wiki/Strontium_chloride" title="Strontium chloride">strontium chloride</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_oxide" title="Mercuric oxide">mercuric oxide</a>, and announced by him in a lecture to the Royal Society on 30 June 1808.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In keeping with the naming of the other alkaline earths, he changed the name to <i>strontium</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first large-scale application of strontium was in the production of sugar from <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>. Although a crystallisation process using strontium hydroxide was patented by <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Pierre_Dubrunfaut" title="Augustin-Pierre Dubrunfaut">Augustin-Pierre Dubrunfaut</a> in 1849<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metalle_in_der_Elektrochemie_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metalle_in_der_Elektrochemie-38">[38]</a></sup> the large scale introduction came with the improvement of the process in the early 1870s. The German <a href="/wiki/Sugar_industry" title="Sugar industry">sugar industry</a> used the process well into the 20th century. Before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the beet sugar industry used  100,000 to 150,000 tons of strontium hydroxide for this <a href="/wiki/Strontian_process" title="Strontian process">process</a> per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.de_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.de-39">[39]</a></sup> The strontium hydroxide was recycled in the process, but the demand to substitute losses during production was high enough to create a significant demand initiating mining of strontianite in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnsterland" title="Münsterland">Münsterland</a>. The  mining of strontianite in Germany ended when mining of the <a href="/wiki/Celestine_(mineral)" title="Celestine (mineral)">celestine</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> These mines supplied most of the world strontium supply from 1884 to 1941. Although the celestine deposits in the Granada basin were known for some time the large scale mining did not start before the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>During atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">nuclear weapons testing</a>, it was observed that strontium-90 is one of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission_product" title="Nuclear fission product">nuclear fission products</a> with a relative high yield. The similarity to calcium and the chance that the strontium-90 might become enriched in bones made research on the metabolism of strontium an important topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Celestine_Poland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="300" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Celestine_Poland.jpg/220px-Celestine_Poland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/17/Celestine_Poland.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Celestine_Poland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The mineral celestine (SrSO<sub>4</sub>)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Strontium_minerals" title="Category:Strontium minerals">Category:Strontium minerals</a>.</div>
<p>Strontium commonly occurs in nature, being the 15th most <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">abundant element</a> on Earth (its heavier congener barium being the 14th), estimated to average approximately 360 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and is found chiefly as the <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> celestine (SrSO<sub>4</sub>) and the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> strontianite (SrCO<sub>3</sub>). Of the two, celestine occurs much more frequently in deposits of sufficient size for mining. Because strontium is used most often in the carbonate form, strontianite would be the more useful of the two common minerals, but few deposits have been discovered that are suitable for development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs10_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs10-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In groundwater strontium behaves chemically much like calcium. At intermediate to acidic <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> Sr<sup>2+</sup> is the dominant strontium species. In the presence of calcium ions, strontium commonly forms <a href="/wiki/Coprecipitation" title="Coprecipitation">coprecipitates</a> with calcium minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and anhydrite at an increased pH. At intermediate to acidic pH, dissolved strontium is bound to soil particles by <a href="/wiki/Cation-exchange_capacity" title="Cation-exchange capacity">cation exchange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heuel-Fabianek_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heuel-Fabianek-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mean strontium content of ocean water is 8 mg/l.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> At a concentration between 82 and 90 µmol/l of strontium, the concentration is considerably lower than the calcium concentration, which is normally between 9.6 and 11.6 mmol/l.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It is nevertheless much higher than that of barium, 13 μg/l.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg"><img alt="Grey and white world map with China colored green representing 50%, Spain colored blue-green representing 30%, Mexico colored light blue representing 20%, Argentina colored dark blue representing below 5% of strontium world production." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg/310px-World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg/465px-World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg/620px-World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Strontium_Production_2014.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Strontium producers in 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs15_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs15-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The three major producers of strontium as celestine as of 2015 are China (150,000 t), Spain (90,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">t</a>), and Mexico (70,000 t); Argentina (10,000 t) and Morocco (2,500 t) are smaller producers. Although strontium deposits occur widely in the United States, they have not been mined since 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs15_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs15-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>A large proportion of mined celestine (SrSO<sub>4</sub>) is converted to the carbonate by two processes. Either the celestine is directly leached with sodium carbonate solution or the celestine is roasted with coal to form the sulfide. The second stage produces a dark-coloured material containing mostly <a href="/wiki/Strontium_sulfide" title="Strontium sulfide">strontium sulfide</a>. This so-called "black ash" is dissolved in water and filtered. Strontium carbonate is precipitated from the strontium sulfide solution by introduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The sulfate is <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbothermic_reduction" title="Carbothermic reduction">carbothermic reduction</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>SrSO<sub>4</sub>  + 2 C   →   SrS  +  2 CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>About 300,000 tons are processed in this way annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>The metal is produced commercially by reducing strontium <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>.  The strontium is <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilled</a> from the mixture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-53">[53]</a></sup> Strontium metal can also be prepared on a small scale by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of a solution of <a href="/wiki/Strontium_chloride" title="Strontium chloride">strontium chloride</a> in molten <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood111_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood111-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Sr<sup>2+</sup> + 2 <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>  →  Sr</dd>
<dd>2 Cl<sup>−</sup> → Cl<sub>2</sub>  +  2 <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monitor.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="762" data-file-width="750" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Monitor.arp.jpg/220px-Monitor.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Monitor.arp.jpg/330px-Monitor.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Monitor.arp.jpg/440px-Monitor.arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monitor.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CRT computer monitor front panel made from strontium and barium oxide-containing glass. This application used to consume most of the world's production of strontium.</div></div></div>
<p>Consuming 75% of production, the primary use for strontium is in glass for colour television <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tubes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-53">[53]</a></sup> where it prevents <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> This application for strontium is declining because CRTs are being replaced by other display methods. This decline has a significant influence on the mining and refining of strontium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs10_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs10-45">[45]</a></sup> All parts of the CRT must absorb X-rays. In the neck and the funnel of the tube, lead glass is used for this purpose, but this type of glass shows a browning effect due to the interaction of the X-rays with the glass. Therefore, the front panel is made from a different glass mixture with strontium and barium to absorb the X-rays. The average values for the glass mixture determined for a recycling study in 2005 is 8.5% <a href="/wiki/Strontium_oxide" title="Strontium oxide">strontium oxide</a> and 10% <a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">barium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because strontium is so similar to calcium, it is incorporated in the bone. All four stable isotopes are incorporated, in roughly the same proportions they are found in nature. However, the actual distribution of the isotopes tends to vary greatly from one geographical location to another. Thus, analyzing the bone of an individual can help determine the region it came from.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PriceSchoeninger1985_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-PriceSchoeninger1985-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SteadmanBrudevold1958_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-SteadmanBrudevold1958-58">[58]</a></sup> This approach helps to identify the ancient migration patterns and the origin of commingled human remains in battlefield burial sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchweissingGrupe2003_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchweissingGrupe2003-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p><sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratios are commonly used to determine the likely provenance areas of sediment in natural systems, especially in marine and fluvial environments. Dasch (1969) showed that surface sediments of Atlantic displayed <sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratios that could be regarded as bulk averages of the <sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratios of geological terranes from adjacent landmasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dasch_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dasch-60">[60]</a></sup> A good example of a fluvial-marine system to which Sr isotope provenance studies have been successfully employed is the River Nile-Mediterranean system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krom1999_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krom1999-61">[61]</a></sup> Due to the differing ages of the rocks that constitute the majority of the Blue and White Nile, catchment areas of the changing provenance of sediment reaching the River Nile delta and East Mediterranean Sea can be discerned through strontium isotopic studies. Such changes are climatically controlled in the Late <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krom1999_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Krom1999-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, <sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratios have also been used to determine the source of ancient archaeological materials such as timbers and corn in Chaco Canyon, New Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benson_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benson-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-English_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-English-63">[63]</a></sup> <sup>87</sup>Sr/<sup>86</sup>Sr ratios in teeth may also be used to track animal migrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnett-Johnson_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett-Johnson-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porder_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Porder-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Strontium_aluminate" title="Strontium aluminate">Strontium aluminate</a> is frequently used in <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">glow in the dark</a> toys, as it is chemically and biologically inert.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_(5).jpg"><img alt="red fireworks" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg/170px-Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg/255px-Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg/340px-Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_%285%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ignis_Brunensis_2010-05-22_(5).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Strontium salts are added to fireworks in order to create red colors</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strontium_carbonate" title="Strontium carbonate">Strontium carbonate</a> and other strontium <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> are added to fireworks to give a deep red colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> This same effect identifies strontium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cations" title="Cations">cations</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a>.  Fireworks consumes about 5% of the world's production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-53">[53]</a></sup> Strontium carbonate is used in the manufacturing of hard <a href="/wiki/Ferrite_(magnet)" title="Ferrite (magnet)">ferrite</a> magnets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Strontium_chloride" title="Strontium chloride">Strontium chloride</a> is sometimes used in toothpastes for sensitive teeth. One popular brand includes 10% total strontium chloride hexahydrate by weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Small amounts are used in the refining of zinc to remove small amounts of lead impurities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-7">[7]</a></sup> The metal itself has a limited use as a <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">getter</a>, to remove unwanted gases in vacuums by reacting with them, although barium may also be used for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood111_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood111-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioactive_strontium">Radioactive strontium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Radioactive strontium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strontium-89" title="Strontium-89"><sup>89</sup>Sr</a> is the active ingredient in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastron" title="Metastron">Metastron</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceutical</a> used for bone pain secondary to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastatic" title="Metastatic">metastatic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_cancer" title="Bone cancer">bone cancer</a>. The strontium is processed like calcium by the body, preferentially incorporating it into bone at sites of increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osteogenesis" title="Osteogenesis">osteogenesis</a>. This localization focuses the radiation exposure on the cancerous lesion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BaumanCharette2005_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-BaumanCharette2005-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soviet_RTG.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="402" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Soviet_RTG.jpg/220px-Soviet_RTG.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Soviet_RTG.jpg/330px-Soviet_RTG.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Soviet_RTG.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soviet_RTG.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>RTGs from Soviet era lighthouses</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90"><sup>90</sup>Sr</a> has been used as a power source for <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generators</a> (RTGs). <sup>90</sup>Sr produces approximately 0.93 watts of heat per gram (it is lower for the form of <sup>90</sup>Sr used in RTGs, which is <a href="/wiki/Strontium_fluoride" title="Strontium fluoride">strontium fluoride</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> However, <sup>90</sup>Sr has one third the lifetime and a lower density than <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-238" title="Plutonium-238"><sup>238</sup>Pu</a>, another RTG fuel. The main advantage of <sup>90</sup>Sr is that it is cheaper than <sup>238</sup>Pu and is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" title="Nuclear waste">nuclear waste</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> deployed nearly 1000 of these RTGs on its northern coast as a power source for lighthouses and meteorology stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O’BrienAmbrosi2008_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-O’BrienAmbrosi2008-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Acantharea" title="Acantharea">Acantharea</a>, a relatively large group of marine <a href="/wiki/Radiolaria" title="Radiolaria">radiolarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>, produce intricate mineral <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeletons</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Strontium_sulfate" title="Strontium sulfate">strontium sulfate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In biological systems, calcium is substituted in a small extent by strontium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
In the human body, most of the absorbed strontium is deposited in the bones. The ratio of strontium to calcium in human bones is between  1:1000 and 1:2000 roughly in the same range as in the blood serum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_the_human_body">Effect on the human body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strontium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on the human body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The human body absorbs strontium as if it were its lighter congener calcium. Because the elements are chemically very similar, stable strontium isotopes do not pose a significant health threat. The average human has an intake of about two milligrams of strontium a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbb_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-78">[78]</a></sup> In adults, strontium consumed tends to attach only to the surface of bones, but in children, strontium can replace calcium in the mineral of the growing bones and thus lead to bone growth problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> of strontium in humans has variously been reported as from 14 to 600 days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> 1000 days,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> 18 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> 30 years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> and, at an upper limit, 49 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The wide-ranging published biological half-life figures are explained by strontium's complex metabolism within the body. However, by averaging all excretion paths, the overall biological half-life is estimated to be about 18 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The elimination rate of strontium is strongly affected by age and sex, due to differences in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_metabolism" title="Bone metabolism">bone metabolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShaginaBougrov2006_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShaginaBougrov2006-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>The drug <a href="/wiki/Strontium_ranelate" title="Strontium ranelate">strontium ranelate</a> aids <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> growth, increases bone density, and lessen the incidence of vertebral, peripheral, and hip <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">fractures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> However, strontium ranelate also increases the risk of venous thromboembolism, pulmonary embolism and serious cardiovascular disorders, including myocardial infarction. Its use is therefore now restricted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Its beneficial effects are also questionable, since the increased bone density is partially caused by the increased density of strontium over the calcium which it replaces. Strontium also <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulates</a> in the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Despite restrictions on <a href="/wiki/Strontium_ranelate" title="Strontium ranelate">strontium ranelate</a>, strontium is still contained in some supplements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD-Strontium_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD-Strontium-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD-StrontiumOsteoporosis_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD-StrontiumOsteoporosis-93">[93]</a></sup> There is not much scientific evidence on risks of strontium chloride when taken by mouth, those with personal or family history of blood clotting disorders are advised to avoid strontium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD-Strontium_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD-Strontium-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebMD-StrontiumOsteoporosis_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-WebMD-StrontiumOsteoporosis-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>Strontium has been shown to inhibit sensory irritation when applied topically to the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Topically applied, strontium has been shown to accelerate the recovery rate of the epidermal permeability barrier (skin barrier).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
