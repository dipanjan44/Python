<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anoxic event</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Intervals in the Earth's past where parts of oceans were depleted of oxygen at depth over a large geographic area</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2094" data-file-width="3577" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg/400px-Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg/600px-Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg/800px-Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aquatic_Dead_Zones.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red circles show the location and size of many dead zones.<br/>Black dots show Ocean dead zones of unknown size.<br/><i>The size and number of marine dead zones—areas where the deep water is so low in dissolved oxygen that sea creatures can't survive—have grown explosively in the past half-century.</i> – <small><a href="/wiki/NASA_Earth_Observatory" title="NASA Earth Observatory">NASA Earth Observatory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></small></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="1100" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/200px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/300px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/400px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This world perspective on oceanic currents demonstrates the interdependencies of transnational regions on circulating currents.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Oceanic anoxic events</b> or <b>anoxic events</b> (<a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxia</a> conditions) were intervals in the Earth's past where portions of <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> become depleted in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>)</a> at depths over a large geographic area. During some of these events, <a href="/wiki/Euxinia" title="Euxinia">euxinia</a>, waters that contained <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S</span> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although anoxic events have not happened for millions of years, the geological record shows that they happened many times in the past. Anoxic events coincided with several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" title="Mass extinctions">mass extinctions</a> and may have contributed to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> These mass extinctions include some that <a href="/wiki/Geobiology" title="Geobiology">geobiologists</a> use as <a href="/wiki/Global_Boundary_Stratotype_Section_and_Point" title="Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point">time markers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Biostratigraphy" title="Biostratigraphy">biostratigraphic</a> dating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many geologists believe oceanic anoxic events are strongly linked to slowing of ocean circulation, climatic warming, and elevated levels of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. Researchers have proposed enhanced <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> (the release of CO<sub>2</sub>) as the "central external trigger for euxinia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kump_2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kump_2008-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of the oceanic anoxic event (OAE) was first proposed in 1976 by Seymour Schlanger (1927–1990) and geologist Hugh Jenkyns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil-2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-2-6">[6]</a></sup> and arose from discoveries made by the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Sea_Drilling_Project" title="Deep Sea Drilling Project">Deep Sea Drilling Project</a> (DSDP) in the Pacific Ocean. It was the finding of black carbon-rich shales in Cretaceous sediments that had accumulated on submarine <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_plateau" title="Volcanic plateau">volcanic plateaus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shatsky_Rise" title="Shatsky Rise">Shatsky Rise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manihiki_Plateau" title="Manihiki Plateau">Manihiki Plateau</a>), coupled with the fact that they were identical in age with similar deposits cored from the Atlantic Ocean and known from outcrops in Europe - particularly in the geological record of the otherwise limestone-dominated <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">Apennines</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil-2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-2-6">[6]</a></sup> chain in Italy - that led to the realization that these widespread similar <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">strata</a> recorded highly unusual oxygen-depleted conditions in the world ocean during several discrete periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_time_scale" title="Geological time scale">geological time</a>.
</p><p>Sedimentological investigations of these organic-rich sediments, which have continued to this day, typically reveal the presence of fine laminations undisturbed by bottom-dwelling fauna, indicating anoxic conditions on the sea floor, believed to be coincident with a low lying poisonous layer of hydrogen sulfide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup> Furthermore, detailed organic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geochemical" title="Geochemical">geochemical</a> studies have recently revealed the presence of molecules (so-called biomarkers) that derive from both <a href="/wiki/Purple_sulfur_bacteria" title="Purple sulfur bacteria">purple sulfur bacteria</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Green_sulfur_bacteria" title="Green sulfur bacteria">green sulfur bacteria</a>: organisms that required both light and free <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide (H<sub>2</sub>S)</a>, illustrating that anoxic conditions extended high into the illuminated upper water column.
</p><p>There are currently several places on earth that are exhibiting the features of anoxic events on a localized scale such as algal/bacterial blooms and localized "dead zones". <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">Dead zones</a> exist off the East Coast of the United States in the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, in the Scandinavian strait <a href="/wiki/Kattegat" title="Kattegat">Kattegat</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> (which may have been anoxic in its deepest levels for millennia, however), in the northern Adriatic as well as a dead zone off the coast of Louisiana. The current<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> surge of <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> worldwide is sometimes regarded as the first stirrings of an anoxic event<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Other marine dead zones have appeared in coastal waters of South America, China, Japan, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. A 2008 study counted 405 dead zones worldwide.<a class="external autonumber" href="https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2008/08/080814154325.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>
</p><p>This is a recent understanding. This picture was only pieced together during the last three decades<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>. The handful of known and suspected anoxic events have been tied geologically to large-scale production of the world's oil reserves in worldwide bands of black <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_record" title="Geologic record">geologic record</a>. Likewise the high relative temperatures believed linked to so called "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Super-greenhouse_event&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Super-greenhouse event (page does not exist)">super-greenhouse events</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euxinia">Euxinia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Euxinia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_cycle" title="Phosphorus cycle">Phosphorus cycle</a></div>
<p>Oceanic <b>anoxic events</b> with <a href="/wiki/Euxinia" title="Euxinia">euxinic</a> (i.e. sulfidic) conditions have been linked to extreme episodes of volcanic outgassing. Thus, volcanism contributed to the buildup of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere, increased global temperatures, causing an accelerated hydrological cycle that introduced nutrients to the oceans to stimulate planktonic productivity. These processes potentially acted as a trigger for euxinia in restricted basins where water-column stratification could develop. Under anoxic to euxinic conditions, oceanic phosphate is not retained in sediment and could hence be released and recycled, aiding continued high productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kump_2008_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kump_2008-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 300px; float: right; clear: right; margin:0 0 1.5em 1.5em"><caption>External image</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;text-align: left">
<img alt="" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/16px-Searchtool.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/24px-Searchtool.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/32px-Searchtool.svg.png 2x" width="16"/> <a class="external text" href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v454/n7202/images/454285a-f1.2.jpg" rel="nofollow">nature.com</a> A flow chart of magma sourcing trace metals, ocean fertilization, stratification, and anoxia.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Temperatures throughout the Jurassic and Cretaceous are generally thought to have been relatively warm, and consequently dissolved oxygen levels in the ocean were lower than today - making anoxia easier to achieve. However, more specific conditions are required to explain the short-period (less than a million years) oceanic anoxic events. Two hypotheses, and variations upon them, have proved most durable.
</p><p>One hypothesis suggests that the anomalous accumulation of organic matter relates to its enhanced preservation under restricted and poorly oxygenated conditions, which themselves were a function of the particular geometry of the ocean basin: such a hypothesis, although readily applicable to the young and relatively narrow Cretaceous Atlantic (which could be likened to a large-scale Black Sea, only poorly connected to the World Ocean), fails to explain the occurrence of coeval black shales on open-ocean Pacific plateaus and shelf seas around the world.  There are suggestions, again from the Atlantic, that a shift in oceanic circulation was responsible, where warm, salty waters at low latitudes became hypersaline and sank to form an intermediate layer, at 500 to 1,000 m (1,640 to 3,281 ft) depth, with a temperature of 20 °C (68 °F) to 25 °C (77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngeo217_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngeo217-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The second hypothesis suggests that oceanic anoxic events record a major change in the fertility of the oceans that resulted in an increase in organic-walled plankton (including bacteria) at the expense of calcareous plankton such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccoliths" title="Coccoliths">coccoliths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>.
Such an accelerated flux of organic matter would have expanded and intensified the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_minimum_zone" title="Oxygen minimum zone">oxygen minimum zone</a>, further enhancing the amount of organic carbon entering the sedimentary record. Essentially this mechanism assumes a major increase in the availability of dissolved nutrients such as nitrate, phosphate and possibly iron to the phytoplankton population living in the illuminated layers of the oceans.
</p><p>For such an increase to occur would have required an accelerated influx of land-derived nutrients coupled with vigorous <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>, requiring major climate change on a global scale. Geochemical data from oxygen-<a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> ratios in carbonate sediments and fossils, and magnesium/calcium ratios in fossils, indicate that all major oceanic anoxic events were associated with thermal maxima, making it likely that global weathering rates, and nutrient flux to the oceans, were increased during these intervals.  Indeed, the reduced solubility of oxygen would lead to phosphate release, further nourishing the ocean and fuelling high productivity, hence a high oxygen demand - sustaining the event through a positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1146.2Fannurev.earth.36.031207.124256_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-10.1146.2Fannurev.earth.36.031207.124256-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Here is another way of looking at oceanic anoxic events.  Assume that the earth releases a huge volume of carbon dioxide during an interval of intense volcanism; global temperatures rise due to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>; global weathering rates and fluvial nutrient flux increase; organic productivity in the oceans increases; organic-<a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon burial</a> in the oceans increases (OAE begins); carbon dioxide is drawn down due to both burial of organic matter and weathering of silicate rocks (inverse greenhouse effect); global temperatures fall, and the ocean–atmosphere system returns to equilibrium (OAE ends).
</p><p>In this way, an oceanic anoxic event can be viewed as the Earth’s response to the injection of excess carbon dioxide into the atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>.  One test of this notion is to look at the age of <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous provinces</a> (LIPs), the extrusion of which would presumably have been accompanied by rapid effusion of vast quantities of volcanogenic gases such as carbon dioxide.  Intriguingly, the age of three LIPs (<a href="/wiki/Karoo-Ferrar" title="Karoo-Ferrar">Karoo-Ferrar</a> flood basalt, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_large_igneous_province" title="Caribbean large igneous province">Caribbean large igneous province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontong_Java_Plateau" title="Ontong Java Plateau">Ontong Java Plateau</a>) correlates uncannily well with that of the major <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> (early <a href="/wiki/Toarcian" title="Toarcian">Toarcian</a>) and Cretaceous (early <a href="/wiki/Aptian_extinction" title="Aptian extinction">Aptian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cenomanian-Turonian_boundary_event" title="Cenomanian-Turonian boundary event">Cenomanian–Turonian</a>) oceanic anoxic events, indicating that a causal link is feasible.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oceanic anoxic events most commonly occurred during periods of very warm <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> characterized by high levels of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and mean surface temperatures probably in excess of 25 °C (77 °F). The <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> levels, the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">period</a>, are just 13 °C (55 °F) in comparison. Such rises in carbon dioxide may have been in response to a great outgassing of the highly flammable <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (methane) that some call an "oceanic burp".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Six_steps_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Six_steps-10">[10]</a></sup> Vast quantities of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> are normally locked into the Earth's crust on the continental plateaus in one of the many deposits consisting of compounds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">methane hydrate</a>, a solid precipitated combination of methane and water much like ice. Because the methane hydrates are unstable, except at cool temperatures and high (deep) pressures, scientists have observed smaller "burps" due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> events.  Studies suggest the huge release of natural gas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup> could be a major climatological trigger, methane itself being a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> many times more powerful than carbon dioxide.  However, anoxia was also rife during the <a href="/wiki/Hirnantian" title="Hirnantian">Hirnantian</a> (late Ordovician) ice age.
</p><p>Oceanic anoxic events have been recognized primarily from the already warm <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">Periods</a>, when numerous examples have been documented,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gronstal_2008_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gronstal_2008-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_2008_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_2008-12">[12]</a></sup> but earlier examples have been suggested to have occurred in the late <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellwasser_event" title="Kellwasser event">Kellwasser event</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> (PETM), which was characterized by a global rise in temperature and deposition of organic-rich shales in some shelf seas, shows many similarities to oceanic anoxic events.
</p><p>Typically, oceanic anoxic events lasted for less than a million years, before a full recovery.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oceanic anoxic events have had many important consequences. It is believed that they have been responsible for mass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinction_events" title="Extinction events">extinctions</a> of marine organisms both in the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1146.2Fannurev.earth.36.031207.124256_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-10.1146.2Fannurev.earth.36.031207.124256-9">[9]</a></sup> The early Toarcian and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cenomanian-Turonian_anoxic_event" title="Cenomanian-Turonian anoxic event">Cenomanian-Turonian anoxic events</a> correlate with the <a href="/wiki/Toarcian_turnover" title="Toarcian turnover">Toarcian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cenomanian-Turonian_extinction_event" title="Cenomanian-Turonian extinction event">Cenomanian-Turonian extinction events</a> of mostly marine life forms. Apart from possible atmospheric effects, many deeper-dwelling marine organisms could not adapt to an ocean where oxygen penetrated only the surface layers.
</p><p>An economically significant consequence of oceanic anoxic events is the fact that the prevailing conditions in so many Mesozoic oceans has helped produce most of the world's <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> reserves. During an oceanic anoxic event, the accumulation and preservation of organic matter was much greater than normal, allowing the generation of potential petroleum <a href="/wiki/Source_rock" title="Source rock">source rocks</a> in many environments across the globe. Consequently, some 70 percent of oil source rocks are Mesozoic in age, and another 15 percent date from the warm Paleogene: only rarely in colder periods were conditions favorable for the production of source rocks on anything other than a local scale.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_effects">Atmospheric effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A model put forward by Lee Kump, Alexander Pavlov and Michael Arthur in 2005 suggests that oceanic anoxic events may have been characterized by upwelling of water rich in highly toxic hydrogen sulfide gas, which was then released into the atmosphere. This phenomenon would probably have poisoned plants and animals and caused mass extinctions. Furthermore, it has been proposed that the hydrogen sulfide rose to the upper atmosphere and attacked the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>, which normally blocks the deadly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The increased UV radiation caused by this <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> would have amplified the destruction of plant and animal life. Fossil spores from strata recording the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic extinction event</a> show deformities consistent with UV radiation. This evidence, combined with fossil <a href="/wiki/Bioindicator" title="Bioindicator">biomarkers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Green_sulfur_bacteria" title="Green sulfur bacteria">green sulfur bacteria</a>, indicates that this process could have played a role in that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinction" title="Mass extinction">mass extinction</a> event, and possibly other extinction events. The trigger for these mass extinctions appears to be a warming of the ocean caused by a rise of carbon dioxide levels to about 1000 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_chemistry_effects">Ocean chemistry effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ocean chemistry effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reduced oxygen levels are expected to lead to increased seawater concentrations of redox-sensitive metals. The reductive dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>–<a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> oxyhydroxides in seafloor sediments under low-oxygen conditions would release those metals and associated trace metals. Sulfate reduction in such sediments could release other metals such as <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>. When heavy-metal-rich anoxic deep water entered continental shelves and encountered increased O<sub>2</sub> levels, precipitation of some of the metals, as well as poisoning of the local biota, would have occurred. In the late Silurian mid-<a href="/wiki/Silurian#Pridoli" title="Silurian">Pridoli</a> event, increases are seen in the Fe, Cu, As, Al, Pb, Ba, Mo and Mn levels in shallow-water sediment and microplankton; this is associated with a marked increase in the malformation rate in <a href="/wiki/Chitinozoan" title="Chitinozoan">chitinozoans</a> and other microplankton types, likely due to metal toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandenbroucke2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vandenbroucke2015-14">[14]</a></sup> Similar metal enrichment has been reported in sediments from the mid-Silurian <a href="/wiki/Ireviken_event" title="Ireviken event">Ireviken event</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Anoxic_events_in_Earth.27s_history"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Anoxic_events_in_Earth's_history">Anoxic events in Earth's history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Anoxic events in Earth's history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cretaceous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cenomanian-Turonian_boundary_event" title="Cenomanian-Turonian boundary event">Cenomanian-Turonian boundary event</a></div>
<p>Sulfidic (or euxinic) conditions, which exist today in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_bodies" title="Water bodies">water bodies</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a> to various land-surrounded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_sea" title="Mediterranean sea">mediterranean seas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dicdef-1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dicdef-1-16">[16]</a></sup> such as the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, were particularly prevalent in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> Atlantic but also characterized other parts of the world ocean. In an ice-free sea of these supposed super-greenhouse worlds, oceanic waters were as much as 200 meters higher, in some eras. During the time spans in question, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_plate" title="Continental plate">continental plates</a> are believed to have been well separated, and the <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> we know today were (mostly) future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> events—meaning the overall landscapes were generally much lower— and even the half super-greenhouse climates would have been eras of highly expedited water erosion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistOfOil_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfOil-7">[7]</a></sup>  carrying massive amounts of nutrients into the world oceans fueling an overall explosive population of microorganisms and their predator species in the oxygenated upper layers.
</p><p>Detailed stratigraphic studies of Cretaceous black shales from many parts of the world have indicated that two oceanic anoxic events (OAEs) were particularly significant in terms of their impact on the chemistry of the oceans, one in the early <a href="/wiki/Aptian" title="Aptian">Aptian</a> (~120 Ma), sometimes called the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Selli_Event&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Selli Event (page does not exist)">Selli Event</a> (or OAE 1a) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oaedef_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-oaedef-17">[17]</a></sup> after the Italian geologist, Raimondo Selli (1916–1983), and another at the <a href="/wiki/Cenomanian" title="Cenomanian">Cenomanian</a>–<a href="/wiki/Turonian" title="Turonian">Turonian</a> boundary (~93 Ma), sometimes called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonarelli_Event" title="Bonarelli Event">Bonarelli Event</a> (or OAE 2)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oaedef_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-oaedef-17">[17]</a></sup> after the Italian geologist, Guido Bonarelli (1871–1951). OAE1a lasted for ~1.0 to 1.3 Myr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The duration of OAE2 is estimated to be ~820 kyr based on a high-resolution study of the significantly expanded OAE2 interval in southern Tibet, China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Insofar as the Cretaceous OAEs can be represented by type localities, it is the striking outcrops of laminated black shales within the vari-colored claystones and pink and white limestones near the town of Gubbio in the Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apennines" title="Apennines">Apennines</a> that are the best candidates.</li>
<li>The 1-meter thick black shale at the Cenomanian–Turonian boundary that crops out near Gubbio is termed the ‘Livello Bonarelli’ after the man who first described it in 1891.</li></ul>
<p>More minor oceanic anoxic events have been proposed for other intervals in the Cretaceous (in the <a href="/wiki/Valanginian" title="Valanginian">Valanginian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hauterivian" title="Hauterivian">Hauterivian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albian" title="Albian">Albian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coniacian" title="Coniacian">Coniacian</a>–<a href="/wiki/Santonian" title="Santonian">Santonian</a> stages), but their sedimentary record, as represented by organic-rich black shales, appears more parochial, being dominantly represented in the Atlantic and neighboring areas, and some researchers relate them to particular local conditions rather than being forced by global change.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic">Jurassic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Jurassic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a></div>
<p>The only oceanic anoxic event documented from the Jurassic took place during the early <a href="/wiki/Toarcian" title="Toarcian">Toarcian</a> (~183 Ma).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gronstal_2008_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gronstal_2008-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_2008_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen_2008-12">[12]</a></sup> Because no DSDP (<a href="/wiki/Deep_Sea_Drilling_Project" title="Deep Sea Drilling Project">Deep Sea Drilling Project</a>) or ODP (<a href="/wiki/Ocean_Drilling_Program" title="Ocean Drilling Program">Ocean Drilling Program</a>) cores have recovered black shales of this age – there being little or no Toarcian ocean crust remaining in the world ocean – the samples of black shale primarily come from outcrops on land.  These outcrops, together with material from some commercial oil wells, are found on all major continents and this event seems similar in kind to the two major Cretaceous examples.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Paleozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a></div>
<p>The boundary between the Ordovician and Silurian periods is marked by repetitive periods of anoxia, interspersed with normal, oxic conditions. In addition, anoxic periods are found during the Silurian.  These anoxic periods occurred at a time of low global temperatures (although CO<sub>2</sub> levels were high), in the midst of a glaciation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page2007_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Page2007-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jeppsson (1990) proposes a mechanism whereby the temperature of polar waters determines the site of formation of downwelling water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1990_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1990-21">[21]</a></sup>  If the high latitude waters are below 5 °C (41 °F), they will be dense enough to sink; as they are cool, oxygen is highly soluble in their waters, and the deep ocean will be oxygenated.  If high latitude waters are warmer than 5 °C (41 °F), their density is too low for them to sink below the cooler deep waters.  Therefore, thermohaline circulation can only be driven by salt-increased density, which tends to form in warm waters where evaporation is high.  This warm water can dissolve less oxygen, and is produced in smaller quantities, producing a sluggish circulation with little deep water oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1990_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1990-21">[21]</a></sup>  The effect of this warm water propagates through the ocean, and reduces the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> that the oceans can hold in solution, which makes the oceans release large quantities of CO<sub>2</sub> into the atmosphere in a geologically short time (tens or thousands of years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>  The warm waters also initiate the <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">release of clathrates</a>, which further increases atmospheric temperature and basin anoxia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>  Similar positive feedbacks operate during cold-pole episodes, amplifying their cooling effects.
</p><p>The periods with cold poles are termed "P-episodes" (short for <i>primo</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>), and are characterised by <a href="/wiki/Bioturbation" title="Bioturbation">bioturbated</a> deep oceans, a humid equator and higher weathering rates, and terminated by extinction events - for example, the <a href="/wiki/Ireviken_event" title="Ireviken event">Ireviken</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lau_event" title="Lau event">Lau events</a>.  The inverse is true for the warmer, oxic "S-episodes" (<i>secundo</i>), where deep ocean sediments are typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graptolit" title="Graptolit">graptolitic</a> black shales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1990_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1990-21">[21]</a></sup>
A typical cycle of <i>secundo-primo</i> episodes and ensuing <i>event</i> typically lasts around 3 Ma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The duration of events is so long compared to their onset because the positive feedbacks must be overwhelmed.  Carbon content in the ocean-atmosphere system is affected by changes in weathering rates, which in turn is dominantly controlled by rainfall. Because this is inversely related to temperature in Silurian times, carbon is gradually drawn down during warm (high CO<sub>2</sub>) S-episodes, while the reverse is true during P-episodes.  On top of this gradual trend is overprinted the signal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milankovic_cycles" title="Milankovic cycles">Milankovic cycles</a>, which ultimately trigger the switch between P- and S- episodes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>These events become longer during the Devonian; the enlarging land plant biota probably acted as a large buffer to carbon dioxide concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeppsson1997_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jeppsson1997-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The end-Ordovician Hirnantian event may alternatively be a result of algal blooms, caused by sudden supply of nutrients through wind-driven upwelling or an influx of nutrient-rich meltwater from melting glaciers, which by virtue of its fresh nature would also slow down oceanic circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lüning2006_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lüning2006-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archean_and_Proterozoic">Archean and Proterozoic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anoxic_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Archean and Proterozoic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been thought that through most of Earth's history, oceans were largely oxygen-deficient. During the <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a>, euxinia was largely absent because of low availability of <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> in the oceans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kump_2008_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kump_2008-5">[5]</a></sup> but during the Proterozoic, it would become more common.
</p>
