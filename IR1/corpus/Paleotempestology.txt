<p><b>Paleotempestology</b> is the study of past <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> activity by means of geological proxies as well as historical documentary records. The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a>.
</p>

<p>Examples of proxies include <a href="/wiki/Overwash" title="Overwash">overwash</a> deposits preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> of coastal lakes and <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Micropaleontology" title="Micropaleontology">microfossils</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phytolith" title="Phytolith">phytoliths</a> contained in coastal sediments, wave-generated or flood-generated sedimentary structures or deposits (called <a href="/wiki/Tempestite" title="Tempestite">tempestites</a>) in marine or <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoonal</a> sediments, storm wave deposited <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> shingle, shell, sand and shell and pure sand shore parallel ridges.
</p><p>The method of using overwash deposits preserved in coastal lake and marsh sediments is adopted from earlier studies of paleotsunami deposits. Both <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a> leave very similar if not identical sedimentary deposits in coastal lakes and marshes and differentiating between the two in a sedimentary record can be difficult. The first studies to examine prehistoric records of tropical cyclones occurred in Australia and the South Pacific during the late 1970s and early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baines1976_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baines1976-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhodes1980_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rhodes1980-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chappell1983_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chappell1983-3">[3]</a></sup> These studies examined multiple shore parallel ridges of coral shingle or sand and marine shells. As many as 50 ridges can be deposited at a site with each representing a past severe <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> over the previous 6,000 years. Tsunamis are not known to deposit multiple sedimentary ridges and therefore these features can be more easily attributed to a past storm at any given site.
</p><p>Coastal sedimentary analyses have been done at the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">U.S. Gulf coast</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu1999-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiuFearn2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiuFearn2000-5">[5]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> coast from <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott2003-6">[6]</a></sup> up to <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly2001a_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly2001a-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly2004-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly2001b_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly2001b-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly2007_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly2007-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCloskey2008_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCloskey2008-11">[11]</a></sup> Also, studies on pre-historic tropical cyclones hitting <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> have been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nott2001-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nott2007-13">[13]</a></sup> A study covering the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> coast has also been published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Markers_in_coral">Markers in coral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleotempestology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Markers in coral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rocks contain certain <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>, known as natural tracers, which describe the conditions under which they formed.  By studying the <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> in coral rock, past sea surface temperature and hurricane information can be revealed.  Heavier <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> isotopes (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen-18" title="Oxygen-18"><sup>18</sup>O</a>) decrease in relation to lighter oxygen isotopes (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen-16" title="Oxygen-16"><sup>16</sup>O</a>) in coral during periods of very heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Since hurricanes are the main source of extreme rainfall in the tropical oceans<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, past hurricane events can be dated to the days of their impact on the coral by looking at the decreased <sup>18</sup>O concentration within the coral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speleothems_and_tree_rings">Speleothems and tree rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleotempestology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Speleothems and tree rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Isotope studies in <a href="/wiki/Speleothem" title="Speleothem">speleothems</a> and tree rings (<a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a>) offers a means by which higher resolution records of long-term tropical cyclone histories can be attained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Unlike the isotope records, the sedimentary records are too coarse in their resolution to register quasi-cyclic activity at decadal to centennial scales. These higher resolution records therefore offer a means for possibly differentiating between the natural variability of tropical cyclone behaviour and the effects of anthropogenically induced global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.  Recent studies with <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> shows that events can be determined on a week-by-week basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frappier2007_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frappier2007-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_records">Historical records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleotempestology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1000" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/300px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/450px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/600px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>The <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_(ship)" title="Royal Charter (ship)">Royal Charter</a></i> which sank in a storm off <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a> in 1859.</center></div></div></div>
<p>Before the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraph</a> in the early to mid-19th century, news was as fast as the fastest horse or stagecoach or ship.  Normally, there was no advance warning of a tropical cyclone impact.  However, the situation changed in the 19th century as seafaring people and land-based researchers, such as Father Viñes in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, came up with systematic methods of reading the sky's appearance or the sea state, which could foretell a tropical cyclone's approach up to a couple days in advance.
</p><p>One of the best documented storms is the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_Storm" title="Royal Charter Storm">Royal Charter Storm</a> of 1859 which caused over 800 deaths in the UK alone. It led directly to the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_Office" title="Meteorological Office">Meteorological Office</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzroy" title="Robert Fitzroy">Robert Fitzroy</a>. However, wind speed could not be measured accurately at the time, methods only becoming available after the <a href="/wiki/Tay_Bridge_disaster" title="Tay Bridge disaster">Tay Bridge disaster</a> of 1879.
One of the better sources for storms in and around <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwreck</a> statistics compiled annually by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>, but which have yet to be analysed in detail.
</p><p>Michael Chenoweth used 18th century journals to reconstruct the climate of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chenoweth2003_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chenoweth2003-19">[19]</a></sup> Together with Dmitry Divine, he also created a 318-year (1690–2007) record of tropical cyclones in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, using newspaper accounts, ships' <a href="/wiki/Logbook" title="Logbook">logbooks</a>, meteorological journals, and other document sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the abundance of historical documentary records in the form of <i>Fang Zhi</i> (semiofficial local <a href="/wiki/Gazette" title="Gazette">gazettes</a>) offers an extraordinary opportunity for providing a high-resolution historical dataset for the frequency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_typhoon" title="Pacific typhoon">typhoon</a> strikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu2001_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu2001-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
