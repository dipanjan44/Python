<p><b>Rhodium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Rh</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 45. It is a rare, silvery-white, hard, <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion-resistant</a> and chemically <a href="/wiki/Chemically_inert" title="Chemically inert">inert</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Noble metal">noble metal</a> and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a>. It has only one naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>103</sup>Rh. Naturally occurring rhodium is usually found as the free metal, alloyed with similar metals, and rarely as a chemical compound in minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Bowieite" title="Bowieite">bowieite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhodplumsite" title="Rhodplumsite">rhodplumsite</a>. It is one of the rarest and most valuable <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>.
</p><p>Rhodium is found in platinum or nickel ores together with the other members of the <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group" title="Platinum group">platinum group</a> metals. It was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> in 1803 by <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a> in one such ore, and named for the rose color of one of its chlorine compounds, produced after it reacted with the powerful acid mixture <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.
</p><p>The element's major use (approximately 80% of world rhodium production) is as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter#Three-way" title="Catalytic converter">three-way catalytic converters</a> in automobiles. Because rhodium metal is inert against corrosion and most aggressive chemicals, and because of its rarity, rhodium is usually <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloyed</a> with <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> and applied in high-temperature and corrosion-resistive coatings. <a href="/wiki/Colored_gold#White_gold" title="Colored gold">White gold</a> is often plated with a thin rhodium layer to improve its appearance while <a href="/wiki/Sterling_silver" title="Sterling silver">sterling silver</a> is often rhodium-plated for tarnish resistance.
</p><p>Rhodium detectors are used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> to measure the <a href="/wiki/Neutron_detection" title="Neutron detection">neutron flux level</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="561" data-file-width="432" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/170px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/255px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg/340px-Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wollaston_William_Hyde_Jackson_color.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a></div></div></div>
<p>Rhodium (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>rhodon</i> (ῥόδον) meaning "rose") was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> in 1803 by <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> soon after his discovery of <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-contr-8">[8]</a></sup> He used crude <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> ore presumably obtained from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> His procedure involved dissolving the ore in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a> and neutralizing the acid with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (NaOH). He then precipitated the platinum as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloroplatinate" title="Ammonium chloroplatinate">ammonium chloroplatinate</a> by adding <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>Cl</span>). Most other metals like <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> and rhodium were precipitated with <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>. Diluted <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> dissolved all but palladium and rhodium. Of these, palladium dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a> but rhodium did not,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the rhodium was precipitated by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> as <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>[RhCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>]·<i>n</i>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span>. After being washed with ethanol, the rose-red precipitate was reacted with zinc, which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Displacement_reaction" title="Displacement reaction">displaced</a> the rhodium in the ionic compound and thereby released the rhodium as free metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-griffith_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-griffith-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the discovery, the rare element had only minor applications; for example, by the turn of the century, rhodium-containing thermocouples were used to measure temperatures up to 1800 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The first major application was electroplating for decorative uses and as corrosion-resistant coating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The introduction of the three-way <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> by <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">Volvo</a> in 1976 increased the demand for rhodium. The previous catalytic converters used platinum or palladium, while the three-way catalytic converter used rhodium to reduce the amount of <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a> in the exhaust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Rhodium is a hard, silvery, durable metal that has a high <a href="/wiki/Reflectance" title="Reflectance">reflectance</a>. Rhodium metal does not normally form an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>, even when heated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASM13B_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASM13B-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> is absorbed from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> only at the <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of rhodium, but is released on solidification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Rhodium has both a higher melting point and lower <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> than <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>. It is not attacked by most <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>: it is completely insoluble in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and dissolves slightly in <a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">aqua regia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="1100" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png/170px-Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png/255px-Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png/340px-Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wilkinson%27s-catalyst-2D.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wilkinson's catalyst</div></div></div>
<p>Rhodium belongs to <a href="/wiki/Group_9_element" title="Group 9 element">group 9</a> of the periodic table, but the configuration of electrons in the outermost shells is atypical for the group. This anomaly is also observed in the neighboring elements, <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a> (41), <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> (44), and <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> (46).

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_rhodium" title="Isotopes of rhodium">Isotopes of rhodium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring rhodium is composed of only one <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <sup>103</sup>Rh. The most stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> are <sup>101</sup>Rh with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.3 years, <sup>102</sup>Rh with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 207 days, <sup>102m</sup>Rh with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 2.9 years, and <sup>99</sup>Rh with a half-life of 16.1 days. Twenty other radioisotopes have been characterized with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weights</a> ranging from 92.926 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>93</sup>Rh) to 116.925 u (<sup>117</sup>Rh). Most of these have half-lives shorter than an hour, except <sup>100</sup>Rh (20.8 hours) and <sup>105</sup>Rh (35.36 hours). It has numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, the most stable being <sup>102m</sup>Rh (0.141 MeV) with a half-life of about 2.9 years and <sup>101m</sup>Rh (0.157 MeV) with a half-life of 4.34 days (see <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_rhodium" title="Isotopes of rhodium">isotopes of rhodium</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In isotopes weighing less than 103 (the stable isotope), the primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and the primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay product</a> is <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>  In isotopes greater than 103, the primary decay mode is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_emission" title="Beta emission">beta emission</a> and the primary product is <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rhodium is one of the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">rarest elements in the Earth's crust</a>, comprising an estimated 0.0002 <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts per million</a> (2 × 10<sup>−10</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  Its rarity affects its price and its use in commercial applications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining_and_price">Mining and price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mining and price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rh_price.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1400" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Rh_price.png/350px-Rh_price.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Rh_price.png/525px-Rh_price.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Rh_price.png/700px-Rh_price.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rh_price.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rh price evolution</div></div></div>
<p>The industrial extraction of rhodium is complex because the ores are mixed with other metals such as <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and there are very few rhodium-bearing <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. It is found in platinum ores and extracted as a white inert metal that is difficult to fuse. Principal sources are located in South Africa; in river sands of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Ural Mountains</a>; and in North America, including the <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>-<a href="/wiki/Nickel_sulfide" title="Nickel sulfide">nickel sulfide</a> mining area of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, region. Although the quantity at Sudbury is very small, the large amount of processed nickel ore makes rhodium recovery cost-effective.
</p><p>The main exporter of rhodium is South Africa (approximately 80% in 2010) followed by Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSYB08_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSYB08-27">[27]</a></sup> The annual world production is 30 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. The price of rhodium is highly variable. In 2007, rhodium cost approximately eight times more than gold, 450 times more than silver, and 27,250 times more than copper by weight. In 2008, the price briefly rose above $10,000 per ounce ($350,000 per kilogram). The economic slowdown of the 3rd quarter of 2008 pushed rhodium prices sharply back below $1,000 per ounce ($35,000 per kilogram); the price rebounded to $2,750 by early 2010 ($97,000 per kilogram) (more than twice the gold price), but in late 2013, the prices were less than $1000.
</p><p>Political and financial problems<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="which problems, where, when? (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> led to very low oil prices and oversupply, causing most metals to drop in price. The economies of China, India and other emerging countries slowed in 2014 and 2015. In 2014 alone, 23,722,890 motor vehicles were produced in China, excluding motorbikes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="not clear whether this random figure represents normal production, increase or decrease (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This resulted in a rhodium price of 740.00 US-$ per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" title="Troy ounce">Troy ounce</a> (31.1 grams) in late November 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Used_nuclear_fuels">Used nuclear fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Used nuclear fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthesis_of_precious_metals" title="Synthesis of precious metals">Synthesis of precious metals</a></div>
<p>Rhodium is a fission product of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>: each kilogram of fission product contains a significant amount of the lighter platinum group metals. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Used_nuclear_fuel" title="Used nuclear fuel">Used nuclear fuel</a> is therefore a potential source of rhodium, but the extraction is complex and expensive, and the presence of rhodium radioisotopes requires a period of cooling storage for multiple half-lives of the longest-lived isotope (about 10 years). These factors make the source unattractive and no large-scale extraction has been attempted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary use of this element is in automobiles as a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a>, changing harmful unburned hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, and nitrogen oxide exhaust emissions into less noxious gases. Of 30,000 kg of rhodium consumed worldwide in 2012, 81% (24,300 kg) went into this application, and 8,060 kg was recovered from old converters. About 964 kg of rhodium was used in the glass industry, mostly for production of fiberglass and flat-panel glass, and  2,520 kg was used in the chemical industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSYB08_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSYB08-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalyst">Catalyst</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Catalyst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodium is preferable to the other platinum metals in the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whyrhodium_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whyrhodium-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>x</i></sub></span></span> → <i>x</i> <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Rhodium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> are used in a number of industrial processes, notably in catalytic carbonylation of <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Monsanto_process" title="Monsanto process">Monsanto process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> It is also used to catalyze addition of hydrosilanes to molecular <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a>, a process important in manufacture of certain silicone rubbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Rhodium catalysts are also used to reduce <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane" title="Cyclohexane">cyclohexane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The complex of a rhodium ion with <a href="/wiki/BINAP" title="BINAP">BINAP</a> is a widely used chiral catalyst for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral_synthesis" title="Chiral synthesis">chiral synthesis</a>, as in the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Menthol" title="Menthol">menthol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ornamental_uses">Ornamental uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ornamental uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodium finds use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a> and for decorations. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electroplated" title="Electroplated">electroplated</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_gold" title="White gold">white gold</a> and platinum to give it a reflective white surface at time of sale, after which the thin layer wears away with use. This is known as rhodium flashing in the jewelry business. It may also be used in coating <a href="/wiki/Sterling_silver" title="Sterling silver">sterling silver</a> to protect against tarnish (<a href="/wiki/Silver_sulfide" title="Silver sulfide">silver sulfide</a>, Ag<sub>2</sub>S, produced from atmospheric hydrogen sulfide, H<sub>2</sub>S). Solid (pure) rhodium jewelry is very rare, more because of the difficulty of fabrication (high melting point and poor malleability) than because of the high price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The high cost ensures that rhodium is applied only as an <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplate</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg/220px-Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg/330px-Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg/440px-Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Example_of_Solid_Rhodium_Ring.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rhodium is rarely seen as jewelry in its pure, solid form. This ring was made by the photographer for his own use as a wedding band from solid, unalloyed .999 rhodium.</div></div></div>
<p>Rhodium has also been used for honors or to signify elite status, when more commonly used metals such as silver, gold or platinum were deemed insufficient. In 1979 the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a></i> gave <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> a rhodium-plated disc for being history's all-time best-selling songwriter and recording artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>s Voudgameing 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rhodium is used as an alloying agent for hardening and improving the corrosion resistance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASM13B_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASM13B-18">[18]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>. These alloys are used in furnace windings, bushings for glass fiber production, <a href="/wiki/Thermocouple" title="Thermocouple">thermocouple</a> elements, <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> for aircraft <a href="/wiki/Spark_plug" title="Spark plug">spark plugs</a>, and laboratory crucibles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Other uses include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Switch#Contacts" title="Switch">Electrical contacts</a>, where it is valued for small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">electrical resistance</a>, small and stable <a href="/wiki/Contact_resistance" title="Contact resistance">contact resistance</a>, and great <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li>Rhodium plated by either <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> or evaporation is extremely hard and useful for optical instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>Filters in <a href="/wiki/Mammography" title="Mammography">mammography</a> systems for the characteristic X-rays it produces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>Rhodium neutron detectors are used in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Combustion_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Combustion engineering (page does not exist)">combustion engineering</a> nuclear reactors to measure neutron flux levels – this method requires a digital filter to determine the current neutron flux level, generating three separate signals: immediate, a few seconds delay, and a minute delay, each with its own signal level; all three are combined in the rhodium detector signal. The three <a href="/wiki/Palo_Verde_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Palo Verde Nuclear Generating Station">Palo Verde</a> nuclear reactors each have 305 rhodium neutron detectors, 61 detectors on each of five vertical levels, providing an accurate 3D "picture" of reactivity and allowing fine tuning to consume the nuclear fuel most economically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>Being a <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Noble metal">noble metal</a>, pure rhodium is inert. However, chemical complexes of rhodium can be reactive. <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">Median lethal dose</a> (LD<sub>50</sub>) for rats is 198 mg of rhodium chloride (<span class="chemf nowrap">RhCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) per kilogram of body weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Like the other noble metals, all of which are too inert to occur as chemical compounds in nature, rhodium has not been found to serve any biological function. In elemental form, the metal is harmless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to rhodium in the workplace by inhalation. The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has specified the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">Permissible exposure limit</a>) for rhodium exposure in the workplace at 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set the <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL), at the same level. At levels of 100 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, rhodium is <a href="/wiki/Immediately_dangerous_to_life_or_health" title="Immediately dangerous to life or health">immediately dangerous to life or health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> For soluble compounds, the PEL and REL are both 0.001 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
