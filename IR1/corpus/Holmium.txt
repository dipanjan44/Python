<p><b>Holmium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Ho</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 67. Part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanide_series" title="Lanthanide series">lanthanide series</a>, holmium is a <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">rare-earth element</a>. Holmium was discovered by Swedish chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Per_Theodor_Cleve" title="Per Theodor Cleve">Per Theodor Cleve</a>. Its oxide was first isolated from rare-earth ores in 1878. The element's name comes from <i>Holmia</i>, the Latin name for the city of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>.
</p><p>Elemental holmium is a relatively soft and malleable silvery-white <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>. It is too reactive to be found uncombined in nature, but when isolated, is relatively stable in dry air at room temperature. However, it reacts with water and corrodes readily and also burns in air when heated.
</p><p>Holmium is found in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> and is usually commercially extracted from monazite using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion-exchange" title="Ion-exchange">ion-exchange</a> techniques. Its compounds in nature and in nearly all of its laboratory chemistry are trivalently oxidized, containing Ho(III) ions. Trivalent holmium ions have fluorescent properties similar to many other rare-earth ions (while yielding their own set of unique emission light lines), and thus are used in the same way as some other rare earths in certain laser and glass-colorant applications.
</p><p>Holmium has the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_permeability" title="Magnetic permeability">magnetic permeability</a> of any element and therefore is used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polepiece" title="Polepiece">polepieces</a> of the strongest static <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnets</a>. Because holmium strongly absorbs neutrons, it is also used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnable_poison" title="Burnable poison">burnable poison</a> in nuclear reactors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holmium(III)_oxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1090" data-file-width="1180" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg/220px-Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg/330px-Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg/440px-Holmium%28III%29_oxide.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Holmium(III)_oxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ho<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, left: natural light, right: under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold-cathode" title="Cold-cathode">cold-cathode</a> fluorescent lamp</div></div></div>
<p>Holmium is a relatively soft and malleable element that is fairly <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>-resistant and stable in dry air at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>. In moist air and at higher <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a>, however, it quickly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidizes</a>, forming a yellowish oxide. In pure form, holmium possesses a metallic, bright silvery luster.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Holmium(III)_oxide" title="Holmium(III) oxide">Holmium oxide</a> has some fairly dramatic color changes depending on the lighting conditions. In daylight, it has a tannish yellow color. Under trichromatic light, it is fiery orange-red, almost indistinguishable from the appearance of erbium oxide under the same lighting conditions. The perceived color change is related to the sharp absorption bands of holmium interacting with a subset of the sharp emission bands of the trivalent ions of europium and terbium, acting as phosphors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Holmium has the highest <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001106000000000000♠</span>10.6 <a href="/wiki/Bohr_magneton" title="Bohr magneton">µ<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:left"><br/>B</span></a></span>) of any naturally occurring element and possesses other unusual magnetic properties. When combined with <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>, it forms highly <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a> compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-appl-3">[3]</a></sup> Holmium is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> at ambient conditions, but is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> at temperatures below <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001190000000000000♠</span>19 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Holmium metal tarnishes slowly in air and burns readily to form <a href="/wiki/Holmium(III)_oxide" title="Holmium(III) oxide">holmium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Ho + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Ho<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Holmium is quite electropositive and is generally trivalent. It reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form holmium hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ho (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Ho(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Holmium metal reacts with all the halogens:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ho (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 HoF<sub>3</sub> (s) [pink]</dd>
<dd>2 Ho (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 <a href="/wiki/Holmium(III)_chloride" title="Holmium(III) chloride">HoCl<sub>3</sub></a> (s) [yellow]</dd>
<dd>2 Ho (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 HoBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [yellow]</dd>
<dd>2 Ho (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 HoI<sub>3</sub> (s) [yellow]</dd></dl>
<p>Holmium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the yellow Ho(III) ions, which exist as a [Ho(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ho (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Ho<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Holmium's most common oxidation state is +3. Holmium in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> is in the form of Ho<sup>3+</sup> surrounded by nine molecules of water. Holmium dissolves in acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature's_building_blocks_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature's_building_blocks-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_holmium" title="Isotopes of holmium">Isotopes of holmium</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Natural</a> holmium contains one <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stable</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, holmium-165. Some synthetic radioactive isotopes are known; the most stable one is holmium-163, with a half-life of 4570 years. All other radioisotopes have ground-state half-lives not greater than 1.117 days, and most have half-lives under 3 hours. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> <sup>166m1</sup>Ho has a half-life of around 1200 years because of its high <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>. This fact, combined with a high excitation energy resulting in a particularly rich spectrum of decay <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> produced when the metastable state de-excites, makes this isotope useful in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> experiments as a means for calibrating energy responses and intrinsic efficiencies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_spectrometer" title="Gamma ray spectrometer">gamma ray spectrometers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Holmium (<i>Holmia</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name for <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>) was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Discovery of the chemical elements">discovered</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jacques-Louis_Soret" title="Jacques-Louis Soret">Jacques-Louis Soret</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Delafontaine" title="Marc Delafontaine">Marc Delafontaine</a> in 1878 who noticed the aberrant <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">spectrographic</a> <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">absorption bands</a> of the then-unknown element (they called it "Element X").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The following year, <a href="/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve" title="Per Teodor Cleve">Per Teodor Cleve</a> independently discovered the element while he was working on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbia" title="Erbia">erbia</a> earth (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erbium_oxide" title="Erbium oxide">erbium oxide</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using the method developed by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a>, Cleve first removed all of the known contaminants from erbia. The result of that effort was two new materials, one brown and one green. He named the brown substance holmia (after the Latin name for Cleve's home town, Stockholm) and the green one thulia. Holmia was later found to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holmium_oxide" title="Holmium oxide">holmium oxide</a>, and thulia was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thulium_oxide" title="Thulium oxide">thulium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-11">[11]</a></sup>
In <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a>'s classic paper<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> on atomic numbers, holmium was assigned an atomic number of 66.  Evidently, the holmium preparation he had been given to investigate had been grossly impure, dominated by neighboring (and unplotted) dysprosium. He would have seen x-ray emission lines for both elements, but assumed that the dominant ones belonged to holmium, instead of the dysprosium impurity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gadolinitas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Gadolinitas.jpg/220px-Gadolinitas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Gadolinitas.jpg/330px-Gadolinitas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Gadolinitas.jpg/440px-Gadolinitas.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gadolinitas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">Gadolinite</a></div></div></div>
<p>Like all other rare earths, holmium is not naturally found as a free element. It does occur combined with other elements in <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a> (the black part of the specimen illustrated to the right), <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and other rare-earth minerals. No holmium-dominant mineral has yet been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The main mining areas are <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> with reserves of holmium estimated as 400,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Holmium makes up 1.4 parts per million of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> by mass. This makes it the 56th most abundant element in the Earth's crust. Holmium makes up 1 part per million of the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, 400 parts per quadrillion of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>, and almost none of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>. Holmium is rare for a lanthanide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-13">[13]</a></sup> It makes up 500 parts per trillion of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> by mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is commercially extracted by <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> from monazite sand (0.05% holmium), but is still difficult to separate from other rare earths. The element has been isolated through the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of its anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> with metallic <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-15">[15]</a></sup> Its estimated abundance in the Earth's crust is 1.3 mg/kg. Holmium obeys the <a href="/wiki/Oddo%E2%80%93Harkins_rule" title="Oddo–Harkins rule">Oddo–Harkins rule</a>: as an odd-numbered element, it is less abundant than its immediate even-numbered neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>. However, it is the most abundant of the odd-numbered heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lanthanides" title="Lanthanides">lanthanides</a>. The principal current source are some of the ion-adsorption clays of southern China. Some of these have a rare-earth composition similar to that found in <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> or gadolinite. <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Yttrium</a> makes up about 2/3 of the total by mass; holmium is around 1.5%. The original ores themselves are very lean, maybe only 0.1% total lanthanide, but are easily extracted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patnaik_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-16">[16]</a></sup> Holmium is relatively inexpensive for a rare-earth metal with the price about 1000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HoOxideSolution.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="242" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/HoOxideSolution.jpg/220px-HoOxideSolution.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/HoOxideSolution.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HoOxideSolution.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A solution of 4% holmium oxide in 10% perchloric acid, permanently fused into a quartz cuvette as an optical calibration standard</div></div></div>
<p>Holmium has the highest magnetic strength of any element, and therefore is used to create the strongest artificially generated <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, when placed within high-strength magnets as a magnetic pole piece (also called a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_flux_concentrator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magnetic flux concentrator (page does not exist)">magnetic flux concentrator</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Since it can absorb nuclear fission-bred neutrons, it is also used as a burnable poison to regulate nuclear reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Holmium is used in yttrium-<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-<a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a> (YIG) and yttrium-<a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a>-fluoride (YLF) <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state lasers</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> equipment (which are in turn found in a variety of medical and dental settings). Holmium lasers emit at 2.1 micrometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> They are used in medical, dental, and fiber-optical applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-appl_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-appl-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Holmium is one of the colorants used for <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic zirconia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, providing yellow or red coloring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Glass containing holmium oxide and holmium oxide solutions (usually in <a href="/wiki/Perchloric_acid" title="Perchloric acid">perchloric acid</a>) have sharp optical absorption peaks in the spectral range 200–900 nm. They are therefore used as a calibration standard for <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">optical spectrophotometers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and are available commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The radioactive but long-lived <sup>166m1</sup>Ho (see "Isotopes" above) is used in calibration of gamma-ray spectrometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> announced that they have developed a technique to store one <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> of data on a single Holmium atom set on a bed of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Holmium plays no biological role in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, but its salts are able to stimulate <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-15">[15]</a></sup> Humans typically consume about a milligram of holmium a year. Plants do not readily take up holmium from the soil. Some vegetables have had their holmium content measured, and it amounted to 100 parts per trillion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature's_building_blocks_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature's_building_blocks-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holmium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large amounts of holmium <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> can cause severe damage if <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhaled" title="Inhaled">inhaled</a>, consumed <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">orally</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Injection_(medicine)" title="Injection (medicine)">injected</a>. The biological effects of holmium over a long period of time are not known. Holmium has a low level of acute toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
