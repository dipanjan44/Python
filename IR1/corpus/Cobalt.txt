<p><b>Cobalt</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Co</b> and atomic number 27. Like nickel, cobalt is found in the Earth's crust only in chemically combined form, save for small deposits found in alloys of natural <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">meteoric iron</a>. The free element, produced by reductive <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>, is a hard, lustrous, silver-gray <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>.
</p><p>Cobalt-based blue pigments (<a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt blue</a>) have been used since ancient times for jewelry and paints, and to impart a distinctive blue tint to glass, but the color was later thought by alchemists to be due to the known metal <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>. Miners had long used the name <i><a href="/wiki/Kobold" title="Kobold">kobold</a> ore</i> (German for <i>goblin ore</i>) for some of the blue-pigment producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a>; they were so named because they were poor in known metals, and gave poisonous <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>-containing fumes when smelted. In 1735, such ores were found to be reducible to a new metal (the first discovered since ancient times), and this was ultimately named for the <i>kobold</i>.
</p><p>Today, some cobalt is produced specifically from one of a number of metallic-lustered ores, such as for example <a href="/wiki/Cobaltite" title="Cobaltite">cobaltite</a> (Co<a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">As</a><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a>). The element is however more usually produced as a by-product of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> mining. <a href="/wiki/Copperbelt" title="Copperbelt">The copper belt</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> (DRC), <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> yields most of the global cobalt production. The DRC alone accounted for more than 50% of world production in 2016 (123,000 tonnes), according to <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt is primarily used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a>, wear-resistant and high-strength <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>. The compounds cobalt silicate and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt(II) aluminate</a> (CoAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, cobalt blue) give a distinctive deep blue color to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">inks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a> and <a href="/wiki/Varnish" title="Varnish">varnishes</a>. Cobalt occurs naturally as only one stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, cobalt-59. <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">Cobalt-60</a> is a commercially important radioisotope, used as a <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_tracer" title="Radioactive tracer">radioactive tracer</a> and for the production of high energy <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a>.
</p><p>Cobalt is the active center of a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coenzymes" title="Coenzymes">coenzymes</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalamin" title="Cobalamin">cobalamins</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B<sub><small>12</small></sub></a>, the best-known example of the type, is an essential <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamin</a> for all animals. Cobalt in inorganic form is also a <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrient</a> for <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kobalt_13g.jpg"><img alt="a sample of pure cobolt" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="589" data-file-width="649" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Kobalt_13g.jpg/220px-Kobalt_13g.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Kobalt_13g.jpg/330px-Kobalt_13g.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Kobalt_13g.jpg/440px-Kobalt_13g.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kobalt_13g.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A block of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolytically</a> refined cobalt (99.9% purity) cut from a large plate</div></div></div>
<p>Cobalt is a <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a> metal with a <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> of 8.9. The <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie temperature</a> is 1,115 °C (2,039 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the magnetic moment is 1.6–1.7 <a href="/wiki/Bohr_magneton" title="Bohr magneton">Bohr magnetons</a> per <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Cobalt has a <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism)" title="Permeability (electromagnetism)">relative permeability</a> two-thirds that of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">Metallic</a> cobalt occurs as two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystallographic_structure" title="Crystallographic structure">crystallographic structures</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_close_packed" title="Hexagonal close packed">hcp</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Face-centered_cubic" title="Face-centered cubic">fcc</a>. The ideal transition temperature between the hcp and fcc structures is 450 °C (842 °F), but in practice the energy difference between them is so small that random intergrowth of the two is common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt is a weakly reducing metal that is protected from oxidation by a <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivating</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> film. It is attacked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halogens" title="Halogens">halogens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>. Heating in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II,III)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II,III) oxide">Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a> which loses oxygen at 900 °C (1,650 °F) to give the <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II) oxide">monoxide</a> CoO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-11">[11]</a></sup> The metal reacts with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F<sub>2</sub></a>) at 520 K to give <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_fluoride" title="Cobalt(III) fluoride">CoF<sub>3</sub></a>; with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl<sub>2</sub></a>), <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br<sub>2</sub></a>) and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I<sub>2</sub></a>), producing equivalent binary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halides" title="Halides">halides</a>. It does not react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen gas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>2</sub></a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_gas" title="Nitrogen gas">nitrogen gas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">N<sub>2</sub></a>) even when heated, but it does react with <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and sulfur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> At ordinary temperatures, it reacts slowly with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_acids" title="Mineral acids">mineral acids</a>, and very slowly with moist, but not with dry, air.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Cobalt_compounds" title="Category:Cobalt compounds">Category:Cobalt compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation_states" title="Oxidation states">oxidation states</a> of cobalt include +2 and +3, although compounds with oxidation states ranging from −3 to <a href="/wiki/Percobaltate" title="Percobaltate">+5</a> are also known. A common oxidation state for simple compounds is +2 (cobalt(II)).  These salts form the pink-colored <a href="/wiki/Metal_aquo_complex" title="Metal aquo complex">metal aquo complex</a> [Co(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> in water. Addition of chloride gives the intensely blue <span class="chemf nowrap">[CoCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> In a borax bead <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a>, cobalt shows deep blue in both oxidizing and reducing flames.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_and_chalcogen_compounds">Oxygen and chalcogen compounds</span></h3>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> of cobalt are known. Green <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II) oxide">cobalt(II) oxide</a> (CoO) has <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">rocksalt</a> structure. It is readily oxidized with water and oxygen to brown cobalt(III) hydroxide (Co(OH)<sub>3</sub>). At temperatures of 600–700 °C, CoO oxidizes to the blue <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II,III)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II,III) oxide">cobalt(II,III) oxide</a> (Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), which has a <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">spinel</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-3">[3]</a></sup> Black <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_oxide" title="Cobalt(III) oxide">cobalt(III) oxide</a> (Co<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is also known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Cobalt oxides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> at low <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>: CoO (<a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9el_temperature" title="Néel temperature">Néel temperature</a> 291 K) and Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (Néel temperature: 40 K), which is analogous to <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), with a mixture of +2 and +3 oxidation states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The principal <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogenides</a> of cobalt include the black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_sulfide" title="Cobalt(II) sulfide">cobalt(II) sulfides</a>, CoS<sub>2</sub>, which adopts a <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>-like structure, and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_sulfide" title="Cobalt sulfide">cobalt(III) sulfide</a> (Co<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halides">Halides</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt(II)-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg"><img alt="purple pile of power of Cobalt(II)-chloride-hexahydrate" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1474" data-file-width="2146" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg/220px-Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg/330px-Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg/440px-Cobalt%28II%29-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt(II)-chloride-hexahydrate-sample.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt(II) chloride hexahydrate</div></div></div>
<p>Four <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dihalide" title="wikt:dihalide">dihalides</a> of cobalt(II) are known: <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_fluoride" title="Cobalt(II) fluoride">cobalt(II) fluoride</a> (CoF<sub>2</sub>, pink), <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_chloride" title="Cobalt(II) chloride">cobalt(II) chloride</a> (CoCl<sub>2</sub>, blue), <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_bromide" title="Cobalt(II) bromide">cobalt(II) bromide</a> (CoBr<sub>2</sub>, green), <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_iodide" title="Cobalt(II) iodide">cobalt(II) iodide</a> (CoI<sub>2</sub>, blue-black). These halides exist in anhydrous and hydrated forms. Whereas the anhydrous dichloride is blue, the hydrate is red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenwood2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood2-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduction potential for the reaction <span class="chemf nowrap">Co<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + e<sup>−</sup> → <span class="chemf nowrap">Co<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> is +1.92 V, beyond that for <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> to chloride, +1.36 V. Consequently, cobalt(III) and chloride would result in the cobalt(III) being reduced to cobalt(II). Because the reduction potential for fluorine to fluoride is so high, +2.87 V, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_fluoride" title="Cobalt(III) fluoride">cobalt(III) fluoride</a> is one of the few simple stable cobalt(III) compounds. Cobalt(III) fluoride, which is used in some fluorination reactions, reacts vigorously with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_compounds">Coordination compounds</span></h3>
<p>As for all metals, molecular compounds and polyatomic ions of cobalt are classified as <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a>, that is, molecules or ions that contain cobalt linked to several <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>. The principles of <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">hardness–softness</a> of a series of ligands can be used to explain the usual oxidation state of cobalt. For example, Co<sup>+3</sup> complexes tend to have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammine" title="Ammine">ammine</a> ligands. Because phosphorus is softer than nitrogen, phosphine ligands tend to feature the <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">softer</a> Co<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>+</sup>, an example being tris(triphenylphosphine)cobalt(I) chloride ((P(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>CoCl). The more electronegative (and harder) oxide and fluoride can stabilize Co<sup>4+</sup> and Co<sup>5+</sup> derivatives, e.g. caesium hexafluorocobaltate (Cs<sub>2</sub>CoF<sub>6</sub>) and potassium <a href="/wiki/Percobaltate" title="Percobaltate">percobaltate</a> (K<sub>3</sub>CoO<sub>4</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Werner" title="Alfred Werner">Alfred Werner</a>, a Nobel-prize winning pioneer in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordination chemistry</a>, worked with compounds of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> [Co(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>]Cl<sub>3</sub>. One of the isomers determined was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_hexammine_chloride" title="Cobalt(III) hexammine chloride">cobalt(III) hexammine chloride</a>. This coordination complex, a typical Werner-type complex, consists of a central cobalt atom coordinated by six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammine" title="Ammine">ammine</a> orthogonal ligands and three <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> counteranions. Using <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelating</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethylenediamine" title="Ethylenediamine">ethylenediamine</a> ligands in place of ammonia gives <a href="/wiki/Tris(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III)_chloride" title="Tris(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III) chloride">tris(ethylenediamine)cobalt(III) chloride</a> ([Co(en)<sub>3</sub>]Cl<sub>3</sub>), which was one of the first <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a> to be resolved into <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">optical isomers</a>. The complex exists in the right- and left-handed forms of a "three-bladed propeller". This complex was first isolated by Werner as yellow-gold needle-like crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organometallic_compounds">Organometallic compounds</span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Cobaltocene" title="Cobaltocene">Cobaltocene</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Structural_analog" title="Structural analog">structural analog</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ferrocene" title="Ferrocene">ferrocene</a>, with cobalt in place of iron. Cobaltocene is much more sensitive to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> than ferrocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Cobalt carbonyl (<a href="/wiki/Dicobalt_octacarbonyl" title="Dicobalt octacarbonyl">Co<sub>2</sub>(CO)<sub>8</sub></a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carbonylation" title="Carbonylation">carbonylation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrosilylation" title="Hydrosilylation">hydrosilylation</a> reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Vitamin B<sub>12</sub> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bush_sickness" title="Bush sickness">below</a>) is an organometallic compound found in nature and is the only <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamin</a> that contains a metal atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> An example of an alkylcobalt complex in the otherwise uncommon +4 oxidation state of cobalt is the homoleptic complex <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrakis(1-norbornyl)cobalt(IV)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tetrakis(1-norbornyl)cobalt(IV) (page does not exist)">tetrakis(1-norbornyl)cobalt(IV)</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cobalt" title="Isotopes of cobalt">Isotopes of cobalt</a></div>
<p><sup>59</sup>Co is the only stable cobalt <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> and the only <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> that exists naturally on Earth. Twenty-two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been characterized; the most stable, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60"><sup>60</sup>Co</a> has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 5.2714 years, and <sup>57</sup>Co has a half-life of 271.8 days, <sup>56</sup>Co a half-life of 77.27 days, and <sup>58</sup>Co a half-life of 70.86 days. All the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes of cobalt have half-lives shorter than 18 hours, and in most cases shorter than 1 second. This element also has 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, all of which have half-lives shorter than 15 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The isotopes of cobalt range in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> from 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> (<sup>50</sup>Co) to 73 u (<sup>73</sup>Co). The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decay_mode" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> for isotopes with atomic mass unit values less than that of the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>59</sup>Co, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> and the primary mode of decay in isotopes with atomic mass greater than 59 atomic mass units is <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_product" title="Decay product">decay products</a> below <sup>59</sup>Co are element 26 (<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>) isotopes; above that the decay products are element 28 (nickel) isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg"><img alt="cobalt blue Chinese porcelain" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2735" data-file-width="2206" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg/170px-Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg/255px-Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg/340px-Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Early_blue_and_white_ware_circa_1335_Jingdezhen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Early Chinese blue and white porcelain, manufactured c. 1335</div></div></div>
<p>Cobalt compounds have been used for centuries to impart a rich blue color to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">glazes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramics_(art)" title="Ceramics (art)">ceramics</a>. Cobalt has been detected in Egyptian sculpture, Persian jewelry from the third millennium BC, in the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, destroyed in 79 AD, and in China, dating from the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618–907 AD) and the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368–1644 AD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt has been used to color glass since the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>. The excavation of the <a href="/wiki/Uluburun_shipwreck" title="Uluburun shipwreck">Uluburun shipwreck</a> yielded an ingot of blue glass, cast during the 14th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pulak_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pulak-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Blue glass from Egypt was either colored with copper, iron, or cobalt. The oldest cobalt-colored glass is from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eighteenth_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth dynasty of Egypt">Eighteenth dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> (1550–1292 BC). The source for the cobalt the Egyptians used is not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <i>cobalt</i> is derived from the German <i><i lang="mhd" title="Mbugu language text">kobalt</i></i>, from <i><a href="/wiki/Kobold" title="Kobold">kobold</a></i> meaning "goblin", a superstitious term used for the <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> of cobalt by miners. The first attempts to smelt those ores for copper or nickel failed, yielding simply powder (cobalt(II) oxide) instead. Because the primary ores of cobalt always contain arsenic, smelting the ore oxidized the arsenic into the highly toxic and volatile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsenic(III)_oxide" title="Arsenic(III) oxide">arsenic oxide</a>, adding to the notoriety of the ore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met1863_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-met1863-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Georg_Brandt" title="Georg Brandt">Georg Brandt</a> (1694–1768) is credited with discovering cobalt circa 1735, showing it to be a previously unknown element, different from bismuth and other traditional metals. Brandt called it a new "semi-metal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-32">[32]</a></sup> He showed that compounds of cobalt metal were the source of the blue color in glass, which previously had been attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> found with cobalt. Cobalt became the first metal to be discovered since the pre-historical period. All other known metals (iron, copper, silver, gold, zinc, mercury, tin, lead and bismuth) had no recorded discoverers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weeks_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weeks-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 19th century, a significant part of the world's production of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt blue</a> (a dye made with cobalt compounds and alumina) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smalt" title="Smalt">smalt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cobalt_glass" title="Cobalt glass">cobalt glass</a> powdered for use for pigment purposes in ceramics and painting) was carried out at the Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Blaafarvev%C3%A6rket" title="Blaafarveværket">Blaafarveværket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The first mines for the production of smalt in the 16th century were located in Norway, Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> and Hungary. With the discovery of cobalt ore in <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> in 1864, the mining of cobalt in Europe declined. With the discovery of ore deposits in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada in 1904 and the discovery of even larger deposits in the <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Katanga Province</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DR_Congo" title="DR Congo">Congo</a> in 1914, the mining operations shifted again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met1863_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-met1863-30">[30]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Shaba_II" title="Shaba II">Shaba conflict</a> started in 1978, the copper mines of Katanga Province nearly stopped production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSnonfuel_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSnonfuel-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glres_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-glres-37">[37]</a></sup> The impact on the world cobalt economy from this conflict was smaller than expected: cobalt is a rare metal, the pigment is highly toxic, and the industry had already established effective ways for recycling cobalt materials. In some cases, industry was able to change to cobalt-free alternatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSnonfuel_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGSnonfuel-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glres_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-glres-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938, John Livingood and <a href="/wiki/Glenn_T._Seaborg" title="Glenn T. Seaborg">Glenn T. Seaborg</a> discovered the radioisotope <a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">cobalt-60</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> This isotope was famously used at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in the 1950s to establish <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">parity</a> violation in radioactive <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>After World War II, the US wanted to guarantee the supply of cobalt ore for military uses (as the Germans had been doing) and prospected for cobalt within the U.S. border. An adequate supply of the ore was found in Idaho near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbird_Mine" title="Blackbird Mine">Blackbird canyon</a> in the side of a mountain. The firm Calera Mining Company started production at the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span></h2>
<p>The stable form of cobalt is produced in <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovas</a> through the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It comprises <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">0.0029% of the Earth's crust</a>. Free cobalt (the <a href="/wiki/Native_metal" title="Native metal">native metal</a>) is not found on Earth because of the oxygen in the atmosphere and the chlorine in the ocean. Both are abundant enough in the upper layers of the Earth's crust to prevent native metal cobalt from forming. Except as recently delivered in meteoric iron, pure cobalt in native metal form is unknown on Earth. The element has a medium abundance but natural compounds of cobalt are numerous and all amounts of cobalt compounds are found in most rocks, soil, plants, and animals.
</p><p>In nature, cobalt is frequently associated with <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>. Both are characteristic components of <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">meteoric iron</a>, though cobalt is much less abundant in iron meteorites than nickel. As with nickel, cobalt in meteoric iron <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> may have been well enough protected from oxygen and moisture to remain as the free (but alloyed) metal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> though neither element is seen in that form in the ancient terrestrial crust.
</p><p>Cobalt in compound form occurs in copper and nickel minerals. It is the major metallic component that combines with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and arsenic in the sulfidic <a href="/wiki/Cobaltite" title="Cobaltite">cobaltite</a> (CoAsS), <a href="/wiki/Safflorite" title="Safflorite">safflorite</a> (CoAs<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Glaucodot" title="Glaucodot">glaucodot</a> ((Co,Fe)AsS), and <a href="/wiki/Skutterudite" title="Skutterudite">skutterudite</a> (CoAs<sub>3</sub>) minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-11">[11]</a></sup> The mineral <a href="/wiki/Cattierite" title="Cattierite">cattierite</a> is similar to <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> and occurs together with <a href="/wiki/Vaesite" title="Vaesite">vaesite</a> in the copper deposits of <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Katanga Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> When it reaches the atmosphere, <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> occurs; the sulfide minerals oxidize and form pink <a href="/wiki/Erythrite" title="Erythrite">erythrite</a> ("cobalt glance": <a href="/wiki/Erythrite" title="Erythrite">Co<sub>3</sub>(AsO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·8H<sub>2</sub>O</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Spherocobaltite" title="Spherocobaltite">spherocobaltite</a> (CoCO<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt is also a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalhoutSchulz2011_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-47">[47]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tobacco_plant" title="Tobacco plant">tobacco plant</a> readily absorbs and accumulates <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> like cobalt from the surrounding soil in its leaves. These are subsequently inhaled during <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco smoking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg"><img alt="cobolt ore specimen" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="432" data-file-width="300" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg/170px-Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg/255px-Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/80/Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_OreUSGOV.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt ore</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg"><img alt="cobolt production in 1000 of tons by year" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="750" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg/220px-Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg/330px-Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg/440px-Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_-_world_production_trend.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World production trend</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_extraction_techniques" title="Cobalt extraction techniques">Cobalt extraction techniques</a></div>
<p>The main ores of cobalt are <a href="/wiki/Cobaltite" title="Cobaltite">cobaltite</a>, erythrite, <a href="/wiki/Glaucodot" title="Glaucodot">glaucodot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skutterudite" title="Skutterudite">skutterudite</a> (see above), but most cobalt is obtained by reducing the cobalt <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a> of nickel and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> mining and <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB2006_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-YB2006-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR2008_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-CR2008-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since cobalt is generally produced as a by-product, the supply of cobalt depends to a great extent on the economic feasibility of copper and  nickel mining in a given market. Demand for cobalt was projected to grow 6% in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ft_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ft-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several methods exist to separate cobalt from copper and nickel, depending on the concentration of cobalt and the exact composition of the used <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a>. One method is <a href="/wiki/Froth_flotation" title="Froth flotation">froth flotation</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a> bind to different ore components, leading to an enrichment of cobalt ores. Subsequent <a href="/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy)" title="Roasting (metallurgy)">roasting</a> converts the ores to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_sulfate" title="Cobalt sulfate">cobalt sulfate</a>, and the copper and the iron are oxidized to the oxide. <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(metallurgy)" title="Leaching (metallurgy)">Leaching</a> with water extracts the sulfate together with the <a href="/wiki/Arsenate" title="Arsenate">arsenates</a>. The residues are further leached with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, yielding a solution of copper sulfate. Cobalt can also be leached from the <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a> of copper smelting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>The products of the above-mentioned processes are transformed into the cobalt oxide (Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>). This oxide is reduced to metal by the <a href="/wiki/Aluminothermic_reaction" title="Aluminothermic reaction">aluminothermic reaction</a> or reduction with carbon in a <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollemanAF_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-HollemanAF-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cobalt_extraction">Cobalt extraction</span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> estimates world reserves of cobalt at 7,100,000 metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGSJan2016_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGSJan2016-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> (DRC) currently produces 63% of the world’s cobalt. This market share may reach 73% by 2025 if planned expansions by mining producers like <a href="/wiki/Glencore" title="Glencore">Glencore</a> Plc take place as expected. But by 2030, global demand could be 47 times more than it was in 2017, Bloomberg New Energy Finance has estimated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Changes that Congo made to mining laws in 2002 led did attract investment in Congolese copper and cobalt projects. However Glencore dominates the coltan market in DRC. Its Mutanda mine shipped 24,500 tons of cobalt from its Mutanda mine last year, 40% of Congo DRC’s output and nearly a quarter of global production. T Glencore’s Katanga Mining project is resuming as well and should produce 300,000 tons of copper and 20,000 tons of cobalt by 2019, according to Glencore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ft_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ft-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conflict_minerals" title="Conflict minerals">Conflict minerals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">Child labor</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strip_mining" title="Strip mining">strip mining</a></div>
<p>In 2005, the top producer of cobalt was the copper deposits in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Katanga Province</a>. Formerly Shaba province, the area had almost 40% of global reserves, reported the <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> By 2015, DRC supplied 60% of world cobalt production, 32,000 tons at $20,000 to $26,000 per ton. Recent growth in production could at least partly be due to how low mining production fell during DRC Congo's very violent civil wars in the early 2000s, or to the changes the country made to its Mining Code in 2002 to encourage foreign and multinational investment and which did bring in a number of investors, including <a href="/wiki/Glencore" title="Glencore">Glencore</a>. Three of its current Congo projects 
<a href="/wiki/Artisanal_mining" title="Artisanal mining">Artisanal mining</a> supplied 10% to 25% of the DRC production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpDRC1_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-wpDRC1-56">[56]</a></sup> Some 100,000 cobalt miners in Congo DRC use hand tools to dig hundreds of feet, with little planning and fewer safety measures, say workers and government and NGO officials, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a> reporters' observations on visits to isolated mines. The lack of safety precautions frequently causes injuries or death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Mining pollutes the vicinity and exposes local wildlife and indigenous communities to toxic metals thought to cause birth defects and breathing difficulties, according to health officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Human rights activists have alleged, and <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalism" title="Investigative journalism">investigative journalism</a> reported confirmation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_labor" title="Child labor">child labor</a> is used in mining cobalt from African <a href="/wiki/Artisanal_mining" title="Artisanal mining">artisanal mines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wpDRC1_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-wpDRC1-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> This revelation prompted cell phone maker <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>, on March 3, 2017, to stop buying ore from suppliers such as <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang_Huayou_Cobalt" title="Zhejiang Huayou Cobalt">Zhejiang Huayou Cobalt</a> who source from artisanal mines in the DRC, and begin using only suppliers that are verified to meet its workplace standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The political and ethnic dynamics of the region have in the past caused horrific outbreaks of violence and years of armed conflict and displaced populations. This instability affected the price of cobalt and also created perverse incentives for the combattants in the First and Second Congo Wars to prolong the fighting, since access to diamond mines and other valuable resources helped to finance their military goals—which frequently amounteed to genocide—and also enriched the fighters themselves. While DR Congo has in the 2010s not recently been invaded by neighboring military forces, some of the richest mineral deposits adjoin areas where Tutsis and Hutus still frequently clash, unrest continues although on a smaller scale and refugees still flee outbreaks of violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt extracted from small Congolese <a href="/wiki/Artisanal_mining" title="Artisanal mining">artisanal mining</a> endeavors in 2007 supplied a single Chinese company, Congo DongFang International Mining. A subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang_Huayou_Cobalt" title="Zhejiang Huayou Cobalt">Zhejiang Huayou Cobalt</a>, one of the world’s largest cobalt producers, Congo DongFang supplied cobalt to some of the world’s largest battery manufacturers, who produced batteries for ubiquitous products like the Apple <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhones</a>. Corporate pieties about an ethical <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> were thus met with some incredulity. A number of observers have called for tech corporations and other manufacturers to avoid sourcing conflict metals in Central Africa at all rather than risk enabling the financial exploitation, <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> abuses like kidnappings for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unfree_labor" title="Unfree labor">unfree labor</a>, environmental devastation and the human toll of violence, poverty and toxic conditions.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mukondo_Mountain" title="Mukondo Mountain">Mukondo Mountain</a> project, operated by the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Mining_and_Exploration_Company" title="Central African Mining and Exploration Company">Central African Mining and Exploration Company</a> (CAMEC) in <a href="/wiki/Katanga_Province" title="Katanga Province">Katanga Province</a>, may be the richest cobalt reserve in the world. It produced an estimated one third of the total global coval production in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IntMining200807_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-IntMining200807-65">[65]</a></sup> In July 2009, CAMEC announced a long-term agreement to deliver its entire annual <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> of cobalt concentrate from Mukondo Mountain to Zhejiang Galico Cobalt &amp; Nickel Materials of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 9, 2018, " President <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kabila" title="Joseph Kabila">Joseph Kabila</a> updated the 2002 mining code, increasing royalty charges and declaring cobalt and <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">coltan</a> "strategic metals." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span></h3>
<p>In 2017, some exploration companies were planning to survey old silver and cobalt mines in the area of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt,_Ontario" title="Cobalt, Ontario">Cobalt, Ontario</a> where significant deposits are believed to lie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The mayor of Cobalt stated that the people of Cobalt welcomed new mining endeavours and pointed out that the local work force is peaceful and English-speaking, and good infrastructure would allow much easier sourcing of spare parts for the equipment or other supplies than were to be found in conflict-zones.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<p>Cobalt has been used in production of high-performance alloys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB2006_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-YB2006-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR2008_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-CR2008-50">[50]</a></sup> It can also be used to make rechargeable batteries, and the advent of electric vehicles and their success with consumers probably has a great deal to do with the DRC's soaring production. Other important factors were the 2002 Mining Code, which encouraged investment by foreign and transnational corporations such as Glencore, and the end of the First and Second Congo Wars.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span></h3>
<p>Cobalt-based <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">superalloys</a> have historically consumed most of the cobalt produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YB2006_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-YB2006-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CR2008_50-2"><a href="#cite_note-CR2008-50">[50]</a></sup> The temperature stability of these alloys makes them suitable for turbine blades for <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a> and aircraft <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>, although nickel-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-crystal" title="Single-crystal">single-crystal</a> alloys surpass them in performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-super_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-super-70">[70]</a></sup> Cobalt-based alloys are also <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>- and wear-resistant, making them, like <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, useful for making orthopedic <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">implants</a> that don't wear down over time. The development of wear-resistant cobalt alloys started in the first decade of the 20th century with the <a href="/wiki/Stellite" title="Stellite">stellite</a> alloys, containing chromium with varying quantities of tungsten and carbon. Alloys with <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbides</a> are very hard and wear-resistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Special cobalt-chromium-<a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> alloys like <a href="/wiki/Vitallium" title="Vitallium">Vitallium</a> are used for <a href="/wiki/Prosthesis" title="Prosthesis">prosthetic</a> parts (hip and knee replacements).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Cobalt alloys are also used for <a href="/wiki/Dental_implant" title="Dental implant">dental</a> prosthetics as a useful substitute for nickel, which may be allergenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/High-speed_steel" title="High-speed steel">high-speed steels</a> also contain cobalt for increased heat and wear resistance. The special alloys of aluminium, nickel, cobalt and iron, known as <a href="/wiki/Alnico" title="Alnico">Alnico</a>, and of samarium and cobalt (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samarium-cobalt_magnet" title="Samarium-cobalt magnet">samarium-cobalt magnet</a>) are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permanent_magnets" title="Permanent magnets">permanent magnets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alnico_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alnico-74">[74]</a></sup> It is also alloyed with 95% <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> for jewelry, yielding an alloy suitable for fine casting, which is also slightly magnetic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batteries">Batteries</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lithium_cobalt_oxide" title="Lithium cobalt oxide">Lithium cobalt oxide</a> (LiCoO<sub>2</sub>) is widely used in <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion battery</a> cathodes. The material is composed of cobalt oxide layers with the lithium <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(chemistry)" title="Intercalation (chemistry)">intercalated</a>. During discharge,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the lithium is released as lithium ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhyCo_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-WhyCo-76">[76]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium_battery" title="Nickel-cadmium battery">Nickel-cadmium</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bat1_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-bat1-77">[77]</a></sup> (NiCd) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_metal_hydride_battery" title="Nickel metal hydride battery">nickel metal hydride</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> (NiMH) batteries also include cobalt to improve the oxidation of nickel in the battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bat1_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-bat1-77">[77]</a></sup> 
Transparency Market Research estimated the global lithium-ion battery market at $30 billion in 2015 and predicted an increase to over US$75 billion by 2024.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although in 2018 most cobalt in batteries was used in a mobile device,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> a more recent application for cobalt is rechargeable batteries for electric cars. This industry has increased five-fold in its demand for cobalt, which makes it urgent to find new raw materials in more stable areas of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cleantechnica1_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-cleantechnica1-81">[81]</a></sup> Demand is expected to continue or increase as the prevalence of electric vehicles increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Exploration in 2016–2017 included the area around <a href="/wiki/Cobalt,_Ontario" title="Cobalt, Ontario">Cobalt, Ontario</a>, an area where many silver mines ceased operation decades ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cleantechnica1_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-cleantechnica1-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since child and slave labor have been repeatedly reported in coltan mining, primarily in the artisanal mines of DR Congo, tech companies seeking an ethical supply chain have faced shortages of this raw material and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> the price of cobalt metal reached a nine-year high in October 2017, more than US$30 a pound, versus US$10 in late 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalysts">Catalysts</span></h3>
<p>Several cobalt compounds are oxidation catalysts. Cobalt acetate is used to convert <a href="/wiki/Xylene" title="Xylene">xylene</a> to <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalic acid</a>, the precursor of the bulk polymer <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a>. Typical catalysts are the cobalt <a href="/wiki/Carboxylate" title="Carboxylate">carboxylates</a> (known as cobalt soaps). They are also used in paints, varnishes, and inks as "drying agents" through the oxidation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drying_oils" title="Drying oils">drying oils</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhyCo_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-WhyCo-76">[76]</a></sup> The same carboxylates are used to improve the adhesion between steel and rubber in steel-belted radial tires. In addition they are used as accelerators in <a href="/wiki/Polyester_resin" title="Polyester resin">polyester resin</a> systems.
</p><p>Cobalt-based catalysts are used in reactions involving <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>. Cobalt is also a catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of carbon monoxide into liquid fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">Hydroformylation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> often uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_octacarbonyl" title="Cobalt octacarbonyl">cobalt octacarbonyl</a> as a catalyst,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> although it is often replaced by more efficient iridium- and rhodium-based catalysts, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Cativa_process" title="Cativa process">Cativa process</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">hydrodesulfurization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> uses a catalyst derived from cobalt and molybdenum. This process helps to clean petroleum of sulfur impurities that interfere with the refining of liquid fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhyCo_76-2"><a href="#cite_note-WhyCo-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigments_and_coloring">Pigments and coloring</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg"><img alt="shelf with blue glass vessels" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="750" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg/220px-Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg/330px-Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg/440px-Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bristol.blue.glass.arp.750pix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt blue glass</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg"><img alt="blue glass bottle with neck" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1071" data-file-width="512" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg/150px-Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg/225px-Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg/300px-Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_blue_flask.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt-colored glass</div></div></div>
<p>Before the 19th century, cobalt was predominantly used as a pigment. It has been used since the Middle Ages to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smalt" title="Smalt">smalt</a>, a blue-colored glass. Smalt is produced by melting a mixture of roasted mineral <a href="/wiki/Smaltite" title="Smaltite">smaltite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a>, which yields a dark blue silicate glass, which is finely ground after the production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Smalt was widely used to color glass and as pigment for paintings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> In 1780, <a href="/wiki/Sven_Rinman" title="Sven Rinman">Sven Rinman</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_green" title="Cobalt green">cobalt green</a>, and in 1802 <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jacques_Th%C3%A9nard" title="Louis Jacques Thénard">Louis Jacques Thénard</a> discovered cobalt blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Cobalt pigments such as <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_blue" title="Cobalt blue">cobalt blue</a> (cobalt aluminate), <a href="/wiki/Cerulean" title="Cerulean">cerulean</a> blue (cobalt(II) stannate), various hues of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_green" title="Cobalt green">cobalt green</a> (a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II) oxide">cobalt(II) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>), and cobalt violet (<a href="/wiki/Cobalt_phosphate" title="Cobalt phosphate">cobalt phosphate</a>) are used as artist's pigments because of their superior chromatic stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venetskii_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venetskii-91">[91]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aureolin" title="Aureolin">Aureolin</a> (cobalt yellow) is now largely replaced by more lightfast<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> yellow pigments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioisotopes">Radioisotopes</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cobalt-60" title="Cobalt-60">Cobalt-60</a> (Co-60 or <sup>60</sup>Co) is useful as a gamma-ray source because they can be produced in predictable quantity and high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_(radioactivity)" title="Activity (radioactivity)">activity</a> by bombarding cobalt with <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>. It produces <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> with energies of 1.17 and 1.33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MeV" title="MeV">MeV</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nubase_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt is used in <a href="/wiki/External_beam_radiotherapy" title="External beam radiotherapy">external beam radiotherapy</a>, sterilization of medical supplies and medical waste, radiation treatment of <a href="/wiki/Food_irradiation" title="Food irradiation">foods for sterilization</a> (cold <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">pasteurization</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Industrial_radiography" title="Industrial radiography">industrial radiography</a> (e.g. weld integrity radiographs), density measurements (e.g. concrete density measurements), and tank fill height switches. The metal has the unfortunate property of producing a fine dust, causing problems with <a href="/wiki/Radiation_protection" title="Radiation protection">radiation protection</a>. Cobalt from radiotherapy machines has been a serious hazard when not discarded properly, and one of the worst radiation contamination accidents in North America occurred in 1984, when a discarded radiotherapy unit containing cobalt-60 was mistakenly disassembled in a junkyard in Juarez, Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt-60 has a radioactive half-life of 5.27 years. Loss of potency requires periodic replacement of the source in radiotherapy and is one reason why cobalt machines have been largely replaced by <a href="/wiki/Linear_particle_accelerator" title="Linear particle accelerator">linear accelerators</a> in modern radiation therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_cobalt" title="Isotopes of cobalt">Cobalt-57</a> (Co-57 or <sup>57</sup>Co) is a cobalt radioisotope most often used in medical tests, as a radiolabel for vitamin B<sub><small>12</small></sub> uptake, and for the <a href="/wiki/Schilling_test" title="Schilling test">Schilling test</a>. Cobalt-57 is used as a source in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectroscopy" title="Mössbauer spectroscopy">Mössbauer spectroscopy</a> and is one of several possible sources in <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">X-ray fluorescence</a> devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_design" title="Nuclear weapon design">Nuclear weapon designs</a> could intentionally incorporate <sup>59</sup>Co, some of which would be activated in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_explosion" title="Nuclear explosion">nuclear explosion</a> to produce <sup>60</sup>Co. The <sup>60</sup>Co, dispersed as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear fallout</a>, is sometimes called a <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_bomb" title="Cobalt bomb">cobalt bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h3>
<ul><li>Cobalt is used in <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> for its attractive appearance, hardness, and resistance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></li>
<li>It is also used as a base primer coat for <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalamin.svg"><img alt="chemical diagram of cobalamin molecule" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="751" data-file-width="501" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Cobalamin.svg/170px-Cobalamin.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Cobalamin.svg/255px-Cobalamin.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Cobalamin.svg/340px-Cobalamin.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalamin.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalamin" title="Cobalamin">Cobalamin</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg"><img alt="two cobalt-deficient sheep facing away from camera" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2657" data-file-width="2283" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg/170px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg/255px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg/340px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_10487_Cobalt_deficient_sheep.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt-deficient sheep</div></div></div>
<p>Cobalt is essential to the metabolism of all <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. It is a key constituent of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">cobalamin</a>, also known as vitamin B<sub>12</sub>, the primary biological reservoir of cobalt as an <a href="/wiki/Ultratrace_element" title="Ultratrace element">ultratrace element</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a> in the stomachs of <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminant</a> animals convert cobalt salts into vitamin B<sub>12</sub>, a compound which can only be produced by bacteria or <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. A minimal presence of cobalt in soils therefore markedly improves the health of <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> animals, and an uptake of 0.20 mg/kg a day is recommended because they have no other source of vitamin B<sub>12</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 20th century, during the development of farming on the <a href="/wiki/North_Island_Volcanic_Plateau" title="North Island Volcanic Plateau">North Island Volcanic Plateau</a> of New Zealand, cattle suffered from what was termed "bush sickness". It was discovered that the volcanic soils lacked the cobalt salts essential for the cattle food chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDoewel_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDoewel-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "coast disease" of sheep in the <a href="/wiki/Ninety_Mile_Desert" title="Ninety Mile Desert">Ninety Mile Desert</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Limestone_Coast" title="Limestone Coast">Southeast</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> in the 1930s was found to originate in nutritional deficiencies of trace elements cobalt and copper. The cobalt deficiency was overcome by the development of "cobalt bullets", dense pellets of cobalt oxide mixed with clay given orally for lodging in the animal's <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDoewel_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-McDoewel-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>Proteins based on cobalamin use <a href="/wiki/Corrin" title="Corrin">corrin</a> to hold the cobalt. Coenzyme B<sub>12</sub> features a reactive C-Co bond that participates in the reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> In humans, B<sub>12</sub> has two types of <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>: <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl</a> and adenosyl. <a href="/wiki/Methylcobalamin" title="Methylcobalamin">MeB<sub>12</sub></a> promotes methyl (−CH<sub>3</sub>) group transfers. The adenosyl version of B<sub>12</sub> catalyzes rearrangements in which a hydrogen atom is directly transferred between two adjacent atoms with concomitant exchange of the second substituent, X, which may be a carbon atom with substituents, an oxygen atom of an alcohol, or an amine. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylmalonyl_coenzyme_A_mutase" title="Methylmalonyl coenzyme A mutase">Methylmalonyl coenzyme A mutase</a> (MUT) converts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-methylmalonyl-CoA" title="L-methylmalonyl-CoA">MMl-CoA</a> to <a href="/wiki/Succinyl-CoA" title="Succinyl-CoA">Su-CoA</a>, an important step in the extraction of energy from proteins and fats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although far less common than other <a href="/wiki/Metalloprotein" title="Metalloprotein">metalloproteins</a> (e.g. those of zinc and iron), other cobaltoproteins are known besides B<sub>12</sub>. These proteins include <a href="/wiki/METAP2" title="METAP2">methionine aminopeptidase 2</a>, an enzyme that occurs in humans and other mammals that does not use the corrin ring of B<sub>12</sub>, but binds cobalt directly. Another non-corrin cobalt enzyme is <a href="/wiki/Nitrile_hydratase" title="Nitrile hydratase">nitrile hydratase</a>, an enzyme in bacteria that metabolizes <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitriles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Cobalt is an essential element for life in minute amounts. The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub>50</sub></a> value for soluble cobalt salts has been estimated to be between 150 and 500 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-112">[112]</a></sup> In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has designated a <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> (PEL) in the workplace as a time-weighted average (TWA) of 0.1 mg/m<sup>3</sup>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 0.05 mg/m<sup>3</sup>, time-weighted average. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDLH" title="IDLH">IDLH</a> (immediately dangerous to life and health) value is 20 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, chronic cobalt ingestion has caused serious health problems at doses far less than the lethal dose. In 1966, the addition of cobalt compounds to stabilize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beer_foam" title="Beer foam">beer foam</a> in Canada led to a peculiar form of toxin-induced <a href="/wiki/Cardiomyopathy" title="Cardiomyopathy">cardiomyopathy</a>, which came to be known as <i>beer drinker's cardiomyopathy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>It causes respiratory problems when inhaled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> It also causes skin problems when touched; after <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and chromium, cobalt is a major cause of <a href="/wiki/Allergic_contact_dermatitis" title="Allergic contact dermatitis">contact dermatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> These risks are faced by cobalt miners.
</p><p>Cobalt can be effectively absorbed by charred pigs' bones; however, this process is inhibited by copper and zinc, which have greater affinities to bone char.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
