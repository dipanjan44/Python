<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Abrupt climate change</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">Runaway climate change</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="745" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/320px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/480px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/640px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">Clathrate hydrates</a> have been identified as a possible agent for abrupt changes.</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>abrupt climate change</b> occurs when the climate system is forced to transition to a new <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate state</a> at a rate that is determined by the climate system <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_radiation_balance" title="Earth's radiation balance">energy-balance</a>, and which is more rapid than the rate of change of the external forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-def2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-def2-1">[1]</a></sup> Past events include the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Rainforest_Collapse" title="Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse">Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broecker_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broecker-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dansgaard-Oeschger_event" title="Dansgaard-Oeschger event">Dansgaard-Oeschger events</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">Heinrich events</a> and possibly also the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term is also used within the context of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to describe sudden climate change that is detectable over the time-scale of a human lifetime. One proposed cause of such events is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loops</a> within the climate system both enhance small perturbations and cause a variety of stable states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Timescales of events described as 'abrupt' may vary dramatically.  Changes recorded in the climate of Greenland at the end of the Younger Dryas, as measured by ice-cores, imply a sudden warming of +10 °C (+18 °F) within a timescale of a few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Other abrupt changes are the +4 °C (+7.2 °F) on Greenland 11,270 years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or the abrupt +6 °C (11 °F) warming 22,000 years ago on <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By contrast, the Paleocene-Eocene thermal maximum may have initiated anywhere between a few decades and several thousand years. Finally, Earth Systems models project that under ongoing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions as early as 2047, the Earth's near surface temperature could depart from the range of variability in the last 150 years, affecting over 3 billion people and most places of great species diversity on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the Committee on Abrupt Climate Change of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-def2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-def2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>There are essentially two definitions of abrupt climate change:
</p>
<ul><li>In terms of physics, it is a <i>transition of the climate system into a different mode on a time scale that is faster than the responsible forcing</i>.</li>
<li>In terms of impacts, <i>"an abrupt change is one that takes place so rapidly and unexpectedly that human or natural systems have difficulty adapting to it"</i>.</li></ul><p>
These definitions are complementary: the former gives some insight into how abrupt climate change comes about ; the latter explains why there is so much research devoted to it.</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Possible <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping elements in the climate system</a> include: <a href="/wiki/Regional_effects_of_global_warming" title="Regional effects of global warming">regional effects of global warming</a>, some of which had abrupt onset and may therefore be regarded as abrupt climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> Scientists have stated that "Our synthesis of present knowledge suggests that a variety of tipping elements could reach their critical point within this century under anthropogenic climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been postulated that teleconnections, oceanic and atmospheric processes, on different timescales, connect both hemispheres during abrupt climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report#Anthropogenic_warming_could_lead_to_some_impacts_that_are_abrupt_or_irreversible" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC states</a> that global warming "could lead to some effects that are abrupt or irreversible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Alley" title="Richard Alley">Richard Alley</a> et al. said "it is conceivable that human forcing of climate change is increasing the probability of large, abrupt events. Were such an event to recur, the economic and ecological impacts could be large and potentially serious."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alley2002_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alley2002-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2013 report from the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a> called for attention to the abrupt impacts of climate change, stating that even steady, gradual change in the physical climate system can have abrupt impacts elsewhere—in human infrastructure and ecosystems for example—if critical thresholds are crossed. The report emphasizes the need for an early warning system that could help society better anticipate sudden changes and emerging impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientific understanding of abrupt climate change is generally poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The probability of abrupt change for some climate related feedbacks may be low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccsp_abrupt_climate_change-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_large_scale_impacts_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_large_scale_impacts-18">[18]</a></sup> Factors that may increase the probability of abrupt climate change include higher magnitudes of global warming, warming that occurs more rapidly, and warming that is sustained over longer time periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_large_scale_impacts_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-tar_large_scale_impacts-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_models">Climate models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">Climate model</a></div>
<p>Climate models are unable yet to predict abrupt climate change events, or most of the past abrupt climate shifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayewski2016_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayewski2016-19">[19]</a></sup> A potential abrupt feedback due to <a href="/wiki/Thermokarst" title="Thermokarst">thermokarst</a> lake formations in the Arctic, in response to thawing permafrost soils, releasing additional greenhouse gas methane, is currently not accounted for in climate models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_precursor">Possible precursor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Possible precursor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most abrupt climate shifts, are likely due to sudden circulation shifts, analogous to a flood cutting a new river channel. The best-known examples are the several dozen shutdowns of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> during the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, affecting climate worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alley2002_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alley2002-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_Arctic" title="Climate change in the Arctic">current warming of the Arctic</a>, the duration of the summer season, is considered abrupt and massive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayewski2016_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mayewski2016-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Antarctic ozone depletion caused significant atmospheric circulation changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayewski2016_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mayewski2016-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>There have also been two occasions when the Atlantic's Meridional Overturning Circulation lost a crucial safety factor. The <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Sea" title="Greenland Sea">Greenland Sea</a> flushing at 75 °N shut down in 1978, recovering over the next decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  Then the second-largest flushing site, the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a>, shut down in 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> for ten years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  While shutdowns overlapping in time have not been seen during the fifty years of observation, previous total shutdowns had severe worldwide climate consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alley2002_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alley2002-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Blue paths represent deep-water currents, while red paths represent surface currents.</div></div></div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cold_blob_(North_Atlantic)" title="Cold blob (North Atlantic)">Cold blob (North Atlantic)</a></div>
<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> have established patterns of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a>. Several potential disruptions to this system of currents have been identified as a result of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>Increasing frequency of <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Potential disruption to the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, such as that which may have occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_doi|10.1038/378165a0_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_doi|10.1038/378165a0-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Changes to the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">North Atlantic oscillation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">Weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hansen et al. 2015 found, that the shutdown or substantial slowdown of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation" title="Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> (AMOC), besides possibly contributing to extreme end-<a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Eemian</a> events, will cause a more general increase of severe weather. Additional surface cooling from ice melt increases surface and lower tropospheric temperature gradients, and causes in model simulations a large increase of mid-latitude <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">eddy energy</a> throughout the midlatitude troposphere. This in turn leads to an increase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroclinicity" title="Baroclinicity">baroclinicity</a> produced by stronger temperature gradients, which provides energy for more severe weather events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequential_effects">Consequential effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Consequential effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Abrupt climate change has likely been the cause of wide ranging and severe effects:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_extinctions" title="Mass extinctions">Mass extinctions</a> in the past, most notably the Permian-Triassic Extinction event (often referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_dying" title="Great dying">great dying</a>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Rainforest_Collapse" title="Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse">Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse</a>, have been suggested as a consequence of abrupt climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Loss of biodiversity. Without interference from abrupt climate change and other extinction events the biodiversity of this planet would continue to grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Rapid <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-33">[33]</a></sup> which can harm marine life (such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_feedback_effects">Climate feedback effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate feedback effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NORTH_POLE_Ice_(19626661335).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4912" data-file-width="7360" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg/320px-NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg/480px-NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg/640px-NORTH_POLE_Ice_%2819626661335%29.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NORTH_POLE_Ice_(19626661335).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The dark ocean surface reflects only 6 percent of incoming solar radiation, instead sea ice reflects 50 to 70 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">Climate change feedback</a> and <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">Runaway climate change</a></div>
<p>One source of abrupt climate change effects is a <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> process, in which a warming event causes a change which leads to further warming.  This can also apply to cooling.  Example of such feedback processes are:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">Ice-albedo feedback</a>, where the advance or retreat of ice cover alters the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">'whiteness'</a> of the earth, and its ability to absorb the sun's energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon_feedback" title="Soil carbon feedback">Soil carbon feedback</a> concerns releases of carbon from soils, in response to global warming.</li>
<li>The dying and burning of forests, as a result of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanism">Volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isostatic_rebound" title="Isostatic rebound">Isostatic rebound</a> in response to glacier retreat (unloading), increased local salinity, have been attributed to increased volcanic activity at the onset of the abrupt <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B8lling-Aller%C3%B8d_warming" title="Bølling-Allerød warming">Bølling-Allerød warming</a>, are associated with the interval of intense volcanic activity, hinting at a interaction between climate and volcanism - enhanced short-term melting of glaciers, possibly via albedo changes from particle fallout on glacier surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_events">Past events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abrupt_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Past events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dryas_drummondii6.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Dryas_drummondii6.jpg/250px-Dryas_drummondii6.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Dryas_drummondii6.jpg/375px-Dryas_drummondii6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Dryas_drummondii6.jpg/500px-Dryas_drummondii6.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dryas_drummondii6.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> period of abrupt climate change is named after the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_plant" title="Alpine plant">Alpine</a> flower, <a href="/wiki/Dryas_(plant)" title="Dryas (plant)">Dryas</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Several periods of abrupt climate change have been identified in the <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatic</a> record.  Notable examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>About 25 climate shifts, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dansgaard-Oeschger_cycles" title="Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles">Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles</a>, which have been identified in the <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> record during the glacial period over the past 100,000 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> event, notably its sudden end. It is the most recent of the Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles and began 12,900 years ago and moved back into a warm-and-wet climate regime about 11,600 years ago.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  It has been suggested that: "The extreme rapidity of these changes in a variable that directly represents regional climate implies that the events at the end of the last glaciation may have been responses to some kind of threshold or trigger in the North Atlantic climate system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  A model for this event based on disruption to the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> has been supported by other studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_doi|10.1038/378165a0_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-cite_doi|10.1038/378165a0-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>, timed at 55 million years ago, which may have been caused by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_effect" title="Clathrate gun effect">clathrate gun effect</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> although potential alternative mechanisms have been identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  This was associated with rapid <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_Extinction_Event" title="Permian-Triassic Extinction Event">Permian-Triassic Extinction Event</a>, also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_dying" title="Great dying">great dying</a>, in which up to 95% of all species became extinct, has been hypothesized to be related to a rapid change in global climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Life on land took 30 million years to recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboniferous_Rainforest_Collapse" title="Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse">Carboniferous Rainforest Collapse</a> occurred 300 million years ago, at which time tropical rainforests were devastated by climate change. The cooler, drier climate had a severe effect on the biodiversity of amphibians, the primary form of vertebrate life on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFalconLang_2010RainforestCollapse-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There are also abrupt climate changes associated with the catastrophic draining of glacial lakes. One example of this is the <a href="/wiki/8.2_kiloyear_event" title="8.2 kiloyear event">8.2 kiloyear event</a>, which associated with the draining of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Agassiz" title="Glacial Lake Agassiz">Glacial Lake Agassiz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Cold_Reversal" title="Antarctic Cold Reversal">Antarctic Cold Reversal</a>, c. 14,500 years before present (<a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>), which is believed to have been caused by a meltwater pulse from the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheet</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These rapid meltwater release events have been hypothesized as a cause for Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2017 study concluded that similar conditions to today's <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Antarctic ozone hole</a> (atmospheric circulation and hydroclimate changes), ∼17,700 years ago, when stratospheric ozone depletion contributed to abrupt accelerated Southern Hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">deglaciation</a>. The event coincidentally happened with an estimated 192-year series of massive volcanic eruptions, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Takahe" title="Mount Takahe">Mount Takahe</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctica" title="West Antarctica">West Antarctica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
