<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">UN number</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>UN numbers</b> (United Nations numbers) are four-digit numbers that identify <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous_materials" title="Hazardous materials">hazardous materials</a>, and articles (such as explosives, Flammable Liquids to oxidizing solid or toxic liquids, etc.) in the framework of international transport. 
Some hazardous substances have their own UN numbers (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">acrylamide</a> has UN 2074), while sometimes groups of chemicals or products with similar properties receive a common UN number (e.g. flammable liquids, not otherwise specified, have UN1993). A chemical in its solid state may receive a different UN number than the liquid phase if their hazardous properties differ significantly; substances with different levels of purity (or concentration in solution) may also receive different UN numbers.
</p><p>UN numbers range from UN 0004 to about UN 3534 (UN 0001 – UN 0003 no longer exist) and are assigned by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Committee of Experts on the Transport of Dangerous Goods. They are published as part of their <i>Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods</i>, also known as the <i>Orange Book</i>. These recommendations are  adopted by the regulatory organization responsible for the different modes of transport.
</p><p>There is no UN number allocated to non-hazardous substances. These will simply not have a UN number.
</p><p>For more details, see <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_UN_numbers" title="Lists of UN numbers">Lists of UN numbers</a>.
</p><p><b>NA numbers</b> (<b>N</b>orth <b>A</b>merica), are issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> and are identical to UN numbers, except that some substances without a UN number may have an NA number. These additional NA numbers use the range NA 9000 - NA 9279. There are some exceptions, for example NA 2212 is all asbestos with UN 2212 limited to Asbestos, amphibole amosite, tremolite, actinolite, anthophyllite, or crocidolite. Another exception, NA 3334, is self-defense spray, non-pressurized while UN 3334 is aviation regulated liquid, not otherwise specified. For the complete list, see <a href="/wiki/NA/UN_exceptions" title="NA/UN exceptions">NA/UN exceptions</a>.
</p><p>For more details see <a href="/wiki/List_of_NA_numbers" title="List of NA numbers">List of NA numbers</a>.
</p><p><b>ID numbers</b> are a third type of identification number used for hazardous substances. Substances with an ID number are associated with proper shipping names recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICAO" title="ICAO">ICAO</a> Technical Instructions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There is only one substance that currently has such a number: ID 8000, Consumer commodity. This substance does not have a UN or NA number, and is classed as a <a href="/wiki/HAZMAT_Class_9_Miscellaneous" title="HAZMAT Class 9 Miscellaneous">Class 9</a> hazardous material.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazard_identifiers">Hazard identifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=UN_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hazard identifiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Associated with each UN number is a <b>hazard identifier</b>, which encodes the general hazard class and subdivision (and, in the case of explosives, their compatibility group). If a substance poses several dangers, then subsidiary risk identifiers may be specified. It is not possible to deduce the hazard class(es) of a substance from its UN number: they have to be looked up in a table.
</p>
