<p>The <b>environmental impacts of irrigation</b>  relate to the changes in quantity and quality of <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and the effects on natural and social conditions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_basin" title="River basin">river basins</a> and downstream of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheme" title="Irrigation scheme">irrigation scheme</a>. The impacts stem from the altered <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrological conditions</a> caused by the installation and operation of the irrigation scheme.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_effects">Direct effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Direct effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheme" title="Irrigation scheme">irrigation scheme</a> draws water from groundwater, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overland_flow" title="Overland flow">overland flow</a>, and distributes it over an area. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological" title="Hydrological">Hydrological</a>, or direct, effects of doing this<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Direct_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Direct-1">[1]</a></sup> include reduction in downstream river flow, increased <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> in the irrigated area, increased level in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watertable" title="Watertable">water table</a> as <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a> in the area is increased and flow increased in the irrigated area. Likewise, irrigation has immediate effects on the provision of moisture to the atmosphere, inducing atmospheric instabilities and increasing downwind rainfall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or in other cases modifies the atmospheric circulation, delivering rain to different downwind areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Increases or decreases in irrigation are a key area of concern in <a href="/wiki/Precipitationshed" title="Precipitationshed">precipitationshed</a> studies, that examine how significant modifications to the delivery of evaporation to the atmosphere can alter downwind rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_Effects">Indirect Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indirect Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Indirect effects are those that have consequences that take longer to develop and may also be longer-lasting. The indirect effects of irrigation include the following:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Waterlogging_(agriculture)" title="Waterlogging (agriculture)">Waterlogging</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salination" title="Soil salination">Soil salination</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecological damage</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomy" title="Socioeconomy">Socioeconomic</a> impacts</li></ul>
<p>The indirect effects of <a href="/wiki/Waterlogging_(agriculture)" title="Waterlogging (agriculture)">waterlogging</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salination" title="Soil salination">soil salination</a> occur directly on the land being irrigated. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">socioeconomic</a> consequences take longer to happen but can be more far-reaching.
</p><p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheme" title="Irrigation scheme">irrigation schemes</a> use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_wells" title="Water wells">water wells</a> for irrigation. As a result, the overall water level decreases. This may cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_mining" title="Water mining">water mining</a>, land/soil <a href="/wiki/Groundwater-related_subsidence" title="Groundwater-related subsidence">subsidence</a>, and, along the coast, <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_intrusion" title="Saltwater intrusion">saltwater intrusion</a>.
</p><p>Irrigated land area worldwide occupies about 16% of the total agricultural area and the crop yield of irrigated land is roughly 40% of the total yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruce_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruce-5">[5]</a></sup> In other words, irrigated land produces 2.5 times more product than non-irrigated land. This article will discuss some of the environmental and socioeconomic impacts of irrigation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverse_impacts">Adverse impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Adverse impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_river_flow">Reduced river flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reduced river flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The reduced downstream river flow may cause:
</p>
<ul><li>reduced downstream flooding</li>
<li>disappearance of ecologically and economically important wetlands or flood forests<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>reduced availability of industrial, municipal, household, and drinking water</li>
<li>reduced shipping routes. Water withdrawal poses a serious threat to the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>. In India, <a href="/wiki/Barrage_(dam)" title="Barrage (dam)">barrages</a> control all of the tributaries to the Ganges and divert roughly 60 percent of river flow to irrigation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>reduced fishing opportunities. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> River in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> faces scarcity due to over-extraction of water for agriculture. The Indus is inhabited by 25 amphibian species and 147 fish species of which 22 are found nowhere else in the world. It harbors the endangered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_River_dolphin" title="Indus River dolphin">Indus River dolphin</a>, one of the world’s rarest mammals. Fish populations, the main source of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> and overall life support systems for many communities, are also being threatened<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>reduced discharge into the sea, which may have various consequences like coastal erosion (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) and salt water intrusion in delta's and estuaries (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aswan_dam" title="Aswan dam">Aswan dam</a>). Current water withdrawal from the river <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> for irrigation is so high that, despite its size, in dry periods the river does not reach the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> has suffered an "environmental catastrophe" due to the interception of river water for irrigation purposes.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Increased_groundwater_recharge.2C_waterlogging.2C_soil_salinity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_groundwater_recharge,_waterlogging,_soil_salinity">Increased groundwater recharge, waterlogging, soil salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Increased groundwater recharge, waterlogging, soil salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soil_Salinity2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1166" data-file-width="1555" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Soil_Salinity2.jpg/300px-Soil_Salinity2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Soil_Salinity2.jpg/450px-Soil_Salinity2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Soil_Salinity2.jpg/600px-Soil_Salinity2.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soil_Salinity2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looking over the shoulder of a Peruvian farmer in the <a href="/wiki/Huarmey" title="Huarmey">Huarmey</a> delta at <a href="/wiki/Waterlogging_(agriculture)" title="Waterlogging (agriculture)">waterlogged</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinised</a> irrigated land with a poor crop stand. <br/> This illustrates an environmental impact of upstream irrigation developments causing an increased flow of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> to this lower-lying area, leading to adverse conditions.</div></div></div>
<p>Increased groundwater recharge stems from the unavoidable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_percolation" title="Deep percolation">deep percolation</a> losses occurring in the irrigation scheme. The lower the irrigation efficiency, the higher the losses. Although fairly high irrigation efficiencies of 70% or more (i.e. losses of 30% or less) can occur with sophisticated techniques like <a href="/wiki/Irrigation#Sprinkler" title="Irrigation">sprinkler irrigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">drip irrigation</a>, or by well managed <a href="/wiki/Surface_irrigation" title="Surface irrigation">surface irrigation</a>, in practice the losses are commonly in the order of 40% to 60%. This may cause the following issues:
</p>
<ul><li>rising water tables</li>
<li>increased storage of groundwater that may be used for irrigation, municipal, household and drinking water by pumping from <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">wells</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Waterlogging_(agriculture)" title="Waterlogging (agriculture)">waterlogging</a> and drainage problems in villages, agricultural lands, and along roads - with mostly negative consequences. The increased level of the water table can lead to reduced agricultural production.</li>
<li>shallow water tables - a sign that the aquifer is unable to cope with the groundwater recharge stemming from the deep percolation losses</li>
<li>where water tables are shallow, the irrigation applications are reduced. As a result, the soil is no longer <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">leached</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">soil salinity</a> problems develop</li>
<li>stagnant water tables at the soil surface are known to increase the incidence of water-borne diseases like <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filariasis" title="Filariasis">filariasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dengue" title="Dengue">dengue</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis</a> (Bilharzia) in many areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Health costs, appraisals of health impacts and mitigation measures are rarely part of irrigation projects, if at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thakkar_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thakkar-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>to mitigate the adverse effects of shallow water tables and soil salinization, some form of <a href="/wiki/Watertable_control" title="Watertable control">watertable control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">soil salinity control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(agriculture)" title="Drainage system (agriculture)">drainage system</a> is needed</li>
<li>as drainage water moves through the soil profile it may dissolve nutrients (either fertilizer-based or naturally occurring) such as nitrates, leading to a buildup of those nutrients in the ground-water aquifer. High nitrate levels in drinking water can be harmful to humans, particularly infants under 6 months, where it is linked to "blue-baby syndrome" (see <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobinemia" title="Methemoglobinemia">Methemoglobinemia</a>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_downstream_river_water_quality">Reduced downstream river water quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reduced downstream river water quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owing to drainage of surface and groundwater in the project area, which waters may be salinized and polluted by agricultural chemicals like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocides" title="Biocides">biocides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, the quality of the river water below the project area can deteriorate, which makes it less fit for industrial, municipal and household use. It may lead to reduced public health.<br/>
Polluted river water entering the sea may adversely affect the ecology along the sea shore (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aswan_dam" title="Aswan dam">Aswan dam</a>).
</p><p>The natural buildup of sedimentation can reduce downstream river flows due to the installation of irrigation systems. Sedimentation is an essential part of the ecosystem that requires the natural flux of the river flow. This natural cycle of sediment dispersion replenishes the nutrients in the soil, that will in turn, determine the livelihood of the plants and animals that rely on the sediments carried downstream. The benefits of heavy deposits of sedimentation can be seen in large rivers like the Nile River. The sediment from the delta has built up to form a giant aquifer during flood season, and retains water in the wetlands. The wetlands that are created and sustained due to built up sediment at the basin of the river is a habitat for numerous species of birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, heavy sedimentation can reduce downstream river water quality and can exacerbate floods up stream. This has been known to happen in the Sanmenxia reservoir in China. The Sanmenxia reservoir is part of a larger man-made project of hydro-electric dams called the Three Gorge Project <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1998, uncertain calculations and heavy sediment greatly affected the reservoir’s ability to properly fulfill its flood-control function <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This also reduces the down stream river water quality. Shifting more towards mass irrigation installments in order to meet more socioeconomic demands is going against the natural balance of nature, and use water pragmatically- use it where it is found<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Affected_downstream_water_users">Affected downstream water users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Affected downstream water users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BaluchNomads.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="825" data-file-width="1714" height="106" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/220px-BaluchNomads.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/330px-BaluchNomads.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/440px-BaluchNomads.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BaluchNomads.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water becomes scarce for nomadic pastoralist in Baluchistan due to new irrigation developments</div></div></div>
<p>Downstream water users often have no legal water rights and may fall victim of the development of irrigation.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastoralists" title="Pastoralists">Pastoralists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomads" title="Nomads">nomadic tribes</a> may find their land and water resources blocked by new irrigation developments without having a legal recourse.
</p><p>Flood-recession cropping may be seriously affected by the upstream interception of river water for irrigation purposes.
</p>
<ul><li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balochistan_(Pakistan)" title="Balochistan (Pakistan)">Baluchistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the development of new small-scale irrigation projects depleted the water resources of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomads" title="Nomads">nomadic tribes</a> traveling annually between Baluchistan and <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, India<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oost_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-oost-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>After the closure of the <a href="/wiki/Kainji_Dam" title="Kainji Dam">Kainji</a> dam, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, 50 to 70 per cent of the downstream area of flood-recession cropping was lost<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:10.34748W_13.10430N.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="702" data-file-width="1024" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/220px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/330px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/440px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:10.34748W_13.10430N.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lake Manantali, 477 km², displaced 12,000 people.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lost_land_use_opportunities">Lost land use opportunities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lost land use opportunities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Irrigation projects may reduce the fishing opportunities of the original population and the grazing opportunities for cattle. The <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> pressure on the remaining lands may increase considerably, because the ousted traditional pastoralist tribes will have to find their <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">subsistence</a> and existence elsewhere, <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a> may increase, followed by serious <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and the loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><br/>
The <a href="/wiki/Lake_Manantali" title="Lake Manantali">Manatali reservoir</a> formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manantali_dam" title="Manantali dam">Manantali dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a> intersects the migration routes of nomadic pastoralists and destroyed 43000 ha of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannah" title="Savannah">savannah</a>, probably leading to overgrazing and erosion elsewhere. Further, the reservoir destroyed 120 km² of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>. The depletion of groundwater aquifers, which is caused by the suppression of the seasonal flood cycle, is damaging the forests downstream of the dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Groundwater_mining_with_wells.2C_land_subsidence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Groundwater_mining_with_wells,_land_subsidence">Groundwater mining with wells, land subsidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Groundwater mining with wells, land subsidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BodemVerzakking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="165" data-file-width="237" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/BodemVerzakking.jpg/220px-BodemVerzakking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/62/BodemVerzakking.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BodemVerzakking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flooding as a consequence of land subsidence</div></div></div>
<p>When more groundwater is pumped from wells than replenished, storage of water in the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> is being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_mining" title="Water mining">mined</a> and the use of that water is  no longer sustainable. As levels fail, it becomes more difficult to extract water and pumps will struggle to maintain the design flowrate and consume more may fenergy per unit of water. Eventually it may become so difficult to extract groundwater that farmers may be forced to abandon irrigated agriculture.<br/>
Some notable examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>The hundreds of tubewells installed in the state of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, India, with World Bank funding have operating periods of 1.4 to 4.7 hours/day, whereas they were designed to operate 16 hours/day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balochistan_(Pakistan)" title="Balochistan (Pakistan)">Baluchistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the development of tubewell irrigation projects was at the expense of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Qanat" title="Qanat">qanat</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karez" title="Karez">karez</a> users<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oost_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-oost-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Groundwater-related_subsidence" title="Groundwater-related subsidence">Groundwater-related subsidence</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> of the land due to mining of groundwater occurred in the United States at a rate of 1m for each 13m that the watertable was lowered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Homes at Greens Bayou near <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, where 5 to 7 feet of subsidence has occurred, were flooded during a storm in June 1989 as shown in the picture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Simulation_and_prediction">Simulation and prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Simulation and prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The effects of irrigation on watertable, soil salinity and salinity of drainage and groundwater, and the effects of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_mitigation" title="Environmental mitigation">mitigative measures</a> can be simulated and predicted using agro-hydro-salinity models like <a href="/wiki/SaltMod" title="SaltMod">SaltMod</a> and <a href="/wiki/SahysMod" title="SahysMod">SahysMod</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_studies">Case studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Case studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol><li>In India 2.19 million ha have been reported to suffer from waterlogging in irrigation canal commands. Also 3.47 million ha were reported to be seriously salt affected,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> Plains in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, more than 2 million hectares of land is waterlogged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green-26">[26]</a></sup> The soil of 13.6 million hectares within the Gross Command Area was surveyed, which revealed that 3.1 million hectares (23%) was saline. 23% of this was in <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a> and 13% in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_(Pakistan)" title="Punjab (Pakistan)">Punjab</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Green-26">[26]</a></sup> More than 3 million ha of water-logged lands have been provided with tube-wells and drains at the cost of billions of rupees, but the reclamation objectives were only partially achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank" title="Asian Development Bank">Asian Development Bank</a> (ADB) states that 38% of the irrigated area is now waterlogged and 14% of the surface is too saline for use<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_delta" title="Nile delta">Nile delta</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, drainage is being installed in millions of hectares to combat the water-logging resulting from the introduction of massive perennial irrigation after completion of the High Dam at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aswan_dam" title="Aswan dam">Assuan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Mexico, 15% of the 3 million ha of irrigable land is salinized and 10% is waterlogged<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> some 0.3 million ha of the 1.05 million ha of irrigable land suffers from degradation (see <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_in_Peru" title="Irrigation in Peru">Irrigation in Peru</a>).</li>
<li>Estimates indicate that roughly one-third of the irrigated land in the major irrigation countries is already badly affected by salinity or is expected to become so in the near future.  Present estimates for Israel are 13% of the irrigated land, Australia 20%, China 15%, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> 50%, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> 30%.  Irrigation-induced salinity occurs in large and small irrigation systems alike<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>FAO has estimated that by 1990 about 52 million ha of irrigated land will need to have improved drainage systems installed, much of it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsurface_drainage" title="Subsurface drainage">subsurface drainage</a> to control salinity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_downstream_drainage_and_groundwater_quality">Reduced downstream drainage and groundwater quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reduced downstream drainage and groundwater quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The downstream drainage water quality may deteriorate owing to <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> of salts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicides" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> with high salinity and alkalinity. There is threat of soils converting into saline or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_soils" title="Alkali soils">alkali soils</a>. This may negatively affect the health of the population at the tail-end of the river basin and downstream of the irrigation scheme, as well as the ecological balance. The <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, for example, is seriously <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">polluted</a> by drainage water.</li>
<li>The downstream quality of the groundwater may deteriorate in a similar way as the downstream drainage water and have similar consequences</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_of_adverse_effects">Mitigation of adverse effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mitigation of adverse effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Irrigation can have a variety negative impacts on <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomy" title="Socioeconomy">socioeconomy</a>, which may be mitigated in a number of ways.
These include siting the irrigation project in a location which minimises negative impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup> The efficiency of existing projects can be improved and existing degraded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croplands" title="Croplands">croplands</a> can be improved rather than establishing a new irrigation project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup>
Developing small-scale, individually owned irrigation systems as an alternative to large-scale, publicly owned and managed schemes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup> The use of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_sprinkler" title="Irrigation sprinkler">sprinkler irrigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Micro-irrigation" title="Micro-irrigation">micro-irrigation</a> systems decreases the risk of <a href="/wiki/Waterlogging_(agriculture)" title="Waterlogging (agriculture)">waterlogging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup>
Where practicable, using treated <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> makes more water available to other users<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup>
Maintaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flow_routing" title="Flow routing">flood flows</a> downstream of the dams can ensure that an adequate area is flooded each year, supporting, amongst other objectives, <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Delayed_environmental_impacts">Delayed environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Delayed environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It often takes time to accurately predict the impact that new irrigation schemes will have on the <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomy" title="Socioeconomy">socioeconomy</a> of a region. By the time these predictions are available, a considerable amount of time and resources may have already been expended in the implementation of that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheme" title="Irrigation scheme">project</a>. When that is the case, the project managers will often only change the project if the impact would be considerably more than they had originally expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_benefits_outweigh_the_potential_disadvantages">Potential benefits outweigh the potential disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Potential benefits outweigh the potential disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frequently <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation schemes</a> are seen as extremely necessary for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socioeconomic" title="Socioeconomic">socioeconomic</a> well-being especially in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>. 
One example of this can be demonstrated from a proposal for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheme" title="Irrigation scheme">irrigation scheme</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a>. Here it was shown that the potential positive effects of the <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> project that was being proposed "outweighed the potential negative impacts". It was stated that the impacts would mostly "be localized, minimal, short term occurring during the construction and operation phases of the Project". In order to help alleviate and prevent major environmental impacts, they would use techniques that minimize the potential negative impacts. As far as the region's socioeconomic well-being, there would be no "displacement and/or resettlement envisioned during the implementation of the Project activities". The original primary purposes of the irrigation project were to reduce poverty, improve food security, create local employment, increase household income and enhance the sustainability of land use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to this careful planning this project was successful both in improving the socialeconomic conditions in the region and ensuring that land and water are sustainability into the future.
</p>
