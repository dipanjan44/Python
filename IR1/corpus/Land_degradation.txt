<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Land degradation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1194" data-file-width="1787" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg/220px-Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg/330px-Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg/440px-Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Karst_following_phosphate_mining_on_Nauru.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Serious land degradation in <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a> after the depletion of the <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> cover through mining</div></div></div>
<p><b>Land degradation</b> is a process in which the value of the <a href="/wiki/Biophysical_environment" title="Biophysical environment">biophysical environment</a> is affected by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human-induced processes</a> acting upon the land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rural_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-rural-1">[1]</a></sup> 
It is viewed as any change or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_disturbance" title="Ecological disturbance">disturbance</a> to the land perceived to be deleterious or undesirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Natural_hazard" title="Natural hazard">Natural hazards</a> are excluded as a cause; however human activities can indirectly affect phenomena such as floods and bush fires.
</p><p>This is considered to be an important topic of the <a href="/wiki/21st_century" title="21st century">21st century</a> due to the implications land degradation has upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agronomic_productivity" title="Agronomic productivity">agronomic productivity</a>, the environment, and its effects on <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is estimated that up to 40% of the world's agricultural land is seriously degraded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures">Measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four main ways of looking at land degradation and its impact on the environment around it:
</p>
<ol><li>A temporary or permanent decline in the <a href="/wiki/Productive_capacity" title="Productive capacity">productive capacity</a> of the land. This can be seen through a loss of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, a loss of actual productivity or in potential productivity, or a loss or change in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetative_cover" title="Vegetative cover">vegetative cover</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_nutrient" title="Soil nutrient">soil nutrients</a>.</li>
<li>Action in the land's capacity to provide resources for human livelihoods. This can be measured from a base line of past land use.</li>
<li>Loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>: A loss of range of species or ecosystem complexity as a decline in the environmental quality.</li>
<li>Shifting ecological risk: increased vulnerability of the environment or people to destruction or crisis. This is measured through a base line in the form of pre-existing risk of crisis or destruction.</li></ol>
<p>A problem with defining land degradation is that what one group of people might view as degradation, others might view as a benefit or opportunity. For example, planting <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> at a location with heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> and steep slopes would create scientific and environmental concern regarding the risk of <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion#Rainfall_and_runoff" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion by water</a>, yet  farmers could view the location as a favourable one for high <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">crop yields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landguide_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-landguide-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_types">Different types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Different types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="692" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg/220px-A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg/330px-A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg/440px-A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_potato_field_with_soil_erosion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Potato field with soil erosion</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to the usual types of land degradation that have been known for centuries (water, wind and mechanical <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, physical, chemical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_degradation" title="Biological degradation">biological degradation</a>), four other types have emerged in the last 50 years:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, often chemical, due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" title="Industrial agriculture">agricultural</a>, industrial, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> or commercial activities;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Land_loss" title="Land loss">loss</a> of arable land due to <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urban construction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_building" title="Road building">road building</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_conversion" title="Land conversion">land conversion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_expansion" title="Agricultural expansion">agricultural expansion</a>, etc.;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_radioactivity" title="Artificial radioactivity">artificial radioactivity</a>, sometimes accidental;</li>
<li>land-use constraints associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_conflict" title="Armed conflict">armed conflicts</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Overall, more than 36 types of land degradation can be assessed. All are induced or aggravated by human activities, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sheet_erosion" title="Sheet erosion">sheet erosion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silting" title="Silting">silting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aridification" title="Aridification">aridification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a>, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cattle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1745" data-file-width="2700" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Cattle.jpg/220px-Cattle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Cattle.jpg/330px-Cattle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Cattle.jpg/440px-Cattle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cattle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overgrazing by <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> can lead to land degradation</div></div></div>
<p>Land degradation caused by mining as it cause landslides
Land degradation is a global problem largely related to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> use. Causes include:
</p>
<ul><li>Land clearance, such as <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clearcutting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a></li>
<li>Agricultural depletion of soil <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> through poor farming practices</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">Livestock</a> including <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overdrafting" title="Overdrafting">overdrafting</a></li>
<li>Inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Overdrafting" title="Overdrafting">overdrafting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">Urban sprawl</a> and commercial development</li>
<li>Vehicle <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">off-roading</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">Quarrying</a> of stone, sand, ore and minerals</li>
<li>Increase in field size due to <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, reducing shelter for wildlife, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedgerow" title="Hedgerow">hedgerows</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copse" title="Copse">copses</a> disappear</li>
<li>Exposure of naked soil after harvesting by heavy equipment</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">Monoculture</a>, destabilizing the local ecosystem</li>
<li>Dumping of non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biodegradable" title="Biodegradable">biodegradable</a> trash, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics" title="Plastics">plastics</a></li>
<li>Invasive Species</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_degradation" title="Soil degradation">Soil degradation</a>, e.g.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">Soil contamination</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">Soil erosion</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_acidification" title="Soil acidification">Soil acidification</a></li>
<li>Loss of <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erosion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2670" data-file-width="1769" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Erosion.jpg/160px-Erosion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Erosion.jpg/240px-Erosion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Erosion.jpg/320px-Erosion.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erosion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soil erosion in a <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> field near <a href="/wiki/Pullman,_Washington" title="Pullman, Washington">Pullman</a>, US</div></div></div>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Overcutting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Overcutting (page does not exist)">Overcutting</a> of vegetation occurs when people cut forests, woodlands and shrublands—to obtain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuelwood" title="Fuelwood">fuelwood</a> and other products—at a pace <a href="/wiki/Ecological_debt" title="Ecological debt">exceeding the rate of natural regrowth</a>. This is frequent in semi-arid environments, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuelwood" title="Fuelwood">fuelwood</a> shortages are often severe. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">Overgrazing</a> is the grazing of natural pastures at stocking intensities above the livestock <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>; the resulting decrease in the vegetation cover is a leading cause of wind and water erosion. It is a significant factor in Afghanistan. The growing population pressure, during 1980-1990, has led to decreases in the already small areas of agricultural land per person in six out of eight countries (14% for India and 21% for Pakistan).
</p><p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Population_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Population pressure (page does not exist)">Population pressure</a> also operates through other mechanisms. Improper agricultural practices, for instance, occur only under constraints such as the saturation of good lands under population pressure which leads settlers to cultivate too shallow or too steep soils, plough fallow land before it has recovered its fertility, or attempt to obtain multiple crops by irrigating unsuitable soils.
</p><p>High population density is not always related to land degradation. Rather, it is the practices of the human population that can cause a landscape to become degraded. Populations can be a benefit to the land and make it more productive than it is in its natural state. Land degradation is an important factor of internal displacement in many African and Asian countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Severe land degradation affects a significant portion of the Earth's arable lands, decreasing the wealth and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> of nations. As the land resource base becomes less productive, <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> is compromised and competition for dwindling <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a> increases, the seeds of <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> and potential conflict are sown.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity_and_resilience">Sensitivity and resilience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sensitivity and resilience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sensitivity and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a> are measures of the vulnerability of a landscape to degradation. These two factors combine to explain the degree of vulnerability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landguide_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-landguide-5">[5]</a></sup> Sensitivity is the degree to which a land system undergoes change due to natural forces, human intervention or a combination of both. Resilience is the ability of a landscape to absorb change, without significantly altering the relationship between the relative importance and numbers of individuals and species that compose the community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It also refers to the ability of the region to return to its original state after being changed in some way. The resilience of a landscape can be increased or decreased through human interaction based upon different methods of land-use management. Land that is degraded becomes less resilient than undegraded land, which can lead to even further degradation through shocks to the landscape.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_degradation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Significant land degradation from <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> <a href="/wiki/Inundation" title="Inundation">inundation</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> and on low-lying islands, is a potential hazard that was identified in a 2007 <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.ipcc.ch/publications_and_data/ar4/syr/en/mains3-3-2.html" rel="nofollow">report</a>.
</p>
