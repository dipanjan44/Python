<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Clean Power Plan</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_greenhouse_gases_under_the_Clean_Air_Act" title="Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act">Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act</a></div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg/400px-Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg/600px-Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg/800px-Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Navajo_generating_station_Page_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Generating_Station" title="Navajo Generating Station">Navajo Generating Station</a>, a coal-fired power plant outside <a href="/wiki/Page,_Arizona" title="Page, Arizona">Page, Arizona</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Clean Power Plan</b> was an <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Barack_Obama" title="Presidency of Barack Obama">Obama administration</a> policy aimed at combating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a> (global warming) that was first proposed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)</a> in June 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The plan was widely expected to be eliminated under President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who signed an executive order on March 28, 2017 mandating the EPA to review the plan. On June 1, 2017, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">United States withdrew from the Paris Agreement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In August 2017, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a> granted the EPA an additional 60 days to review the CPP and submit their position to the court, before continuing the process to settle the case about the legality of the CPP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Court_Status_Aug_2017_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Status_Aug_2017-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The final version of the plan was unveiled by President Obama on August 3, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-8">[8]</a></sup> The 460-page rule (RIN 2060–AR33) titled "Carbon Pollution Emission Guidelines for Existing Stationary Sources:
Electric Utility Generating Units" was published in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a> on October 23, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Obama administration designed the plan to lower the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emitted by power generators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was revealed on October 4, 2017, that the EPA under the Trump Administration was planning to end the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HillObama_era_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-HillObama_era-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FortuneRepeal_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FortuneRepeal-12">[12]</a></sup> Two days later, it was revealed that the President was considering alternatives to the plan through public discussion, and guidelines on how they intended to repeal the plan were leaked as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpooct6_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-washpooct6-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct6_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct6-14">[14]</a></sup> EPA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Scott_Pruitt" title="Scott Pruitt">Scott Pruitt</a> announced the formal process to change EPA rules and repeal the plan would begin on October 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct710_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct710-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTRepeal_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTRepeal-16">[16]</a></sup> Pruitt then signed a formal proposal that would lead to the repeal of the Clean Power Plan. The standard federal regulatory procedures to implement or change a regulation will likely to take up to two years. Potential legal challenges may have caused delays to repeal the regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-“veconomist”_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-“veconomist”-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the US Supreme Court rejected any further court challenges to the Trump Administration's decision to repeal the Clean Power Plan on October 9, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct9_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct9-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aims">Aims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The final version of the plan aims to reduce carbon dioxide emissions from electrical power generation by 32 percent by 2030, relative to 2005 levels.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The plan is focused on reducing emissions from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>-burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>, as well as increasing the use of renewable energy, and energy conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-22">[22]</a></sup> White House officials also hoped that the plan would help persuade other countries that emit large amounts of carbon dioxide to officially pledge to reduce their emissions at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Requirements">Requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The plan will require individual states to meet specific standards with respect to reduction of carbon dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> States are free to reduce emissions by various means, and must submit emissions reductions plans by September 2016, or, with an extension approval, by September 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vox_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-vox-25">[25]</a></sup> If a state has not submitted a plan by then, the EPA will impose its own plan on that state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vox_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-vox-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The EPA divided the country into three regions based on connected regional electricity grids to determine a state's goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> States are to implement their plans by focusing on three building blocks: increasing the generation efficiency of existing fossil fuel plants, substituting lower carbon dioxide emitting natural gas generation for coal powered generation, and substituting generation from new zero carbon dioxide emitting renewable sources for fossil fuel powered generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa2-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>States may use regionally available low carbon generation sources when substituting for in-state coal generation and coordinate with other states to develop multi-state plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa2-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EPA estimates the Clean Power Plan will reduce the pollutants that contribute to smog and soot by 25 percent, and the reduction will lead to net climate and health benefits of an estimated $25 billion to $45 billion per year in 2030.  That includes the avoidance of 140,000 to 150,000 asthma attacks among children and 2,700 to 6,600 premature deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> EPA projects that the plan will save the average American family $85 per year in energy bills in 2030, and it will save enough energy to power 30 million homes and save consumers $155 billion from 2020–2030. The plan would create 30 percent more renewable energy generation in 2030 and help to lower the costs of renewable energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  It  also would create hundreds of thousands of jobs, according to the NRDC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_CO2_emissions">Reduced CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reduced CO2 emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">Energy Information Administration</a> (EIA), coal in 2015 in the United States produced 1,364,000,000 metric tons of CO<sub>2</sub>. This amounted to 71% of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from the electric power sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> By switching this coal generation to a cleaner source such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, CO<sub>2</sub> emissions could be significantly reduced. In addition, evidence suggests that wind power now has a lower cost of production than coal or natural gas, even when subsidies are taken into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Conservation_Voters" title="League of Conservation Voters">League of Conservation Voters</a> in 2015, the Clean Power Plan "established the first national limits on carbon pollution from existing power plants—our nation's single largest source of the pollution fueling climate change" and was "the biggest step" the United States had "ever taken to address climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcvsjres24_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-lcvsjres24-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States' enactment of the Clean Power Plan was one of the first major global initiatives to curb internal greenhouse gas emissions. Since the plan was established in 2014, there have been various global efforts made to decrease toxic particulate matter emissions by other developed nations. <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">The Paris Agreement</a> was agreed upon in October 2016 and entered into force in November 2016. The Paris Agreement aims to combat global climate change by keeping a global temperature rise this century well below 2 degrees Celsius and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase even further to 1.5 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:32-34">[34]</a></sup> In order to enact the plan, 194 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">UNFCCC</a> member nations have signed the treaty, 172 of which have ratified it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:32_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:32-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The poorest, most underdeveloped nations emit the lowest levels of carbon dioxide and greenhouse gasses. According to the World Bank, greenhouse gas emissions from large nations such as the United States and China disproportionately affect developing nations who don't have the infrastructure to combat climate change induced drought, famine, and other natural and anthropogenic disasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:52_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-:52-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_environmental_justice_for_households">Economic environmental justice for households</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic environmental justice for households">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The economic impact of the Clean Power Plan (CPP), not including the impact on employment, can be measured by many variables including its impact on electricity prices and health expenditures. In four major studies conducted on the economic impact of the CPP, findings varied widely due to the assumptions made and the variables analyzed. Ultimately, the effect of the CPP on households is most influenced by how states decide to meet their emissions goals, allocate the revenue generated by the carbon tax, and collaborate with other states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wri.org_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-wri.org-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data on the economic impact of the Clean Power Plan on electricity prices relies heavily on four studies conducted separately by Synapse Energy Economics, M.J. Bradley &amp; Associates, NERA Economic Consulting, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA). Synapse Energy Economics relied on assumptions from a 2012 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> (DOE) study on future potential of energy and reported findings indicating that the CPP will decrease the cost of electricity. M.J. Bradley &amp; Associates rely on data from <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a> (NREL) and reported generally optimistic findings, with large decreases in costs due to the CPP. NERA Economic Consulting, funded by coal lobbyists,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> relied on <a href="/wiki/Energy_Information_Administration" title="Energy Information Administration">U.S. Energy Information Administration</a> (EIA) data with pessimistic assumptions, resulting in pessimistic findings stating that some states may even face double-digit price increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  The EPA drew from the NREL for data and made middle-ground assumptions, ultimately reporting findings that are similarly "middle-ground" compared with other studies. The ability to measure and determine the impact on at-risk communities is confounded by these varying conclusions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Differences between states aside, three key at-risk groups are lower-income communities, higher-income communities, and coal miner communities. Lower-income households may disproportionately experience increases in expenditures due to a large share of their consumption falling into the energy-intensive category, including products and services like electricity, heating, and gasoline. However, lower-income communities are also likely to benefit from increased air quality, and therefore decreased health care expenditures. In order to combat any negative impact of the CPP, states may choose to allocate roughly 10% of their carbon pricing revenue to protect low-income communities. Higher-income communities may be disproportionately affected by the CPP because of decreased income levels, due to greater dependence on capital income, rather than wages. Coal miners, making up 0.057% of the total U.S. employment, may be disproportionately affected by the CPP due to potential layoffs in the coal industry. In contrast, coal miners disproportionately benefit from increased clean air and decreased health expenditures. Just one to five percent of the revenue generated from a moderate carbon price would offset any detriment to coal miner communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wri.org_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-wri.org-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_impact">Health impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Energy Innovation’s Energy Policy Simulator, a repeal of the Clean Power Plan would lead to an increase in carbon dioxide emissions of more than 500 million metric tons by 2030, and by 2050, that figure would rise to more than 1,200 million metric tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, the EPA's proximity analysis concludes that a higher percentage of minority and low-income communities live near power plants when compared to the national averages, increasing risk of disease and death due to toxic particulate matter emissions and air pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The EPA has determined that greenhouse gas pollution causes global temperature warming, leading to harmful changes to the environment and human health globally such as increased drought and increased famine due to decrease in water supply and agricultural production. According to the EPA fact sheet on the Clean Power Plan, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> is responsible for everything from stronger storms to longer droughts and increased insurance premiums, food prices and allergy seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup> The populations most vulnerable to the adverse effects of climate change include children, older adults, people with heart or lung disease and people living in poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup> The repeal of the Clean Power Plan will increase greenhouse gas emissions, expediting the damaging environmental changes due to climate change that disproportionately affect subaltern populations around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment_and_community_engagement_impact">Employment and community engagement impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Employment and community engagement impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As aforementioned, a major part of the Clean Power Plan's mission is to regulate and reduce greenhouse gas emissions from industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-43">[43]</a></sup> Opponents of the Clean Power Plan have stated that the attempt in reducing these emissions is also going to be reducing the number of jobs in the United States because of the shrinkage in the industry sector. More specifically,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-43">[43]</a></sup> there will be a 19% reduction in the iron and steel production, 21% reduction in cement production, and 11% in refining production. On the other hand, those who argue favorably for the Clean Power Plan have addressed the employment concerns of critics of the Clean Power Plan. While jobs will be decreasing in the industrial sector, there has also been an increase nationwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> in the solar sector, wind sector, and energy efficient sector.
</p><p>While some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> are skeptical of the Clean Power Plan because of its job loss in the industrial sector, the EPA has made clear that in order for the Clean Energy Plan to be effective community engagement <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup> is essential, particularly low income, minority and tribal communities. To ensure opportunities in communities, the EPA is requiring all states demonstrate how they are actively engaging with communities. The EPA has created a Clean Energy Incentive Plan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup> that will reward communities who invest in wind and solar generations; the premise is to increase demand for energy efficient programs in low-income communities.  In addition to incentivizing public engagement, they will also be testing air quality evaluations and providing demographic information in order to gauge the impact of air pollution on communities who are located near power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-:22-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2015_announcement">2015 announcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2015 announcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>President Obama announced the plan in a speech given at the White House on August 3, 2015. In his announcement, Obama stated that the plan includes the first standards on carbon dioxide emissions from power plants ever proposed by the Environmental Protection Agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr-45">[45]</a></sup> He also called the plan "the single most important step that America has ever made in the fight against global climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-npr-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Obama called his plan "a moral obligation" and made reference to the encyclical <i><a href="/wiki/Laudato_si%27" title="Laudato si'">Laudato si'</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>The policy has been described as "[Obama's] most ambitious climate policy to date."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vox_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-vox-25">[25]</a></sup> In response to Obama's 2015 announcement, hundreds of businesses voiced support for the plan, including <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a>, and <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  To show support for the Clean Power Plan, 360 other companies and investors sent letters to their governors. The companies and investors signing the letter represent all 50 states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 2016, 2/3 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_utilities" title="Electric utilities">electric utilities</a> supported the plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Congressional_challenge">Congressional challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Congressional challenge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2015 Senator <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Moore_Capito" title="Shelley Moore Capito">Shelley Moore Capito</a>, Republican of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, introduced Senate Joint Resolution 24 (S. J. Res. 24), a “Resolution of Disapproval” under the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Review_Act" title="Congressional Review Act">Congressional Review Act</a>, which act empowered Congress to over-rule rule-making by federal agencies. S. J. Res. 24 would have permanently blocked the Clean Power Plan,  prohibiting the EPA from developing “substantially similar” standards. S. J. Res. 24 was approved by the Senate on November 17 by a vote of 52–46 and by the House on December 1 by a vote of 242–180. Obama vetoed the resolution on December 18. According to the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Conservation_Voters" title="League of Conservation Voters">League of Conservation Voters</a>, the resolution was "an extreme measure...threatening our health and our future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcvsjres24_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-lcvsjres24-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The votes on the resolution were considered key votes by the League and <a href="/wiki/Americans_for_Prosperity" title="Americans for Prosperity">Americans for Prosperity</a> (AFP) Congressional scorecards. AFP said the Clean Power Plan would have a "devastating effect on the economy" and that the resolution would send a "clear signal to the Paris climate negotiators that that President Obama's expansive green energy agenda does not have support on Capitol Hill."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_challenge">Court challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Court challenge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_greenhouse_gases_under_the_Clean_Air_Act" title="Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act">Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act</a></div>
<p>In the June 18, 2014, proposed rule, EPA argued that because the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> Amendment is ambiguous, EPA's interpretation is entitled to <a href="/wiki/Judicial_deference" title="Judicial deference">judicial deference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harv_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-harv-54">[54]</a></sup> EPA found the statute to be ambiguous because the language in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">United States Code</a> is from a May 23, 1990, House amendment that conflicts with a never <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codified</a> April 3 Senate conforming amendment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the U.S. Supreme Court in <i><a href="/wiki/King_v._Burwell" title="King v. Burwell">King v. Burwell</a></i> upheld the Affordable Care Act on June 25, 2015, however, the EPA adopted a more aggressive <a href="/wiki/Statutory_interpretation" title="Statutory interpretation">statutory interpretation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harv_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-harv-54">[54]</a></sup> In the final rule announced on August 3, the EPA argued that the Senate's language unambiguously allows it to regulate, while the House language in the U.S. Code should be ignored because it is unreasonable under the Clean Air Act's "comprehensive scheme".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harv_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-harv-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Opponents immediately declared the Plan was illegal, attempting to sue before the agency finalized the rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Only ten days after the EPA announced the final rule, twenty-seven states petitioned the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a> for an emergency stay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harv_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-harv-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Energy" title="Peabody Energy">Peabody Energy</a> hired <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Tribe" title="Laurence Tribe">Laurence Tribe</a>, President Obama's mentor at Harvard Law School, to author a brief which was later acclaimed on the Senate floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  Tribe would go on to testify before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_Energy_and_Commerce_Committee" title="House Energy and Commerce Committee">House Energy and Commerce Committee</a> that the EPA's energy policy was "burning the Constitution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Challengers argue that EPA overstepped its legal authority in issuing the CPP, in regards to the power plants covered by the plan, and that the scope of the "building blocks" for action goes beyond standards applied to specific electric generating units, as called for by the Clean Air Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>  Eighteen states (California, Connecticut, Delaware, Hawaii, Illinois, Iowa, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia and Washington) have joined the litigation in support of the EPA's plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enforcement_halt_by_Supreme_Court">Enforcement halt by Supreme Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Enforcement halt by Supreme Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On February 9, 2016, the United States Supreme Court ordered the EPA to halt enforcement of the plan until a lower court rules in the lawsuit against the plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title='Why should a lower court decide this "enforcement", nvm rule against it? (August 2018)'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  The 5–4 vote was the first time the Supreme Court had ever <a href="/wiki/Stay_of_proceedings" title="Stay of proceedings">stayed</a> a regulation before a judgment by the lower Court of Appeals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of July 2016, several states – including Republican-held ones such as <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> – are moving forward to meet the Plan's requirements although sometimes indirectly, regardless of open opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D.C._Circuit_Court_hears_argument">D.C. Circuit Court hears argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: D.C. Circuit Court hears argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 27, 2016, the case against the CPP was heard in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a>.  The chief judge of the court, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merrick_B._Garland" title="Merrick B. Garland">Merrick B. Garland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recused" title="Recused">recused</a> himself, as he was also President Obama's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Supreme_Court" title="US Supreme Court">US Supreme Court</a> <a href="/wiki/Merrick_Garland_Supreme_Court_nomination" title="Merrick Garland Supreme Court nomination">nominee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>The argument has sparked debate about both the <a href="/wiki/Constitutionality" title="Constitutionality">constitutionality</a> and the political effects of the Clean Power Plan. The <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a> Editorial Board published an editorial arguing that the D.C. Circuit should uphold the plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2017, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_District_of_Columbia_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit</a> granted the EPA an additional 60 days to review the CPP and submit their position to the court, before continuing the process to settle the case about the legality of the CPP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Court_Status_Aug_2017_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Court_Status_Aug_2017-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supreme_Court_Upholds_Repeal">Supreme Court Upholds Repeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Supreme Court Upholds Repeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On October 9, 2018, the US Supreme Court issued a 5-4 ruling blocking any attempt to appeal the Trump Administration's decision to overturn the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct9_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-oct9-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_actions_under_President_Trump">Proposed actions under President Trump</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Proposed actions under President Trump">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_of_the_Trump_administration" title="Environmental policy of the Trump administration">Environmental policy of the Trump administration</a></div>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>'s proposed <a href="/wiki/2018_United_States_federal_budget" title="2018 United States federal budget">2018 United States federal budget</a> defunded the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>  On March 28, 2017, President Trump signed an <a href="/wiki/Executive_order" title="Executive order">executive order</a> directing EPA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Scott_Pruitt" title="Scott Pruitt">Scott Pruitt</a> to review the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>  EPA will need to go through the formal rulemaking process to change the existing rule,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and in 2007, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in the case <i><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_v._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Massachusetts v. Environmental Protection Agency">Massachusetts v. Environmental Protection Agency</a></i> that EPA regulation of carbon dioxide is actually required by the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, which is still in effect.  Trump explained this decision calling the Clean Power Plan a "job-killing regulation" which some see as false, saying "the potential for job growth in the clean energy sector dwarfs any potential job growth in the fossil fuel economy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn1_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Opposition argues that with the repeal of the Clean Power Plan, the United States will not be able to meet the greenhouse gas emission standards agreed to under the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>, and in turn, will have to withdraw from the agreement.  Without it, the United States is projected to fall over 20% short of its pledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-:42-70">[70]</a></sup> Because the Clean Power Plan was a significant part of how the United States intended to meet the emission targets it set for the Paris Agreement, this action may discourage other countries from upholding their own commitments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Janet_McCabe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Janet McCabe (page does not exist)">Janet McCabe</a>, an Obama Administration EPA department head, stated that the decision completely disregards the impacts of climate and the cost and benefits associated with the started programs. According to her it will lead to several more years of uncertainty and potentially lost opportunity as well as a worsening public image of the United States internationally. However she is hopeful that the decision's impact on the industry's direction toward a cleaner energy system won't be severe as several states already meet the 2022 target carbon dioxide emissions established in the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 1, 2017, Donald Trump announced <a href="/wiki/United_States_withdrawal_from_the_Paris_Agreement" title="United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement">United States withdrawal from the Paris Agreement</a>, but a number of U.S. states formed the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Climate_Alliance" title="United States Climate Alliance">United States Climate Alliance</a> to maintain within state borders the objectives of the Clean Power Plan separately from the federal government.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Repeal">Repeal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Power_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Repeal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 4, 2017, an EPA document obtained by Reuters revealed that the EPA was planning to repeal the Clean Power Plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReutersRepeal_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReutersRepeal-73">[73]</a></sup>  A list of potential alternatives to the Clean Power Plan following public discussion were leaked to Bloomberg News on October 6th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct6_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-oct6-14">[14]</a></sup>  Likewise, <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a> and <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> reported that the EPA would repeal the plan and limit the alternatives to advice for local utilities on October 10th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpooct6_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-washpooct6-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNRepeal_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNNRepeal-75">[75]</a></sup>  EPA Administrator Scott Pruitt signed a proposed rule to repeal the Clean Power Plan on that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> New York’s and Massachusetts’ attorneys general planned to sue the EPA over the repeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-“veconomist”_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-“veconomist”-19">[19]</a></sup> The EPA held a hearing, titled, "Proposal to Repeal the Clean Power Plan", on Nov. 28-29, 2017 in Charleston, West Virginia. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The hearing was live-streamed from the West Virginia capitol building, where it was held. 
</p>
