<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oxygen mask</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">An interface between the oxygen delivery system and the human user</div><p>An <b>oxygen mask</b> provides a method to transfer <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing oxygen gas</a> from a storage tank to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a>. Oxygen masks may cover only the nose and mouth (oral nasal mask) or the entire face (full-face mask). They may be made of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a>.
</p><p>In certain circumstances, oxygen may be delivered via a <a href="/wiki/Nasal_cannula" title="Nasal cannula">nasal cannula</a> instead of a mask.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_plastic_oxygen_masks">Medical plastic oxygen masks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Medical plastic oxygen masks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical plastic oxygen masks are used primarily by medical care providers for <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">oxygen therapy</a> because they are disposable and so reduce cleaning costs and infection risks. Mask design can determine accuracy of oxygen delivered with many various medical situations requiring treatment with oxygen. Oxygen is naturally occurring in room air at 21% and higher percentages are often essential in medical treatment. Oxygen in these higher percentages is classified as a drug with too much oxygen being potentially harmful to a patient's health, resulting in oxygen dependence over time, and in extreme circumstances patient blindness. For these reasons oxygen therapy is closely monitored. Masks are light in weight and attached using an elasticated headband or ear loops. They are transparent for allowing the face to be visible for patient assessment by healthcare providers, and reducing a sensation of claustrophobia experienced by some patients when wearing an oxygen mask. The vast majority of patients having an operation will at some stage wear an oxygen mask; they may alternatively wear a nasal cannula but oxygen delivered in this way is less accurate and restricted in concentration.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicone_and_rubber_masks">Silicone and rubber masks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Silicone and rubber masks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silicone and rubber oxygen masks are heavier than plastic masks. They are designed to provide a good seal for long-duration use by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">aviators</a>, medical research subjects, and <a href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_medicine" title="Hyperbaric medicine">hyperbaric chamber</a> and other patients who require administration of pure oxygen, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">carbon monoxide poisoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> victims. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_H._Bulbulian" title="Arthur H. Bulbulian">Arthur H. Bulbulian</a> pioneered the first modern viable oxygen mask, worn by World War II pilots and used by hospitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8931286_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8931286-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valves" title="Valves">Valves</a> inside these tight-fitting masks control the flow of gases into and out of the masks, so that rebreathing of exhaled gas is minimised.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoses_and_tubing_and_oxygen_regulators">Hoses and tubing and oxygen regulators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hoses and tubing and oxygen regulators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hoses or tubing connect an oxygen mask to the oxygen supply. Hoses are larger in diameter than tubing and can allow greater oxygen flow. When a hose is used it may have a ribbed or corrugated design to allow bending of the hose while preventing twisting and cutting off the oxygen flow. The quantity of oxygen delivered from the storage tank to the oxygen mask is controlled by a valve called a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_regulator" title="Pressure regulator">regulator</a>. Some types of oxygen masks have a breathing bag made of plastic or rubber attached to the mask or oxygen supply hose to store a supply of oxygen to allow deep breathing without waste of oxygen with use of simple fixed flow regulators.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_masks_for_aviators"><span id="Oxygen_masks_for_aviators">Oxygen masks for aviators</span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxygen masks for aviators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An early 1919 high-altitude oxygen system used a vacuum flask of liquid oxygen to supply two people for one hour at 15,000 ft (4,600 m). The liquid passed through several warming stages before use, as expansion when it evaporated, and absorbed <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">latent heat of vaporization</a>, would make the gasified oxygen so cold that it could cause instant <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a> of the lungs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first successful creation for the oxygen mask was by Armenian born <a href="/wiki/Arthur_H._Bulbulian" title="Arthur H. Bulbulian">Dr. Arthur Bulbulian</a>, in the field of facial prosthetics, in 1941.
</p><p>Many designs of aviator's oxygen masks contain a microphone to transmit speech to other crew members and to the aircraft's radio. Military aviators' oxygen masks have face pieces that partially cover the sides of the face and protect the face against flash burns, flying particles, and effects of a high speed air stream hitting the face during emergency evacuation from the aircraft by <a href="/wiki/Ejection_seat" title="Ejection seat">ejection seat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a>. They are often part of a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_suit" title="Pressure suit">pressure suit</a> or intended for use with a <a href="/wiki/Flight_helmet" title="Flight helmet">flight helmet</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three main kinds of oxygen masks are used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviators" title="Aviators">pilots</a> and crews who fly at high altitudes: continuous flow, diluter demand, and pressure demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAAoxygen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAAoxygen-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a <i>continuous-flow system</i>, oxygen is provided to the user continuously. It does not matter if the user is exhaling or inhaling as oxygen is flowing from the time the system is activated. Below the oxygen mask is a rebreather bag that collects oxygen during exhalation and as a result allows a higher flow rate during the inhalation cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA-H-8083-25A_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-H-8083-25A-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Diluter-demand</i> and <i>pressure-demand</i> masks supply oxygen only when the user inhales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAAdemand_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAAdemand-5">[5]</a></sup> They each require a good seal between the mask and the user’s face.
</p><p>In a <i>diluter-demand system,</i> as the altitude increases (ambient pressure, and therefore the partial pressure of ambient oxygen, decreases), the oxygen flow increases such that the partial pressure of oxygen is roughly constant. Diluter-demand oxygen systems can be used up to 40,000 ft (12,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA-H-8083-25A_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-H-8083-25A-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a <i>pressure-demand system</i>, oxygen in the mask is above ambient pressure, permitting breathing above 40,000 feet (12,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA-H-8083-25A_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAA-H-8083-25A-4">[4]</a></sup> Because the pressure inside the mask is greater than the pressure around the user’s torso, inhalation is easy, but exhalation requires more effort. Aviators are trained in pressure-demand breathing in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_chamber" title="Altitude chamber">altitude chambers</a>. Because they seal tightly, pressure-demand-type oxygen masks are also used in hyperbaric oxygen chambers and for oxygen breathing research projects with standard oxygen regulators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8931286_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8931286-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Supplemental oxygen is needed for flying more than 30 minutes at cabin pressure altitudes of 12,500 feet or higher, pilots must use oxygen at all times above 14,000 feet and each occupant must be provided supplemental oxygen above 15,000 ft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_passenger_masks_and_emergency_oxygen_systems">Aviation passenger masks and emergency oxygen systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aviation passenger masks and emergency oxygen systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Emergency_oxygen_system" title="Emergency oxygen system">Emergency oxygen system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="745" data-file-width="511" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/220px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/330px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/440px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Emergency oxygen masks deployed</div></div></div>
<p>Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_aircraft" title="Commercial aircraft">commercial aircraft</a> are fitted with oxygen masks for use when cabin pressurization fails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid6444812_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid6444812-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1275837_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1275837-8">[8]</a></sup> In general, commercial aircraft are <a href="/wiki/Cabin_pressurization" title="Cabin pressurization">pressurized</a> so that the cabin air is at a pressure equivalent to no more than 8,000 feet (2,400 m) altitude (usually somewhat lower altitude), where one can breathe normally without an oxygen mask. If the oxygen pressure in the cabin drops below a safe level, risking <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>, compartments containing the oxygen masks will open automatically, either above or in front of the passenger and crew seats, and in the lavatories.
</p><p>In the early years of commercial flight, before pressurized cabins were invented, airliner passengers sometimes had to wear oxygen masks during routine flights.
</p>
<h2><span id="Self-contained_breathing_apparatus_.28SCBA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-contained_breathing_apparatus_(SCBA)">Self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">Firefighters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emergency_service" title="Emergency service">emergency service</a> workers use full face masks that provide breathing air as well as eye and face protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16803723_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16803723-9">[9]</a></sup> These masks are typically attached to a tank carried upon the back of the wearer and are called <a href="/wiki/Self-contained_breathing_apparatus" title="Self-contained breathing apparatus">self-contained breathing apparatuses</a> (SCBA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8209837_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8209837-10">[10]</a></sup> Open circuit SCBAs do not normally supply oxygen, as it is not necessary and constitutes a fire hazard. Rebreather SCBAs usually supply oxygen as this is the lightest and most compact option, and uses a simpler mechanism than other types of rebreather.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_masks_for_astronauts">Specialized masks for astronauts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Specialized masks for astronauts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specialized full-face masks that supply oxygen or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_gases" title="Breathing gases">breathing gases</a> are used by <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> to remove nitrogen from their blood before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_walk" title="Space walk">space walks</a> (EVA).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_masks_for_pets">Specialized masks for pets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Specialized masks for pets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specialized snout masks which supply oxygen to revive family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pets" title="Pets">pets</a> have been donated to <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire departments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_delivery_to_divers">Oxygen delivery to divers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Oxygen delivery to divers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Divers only use pure oxygen for <a href="/wiki/Decompression_practice#Accelerated_decompression" title="Decompression practice">accelerated decompression</a>, or from oxygen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rebreathers" title="Rebreathers">rebreathers</a> at shallow depths where the risk of acute <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity" title="Oxygen toxicity">oxygen toxicity</a> is acceptable. Oxygen supply during in-water decompression is via rebreather, <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Demand_valve" title="Diving regulator">open circuit diving regulator</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_mask" title="Full-face mask">full-face mask</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">diving helmet</a> which has been prepared for <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_underwater_diving_terminology#oxygen_service" title="Glossary of underwater diving terminology">oxygen service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid3120386_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid3120386-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Built-in_breathing_system">Built-in breathing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Built-in breathing system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Built-in_breathing_system" title="Built-in breathing system">Built-in breathing system</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_070208-N-8268B-001_Navy_Diver_1st_class_Mike_Barnett_and_Navy_Diver_1st_Class_Chad_Christensen_test_built-in_breathing_masks_inside_a_recompression_chamber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2100" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/US_Navy_070208-N-8268B-001_Navy_Diver_1st_class_Mike_Barnett_and_Navy_Diver_1st_Class_Chad_Christensen_test_built-in_breathing_masks_inside_a_recompression_chamber.jpg/220px-thumbnail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/US_Navy_070208-N-8268B-001_Navy_Diver_1st_class_Mike_Barnett_and_Navy_Diver_1st_Class_Chad_Christensen_test_built-in_breathing_masks_inside_a_recompression_chamber.jpg/330px-thumbnail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/US_Navy_070208-N-8268B-001_Navy_Diver_1st_class_Mike_Barnett_and_Navy_Diver_1st_Class_Chad_Christensen_test_built-in_breathing_masks_inside_a_recompression_chamber.jpg/440px-thumbnail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_070208-N-8268B-001_Navy_Diver_1st_class_Mike_Barnett_and_Navy_Diver_1st_Class_Chad_Christensen_test_built-in_breathing_masks_inside_a_recompression_chamber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US Navy Divers test built-in breathing masks inside a recompression chamber</div></div></div>
<p>Oxygen supply to divers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decompression_chamber" title="Decompression chamber">decompression chambers</a> is preferably through a built-in breathing system, which uses an oxygen mask plumbed into supply and exhaust hoses which supply oxygen from outside the chamber, and discharge the exhaled oxygen-rich gas outside the chamber, using a system equivalent to two <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Demand_valve" title="Diving regulator">demand valves</a>, one upstream of the diver, to supply oxygen on demand, and the other downstream, to exhaust exhaled gas on demand, so that the oxygen partial pressure in the chamber is limited to relatively safe levels. If oxygen masks are used that discharge into the chamber, the chamber air must be replaced frequently to keep the oxygen level within safe operating limits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anesthesia_oxygen_masks">Anesthesia oxygen masks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Anesthesia oxygen masks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anesthesia masks are face masks that are designed to administer <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> gases to a patient through inhalation. Anesthesia masks are either made of anti-static silicone or rubber, as a static electricity spark may ignite some anesthetic gases. They are either black rubber or clear silicone. Anesthesia masks fit over the mouth and nose and have a double hose system. One hose carries inhaled anesthetic gas to the mask and the other brings exhaled anesthetic gas back to the machine. Anesthesia masks have 4 point head strap harnesses to securely fit on the head to hold the mask in place as the anaethesia doctor controls the gases and oxygen inhaled.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Masks_for_high-altitude_climbers">Masks for high-altitude climbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Masks for high-altitude climbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxygen masks are used by <a href="/wiki/Climbing" title="Climbing">climbers</a> of high peaks such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Everest" title="Mt. Everest">Mt. Everest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17173516_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17173516-15">[15]</a></sup> Because of the severe cold and harsh conditions oxygen masks for use at extreme altitude must be robust and effective. The oxygen storage tanks used with the masks (called oxygen bottles) are made of lightweight, high-strength metals and are covered in high-strength fiber such as <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a>. These special oxygen bottles are filled with oxygen at a very high pressure which provides a longer time duration of oxygen for breathing than standard pressure oxygen bottles. These systems are generally only used above 7,000 metres (23,000 ft).
</p><p>In recent years oxygen mask systems for high-altitude climbing which pump oxygen constantly have been increasingly replaced by systems supplying oxygen on demand via <a href="/wiki/Nasal_cannula" title="Nasal cannula">nasal cannulas</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_helmets">Oxygen helmets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Oxygen helmets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxygen helmets are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygen" title="Hyperbaric oxygen">hyperbaric oxygen</a> chambers for oxygen administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8931286_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8931286-1">[1]</a></sup> They are transparent, lightweight plastic helmets with a seal that goes around the wearer's neck that looks like a <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">space suit</a> <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmet</a>. They offer a good visual field. Light weight plastic hoses provide oxygen to the helmet and remove exhaled gas to the outside of the chamber. Oxygen helmets are often preferred for oxygen administration in hyperbaric oxygen chambers for children and patients that are uncomfortable wearing an oxygen mask.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mask_retention_systems">Mask retention systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxygen_mask&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mask retention systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical oxygen masks are held in place by medical personnel or the user by hand, or they may be fitted with a lightweight elastic headband so the mask can be removed quickly. Full-face masks are secured by several straps. Tightly fitting oxygen masks are secured at four points by two head straps. Aviators’ masks are often equipped with “quick don” harnesses that allow those in pressurized aircraft to rapidly don the masks in emergencies. Military aviators’ oxygen masks are secured to flight helmets with quick-release systems.
</p>
