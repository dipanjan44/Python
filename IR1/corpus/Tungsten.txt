<p><b>Tungsten</b>, or <b>wolfram</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>W</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 74. The name <i>tungsten</i> comes from the former Swedish name for the <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a></i>, <i>tung sten</i> or "heavy stone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Tungsten is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_metal" title="Rare metal">rare metal</a> found naturally on Earth almost exclusively combined with other elements in chemical compounds rather than alone. It was identified as a new element in 1781 and first isolated as a metal in 1783. Its important <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> include <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Free_element" title="Free element">free element</a> is remarkable for its robustness, especially the fact that it has the highest <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of all the elements discovered, melting at 3422 °C (6192 °F, 3695 K). It also has the  highest <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>, at 5930 °C (10706 °F, 6203 K)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Its density is 19.3 times that of water, comparable to that of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, and much higher (about 1.7 times) than that of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintith_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-daintith-10">[10]</a></sup> Polycrystalline tungsten is an intrinsically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hard</a> material (under standard conditions, when uncombined), making it difficult to <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">work</a>. However, pure single-crystalline tungsten is more <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductile</a> and can be cut with a hard-steel <a href="/wiki/Hacksaw" title="Hacksaw">hacksaw</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albert_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-albert-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten's many alloys have numerous applications, including incandescent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulb</a> filaments, <a href="/wiki/X-ray_tube" title="X-ray tube">X-ray tubes</a> (as both the filament and target), electrodes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TIG_welding" title="TIG welding">TIG welding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superalloys" title="Superalloys">superalloys</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiation_protection" title="Radiation protection">radiation shielding</a>. Tungsten's hardness and high <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> give it military applications in penetrating <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a>. Tungsten compounds are also often used as industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>.
</p><p>Tungsten is the only metal from the third <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition</a> series that is known to occur in <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a> that are found in a few species of bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. It is the heaviest element known to be essential to any living organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Tungsten interferes with <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> metabolism and is somewhat toxic to animal life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its raw form, tungsten is a hard steel-grey <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that is often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">brittle</a> and hard to <a href="/wiki/Metalworking" title="Metalworking">work</a>. If made very pure, tungsten retains its <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a> (which exceeds that of many steels), and becomes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a> enough that it can be worked easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albert_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-albert-13">[13]</a></sup> It is worked by <a href="/wiki/Forging" title="Forging">forging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drawing_(manufacturing)" title="Drawing (manufacturing)">drawing</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Extrusion" title="Extrusion">extruding</a>. Tungsten objects are also commonly formed by <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a>.
</p><p>Of all metals in pure form, tungsten has the highest <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> (3422 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>, 6192 °<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">F</a>), lowest <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> (at temperatures above 1650 °C, 3000 °F), and the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desu_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-desu-17">[17]</a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> remains solid at higher temperatures than tungsten, carbon <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimes</a> at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> instead of melting, so it has no melting point. Tungsten has the lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a> of any pure metal. The low thermal expansion and high melting point and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of tungsten originate from strong <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> formed between tungsten atoms by the 5d electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
Alloying small quantities of tungsten with <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> greatly increases its <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">toughness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintith_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-daintith-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten exists in two major <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystalline</a> forms: α and β. The former has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure and is the more stable form. The structure of the β phase is called <a href="/wiki/A15_phases" title="A15 phases">A15 cubic</a>; it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a>, but can coexist with the α phase at ambient conditions owing to non-equilibrium synthesis or stabilization by impurities. Contrary to the α phase which crystallizes in isometric grains, the β form exhibits a columnar <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">habit</a>. The α phase has one third of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity" title="Electrical resistivity">electrical resistivity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and a much lower <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconducting transition temperature</a> T<sub>C</sub> relative to the β phase: ca. 0.015 K vs. 1–4 K; mixing the two phases allows obtaining intermediate T<sub>C</sub> values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The T<sub>C</sub> value can also be raised by <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloying</a> tungsten with another metal (e.g. 7.9 K for W-<a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">Tc</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Such tungsten alloys are sometimes used in low-temperature superconducting circuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_tungsten" title="Isotopes of tungsten">Isotopes of tungsten</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring tungsten consists of five <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-lives</a> are so long that they can be considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable</a>. Theoretically, all five can decay into isotopes of element 72 (<a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_emission" title="Alpha emission">alpha emission</a>, but only <sup>180</sup>W has been observed to do so with a half-life of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018180000000000000♠</span>(1.8<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.2)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup></span> years;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> on average, this yields about two alpha decays of <sup>180</sup>W per gram of natural tungsten per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isotopes_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-isotopes-28">[28]</a></sup> The other naturally occurring isotopes have not been observed to decay, constraining their half-lives to be at least 4 × 10<sup>21</sup> years.
</p><p>Another 30 artificial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> of tungsten have been characterized, the most stable of which are <sup>181</sup>W with a half-life of 121.2 days, <sup>185</sup>W with a half-life of 75.1 days, <sup>188</sup>W with a half-life of 69.4 days, <sup>178</sup>W with a half-life of 21.6 days, and <sup>187</sup>W with a half-life of 23.72 h.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isotopes_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-isotopes-28">[28]</a></sup> All of the remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives of less than 3 hours, and most of these have half-lives below 8 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isotopes_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-isotopes-28">[28]</a></sup> Tungsten also has 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta_state" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, the most stable being <sup>179m</sup>W (<i>t</i><sub>1/2</sub> 6.4 minutes).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Elemental tungsten resists <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">attack by oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common formal <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of tungsten is +6, but it exhibits all oxidation states from −2 to +6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emsley_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-emsley-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Tungsten typically combines with oxygen to form the yellow <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_trioxide" title="Tungsten trioxide">tungstic oxide</a>, WO<sub>3</sub>, which dissolves in aqueous alkaline solutions to form tungstate ions, <span class="chemf nowrap">WO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">Tungsten carbides</a> (W<sub>2</sub>C and WC) are produced by heating powdered tungsten with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>. W<sub>2</sub>C is resistant to chemical attack, although it reacts strongly with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_hexachloride" title="Tungsten hexachloride">tungsten hexachloride</a> (WCl<sub>6</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintith_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-daintith-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In aqueous solution, tungstate gives the <a href="/wiki/Heteropoly_acid" title="Heteropoly acid">heteropoly acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">polyoxometalate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> under neutral and acidic conditions. As <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a> is progressively treated with acid, it first yields the soluble, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> "paratungstate A" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">W</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span><span class="chemf nowrap">O</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6–</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">24</sub></span>, which over time converts to the less soluble "paratungstate B" anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">H</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span><span class="chemf nowrap">W</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sub></span><span class="chemf nowrap">O</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10–</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">42</sub></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithBJ_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithBJ-31">[31]</a></sup> Further acidification produces the very soluble metatungstate anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">H</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span><span class="chemf nowrap">W</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sub></span><span class="chemf nowrap">O</span><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6–</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">40</sub></span>, after which equilibrium is reached. The metatungstate ion exists as a symmetric cluster of twelve tungsten-<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Octahedron" title="Octahedron">octahedra</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Keggin_structure" title="Keggin structure">Keggin</a> anion. Many other polyoxometalate anions exist as metastable species. The inclusion of a different atom such as <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> in place of the two central <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogens</a> in metatungstate produces a wide variety of heteropoly acids, such as <a href="/wiki/Phosphotungstic_acid" title="Phosphotungstic acid">phosphotungstic acid</a> H<sub>3</sub>PW<sub>12</sub>O<sub>40</sub>.
</p><p>Tungsten trioxide can form <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(chemistry)" title="Intercalation (chemistry)">intercalation</a> compounds with alkali metals. These are known as <i>bronzes</i>; an example is <a href="/wiki/Sodium_tungsten_bronze" title="Sodium tungsten bronze">sodium tungsten bronze</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1781, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> discovered that a new <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungstic_acid" title="Tungstic acid">tungstic acid</a>, could be made from <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a> (at the time named tungsten).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Scheele and <a href="/wiki/Torbern_Bergman" title="Torbern Bergman">Torbern Bergman</a> suggested that it might be possible to obtain a new metal by reducing this acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaundersN_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-SaundersN-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1783, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Elhuyar" title="Juan José Elhuyar">José</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fausto_Elhuyar" title="Fausto Elhuyar">Fausto Elhuyar</a> found an acid made from <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a> that was identical to tungstic acid. Later that year, at the <a href="/wiki/Real_Sociedad_Bascongada_de_Amigos_del_Pa%C3%ADs" title="Real Sociedad Bascongada de Amigos del País">Royal Basque Society</a> in the town of <a href="/wiki/Bergara" title="Bergara">Bergara</a>, Spain, the brothers succeeded in isolating tungsten by reduction of this acid with <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, and they are credited with the discovery of the element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITIAnews_0605_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITIAnews_0605-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITIAnews_1205_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITIAnews_1205-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The strategic value of tungsten came to notice in the early 20th century. British authorities acted in 1912 to free the Carrock mine from the German owned Cumbrian Mining Company and, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, restrict German access elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, tungsten played a more significant role in background political dealings. Portugal, as the main European source of the element, was put under pressure from both sides, because of its deposits of wolframite ore at <a href="/wiki/Panasqueira" title="Panasqueira">Panasqueira</a>. Tungsten's desirable properties such as resistance to high temperatures, its hardness and density, and its strengthening of alloys made it an important raw material for the arms industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portugal_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-portugal-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> both as a constituent of weapons and equipment and employed in production itself, e.g., in <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a> cutting tools for machining steel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name "tungsten" (from the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> <i>tung sten</i>, "heavy stone") is used in English, French, and many other languages as the name of the element, but not in the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>. Tungsten was the old Swedish name for the mineral <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a>. "Wolfram" (or "volfram") is used in most European (especially Germanic and Slavic) languages and is derived from the mineral <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a>, which is the origin of the chemical symbol <b>W</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albert_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-albert-13">[13]</a></sup> The name "wolframite" is derived from German "<i>wolf rahm</i>" ("wolf soot" or "wolf cream"), the name given to tungsten by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gottschalk_Wallerius" title="Johan Gottschalk Wallerius">Johan Gottschalk Wallerius</a> in 1747. This, in turn, derives from "<i>lupi spuma</i>", the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Agricola" title="Georg Agricola">Georg Agricola</a> used for the element in 1546, which translates into English as "wolf's froth" and is a reference to the large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> consumed by the mineral during its extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sweetums_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-sweetums-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="653" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg/220px-Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg/330px-Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg/440px-Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wolframite_from_Portugal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wolframite mineral, with a scale in cm.</div></div></div>
<p>Tungsten is found mainly in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>–<a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> tungstate (Fe,Mn)WO<sub>4</sub>, which is a solid solution of the two minerals <a href="/wiki/Ferberite" title="Ferberite">ferberite</a> FeWO<sub>4</sub>, and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCbnerite" title="Hübnerite">hübnerite</a> MnWO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> tungstate (CaWO<sub>4</sub>). Other tungsten minerals range in their level of abundance from moderate to very rare, and have almost no economical value.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_compounds">Chemical compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemical compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Tungsten_compounds" title="Category:Tungsten compounds">Category:Tungsten compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Tungsten forms chemical compounds in oxidation states from -II to VI.  Higher oxidation states, always as oxides, are relevant to its terrestrial occurrence and its biological roles, mid-level oxidation states are often associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_cluster" title="Metal cluster">metal clusters</a>, and very low oxidation states are typically associated with <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">CO complexes</a>.  Mo and W chemistry shows strong similarities.  The relative rarity of tungsten(III), for example, contrasts with the pervasiveness of the chromium(III) compounds. The highest oxidation state is seen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten(VI)_oxide" title="Tungsten(VI) oxide">tungsten(VI) oxide</a> (WO<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-41">[41]</a></sup> The trioxide, which is volatile at high temperatures, is the precursor to virtually all other Mo compounds as well as alloys. Molybdenum has several <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a>, the most stable being +4 and +6 (bolded in the table at left).
</p><p>Tungsten(VI) oxide is soluble in aqueous <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> , forming tungstate (WO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>).  This <a href="/wiki/Oxyanion" title="Oxyanion">oxyanion</a>  condenses at lower <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> values, forming <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">polyoxotungstates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:146px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JAFYAQ.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1411" data-file-width="1200" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JAFYAQ.png/144px-JAFYAQ.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JAFYAQ.png/216px-JAFYAQ.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JAFYAQ.png/288px-JAFYAQ.png 2x" width="144"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JAFYAQ.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structure of W<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>18</sub> ("tungsten trichloride").</div></div></div>
<p>The broad range of <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> of tungsten is reflected in it various chlorides:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tungsten(II)_chloride" title="Tungsten(II) chloride">Tungsten(II) chloride</a>, which exists as the hexamer W<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>12</sub></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tungsten(III)_chloride" title="Tungsten(III) chloride">Tungsten(III) chloride</a>, which exists as the hexamer W<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>18</sub></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tungsten(IV)_chloride" title="Tungsten(IV) chloride">Tungsten(IV) chloride</a>, WCl<sub>4</sub>, a black solid, which adopts a polymeric structure.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tungsten(V)_chloride" title="Tungsten(V) chloride">Tungsten(V) chloride</a> WCl<sub>5</sub>, a black solid which adopts a dimeric structure.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten(VI)_chloride" title="Tungsten(VI) chloride">Tungsten(VI) chloride</a> WCl<sub>6</sub>, which contrasts with the instability of MoCl<sub>6</sub>.</li></ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organomolybdenum" title="Organomolybdenum">Organotungsten compounds</a> are numerous and also span a range of oxidation states.  Notable examples include the trigonal prismatic <a href="/wiki/Hexamethyltungsten" title="Hexamethyltungsten">W(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub></a> and octahedral <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_hexacarbonyl" title="Tungsten hexacarbonyl">W(CO)<sub>6</sub></a>.
</p>

<p>There is additional production in the U.S., but the amount is proprietary company information. U.S. reserves are 140,000 tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-45">[45]</a></sup> US industrial use of wolfram is 20,000 tonnes: 15,000 tonnes are imported and the remaining 5,000 tonnes come from domestic recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten is considered to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conflict_mineral" title="Conflict mineral">conflict mineral</a> due to the unethical mining practices observed in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a large deposit of tungsten ore on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, which was exploited during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemerdon_Mine" title="Hemerdon Mine">Hemerdon Mine</a>. With recent increases in tungsten prices, as of 2014 this mine has been reactivated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten is extracted from its ores in several stages. The ore is eventually converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten(VI)_oxide" title="Tungsten(VI) oxide">tungsten(VI) oxide</a> (WO<sub>3</sub>), which is heated with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to produce powdered tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaundersN_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-SaundersN-33">[33]</a></sup> Because of tungsten's high melting point, it is not commercially feasible to cast tungsten <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingots</a>. Instead, powdered tungsten is mixed with small amounts of powdered nickel or other metals, and <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintered</a>.  During the sintering process, the nickel diffuses into the tungsten, producing an alloy.
</p><p>Tungsten can also be extracted by hydrogen reduction of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_hexafluoride" title="Tungsten hexafluoride">WF<sub>6</sub></a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>WF<sub>6</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub> → W + 6 HF</dd></dl>
<p>or <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolytic decomposition</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manny_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-manny-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>WF<sub>6</sub> → W + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_reaction" title="Heat of reaction">Δ<i>H</i><sub>r</sub></a> = +)</dd></dl>
<p>Tungsten is not traded as a futures contract and cannot be tracked on exchanges like the <a href="/wiki/London_Metal_Exchange" title="London Metal Exchange">London Metal Exchange</a>. The prices are usually quoted for tungsten concentrate or WO<sub>3</sub>. If converted to the metal equivalent, they were about US$19 per kilogram in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-production-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Approximately half of the tungsten is consumed for the production of hard materials – namely <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a> – with the remaining major use being in alloys and steels. Less than 10% is used in other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_materials">Hard materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hard materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tungsten is mainly used in the production of hard materials based on tungsten carbide, one of the hardest <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbides</a>, with a melting point of 2770 °C. WC is an efficient <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">electrical conductor</a>, but W<sub>2</sub>C is less so. WC is used to make wear-resistant <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasives</a>, and "carbide" cutting tools such as knives, drills, <a href="/wiki/Circular_saw" title="Circular saw">circular saws</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milling_machine" title="Milling machine">milling</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lathe_(metal)" title="Lathe (metal)">turning</a> tools used by the metalworking, woodworking, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and construction industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daintith_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-daintith-10">[10]</a></sup> Carbide tooling is actually a ceramic/metal composite, where metallic cobalt acts as a binding <a href="/wiki/Metal_matrix_composite" title="Metal matrix composite">(matrix) material</a> to hold the WC particles in place. This type of industrial use accounts for about 60% of current tungsten consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a> industry makes rings of sintered tungsten carbide, tungsten carbide/metal composites, and also metallic tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> WC/metal composite rings use nickel as the metal matrix in place of cobalt because it takes a higher luster when polished. Sometimes manufacturers or retailers refer to tungsten carbide as a metal, but it is a ceramic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Because of tungsten carbide's hardness, rings made of this material are extremely abrasion resistant, and will hold a burnished finish longer than rings made of metallic tungsten. Tungsten carbide rings are brittle, however, and may crack under a sharp blow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alloys">Alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tantalum-tungsten_alloys" title="Tantalum-tungsten alloys">Tantalum-tungsten alloys</a></div>
<p>The hardness and density of tungsten are applied in obtaining heavy metal <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a>. A good example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_speed_steel" title="High speed steel">high speed steel</a>, which can contain as much as 18% tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-w-apps_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-w-apps-56">[56]</a></sup> Tungsten's high melting point makes tungsten a good material for applications like <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_nozzle" title="Rocket engine nozzle">rocket nozzles</a>, for example in the <a href="/wiki/UGM-27_Polaris" title="UGM-27 Polaris">UGM-27 Polaris</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Tungsten alloys are used in a wide range of different applications, including the aerospace and automotive industries and radiation shielding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Superalloy" title="Superalloy">Superalloys</a> containing tungsten, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hastelloy" title="Hastelloy">Hastelloy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stellite" title="Stellite">Stellite</a>, are used in <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> blades and wear-resistant parts and coatings.
</p><p>Quenched (martensitic) tungsten steel (approx. 5.5% to 7.0% W with 0.5% to 0.7% C) was used for making hard permanent magnets, due to its high <a href="/wiki/Remanence" title="Remanence">remanence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a>, as noted by <a href="/wiki/John_Hopkinson" title="John Hopkinson">John Hopkinson</a> (1849 - 1898) as early as 1886.  The magnetic properties of a metal or an alloy are very sensitive to microstructure.  For example, while the element tungsten is not ferromagnetic (but <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> is), when present in steel in these proportions, it stabilizes the <a href="/wiki/Martensite" title="Martensite">martensite</a> phase, which has an enhanced ferromagnetism, as compared to the <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_iron" title="Allotropes of iron">ferrite (iron)</a> phase, due to its greater resistance to magnetic domain wall motion.
</p><p>Tungsten's heat resistance makes it useful in <a href="/wiki/Arc_welding" title="Arc welding">arc welding</a> applications when combined with another highly-conductive metal such as silver or copper.  The silver or copper provides the necessary conductivity and the tungsten allows the welding rod to withstand the high-temperatures of the arc welding environment.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mallory_metal" title="Mallory metal">Mallory metal</a> is proprietary name for an alloy of tungsten, with other metallic elements added to improve machining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armaments">Armaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Armaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tungsten, usually alloyed with <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> to form heavy alloys, is used in <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy_penetrator" title="Kinetic energy penetrator">kinetic energy penetrators</a> as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">depleted uranium</a>, in applications where uranium's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">radioactivity</a> is problematic even in depleted form, or where uranium's additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> properties are not required (for example, in ordinary small arms bullets designed to penetrate body armor). Similarly, tungsten alloys have also been used in cannon shells, <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a>, to create supersonic shrapnel. Germany used tungsten during World War II to produce shells for anti-tank gun designs using the Gerlich <a href="/wiki/Squeeze_bore" title="Squeeze bore">squeeze bore</a> principle to achieve very high muzzle velocity and enhanced armor penetration from comparatively small caliber and light weight field artillery. The weapons were highly effective but a shortage of tungsten used in the shell core limited that effectiveness.
</p><p>Tungsten has also been used in <a href="/wiki/Dense_Inert_Metal_Explosive" title="Dense Inert Metal Explosive">Dense Inert Metal Explosives</a>, which use it as dense powder to reduce collateral damage while increasing the lethality of explosives within a small radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_applications">Chemical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chemical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungsten(IV)_sulfide" title="Tungsten(IV) sulfide">Tungsten(IV) sulfide</a> is a high temperature <a href="/wiki/Lubricant" title="Lubricant">lubricant</a> and is a component of catalysts for <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">hydrodesulfurization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> MoS<sub>2</sub> is more commonly used for such applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> are used in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> glazes and <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> tungstates are used widely in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting">fluorescent lighting</a>. Crystal <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstates</a> are used as <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillation detectors</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">nuclear medicine</a>. Other salts that contain tungsten are used in the chemical and <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a> industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desu_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-desu-17">[17]</a></sup>
Tungsten oxide (WO<sub>3</sub>) is incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">selective catalytic reduction</a> (SCR) catalysts found in coal-fired power plants. These catalysts convert <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (<a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a>) to nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) and water (H<sub>2</sub>O) using ammonia (NH<sub>3</sub>).  The tungsten oxide helps with the physical strength of the catalyst and extends catalyst life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Applications requiring its high density include weights, <a href="/wiki/Mallory_metal" title="Mallory metal">counterweights</a>, ballast keels for yachts, tail ballast for commercial aircraft, and as ballast in race cars for <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a>; <a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">depleted uranium</a> is also used for these purposes, due to similarly high density.  Seventy-five-kg blocks of tungsten were used as "cruise balance mass devices" on the entry vehicle portion of the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> spacecraft.  It is an ideal material to use as a <a href="/wiki/Dolly_(tool)" title="Dolly (tool)">dolly</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riveting" title="Riveting">riveting</a>, where the mass necessary for good results can be achieved in a compact bar. High-density alloys of tungsten with nickel, copper or iron are used in high-quality <a href="/wiki/Darts" title="Darts">darts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> (to allow for a smaller diameter and thus tighter groupings) or for <a href="/wiki/Fishing_lure" title="Fishing lure">fishing lures</a> (tungsten beads allow the fly to sink rapidly). Tungsten has seen use recently in nozzles for <a href="/wiki/3D_printing" title="3D printing">3D printing</a>; the high wear resistance and thermal conductivity of tungsten carbide improves the printing of abrasive filaments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>. Some <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cello</a> C strings are wound with tungsten. The extra density gives this string more projection and often cellists will buy just this string and use it with three strings from a different set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. commercial sales pages such as this may exaggerate to promote their products (October 2017)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> Tungsten is used as an absorber on the electron telescope on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Ray_System" title="Cosmic Ray System">Cosmic Ray System</a> of the two <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sodium_tungstate" title="Sodium tungstate">Sodium tungstate</a> is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folin-Ciocalteu" title="Folin-Ciocalteu">Folin-Ciocalteu</a>'s reagent, a mixture of different chemicals used in the "Lowry Assay" for protein content analysis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gold_substitution">Gold substitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Gold substitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its density, similar to that of gold, allows tungsten to be used in jewelry as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> or <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albert_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-albert-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Metallic tungsten is <a href="/wiki/Hypoallergenic" title="Hypoallergenic">hypoallergenic</a>, and is harder than gold alloys (though not as hard as tungsten carbide), making it useful for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring_(finger)" title="Ring (finger)">rings</a> that will resist scratching, especially in designs with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brushed_finish" title="Brushed finish">brushed finish</a>.
</p><p>Because the density is so similar to that of gold (tungsten is only 0.36% less dense), tungsten can also be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterfeiting" title="Counterfeiting">counterfeiting</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gold_bar" title="Gold bar">gold bars</a>, such as by plating a tungsten bar with gold,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-popsci-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> which has been observed since the 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> or taking an existing gold bar, drilling holes, and replacing the removed gold with tungsten rods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The densities are not exactly the same, and other properties of gold and tungsten differ, but gold-plated tungsten will pass superficial tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-popsci-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold-plated tungsten is available commercially from China (the main source of tungsten), both in jewelry and as bars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because it retains its strength at high temperatures and has a high <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>, elemental tungsten is used in many high-temperature applications,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulb</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube" title="Cathode-ray tube">cathode-ray tube</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tube</a> filaments, <a href="/wiki/Heating_element" title="Heating element">heating elements</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a> nozzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-albert_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-albert-13">[13]</a></sup> Its high melting point also makes tungsten suitable for aerospace and high-temperature uses such as electrical, heating, and welding applications, notably in the <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">gas tungsten arc welding</a> process (also called tungsten inert gas (TIG) welding).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="458" data-file-width="628" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG/220px-TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG/330px-TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG/440px-TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TIG_torch_breakdown.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tungsten electrode used in a <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">Gas tungsten arc welding</a> torch</div></div></div>
<p>Because of its conductive properties and relative chemical inertness, tungsten is also used in <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>, and in the emitter tips in electron-beam instruments that use <a href="/wiki/Field_emission_gun" title="Field emission gun">field emission guns</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopes</a>. In electronics, tungsten is used as an interconnect material in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> material and the transistors. It is used in metallic films, which replace the wiring used in conventional electronics with a coat of tungsten (or <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>) on <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manny_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-manny-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The electronic structure of tungsten makes it one of the main sources for <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> targets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patent_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-patent-77">[77]</a></sup> and also for shielding from high-energy radiations (such as in the <a href="/wiki/Radiopharmaceutical" title="Radiopharmaceutical">radiopharmaceutical</a> industry for shielding radioactive samples of <a href="/wiki/Fludeoxyglucose_(18F)" title="Fludeoxyglucose (18F)">FDG</a>). It is also used in gamma imaging as a material from which coded apertures are made, due to its excellent shielding properties.  Tungsten powder is used as a filler material in <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> composites, which are used as a nontoxic substitute for <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_shot" title="Lead shot">shot</a>, and radiation shields. Since this element's thermal expansion is similar to <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a>, it is used for making glass-to-metal seals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desu_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-desu-17">[17]</a></sup> In addition to its high melting point, when tungsten is doped with potassium, it leads to an increased shape stability (compared to non-doped tungsten). This ensures that the filament does not sag, and no undesired changes occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanowires">Nanowires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Nanowires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through top-down <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanofabrication" title="Nanofabrication">nanofabrication</a> processes, tungsten <a href="/wiki/Nanowire" title="Nanowire">nanowires</a> have been fabricated and studied since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Due to a particularly high surface to volume ratio, the formation of a surface oxide layer and the single crystal nature of such material, the mechanical properties differ fundamentally from those of bulk tungsten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Such tungsten nanowires have potential applications in <a href="/wiki/Nanoelectronics" title="Nanoelectronics">nanoelectronics</a> and importantly as pH probes and gas sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> In similarity to <a href="/wiki/Silicon_nanowire" title="Silicon nanowire">silicon nanowires</a>, tungsten nanowires are frequently produced from a bulk tungsten precursor followed by a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_oxidation" title="Thermal oxidation">thermal oxidation</a> step to control morphology in terms of length and aspect ratio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Using the <a href="/wiki/Deal%E2%80%93Grove_model" title="Deal–Grove model">Deal–Grove model</a> it is possible to predict the oxidation kinetics of nanowires fabricated through such thermal oxidation processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tungsten, at atomic number <i>Z</i> = 74, is the heaviest element known to be biologically functional. It is used by some bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> but not in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> called <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductases</a> use tungsten similarly to <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> by using it in a tungsten-<a href="/wiki/Pterin" title="Pterin">pterin</a> complex with <a href="/wiki/Molybdopterin" title="Molybdopterin">molybdopterin</a> (molybdopterin, despite its name, does not contain molybdenum, but may complex with either molybdenum or tungsten in use by living organisms). Tungsten-using enzymes typically reduce carboxylic acids to aldehydes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tungsten_orgs_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-tungsten_orgs-85">[85]</a></sup> The tungsten <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductases</a> may also catalyse oxidations. The first tungsten-requiring enzyme to be discovered also requires selenium, and in this case the tungsten-selenium pair may function analogously to the molybdenum-sulfur pairing of some <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_cofactor" title="Molybdenum cofactor">molybdenum cofactor</a>-requiring enzymes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> One of the enzymes in the oxidoreductase family which sometimes employ tungsten (bacterial <a href="/wiki/Formate_dehydrogenase" title="Formate dehydrogenase">formate dehydrogenase</a> H) is known to use a selenium-molybdenum version of molybdopterin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Acetylene_hydratase" title="Acetylene hydratase">Acetylene hydratase</a> is an unusual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalloenzyme" title="Metalloenzyme">metalloenzyme</a> in that it catalyzes a hydration reaction. Two reaction mechanisms have been proposed, in one of which there is a direct interaction between the tungsten atom and the C≡C triple bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Although a tungsten-containing <a href="/wiki/Xanthine_dehydrogenase" title="Xanthine dehydrogenase">xanthine dehydrogenase</a> from bacteria has been found to contain tungsten-molydopterin and also non-protein bound selenium, a tungsten-selenium molybdopterin complex has not been definitively described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>In soil, tungsten metal oxidizes to the <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a> anion. It can be selectively or non-selectively imported by some <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotic organisms</a> and may substitute for <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdate</a> in certain <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>. Its effect on the action of these enzymes is in some cases inhibitory and in others positive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> The soil's chemistry determines how the tungsten polymerizes; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> soils cause monomeric tungstates; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> soils cause polymeric tungstates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sodium_tungstate" title="Sodium tungstate">Sodium tungstate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> have been studied for their effect on <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>. Lead was found to be lethal at low levels and sodium tungstate was much less toxic, but the tungstate completely inhibited their <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproductive ability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tungsten has been studied as a biological copper metabolic <a href="/wiki/Receptor_antagonist" title="Receptor antagonist">antagonist</a>, in a role similar to the action of molybdenum. It has been found that tetrathiotungstates may be used as biological copper <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelation</a> chemicals, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Tetrathiomolybdate" title="Tetrathiomolybdate">tetrathiomolybdates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_archaea">In archaea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In archaea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tungsten is essential for some archaea. The following tungsten-utilizing enzymes are known:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aldehyde_ferredoxin_oxidoreductase" title="Aldehyde ferredoxin oxidoreductase">Aldehyde ferredoxin oxidoreductase</a> (AOR) in <i><a href="/wiki/Thermococcus" title="Thermococcus">Thermococcus</a></i> strain ES-1</li>
<li>Formaldehyde ferredoxin oxidoreductase (FOR) in <i><a href="/wiki/Thermococcus_litoralis" title="Thermococcus litoralis">Thermococcus litoralis</a></i></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate_ferredoxin_oxidoreductase" title="Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate ferredoxin oxidoreductase">Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate ferredoxin oxidoreductase</a> (GAPOR) in <i><a href="/wiki/Pyrococcus_furiosus" title="Pyrococcus furiosus">Pyrococcus furiosus</a></i></li></ul>
<p>A <i>wtp</i> system is known to selectively transport tungsten in archaea:
</p>
<ul><li>WtpA is tungten-binding protein of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_transporter" title="ABC transporter">ABC family of transporters</a></li>
<li>WptB is a <a href="/wiki/Permease" title="Permease">permease</a></li>
<li>WtpC is <a href="/wiki/ATPase" title="ATPase">ATPase</a><i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></i></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_factors">Health factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Health factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because tungsten is rare<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and its compounds are generally inert, the effects of tungsten on the environment are limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
It was first believed to be relatively inert and an only slightly toxic metal, beginning in the year 2000, the risk presented by tungsten alloys, its dusts and particulates to induce cancer and several other adverse effects in animals as well as humans has been highlighted from in vitro and in vivo experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> LD<sub>50</sub> depends strongly on the animal and the method of administration and varies between 59 mg/kg (intravenous, rabbits)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> and 5000 mg/kg (tungsten metal powder, <a href="/wiki/Intraperitoneal_injection" title="Intraperitoneal injection">intraperitoneal</a>, rats).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to tungsten in the workplace by breathing it in, swallowing it, skin contact, and eye contact. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday and a short term limit of 10 mg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_claim">Patent claim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Patent claim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tungsten is unique amongst the elements in that it has been the subject of patent proceedings. In 1928, a US court rejected <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>'s attempt to patent it, overturning <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US1082933" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 1,082,933</a></span> granted in 1913 to <a href="/wiki/William_D._Coolidge" title="William D. Coolidge">William D. Coolidge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
