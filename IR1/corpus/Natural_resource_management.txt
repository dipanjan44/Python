<p><b>Natural resource management</b> refers to the management of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Land_(economics)" title="Land (economics)">land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, with a particular focus on how management affects the <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> for both present and future generations (<a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a>).
</p><p>Natural resource management deals with managing the way in which people and natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscapes" title="Landscapes">landscapes</a> interact. It brings together land use planning, water management, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">biodiversity conservation</a>, and the future sustainability of industries like <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>. It recognises that people and their livelihoods rely on the health and productivity of our landscapes, and their actions as stewards of the land play a critical role in maintaining this health and productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural resource management specifically focuses on a scientific and technical understanding of resources and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and the life-supporting capacity of those resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_management" title="Environmental management">Environmental management</a> is also similar to natural resource management. In academic contexts, the <a href="/wiki/Rural_sociology" title="Rural sociology">sociology of natural resources</a> is closely related to, but distinct from, natural resource management.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:137px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="720" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg/135px-US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg/203px-US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg/270px-US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg.png 2x" width="135"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US-DOI-BureauOfLandManagement-Logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">Bureau of Land Management</a> in the United States manages America's <a href="/wiki/Public_land" title="Public land">public lands</a>, totaling approximately 264 million acres (1,070,000 km2) or one-eighth of the landmass of the country.</div></div></div>
<p>The emphasis on sustainability can be traced back to early attempts to understand the ecological nature of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Rangeland" title="Rangeland">rangelands</a> in the late 19th century, and the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">resource conservation</a> movement of the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  This type of analysis coalesced in the 20th century with recognition that preservationist <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">conservation</a> strategies had not been effective in halting the decline of natural resources. A more integrated approach was implemented recognising the intertwined social, cultural, economic and political aspects of resource management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thakadu2005_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thakadu2005-5">[5]</a></sup> A more holistic, national and even global form evolved, from the <a href="/wiki/Brundtland_Commission" title="Brundtland Commission">Brundtland Commission</a> and the advocacy of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.
</p><p>In 2005 the government of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, established a <i>Standard for Quality Natural Resource Management</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup> to improve the consistency of practice, based on an <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">adaptive management</a> approach.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the most active areas of natural resource management are <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_management" title="Wildlife management">wildlife management</a> often associated with <a href="/wiki/Ecotourism" title="Ecotourism">ecotourism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rangeland_management" title="Rangeland management">rangeland management</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, water sharing, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murray_Darling_Basin" title="Murray Darling Basin">Murray Darling Basin Plan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catchment_management" title="Catchment management">catchment management</a> are also significant.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_regimes">Ownership regimes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ownership regimes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural resource management approaches can be categorised according to the kind and right of <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>, natural resources:
</p>
<ul><li>State property:  Ownership and control over the use of resources is in hands of the state. Individuals or groups may be able to make use of the resources, but only at the permission of the state. National forest, National parks and military reservations are some US  examples.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Private property: Any property owned by a defined individual or corporate entity. Both the benefit and duties to the resources fall to the owner(s). Private land is the most common example.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Common property: It is a private property of a group. The group may vary in size, nature and internal structure e.g. indigenous neighbours of village. Some examples of common property are community forests.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Non-property (open access): There is no definite owner of these properties. Each potential user has equal ability to use it as they wish. These areas are the most exploited. It is said that "Everybody's property is nobody's property". An example is a lake fishery. <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">Common land</a> may exist without ownership, in which case in the UK it is vested in a local authority.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Hybrid: Many ownership regimes governing natural resources will contain parts of more than one of the regimes described above, so natural resource managers need to consider the impact of hybrid regimes. An example of such a hybrid is native vegetation management in NSW, Australia, where legislation recognises a public interest in the preservation of native vegetation, but where most native vegetation exists on private land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stakeholder_analysis">Stakeholder analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stakeholder analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_analysis" title="Stakeholder analysis">Stakeholder analysis</a> originated from business management practices and has been incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> management in ever growing popularity. Stakeholder analysis in the context of natural resource management identifies distinctive interest groups affected in the utilisation and conservation of natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dandy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dandy-8">[8]</a></sup>

<p>Therefore, it is dependent upon the circumstances of the stakeholders involved with natural resource as to which definition and subsequent theory is utilised.
</p><p>Billgrena and Holme<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billgrena_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Billgrena-9">[9]</a></sup> identified the aims of stakeholder analysis in natural resource management:
</p>
<ul><li>Identify and categorise the stakeholders that may have influence</li>
<li>Develop an understanding of why changes occur</li>
<li>Establish who can make changes happen</li>
<li>How to best manage natural resources</li></ul>
<p>This gives transparency and clarity to policy making allowing stakeholders to recognise conflicts of interest and facilitate resolutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billgrena_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Billgrena-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup>
There are numerous stakeholder theories such as Mitchell et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> however Grimble<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup> created a framework of stages for a Stakeholder Analysis in natural resource management. Grimble<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup>  designed this framework to ensure that the analysis is specific to the essential aspects of natural resource management.
</p><p><b>Stages in Stakeholder analysis:</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Clarify objectives of the analysis</li>
<li>Place issues in a systems context</li>
<li>Identify decision-makers and stakeholders</li>
<li>Investigate stakeholder interests and agendas</li>
<li>Investigate patterns of inter-action and dependence  (e.g. conflicts and compatibilities, trade-offs and synergies)</li></ol>
<p><b>Application:</b>
</p><p>Grimble and Wellard<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrimbleWellard_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-GrimbleWellard-13">[13]</a></sup> established that Stakeholder analysis in natural resource management is most relevant where issued can be characterised as;
</p>
<ul><li>Cross-cutting systems and stakeholder interests</li>
<li>Multiple uses and users of the resource.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">Market failure</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Subtractability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Subtractability (page does not exist)">Subtractability</a> and temporal trade-offs</li>
<li>Unclear or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-access" title="Open-access">open-access</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_rights" title="Property rights">property rights</a></li>
<li>Untraded products and services</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">Poverty</a> and under-representation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrimbleWellard_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-GrimbleWellard-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><b>Case studies:</b>
</p><p>In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Bwindi_Impenetrable_National_Park" title="Bwindi Impenetrable National Park">Bwindi Impenetrable National Park</a>, a comprehensive stakeholder analysis would have been relevant and the Batwa people would have potentially been acknowledged as stakeholders preventing the loss of people's livelihoods and loss of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrimbleWellard_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-GrimbleWellard-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimble_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Grimble-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nepal, Indonesia and Koreas' <a href="/wiki/Community_forestry#5Best_Practices" title="Community forestry">community forestry</a> are successful examples of how stakeholder analysis can be incorporated into the management of natural resources. This allowed the stakeholders to identify their needs and level of involvement with the forests.
</p><p><b>Criticisms:</b>
</p>
<ul><li>Natural resource management stakeholder analysis tends to include too many stakeholders which can create problems in of its self as suggested by Clarkson. ‘‘Stakeholder theory should not be used to weave a basket big enough to hold the world's misery.’’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Starik<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> proposed that nature needs to be represented as stakeholder. However this has been rejected by many scholars as it would be difficult to find appropriate representation and this representation could also be disputed by other stakeholders causing further issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billgrena_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Billgrena-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Stakeholder analysis can be used exploited and abused in order to marginalise other stakeholders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dandy_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dandy-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Identifying the relevant stakeholders for participatory processes is complex as certain stakeholder groups may have been excluded from previous decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prell_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prell-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>On-going conflicts and lack of trust between stakeholders can prevent compromise and resolutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prell_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prell-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><b>Alternatives/ Complementary forms of analysis:</b>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Social_network_analysis" title="Social network analysis">Social network analysis</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_pool_resource" title="Common pool resource">Common pool resource</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_of_the_resources">Management of the resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Management of the resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural resource management issues are inherently complex. They involve the ecological cycles, hydrological cycles, climate, animals, plants and geography, etc. All these are dynamic and inter-related. A change in one of them may have far reaching and/or long term impacts which may even be irreversible. In addition to the natural systems, natural resource management also has to manage various stakeholders and their interests, policies, politics, geographical boundaries, economic implications and the list goes on. It is a very difficult to satisfy all aspects at the same time. This results in conflicting situations.
</p><p>After the United Nations Conference for the Environment and Development (UNCED) held in Rio de Janeiro in 1992, most nations subscribed to new principles for the integrated management of land, water, and forests. Although program names vary from nation to nation, all express similar aims.
</p><p>The various approaches applied to natural resource management include:
</p>
<ul><li>Top-down (command and control)</li>
<li>Community-based natural resource management</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptive" title="Adaptive">Adaptive</a> management</li>
<li>Precautionary approach</li>
<li>Integrated natural resource management</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community-based_natural_resource_management">Community-based natural resource management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Community-based natural resource management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The community-based natural resource management (CBNRM) approach combines conservation objectives with the generation of economic benefits for rural communities. The three key assumptions being that: locals are better placed to conserve natural resources, people will conserve a resource only if benefits exceed the costs of conservation, and people will conserve a resource that is linked directly to their quality of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thakadu2005_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thakadu2005-5">[5]</a></sup> When a local people's quality of life is enhanced, their efforts and commitment to ensure the future well-being of the resource are also enhanced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Regional and community based natural resource management is also based on the principle of <a href="/wiki/Subsidiarity" title="Subsidiarity">subsidiarity</a>.
</p><p>The United Nations advocates CBNRM in the Convention on Biodiversity and the Convention to Combat Desertification. Unless clearly defined, decentralised NRM can result an ambiguous socio-legal environment with local communities racing to exploit natural resources while they can e.g. forest communities in central Kalimantan (Indonesia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>A problem of CBNRM is the difficulty of reconciling and harmonising the objectives of socioeconomic development, biodiversity protection and sustainable resource utilisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The concept and conflicting interests of CBNRM,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> show how the motives behind the participation are differentiated as either people-centred (active or participatory results that are truly empowering)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> or planner-centred (nominal and results in passive recipients). Understanding power relations is crucial to the success of community based NRM. Locals may be reluctant to challenge government recommendations for fear of losing promised benefits.
</p><p>CBNRM is based particularly on advocacy by nongovernmental organizations working with local groups and communities, on the one hand, and national and transnational organizations, on the other, to build and extend new versions of environmental and social advocacy that link social justice and environmental management agendas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> with both direct and indirect benefits observed including a share of revenues, employment, diversification of livelihoods and increased pride and identity. Ecological and societal successes and failures of CBNRM projects have been documented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> CBNRM has raised new challenges, as concepts of community, territory, conservation, and indigenous are worked into politically varied plans and programs in disparate sites. Warner and Jones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> address strategies for effectively managing conflict in CBNRM.
</p><p>The capacity of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">indigenous</a> communities to conserve natural resources has been acknowledged by the Australian Government with the Caring for Country<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Program. Caring for our Country is an Australian Government initiative jointly administered by the Australian Government Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry and the Department of the Environment, Water, Heritage and the Arts. These Departments share responsibility for delivery of the Australian Government's environment and sustainable agriculture programs, which have traditionally been broadly referred to under the banner of ‘natural resource management’. These programs have been delivered regionally, through 56 State government bodies, successfully allowing regional communities to decide the natural resource priorities for their regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>More broadly, a research study based in Tanzania and the Pacific researched what motivates communities to adopt CBNRM's and found that aspects of the specific CBNRM program, of the community that has adopted the program, and of the broader social-ecological context together shape the why CBNRM's are adopted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup>.  However, overall, program adoption seemed to mirror the relative advantage of CBNRM programs to local villagers and villager access to external technical assistance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup>. There have been socioeconomic critiques of CBNRM in Africa,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> but ecological effectiveness of CBNRM measured by wildlife population densities has been shown repeatedly in Tanzania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Governance is seen as a key consideration for delivering community-based or regional natural resource management. In the State of NSW, the 13 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Catchment_Management_Authority_of_(New_South_Wales)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Catchment Management Authority of (New South Wales) (page does not exist)">catchment management authorities</a> (CMAs) are overseen by the <a class="external text" href="http://nrc.nsw.gov.au/" rel="nofollow">Natural Resources Commission</a> (NRC), responsible for undertaking audits of the effectiveness of regional natural resource management programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptive_management">Adaptive management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adaptive management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The primary methodological approach adopted by <a href="/wiki/Catchment_Management_Authority_(New_South_Wales)" title="Catchment Management Authority (New South Wales)">catchment management authorities</a> (CMAs) for regional natural resource management in Australia is <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">adaptive management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>This approach includes recognition that adaption occurs through a process of ‘plan-do-review-act’. It also recognises seven key components that should be considered for quality natural resource management practice:
</p>
<ul><li>Determination of scale</li>
<li>Collection and use of knowledge</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_management" title="Information management">Information management</a></li>
<li>Monitoring and evaluation</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">Risk management</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Community_engagement" title="Community engagement">Community engagement</a></li>
<li>Opportunities for <a href="/wiki/Collaboration" title="Collaboration">collaboration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Integrated_natural_resource_management">Integrated natural resource management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Integrated natural resource management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Integrated natural resource management (INRM) is a process of managing natural resources in a systematic way, which includes multiple aspects of natural resource use (biophysical, socio-political, and economic) meet production goals of producers and other direct users (e.g., food security, profitability, risk aversion) as well as goals of the wider community (e.g., poverty alleviation, welfare of future generations, environmental conservation). It focuses on sustainability and at the same time tries to incorporate all possible stakeholders from the planning level itself, reducing possible future conflicts. The conceptual basis of INRM has evolved in recent years through the convergence of research in diverse areas such as sustainable land use, participatory planning, integrated watershed management, and adaptive management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conservation_Ecology_52:_25_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conservation_Ecology_52:_25-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holling_C.S_pages_328_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holling_C.S_pages_328-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holling_C.S_pages_328_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holling_C.S_pages_328-40">[40]</a></sup> INRM is being used extensively and been successful in regional and community based natural management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frameworks_and_modelling">Frameworks and modelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Frameworks and modelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various frameworks and computer models developed to assist natural resource management.
</p><p><b>Geographic Information Systems (GIS)</b>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GIS" title="GIS">GIS</a> is a powerful analytical tool as it is capable of overlaying datasets to identify links. A bush regeneration scheme can be informed by the overlay of rainfall, cleared land and erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In Australia, Metadata Directories such as NDAR provide data on Australian natural resources such as vegetation, fisheries, soils and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> These are limited by the potential for subjective input and data manipulation.
</p><p><b>Natural Resources Management Audit Frameworks</b>
</p><p>The NSW Government in Australia has published an audit framework<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> for natural resource management, to assist the establishment of a performance audit role in the governance of regional natural resource management. This audit framework builds from other established audit methodologies, including <a href="/wiki/Performance_audit" title="Performance audit">performance audit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_audit" title="Environmental audit">environmental audit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internal_audit" title="Internal audit">internal audit</a>. Audits undertaken using this framework have provided confidence to stakeholders, identified areas for improvement and described policy expectations for the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Australian Government has established a framework for auditing greenhouse emissions and energy reporting, which closely follows Australian Standards for Assurance Engagements.
</p><p>The Australian Government is also currently preparing an audit framework for auditing water management, focussing on the implementation of the Murray Darling Basin Plan.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_elements">Other elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Biodiversity Conservation</dt></dl>
<p>The issue of biodiversity conservation is regarded as an important element in natural resource management. What is biodiversity? Biodiversity is a comprehensive concept, which is a description of the extent of natural diversity. Gaston and Spicer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> (p. 3) point out that biodiversity is "the variety of life" and relate to different kinds of "biodiversity organization". According to Gray<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> (p. 154), the first widespread use of the definition of biodiversity, was put forward by the United Nations in 1992, involving different aspects of biological diversity.
</p>
<dl><dt>Precautionary Biodiversity Management</dt></dl>
<p>The "threats" wreaking havoc on biodiversity include; <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a>, putting a strain on the already stretched biological resources; forest deterioration and deforestation; the invasion of "alien species" and "climate change"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>( p. 2). Since these threats have received increasing attention from environmentalists and the public, the precautionary management of biodiversity becomes an important part of natural resources management. According to Cooney, there are material measures to carry out precautionary management of biodiversity in natural resource management.
</p>
<dl><dt>Concrete "policy tools"</dt></dl>
<p>Cooney claims that the policy making is dependent on "evidences", relating to "high standard of proof", the forbidding of special "activities" and "information and monitoring requirements". Before making the policy of precaution, categorical evidence is needed. When the potential menace of "activities" is regarded as a critical and "irreversible" endangerment, these "activities" should be forbidden. For example, since explosives and toxicants will have serious consequences to endanger human and natural environment, the South Africa Marine Living Resources Act promulgated a series of policies on completely forbidding to "catch fish" by using explosives and toxicants.
</p>
<dl><dt>Administration and guidelines</dt></dl>
<p>According to Cooney, there are 4 methods to manage the precaution of biodiversity in natural resources management;
</p>
<ol><li>"<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem-based_management" title="Ecosystem-based management">Ecosystem-based management</a>" including "more risk-averse and precautionary management", where "given prevailing uncertainty regarding ecosystem structure, function, and inter-specific interactions, precaution demands an ecosystem rather than single-species approach to management".</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Adaptive_management" title="Adaptive management">Adaptive management</a>" is "a management approach that expressly tackles the uncertainty and dynamism of complex systems".</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">Environmental impact assessment</a>" and exposure ratings decrease the "uncertainties" of precaution, even though it has deficiencies, and</li>
<li>"Protectionist approaches", which "most frequently links to" biodiversity conservation in natural resources management.</li></ol>
<dl><dt>Land management</dt></dl>
<p>In order to have a sustainable environment, understanding and using appropriate management strategies is important. In terms of understanding, Young<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> emphasises some important points of land management:
</p>
<ul><li>Comprehending the processes of nature including ecosystem, water, soils</li>
<li>Using appropriate and adapting management systems in local situations</li>
<li>Cooperation between scientists who have knowledge and resources and local people who have knowledge and skills</li></ul>
<p>Dale et al. (2000)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> study has shown that there are five fundamental and helpful ecological principles for the land manager and people who need them. The ecological principles relate to time, place, species, disturbance and the landscape and they interact in many ways.It is suggested that land managers could follow these guidelines:
</p>
<ul><li>Examine impacts of local decisions in a regional context, and the effects on natural resources.</li>
<li>Plan for long-term change and unexpected events.</li>
<li>Preserve rare landscape elements and associated species.</li>
<li>Avoid land uses that deplete natural resources.</li>
<li>Retain large contiguous or connected areas that contain critical habitats.</li>
<li>Minimize the introduction and spread of non-native species.</li>
<li>Avoid or compensate for the effects of development on ecological processes.</li>
<li>Implement land-use and land-management practices that are compatible with the natural potential of the area.</li></ul>
