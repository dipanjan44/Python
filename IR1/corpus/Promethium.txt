<p><b>Promethium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Pm</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 61. All of its <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> are <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive</a>; it is extremely rare, with only about 500-600 grams naturally occurring in Earth's crust at any given time, and one of only two such elements that are followed in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> by elements with stable forms, a distinction shared with <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>. Chemically, promethium is a <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a>. Promethium shows only one stable oxidation state of +3.
</p><p>In 1902 <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Brauner" title="Bohuslav Brauner">Bohuslav Brauner</a> suggested that there was a then-unknown element with properties intermediate between those of the known elements <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> (60) and <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> (62); this was confirmed in 1914 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> who, having measured the atomic numbers of all the elements then known, found that atomic number 61 was missing. In 1926, two groups (one Italian and one American) claimed to have isolated a sample of element 61; both "discoveries" were soon proven to be false. In 1938, during a nuclear experiment conducted at <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>, a few radioactive nuclides were produced that certainly were not radioisotopes of neodymium or samarium, but there was a lack of chemical proof that element 61 was produced, and the discovery was not generally recognized. Promethium was first produced and characterized at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> in 1945 by the separation and analysis of the fission products of uranium fuel irradiated in a graphite reactor. The discoverers proposed the name "prometheum" (the spelling was subsequently changed), derived from <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>, the Titan in Greek mythology who stole fire from Mount Olympus and brought it down to humans, to symbolize "both the daring and the possible misuse of mankind's intellect". However, a sample of the metal was made only in 1963.
</p><p>There are two possible sources for natural promethium: rare <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">decays</a> of natural <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>-151 (producing promethium-147), and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> (various isotopes). Practical applications exist only for chemical compounds of promethium-147, which are used in <a href="/wiki/Luminous_paint" title="Luminous paint">luminous paint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_battery" title="Atomic battery">atomic batteries</a> and thickness measurement devices, even though promethium-145 is the most stable promethium isotope. Because natural promethium is exceedingly scarce, it is typically synthesized by bombarding uranium-235 (<a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enriched uranium</a>) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutrons</a> to produce promethium-147 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_product" title="Fission product">fission product</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A promethium atom has 61 electrons, arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">configuration</a> [<a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">Xe</a>]4f<sup>5</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton-3">[3]</a></sup> In forming compounds, the atom loses its two outermost electrons and one of the 4f-electrons, which belongs to an open subshell. The element's atomic radius is the second largest among all the lanthanides but is only slightly greater than those of the neighboring elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the most notable exception to the general trend of the contraction of lanthanide atoms with the increase of their atomic numbers. (see <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>).
Many properties of promethium rely on its position among lanthanides and are intermediate between those of neodymium and samarium. For example, the melting point, the first three ionization energies, and the hydration energy are greater than those of neodymium and lower than those of samarium;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton-3">[3]</a></sup> similarly, the estimate for the boiling point, ionic (Pm<sup>3+</sup>) radius, and standard heat of formation of monatomic gas are greater than those of samarium and less than those of neodymium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Promethium has a <a href="/wiki/Close-packing_of_equal_spheres" title="Close-packing of equal spheres">double hexagonal close packed</a> (dhcp) structure and a hardness of 63 kg/mm<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-str_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-str-5">[5]</a></sup> This low-temperature alpha form converts into a beta, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">body-centered cubic</a> (bcc) phase upon heating to 890 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCrare_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRCrare-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties_and_compounds">Chemical properties and compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties and compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Promethium belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerium_group" title="Cerium group">cerium group</a> of lanthanides and is chemically very similar to the neighboring elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966120_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966120-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of its instability, chemical studies of promethium are incomplete. Even though a few compounds have been synthesized, they are not fully studied; in general, they tend to be pink or red in color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-9">[9]</a></sup> Treatment of acidic solutions containing Pm<sup>3+</sup> ions with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> results in a gelatinous light-brown sediment of hydroxide, Pm(OH)<sub>3</sub>, which is insoluble in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121-10">[10]</a></sup> When dissolved in hydrochloric acid, a water-soluble yellow salt, PmCl<sub>3</sub>, is produced;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121-10">[10]</a></sup> similarly, when dissolved in nitric acid, a nitrate results, Pm(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>. The latter is also well-soluble; when dried, it forms pink crystals, similar to Nd(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121-10">[10]</a></sup> The electron configuration for Pm<sup>3+</sup> is [Xe] 4f<sup>4</sup>, and the color of the ion is pink. The ground state term symbol is <sup>5</sup>I<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The sulfate is slightly soluble, like the other cerium group sulfates. Cell parameters have been calculated for its octahydrate; they lead to conclusion that the density of Pm<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·8 H<sub>2</sub>O is 2.86 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966122_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966122-12">[12]</a></sup> The oxalate, Pm<sub>2</sub>(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·10 H<sub>2</sub>O, has the lowest solubility of all lanthanide oxalates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the nitrate, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promethium_oxide" title="Promethium oxide">oxide</a> is similar to the corresponding samarium salt and not the neodymium salt. As-synthesized, e.g. by heating the oxalate, it is a white or lavender-colored powder with disordered structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966121-10">[10]</a></sup> This powder crystallizes in a cubic lattice upon heating to 600 °C. Further annealing at 800 °C and then at 1750 °C irreversibly transforms it to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_system" title="Hexagonal crystal system">hexagonal</a> phases, respectively, and the last two phases can be interconverted by adjusting the annealing time and temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PmO_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-PmO-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Promethium forms only one stable oxidation state, +3, in the form of ions; this is in line with other lanthanides. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_promethium" title="Isotopes of promethium">Isotopes of promethium</a></div>
<p>Promethium is the only <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> and one of only two elements among the first 83 that has no stable (or even long-lived) isotopes. This is a result of a <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_technetium#Stability_of_technetium_isotopes" title="Isotopes of technetium">rarely occurring effect</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_drop_model" title="Liquid drop model">liquid drop model</a> and stabilities of neighbor element isotopes; it is also the least stable element of the first 84.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-c-16">[16]</a></sup> The primary decay products are <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a> isotopes (promethium-146 decays to both, the lighter isotopes generally to neodymium via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positron_decay" title="Positron decay">positron decay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, and the heavier isotopes to samarium via beta decay). Promethium <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a> may decay to other promethium isotopes and one isotope (<sup>145</sup>Pm) has a very rare alpha decay mode to stable <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">praseodymium</a>-141.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-c-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most stable isotope of the element is promethium-145, which has a specific activity of 940 <a href="/wiki/Curie" title="Curie">Ci</a>/<a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> (35 <a href="/wiki/Becquerel" title="Becquerel">TBq</a>/g) and a half-life of 17.7 years via <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-c-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup> Because it has 84 neutrons (two more than 82, which is a <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(physics)" title="Magic number (physics)">magic number</a> which corresponds to a stable neutron configuration), it may emit an <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particle</a> (which has 2 neutrons) to form praseodymium-141 with 82 neutrons. Thus it is the only promethium isotope with an experimentally observed <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966114_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966114-18">[18]</a></sup> Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_half-life" title="Partial half-life">partial half-life</a> for alpha decay is about 6.3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">9</span></sup> years, and the relative probability for a <sup>145</sup>Pm nucleus to decay in this way is 2.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">7</span></sup> %. Several other promethium isotopes (<sup>144</sup>Pm, <sup>146</sup>Pm, <sup>147</sup>Pm etc.) also have a positive energy release for alpha decay; their alpha decays are predicted to occur but have not been observed.
</p><p>The element also has 18 nuclear isomers, with <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass numbers</a> of 133 to 142, 144, 148, 149, 152, and 154 (some mass numbers have more than one isomer). The most stable of them is promethium-148m, with a half-life of 43.1 days; this is longer than the half-lives of the ground states of all promethium isotopes, except for promethium-143 to 147. In fact, promethium-148m has a longer half-life than its ground state, promethium-148.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-c-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1947" data-file-width="2617" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG/220px-Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG/330px-Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG/440px-Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pitchblende_schlema-alberoda.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Uraninite" title="Uraninite">Uraninite</a>, a uranium ore and the host for most of Earth's promethium</div></div></div>
<p>In 1934, <a href="/wiki/Willard_Libby" title="Willard Libby">Willard Libby</a> reported that he had found weak beta activity in pure neodymium, which was attributed to a half-life over 10<sup>12</sup> years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117-19">[19]</a></sup> Almost 20 years later, it was claimed that the element occurs in natural neodymium in equilibrium in quantities below 10<sup>−20</sup> grams of promethium per one gram of neodymium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117-19">[19]</a></sup> However, these observations were disproved by newer investigations, because for all seven naturally occurring neodymium isotopes, any single beta decays (which can produce promethium isotopes) are forbidden by energy conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In particular, careful measurements of atomic masses show that the mass difference <sup>150</sup>Nd-<sup>150</sup>Pm is negative (−87 keV), which absolutely prevents the single beta decay of <sup>150</sup>Nd to <sup>150</sup>Pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1965 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Olavi_Er%C3%A4mets%C3%A4&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Olavi Erämetsä (page does not exist)">Olavi Erämetsä</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fi.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olavi_Er%C3%A4mets%C3%A4" title="fi:Olavi Erämetsä">fi</a>)</span> separated out traces of <sup>145</sup>Pm from a rare earth concentrate purified from <a href="/wiki/Apatite" title="Apatite">apatite</a>, resulting in an upper limit of 10<sup>−21</sup> for the abundance of promethium in nature; this may have been produced by the natural nuclear fission of uranium, or by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation" title="Cosmic ray spallation">cosmic ray spallation</a> of <sup>146</sup>Nd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both isotopes of natural europium have larger <a href="/wiki/Mass_excess" title="Mass excess">mass excesses</a> than sums of those of their potential alpha daughters plus that of an alpha particle; therefore, they (stable in practice) may alpha decay to promethium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mammamia_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mammamia-23">[23]</a></sup> Research at <a href="/wiki/Laboratori_Nazionali_del_Gran_Sasso" title="Laboratori Nazionali del Gran Sasso">Laboratori Nazionali del Gran Sasso</a> showed that europium-151 experimentally decays to promethium-147 with the half-life of 5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup> years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mammamia_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mammamia-23">[23]</a></sup> It has been shown that europium is "responsible" for about 12 grams of promethium in the Earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mammamia_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mammamia-23">[23]</a></sup> Alpha decays for europium-153 have not been found yet, and its theoretically calculated half-life is so high (due to low energy of decay) that this process will probably not be observed in the near future.
</p><p>Finally, promethium can be formed in nature as a product of <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_fission" title="Spontaneous fission">spontaneous fission</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">uranium-238</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966117-19">[19]</a></sup> Only trace amounts can be found in naturally occurring ores: a sample of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitchblende" title="Pitchblende">pitchblende</a> has been found to contain promethium at a concentration of four parts per quintillion (4<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup>) by mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Uranium is thus "responsible" for 560 g of promethium in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mammamia_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mammamia-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Promethium has also been identified in the spectrum of the star <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HR_465" title="HR 465">HR 465</a> in <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_(constellation)" title="Andromeda (constellation)">Andromeda</a>; it also has been found in HD 101065 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Przybylski%27s_star" title="Przybylski's star">Przybylski's star</a>) and HD 965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Because of the short half-life of promethium isotopes, they should be formed near the surface of those stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Searches_for_element_61">Searches for element 61</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Searches for element 61">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1902, Czech chemist <a href="/wiki/Bohuslav_Brauner" title="Bohuslav Brauner">Bohuslav Brauner</a> found out that the differences in properties between neodymium and samarium were the largest between any two consecutive lanthanides in the sequence then known; as a conclusion, he suggested there was an element with intermediate properties between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61a_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-61a-26">[26]</a></sup> This prediction was supported in 1914 by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moseley" title="Henry Moseley">Henry Moseley</a> who, having discovered that <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> was an experimentally measurable property of elements, found that a few atomic numbers had no known corresponding elements: the gaps were 43, 61, 72, 75, 85, and 87.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> With the knowledge of a gap in the periodic table several groups started to search for the predicted element among other rare earths in the natural environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first claim of a discovery was published by Luigi Rolla and Lorenzo Fernandes of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, Italy. After separating a mixture of a few rare earth elements nitrate concentrate from the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> mineral <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> by fractionated crystallization, they yielded a solution containing mostly samarium. This solution gave x-ray spectra attributed to samarium and element 61. In honor of their city, they named element 61 "florentium". The results were published in 1926, but the scientists claimed that the experiments were done in 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Also in 1926, a group of scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign</a>, Smith Hopkins and Len Yntema published the discovery of element 61. They named it "illinium," after the university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Both of these reported discoveries were shown to be erroneous because the spectrum line that "corresponded" to element 61 was identical to that of <a href="/wiki/Didymium" title="Didymium">didymium</a>; the lines thought to belong to element 61 turned out to belong to a few impurities (barium, chromium, and platinum).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1934, <a href="/wiki/Josef_Mattauch" title="Josef Mattauch">Josef Mattauch</a> finally formulated the <a href="/wiki/Mattauch_isobar_rule" title="Mattauch isobar rule">isobar rule</a>. One of the indirect consequences of this rule was that element 61 was unable to form stable isotopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966108-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rare-earth-handbook_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-rare-earth-handbook-38">[38]</a></sup> From 1938, a nuclear experiment was conducted by H. B. Law <i>et al.</i> at <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>. Nuclides were produced in 1941 which were not radioisotopes of neodymium or samarium, and the name "cyclonium" was proposed, but there was a lack of chemical proof that element 61 was produced and the discovery not largely recognized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_synthesis_of_promethium_metal">Discovery and synthesis of promethium metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discovery and synthesis of promethium metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Promethium was first produced and characterized at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> (Clinton Laboratories at that time) in 1945 by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_A._Marinsky" title="Jacob A. Marinsky">Jacob A. Marinsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_E._Glendenin" title="Lawrence E. Glendenin">Lawrence E. Glendenin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_D._Coryell" title="Charles D. Coryell">Charles D. Coryell</a> by separation and analysis of the fission products of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> fuel irradiated in the <a href="/wiki/X-10_Graphite_Reactor" title="X-10 Graphite Reactor">graphite reactor</a>; however, being too busy with military-related research during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, they did not announce their discovery until 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The original proposed name was "clintonium", after the laboratory where the work was conducted; however, the name "prometheum" was suggested by Grace Mary Coryell, the wife of one of the discoverers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> It is derived from <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>, the Titan in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> who stole fire from Mount Olympus and brought it down to humans<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> and symbolizes "both the daring and the possible misuse of the mankind intellect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The spelling was then changed to "promethium," as this was in accordance with most other metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1963, promethium(III) fluoride was used to make promethium metal. Provisionally purified from impurities of samarium, neodymium, and americium, it was put into a <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> crucible which was located in another tantalum crucible; the outer crucible contained lithium metal (10 times excess compared to promethium).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123-13">[13]</a></sup> After creating a vacuum, the chemicals were mixed to produce promethium metal:
</p>
<dl><dd>PmF<sub>3</sub> + 3 Li → Pm + 3 LiF</dd></dl>
<p>The promethium sample produced was used to measure a few of the metal's properties, such as its <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966123-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1963, ion-exchange methods were used at ORNL to prepare about ten grams of promethium from nuclear reactor fuel processing wastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, promethium is still recovered from the byproducts of uranium fission; it can also be produced by bombarding <sup>146</sup>Nd with <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, turning it into <sup>147</sup>Nd which decays into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promethium-147" title="Promethium-147"><sup>147</sup>Pm</a> through beta decay with a half-life of 11 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The production methods for different isotopes vary, and only those for promethium-147 are given because it is the only isotope with industrial applications. Promethium-147 is produced in large quantities (compared to other isotopes) by bombarding uranium-235 with thermal neutrons. The output is relatively high, at 2.6% of the total product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966115_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966115-47">[47]</a></sup> Another way to produce promethium-147 is via neodymium-147, which decays to promethium-147 with a short half-life. Neodymium-147 can be obtained either by bombarding enriched neodymium-146 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutrons</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russia_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russia-48">[48]</a></sup> or by bombarding a <a href="/wiki/Uranium_carbide" title="Uranium carbide">uranium carbide</a> target with energetic protons in a particle accelerator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Another method is to bombard uranium-238 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_neutron" title="Fast neutron">fast neutrons</a> to cause <a href="/wiki/Fast_fission" title="Fast fission">fast fission</a>, which, among multiple reaction products, creates promethium-147.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De2001_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-De2001-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>As early as the 1960s, Oak Ridge National Laboratory could produce 650 grams of promethium per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966116_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966116-51">[51]</a></sup> and was the world's only large-volume synthesis facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Gram-scale production of promethium has been discontinued in the U.S. in the early 1980s, but will possibly be resumed after 2010 at the <a href="/wiki/High_Flux_Isotope_Reactor" title="High Flux Isotope Reactor">High Flux Isotope Reactor</a>. Currently, Russia is the only country producing promethium-147 on a relatively large scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russia_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russia-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pm,61.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="228" data-file-width="228" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5d/Pm%2C61.jpg/200px-Pm%2C61.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5d/Pm%2C61.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pm,61.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Promethium(III) chloride being used as a light source for signals in a heat button</div></div></div>
<p>Most promethium is used only for research purposes, except for promethium-147, which can be found outside laboratories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> It is obtained as the oxide or chloride,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118-53">[53]</a></sup> in milligram quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> This isotope does not emit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a>, and its radiation has a relatively small penetration depth in matter and a relatively long half-life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some signal lights use a <a href="/wiki/Luminous_paint" title="Luminous paint">luminous paint</a>, containing a <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphor</a> that absorbs the beta radiation emitted by promethium-147 and emits light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> This isotope does not cause aging of the phosphor, as alpha emitters do,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118-53">[53]</a></sup> and therefore the light emission is stable for a few years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELavrukhinaPozdnyakov1966118-53">[53]</a></sup> Originally, <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>-226 was used for the purpose, but it was later replaced by promethium-147 and <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> (hydrogen-3).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Promethium may be favored over tritium for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_safety" title="Nuclear safety">nuclear safety reasons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deeter1993_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deeter1993-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Atomic_battery" title="Atomic battery">atomic batteries</a>, the beta particles emitted by promethium-147 are converted into electric current by sandwiching a small promethium source between two semiconductor plates. These batteries have a useful lifetime of about five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428_39-7"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011428-39">[39]</a></sup> The first promethium-based battery was assembled in 1964 and generated "a few milliwatts of power from a volume of about 2 cubic inches, including shielding".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Promethium is also used to measure the thickness of materials by evaluating the amount of radiation from a promethium source that passes through the sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRCel_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-CRCel-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEmsley2011429-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> It has possible future uses in portable X-ray sources, and as auxiliary heat or power sources for space probes and satellites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stwertka2002_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stwertka2002-58">[58]</a></sup> (although the alpha emitter <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-238" title="Plutonium-238">plutonium-238</a> has become standard for most space-exploration-related uses).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radioisotope2009_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radioisotope2009-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Promethium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The element, like other lanthanides, has no biological role. Promethium-147 can emit <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> during its <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons1964_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons1964-60">[60]</a></sup> which are dangerous for all lifeforms. Interactions with tiny quantities of promethium-147 are not hazardous if certain precautions are observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Army1987_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Army1987-61">[61]</a></sup> In general, gloves, footwear covers, safety glasses, and an outer layer of easily removed protective clothing should be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pmlist_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pmlist-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not known what human organs are affected by interaction with promethium; a possible candidate is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_tissue" title="Bone tissue">bone tissues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pmlist_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pmlist-62">[62]</a></sup> Sealed promethium-147 is not dangerous. However, if the packaging is damaged, then promethium becomes dangerous to the environment and humans. If <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a> is found, the contaminated area should be washed with water and soap, but, even though promethium mainly affects the skin, the skin should not be abraded. If a promethium leak is found, the area should be identified as hazardous and evacuated, and emergency services must be contacted. No dangers from promethium aside from the radioactivity are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pmlist_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pmlist-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
