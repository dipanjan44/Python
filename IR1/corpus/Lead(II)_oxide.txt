<p><b>Lead(II) oxide</b>, also called <b>lead monoxide</b>, is the inorganic compound with the molecular formula <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Pb</a><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a>. PbO occurs in two <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>: <a href="/wiki/Litharge" title="Litharge">litharge</a> having a <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_crystal_system" title="Tetragonal crystal system">tetragonal crystal structure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Massicot" title="Massicot">massicot</a> having an <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic_crystal_system" title="Orthorhombic crystal system">orthorhombic crystal structure</a>. Modern applications for PbO are mostly in <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>-based industrial <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">glass</a> and industrial ceramics, including computer components. It is an <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a> oxide.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>PbO may be prepared by heating lead metal in air at approximately 600 °C. At this temperature it is also the end product of oxidation of other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_oxide_(disambiguation)" title="Lead oxide (disambiguation)">lead oxides</a> in air:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO<sub>2</sub> <span class="sfrac nowrap;"><span style="display:none; display:inline-block; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:0.8em; font-size:70%; padding:0 0.1em;">293 °C</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;">→</span></span></span> Pb<sub>12</sub>O<sub>19</sub> <span class="sfrac nowrap;"><span style="display:none; display:inline-block; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:0.8em; font-size:70%; padding:0 0.1em;">351 °C</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;">→</span></span></span> Pb<sub>12</sub>O<sub>17</sub> <span class="sfrac nowrap;"><span style="display:none; display:inline-block; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:0.8em; font-size:70%; padding:0 0.1em;">375 °C</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;">→</span></span></span> Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> <span class="sfrac nowrap;"><span style="display:none; display:inline-block; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:0.8em; font-size:70%; padding:0 0.1em;">605 °C</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; padding:0 0.1em;">→</span></span></span> PbO</dd></dl>
<p>Thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_nitrate" title="Lead(II) nitrate">lead(II) nitrate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lead_carbonate" title="Lead carbonate">lead(II) carbonate</a> also results in the formation of PbO:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Pb(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> → 2 PbO + 4 <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">NO<sub>2</sub></a> + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>PbCO<sub>3</sub> → PbO + <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a></dd></dl>
<p>PbO is produced on a large scale as an intermediate product in refining raw lead ores into metallic lead. The usual lead ore is <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_sulfide" title="Lead(II) sulfide">lead(II) sulfide</a>). At high temperature (1,000 °C) the sulfide is converted to the oxide:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 PbS + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 PbO + 2 <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a></dd></dl>
<p>Metallic lead is obtained by reducing the PbO with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> at around 1,200 °C:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO + CO → Pb + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As determined by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>, both polymorphs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetragonal" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> feature a pyramidal four-coordinate lead center. In the tetragonal form the four Pb-O bonds have the same length, but in the orthorhombic two are shorter and two longer. The pyramidal nature indicates the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry" title="Stereochemistry">stereochemically</a> active <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a> of electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> When PbO occurs in tetragonal lattice structure it is called <a href="/wiki/Litharge" title="Litharge">litharge</a>; and when the PbO has orthorhombic lattice structure it is called <a href="/wiki/Massicot" title="Massicot">massicot</a>. The PbO can be changed from massicot to litharge or vice versa by controlled heating and cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The tetragonal form is usually red or orange color, while the orthorhombic is usually yellow or orange, but the color is not a very reliable indicator of the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The tetragonal and orthorhombic <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">forms</a> of PbO occur naturally as rare minerals.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red and yellow forms of this material are related by a small change in <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO(red) → PbO(yellow) <span style="padding-left:5em;"> </span> Δ<i>H</i> = 1.6 kJ/mol</dd></dl>
<p>PbO is <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a>, which means that it reacts with both acids and with bases. With acids, it forms salts of Pb<sup>2+</sup> via the intermediacy of oxo <a href="/wiki/Cluster_chemistry" title="Cluster chemistry">clusters</a> such as [Pb<sub>6</sub>O(OH)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4+</sup>. With strong bases, PbO dissolves to form <a href="/wiki/Plumbite" title="Plumbite">plumbite</a> (also called plumbate(II)) salts:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO + H<sub>2</sub>O + OH<sup>−</sup> → [Pb(OH)<sub>3</sub>]<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kind of lead in <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">lead glass</a> is normally PbO, and PbO is used extensively in making glass. Depending on the glass, the benefit of using PbO in glass can be one or more of increasing the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of the glass, decreasing the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the glass, increasing the electrical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">resistivity</a> of the glass, and increasing the ability of the glass to absorb <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">X-rays</a>. Adding PbO to industrial ceramics (as well as glass) makes the materials more magnetically and electrically inert (raises the <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie temperature</a>) and is often used for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Historically PbO was also used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">ceramic glazes</a> for household ceramics, and it is still used, but not extensively any more. Other less dominant applications include the <a href="/wiki/Vulcanization" title="Vulcanization">vulcanization</a> of rubber and the production of certain pigments and paints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> PbO is used in <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a> glass to block <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> emission, but mainly in the neck and funnel because it can cause discoloration when used in the faceplate. <a href="/wiki/Strontium_oxide" title="Strontium oxide">Strontium oxide</a> is preferred for the faceplate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The consumption of lead, and hence the processing of PbO, correlates with the number of automobiles because it remains the key component of automotive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead-acid_batteries" title="Lead-acid batteries">lead-acid batteries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_or_declining_uses">Niche or declining uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Niche or declining uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A mixture of PbO with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycerine" title="Glycerine">glycerine</a> sets to a hard, waterproof <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> that has been used to join the flat glass sides and bottoms of <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquariums</a>, and was also once used to seal glass panels in window frames. It is a component of <a href="/wiki/Lead_paint" title="Lead paint">lead paints</a>.
</p><p>In powdered tetragonal litharge form, it can be mixed with <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a> and then boiled to create a weather-resistant <a href="/wiki/Sizing" title="Sizing">sizing</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilding</a>. The litharge would give the sizing a dark red color that made the gold leaf appear warm and lustrous, while the linseed oil would impart adhesion and a flat durable binding surface.
</p><p>PbO is used in certain <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation reactions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>PbO is the input photoconductor in a video camera tube called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumbicon" title="Plumbicon">Plumbicon</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues">Health issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PbOlabel.jpg"><img alt="PbOlabel.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="530" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/89/PbOlabel.jpg/220px-PbOlabel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/89/PbOlabel.jpg/330px-PbOlabel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/89/PbOlabel.jpg/440px-PbOlabel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PbOlabel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">Lead poisoning</a></div>
<p>Lead oxide may be fatal if swallowed or inhaled. It causes irritation to skin, eyes, and respiratory tract. It affects gum tissue, central nervous system, kidneys, blood, and reproductive system. It can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioaccumulate" title="Bioaccumulate">bioaccumulate</a> in plants and in mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
