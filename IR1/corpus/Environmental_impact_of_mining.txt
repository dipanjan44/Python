<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of mining</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="650" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg/220px-Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg/330px-Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg/440px-Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rio_tinto_river_CarolStoker_NASA_Ames_Research_Center.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">Acid mine drainage</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_River" title="Rio Tinto River">Rio Tinto River</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of mining</b> includes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erosion_in_the_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erosion in the environment (page does not exist)">erosion in the environment</a>, formation of <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkholes</a>, loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contamination_of_soil" title="Contamination of soil">contamination of soil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groundwater_contamination" title="Groundwater contamination">groundwater,</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> by chemicals from <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> processes. Besides creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_damage" title="Environmental damage">environmental damage</a>, the contamination resulting from leakage of chemicals also affects the health of the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Mining companies in some countries are required to follow environmental and rehabilitation codes, ensuring the area mined is returned close to its original state. Some mining methods may have significant environmental and public health effects. Nuss and Eckelman (2014)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duese_and_Eckleman_(2014)_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duese_and_Eckleman_(2014)-2">[2]</a></sup> provide an overview of the life-cycle wide environmental impacts of metal production associated with 62 metals in year 2008.
</p><p><br/>
</p><p>Erosion of exposed hillsides, mine dumps, <a href="/wiki/Tailings_dam" title="Tailings dam">tailings dams</a> and resultant <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a> of drainages, creeks and rivers can significantly impact the surrounding areas, a prime example being the giant <a href="/wiki/Ok_Tedi_Mine" title="Ok Tedi Mine">Ok Tedi Mine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>. In wilderness areas mining may cause destruction and disturbance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> and habitats, and in areas of farming it may disturb or destroy productive grazing and croplands. In urbanised environments mining may produce <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_pollution&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dust pollution (page does not exist)">dust pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visual_pollution" title="Visual pollution">visual pollution</a>.
</p><p><br/>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_pollution">Water pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Water pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg/220px-Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg/330px-Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg/440px-Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lagoa_vermelha_na_Mina_do_Losal_05.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">Acid mine drainage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a></div></div></div>
<p>Mining can have harmful effects on surrounding surface and groundwater if protective measures are not taken. The result can be unnaturally high concentrations of some chemicals, such as <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> over a significant area of surface or subsurface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Runoff of mere soil or rock debris -although non-toxic- also devastates the surrounding vegetation. The dumping of the runoff in surface waters or in  a lot of forests is the worst option here. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Submarine_tailings_disposal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Submarine tailings disposal (page does not exist)">Submarine tailings disposal</a> is regarded as a better option (if the soil is pumped to a great depth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mere land storage and refilling of the mine after it has been depleted is even better, if no forests need to be cleared for the storage of the debris. There is potential for massive contamination of the area surrounding mines due to the various chemicals used in the mining process as well as the potentially damaging compounds and metals removed from the ground with the ore.  Large amounts of water produced from mine drainage, mine cooling, aqueous extraction and other mining processes increases the potential for these chemicals to contaminate ground and surface water. In well-regulated mines, hydrologists and geologists take careful measurements of water and soil to exclude any type of <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water contamination</a> that could be caused by the mine's operations. The reducing or eliminating of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> is enforced in modern American mining by federal and state law, by restricting operators to meet standards for protecting surface and ground water from contamination. This is best done through the use of non-toxic extraction processes as <a href="/wiki/Bioleaching" title="Bioleaching">bioleaching</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid_rock_drainage">Acid rock drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acid rock drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">Acid mine drainage</a></div>
<p>Sub-surface mining often progresses below the water table, so water must be constantly pumped out of the mine in order to prevent flooding. When a mine is abandoned, the pumping ceases, and water floods the mine. This introduction of water is the initial step in most acid rock drainage situations.
</p><p>Acid rock drainage occurs naturally within some environments as part of the rock weathering process but is exacerbated by large-scale earth disturbances characteristic of mining and other large construction activities, usually within rocks containing an abundance of sulfide minerals. Areas where the earth has been disturbed (e.g. construction sites, subdivisions, and transportation corridors) may create acid rock drainage. In many localities, the liquid that drains from coal stocks, coal handling facilities, coal washeries, and coal waste tips can be highly acidic, and in such cases it is treated as acid mine drainage (AMD).
</p><p>The same type of chemical reactions and processes may occur through the disturbance of acid sulfate soils formed under coastal or estuarine conditions after the last major sea level rise, and constitutes a similar environmental hazard.
</p><p>The five principal technologies used to monitor and control water flow at mine sites are diversion systems, containment ponds, groundwater pumping systems, subsurface drainage systems, and subsurface barriers. In the case of AMD, contaminated water is generally pumped to a treatment facility that neutralizes the contaminants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2006 review of environmental impact statements found that "water quality predictions made after considering the effects of mitigations largely underestimated actual impacts to
groundwater, seeps, and surface water".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_metals">Heavy metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Heavy metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Dissolution and transport of metals and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> by run-off and ground water is another example of environmental problems with mining, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britannia_Beach,_British_Columbia" title="Britannia Beach, British Columbia">Britannia Mine</a>, a former copper mine near <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver, British Columbia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tar_Creek_Superfund_site" title="Tar Creek Superfund site">Tar Creek</a>, an abandoned mining area in Picher,Oklahoma that is now an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfund_(environmental_law)" title="Superfund (environmental law)">superfund</a> site, also suffers from heavy metal contamination.  Water in the mine containing dissolved heavy metals such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> leaked into local groundwater, contaminating it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Long-term storage of tailings and dust can lead to additional problems, as they can be easily blown off site by wind, as occurred at <a href="/wiki/Cyprus_Mines_Corporation" title="Cyprus Mines Corporation">Skouriotissa</a>, an abandoned copper mine in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_biodiversity">Effects on biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects on biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tabubil-world_wind.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="663" data-file-width="795" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tabubil-world_wind.png/220px-Tabubil-world_wind.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tabubil-world_wind.png/330px-Tabubil-world_wind.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Tabubil-world_wind.png/440px-Tabubil-world_wind.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tabubil-world_wind.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Ok_Tedi_River" title="Ok Tedi River">Ok Tedi River</a> is contaminated by tailings from a nearby mine.</div></div></div>
<p>The implantation of a mine is a major habitat modification, and smaller perturbations occur on a larger scale than exploitation site, mine-waste residuals contamination of the environment for example. Adverse effects can be observed long after the end of the mine activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-art1-8">[8]</a></sup>
Destruction or drastic modification of the original site and <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> substances release can have major impact on <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> in the area. Destruction of the habitat is the main component of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity losses</a>, but direct poisoning caused by mine-extracted material, and indirect poisoning through food and water, can also affect animals, vegetation and microorganisms. Habitat modification such as pH and temperature modification disturb communities in the area. <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">Endemic</a> species are especially sensitive, since they need very specific environmental conditions. Destruction or slight modification of their habitat puts them at the risk of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a>. Habitats can be damaged when there is not enough terrestrial product as well as by non-chemicals products, such as large rocks from the mines that are discarded in the surrounding landscape with no concern for impacts on natural habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-art3-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a> are known to decrease with distance from the mine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-art1-8">[8]</a></sup> and effects on biodiversity follow the same pattern. Impacts can vary greatly depending on mobility and <a href="/wiki/Bioavailability" title="Bioavailability">bioavailability</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contaminant" title="Contaminant">contaminant</a>: less-mobile molecules will stay inert in the environment while highly mobile molecules will easily move into another compartment or be taken up by organisms. For example, <a href="/wiki/Ion_speciation" title="Ion speciation">speciation</a> of metals in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediments" title="Sediments">sediments</a> could modify their bioavailability, and thus their toxicity for aquatic organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art13_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-art13-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Biomagnification" title="Biomagnification">Biomagnification</a> plays an important role in polluted habitats: mining impacts on biodiversity should be, assuming that concentration levels are not high enough to directly kill exposed organisms, greater on the species on top of the food chain because of this phenomenon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Adverse mining effects on biodiversity depend  a great extent on the nature of the contaminant, the level of concentration at which it can be found in the environment, and the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> itself. Some species are quite resistant to anthropogenic disturbances, while some others will completely disappear from the contaminated zone. Time alone does not seem to allow the habitat to recover completely from the contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art11_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-art11-11">[11]</a></sup> Remediation takes time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup> and in most cases will not enable the recovery of the original diversity present before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mining_industry" title="Mining industry">mining activity</a> took place.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquatic_organisms">Aquatic organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aquatic organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4><p>
The mining industry can impact aquatic biodiversity through different ways. Direct poisoning is the first one,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art8_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-art8-14">[14]</a></sup> and risks are higher when contaminants are mobile in the sediment <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> or bioavailable in the water. Mine drainage can modify water pH,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art14_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-art14-15">[15]</a></sup> and it is hard to differentiate direct impact on organisms from impacts caused by pH changes. Effects can nonetheless be observed and proved to be caused by pH modifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art8_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-art8-14">[14]</a></sup> Contaminants can also affect aquatic organisms through physical effects:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art8_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-art8-14">[14]</a></sup> streams with high concentrations of suspended sediment limit light, thus diminishing algae biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art15_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-art15-16">[16]</a></sup> Metal oxide deposition can limit biomass by coating algae or their substrate, thereby preventing colonization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art8_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-art8-14">[14]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osisko_lake.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1475" data-file-width="2592" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Osisko_lake.JPG/220px-Osisko_lake.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Osisko_lake.JPG/330px-Osisko_lake.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Osisko_lake.JPG/440px-Osisko_lake.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osisko_lake.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contaminated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osisko_lake" title="Osisko lake">Osisko lake</a> in Rouyn-Noranda</div></div></div>
<p>Factors that impact communities in <a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">acid mine drainage</a> sites vary temporarily and seasonally: temperature, rainfall, pH, salinisation and metal quantity all display variations on the long term, and can heavily affect communities. Changes in pH or temperature can affect metal solubility, and thereby the bioavailable quantity that directly impact organisms. Moreover, contamination persists over time: ninety years after a <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> mine closure, water pH was still very low and microorganisms populations consisted mainly of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidophil" title="Acidophil">acidophil</a> bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art6_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-art6-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Microorganisms">Microorganisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microorganisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Algae communities are less diverse in acidic water containing high <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> concentration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art8_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-art8-14">[14]</a></sup> and mine drainage stress decrease their primary production. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatoms" title="Diatoms">Diatoms</a> community is greatly modified by any chemical change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art5_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-art5-18">[18]</a></sup> pH <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> assemblage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art16_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-art16-19">[19]</a></sup> and high metal concentration diminishes the abundance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planktonic" title="Planktonic">planktonic</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art5_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-art5-18">[18]</a></sup> Some diatom species may however grow in high-metal-concentration sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art5_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-art5-18">[18]</a></sup> In sediments close to the surface, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cysts" title="Cysts">cysts</a> suffer from <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> and heavy coating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art5_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-art5-18">[18]</a></sup> In very polluted conditions, total algae biomass is quite low, and the planktonic diatom community missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art5_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-art5-18">[18]</a></sup> In case of functional complementarity however, it is possible that phytoplankton and <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> mass remains stable.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Macroorganisms">Macroorganisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Macroorganisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Water <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustacean</a> communities are modified around a mine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art9_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-art9-20">[20]</a></sup> resulting in a low trophic completeness and community being dominated by predators. However, biodiversity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroinvertebrates" title="Macroinvertebrates">macroinvertebrates</a> can remain high, if sensitive species are replaced with tolerant ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art10_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-art10-21">[21]</a></sup> When diversity is on the contrary reduced, there is sometimes no effect of stream contamination on abundance or biomass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art10_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-art10-21">[21]</a></sup> suggesting that tolerant species fulfilling the same function take the place of sensible species in polluted sites. pH diminution in addition to elevated metal concentration can also have adverse effects on macroinvertebrates' behaviour, showing that direct toxicity is not the only issue. Fishes are also affected by pH,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art17_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-art17-22">[22]</a></sup> temperature variations and chemical concentrations.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_organisms">Terrestrial organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Terrestrial organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation">Vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Soils' texture and water content can be greatly modified in disturbed sites,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup> leading to plants communities changes in the area. Most of the plants have a low concentration tolerance for metals in the soil, but sensitivity differs among species. Grass diversity and total cover is less affected by high contaminant concentration than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbs" title="Forbs">forbs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrubs" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup> Mines waste-material rejects or traces due to mining activity can be found in the vicinity of the mine, sometimes pretty far away from the source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup> Established plants cannot move away from perturbations, and will eventually die if their habitat is contaminated by heavy metals or <a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloids</a> at concentration too elevated for their physiology. Some species are more resistant and will survive these levels, and some non-native species that can tolerate these concentrations in the soil, will migrate in the mine surrounding lands to occupy the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a>.
</p><p>Plants can be affected through direct poisoning, for example <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> soil content reduces <a href="/wiki/Bryophyte" title="Bryophyte">bryophyte</a> diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> Soil acidification through pH diminution by chemical contamination can also lead to a diminished species number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> Contaminants can modify or disturb microorganisms, thus modifying nutrient availability, causing a loss of vegetation in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> 
Some tree roots avoid the deeper soil layer in order to avoid the contaminated zone, and thus miss anchorage and might be uprooted by the wind when their height and shoot weight increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup> In general, root exploration is reduced in contaminated areas compared to non-polluted ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup> Even in reclaimed habitats, plant species diversity is lower than in undisturbed areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cultivated crops might be a problem near mines. Most crops can grow on weakly contaminated sites, but yield is generally lower than it would have been in regular growing conditions. Plants also tend to accumulate heavy metals in their aerian organs, possibly leading to human intake through fruits and vegetables. Regular consumptions might lead to health problems caused by long-term metal exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-art1-8">[8]</a></sup> Cigarettes made from tobacco growing on contaminated sites might as well have adverse effects on human population, as tobacco tends to accumulate cadmium and zinc in its leaves.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG/220px-Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG/330px-Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG/440px-Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Malartik_mine_-_Osisko.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Malartic,_Quebec" title="Malartic, Quebec">Malartic</a> mine - Osisko</div></div></div>
<p>Habitat destruction is one of the main issues of mining activity. Huge areas of natural habitat are destroyed during mine construction and exploitation, forcing animals to leave the site.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Animals can be poisoned directly by mine products and residuals. <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">Bioaccumulation</a> in the plants or the smaller organisms they eat can also lead to poisoning: horses, goats and sheep are exposed in certain areas to potentially toxic concentration of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in grass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art11_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-art11-11">[11]</a></sup> There are fewer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ants" title="Ants">ant</a> species in soil containing high copper levels, in the vicinity of a copper mine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-art3-9">[9]</a></sup> If fewer ants are found, chances are great that other organisms leaving in the surrounding landscape are strongly affected as well by this high copper levels, since ants are a good environmental control: they live directly in the soil and are thus pretty sensitive to environmental disruptions.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Microorganisms_2">Microorganisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Microorganisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Because of their size, microorganisms are extremely sensitive to environmental modification,such as modified pH,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> temperature changes or  chemicals concentration. For example, the presence of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> in soils led to a diminution in total soil bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> Moreover, as in  water, a small change in the soil pH can provoke the remobilization of contaminants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art12_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-art12-24">[24]</a></sup> in addition to the direct impact on pH-sensitive organisms.
</p><p>Microorganisms have a wide variety of genes among their total population, so there is a greater chance of survival of the species due to the existence of resistance or tolerance genes in some colonies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art62_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-art62-25">[25]</a></sup> as long as modifications are not too extreme. Nevertheless, survival in these conditions will imply a big loss of gene diversity, resulting in reduced potential adaptations to subsequent changes. The presence of few developed soil in heavy metal contaminated areas could be a sign of reduced activity by soils microfauna and microflora, indicating a reduced number of individuals or reduced activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art2_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-art2-13">[13]</a></sup> 
Twenty years after disturbance, even in rehabilitation area, microbial biomass is still greatly reduced compared to undisturbed habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art7_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-art7-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arbuscular_mycorrhiza" title="Arbuscular mycorrhiza">Arbuscular mycorrhiza</a> fungi are especially sensitive to the presence of chemicals, and the soil is sometimes so disturbed that they are no longer able to associate with root plants. Some fungi possess however contaminant accumulation capacity, soil cleaning capacity by changing the biodisponibility of contaminants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup> and can protect plants from damages caused by chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup> Their presence in contaminated sites could prevent loss of biodiversity due to mine-waste contamination,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup> or allow <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a>, that is, the removal of undesired chemicals from contaminated soils. 
On the contrary, some microbes can deteriorate the environment: which mean elevated SO4 in the water can also increase microbial production of hydrogen sulfide, a toxin for many aquatic plants and organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-art4_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-art4-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_mine_pollution_on_humans">Effects of mine pollution on humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects of mine pollution on humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Coalworker%27s_pneumoconiosis" title="Coalworker's pneumoconiosis">Coalworker's pneumoconiosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toroku_arsenic_disease" title="Toroku arsenic disease">Toroku arsenic disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Itai-itai_disease" title="Itai-itai disease">Itai-itai disease</a></div>
<p>Humans are also affected by mining. There are many diseases that can come from the pollutants that are released into the air and water during the mining process. For example, during smelting operations enormous quantities<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="vague (August 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of air pollutants, such as the suspended particulate matter, SO<sub>x</sub>, arsenic particles and cadmium, are emitted. Metals are usually emitted into the air as particulates.
</p><p>There are also many occupational health hazards. Most of the miners suffer from various respiratory and skin diseases. Miners working in different types of mines suffer from asbestosis, silicosis, or black lung disease.. Humans are also affected by the occurrence of landslides and floods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

Although the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> due to mining may be small compared to the total amount it may lead to species extinction if there is a high level of local <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sand_mining">Sand mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sand mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sand_mining" title="Sand mining">Sand mining</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sand_mining" title="Sand mining">Sand mining</a> and gravel mining creates large pits and fissures in the earth's surface.  At times, mining can extend so deeply that it affects ground water, springs, underground wells, and the water table.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidence">Subsidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Subsidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="2352" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG/220px-2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG/330px-2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG/440px-2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2008-05-23_Haus_Ecke_Kamp_und_Johowstrasse.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>House in <a href="/wiki/Gladbeck" title="Gladbeck">Gladbeck</a>, Germany, with fissures caused by gravity erosion due to mining.</div></div></div>
<p>Salt mining and <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt dome</a> collapsing in <a href="/wiki/Assumption_Parish,_Louisiana" title="Assumption Parish, Louisiana">Assumption Parish, Louisiana</a> caused the <a href="/wiki/Bayou_Corne_sinkhole" title="Bayou Corne sinkhole">Bayou Corne sinkhole</a> in 2012. As of August 2013, the sinkhole continues to expand.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation">Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Responsible_mining" title="Responsible mining">Responsible mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Restoration_ecology" title="Restoration ecology">Restoration ecology</a></div>
<p>To ensure completion of <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">reclamation</a>, or restoring mine land for future use, many governments and regulatory authorities around the world require that mining companies post a bond to be held in escrow until productivity of <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">reclaimed land</a> has been convincingly demonstrated, although if cleanup procedures are more expensive than the size of the bond, the bond may simply be abandoned. Since 1978 the mining industry has reclaimed more than 2 million acres (8,000 km²) of land in the United States alone. This reclaimed land has renewed vegetation and wildlife in previous mining lands and can even be used for farming and ranching.
</p>
