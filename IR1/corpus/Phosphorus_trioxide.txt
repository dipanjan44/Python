<p><b>Phosphorus trioxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the molecular formula P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>.  Although it should properly be named tetraphosphorus hexoxide, the name phosphorus trioxide preceded the knowledge of the compound's molecular structure, and its usage continues today.  This colorless solid is structurally related to <a href="/wiki/Adamantane" title="Adamantane">adamantane</a>.  It is formally the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydride" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorous_acid" title="Phosphorous acid">phosphorous acid</a>, H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub>, but cannot be obtained by the dehydration of the acid.  It is a white, waxy, crystalline and highly toxic solid with garlic odour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is obtained by the combustion of phosphorus in a limited supply of air at low temperature.
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>(s) + 3 O<sub>2</sub>(g) → P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>(s)</dd></dl>
<p>By-products include red phosphorus suboxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_Properties">Chemical Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phosphorus trioxide reacts with water to form <a href="/wiki/Phosphorous_acid" title="Phosphorous acid">phosphorous acid</a>, reflecting the fact that it is the anhydride of that acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O  →   4 H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Phosphorous_acid" title="Phosphorous acid">H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trichloride" title="Phosphorus trichloride">phosphorus trichloride</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 6 HCl → 2 H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub> + 2 PCl<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>With chlorine or bromine it forms the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphorus_chlorides#Oxyhalides_and_thiohalides" title="Phosphorus chlorides">phosphoryl halide</a>, and it reacts with iodine in a sealed tube to form <a href="/wiki/Diphosphorus_tetraiodide" title="Diphosphorus tetraiodide">diphosphorus tetraiodide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>When P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is allowed to react with <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> at 195 K in <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>, the highly unstable compound P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>18</sub> is formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png"><img alt="Reaction of phosphoprus trioxide with ozone.png" data-file-height="403" data-file-width="950" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png/380px-Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png/570px-Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png/760px-Reaction_of_phosphoprus_trioxide_with_ozone.png 2x" width="380"/></a></div>
<p>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>18</sub> decomposes above 238 K in solution with the release of O<sub>2</sub> gas.  Decomposition of dry P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>18</sub> is explosive.
</p><p>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is converted into the mixed P(III)P(V) species P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>8</sub> when heated in a sealed tube at 710 K, with the side product being red phosphorus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_ligand">As a ligand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As a ligand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is a ligand for transition metals, comparable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphite" title="Phosphite">phosphite</a>. Tetracarbonyl(tetraphosphorus hexaoxide)iron, P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>·Fe(CO)<sub>4</sub>, is an example of a complex containing the phosphorus trioxide cage as a ligand. Its molecular structure as determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is shown below:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="710" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/100px-Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/150px-Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png/200px-Tetraphosphorus-hexaoxide-iron-tetracarbonyl-complex-from-xtal-3D-balls.png 2x" width="100"/></a>
</p><p>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> can also donate electrons to Lewis acids such as BH<sub>3</sub>. Adducts with one to two equivalents of BH<sub>3</sub> complexed with one mole of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> has been reported. When P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is reacted with one molar equivalent of (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>S•BH<sub>3</sub>, followed by slow crystallization from the toluene solution at 244K, P<sub>8</sub>O<sub>12</sub>(BH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> rather than an adduct of P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is formed:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
