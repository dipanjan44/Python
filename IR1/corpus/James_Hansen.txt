<p><b>James Edward Hansen</b> (born 29 March 1941) is an American <a href="/wiki/Adjunct_professor" title="Adjunct professor">adjunct professor</a> directing the Program on Climate Science, Awareness and Solutions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/The_Earth_Institute" title="The Earth Institute">Earth Institute</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>. He is best known for his research in <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, his 1988 Congressional testimony on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> that helped raise broad awareness of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and his advocacy of action to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">avoid dangerous climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scopus_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-scopus-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In recent years he has become a climate <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activist</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigate</a> the effects of climate change, on a few occasions leading to his arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hansen was born in <a href="/wiki/Denison,_Iowa" title="Denison, Iowa">Denison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a>, to James Ivan Hansen and Gladys Ray Hansen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He was trained in physics and astronomy in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">space science</a> program of <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>. He obtained a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in Physics and Mathematics with highest distinction in 1963, an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">M.S.</a> in Astronomy in 1965 and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in Physics in 1967, all three degrees from the University of Iowa. He participated in the NASA graduate traineeship from 1962 to 1966 and, at the same time, between 1965 and 1966, he was a visiting student at the Institute of Astrophysics at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Kyoto" title="University of Kyoto">University of Kyoto</a> and in the Department of Astronomy at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>. He then began work at the <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Institute_for_Space_Studies" title="Goddard Institute for Space Studies">Goddard Institute for Space Studies</a> in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tracker_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tracker-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a>, Hansen continued his work with <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_radiative_transfer_codes" title="Atmospheric radiative transfer codes">radiative transfer models</a>, attempting to understand the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">Venusian atmosphere</a>. He later applied and refined these models to understand the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, and in particular, the effects that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trace_gases" title="Trace gases">trace gases</a> have on Earth's climate. His development and use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a> has contributed to the further understanding of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_climate" title="Earth's climate">Earth's climate</a>. In 2009 his first book, <i><a href="/wiki/Storms_of_My_Grandchildren" title="Storms of My Grandchildren">Storms of My Grandchildren</a></i>, was published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2012 he presented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TED_Talk" title="TED Talk">TED Talk</a> "Why I must speak out about climate change".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1981 to 2013, he was the director of the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Institute_for_Space_Studies" title="Goddard Institute for Space Studies">Goddard Institute for Space Studies</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, a part of the <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>.
</p><p>As of  2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Hansen directs the Program on Climate Science, Awareness and Solutions at Columbia University's Earth Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The program is working to continue to "connect the dots" from advancing basic climate science to promoting public awareness to advocating policy actions.
</p><p>Hansen is representing his granddaughter as well as "future generations" as plaintiffs in the <i><a href="/wiki/Juliana_v._United_States" title="Juliana v. United States">Juliana v. United States</a></i> lawsuit, which is suing the United States government and some of its executive branch's positions for not protecting a stable climate system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_publications">Research and publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research and publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a college student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>, Hansen was attracted to science and the research done by James Van Allen's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">space science</a> program in the physics and astronomy department. A decade later, his focus shifted to planetary research that involved trying to understand the climate change on earth that will result from <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> changes of the atmospheric composition.
</p><p>Hansen has stated that one of his research interests is radiative transfer in planetary atmospheres, especially the interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> of the Earth's atmosphere and surface from satellites. Because of the ability of satellites to monitor the entire globe, they may be one of the most effective ways to monitor and study global change. His other interests include the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_circulation_model" title="Global circulation model">global circulation models</a> to help understand the observed climate trends, and diagnosing human impacts on climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansenbio_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansenbio-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_of_Venus">Studies of Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Studies of Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venus-real_color.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="480" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Venus-real_color.jpg/220px-Venus-real_color.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Venus-real_color.jpg/330px-Venus-real_color.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Venus-real_color.jpg/440px-Venus-real_color.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venus-real_color.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Venus is surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">thick atmosphere</a> composed mainly of carbon dioxide and nitrogen, and its clouds are sulfuric acid. The thickness of the atmosphere initially made it difficult to determine why the surface was so hot.</div></div></div>
<p>In the late 1960s and early 1970s, following his Ph.D. dissertation, Hansen published several papers on the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>. Venus has a high <a href="/wiki/Brightness_temperature" title="Brightness temperature">brightness temperature</a> in the radio frequencies compared to the infrared. He proposed that the hot surface was the result of aerosols trapping the internal energy of the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dustmodel1967_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-dustmodel1967-15">[15]</a></sup> More recent studies have suggested that several billion years ago, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">Venus's atmosphere</a> was much more like Earth's than it is now and that there were probably substantial quantities of liquid water on the surface, but a <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a> was caused by the evaporation of that original water, which generated a critical level of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> in its atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kasting-greenhouse_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-kasting-greenhouse-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen continued his study of Venus by looking at the composition of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>. He looked at the near-infrared reflectivity of ice clouds, compared them to observations of Venus, and found that they qualitatively agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venus1968_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-venus1968-17">[17]</a></sup> He also was able to use a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_radiative_transfer_codes" title="Atmospheric radiative transfer codes">radiative transfer model</a> to establish an upper limit to the size of the ice particles if the clouds were actually made of ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venusclouds1968_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-venusclouds1968-18">[18]</a></sup> Evidence published in the early 1980s showed that the clouds consist mainly of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> droplets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venus-clouds_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-venus-clouds-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1974, the composition of Venus' clouds had not yet been determined, with many scientists proposing a wide variety of compounds, including liquid water and aqueous solutions of ferrous chloride. Hansen and Hovenier used the polarization of sunlight reflected from the planet to establish that the clouds were spherical and had a <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_drop_effective_radius" title="Cloud drop effective radius">cloud drop effective radius</a> which eliminated all of the proposed cloud types except sulfuric acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polarization1974_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-polarization1974-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kiyoshi_Kawabata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kiyoshi Kawabata (page does not exist)">Kiyoshi Kawabata</a> and Hansen expanded upon this work by looking at the variation of polarization on Venus. They found that the visible clouds are a diffuse haze rather than a thick cloud, confirming the same results obtained from transits across the sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polarization1975_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-polarization1975-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus_project" title="Pioneer Venus project">Pioneer Venus project</a> was launched in May 1978 and reached Venus late that same year. Hansen collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Larry_Travis" title="Larry Travis">Larry Travis</a> and other colleagues in a 1979 <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> article that reported on the development and variability of clouds in the ultraviolet spectrum. They conclude that there are at least three different cloud materials that contribute to the images: a thin haze layer, sulfuric acid clouds and an unknown ultraviolet absorber below the sulfuric acid cloud layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-travis-venus-1979_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-travis-venus-1979-22">[22]</a></sup> The linear polarization data obtained from the same mission confirmed that the low- and mid-level clouds were <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> with radius of about 1 micrometer. Above the cloud layer was a layer of submicrometre haze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kawabata-venus-polarimetry_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-kawabata-venus-polarimetry-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_temperature_analysis">Global temperature analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Global temperature analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peoria_ASOS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Peoria_ASOS.jpg/220px-Peoria_ASOS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Peoria_ASOS.jpg/330px-Peoria_ASOS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Peoria_ASOS.jpg/440px-Peoria_ASOS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peoria_ASOS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical <a href="/wiki/Automated_airport_weather_station" title="Automated airport weather station">automated airport weather station</a> which records the routine hourly weather observations of temperature, weather type, wind, sky condition, and visibility. These surface stations are located around the world, and are used to derive a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_temperature" title="Global temperature">global temperature</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GISS" title="GISS">GISS</a> (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Institute_for_Space_Studies" title="Goddard Institute for Space Studies">Goddard Institute for Space Studies</a>) global temperature analysis was published in 1981. Hansen and his co-author analyzed the surface air temperature at meteorological stations focusing on the years from 1880 to 1985. Temperatures for stations closer together than 1000 kilometers were shown to be highly correlated, especially in the mid-latitudes, providing a way to combine the station data to provide accurate long-term variations. They conclude that global mean temperatures can be determined even though meteorological stations are typically in the Northern hemisphere and confined to continental regions. Warming in the past century was found to be <span class="nowrap">0.5-0.7 °C</span>, with warming similar in both hemispheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temptrends-1987_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-temptrends-1987-24">[24]</a></sup> When the analysis was updated in 1988, the four warmest years on record were all in the 1980s. The two warmest years were 1981 and 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temptrends-1988_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-temptrends-1988-25">[25]</a></sup> During a senate meeting on June 23, 1988, Hansen reported that he was ninety-nine percent certain the earth was warmer then than it had ever been measured to be, there was a clear cause and effect relationship with the greenhouse effect and lastly that due to global warming, the likelihood of freak weather was steadily increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo, 1992 saw a cooling in the global temperatures. There was speculation that this would cause the next couple years to be cooler because of the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serial_correlation" title="Serial correlation">serial correlation</a> in the global temperatures. Bassett and Lin found the statistical odds of a new temperature record to be small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bassettandlin_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-bassettandlin-27">[27]</a></sup> Hansen countered by saying that having insider information shifts the odds to those who know the physics of the climate system, and that whether there is a new temperature record depends upon the particular data set used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temptrends-1993_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-temptrends-1993-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The temperature data was updated in 1999 to report that 1998 was the warmest year since the instrumental data began in 1880. They also found that the rate of temperature change was larger than at any time in instrument history, and conclude that the recent <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> was not totally responsible for the large temperature anomaly in 1998. In spite of this, the United States had seen a smaller degree of warming, and a region in the eastern U.S. and the western Atlantic Ocean had actually cooled slightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-temptrends-1999_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-temptrends-1999-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>2001 saw a major update to how the temperature was calculated. It incorporated corrections due to the following reasons: time-of-observation bias; station history changes; classification of rural/urban station; the urban adjustment based on satellite measurements of night light intensity, and relying more on rural station than urban. Evidence was found of local urban warming in urban, suburban and small-town records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ustemptrends-2001_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ustemptrends-2001-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The anomalously high global temperature in 1998 due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> resulted in a brief drop in subsequent years. However, a 2001 Hansen report in the journal <i>Science</i> states that global warming continues, and that the increasing temperatures should stimulate discussions on how to slow global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen2002science_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen2002science-31">[31]</a></sup> The temperature data was updated in 2006 to report that temperatures are now <span class="nowrap"> 0.8 °C</span> warmer than a century ago, and conclude that the recent global warming is a real climate change and not an artifact from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island_effect" title="Urban heat island effect">urban heat island effect</a>. The regional variation of warming, with more warming in the higher latitudes, is further evidence of warming that is anthropogenic in origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansenPNAS2006_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansenPNAS2006-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_McIntyre" title="Stephen McIntyre">Stephen McIntyre</a> notified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GISS" title="GISS">GISS</a> that many of the U.S. temperature records from the <a href="/wiki/Global_Historical_Climatology_Network" title="Global Historical Climatology Network">Historical Climatology Network</a> (USHCN) displayed a discontinuity around the year 2000. NASA corrected the computer code used to process the data and credited McIntyre with pointing out the flaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Hansen indicated that he felt that several news organizations had overreacted to this mistake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usufruct_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-usufruct-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2010, Hansen published a paper entitled "Global Surface Temperature Change" describing current global temperature analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globalsurfacetemperaturechange-2010_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Globalsurfacetemperaturechange-2010-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_carbon_studies">Black carbon studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Black carbon studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1251" data-file-width="1999" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg/220px-Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg/330px-Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg/440px-Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fire_above_Snake_River.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The incomplete combustion of biomass during the <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_fires_of_1988" title="Yellowstone fires of 1988">Yellowstone fires of 1988</a> near the Snake River introduced a large quantity of black carbon particles into the atmosphere.</div></div></div>
<p>Hansen has also contributed toward the understanding of <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> on regional climate. In recent decades, northern China has experienced increased drought, and southern China has received increased summer rain resulting in a larger number of floods. Southern China has had a decrease in temperatures while most of the world has warmed. In a paper with Menon and colleagues, through the use of observations and climate models results, they conclude that the black carbon heats the air, increases convection and precipitation, and leads to larger surface cooling than if the aerosols were sulfates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-menon2002_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-menon2002-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>A year later, Hansen teamed with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Makiko_Sato&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Makiko Sato (page does not exist)">Makiko Sato</a> to publish a study on black carbon using the global network of <a href="/wiki/AERONET" title="AERONET">AERONET</a> sun photometers. While the location of the AERONET instruments did not represent a global sample, they could still be used to validate global aerosol climatologies. They found that most aerosol climatologies underestimated the amount of black carbon by a factor of at least 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novakov2004_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-novakov2004-38">[38]</a></sup> This corresponds to an increase in the climate forcing of around <span class="nowrap"> 1 W/m<sup>2</sup></span>, which they hypothesize is partially offset by the cooling of non-absorbing aerosols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sato2003_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-sato2003-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Estimations of trends in black carbon emissions show that there was a rapid increase in the 1880s after the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, and a leveling off from 1900–1950 as environmental laws were enacted. China and India have recently increased their emissions of black carbon corresponding to their rapid development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novakov2003_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-novakov2003-40">[40]</a></sup> The emissions from the United Kingdom were estimated using a network of stations that measured black smoke and sulfur dioxide. They report that atmospheric black carbon concentrations have been decreasing since the beginning of the record in the 1960s, and that the decline was faster than the decline in black-carbon-producing fuel use.
</p><p>A 2007 paper used the GISS climate model in an attempt to determine the origin of black carbon in the arctic. Much of the arctic aerosol comes from south Asia. Countries such as the United States and Russia have a lower contribution than previously assumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koch-2005_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-koch-2005-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_impact_on_climate">Anthropogenic impact on climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic impact on climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miamibeachnorth.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1355" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Miamibeachnorth.jpg/220px-Miamibeachnorth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Miamibeachnorth.jpg/330px-Miamibeachnorth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Miamibeachnorth.jpg/440px-Miamibeachnorth.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miamibeachnorth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hansen has warned that low-lying coastal areas such as Florida (seen here), East Anglia, the Netherlands, oceanic islands and Bangladesh are vulnerable to sea levels rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> is an international environmental treaty that has the objective of stabilizing greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system</a>.
</p><p>In 2000, Hansen advanced an alternative view of global warming over the last 100 years, arguing that during that time frame the negative forcing via aerosols and the positive forcing via carbon dioxide (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>) largely balanced each other out, and that the 0.74±0.18 °C net rise in average global temperatures could mostly be explained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> other than carbon dioxide, such as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a>. However, even then he wrote "the future balance of forcings is likely to shift toward dominance of CO<sub>2</sub> over aerosols".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, Hansen wrote a paper called "Can We Defuse the Global Warming Time Bomb?" in which he argued that human-caused forces on the climate are now greater than natural ones, and that this, over a long time period, can cause large climate changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> He further states that a lower limit on "dangerous anthropogenic interference" is set by the stability of the Greenland and Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>. His view on actions to mitigate climate change is that "halting global warming requires urgent, unprecedented international cooperation, but the needed actions are feasible and have additional benefits for human health, agriculture and the environment."
</p><p>In a 2004 presentation at the University of Iowa, Hansen announced that he was told by high-ranking government officials not to talk about how anthropogenic influence could have a dangerous effect on climate because it's not understood what 'dangerous' means, or how humans are actually affecting climate. He describes this as a <a href="/wiki/Deal_with_the_Devil" title="Deal with the Devil">Faustian bargain</a> because atmospheric aerosols have health risks, and should be reduced, but doing so will effectively increase the warming effects from CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Faustianbargain2004_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Faustianbargain2004-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen and coauthors propose that the global mean temperature is a good tool to diagnose dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system. Two elements are particularly important when discussing dangerous anthropogenic interference: sea level rise and the extinction of species. They describe a business-as-usual scenario, which has greenhouse gases growing at approximately 2% per year; and an alternate scenario, in which greenhouse gases concentrations decline. Under the alternate scenario, sea levels could rise by 1 meter per century, causing problems due to the dense population in coastal areas. But this would be minor compared to the 10-meter increase in sea level under the business-as-usual scenario. Hansen describes the situation with species extinction similarly to that of sea level rise. Assuming the alternate scenario, the situation is not good, but it is much worse for business as usual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansenPNAS2006_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansenPNAS2006-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of dangerous anthropogenic interference was clarified in a 2007 paper, finding that further warming of 1 °C would be highly disruptive to humans. An alternate scenario would keep the warming to below this if climate sensitivity were below 3 °C for doubled CO<sub>2</sub>. The conclusion is that CO<sub>2</sub> levels above 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> are considered dangerous, but that reduction in non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gases can provide temporary relief from drastic CO<sub>2</sub> cuts. Further findings are that arctic climate change has been forced by non-CO<sub>2</sub> constituents as much as by CO<sub>2</sub>. The 2007 paper cautions that prompt action is needed to slow CO<sub>2</sub> growth and to prevent a dangerous anthropogenic interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen-interference-2007_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-interference-2007-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_model_development_and_projections">Climate model development and projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate model development and projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="520" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg/220px-Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg/330px-Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg/440px-Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hansen_2006_temperature_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A comparison of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_surface_temperature" title="Global surface temperature">global surface temperature</a> computed for three scenarios and compared with two analysis of observational data.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Bjerknes" title="Vilhelm Bjerknes">Vilhelm Bjerknes</a> began the modern development of the general circulation model in the early 20th century. The progress of numerical modeling was slow due to the slow speed of early computers and the lack of adequate observations. It wasn't until the 1950s that the numerical models were getting close to being realistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Hansen's first contribution to numerical climate models came with the 1974 publication of the GISS model. He and his colleagues claimed that the model was successful in simulating the major features of sea-level pressure and 500mb heights in the North American region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GISS-model-1974_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-GISS-model-1974-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 1981 <i>Science</i> publication by Hansen and a team of scientists at Goddard concluded that carbon dioxide in the atmosphere would lead to warming sooner than previously predicted. They used a one-dimensional radiative-convective model that calculates temperature as a function of height. They reported that the results from the 1D model are similar to those of the more complex 3D models, and can simulate basic mechanisms and feedbacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen1981_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen1981-49">[49]</a></sup> Hansen predicted that temperatures would rise out of the climate noise by the 1990s, much earlier than predicted by other researches. He also predicted that it would be difficult to convince politicians and the public to react.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the early 1980s, the computational speed of computers, along with refinements in climate models, allowed longer experiments. The models now included physics beyond the previous equations, such as convection schemes, diurnal changes, and snow-depth calculations. The advances in computational efficiency, combined with the added physics, meant the GISS model could be run for five years. It was shown that global climate can be simulated reasonably well with a grid-point resolution as coarse as 1000 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3dmodels-1983_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-3dmodels-1983-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first climate prediction computed from a general circulation model that was published by Hansen was in 1988, the same year as his well-known Senate testimony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt19880624_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt19880624-52">[52]</a></sup> The second generation of the GISS model was used to estimate the change in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_surface_temperature" title="Mean surface temperature">mean surface temperature</a> based on a variety of scenarios of future greenhouse gas emissions. Hansen concluded that global warming would be evident within the next few decades, and that it would result in temperatures at least as high as during the <a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Eemian</a>. He argued that, if the temperature rises <span class="nowrap"> 0.4 °C</span> above the 1950–1980 mean for a few years, it is the "smoking gun" pointing to human-caused global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3dforecast-1988_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-3dforecast-1988-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Hansen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="242" data-file-width="348" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/James_Hansen.jpg/220px-James_Hansen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/James_Hansen.jpg/330px-James_Hansen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/85/James_Hansen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Hansen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hansen giving testimony before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> in 1988.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2006, Hansen and colleagues compared the observations with the projections made by Hansen in his 1988 testimony before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>. They described the intermediate scenario as the most likely, and that real-world greenhouse gas forcing has been closest to this scenario. It contained the effects of three volcanic eruptions in the fifty-year projections, with one in the 1995, whereas the recent <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> eruption was in 1991. They found that the observed warming was similar to two of the three scenarios. The warming rates of the two most modest warming scenarios are nearly the same through the year 2000, and they were unable to provide a precise model assessment. They did note that the agreement between the observations and the intermediate scenario was accidental because the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a> used was higher than current estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansenPNAS2006_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-hansenPNAS2006-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>A year later, Hansen joined with <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Rahmstorf" title="Stefan Rahmstorf">Rahmstorf</a> and colleagues comparing climate projections with observations. The comparison is done from 1990 through January 2007 against physics-based models that are independent from the observations after 1990. They show that the climate system may be responding faster than the models indicate. Rahmstorf and coauthors show concern that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea levels are rising</a> at the high range of the IPCC projections, and that it is due to thermal expansion and not from melting of the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheets" title="Antarctic ice sheets">Antarctic ice sheets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rahmstorf2007_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-rahmstorf2007-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the launch of spacecraft capable of determining temperatures, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Spencer_(scientist)" title="Roy Spencer (scientist)">Roy Spencer</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Christy" title="John Christy">John Christy</a> published the first version of their <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">satellite temperature measurements</a> in 1990. Contrary to climate models and surface measurements, their results showed a cooling in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spencer_and_christy_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-spencer_and_christy-55">[55]</a></sup> However, in 1998, Wentz and Schabel determined that <a href="/wiki/Orbital_decay" title="Orbital decay">orbital decay</a> had an effect on the derived temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wentzand_schabel_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-wentzand_schabel-56">[56]</a></sup> Hansen compared the corrected troposphere temperatures with the results of the published GISS model, and concluded that the model is in good agreement with the observations, noting that the satellite temperature data had been the last holdout of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">global warming denialists</a>, and that the correction of the data would result in a change from discussing whether global warming is occurring to what is the rate of global warming, and what should be done about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science-1998_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-science-1998-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen has continued the development and diagnostics of climate models. For instance, he has helped look at the decadal trends in <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> height, which could be a useful tool for determining the human "fingerprint" on climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-santer-2003_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-santer-2003-58">[58]</a></sup> As of  12 February  2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the current version of the GISS model is Model E. This version has seen improvements in many areas, including upper-level winds, cloud height, and precipitation. This model still has problems with regions of <a href="/wiki/Marine_stratocumulus" title="Marine stratocumulus">marine stratocumulus</a> clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt-2006_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt-2006-59">[59]</a></sup> A later paper showed that the model's main problems are having too weak of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">ENSO</a>-like variability, and poor sea ice modeling, resulting in too little ice in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> and too much in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen-modele-2007_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-modele-2007-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Climate_forcings.2C_feedbacks.2C_and_sensitivity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_forcings,_feedbacks,_and_sensitivity">Climate forcings, feedbacks, and sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate forcings, feedbacks, and sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NasaHansenFig1.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="462" data-file-width="1160" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/NasaHansenFig1.gif/220px-NasaHansenFig1.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/NasaHansenFig1.gif/330px-NasaHansenFig1.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/NasaHansenFig1.gif/440px-NasaHansenFig1.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NasaHansenFig1.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcings" title="Climate forcings">climate forcings</a> between 1850 and 2000</div></div></div>
<p>In 2000 Hansen authored a paper called "Global warming in the twenty-first century: an alternative scenario" in which he presented a more optimistic way of dealing with global warming, focusing on non-CO<sub>2</sub> gases and <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a> in the short run, giving more time to make reductions in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_emissions" title="Fossil fuel emissions">fossil fuel emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> He notes that the net warming observed to date is roughly as big as that expected from non-CO<sub>2</sub> gases only. This is because CO<sub>2</sub> warming is offset by climate-cooling aerosols emitted with fossil fuel burning and because at that time non-CO<sub>2</sub> gases, taken together, were responsible for roughly 50% of the anthropogenic greenhouse gas warming.
</p><p>In a 2007 paper, Hansen discussed the potential danger of "fast-feedback" effects causing <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> disintegration, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a> data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trace_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-trace-62">[62]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> reports "The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> predicts that sea levels could rise by as much as 59 centimetres (1.94 ft) this century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Hansen's paper argues that the slow melting of ice sheets the panel expects doesn't fit the data. The geological record suggests that ice at the poles does not melt in a gradual and linear fashion, but flips suddenly from one state to another. When temperatures increased to 2–3°C (3.6–5.4°F) above today's level 3.5 million years ago, sea levels rose not by 59 centimeters but by 25 metres (82 ft). The ice responded immediately to changes in temperature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen stressed the uncertainties around these predictions. "It is difficult to predict time of collapse in such a nonlinear problem … An ice sheet response time of centuries seems probable, and we cannot rule out large changes on decadal time-scales once wide-scale surface melt is underway."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trace_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-trace-62">[62]</a></sup> He concludes that "present knowledge does not permit accurate specification of the dangerous level of human-made [greehouse gases]. However, it is much lower than has commonly been assumed. If we have not already passed the dangerous level, the energy infrastructure in place ensures that we will pass it within several decades."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trace_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-trace-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, Hansen authored a paper called "Climate sensitivity, sea level and atmospheric carbon dioxide," in which he estimated climate sensitivity to be (3±1)<span class="nowrap"> </span>°C based on <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a> data. The paper also concluded that burning all fossil fuels "would make most of the planet uninhabitable by humans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, a team of 19 researchers led by Hansen published a paper "Ice melt, sea level rise and superstorms: evidence from paleoclimate data, climate modeling, and modern observations that 2 °C global warming could be dangerous" describing the effect of meltwater from ice sheets on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_meridional_overturning_circulation" title="Atlantic meridional overturning circulation">Atlantic meridional overturning circulation</a> (slowing it or even stopping) and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_bottom_water" title="Antarctic bottom water">Antarctic bottom water</a> formation. This would speed up ice sheet melting and sea level rise by increasing the water temperature at hundreds of meters depth, thawing ice shelves from below. And the cool fresh meltwater on the ocean close to Greenland and Antarctica leads to larger temperature difference between tropics and middle latitudes, what would enable storms as strong as in the last interglacial, <a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Eemian</a>, whose evidence are, among others, megaboulders on Bahamas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_of_climate_change_causation">Analysis of climate change causation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Analysis of climate change causation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">The first action that people should take is to use the democratic process. What is frustrating people, me included, is that democratic action affects elections but what we get then from political leaders is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwash" title="Greenwash">greenwash</a>.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">— James Hansen (March 2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adam_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adam-68">[68]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Hansen noted that in determining responsibility for climate change, the effect of greenhouse gas emissions on climate is determined not by current emissions, but by accumulated emissions over the lifetime of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.
</p><p>By this measure, the U.K. is still the largest single cause of climate change, followed by the U.S. and Germany, even though its current emissions are surpassed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen-testimony_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-testimony-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>On public policy, Hansen is critical of what he sees as efforts to mislead the public on the issue of climate change. He points specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Competitive_Enterprise_Institute" title="Competitive Enterprise Institute">Competitive Enterprise Institute</a>'s commercials with the tagline "carbon dioxide—they call it pollution, we call it life",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and politicians who accept money from fossil-fuel interests and then describe global warming as "a great hoax."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> He also says that changes needed to reduce global warming do not require hardship or reduction in the quality of life, but will also produce benefits such as cleaner air and water, and growth of high-tech industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> He was a critic of both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinton_Administration" title="Clinton Administration">Clinton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_Administration" title="George W. Bush Administration">George W. Bush Administrations</a>' stances on climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbs-73">[73]</a></sup> Addressing the potential effects of climate change, Hansen has stated in an interview in January, 2009, "We cannot now afford to put off change any longer. We have to get on a new path within this new administration. We have only four years left for Obama to set an example to the rest of the world. America must take the lead."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_activism">Climate change activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate change activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Senate_committee_testimony">US Senate committee testimony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: US Senate committee testimony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hansen was invited to testify before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Energy_and_Natural_Resources" title="United States Senate Committee on Energy and Natural Resources">United States Senate Committee on Energy and Natural Resources</a> on June 23, 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-besel2013_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-besel2013-75">[75]</a></sup> Hansen testified that "Global warming has reached a level such that we can ascribe with a high degree of confidence a cause and effect relationship between the greenhouse effect and observed warming...It is already happening now"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt19880624_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt19880624-52">[52]</a></sup> and "The greenhouse effect has been detected and it is changing our climate now...We already reached the point where the greenhouse effect is important."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp19880624_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-wp19880624-76">[76]</a></sup> Hansen said that NASA was 99% confident that the warming was caused by the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere and not a random fluctuation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt19880624_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt19880624-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp19880624_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-wp19880624-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to science historian <a href="/wiki/Spencer_R._Weart" title="Spencer R. Weart">Spencer R. Weart</a>, Hansen's testimony increased public awareness of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> According to Richard Besel of <a href="/wiki/California_Polytechnic_State_University" title="California Polytechnic State University">California Polytechnic State University</a>, Hansen's testimony "was an important turning point in the history of global climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-besel2013_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-besel2013-75">[75]</a></sup> According to Timothy M. O'Donnell of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mary_Washington" title="University of Mary Washington">University of Mary Washington</a>, Hansen's testimony was "pivotal," "ignited public discussion of global warming and moved the controversy from a largely scientific discussion to a full blown science policy debate," and marked "the official beginning of the global warming policy debate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Roger_A._Pielke" title="Roger A. Pielke">Roger A. Pielke</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a>, Hansen's "call to action" "elevated the subject of global warming and the specter of associated impacts such as more hurricanes, floods, and heat waves, to unprecedented levels of attention from the public, media, and policy makers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_coal_industry">Criticism of coal industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticism of coal industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hansen has been particularly critical of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_industry" title="Coal industry">coal industry</a>, stating that coal contributes the largest percentage of anthropogenic carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia.edu_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-columbia.edu-80">[80]</a></sup> He acknowledges that a molecule of carbon dioxide emitted from burning coal has the same effect as a molecule emitted from burning oil. The difference is where the fuel originally resides. He says that most oil comes from <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, and that no matter how fuel-efficient automobiles become, the oil will eventually be burned and the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted. In a 2007 testimony before the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Utilities_Board" title="Iowa Utilities Board">Iowa Utilities Board</a>, he stated that the United States has a large <a href="/wiki/Coal#World_coal_reserves" title="Coal">reservoir of coal</a>, which makes it a resource that can be controlled through action by U.S. politicians, unlike oil which is controlled by other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia.edu_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-columbia.edu-80">[80]</a></sup> He has called for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_phase_out" title="Coal phase out">phasing out coal power</a> completely by the year 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>During his testimony before the Iowa Utilities Board in 2007, Hansen likened coal trains to "death trains" and asserted that these would be "no less gruesome than if they were boxcars headed to crematoria, loaded with uncountable irreplaceable species."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTBlog_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTBlog-82">[82]</a></sup> In response, the <a href="/wiki/National_Mining_Association" title="National Mining Association">National Mining Association</a> stated that his comparison "trivialized the suffering of millions" and "undermined his credibility."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Citing the reactions of "several people" and "three of his scientific colleagues" as his primary motivation, Hansen stated that he certainly did not mean to trivialize suffering by the families who lost relatives in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> and then apologized, saying he regretted that his words caused pain to some readers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountaintop_removal_mining">Mountaintop removal mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mountaintop removal mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On June 23, 2009, James Hansen, along with 30 other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protester" title="Protester">protesters</a> including actress <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Hannah" title="Daryl Hannah">Daryl Hannah</a>, was arrested on <a href="/wiki/Misdemeanor" title="Misdemeanor">misdemeanor</a> charges of obstructing police and impeding traffic, during a protest against <a href="/wiki/Mountaintop_removal_mining" title="Mountaintop removal mining">mountaintop removal mining</a> in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_County,_West_Virginia" title="Raleigh County, West Virginia">Raleigh County, West Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The protesters intended to enter the property of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massey_Energy_Company" title="Massey Energy Company">Massey Energy Company</a>, but were blocked by a crowd of several hundred coal miners and supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> Hansen said that mountaintop removal for coal mining "[provides] only a small fraction of our energy" and "should be abolished."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT.E_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT.E-88">[88]</a></sup> Hansen called on President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> to abolish mountaintop coal mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen and about 100 other people were arrested in September 2010 in front of the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> in Washington, DC. The group was seeking a ban on mountaintop removal or <a href="/wiki/Surface_mining" title="Surface mining">surface mining</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cap_and_trade">Cap and trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cap and trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009 Hansen spoke out against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_and_trade" title="Cap and trade">cap and trade</a>, advocating instead what he believes would be a progressive <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> at source carbon as oil, gas or coal, with a 100% dividend returned to citizens in equal shares, as proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens_Climate_Lobby" title="Citizens Climate Lobby">Citizens Climate Lobby</a>. He has made many appearances and talks supporting the work of CCL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_Hansen_2009_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-James_Hansen_2009-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TellTruth_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-TellTruth-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_from_NASA">Retirement from NASA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Retirement from NASA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hansen retired from NASA in April 2013 after 46 years of government service, saying he planned to take a more active role in the political and legal efforts to limit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The same month, the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Science_Education" title="National Center for Science Education">National Center for Science Education</a>, an organization noted for defending the teaching of evolution in United States science classrooms, named Hansen as an advisor to support the extension of its area of concern into the teaching of climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keystone_Pipeline">Keystone Pipeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Keystone Pipeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a CBC interview aired in April 2013, as Canadian Natural Resources Minister <a href="/wiki/Joe_Oliver_(politician)" title="Joe Oliver (politician)">Joe Oliver</a> lobbied in Washington, DC for approval of <a href="/wiki/Keystone_Pipeline#Keystone_XL" title="Keystone Pipeline">Keystone pipeline extension</a> intended to carry more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude_oil" title="Synthetic crude oil">synthetic crude oil</a> from Canada's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Oil_Sands" title="Athabasca Oil Sands">Athabasca Oil Sands</a> to the Gulf of Mexico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mongabay2011_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-mongabay2011-99">[99]</a></sup> Hansen forcefully argued against the use of these unconventional fossil fuels. According to <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) and other energy organizations "there is more than twice as much carbon in the tar sands oil" than in conventional oil. Hansen argued that coal, tar sands, and tar shale should not be used as energy sources because of their carbon emissions and claimed that the completion of the Keystone pipeline would increase the extraction of oil from oil sands. He explained that the effects of climate change may not be apparent until the far future: "It's not the case where you emit something and you see the effect. We see the beginnings of the effect but the large impacts are going to be in future decades and that science is crystal clear … Effects come slowly because of the inertia of the climate system. It takes decades, even centuries to get the full response. But we know the last time the world was 2 degrees warmer, sea level was 6 meters or 20 feet higher."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neanderthal27april2013_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-neanderthal27april2013-100">[100]</a></sup> Hansen urged President Obama to reject the <a href="/wiki/Keystone_Pipeline#Keystone_XL" title="Keystone Pipeline">Keystone pipeline extension</a> intended to carry more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude_oil" title="Synthetic crude oil">synthetic crude oil</a> from Canada's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Oil_Sands" title="Athabasca Oil Sands">Athabasca Oil Sands</a> to the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mongabay2011_99-1"><a href="#cite_note-mongabay2011-99">[99]</a></sup> On February 13, 2013, Hansen was again arrested at the White House, along with Daryl Hannah and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy,_Jr." title="Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.">Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.</a>, during a further protest against the proposed Keystone pipeline extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhxl_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-dhxl-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_solutions">Proposed solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Proposed solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most recently Hansen has stated his support for a revenue-neutral <a href="/wiki/Fee_and_dividend" title="Fee and dividend">fee and dividend</a> system to impose a price on carbon that returns the money collected from the fossil fuel industry equally to all legal residents of the United States. In an interview on CBC television on March 3, 2015, Dr Hansen stated "The solution [to climate change] has to be a rising price on carbon and then the really dirty fuels like tar sands would fall on the table very quickly. They make no sense at all if you look at it from an economic-wide perspective. If we would simply put a fee on carbon – you would collect from the fossil fuel companies at the source (the domestic mines or the ports of entry) and then distribute that money to the public, an equal amount to all legal residents, that would begin to make the prices honest. And that's what the economy needs in order to be most efficient. Right now the external costs of fossil fuels are borne completely by the public. If your child gets asthma, you pay the bill, the fossil fuel company doesn't. What we need is to make the system honest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of 2008, Hansen stated five priorities that he felt then President-elect Obama should adopt "for solving the climate and energy problems, while stimulating the economy": <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">efficient energy use</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Generation_IV_reactor" title="Generation IV reactor">generation IV nuclear reactors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>. Regarding nuclear, he expressed opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Yucca_Mountain_nuclear_waste_repository" title="Yucca Mountain nuclear waste repository">Yucca Mountain nuclear waste repository</a>, stating that the $25 Billion (US) surplus held in the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Waste_Policy_Act" title="Nuclear Waste Policy Act">Nuclear Waste Fund</a> "should be used to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_Fast_Reactor" title="Integral Fast Reactor">fast reactors</a> that consume nuclear waste, and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fluoride_thorium_reactor" title="Liquid fluoride thorium reactor">thorium reactors</a> to prevent the creation of new long-lived nuclear waste."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TellTruth_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-TellTruth-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009 Hansen wrote an open letter to President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> where he advocated a "Moratorium and phase-out of coal plants that do not capture and store CO<sub>2</sub>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_Hansen_2009_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-James_Hansen_2009-92">[92]</a></sup> In his first book <i>Storms of My Grandchildren</i>, similarly, Hansen discusses his <i>Declaration of Stewardship</i>, the first principle of which requires "a moratorium on coal-fired power plants that do not capture and sequester carbon dioxide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2013, James Hansen co-authored a paper in <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Science_%26_Technology" title="Environmental Science &amp; Technology">Environmental Science &amp; Technology</a></i>, entitled "Prevented mortality and greenhouse gas emissions from historical and projected nuclear power". The paper examined mortality levels per unit of electrical power produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> (coal and natural gas) as well as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>. It estimated that 1.8 million air pollution-caused deaths were prevented worldwide between 1971 and 2009, through the use of nuclear power instead of fossil fuels. The paper also concluded that the emission of some 64 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent were avoided by nuclear power use between 1971 and 2009. Looking to the future, between 2010 and 2050, it was estimated that nuclear could additionally avoid up to 420,000 to 7 million premature deaths and 80 to 240 billion tonnes of greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>This paper elicited a critical response to Kharecha and Hansen's analysis, from an international group of senior academic energy policy analysts, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_Sovacool" title="Benjamin Sovacool">Benjamin Sovacool</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.V._Ramana" title="M.V. Ramana">M.V. Ramana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Z._Jacobson" title="Mark Z. Jacobson">Mark Z. Jacobson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Diesendorf" title="Mark Diesendorf">Mark Diesendorf</a>. They asserted that nuclear power needs large subsidies to be economically viable, and typically there are substantial construction delays 
and cost overruns associated with nuclear plants. Sovacool et al. also claim that Kharecha and Hansen's estimates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chernobyl_Disaster" title="Chernobyl Disaster">Chernobyl Disaster</a> mortalities is very low, which biases their conclusions. All of these factors are said to make Kharecha and Hansen's article "incomplete and misleading".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Kharecha and Hansen then countered each of their attempts at rebutting their paper and argued that all the data these scientists use to make their criticism, "lacks credibility".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, with three other leading climate experts, Hansen was co-author of an open letter to policy makers, which stated that "continued opposition to nuclear power threatens humanity's ability to avoid dangerous climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> The reaction from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear" title="Anti-nuclear">anti-nuclear</a> environmental groups (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>) was negative, citing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_safety_and_security" title="Nuclear safety and security">nuclear safety and security</a> issues, and the <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_nuclear_power_plants" title="Economics of nuclear power plants">economics of nuclear power plants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_awards">Honors and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Honors and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hansen was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1996 for his "development of pioneering radiative transfer models and studies of planetary atmospheres; development of simplified and three-dimensional global climate models; explication of climate forcing mechanisms; analysis of current climate trends from observational data; and projections of anthropogenic impacts on the global climate system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> In 2001, he received the 7th Annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinz_Award" title="Heinz Award">Heinz Award</a> in the Environment (endowed with US$250,000) for his research on global warming,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> and was listed as one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Magazine" title="Time Magazine">Time Magazine</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100">100 Most Influential People</a> in 2006. Also in 2006, the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> (AAAS) selected James Hansen to receive its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Award_for_Scientific_Freedom_and_Responsibility" title="Award for Scientific Freedom and Responsibility">Award for Scientific Freedom and Responsibility</a> "for his courageous and steadfast advocacy in support of scientists' responsibilities to communicate their scientific opinions and findings openly and honestly on matters of public importance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, Hansen shared the US $1-million <a href="/wiki/Dan_David_Prize" title="Dan David Prize">Dan David Prize</a> for "achievements having an outstanding scientific, technological, cultural or social impact on our world". In 2008, he received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PNC_Bank" title="PNC Bank">PNC Bank</a> <a href="/wiki/Common_Wealth_Award_of_Distinguished_Service" title="Common Wealth Award of Distinguished Service">Common Wealth Award of Distinguished Service</a> for his "outstanding achievements" in science. At the end of 2008, Hansen was named by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EarthSky_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EarthSky Communications (page does not exist)">EarthSky Communications</a> and a panel of 600 scientist-advisors as the <i>Scientist Communicator of the Year</i>, citing him as an "outspoken authority on climate change" who had "best communicated with the public about vital science issues or concepts during 2008."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossby_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-rossby-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, Hansen was awarded the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Carl-Gustaf_Rossby_Research_Medal" title="Carl-Gustaf Rossby Research Medal">Carl-Gustaf Rossby Research Medal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rossby_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-rossby-112">[112]</a></sup> the highest honor bestowed by the <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a>, for his "outstanding contributions to climate modeling, understanding climate change forcings and sensitivity, and for clear communication of climate science in the public arena."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hansen won the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Prize" title="Sophie Prize">Sophie Prize</a>, set up in 1997 by Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Jostein_Gaarder" title="Jostein Gaarder">Jostein Gaarder</a>, the author of the 1991 best-selling novel and teenagers' guide to philosophy <i><a href="/wiki/Sophie%27s_World" title="Sophie's World">Sophie's World</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> for his " key role for the development of our understanding of human-induced climate change."
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Policy" title="Foreign Policy">Foreign Policy</a></i> named Hansen one of its 2012 <a href="/wiki/FP_Top_100_Global_Thinkers" title="FP Top 100 Global Thinkers">FP Top 100 Global Thinkers</a> "for sounding the alarm on climate change, early and often".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2012, Hansen received the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Club_of_California" title="Commonwealth Club of California">Commonwealth Club of California</a>'s annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_H._Schneider" title="Stephen H. Schneider">Stephen H. Schneider</a> Award for Outstanding Climate Science Communications at a ceremony in San Francisco<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 7, 2013 Hansen received the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> Award at <a href="/wiki/Dickinson_College" title="Dickinson College">Dickinson College</a> in Carlisle, Pennsylvania "...for his work advancing our understanding of climate change, including the early application of numerical models to better understand observed climate trends and to project humans' impact on climate, and for his leadership in promoting public understanding of climate and linking the knowledge to action on climate policy." He delivered a lecture, entitled, "White House Arrest and the Climate Crisis," later that same day at Anita Tuvin Schlechter Auditorium on the college's campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p><p>James Hansen was co-winner with climatologist <a href="/wiki/Syukuro_Manabe" title="Syukuro Manabe">Syukuro Manabe</a> of the <a href="/wiki/BBVA_Foundation_Frontiers_of_Knowledge_Award" title="BBVA Foundation Frontiers of Knowledge Award">BBVA Foundation Frontiers of Knowledge Award</a> in the Climate Change category in the ninth edition (2016) of the awards. The two laureates were separately responsible for constructing the first computational models with the power to simulate climate behavior. Decades ago, they correctly predicted how much Earth's temperature would rise due to increasing atmospheric CO2. The scores of models currently in use to chart climate evolution are heirs to those developed by Manabe and Hansen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In June 2018, Hansen was named joint winner, with <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">Veerabhadran Ramanathan</a>, of Taiwan's <a href="/wiki/Tang_Prize" title="Tang Prize">Tang Prize</a> Foundation prize.  Hansen's prize had a total value of NT$25 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_interference_at_NASA">Political interference at NASA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Political interference at NASA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, Hansen alleged that <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> administrators had attempted to influence his public statements about the <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">causes of climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> Hansen said that NASA public relations staff were ordered to review his public statements and interviews after a December 2005 lecture at the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco</a>. NASA responded that its policies are similar to those of any other federal agency in requiring employees to coordinate all statements with the public affairs office without exception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC1_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC1-120">[120]</a></sup> Two years after Hansen and other agency employees described a pattern of distortion and suppression of climate science by political <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/appointee" title="wikt:appointee">appointees</a>, the agency's inspector general confirmed that such activities had taken place, with the NASA Office of Public Affairs having "reduced, marginalized or mischaracterized climate change science made available to the general public".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2006, Hansen appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i> stating that the George W. Bush White House had edited climate-related press releases reported by federal agencies to make global warming seem less threatening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbs-73">[73]</a></sup> He also stated that he was unable to speak freely without the backlash of other government officials, and that he had not experienced that level of restrictions on communicating with the public during his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-cbs-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trials_for_energy_company_executives">Trials for energy company executives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Trials for energy company executives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008 interviews with <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, and in a separate op-ed, Hansen has called for putting <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> company executives, including the CEOs of <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peabody_Coal" title="Peabody Coal">Peabody Coal</a>, on trial for "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_crimes" title="High crimes">high crimes</a> against humanity and nature", on the grounds that these and other fossil-fuel companies had actively spread doubt and misinformation about <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, in the same way that <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> companies tried to hide the link between smoking and cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc20ylater_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc20ylater-122">[122]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardtrial_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardtrial-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ww20ylater_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-ww20ylater-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrest_for_protest_demonstration">Arrest for protest demonstration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Arrest for protest demonstration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Hansen urged President Obama to reject the <a href="/wiki/Keystone_Pipeline#Keystone_XL" title="Keystone Pipeline">Keystone pipeline extension</a> intended to carry more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude_oil" title="Synthetic crude oil">synthetic crude oil</a> from Canada's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Tar_Sands" title="Athabasca Tar Sands">Athabasca Tar Sands</a> to the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mongabay2011_99-2"><a href="#cite_note-mongabay2011-99">[99]</a></sup> On February 13, 2013, Hansen was again arrested at the White House, along with Daryl Hannah and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy,_Jr." title="Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.</a>, during a further protest against the proposed Keystone pipeline extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhxl_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-dhxl-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Hansen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2009, Andrew Freedman wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, that the <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> had erred in giving Hansen its <a href="/wiki/Carl-Gustaf_Rossby_Research_Medal" title="Carl-Gustaf Rossby Research Medal">Carl-Gustaf Rossby Research Medal</a>: "His body of work is not at issue... Rather, the problem arises due to the AMS' recognition of Hansen's public communication work on climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-125">[125]</a></sup> Former AMS member <a href="/wiki/Joseph_D%27Aleo" title="Joseph D'Aleo">Joseph D'Aleo</a>, a skeptic of human-caused climate change, also criticized the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_125-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2009, physicist <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a> criticised Hansen's climate-change activism. "The person who is really responsible for this overestimate of global warming is Jim Hansen. He consistently exaggerates all the dangers... Hansen has turned his science into ideology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNYT_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-DNYT-127">[127]</a></sup> Hansen responded that if Dyson "is going to wander into something with major consequences for humanity and other life on the planet, then he should first do his homework".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DNYT_127-1"><a href="#cite_note-DNYT-127">[127]</a></sup> Dyson stated in an interview that the argument with Hansen was exaggerated by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, stating that he and Hansen are "friends, but we don't agree on everything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup>
</p><p>After Hansen's arrest in 2009 in West Virginia, <i>New York Times</i> columnist <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Revkin" title="Andrew Revkin">Andrew Revkin</a> wrote: "Dr. Hansen has pushed far beyond the boundaries of the conventional role of scientists, particularly government scientists, in the environmental policy debate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT.E_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT.E-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">New Yorker</a></i> journalist <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Kolbert" title="Elizabeth Kolbert">Elizabeth Kolbert</a> wrote that Hansen is "increasingly isolated among climate activists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EKNY_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-EKNY-129">[129]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eileen_Claussen" title="Eileen Claussen">Eileen Claussen</a>, president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pew_Center_on_Global_Climate_Change" title="Pew Center on Global Climate Change">Pew Center on Global Climate Change</a>, said that "I view Jim Hansen as heroic as a scientist.... But I wish he would stick to what he really knows. Because I don't think he has a realistic idea of what is politically possible, or what the best policies would be to deal with this problem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EKNY_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-EKNY-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2009, <i>New York Times</i> climate columnist Christa Marshall asked if Hansen still matters in the ongoing climate debate, noting that he "has irked many longtime supporters with his scathing attacks against President Obama's plan for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap-and-trade" title="Cap-and-trade">cap-and-trade</a> system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMa_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-CMa-130">[130]</a></sup> "The right wing loves what he's doing," said <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Romm" title="Joseph Romm">Joseph Romm</a>, a senior fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_American_Progress" title="Center for American Progress">Center for American Progress</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMa_130-1"><a href="#cite_note-CMa-130">[130]</a></sup> Hansen said that he had to speak out, since few others could explain the links between politics and the climate models. "You just have to say what you think is right," he said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMa_130-2"><a href="#cite_note-CMa-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
